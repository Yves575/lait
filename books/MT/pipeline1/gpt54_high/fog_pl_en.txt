Prologue


He was due to meet Zofia at eight. She had called that morning and asked to see him. He thought: No. He said: All right. Then he regretted it, but it was too late. She seemed agitated. She stammered into the receiver and drew breath loudly.

—I have to see you, she said. —I wouldn’t ask if it weren’t really important.

As always, he felt helpless before her quiet, low voice, before the łs she pronounced with particular care, and the ą she made too nasal, as if some round little creature with a lively tail had settled deep in her throat and was tickling her larynx. He gave in, involuntarily, to a rush of feeling before he had time to think, to remember that all those elegant-sounding syllables were no longer his. Delicate, exquisite Zofia with the charming speech defect had ceased to see him. And yet she had called… Why? What for? Had something happened? Unease seized him again. Zofia had always been wrapped in an aura of mystery. That was what drew him most. From the very beginning.



On Thursdays she always arrived last. During the meetings she sat off to one side. She hardly spoke, then left quietly, without saying goodbye to anyone. At first she made people uneasy, but in time everyone got used to her silent presence. Every now and then the leader would ask her a question, and she answered briefly and to the point. The others quickly lost interest in her. Everyone except him. He waited for Zofia every Thursday. He watched on the sly her white hands, her brown, moist eyes, the chestnut hair veiling her face. After a month, without asking permission, he changed his place in the circle and sat against the wall. That day he asked her for coffee. Two weeks later the leader summoned them for a serious talk. He forbade private contact and ordered them to sit apart. He spoke sharply and decisively, saying he had no intention of tolerating insubordination in the group. He addressed himself mostly to Zofia.

—I’ve been watching you for quite some time, he said. —I don’t understand why you come here at all. You should think seriously about that. You’re a bad influence on the others.

She listened calmly, then picked up her coat and handbag.

—All right.

She turned in the doorway.

—I won’t be coming back.

He ran after her. He knew he would have to give up the meetings, but he wasn’t worried about that at all. That was how the most beautiful three months of his life began.



He lowered his head and gave a start, as if the sight of his own hands had surprised and frightened him. The old feelings came back in a trembling, heaving wave. Three months, he thought with a grimace. Three months in which he had believed he truly mattered to someone. It didn’t last long… The affair ended as abruptly as it had begun. They hadn’t even had time to get to know each other properly. He knew so little about her… But he hadn’t asked questions. He preferred love to blossom slowly, to gather strength and momentum. He didn’t want to press her or rush anything. He dreamed of a holiday together, waited for the day she would finally introduce him to her parents, then he would move in with her, they’d renovate the place, put in a double bed… In time they’d buy a bigger apartment for the children… For three months he spun ever more expansive plans for the future; he felt calm and sure of himself. And then suddenly it was over. Zofia stopped answering the phone. He went to see what was happening to her. He knocked on the door. She didn’t open, but he had the irresistible impression that someone was inside the flat. Sitting on the stairs, he heard the muffled sound of conversation, shuffling, the creak of floorboards. Then for the first time in three months he thought of the Thursday meetings, and fear took hold of him. He went home. The next day he went back to her. She opened the door but didn’t let him in, blocking the entrance. At once it came to him that someone was hiding behind her. A lover? he thought, and such despair and weakness swept over him that he had to lean against the wall. Zofia looked at him gravely with her large, moist eyes.

—I’m sorry, she said. —I shouldn’t be ending it this way, but…

He heard his own loud, uneven breathing. He felt himself going pale. His head was spinning.



—Something’s happened, she went on firmly. —We can’t see each other anymore.

That was all. Like the stroke of a guillotine. No explanations, no clarification. They met once more and she repeated exactly the same thing: certain changes had taken place in her life, they wouldn’t be seeing each other anymore. Her eyes had lost their former softness. They had become cold and resolute. “Certain changes” could only mean a third person; he had no doubt of that.

The following Thursday he tried to return to the circle. They wouldn’t take him back, but the leader assigned him individual meetings. He didn’t hide how worried he was. He claimed the upheaval caused by his relationship with Zofia could have serious consequences, not only for him but for the whole group. He went on at length about responsibility, obligations, accepted rules of conduct. Then he questioned him closely about his relationship with Zofia: what they had done over those three months, where they met, what they talked about. It seemed to him the leader was being too pushy and intrusive, but he tried to answer. It got him nowhere. He suffered from a kind of strange amnesia. Nothing remained in his memory except despair, slowly turning into rage and uncontrollable jealousy. If Zofia didn’t want to be with him, then she couldn’t—had no right to—be with anyone else!



But she’d called today, he thought. Could it be she had finally decided to apologize after all? His heart began to pound. Come back, make it up, after using him and casting him off? What on earth did she think she was doing? He felt humiliated at the thought of the past weeks, of all those pleading attempts to get in touch, the tearful phone calls, the letters, the sleepless nights. He kept trying to get to Zofia, force her to talk, recover the old closeness that surely couldn’t simply vanish, dissolve without leaving a trace… He still thought he would find the right words, convince her, undo the spell, and once again the old gentle Zofia would stand before him, his Zofia! Meanwhile she remained as indifferent as ever, distant and alien… He sprang from his chair and began pacing the room, raising his hands to his face and tugging at the hair that fell over his forehead. He went to the window. He looked out at the wet, ugly city: squares covered in mud and melting snow, gray pavements, the colorless facades of the apartment blocks opposite. Now and then a blurred figure flickered behind one of the windows. In front of the neighborhood shop a few people stamped their feet and hunched under umbrellas. She had used him as if he were a thing, a dead object that could be used up and then thrown away, not a living, feeling creature. A human being. A man, he corrected himself. He paced the room again, wheeling sharply at the door and the opposite wall, only to stop short by the window a moment later. In every direction stretched dozens of apartment blocks: heavy, gloomy, smelling faintly of mildew. In small concrete flats strangers bustled about. Identical concrete units, vertically and horizontally. Inside, the same tables, beds and armchairs. Almost identical men and women cooked dinner, watched television together, went to the bathroom to take a hot bath or have a piss. Did she know, did she even suspect, how badly she had hurt him? He looked at his watch. It was nearly one.



He was in a state of extreme irritation all day. He poked around online, watched two episodes of *Brigada*, the Russian mafia series. Early in the afternoon he went to the neighborhood gym. He ran for an hour, then lifted weights and worked his abs. He tried to switch off, to think only of Sasha, Kosmos, Bee and Phil—the brutal heroes of the series, ready to risk anything. Afterward he went to the sauna and took a cold shower. When he got home, he took the milk from the fridge and sat at the table. He drank straight from the carton in long, slow swallows. The irritation hardened into a brutal, solid fury. Once more he replayed the morning’s conversation and came to the conclusion that Zofia had not called with reconciliation in mind. Her voice had been uncertain, but there had been no remorse in it. Short, matter-of-fact sentences, no “I’ve missed you” or “I’m glad I’ll see you.” A perfunctory “Goodbye” at the end. Bitch, he thought, watching a plane climbing into the sky. A ragged streak tore through the dense gray clouds. But what could Zofia want from him? For the hundredth time that day he asked himself the same question. It didn’t matter. This would be his moment. She had invited him, so she would politely listen to what he had to say. He pictured himself entering the flat, telling her to sit down, explaining things briefly, succinctly, in a firm voice, and at last she would understand… And if she didn’t want to listen? He could hit her, even tie her up. Tape her mouth shut and watch her cry soundlessly, terrified, helpless, at his mercy. The plane climbed higher and vanished above the clouds. In the flat upstairs someone put on loud dance music. He could hear muffled laughter and the sound of quick, light footsteps.



It was nearing half past six. He put the milk back in the fridge. He pulled on a short jacket with an elastic waistband, gloves, and trainers. He tugged a cotton hood over his head. Outside, a strong, piercing wind was blowing; wet snow clung to his shoes, which were soaked through at once. Even so, he didn’t turn toward the metro. He walked stubbornly along the pavement by KEN Avenue, neither stopping nor getting out of anyone’s way. Zofia’s face rose before his eyes, her broad mouth, straight nose, the small mole at the corner of her eye, and the sidelong glance she gave him when she said: “You finally have to understand that I want nothing to do with you.” He remembered his own agitation, his fear, the burning feeling of rejection. How thoroughly he had humiliated himself, made a fool of himself… He quickened his pace until at last he began to run, driven on by grief and helpless anger. “Yob tvoyu mat’! Srat’ tebe v rot!” he repeated, unconsciously imitating the voice of one of the heroes of *Brigada*. He didn’t stop until Służewiec; then he went down to the platform, took the metro to Racławicka, and set off again along the snowy street, passing the lit windows of cafés, workshops, and small neighborhood shops. Just beyond the school playing field he turned into a gateway and nearly collided with an elderly man wearing a thick wool coat and a fur hat.

—Idi na chuj! he snarled, then shoved past him brutally, crushing the man against the wall.

He was struggling for breath. His obsessive thoughts went round and round, making him sickeningly dizzy. Beads of sweat ran down his face.



Zofia got home before five. She set her shopping bag on the kitchen table, took off her soaked shoes and, leaving wet footprints on the floor, went into the bathroom. She lay in the tub thinking how much her life had become complicated over the past three months. And whose fault is that? she wondered, wringing out the sponge. “Whose fault?” she repeated aloud. I’m a magnet for bad luck, she thought, standing up and taking the soap in her hand. The more I like someone, the bigger a nutcase he turns out to be. But is it that, for some incomprehensible reason, only lunatics like me, or do I seek them out myself? She rinsed off the foam. She got out of the tub and wrapped a towel around her head. From a wicker basket she took gray tracksuit bottoms and warm wool socks. For a moment she studied her face in the mirror. What am I supposed to do now? she asked her reflection. What am I supposed to do?

Despite her worries, the woman in the mirror plainly had no intention of looking tired or neglected. Zofia pressed two fingers to her cheeks and drew the skin slightly toward her temples. She raised her eyebrows, then reached for powder and mascara. Refreshed and carefully made up, she came out of the bathroom. In the kitchen she made tea. She set out two cups and a plate of French biscuits. Suddenly she stopped in mid-step and stood for a moment staring at the laid table, as if trying to settle some important question in her mind. Then she took out her mobile phone and twice, unsuccessfully, called the same number. At last she left a voice message.

—I wanted to cancel our meeting, she said, lowering her voice to make it sound firmer. —Something important has come up. I’m very sorry. I’ll call tomorrow.



She looked at the phone uncertainly. There was no guarantee the message would be heard. For a moment she stood drumming her fingers nervously on the windowsill. At last she shrugged and sat down in the window recess. It was nearing seven. She looked out at the little square blurred with fog and the abandoned construction site fenced off from the street by a wooden barrier. A solitary rook perched on the dark branch of a maple. She ought to make concrete decisions and weigh their consequences. But nothing sensible came to mind. No theoretical solution seemed good. She was trapped, and there was no way out. The rook shook its head, ruffled its feathers, and hobbled sideways toward the trunk. Zofia drew her knees up to her chin. Along the dark street the wind chased an empty plastic cup. She closed her eyes.

At a quarter past seven the intercom buzzed. She sighed and, without a word, pressed the button by the door. Then, on a sudden impulse, she snatched up the handbag lying under the coat rack. With a decisive movement she took a doctor’s certificate from it. As she walked toward the bathroom, she tore it into tiny pieces and threw them into the toilet.

—I’m in the bathroom! she shouted, hearing the slam of the door. —I’ll be right there, she added, flushing.

At that moment she felt a heavy blow to the temple. One, then another. It didn’t hurt. She wasn’t afraid. An immense weariness came over her. “Who is it?” she wanted to ask before she fell asleep, but blood poured from her open mouth. She tried once more to turn and look at her attacker, but only moaned and sank into deep darkness.



At eight a cutting wind blew in off the icy river. At half past eight the riverside streetlights went out because of a fault. Darkness fell. Only in the middle of the Vistula, where the current was strongest, did the paler outline of a drifting ice floe loom faintly. At nine a sweep of headlights skimmed hurriedly over the water and vanished into the fog. In the brush a startled bird gave a sharp cry. On the escarpment the city rose haloed with light. Above it another river spread itself. Wide and dark. Slowly a slick, polished moon drifted onto it and glided smoothly among the clouds.

She came up out of sleep as if from a well. She opened her eyes and found herself lying beside a polar bear. It wasn’t large. Ada knew, though, that white bears, even the smallest of them, were among the most dangerous animals in the world. She carefully turned onto her left side and curled up among the droppings strewn over the sheet. He must have been relieving himself in the bed all night, she thought. We’ll have to sort that out somehow in future, buy a litter tray or something… The bear heaved itself up, slid to the floor, and began prowling around the room, sniffing and skirting the snowdrifts.



—Get up, it’s breakfast time. A pale, disheveled woman sat down on the other side of the bed. She took an infant from her breast and laid it on the pillows. —Do you think I can leave her here? she asked.

—Sure.

Ada was glad Kasia hadn’t noticed the soiled bedding. Otherwise she probably wouldn’t have laid the baby right in the middle of the filth.

—Sure, she repeated. —The bear is as gentle as a lamb, he loves babies.

At that moment she was convinced she was telling the truth.

They put on slippers, closed the door behind them, and set off down the dimly lit corridor. The floor of rough, unplaned boards creaked under their feet. The kitchen was narrow, dark, tiled the color of tomato soup. A small barred window looked out onto a little yard enclosed by wire fencing. It smelled of burning and rotting rags. Kasia poured tea into glasses and made sandwiches. Only when they were on their way back to the room did Ada remember that the day before the bear had eaten two puppies and the neighbor’s cat. There was nothing to be done now. She opened the door. The white beast lay on the bed licking its muzzle. All that remained of the baby was a bloody stain on the pillow.

—Kasia, she whispered, catching her sister by the shoulders. —Kasia…



A desperate cry woke her. Filip-Oleńka stood at the foot of the bed, meowing. Kasia doesn’t have children—that was the first thought that came to her. She felt relief. She sat up, ran her fingers through her short, rumpled hair, stroked Filip-Oleńka, then got up and, stumbling over the cat as it kept darting under her feet, headed for the kitchen. The first cold stains of dawn were spreading across the windowsill. A garbage truck pulled up outside the house; somewhere high beneath the paling sky terns were crying. She switched on the light. The kitchen looked nothing like the one in the dream. A bright, clean floor of white-painted boards, wooden cupboards, a large square window, the whole thing designed straight out of an IKEA catalogue. She took two ice cubes from the fridge and carefully rubbed them over her face, set the coffee machine going, and went to the bathroom to take a shower. Five minutes later she stood clean and wrapped in a towel before the open wardrobe. Perfectly ironed shirts and trousers hung in rows by color: beige, gray, navy, green, like a luxury clothes shop. Even the underwear on the shelves was folded into neat white squares. Ada took out tight jeans, an orange T-shirt, and a brown sweater with a wide roll neck. She dressed quickly and sat down at the antique-style dressing table.



She was nearing forty, but had kept her slim figure and her light, girlish way of moving. She was used to thinking she still looked like a student. That had still been true ten years earlier, but time moved inexorably on and marked her pale, delicate face with a merciless stamp she had stubbornly decided not to notice. The skin on her cheeks had slackened and sagged slightly around the chin. Her eyes, dark and attentive, had lost their former brightness, giving way to an expression of cool, matter-of-fact assurance, while her broad mouth, oddly as if cut upside down, had acquired the shy parentheses of first wrinkles. Ada took powder, blusher, and mascara from the drawer. A few short, decisive strokes of the brush soon gave her the look of someone whose age could not possibly be guessed. She swept her dark, ragged fringe to one side, tucked her hair behind her ears, then nodded to her reflection as if greeting someone coolly and with reserve, while curtly paying due respect.

She went back to the kitchen and switched off the coffee machine. Filip-Oleńka leapt onto the table so suddenly and silently that she flinched and spilled coffee.

—You mustn’t, she scolded sharply.

She added milk and sat over the steaming cup. For a moment she looked at the graying square of the window. The remnants of the dream were coming loose from her slowly. All it had left behind was a vague sense of unease, and Ada pushed it away with irritation. The cat kept meowing.

—Not now! she raised her voice, trying to make it sound firm, and yet she couldn’t suppress a smile as she watched the animal shifting from paw to paw, arching its back and striking ever more ingratiating poses.



—You can’t eat so much or you’ll get fat, and I don’t think we want that, she said more gently, and went into the hall. Filip-Oleńka trotted in front of her with soft, stubborn steps. She moved him aside. She put on her coat, gloves, tall brown leather boots, grabbed her handbag, and closed the door behind her.

Inspector Marcin Sawicki yanked the old wheezing Opel into a sharp turn.

—Police, you asshole! he shouted at the blaring Nissan driver he had just cut off, and braked on the pavement just in front of a no-parking sign.

—A woman, he muttered, freeing himself from the seat belt. —A woman, he repeated, walking down the street in the direction he absolutely ought not to have been heading. He passed the Coffee Bar, a hosiery shop, a kiosk, and pushed open the door of a Starbucks. He shuddered and then, against all common sense, ordered a double espresso and a chicken bagel. He paid eighteen zlotys and immediately felt an unpleasant stab of guilt. Almost twenty zlotys, he thought in horror while waiting for the coffee. Money gone for good. He tried to work out how much he spent each month on food outside the home, but stopped at once when it occurred to him that on top of that there were cigarettes, beer, and the chocolate bars he was addicted to. He was nearly six foot three, but now, standing there leaning against the counter, wedging his elbow between bowls of white and brown sugar and nervously picking at a paper napkin that said *Have a nice day*, he seemed to shrink and cave in on himself. His shoulders hunched, his belly stuck out, and his broad, friendly face took on the unhappy expression of a wronged little boy. He felt his cheeks trembling.

—Espresso-bagel! called a pretty dark-haired girl in a company apron.



He gave a start, cast the waitress the look of a scolded dog, and took his order. He made his way to a table with tired steps. He hadn’t slept a wink the night before. He had spent long hours in front of the computer, watching episode after episode of an American series, masturbating during the uncensored sex scenes and hoping the darkness outside the window would never be broken by the pink streak of dawn. But dawn had come. Not pale pink, but corpse-blue, and Sawicki had had to get up and face reality.

This isn’t good, he thought, swallowing the last bites of the bagel, from which he had earlier removed the arugula. He had nothing against women working in the police, for instance as secretaries. Even in patrol. But a woman in CID… a woman detective… and in Terror, on top of that… A detectress, maybe, he thought with a sour laugh as he finished his coffee.

Detective or detectress, Sawicki would have to babysit her over the coming months, until whoever upstairs had gone out of their mind finally came to their senses and transferred her. That it was only a temporary situation he had no doubt. No one in their right mind surely believed a woman could be an operative—an operativette, or whatever. He had heard of such cases, admittedly: women detectives, mannish broads with no families, no friends, no personal lives, possessed by a mania for hunting criminals. Supposedly they were good at it. But at Mostowski Palace no female detective had worked in the field for a long time. And everyone was happy about that.



It was after nine. He set down his cup, wiped his mouth on the words *Wonderful coffee, wonderful life*, heaved himself to his feet, and left the café. It was the last day of February. All of Warsaw was waiting tensely for spring, but spring was in no hurry to arrive. Pale winter sun had risen above the rooftops, striking the snow with thousands of glittering sparks like thousands of painful little needles. On the plastic roofs of tram shelters huddled puffed-up pigeons; cooing and shuffling in place, they followed every passing car with dull eyes. Spindly leafless trees stood in disciplined rows along the pavements. A crowd bundled in down jackets and woolen caps streamed across Bank Square, the caps being worn that season in an exceptionally idiotic way, shoved back from the forehead to the middle of the head. Sawicki narrowed his eyes, gave a quiet groan, and, wishing sinusitis on all the fashionably dressed passersby, headed for Mostowski Palace.

A few people were pacing nervously in front of reception: a young man in a short military jacket with an alarmingly skinny girl whose hair was dyed pink, an older gentleman, and two men in work overalls. The duty officer had vanished. Sawicki opened the gate with his magnetic card, crossed the inner courtyard and, dragging his feet, climbed to the second floor. The door to the secretariat stood open.

—Good morning. But it’s cold, isn’t it? Ms. Dagmara shook her teased blond mane from behind the desk and the green fern standing on it. —In-tol-er-able.

—Cold, Sawicki agreed, rubbing his whitened hands.

The secretary gave him a kindly smile, sniffed, and went back to staring into space. He glanced at the blue elephant-shaped wall clock hanging just above her head. He was nearly twenty minutes late; that woman must already have been waiting. Or maybe she hadn’t come at all? flashed through his mind. Maybe she had given up or been transferred somewhere else, to Kraków perhaps, or Olsztyn or Białystok? Who knew what might have happened in the past month…

—Any messages for me? he asked Ms. Dagmara.

—No, she replied, turning her head with cool, deeply satisfied slowness.

—Anything worth telling me about… today? he pressed.

—Noooo, she repeated, touching the tip of her nose with her forefinger. —No.



For some reason her skillfully made-up face and her jangling earrings of colored glass irritated Sawicki more than usual, so unexpectedly and violently that he had trouble controlling his voice.

—Did anyone ask for me? Ms. Dagmara, please concentrate! he said much too loudly.

The secretary jumped, shook her bleached fringe, and widened her eyes with hysterical intensity.

—Actually yes, she admitted at last, and her face at once resumed its vague expression of contentment. —The deputy chief is waiting with some lady in the briefing room. The new one, because they’re renovating the old one… For a month now, she added by way of explanation.

—Really? he hissed. —I had no idea.

—Really, she nodded cheerfully. —A pipe burst upstairs and—

—I know, Ms. Dagmara, I work here too! he interrupted with cold, undisguised brutality. —What I’d like, however, is for important information to reach me. That’s what we employ you for!

—But there wasn’t any information, she protested calmly, lowering her voice like a well-brought-up child. —Only that lady came and is waiting with Rogowski. In the briefing room… the new one… she added uncertainly.

Sawicki sighed, ran a hand through the remnants of his red hair, and trudged deeper into the corridor. Before he reached the door, he regretted his outburst. I’ll have to apologize to her, he decided, and pressed down the handle.

Two people were waiting in the room. A small dark-haired woman whose face he couldn’t yet make out, and the deputy chief of Warsaw Police Headquarters’ Criminal Terror and Homicide Division, Artur Rogowski, known to his colleagues as Rog.

—You’re twenty minutes late, Rog growled, though there was relief in his face. Evidently he had been prepared for the possibility that Sawicki might not come at all. —This is Ms. Adela Rochniewicz… He coughed and wiped his bald skull with a linen handkerchief that looked heavily used. —Meet each other.



He stood there flushed and slovenly, squinting with reddened eyes and clearing his throat loudly. Plainly he had no idea how to behave. So he shifted from foot to foot and rubbed his forehead with the dirty handkerchief until the silence in the room became decidedly too awkward. At last he grimaced slightly, as if to say, *Sorry, old man.* But all he said was:

—I’ll leave you two to it and get back to my office.

He went out, hitching up his slipping trousers, coughing and snuffling loudly, which struck Sawicki as even more repulsive than usual.

Ada Rochniewicz was sitting sideways to the door. Slanting rays came through the window, brightening the small room grandly known as the new briefing room. Quivering patches of sunlight lay on the wall and gray carpet. Lit motes of dust danced in the air. Ada, however, remained in shadow. Sawicki could see only the delicate, fragile line of her back and bowed head, and the sight allowed him, for a moment, to regain his equilibrium. He walked up to the table with a firm step. She was pretty. Dressed expensively, fashionably, but with restraint, as if she were going out to lunch in a restaurant rather than to a meeting at police headquarters. She could not have been more than thirty, had short bangs and a wide mouth of a curious shape that gave her face a slightly plaintive expression. And yet he vaguely sensed that behind that innocent little face there was something else. Something he absolutely should not underestimate.

—Marcin Sawicki, he introduced himself gruffly.

—Good morning, she said.

—Sorry I’m late, he muttered, sitting down. —Traffic… I understand you’d like to work with us, he began, taking a bundle of yellowed documents from his briefcase. It was a copy of the contract he had signed with a cable TV company a few months earlier, but he felt more secure laying the stack of papers in front of him. —Apart from this—he tapped the contract with his finger, at the same time covering the company’s colorful logo with his elbow—is there anything else I should know?



For the first time she looked him straight in the eyes. She wasn’t smiling, but the corners of her mouth trembled slightly, as if she were suppressing amusement. He clenched his teeth.

—What professional experience do you have? Would you describe yourself as someone with a flexible schedule? he went on, though she hadn’t answered any of his previous questions.

He was aware that he was behaving ridiculously, pretending to this strange woman that he was her future employer, when they both knew perfectly well he had not the slightest influence over the decision to transfer her. Suddenly, however, it seemed to him that with sheer willpower and stubbornness he could force her to retreat. He would win this last skirmish, hand her back her papers, and say: “I’m sorry, we have better candidates for this post. Maybe next time.”

—I was posted here, she said calmly.

—Yes, yes… So you’re looking for work in the police. In the police, he repeated, stressing the last word, hoping in that way to prove the firmness of his own character and conceal the obvious absurdity of the situation.

—I’m not looking. Her voice didn’t change. —I was assigned here.

She had to be older than thirty. At first her slim, girlish figure had deceived him. Now he could see quite clearly the fine wrinkles at the corners of her eyes and the horizontal furrows crossing her forehead.

—Police work comes with many inconveniences, he continued, hoping at last to throw her off balance. —Especially for women. We here, madam, operate under conditions hard to endure even for a man in the prime of life. You never know when and where we’ll be sent out. A young girl like you… How exactly do you propose to reconcile family obligations, husband, children, with—

—I don’t have children, she interrupted. —And I don’t intend to.

He sensed in those words the promise of trouble. He immediately lifted his head, but could see nothing in her face he might seize on and use.

—I don’t make a secret of that, though I know these are questions you shouldn’t be asking, she said quietly and calmly, but her dark eyes remained steady and alert, like a surgeon’s. —I didn’t come here for a job interview, and you know it perfectly well. You’re not my superior, only my new colleague. I’d prefer it if we finally stopped acting out this strange comedy.

Sawicki’s determination began to melt with alarming speed.

—That depends on whether we can get along, he muttered. He tried to keep up appearances, but his body refused to cooperate and he noticed with horror that he was squirming in his chair and reflexively wiping his sweaty hands on the tabletop.

—That’s true. Ada smiled calmly. —I hope we’ll manage somehow. And now perhaps I could have a look around headquarters and meet the rest of the team?

—Tomorrow, he almost shouted. —Yes, tomorrow, because tomorrow is the first… You don’t start until tomorrow…

—Ada, she interrupted quickly. —My name is Ada.

—Right, Ada, he cleared his throat. —Today we’ve got renovations going on here and total chaos. Some of the boys are out in the field… So let’s begin tomorrow at eight.

—All right. See you tomorrow, then.

She stood up, took her bag, and was gone before he had time to answer.

Stunned, Sawicki stared blankly at the wall. Was that it? All over? A done deal? Why, the whole conversation hadn’t lasted even five minutes. He had been defeated, pinned to the mat with a single gentle gesture by that tiny woman with the looks of an aging teenager. He ran his hand over his face.



Ada Rochniewicz had worked for eight years in the Wrocław police. He had received that information a month earlier, along with the announcement that from the beginning of March he would have a woman for a colleague, a woman cop, a woman detective, neither fish nor fowl, and absolutely no possibility of appealing his superiors’ decision. He knew little else about her.

At the beginning of February Rogowski, after a meeting with the chief, came back to the room they shared and sat down at his desk, coughing and sniffling.

—A chick? You’re sure? Sawicki asked without taking his eyes off the computer.

—A chick, a chick. The old man’s trying to soften our image. Our political correctness will improve when we take her on.

He was fidgeting in his chair. Sawicki knew Rog was casting anxious glances at him, but he kept stubbornly tapping away at the keyboard. He had plenty of work; he didn’t feel like talking. Rogowski snorted and blew his nose loudly into a checkered handkerchief. Sawicki pushed out his lower lip.

—What? he finally asked, hitting Delete instead of Tab and erasing a painstakingly completed line of the report.

—You’re supposed to take care of her, Rogowski explained, and immediately broke into the dry cough of a chain smoker.

Sawicki was dumbstruck. He stared at the monitor for a moment, then slapped the desk with his open palm.

—Are you out of your fucking mind? Haven’t I got enough work without babysitting some twat as well? he flared up. —This your idea? His voice rose higher and higher as Rogowski’s coughing intensified. —Get that out of your bald head.

He was furious in earnest. Rogowski was deputy chief, but everyone knew that unofficially he acted as head of the section. He made most of the decisions. Sawicki jumped up and left the room.

The next day, when his anger had eased a little, they managed to talk calmly.



—It’s not bad, Rogowski explained in a soothing tone. —I’ve seen her papers. She completed officer training. Before that, nursing school… Apparently she’s a good investigator. She’s solved several serious cases. You’ll be pleased.

Sawicki shrugged.

—Eight years in the Wrocław police, Rog went on, undeterred. —Enthusiastic letters of recommendation. A commendation for taking part in breaking up the BAF organized crime group, he declaimed like a schoolboy reciting at a Constitution Day assembly.

Sawicki remembered having heard something about it once. Seven people had been arrested and forty kilos of cocaine seized that had been meant for smuggling across the German border.

—But… Rog added less confidently now, —in 2010 she was transferred to SDS. Desk work…

Sawicki raised his eyebrows in surprise. A desk jockey? Why would they suddenly send her back to Criminal now? After two years warming a chair and filling out forms, you didn’t go back to the streets…

—It doesn’t add up, he said.

—I know. Unless…

—Unless someone high up really wanted to transfer Adela Rochniewicz, nurse-investigator, to Warsaw and pulled the right strings, he finished angrily. —Has she got a husband? Any children?

—The marital-status box was blank.

—Why doesn’t that surprise me? he muttered.

They didn’t speak of her again until yesterday, when Rogowski told him to report to the briefing room at nine the next day and take charge of the new lady inspector.

Sawicki sighed, stretched his arms in front of him, laced his fingers together, and twisted his hands until the joints cracked, then slowly got up and went down to reception to buy coffee from the vending machine. The men in overalls were dozing on the wooden benches by the wall, the boy in the military jacket was swearing loudly, and the underfed girl was trying to calm him down. A large blue sign above the counter read: PUBLIC ENQUIRIES. The duty policeman was still nowhere in sight. On his way back upstairs he picked up forms from the secretariat and decided that today he would do nothing except fill out paperwork.







3


On Friday morning Sawicki woke up almost refreshed. He had slept for seven hours. He got up a few minutes after six. He dressed quietly and limited his ablutions to brushing his teeth. He wanted to drink his coffee alone, in peace. He put the kettle on. The thermometer outside the kitchen window read zero. The sky was overcast. Heavy wet snow had begun to fall. At seven he woke his wife and little daughters. Shortly before eight he arrived at work. Reception was completely quiet, with no trace of visitors. The duty officer was dozing with his head propped on one hand. Upstairs, the door to the secretariat was closed. As he walked down the corridor, he wondered whether Adela Rochniewicz was already waiting for him. He had no doubt punctuality was one of her traits. Punctuality, order, common sense, he thought, taking off his jacket and looking uncertainly around the room. Both desks were piled high with papers. In the cabinet there were heaps of clothes of unknown origin and old sweat-soaked shoes. A potted plant on the windowsill was withering. Algae had taken up residence in the water dispenser. He wiped dust from the sill with his sleeve, removed the papers from the desk that had until then belonged to Rogowski, and cautiously peered into the drawer. He found a rotting sandwich in a plastic bag and an empty vodka bottle.

Ada arrived at three minutes past eight. She shook his hand briefly, like a man. She took off a sailor-style jacket and a jaunty cap with colored trim. Where does she get the money for clothes like that? he wondered. Sweaters like that, woolen shorts, elegant boots? She must be wearing the equivalent of two police salaries. Watching the new lady inspector from the corner of his eye, he recalled with distaste the tacky, colorful clothes his own wife wore.

—Come on, then, I’ll introduce you to the boys, he said with a strained smile.



He wanted to get it over with as quickly as possible. He felt awkward leading her down the corridor. He opened one door after another. He kept repeating, “This is Inspector Adela Rochniewicz. She’ll be working with us from today.” Ada greeted people briefly and politely. Her face wore a cool, official expression. Not once did she show any curiosity. She did not react to the quiet whistles and laughter behind their backs. When they entered the biggest room on the floor, several policemen looked up in surprise. They seemed taken aback. Ada fitted police headquarters like the Queen of England in a baseball-club locker room.

—What a little mimosa you’ve found yourself… muttered Kapusta, a fair-haired policeman with pockmarked, unhealthy skin and a reddened forehead. He said it quietly, as though only to himself, but everyone heard perfectly well. Muffled snickering rose in the room. Sawicki clenched his jaw. Mimosa? Since when did that idiot know words like that? Ada’s face remained unmoved.

In the secretariat Ms. Dagmara took doughnuts from the fridge and rushed to switch on the electric kettle.

—I knew you’d be coming today, and I’m prepared, she chattered. —You absolutely must sit down for a moment and have coffee with me. And you really must come here often! Sawicki is such a boor, she added, ignoring Marcin completely. —Working with men is hard enough. But you know all about that…

Dark mascara had smudged around her left eye. Heavy breasts swayed beneath her pink blouse. She hadn’t even had time to take out the glasses when Rogowski’s round bald head appeared in the doorway.



—A murder’s been reported in Upper Mokotów, he said. —Looks like some young chick’s had her head smashed in. The two of you are going. Kapusta and Chlor are interviewing the car-smuggling lot. Wiktor and I are taking the Old Man out to Praga.

—But she’s only just arrived… Sawicki muttered unwillingly.

—All the better, let her get broken in straight away. I don’t know how they do it in Wrocław, but here we don’t start the day by eating pastries.

—A girl with her head smashed in? Sawicki tried to protest. —Can’t the district cops handle it? We’ve got enough of our own work.

—They’re already there. Rogowski gave an ugly cough. —The district boys from Two, and the ambulance. And Modzelewski, he added pointedly. Then he thrust out a plump hand, snatched a doughnut, and disappeared through the door.

Ada raised her eyebrows.

—Modzelewski is the prosecutor, Dagmara explained hurriedly. —He always calls in headquarters. For every murder. Evidently he’s on duty today.

—And we’re unlucky, Sawicki finished.



It was nearly nine when they left headquarters. The snow had stopped falling, and through the clouds a pale, distant sun showed faintly. The inner courtyard was coated with a layer of ice streaked with trickles of brown water. Slipping, they made their way in silence to the Opel and drove toward Mokotów. The morning traffic jams were just ending. They didn’t turn on the siren. Within fifteen minutes they were there. They parked beneath a four-story prewar tenement and went through a narrow gate into the courtyard. A pair of ruffled crows watched them from a leafless tree. Sawicki pushed open the stairwell door propped with a brick. Ada felt the skin at the nape of her neck prickle. A cold shiver ran down her spine. Suddenly it hit her that she was beginning an investigation. She was in the field again. Two years had passed since the accident that had interrupted her settled life in Wrocław. Two years she had spent excluded, hemmed in by a wall of hostility and distrust. Now she was returning to work. Once again she could do what she was best suited to, what she was truly good at. The long-forgotten sense of excitement returned, then the moment of concentration, and finally a sudden, invigorating rush of energy, like what a wrestler feels just before stepping onto the mat. Ada drew a deep breath, passed Sawicki, and started up the stairs first.



On the landing between the second and third floors two identical-looking men were waiting, dressed in identical leather jackets and the same sharply creased wool trousers. Both were smoking, lifting their cigarettes to their mouths with the same quick, nervous movement. Both inhaled briefly and blew out the smoke loudly through half-open lips. It seemed to Ada as though someone had put a mirror on the landing.

—These are the famous Mokotów twins. Sawicki laughed, seeing her surprise. —Inspector Rochniewicz, he introduced Ada.

—Hi, they replied in unison.

She nodded.

—So you’re taking over the case. The twin on the left rubbed his hands with satisfaction.

—Modzelewski’s already left. You need to drop the paperwork at the office this evening, said the one on the right. —The ambulance was here too. They pronounced her dead. The mortuary people will turn up in forty minutes. At least that’s what they say.

—We haven’t called the undertakers yet, they added simultaneously, and began zipping up their jackets.

At that moment sobbing reached them. In front of the open door on the third floor sat a pale, disheveled girl in an unbuttoned coat. Right beside her, with his back against the wall, stood a uniformed policeman reading a morning tabloid. Clearly the crying made no impression on him whatsoever.

—Ah… this is Monika Lewicka. A friend of the deceased, the twin on the right explained.

—She’s the one who reported the murder, added the one on the left.

Sawicki nodded.

—Right, we’re off. Have fun.

As if on command, they turned and ran down the stairs. The uniformed officer moved after them.

—Just a moment, Ada stopped him mid-step. —Please buy this lady some tea. She indicated the sobbing girl with her chin. —At the café downstairs.



The policeman stopped in surprise.

—Have you got some kind of problem? Ada raised her voice.

—Problem? His eyes bulged and his moustache twitched uneasily.

—A problem, she repeated sharply. —You’ve been standing here for an hour reading a newspaper. My witness is about to pass out. Have you lost every human instinct?

The policeman turned violently red.

—Tea? he spluttered. —With my own money?

Ada opened her wallet.

—Here. She took out ten zlotys.

He recoiled indignantly and opened his mouth to protest, but closed it at once when he saw her knitted brows. He sighed, shuffled from foot to foot like a scolded dog, then reluctantly took the money.

—And one more thing! she called after him.

—Yes?

—Bring it up in twenty minutes, not sooner. And it has to be hot. With sugar, she added pointedly.

He nodded obediently, looked at his watch, and went downstairs.

Sawicki was so amused by the sight of the fleeing moustached man that he bit his lips to keep from bursting out laughing.

—Monika Lewicka? he said to the crying girl. —You’re the one who called the police?

—Yes… she answered, unsuccessfully trying to suppress her sobs. —She’s inside, in the bathroom… she added with effort, lifting her head and brushing the hair from her swollen face.

He pushed the half-open door. Just beyond the threshold he came upon a puddle of vomit.

—I’m sorry, the girl mumbled. —That was me…



Ada took a long step over the threshold and went in first. Sawicki followed her. She stopped in the bathroom doorway. He looked over her shoulder, gave a start, and lightly touched her back. She turned her head. Their eyes met for a moment. On the tiled floor lay a dead woman on her stomach. Her legs and arms were flung out limply, as though she had fallen from a great height. Blood had dried in her long hair. Ada crouched and bent over the body. He didn’t notice the slightest hesitation in her movements. She pulled on silicone gloves. Does she carry them in her jacket pocket all the time? flashed through his mind. Gently she swept aside the matted hair and felt the back of the skull. There was something alien and unreal in the sight of those two women: the dead one and the living one, as impassive as the corpse at her feet. It was not the first time Sawicki had examined a crime scene. He was used to the sight of mangled bodies too. And yet he had expected something else from his new colleague besides a stony face and a pair of white gloves. What exactly? Tears? Hand-wringing? He knew she was an experienced officer… She straightened slowly.

—I think we should call the scene-of-crime team, she said calmly.



Ada sensed Sawicki’s tension at once. For a moment something like sympathy stirred in her for this big, strong man who was plainly afraid of her reaction. She didn’t want to make him uncomfortable. She took the gloves from her pocket and crouched over the body. It was a young woman, in her twenties. Slim, athletic. Ada noticed the muscular calf. There wasn’t much blood. It had flowed from the wound at the temple. The skull at that point was deformed, horribly caved in. Whoever had struck the blow had hit very hard. She gently moved aside the tangled hair. The eyes, wide open, had turned cloudy and were covered with a yellow film. The lips and nostrils had taken on a bluish-brown color. She lifted the edge of the sweatshirt to assess the lividity. Then she stood up.

—She’s been dead at least four hours, she said, and tried deftly to slip past Sawicki, but he stopped her and pointed to the floor. Right beside the toilet lay an abandoned object.

—Murder weapon…

—Yes. He nodded. —The killer left it.

For a while they stared blankly at the bloodied hammer. On the wooden handle there was a clear stamp: “AM-NAT Hardware Store.”

—That’s very odd, she said at last.

Sawicki gave a start and took his mobile out of his pocket.



While he was calling the crime-scene specialists, Ada looked around the pleasant, spacious bedroom: clean floor, neatly made bed, armchair, and on it a folded tartan blanket. She smiled in spite of herself. She liked order. Reflexively she straightened a black-and-white print that hung slightly askew. Light curtains hung at the window. There were no net curtains. She went into the kitchen: smooth white tiles, a broad teak table, a sink with a pull-out tap, dishwasher, electric grill… Everything breathed a prosperous bourgeois atmosphere. She had recently read about wealthy Warsaw residents buying flats in neglected prewar tenements. Praga and Old Mokotów were fashionable now. On the table stood a porcelain teapot painted with forget-me-nots, two cups, and a plate of biscuits.

She went back to the hall. In the open doorway she saw Sawicki leaning against the metal railing on the staircase and questioning Monika Lewicka.

—Police, he said to the curious neighbors who were gathering. —Please go back to your flats and do not interfere with the investigation. If we need your help, we will come to you ourselves, he added in a tone so sharp and decisive that the stairs emptied at once.

She moved toward the next room.

—Marcin! she suddenly called. —Come here. I think you’ll want to see this.

The entire furnishing of the small room consisted of a desk with a laptop on it connected to a huge monitor, a chair, and an old chest of drawers. All the drawers stood open. Papers, binders, and cardboard boxes lay gutted on the floor. Sawicki opened his eyes wide.

—This couldn’t have been an ordinary burglary, he said in surprise. —No sign of forced entry. The computer and tablet are still on the desk. Together they must be worth a good few thousand.

—The other rooms are in perfect order, Ada agreed.

—It looks as if someone was searching for something specific and knew where it might be… Must have had a key of his own and surprised our deceased in the bathroom.

—I don’t think so. I think she let him in herself. In any case, she was expecting someone. There are two empty cups and biscuits in the kitchen.



A moment later the crime-scene technicians arrived. Two grave men in protective overalls, silicone gloves, and blue plastic shoe covers stretched police tape across the doorway and entered the flat. They set numbered markers beside the body, the abandoned hammer, and the streaks of blood on the bathtub, then set about securing evidence. Every few moments the camera flash went off. Ada stood motionless in the bedroom doorway.

—You need to leave now, said a round-faced technician. —We’ll be done in an hour.

Downstairs a door banged. Footsteps could be heard approaching. A small man in a white coat showing beneath a short padded jacket was climbing the stairs. He gave a strange, unsettling impression. His head and hands were disproportionately large in relation to the rest of his body; he had stopped just short of deformity, reaching barely five feet in height. Ada caught a strong smell of formalin and male sweat. In spite of herself she stepped back in disgust. The little man peered into the flat and, without speaking to anyone, strode straight to the bathroom.

—That’s the creep from the morgue, Sawicki whispered. —Best not bother him, he’s not exactly pleasant…

Five minutes later the little man reappeared on the staircase.



—No two ways about it, somebody clobbered her over the head, he said, darting quick, nervous glances from under bristling brows. —No way she did that to herself, he added with a thin, unpleasant laugh. —This wasn’t an accident. He bent the fingers of his left hand so hard the joints cracked. —She’s been dead at least four hours. That’s all I can tell you for now. I haven’t got time to mess about with you lot. There are three hides queued up for a dance at Oczki. Call the undertakers and get in touch with me on Monday.

He left without saying goodbye to anyone. An awkward silence fell. Sawicki searched for his mobile. At last he found it in the inside pocket of his jacket and called the funeral service.

Monika Lewicka was still sitting on the stairs. She had stopped sobbing, but she was very pale and shaking as if with fever. Sawicki touched her shoulder.

—I’m sorry, but we need to ask you a few more questions, he said, and Ada heard the gentler note in his voice. —The first hours after a murder is discovered are the most important…

—I understand, she nodded, and began to cry again.
