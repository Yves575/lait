May 1980

At eight, I like the feel of my upper body hanging in midair, my bent knees against the metal. I like the instant when I squeeze my eyes shut, let go, and let the vertigo pass through me. When my hands are flat on the black asphalt, it means I’ve gone beyond fear. And then the image of my favorite gymnast, Nadia Comăneci, appears. Her arms are spread wide. Victory.

I spend recess hanging upside down by my knees, waiting for Ana, my sister. This morning, as she left, she said to me, See you here, and on time, okay? Otherwise I’m going home alone. “Here” means at the foot of the stairs, near the metal bar that separates the parking lot from the schoolyard.

Ilaria! Get down from there! We’re going to Chez Léon. Come on, hurry!

I recognize Papa’s voice. Startled, I lift the hem of my dress that’s blocking my view. Yes, it’s the tips of his shoes, yes, that impatient tone. I do a pirouette, land on my feet, smooth my dress.

Ana’s going to be here soon.

No, no. Plans have changed. Maman is picking her up from school and we’ll all meet at Chez Léon. Come on!

I grab his hand; it’s clammy.

Since our parents split up and Papa settled in Turin, we see each other once a month, at a restaurant. It was Maman’s idea. She prefers neutral ground. She says that at home they fight too loudly. And it’s true that at Chez Léon they hold themselves back. Even if Papa clenches his jaw and Maman stares into the distance with an indifferent air.

No, Papa still hasn’t found a job. When he says “no-no-work,” his voice is sad, tired. Maman turns her head slightly to hide her smile and Papa gets angry. He keeps repeating the word “humiliation.” Luckily the waiter arrives and sets down plates of perch fillets or bowls of meringues with whipped cream. Thank you.

After dessert, Ana and I leave the table and go down to the little beach to pick pebbles. We practice skipping them.

Did you see?

What?

Papa took Maman’s hand.

To get to Chez Léon, we go through the village of Hermance, cross the Franco-Swiss border, and continue along the road to Yvoire. Papa has a navy-blue BMW, a 320 coupé.

If you see a phone booth, tell me. He lights a cigarette. There! He pulls over, gets out of the car, and takes coins from his trouser pocket. With his back pressed to the glass, the folds of his shirt make V’s and W’s. I wait, roll down the window, let a little air in. The leather seat no longer burns the backs of my thighs; it’s even soft when I stroke it with the palm of my hand.

In the booth, Papa speaks loudly, raises his voice, turns around. Our eyes meet. He smiles, lowers his eyes. From the way he moves his hands I can tell he’s agitated. His body is stiff. It worries me. When he comes back, he says Maman has changed her mind and doesn’t have time for lunch after all. We’ll spend the weekend together. And school? You’ll only miss a few days of school... It’s not that serious.

Papa’s voice is sharp. I count on my fingertips: Thursday, Friday, Saturday, Sunday. Four days. And Ana? I’d like to protest, but when Papa is nervous, it’s better to keep quiet.

He starts the car abruptly and crushes his cigarette with a sharp movement. His forehead is covered in sweat.

Mont Blanc Tunnel, the Franco-Italian border, tunnel vaults, the bends of the Val d’Aosta, car sickness. We stop under a sky covered with a gray sheet. The landscape is metallic. I throw up by the side of the road, and Papa hands me a white cotton handkerchief. Let’s go have something to drink, it’ll do you good. A few kilometers farther on, in the service-station bar, Papa’s face is pale. It must be the neon light. He pays the cashier for two slices of Margherita pizza, a whisky, a coffee, and a lemon soda. I hate lemon soda, but I don’t say anything; my mouth is dry.

Do you have any phone tokens you can sell me?

How many?

About twenty.

The cashier carefully counts out the yellowish tokens and hands them to Papa.

The booth is outside, on the left.

Her nails are very long and painted a very bright red. I follow Papa.

What are those tokens for?

I need them to make calls. In Italy you can’t put coins in phone booths.

Between Geneva and Turin, Papa makes several calls. Five in all. Every time he spots a service station, he pulls in. Are you happy to be spending the weekend with me? Lost your tongue? What are you thinking about? Nothing.

Turin. We circle for a long time looking for a parking space, and when Papa shoves open the door of his building with his shoulder, he looks relieved. I dash up the stairs. What floor is it? The fourth. On the landing, he looks for his keys. You’re going to see your room. Are you happy? I say yes. Yes, I’m happy. Listen, Ilaria—he turns the key in the lock—I have to introduce you to someone I haven’t told you about yet, a certain Geneviève. Who is she? The lady of the house. Papa opens the door and points to a lifeless mannequin dressed in a black dress with a white lace collar and a hat. He bursts out laughing. Geneviève’s face is white polystyrene; it has no eyes. Come on! Get in. Your room is at the back, past the bathroom.

Impatient to see it, I cast a quick glance at the other rooms and switch on the light in my bedroom. The room is crowded with two beds and two dressers. I move closer to the Topolino poster. Mickey looks at me with eyes like tunnels. Papa stays in the doorway.

I haven’t found nice bedside lamps yet. We can buy some together tomorrow, can’t we? If you want. Running my hand over the fabric, I ask him if these are the sheets from Florence.

You remember?

I think so, yes. When was Florence?

Two years ago.

The orange sunflower patterns stand out against the sheets. They remind me of the blurred images I keep from that time. Two years, and yet I feel as though it’s been centuries since Maman, Ana, and I moved to Switzerland.

The next day we wake up early and go for a giro, a drive around Turin. Papa tells me about the FIAT kingdom, the workers’ strikes, the arrival of immigrants from the South. He says the people of Turin hate them.

Through the windshield, he points out the palazzi, the Po, the basilica of Superga, the statues. You see those kids over there between the cars? They’re posteggiatori. That’s how they make money, helping people park.

When we walk under the arcades on Corso Vittorio Emanuele, Papa holds my hand tightly, his still clammy, and I don’t dare pull mine away until, in front of the window of the Al Sogno toy shop, I let go.

The display looks like a zoo of harmless animals. Even the snakes look friendly. Papa follows my gaze. Is it the teddy bear you’re looking at? Come on, let’s buy it. The shop is full of wooden toys, mobiles, dolls in shimmering dresses. I’m enchanted. And for Ana? What are we getting her? Yes, a doll... Ana loves them. The one I choose has very long lashes, and when she’s laid down, her eyelids close. While the shopgirl wraps it in pretty gift paper, Papa hands me the teddy bear. What shall we call him? How about Birillo? Yes. I look at Birillo and am immediately won over by his milk-chocolate eyes, shining, his cream-colored belly, his soft fur. Papa pinches my cheek between his forefinger and middle finger, his eyes all soft.

That gesture lands on my cheek like his signature. He will repeat it for the next two years, and I’ll end up hating it. Are you happy?

At eight, I am a taciturn, docile child, rather scrawny. I don’t complain when Papa leaves me alone in the apartment to go make his phone calls at Carmelo’s, the bar downstairs. He doesn’t have a telephone. I’ll be right back. He always promises he’ll only be “two minutes.” Alone, lying on my bed, I draw, stick Sarah Kay stickers into my new album. Papa bought it for me at the kiosk. And when I get tired of waiting for him, I know where to find him: at the counter. My landmark is the big coffee machine that gives off a smell of burnt caramel.

At Carmelo’s, the local workmen come in for a sandwich at noon or a drink before going home. They all have their first names and their habits. Una birra alla spina per Emilio e due tramezzini per Marco. Gianna is the owner. She punches the big keys of the till very fast. Sei mila lire. Perched on her little platform, she smiles, hands over scontrini, receipts, packs of cigarettes, and when there’s no one around, she sweeps the floor. Carmelo, meanwhile, polishes the counter, serves coffees, dries glasses with a dishcloth, levels the sugar in the sugar bowls.

When I go into the café, the place is lively. The workmen are all singing along together to a song on the radio. Vorrei essere libero, libero come un uomo. To reach the counter I slip between them and hoist myself up onto the tall stool. Listen, listen! It’s a song by Giorgio Gaber, La libertà. Carmelo laughs and I see metallic spots at the back of his mouth. How many cavities? I don’t have time to count them. Looking for Fulvio? He points to the phone booth with his hand covered in soap bubbles.

And how was your day? Very good. We’re going back to Geneva tomorrow. You’ll come back soon, right? Carmelo’s smile is gentle. I’m going to be very sad not to see you anymore. Promise? I promise.

Later Papa appears. His legs are completely stiff. With a strange expression on his face, he bends down to my ear. We’re leaving tonight. His breath is like a draft. Hurry up. We have to go upstairs and pack our bags.

Say goodbye to Carmelo and Gianna properly, and thank them.

In the back seat of the car, I can hardly breathe. Where are we going? To the seaside. And Ana? And Maman? When do we see them again? Stop sniffling. We’ll go back to Geneva in a month. A month isn’t long. And the school parade? You told me we were only spending the weekend together... You’ll go next year. Stop with the questions now.

The image of the pretty dress Maman and I chose for the procession comes back to me. I think of Sarah, my best friend, of my teacher, of the little flag we made in class, of Ana. My head is heavy. I fold my knees under my chin and press Birillo tight against me. My sleep is cold, restless.

I want to hear your voice. STOP. The little one is fine. STOP. She would like to speak to you. STOP. I’m waiting for you. STOP. Wait for me.

Your husband

Here! Put that in the glove compartment.

Now we stop not only at phone booths but at post offices too. Papa sends a pile of telegrams and keeps a copy of them. I don’t dare read them when he hands them to me, but curious, I slow my movements and glance at them so I can catch a few words.

Zzaaak, zzzaaak. A family, a man by himself, a woman. Two white mops of hair are rolling along at thirty kilometers an hour in the right-hand lane. Papa grows impatient, lets go of the wheel, lights a cigarette. Come on!!! Let me pass. He slaloms between the vehicles. Are you scared? A little. He notices my hand clamped around the door handle. Traffic is heavy at the entrance to Genova, the tunnels numerous. Shadow-light, shadow-light. The lanes are full of cars and long trucks. I can’t take this road anymore. Get out the map, Ilaria.

Genova, Milano, Brescia, Alessandria. Yes, Brescia, then we’ll go to Alessandria. I trace our route with my fingertip. But there’s no sea in Alessandria! Doesn’t matter. I’m showing you your country. Papa’s tone is playful. He looks as if he’s about to wink at me.

Papa shifts down and takes the exit for the service station.

I need to make a call, go into the bar.

No, I’m staying here.

While I wait for him, I hop on one foot around the booth. Papa slips the tokens into the slot of the telephone, lights a cigarette. Pronto? The conversation goes on.

Sitting in the car, I leave the door open and dangle my legs in the air. The sky is streaked with white, the clouds like balls of wool. The air is heavy; I’m thirsty and hot. Other cars arrive, leave, back up in the parking lot. Children run. Careful, look before you cross! their mothers shout. The families going by seem perfect to me, washed, combed, ironed, headed somewhere precise. Where are we going?

Roads, phone booths, post offices, little hotels, bars. The days pile up. We often stop at Autogrills to get gas, eat a sandwich, go to the toilet. I like those places. Inside, everything is colorful: stacks of candy, baskets of food wrapped in crackly paper, bins of music cassettes, games, stuffed toys. And while I wander through the aisles, Papa talks to customers at the bar counter.

He loves to talk.

He can’t help himself; he has to talk to someone. Anyone.

And he’s very good at striking up a conversation. It always starts with a remark about the traffic or the weather or a joke. Papa knows how to put even the shyest people at ease. He looks at home there, in the way he leans on the counter, the way he talks to the barman. When he plays the clown, he’s very quickly surrounded by smiling faces. I prefer seeing him like that, happy, laughter in his eyes, rather than having to listen to his theories about people.

Yes, yes, we’re going. Always one last one. He drinks very fast, and when he reaches for his glass, I’m sure that even if he were blind, he’d have the same certainty. His lips would tremble just the same. He gives me the impression he’s afraid he won’t have time to finish his whisky.

He once told me it was medicine for him, and pinched my cheek.

Say thank you. I thank the barman, say goodbye to everyone.

On the road again. That’s how we leave the counter, just like that, abruptly.
