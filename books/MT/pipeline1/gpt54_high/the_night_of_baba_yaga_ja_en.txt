I





The white sedan speeding down the Koshu Highway as dusk began to fall was thick with the smell of tobacco and blood.

In the back seat, wedged between a man in a loud tie and another in a patterned shirt, a long-haired woman sat slumped with her head hanging. She wore only a cheap T-shirt and jeans ripped in several places. The hand dangling limply at her side was grimy. When the car jolted, a thick, sticky drop of blood plopped onto the stomach of the yellow T-shirt. The men kept stealing uncomfortable glances at it. The young man driving, too, threw the woman uneasy looks in the rearview mirror every time they stopped at a light.

The sedan was following the Ford directly ahead. Its polished black body caught the streetlamps and neon signs just beginning to come on and shone with a wet, oily gleam. It was a vicious sunset. All over Shinjuku, the glass windows of the buildings blazed a deep red, as though the towers were bleeding from every inch of themselves.

Soon the two cars entered a quiet residential district in Setagaya. It was neat and placid, hard to believe it belonged to the same Tokyo as cluttered Shinjuku. But after two or three turns, a high stone wall suddenly appeared, looming over the neighborhood as if to cow it. The pale Shirakawa stones were stacked tight without a gap, higher than a man, with barbed wire stretched along the top. When the cars stopped before that broad enclosure, built to hide whatever lay inside, an iron gate fitted with menacing security cameras opened after a moment.

Inside the wall sat a huge single-story mansion and a splendid Japanese garden. There was even a small five-storied pagoda in one corner. The cars moved slowly along the stone-paved drive and stopped near it.

At some point, men had appeared from somewhere and gathered around the cars as if to encircle them. All were young, hard-faced, and dressed in white shirts with matching patterned ties.



A man sprang from the driver’s seat of the Ford and opened the rear door. A pair of brilliantly polished shoes slid out, followed by a long, narrow pair of legs. Then a man in a black suit, as if just back from a funeral, stepped down. He was tall, with cheeks pared hollow and flattened cauliflower ears.

At a jerk of his chin, the doors of the white sedan opened at once, and the men inside began dragging the limp woman out. She was long-limbed, not fat but thick through the body, solidly built, and she did not look easy to carry even for two men.

They dumped her onto the paving stones as carelessly as luggage. Her back jerked once. But face-down, she did not get up.

“Hey. She alive?”

At the black-suited man’s words, the man in the patterned shirt hurriedly kicked her hard around the shin.

A hoarse moan escaped her. Still sprawled on the ground, the woman slowly raised her face like a crocodile. Hair stiff with blood clung filthily to her cheek. Dark blood ran from her nostrils. The skin beside her right eye was split and swollen a blackish purple.

“Ugly bitch.”

The man in the black suit gave a nasal snort of laughter, and the men around him snickered along with him.

“Get her up.”

The patterned shirt nodded, grabbed the woman’s left arm, and hauled. She submitted, leaning into the pull, and rose unsteadily to her feet. Then—

“Bwegh—”

A sound like a toad being crushed burst out. The patterned shirt’s body lifted clean off the ground, spun in a full circle, and slammed down on the paving stones flat on its back.

The men stirred. The patterned shirt let out a thin hiss of breath, rolled up the whites of his eyes, and stopped moving.

This time the woman did not sway. She planted herself there like a temple guardian, opened her palms, spread her arms, and slowly raked the men with a sticky, contemptuous gaze. Then she gaped her mouth open. Blood-smeared teeth showed.



One of the men in white, realizing the grin was meant to provoke them, twisted his face and lunged straight at her.

“Tss.”

A sound like air tearing slipped from between the woman’s teeth. She bent her knees, lowered her head, and charged. The man took a boar’s headbutt full in the stomach and flew backward, crashing shoulder-first onto the hard ground before he could break his fall. A dull pop sounded—the noise of a bone leaving its socket. A mangled scream burst from him.

For a moment there was a hesitation, as if everyone had forgotten to breathe. Then the others went at her too.

“Tss.”

The man coming in from the right took a fist straight to the Adam’s apple. Unable to cry out, unable even to breathe, he dropped onto his backside and kicked his legs wildly. The man rushing from the left got a steel-toed shoe driven into his kneecap. The sickening crack of the joint breaking rang clear even through the shouts and curses. Another man’s fist smashed into the woman’s cheek with everything he had. She staggered back several steps, then at once reset her stance and brought both arms up to guard her head like a boxer.

“Tss.”

She caught the next punch on her forearm and at the same instant drove her steel-capped toe into the man’s groin. A scream. Roars of anger. A mass of white-shirted men was gathering in front of the garage.

Every one of them had cheeks reddened by anger and tension, yet somewhere in their faces was the look of men caught in a dream. As if they could not absorb, could not believe, what was right in front of them. Amid the anger and confusion, only the blood-soaked woman was baring her teeth in a grin—laughing as she went on punching and kicking without pause.



Mixed in with the deep-throated curses came the sound of barking. Through a gap in the ring of white shirts, a black-and-rust blur shot out. A huge Doberman in a thick black leather collar sprang straight at the woman. Hit head-on by forty kilos of charging dog, she went down. The men swarmed her at once, trying to pin her body. Men and dog growled. Cloth tore.

“Hey, don’t kill her. She’s a present for the old man. Handle her carefully.”

The man in the black suit watched, amused. The sun set. Night fell.





In the middle of the elegant gravel garden, the woman was pinned to the ground by four forked mancatchers. The men wielding them had not come away unhurt either; frowning with naked resentment, they glared down at the woman sprawled on the ground. Her clothes and hair were in even worse tatters now, but she did not struggle or groan. Face-down, she simply breathed quietly. Her shoes had been removed, and the black underwear exposed beneath her ripped T-shirt gave her the look of a wounded tiger. In the stillness of early evening, the sound of koi splashing in the pond rang out.

“Yanagi, what is that? A woman?”

A liquor-roughened voice carried across the garden. On a leather sofa set on the veranda facing the garden sat a man in a striped yukata. Around sixty-five. A bald pate, a belly jutting out, a short bull neck sunk between sloping shoulders.

“Looks female, at least. I thought it might be good for that job, so I brought it back.”

The man called Yanagi took an old orange leather wallet from his pocket and slipped out a driver’s license.

“Shindo Yoriko… twenty-two. Hokkaido-born.”

“What is she?”

“There was a racket outside the office, so I sent some of the youngsters to take a look. This was raising hell. Rare creature, so I scouted it.”

At that, the man on the veranda laughed in a rasping, unpleasant voice. Bright traditional tattooing showed at his loosened collar.

“Scouted it, huh.”

“It took two or three good cracks with a beer bottle before it agreed to accompany us. We’ll look into its background properly later, but its strength in a fight is the real thing. It can take on ten professionals at once and stay on its feet. If it weren’t female, I’d want it as one of my boys.”

“It really is a woman, right? Lately there are plenty dressed like women who still have cocks.”

“We’ll take our time checking that too, later.”

A mean, vulgar laugh went up from the men.

The woman—Shindo Yoriko—listened to all this in silence, keeping her gaze fixed on the man on the veranda. Not glaring. Not pleading. Simply looking.

“While you’re checking, wash her out and clean her up. Don’t put her in front of that one looking like this.”

With that, the man on the veranda rose as if it were a nuisance and went back inside. White-shirted young men appeared at once like stagehands to clear away the ashtray and chairs.

“Hey. Get a hose. Turn on the water.”

Grabbing the hose hauled over from somewhere in the garden, Yanagi began dousing Shindo with cold water.

“What’s this? Quiet now? Finally given up?”

The hard stream struck her blood-soaked face full on. Shindo opened her mouth and gulped the water down greedily.

Yanagi laughed.

“You’ve got some balls on you. Or are you too stupid to even know what’s happening?”

The blood had washed off her face, but the fresh bruises she’d taken in the brawl had swollen it all the more, making it hard even to imagine her original features.

“If you’re an idiot, that works even better for us. Listen, Yoriko-chan. Starting today, you’re going to work in this house. If you don’t like it, we’ll kill you right here. Understand?”

Shindo said nothing. She gave a single nod.

“Good. What happened up to now was just play. From this second on, if you defy me, you die. Got it?”

Yanagi pulled his suit jacket slightly open. A dagger in a white wooden sheath was tucked into his waistband. Shindo nodded again.

“You lot, back off.”

At Yanagi’s signal, the men holding the mancatchers slowly lifted the metal restraints away from Shindo’s body.

“Stand.”

Shindo rose smoothly to her feet, not so much as wavering. The skin visible through her torn clothes was covered in fresh cuts and bruises, but she showed no sign of holding back pain.

“You heard what we were saying. Strip. Show me if you’re really female.”

Shindo silently pulled off the T-shirt, already little more than a rag, and tossed it onto the ground.

“Underwear too.”

She stripped off the black bra without a flicker of hesitation and threw that aside as well. A few of the men around her grinned, but more imposing than the naked breasts were the magnificent ridges of abdominal muscle beneath them. The arms that had been hidden by the T-shirt were packed solid with muscle too, thick and swelling. It was a body like one of the guardian kings at Todai-ji’s Great South Gate, as if carved from a massive tree trunk.

“Panties too. That’s the important part.”

Without taking her eyes off Yanagi, Shindo started undoing the button fly of her jeans. The curve of her large backside wrapped in black underwear, the taut thighs beneath it, came into view. The men’s eyes gathered there.

The next instant, Shindo swung the wet jeans and cracked them across Yanagi’s face like a whip.

“Shit!”

The hard, wet denim smacked into his head. In the split second before Yanagi could bat it away, Shindo sprang straight forward. As if clinging to him, she drove into his chest, grabbed the lapel of his suit with her left hand, and yanked the dagger from his waist with her right. At the same time Yanagi moved. He seized her by the hair, dragged her in, and with a smooth sweeping reap threw her to the ground. He tried to kick away the hand holding the dagger, but Shindo rolled clear and came up again at once.

“Get her!”

Someone shouted. The Doberman from before, obeying its master’s command, came at her half-naked body with white fangs bared. Instantly Shindo snatched up the jeans from the ground, whipped them around her arm, and thrust it out in front of the dog.

With a growl, the dog bit down through the wet denim onto her arm. It kept clamping, with the sort of power that can tear flesh and break bone in an ordinary person, and with a fierce determination never to let go. The more one tried to shake it off, the more stubborn it became.

The beast and the beastlike woman locked eyes.

Shindo bared her teeth and let out a raw bark. A declaration of which creature was stronger. The dog did not release her, but for an instant fear flickered in its large wet black eyes. Then, with forty kilos of deadweight attached to her and her movements slowed, mancatchers came flying at Shindo from all directions again and forced her back to the ground. At the order to stop, the dog released her at once and returned to its master’s side.



Running his fingers through his disordered hair, Yanagi spat on the ground. Then he planted a leather shoe on the wrist of Shindo’s dagger hand.

“What are you, insane? Everyone here is the real thing. Real yakuza. Aren’t you afraid of dying?”

Yanagi’s cruel-looking face held a smile that said he was enjoying this far too much.

“Don’t act familiar, you piece of trash.”

“What, so you can talk.”

Yanagi pressed harder on her wrist. The dagger slipped from her palm with a clatter.

“You… why didn’t you stab the dog?”

Shindo twisted her face and spat a mouthful of blood-tinged saliva.

“Nishi, bring that dog here.”

The white-shirted man called Nishi nodded and led over the Doberman on a leash clipped to its collar. Yanagi picked up the dagger.

“I’ll say it one more time. Starting today, you work here. If you say no, we kill you right here.”

He pointed the blade at Shindo’s face.

“Do whatever you want. Better dead than doing what you say.”

“I see.”

With that, Yanagi grabbed the collar of the dog, which had been sitting there obediently wagging its tail, and pressed the blade to its throat. Shindo’s eyes flew wide.

“—Stop.”

A thin whine escaped it. A powerful animal that could kill a human with a single bite, if it chose, now trembled with its own life under threat, unable even to resist.

“Didn’t catch that.”

The Doberman’s tail tucked under, and its cry rose higher, sharper with fear.

“Stop!”

Its big ears flattened to its head. Its black eyes opened wide and round.

“So you’re fine with me killing it, then? It’ll die because of you. Poor thing.”

He pressed the blade in. The dog’s wet eyes stared fixedly at Shindo.

“I’ll do what you say.”

“What was that?”

“I’ll do what you say!”

At once Yanagi let go of the collar. The dog gave a small whine, then sank sadly to the ground and looked up at Yanagi from under its brow. There was no resentment in those eyes. No anger.

“You can crush a man’s balls, but the poor dog is too much for you? That’s kind of you, Yoriko-chan. Listen carefully. If you defy me again or try to run from here, I’ll slit its belly open and skin it alive. Right in front of you.”

With that, Yanagi slid the unstained dagger back into the sheath at his waist.





On the connecting corridor polished bright enough to reflect a face, Shindo sat alone in formal seiza. Her reddish-brown hair had been pulled back with an office rubber band, and she wore an oversized white shirt and black trousers that didn’t fit. Gauze and adhesive bandages had been plastered over her face so thickly they covered almost everything but her eyes, nose, and mouth, making her look like a botched mummy. It was less treatment than concealment.

While she changed into clothes borrowed from one of the white-shirted junior men who lived in the house, Yanagi had given her a one-sided explanation. This was the residence of Naiki Genzo, chairman of the Naiki Association, a direct affiliate of the Okitsu-gumi, the largest yakuza syndicate in Kanto, and Yanagi was an assistant to the Naiki Association’s underboss. Everything in the estate, from cleaning to cooking, was handled by carefully selected young men from subordinate groups, but for a certain job they absolutely needed female hands, and so they had “scouted” Shindo.

He had said he would explain what kind of job it was later, and told her to sit and wait. It had been a little over thirty minutes. It was dark now, and there was no one around. The nearest stone wall was only about twenty meters away. If she wanted, she could run for it at once.

But Shindo could not move.

The Doberman’s thin cry—that sound of despair and resignation tangled together—still clung to her ears.

The dog had done nothing wrong. No matter how vile the human who owned it might be, the dog had done nothing wrong. Those eyes… eyes full of despair, and yet offering no resistance to the one who held its life in his hands. That pitiful whine. Dogs were all like that. For some reason they pledged loyalty even to hopeless human beings.

She let out a breath, and something around her ribs hurt a little. It did not feel broken, but there might be a crack. She looked back over the day.

One of her two part-time jobs was bicycle meal delivery, and she had ridden into Shinjuku for that and worked until evening. Then she had gone back once to her apartment in Shin-Okubo, left the bicycle, washed off the sweat, changed clothes, and headed into Shinjuku again on foot. If all she wanted was something to eat, she could have done that near home, but she had thought she might catch a movie while she was at it.

But just after entering Kabukicho, a small-time punk obviously drunk on his ass smacked her on the rear as he went by, cackling. She turned on her heel at once, grabbed the back of his collar from behind, swept his legs, and drove him face-first into the asphalt. It was almost funny that his buddy kept walking several meters without noticing, but before long he came hurrying back in a panic and threw a huge looping punch. She could have dodged it if she’d wanted, but a crowd had already started to gather, and she figured it would be less trouble if she let herself get hit once first, so she took it on purpose, full in the face. Whether because he was drunk or because he simply had no skill, the punch hardly amounted to an itch. She caught his wrist from below and twisted it sharply inward. A nasty grunch traveled up her hand; not a pleasant feeling. Nicely, the wrist joint came out in one go. A scream. Panicking, the man started punching wildly at her stomach with his good hand, so she let go, kicked him away, and opened the distance.

The gawkers multiplied in an instant. The man she’d dropped on the asphalt was rolling around with a gush of blood from his nose, groaning. She would have liked to hit them a few more times, but they were no challenge, and if someone called the police it would be a pain, so she decided to leave. But then several obviously rough-looking men came out of the crowd and blocked her path. More, she thought—and at that same moment a kick came flying. It was a far better high kick than the drunk punk’s. “Fucking bitch! Pig!” he shouted as he kicked at her again and again. She evaded the second and third and caught the fourth. He was short. She stamped on his planted foot to pin it, slung the other leg over her shoulder, and dropped hard, forcing his hips into a hundred-and-eighty-degree split. A scream like a newborn baby’s tore out of him.

When she stood up, a blow hit her in the back. Probably a flying kick. She turned, and the man who had kicked her stumbled the instant he landed, so she put a high kick straight into the open side of his neck. It made a fine sharp sound. The next thing she knew, pain bloomed in her side. Looking, she saw she’d been hit in a sideways swing with an empty wine bottle. That was probably the injury throbbing now. In a place this cramped, once someone brought in a weapon she was at a disadvantage. A spectator could get hurt too. She looked for a way out, but the man with the bottle drew back to swing again, so she drove her fist straight into his face.

Through gaps in the ring of onlookers, murderous intent was drawing nearer. More were coming. More, coming to get beaten by me. A grin spread across Shindo’s face all by itself. If only there were more room, if only no one got in the way, I could take every last one of you. She cursed the city’s tightness, its crowds. Then a hard blow cracked into the back of her head. If Yanagi was telling the truth, it had been a beer bottle. Another blow followed immediately, and then everything went black.

A night wind, chilly for May, passed through. Sitting motionless on the cold, hard corridor, she drew one more deep breath and rubbed at her throbbing ribs and the back of her head.
