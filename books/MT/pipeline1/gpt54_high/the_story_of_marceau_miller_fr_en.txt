Prologue

A flash bores through my brain; a violent blow to the skull knocks me off balance. I am two hundred metres above the void, climbing a rock face I know well. My nails scrape at the granite, my hands lose their hold. In that instant I understand that it’s all over. For the thrill of it, perhaps out of pride, I have always climbed with my bare hands, without roping up. I knew that one day I would pay the price.

First I see the sky. Then a hundred metres of rock face race past in the space of a few blinks. I can barely make out the silhouette disappearing beyond the summit I was about to reach. That silhouette is familiar. It has just sent me plunging into the void, and I am going to die. One thought soothes me strangely: I am going to join my sister. She has been gone twenty years; when I got up this morning, I was far from imagining we would be meeting today.

My name is Marceau Miller. I am a bestselling novelist. Tomorrow, on my Wikipedia page, you will be able to read, just below the line that says “Born”: “Died: 16 May 2021 (aged 40).” By a cruel irony, I spent years writing as if every day might be the last. I am leaving behind the most important manuscript of my life. As though some part of me had sensed this moment, had anticipated this unforeseeable rendezvous with destiny. The Grim Reaper does not send you a registered letter to warn you of the day and hour of your death. I leave it to them—to Sarah, to the others. They can make of it what they can. I owed them the truth.

My body hangs in the air; I control nothing now. The ground is rushing up at a terrifying speed. My heart tightens for my children, Hermione and Benjamin, and for my wife, Sarah, with whom I built everything.

I catch sight of my pick-up parked at the foot of the cliff and understand that I am going to smash down right beside it. Nothing else crosses my mind. And yet they say that in such a moment your whole life flashes before your eyes. We’ve all had that awful nightmare: falling into empty space, with nothing left to hold you. You wake drenched in sweat, your heart on the verge of bursting, with a feeling of unease that stays with you for hours.

I have always wondered whether our brains shut down before the fatal impact. In a few hundredths of a second, I’ll know.

Fifty metres.

Ten metres.

Darkness.

PART ONE

THE FALL

Chapter 1

Saturday, 15 May 2021

The day before Marceau Miller’s death

Sitting in the rocking chair on the chalet terrace, I lift my head from the novel that had me completely absorbed. My tea is still steaming. This is where I love to be, looking out over Lake Geneva, my lake, as though it belonged to me. In every story my husband writes, nothing warns you of the storm about to break over his characters. It usually creeps up on them, then hits full force. I take a sip of my Saint James, a strong black tea with chocolate notes, and set the cup down. This is his twentieth book, and he can still surprise me. I don’t fully grasp everything about Marceau, but one thing has to be said for him: he deserves his success. No one does suspense like he does. Too much so, even. Everywhere, all the time.

The drone of an engine pulls me fully out of my reading. A boat is approaching the dock at the far end of the garden. I serviced it last week; it’s part of the fleet Karen, my business partner, and I own. That little speedboat is nothing now but a patched-up tub we would never dream of offering our clients. But the children adore it. Sometimes I take it to the agency, following the shoreline to Yvoire. It takes a quarter of an hour longer than driving, but it’s every bit as handy as Marceau’s old pick-up for hauling equipment.

Benjamin is at the helm. At ten, he already handles the speedboat well, but he’s coming in a little too fast for my liking. Marceau is waving his arms. He must be telling him to slow down. I let out a breath without meaning to when the boat slides in alongside the dock without a bump. They’ve seen me. All three wave broadly. I smile and wave back. Hermione, our eldest, stationed at the bow, springs onto the dock and catches the mooring line her father throws her. I keep an eye on the manoeuvre. The dock on pilings isn’t very wide; its long wooden legs sink into the depths and root themselves in concrete footings embedded in mud and green-stained pebbles. At this time of year, with the sun still so warm, the planks aren’t slippery; all is well. Benjamin cuts the engine and joins his father and sister.

I set my book down without even slipping in a bookmark. I memorised the page number. Forty—Marceau’s age and mine. And by page forty things are already going badly for the characters. It’s only the beginning, I know that.

I get up and stretch. The shade under the pergola is cool on this terrace built by my grandfather, a cabinetmaker—his last piece of work before he died suddenly about ten years ago. The chalet’s exterior, partly overgrown with vegetation, gives the place the feel of a holiday home. The garden stretches for nearly eighty metres, bordered by woodland left wild and a hedge equally untended. In summer, thanks to the nearby lake, the lawn stays green for a long time. It was mown only recently and edged along the shore with a fringe of grey sand and pebbles. I head toward the children. Benjamin is already trying to tell me about his adventures as he runs to me, but he’s still too far away; I can’t hear a thing. Hermione is coming up with her father, both of them loaded down with diving gear.

“Dad and I went down to five metres! I saw a féra this big!”

My boy’s outstretched arms seem too short for the enormous fish he claims to have seen in the lake. Marceau reaches me and kisses my forehead. His lips still carry the coolness of the lake. Benjamin tugs at his sleeve so he’ll back up his story about the miracle with the shining scales. Hermione catches him off guard by planting on his head the cap he dropped behind him. At that moment I feel how fragile happiness is—fleeting as a fish vanishing into the depths. It makes me dizzy. I suddenly become aware of everything I hold most dear, and of the pain I would feel if I lost any one of them. Happiness reaches out its hand without warning. It can snatch it back just as quickly.

“Sarah?”

Marceau smiles at me, then goes off a little farther away to shrug off the diving gear. I catch his eye; I have the impression I can read in it exactly what I’m feeling. But he knows the cost and the impermanence of moments like these better than anyone. He has glued back together so many pieces after happiness shattered. He makes me think of that Japanese art of repairing broken ceramics: kintsugi.

I can almost trace with my finger the gold scars his memories have left inside him.

Chapter 2

Saturday, 15 May 2021

The day before Marceau Miller’s death

It isn’t yet eight in the living room. The clock’s familiar clicking fades beneath a sound coming from outside. Daylight is dying; I see the headlights of several vehicles filtering through the maples in the drive. The deep purr, unmistakable as Alexis’s sports car, makes the panes of the old bookcase I inherited from my grandmother Louise vibrate. Through the window I soon see him climbing out of his machine with a broad grin. Alexis, true to himself as ever. Benjamin is already circling the gleaming car. Alexis grabs him and dangles him upside down by the feet like a rag doll. I hear their bursts of laughter; I smile too. Then they tear off in a wild race, disappearing toward the back of the garden, shouting.

Marceau comes down the staircase, repainted white, the dominant colour inside our house. I like light. The wide windows looking out over the lake, and the reflection of the mountains, mean we never lose sight of it. I designed the chalet’s ground floor like a loft—a modern open kitchen blended with older features, opening onto the sitting room and library corner. A spacious living room, perhaps a little overfurnished, I’ll admit, but comfortable and warm. Marceau joins me by the sofa. He takes me in his arms; I know he loves my smile. He gives it back to me without taking his eyes off mine.

“Are you ready?”

“Never really for evenings like this, you know that. I’d rather slip under the sheets with you.”

That idiot melts me. This phase is the one I love best: between the moment he finishes a novel and the moment he disappears into a new one. That’s when I get him back, when we’re closest.

“Then why are you so set on organising this ritual every year for the release of your novels?”

“Ritual, Sarah, is the key for a writer. A writer without ritual is a dead writer.”

“You’ve become superstitious? Seriously, what are you afraid of? That it’ll stop? That your readers won’t like you any more?”

“Second thing, pretty lady: a writer with no fear is a writer who’s done for. You always have to write with passion layered over fear.”

“That’s a great line. You should save it for your next interview... Ah, speaking of which, here’s your publisher. Early, as always. He adores this evening. And above all my fresh goat’s cheese empanadas. Soon we won’t be able to see his belt.”

Édouard Payet, genially plump, lets his gaze travel over the façade of the chalet as if he were hesitating to come in. I know he’s taking in the whole house. The woodwork, the openings, the roof-support beams, the little upstairs balcony, the terrace, the greenery maintained with calculated neglect. He has always loved this place, the lair of his star author. He knocks; we go to greet him. In the publisher’s wake, Freud, his Welsh Corgi, waddles into the little hall I cleared for the occasion of all the things Benjamin always leaves lying around. The little dog’s claws skid on the polished floorboards. Short-legged and rather plump, barking nonstop, he looks like his master. Both of them are eyeing the food within sight farther off on the kitchen island. Honestly. Édouard already has a handful of peanuts in his hand. I was just wondering where I’d put the bowl. He has clearly found it. In one movement he lifts them to his mouth and swallows them without chewing, like pills. An ogre—but one with a jovial smile and disarming patter.

“Sarah, Marceau! I look forward to this day every year with real happiness. Nothing changes here! And it mustn’t—that’s the secret of my star writer.”

His hearty hug is as manly as ever, and on my shoulder I can feel the grains of salt from the peanuts left by his fingers—Freud didn’t have time to lick them off. Édouard suddenly leans toward Marceau, his expression complicit.

“Did you get my little present?”

I look at Marceau, who seems embarrassed. I have no idea what he means.

“Listen, Édouard, it’s magnificent, but... I’m not really a watch man, and... I’d hate to damage it.”

“A Breitling Aviator is made for you! You pilot that old Savage Bobber of yours almost every week. Marceau, loosen up a little! You’re a drudge who never treats himself enough. With what you make, you can afford it!”

“The house by the lake and my plane are enough for me. You know me—I don’t want to be caught off guard if one day it all stops, just like that, all of a sudden, without warning.”

“I could publish you with my eyes closed, Marceau.”

“Never do that.”

“Don’t worry, I’m not senile yet. And if the text wasn’t good, Marceau, rest easy, I’ve got good ghostwriters.”

Marceau’s face changes almost imperceptibly. He’s just tipped into his own world.

“An accident in a text... with me, that would be a fatal crash, Édouard.”

“Oh, I’m teasing. I’m always impatient to know what you’re going to bring me, you know that.”

I hold out the tray of empanadas to Édouard while Marceau slips away without a word. Freud lifts his snout and waits for crumbs, but Benjamin comes running and distracts him from his aim. My son strokes the dog’s head and leads him away, calling him cheerfully.

Édouard swallows his last mouthful with undisguised satisfaction, then heads for the terrace with almost ceremonial steps. The publisher’s ritual can begin, the one he always performs far from my living room, where I have little tolerance for those heavy coils of smoke. From the inside pocket of his tweed jacket he reverently extracts a Cohiba Behike 56, the Holy Grail of aficionados, nestled in its Spanish cedar case.

His gestures are precise, almost liturgical. First he takes out his Xikar pocket hygrometer to check the ideal moisture level of the wrapper leaf. Then, between his expert fingers, he rolls the cigar beneath his nostrils, inhaling the scents of leather and precious wood. The engine-turned silver cutter—a gift from Marceau—makes a clean cut, releasing the first earthy notes. Édouard studies the cut end as carefully as an oenophile examining the robe of a grand cru.

His ST Dupont lighter produces that characteristic blue flame. The ritual of toasting begins: the flame delicately licks the foot of the cigar, preparing the tobacco for ignition. Édouard then slowly turns the Cohiba above the flame in a choreography measured to the millimetre. The first wisps rise, carrying the promise of the complex aromas to come—notes of roasted coffee, bitter cocoa and eastern spices.

A smile of contentment appears on the publisher’s lips as he draws those first puffs, eyes half closed, savouring the moment as one savours an instant of eternity. The coming success of Marceau’s new novel is already mingling with the ribbons of smoke rising into the evening air, dancing above the calm waters of the lake.

I turn up the hi-fi; we far too rarely use the full power of those speakers. The sound fills the house and carries all the way to the lake. Some evenings I like to break the silence, to flood the chalet with joy and noise. I glimpse Karen dancing on the terrace with her daughter Zoé and mine. I hadn’t seen them arrive. Karen is wearing a burnt-orange jumpsuit with butterfly sleeves, loose and belted high at the waist. Light fabric, a plunging neckline flattering her slender figure. The whole thing set off with ankle-strap wedge heels that lift her to nearly six feet. At that moment I regret not having chosen my linen trousers, the ones that show off my curves. I rise onto my toes to kiss Karen and give her a wink, brushing a hand over her outfit; she smiles. She is swept away again by the girls as I ask where Rollin, her husband, is. Between dance steps she points toward the garden. Farther off, on the lawn, Alexis is getting Marceau and Rollin—his best mates of twenty years—worked up, ready to set sparks flying tonight. Alexis loosens them up, drink in hand, and they launch into one suggestive hip swing after another. Rollin, big and muscular, is as clumsy as ever, but he goes along with it. He’s contemplative, warm by nature, the gentle dreamer of the group, unbeatable when you quiz him about fauna and flora on our outings. Nonconformist to the tips of his nails, he has never given up his freedom and scrapes a living with his food truck. From the front steps, Édouard exhales heavy streamers of smoke after drawing on his glowing cigar. Face alight, eyes fixed on Marceau. The truth is, in moments like this, I’m always afraid something will happen and break everything—just like in my husband’s novels. There are false alarms, and there are real tragedies.

22:00. The automatic sprinklers come on. I’d forgotten; my heart jumps. A few drops hit me, cries of surprise and laughter burst out. Alexis holds Rollin under the artificial rain while Marceau runs in long strides toward the shed to stop the system. And while the three friends entertain the crowd, my son tugs at my sleeve and drags me under the fine droplets, refreshing in this hot evening with its false air of summer. I sober up a little, my heart hammering, but I breathe—and let go.

The magic ends as abruptly as it began. The sprinkler system is shut off; the boys are drenched. Alexis strips off his clothes and tries to lure Rollin toward the lake. Rollin resists the drunken troublemaker and lets him go. He’s had enough already, soaked from head to foot. Alexis, jeering, almost naked in the middle of the vast garden at dusk, in the wash of the chalet lights, makes one last appeal to the whole gathering. Rollin shakes his head while Alexis tears across the grass and vanishes in a dive into the lake, gone black with night. Several seconds pass, then he bursts back up from the depths in a loud splash, grinning. The slap of water around him makes the surface shimmer, where the points of light from the lamps dance on the immensity of the lake. Far away, the Swiss shore outlines itself like a lit serpent. The water is shockingly cold, I know that. Even after several drinks, you can’t escape its icy bite. The main thing is that it doesn’t take the lives of those I love; it already spat me back out once, just before I drowned. I owe it my becoming a strong swimmer, with even a few medals to my name. It’s always been the lake and me, like the forests and mountains around it.

Rollin’s clothes are clinging to his skin, showing his broad, stocky shoulders.

“I think I need a bath towel now.”

“Idiot, that won’t be enough,” I tell him, laughing. “You know the house—go to the bathroom and take something from Marceau’s wardrobe. It’ll just be a little tight on you.”

Karen and I burst out laughing while Rollin heads inside. We’ve known each other a long time, through our two men. Our friendship and the trust we place in each other made it possible for us to imagine joining forces to set up this boat-rental agency, which is doing rather well now. A few metres from shore, Alexis whistles and throws his soaked underpants toward the garden. Freud needs no urging; he catches the sodden cloth in mid-air and brings it back to his master. Alexis moves toward the bank and backs out of the water, turning his white backside toward us. Karen reacts first.

“Alexis, the children! Honestly!”

Alexis keeps grandstanding as he reaches the dock, which he uses as a launching ramp to dive into the lake again, with exaggerated clumsiness. Benjamin starts undressing in his turn, but I stop him dead. Disappointed, he goes down to the shore and cheers Alexis on, who from a distance tries to convince me. It’s hopeless, boys.

Rollin reappears a little later, dry, in Marceau’s ill-fitting clothes. There’s something odd about him—is he tired? He has lost his smile and is striding over with a large towel to rescue his naked friend, now out of the water and beginning to shiver. Alexis doesn’t have the anatomy to justify anything that big, but he’ll look more civilised draped in it for the end of the evening.

The two of them move a little way off together toward the back of the garden, under cover of the foliage, at the edge of the woods, not far from the boat still rocking at its mooring after Alexis’s antics. They seem deep in conference. I hope Alexis isn’t dragging Rollin into some new hare-brained idea. These evenings have this special quality: they seem to throw us back twenty years, into the carefree days we had then. Marceau comes out of the house in his turn, looking serious, as though he were already elsewhere—a worried expression I’ve seen on him from time to time. Why tonight? Will I ever have an answer to that mystery? On the rare occasions when I have tried to find out more about the feelings moving through him, I have found myself as if standing at the edge of a bottomless precipice. That is the limit of our marriage. An unspoken rule, a protective distance—for his protection.

It’s after midnight now; the swim changed the mood. The buffet is withstanding the last assaults. Freud’s barking draws everyone’s attention. His fur is standing on end and he is facing the little wood beside the garden. Édouard whistles to him, to no effect. In the end he goes over to fetch him back. The area around the chalet is full of nocturnal animals. The children sometimes come across them when they venture into the woods.

Édouard comes back. In his master’s arms, tongue hanging out, Freud already seems to have forgotten everything. Édouard thanks me for the evening and reminds Marceau of the schedule for the next seventy-two hours, notably the television appearance on the big literary programme and the studio recording for a radio spot. The publicist will send him his updated schedule for the bookshop signings. What I have learned over the years is that this business of being a writer is nothing more than a form of parole. I share my husband with Édouard, the journalists, the readers... but also with a great many fictional characters. If they could sometimes step aside and leave me a little more of Marceau, I’d like that better. It isn’t enough for me when he merely shuts his laptop for as long as it takes the machine to digest the manuscript he’s working on. But it isn’t up to me, and I’m not even sure he truly decides it himself.

At 1:45, everyone has gone home. Hermione and Benjamin are finally in their room, dead with sleep. I knock softly at the door of Marceau’s office, his sanctuary. I hear his muffled voice just behind it.

“Five more minutes. I’m finishing a message. I’m coming.”

In my head I translate... half an hour, or an hour. I slip into the bathroom and swallow a Dafalgan. Fatigue, alcohol—the headache won’t be long now. I’m exhausted but satisfied; the evening was a success. As I slide under the sheets, exhaustion and the slight floaty feeling from too much alcohol carry me away. I don’t like the idea of falling asleep without my man’s warmth beside me; I resist. I need him. I resist a little longer, but my consciousness switches off.

Chapter 3

Sunday, 16 May 2021

The day of Marceau Miller’s death

Blurred glow: 5:10 on the alarm clock display. Marceau isn’t beside me. Damned writer. I frown, but my eyes close again. I sink back under.

8:15, slight headache, and I can tell from the shutters that the weather is fine. The light assaults me. Beside me, the sheets aren’t even rumpled. Did he sleep in his office or what? On quiet feet I cross the corridor without waking Hermione and Benjamin. What mother doesn’t protect her children’s sleep? Marceau isn’t in his office. I don’t go in; I hardly ever set foot there: he doesn’t like it.

My memory struggles in the fog of waking. I search my recollections; nothing comes back to me. He should be here, with me. The living room is in disarray; I pick up here and there a few remains of last night’s party and scan the garden: no one. I go outside, barefoot on the terrace, facing the lake, calm, not a breath of wind disturbing it. Our little speedboat is tied up, solitary, at the dock. The air is mild, almost as warm as inside the chalet. Off in the distance, by the hangar, I can’t make out our old pick-up. I thought it was still parked there yesterday. I go around the chalet, in the silence of fresh grass absorbing my footsteps. I feel the cold blades tickling the soles of my feet. I draw near the hangar, wide open as usual. The Savage Bobber is there, its propeller gleaming, its broad wings spread. I’d have heard it take off from the meadow next door anyway. Still no pick-up in sight. I turn in a circle and look toward the woods and the track leading to the road in front of the house. In a voice still thick with sleep, I call Marceau, but only a bird nearby answers, beating its wings and rising above the chalet. For a moment I envy its position above the world, and its view over the lake and the mountains. Can it see Marceau from up there?

Back in the house, I open the cupboards by the entrance. I find the trainers, Marceau’s neatly beside mine, and above all I check his climbing bag: empty. His climbing shoes and chalk bag aren’t there any more. I flinch, then remember: he got rid of them the day he promised me he would stop his free solos, that thrill of climbing without rope, without protection... and, in my view, without any common sense. Jackets and coats are hanging on the hooks or in the wardrobe. If he took nothing with him, he’ll be back soon. I send a text, reminding him in passing that we need to coordinate our schedules for the next few days, between the obligations tied to promoting his novel and, on my side, the boat rentals that never let up at this time of year. Not to mention the fact that the children still don’t take care of themselves.

Noon. I peel potatoes for the chips Benjamin keeps demanding, growing more irritated by the minute. And for at least the tenth time I answer the children that no, I don’t know where their father is. In the land of promises, Marceau is always the first to desert. Hermione was counting on him to install her connected lighting system, Benjamin on him for the complicated assembly of his new radio-controlled plane. I advise them to negotiate compensation. On my phone, no new message. I call for the third time, and still there isn’t even a ring: “Hello, you’ve reached Marceau Miller...” I hang up. By the fifth text, my tone has changed.

For fuck’s sake, where are you?

Chapter 4

Monday, 17 May 2021

The day after Marceau Miller’s death

8:00.

I call Benjamin one last time. Otherwise it’ll be school without breakfast. Hermione is on edge. Asking her to clear the table is enough to make her storm off, cursing, and slam her bedroom door. A normal Monday morning—well, almost normal. Usually Marceau gets on Hermione’s nerves before I do.

Last night I fed them my first lie: Daddy’s away for the launch of his new novel, I’d forgotten, he mentioned it to me. I still haven’t started the round of calls to friends and acquaintances; I’m clinging, without really believing it, to the idea that Marceau will walk in with an explanation I hadn’t thought of, to the idea that everything will start again, normally.

9:15.

A bitterness in my throat—it’s stress; my stomach can’t take it. In the bathroom wall cabinet, I search desperately for medicine. I find nothing; half of it is out of date, it all needs sorting. It’ll be fine. As soon as I hear from him, everything will go back to normal. Shaky, I get my things together, trying not to forget anything for what is shaping up to be a bad day at work.

I’m late to the agency and in no state for the usual emergencies. Karen is already dealing with the first clients. Probably because of the dazed look on my face, she asks whether everything’s all right, and how the rest of the weekend went after Saturday night’s party. And in the end I crack. I admit that I haven’t heard from Marceau since late that evening, as a matter of fact. Does she have any idea where he might be? I see the bewilderment in her eyes. I must look ridiculous. She hesitates, searching for a reassuring explanation. Could it be connected to the release of his book? That doesn’t hold up; he would have left me a note, even if he wanted to shut himself away for a while, as he sometimes does. I pace around the agency as if, naïvely, I might find him here too. Karen makes me tea and checks the list of our available boats while I count them one by one where they’re tied up at the agency dock. On rare occasions, Marceau has borrowed one, just as Alexis or Rollin have. Karen calls Rollin while I try Alexis. My heart speeds up as every handhold I cling to gives way. They know nothing. I move on to his publisher. Édouard can think of nothing better than to remind me how urgent the TV appearance is and the radio interviews in the next few days. One dead end after another, even after I contact friends of friends. My knuckles whiten from gripping my phone. A warning light flares in my mind. I see the same barely veiled anxiety in Karen’s eyes. She assures me she can run the agency alone and all but shows me the door; I have better things to do, Marceau has to be found.

I have always been able to count on Karen. We understand each other perfectly—able to laugh at the same things, keep cheap judgements at bay, live with our flaws. Real partners, with limitless trust. So limitless it’s almost dizzying: she could take everything from me if she wanted to; I’d keep my eyes shut. Truly, going into business with her to set up the agency on the lake was one of the best decisions I ever made. Choosing to be independent was another, and one in keeping with my values. Finding my place here, on the lake, surrounded by forests and mountains—my stronghold, my roots.

But Marceau is my roots too. To leave without warning, without answering his phone—still more so at the very moment his new book is coming out—is not normal. It’s as if I were being asked to move forward in darkness, with nothing to cling to. My heart leaps at every text and every call I get, more and more of them, from our circle, people as worried as I am, asking for news, sometimes offering suggestions... and no doubt drawing their own conclusions in silence.

15:20.

I’m back from a drive along the lakeside, from Yvoire to Meillerie, all the way to the Swiss border. I stopped everywhere—restaurants, sports clubs, every place Marceau goes, sometimes very isolated ones when he wants to write far away from everything. I have to act. I need help. I park outside the gendarmerie in Thonon-les-Bains, the nearest and biggest.

I walk in with a knot in my stomach: what am I doing here? This is where I am now, with nothing but my anxiety for luggage. They are my last option. A young gendarme listens to my distress and notes down everything I tell him. He tries to reassure me and insists there’s nothing alarming yet. He starts asking questions about my marriage, any conflicts, any infidelities there may have been... My face hardens. I remind him that my husband has disappeared and that I’m here precisely because I have no explanation. He tells me to calm down. Did I raise my voice?

In the end, a Captain Robin Delmas comes to see me. An arrogant type, looking down his nose at me. In the office where he receives me, I have to go through it all again, step by step, explain it all again, once more—the party, the alcohol, yes, no other substances, of course not! I struggle to keep my voice down. On the verge of losing my temper, I stammer. Who does he think we are? Among celebrities, illegal substances are common, he says. At that point I get properly angry. No, Marceau doesn’t do coke. The first and last joint we ever smoked dates back to our schooldays. His condescending look is unbearable. Marceau left no note, nothing is missing from the house? No. No warning signs, no particular changes in his habits? I’ve already gone over all those questions myself. I let out yet another clipped “No,” short of breath. He left with the pick-up, that’s all.

Delmas concludes that since Marceau has been gone less than forty-eight hours, he cannot set anything in motion for a forty-year-old adult in full possession of his mental faculties, with no particular conflicts or worrying reason for concern. And that’s that. He looks me up and down, with an almost satisfied little smile for good measure. I feel the urge to hit him rising in me. Plainly, I’m the only one who understands that nothing about this situation is normal. I have the impression that whatever I say, he won’t hear a word of it. Faced with a wall, my anger comes slamming back into my own face. They show me out; I’m to keep him informed, case closed.

I come out feeling worse than when I went in, struggling to breathe, with a sense of emptiness, of abandonment. Misunderstood, despised, but no less determined for that. Delmas hasn’t seen the last of me, that much he can be sure of. My brain is racing. There have to be answers... And then a flash. An idea. I climb into my vehicle and tear off.

Chapter 5

Monday, 17 May 2021

The day after Marceau Miller’s death

Thonon-les-Bains is in my rear-view mirror. Captain Delmas’s exasperating voice is still echoing, as if he were hooked to my bumper. I’d like to accelerate and leave him far behind me, but traffic on the main road is jammed. I turn off onto the side roads, closer to the lake. It won’t save me any time, but at least I can take it out on the steering wheel. The lake flickers into view through gaps in the greenery—ash, beech, maple. At least it doesn’t betray me. I race along the small roads between houses with tiled roofs weathered by time, sometimes tinged a pale green with moss. Timber-clad homes melt into the landscape. At last I’m nearing home. Familiar scenery, both comforting and cruelly empty. My heart beats to the rhythm of the last bends, wavering between the nagging hope of finding Marceau and the icy fear of his absence.

As I reach the chalet, I spot a vehicle parked in front of the house. The hedge trees barely let me make it out. My heart is pounding. I accelerate along the dusty track, a mix of shell-grit and earth where the holes and stones spare neither tyres nor shock absorbers. In my wake, a brown cloud rises and dusts the leaves around it white. Maybe Marceau has finally come back? There is no room in me for anger, so completely am I invaded by fear. Pins and needles ripple through my body. Fear feeds on me, as though everything were slipping away. Like that strange intuition you get just before something happens.

A figure suddenly comes out of the woods and starts waving wildly at me. I realise I’m driving far too fast. Instinctively, with the surge of adrenaline, my right foot slams the brake pedal, my tyres skidding for several metres. I stop level with the man, who, a little shaken, fans away the air and dust in front of his face. It’s old Reynaud. Then I understand that the four-wheel drive is his. Despite the collapse of my mad hope, seeing him here comforts me. Yves Reynaud knows me as well as he knows Marceau—ever since we were teenagers. He’s also, above all, a retired gendarme, solid as a rock, who doesn’t look his seventy years. We don’t see each other often, but with him it’s as if time swallowed up the silences and the distance. He’s always there, faithful, loyal. Paternal. He comes around the car and climbs into the passenger seat. The engine is still running; I can’t speak, my face must look wrecked. He leans toward me and gathers me into his arms. I suddenly feel overwhelmed and bite my lips to keep from crying. On his shirt I smell summer warmth and the scent of forests, just as I do on Marceau when he comes back from his long walks. Reynaud was born here; this region is his. I cling to him like a godsend.

“Tell me everything from the beginning, Sarah. What’s going on with Marceau?”

In him I see the solicitude of the friend, eager to reassure, ready to do anything to help me, and also the reflexes of the former gendarme, more worried. At least he listens to me. This old fox knows the region like the back of his hand. I tell it all again. That’s always how it goes in cases like this. Just saying the word case frightens me. All this throws us back twenty years. My mind refuses to remember. I know Reynaud is thinking about it. I start the car again more calmly and park in front of the house.

We get out; he doesn’t leave my side, keeps watching me. I lead him to the garden shed; I hadn’t yet thought to inspect it.

“What are you looking for here, Sarah?”

I open the plastic storage bins and catch sight of our diving gear. I rummage through the clutter on the homemade workbench that serves as a desk. I know Marceau comes here from time to time. I find only papers, tools, nothing that catches my attention. I slam my fist down and send a set of screwdrivers flying. Reynaud pulls down the wooden bins from the high shelves. I warn him to be careful; they’re full of nails, screws, tacks. Reynaud handles them cautiously; they’re heavy. One of the bins is empty, but before he puts it back, I rub my fingers over the bottom: a fine film clings to the pads of my index and middle fingers. I bring it to my nose. Chalk. The powder climbers rub on their palms to keep them dry and secure their grip. Reynaud meets my gaze.

“He promised me he’d stop.”

A sob cuts me off and keeps me from saying any more. Reynaud takes me in his arms. Then I want to cry, but nothing comes, as though shedding a tear would confirm that the worst has happened. I’m afraid, as if I already know. All it took was that faint trace of powder, almost imperceptible. No secret can ever truly hide. I feel Reynaud’s heart pounding in his chest. He places his calloused hands on my shoulders; his face changes. Then he lowers his eyes before speaking. I search for them; he lifts them back to me.

“Listen, Sarah, I know Marceau well. We do see each other from time to time.”

“What do you mean? Without me? Why? He never told me.”

I pull away from his embrace.

“We all have our old demons.”

My blood turns to ice. The door to my worst memory creaks on its hinges. Flashes make me shudder. I reject those images. The pain is like the needle on the syringe I dreaded so much as a child—I would thrash and scream. She was my best friend, and it was twenty years ago. She was Marceau’s sister. Born the same year as him, less than a year apart, we were inseparable.

I stare at Reynaud for a long time before asking:

“You’re talking about Jade, aren’t you?”

“That was my investigation back then. I don’t know where we went wrong...”

The ground seems to give way beneath my feet.

“Where is he now, Marceau? Talk, damn it!”

“I don’t know, Sarah, I don’t know... but it has happened that I’ve caught sight of him near...”

He falls silent, as though giving me time to absorb it. But I don’t want to waste time, I want to know.

“Near the goddamned place where Jade disappeared, up in the mountains, is that it? Why rake all that up again? Do you know how much it hurts?”

Everything comes surging back. It hasn’t passed for me either.

Chapter 6

Monday, 17 May 2021

The day after Marceau Miller’s death

Reynaud’s four-wheel drive devours the mountain road toward the border between France and Switzerland, less than forty kilometres away. On the narrow D25, between hedgerows and forests, we pass through peaceful villages in bloom, where the houses with exposed timber and broad roofs seem to have been there for ever, like something from our grandparents’ postcards. At Sciez, we continue on the D1005, the main road leading all the way into Switzerland. Reynaud accelerates and strings together overtakes on the stretch that passes through Évian. There we run along the lake, the Alps on the horizon with their white-capped peaks. As we go on, the hillsides with houses clinging to them assert their dominance. Kilometre by kilometre, the landscape around us turns into mountain. We pass Lugrin, then the border at Saint-Gingolph. I send Karen a text asking if she can handle Benjamin until I get back. I haven’t been back to the exact place we’re heading in years. And yet Marceau and I do sometimes hike in the surrounding area. We have always shared that irrepressible need for nature, for forests, mountains and escape. But right now the immensity around us suddenly makes me dizzy.

The road gives way to a track that gets stonier and stonier. Jolted inside the vehicle, I hold on. The tyres bite into the mountain as we climb, and my eardrums pop. The track narrows and narrows, as thin as a stream at its source. We crawl past the La Planche car park, a tiny patch of dirt and stones isolated among the trees, the last place still reachable by car. Reynaud keeps going on the increasingly unpaved trail. Then, on a bend, a few hundred metres from Switzerland, he cuts through the trees. Branches crack under the tyres. He keeps the four-wheel drive grinding forward in first gear until we can go no farther and stop. We’re at nearly 1,800 metres, about a forty-minute walk from Lake Neuteu. We exchange a glance; Reynaud takes out a flask, slips it into a backpack, and picks up his walking poles. This is the first stage, the easiest. Around us, the forest—but not just any forest. Not for me, not for Reynaud. My heart is in turmoil; I breathe out and set off. Reynaud is already dozens of metres ahead. This climb, which looks like nothing more than a hike, unsettles me. I watch where I put my feet, avoiding holes, the mountain’s traps. I begin to feel heat in my thighs and calves. A film of sweat veils my face. Then it appears before us, Lake Neuteu, ringed by jagged massifs, endless forest and oppressive memories. I drive away the flashes blinding my mind, but the anxiety remains, clinging on. The varnish of the years has never truly covered over the tragedy at the core of my being.

I get there first, galvanised by fear. I’ve overtaken Reynaud, who has fallen behind. My gaze sweeps the perimeter of the lake, isolated in these mountains, like a laser beam. Nearly two hundred metres long and barely a hundred wide, edged with close-cropped grass racing toward the peaks and vanishing wherever the bare rock takes over. Absolute desolation, not a soul on the horizon. The one landmark likely to tempt a loner like Marceau rises there, roughly a kilometre away. The highest rocky peak in the landscape, a monster of sheer verticality made of stone and challenge. Not exactly an easy stroll, more a route for an experienced mountaineer. Reynaud comes up beside me. I think of the chalk we found in the garden shed; a shiver runs down my spine. Reynaud names it for me.

“La Dent du Vélan.”
