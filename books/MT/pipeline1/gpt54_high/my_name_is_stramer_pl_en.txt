I


NATHAN

He came back from America for Rywka. He said that for four years he had thought about that girl every single day, until finally he bought a ticket back on a ship. What he did not say was that he came back without a penny, and that even for that ticket he had had to borrow money from his older brother. But he still liked slipping American words into conversation, though no one in the family understood them.

From America he brought back a leather belt with a brass buckle. A parting gift from his brother for the journey back to Poland. On the New York docks they embraced and never saw each other again. From the wear on one hole after another, you could trace how his figure had changed over the years. Nathan himself could hardly believe he had once been so thin.

Like his father and grandfather, he looked like a wrestler. An old wrestler. Broad shoulders, a neck barely visible, and a chest thrust forward like a rooster’s. He was proud that people in town called him “Boss.”

On the street, when asked how things were, he answered:

— It’s good, but not hopeless.

He touched the brim of his hat and walked on.







Every few months a letter would arrive from New York. Ben wrote that unfortunately, for now, he still had to put off the trip to Tarnów. He could not leave the stationery shop just then. The school year was starting and he had too many orders. He had to let a dishonest assistant go and still had not found a new one he could trust. The Christians’ Christmas was coming soon, the season of presents, and over here it isn’t only Jews who buy! His wife had fallen ill again. The crisis had begun; if he left the shop now, there would be nothing for him to come back to in America. But he missed them, and he was planning for next year. He could hardly wait to meet Rywka and their sons and daughters.



As their parents, you know best what they need, so buy it for them from me.



Into the letter, written on company stationery (BEN STRAMER GENERAL MERCHANDISE, 33 Grand St, NY) and folded in thirds, he tucked green banknotes. He wrapped them as well in purple tissue paper, so that no one could see what was inside. Every time the tissue paper stained the bills, so that when they came out of the envelope they were green and purple, and Rywka had to wash them gently, then dry them over the stove in the kitchen.

Nathan gave the children only the American stamps from the envelopes. The dollars he put away.

— I work all the time, so I have no time to make money, he explained to his wife.







And he waited. Waited and waited and waited. For a good deal to turn up. A deal that would change the Stramers’ lives. Lift them out of the one-room flat with the little kitchen on the ground floor at the very end of Goldhammera Street, raise them high, and set them down only on the top floors of tenement houses with colored stained-glass windows in the stairwells, in the elegant Polish-Jewish quarter, preferably near a stop on the tram line Tarnów was so proud of. In one of those spacious, many-roomed, high-ceilinged apartments, with a toilet and a bathroom with running water, electric light, and a flower-filled balcony.

For a short while it seemed as though it might come true. He had managed to buy, cheaply, a whole railcar of rosin. He had been told violinists used it on the hair of their bows. How was he to know there were so few violinists in Tarnów? In the end he even bought a violin and bow himself and made Salek, of whom Rywka said he had beautiful long fingers, play it.

Though he had never been to the philharmonic or the opera, in his mind’s eye he could already see his seven-year-old son there—on the stages of Kraków, even Vienna. Rywka too found it easiest to picture him in a little tuxedo, with a bow tie and a shock of curly hair. Unlike his brothers, he did not like fighting or getting dirty.

— Here, Nathan said, handing him the instrument. — A famous violinist has never done any family any harm.

And everyone knew how much money there was in ticket sales.

But nothing came of that either.







He did send him a few times to violin lessons with a Polish teacher. Only did Salek really get there? Because whenever guests came and Nathan asked him to give them a “little concert,” Salek made excuses. Until after half a year Nathan stopped asking and ordered him to bring the violin “this instant.”

After the performance he said nothing.

Only when the guests had left did he say, shaking his head on that barely visible neck:

— Goddammit!

No one understood, so he added:

— Money down the drain.

That evening, for the first time, instead of reaching for the belt, he reached for the bow. At least it would be good for something! And it was. He did not have to strike hard at all, and Salek made sounds much like the ones he himself had been drawing from the violin only moments before, during the “little concert.”

At work, behind the till in the Jewish slaughterhouse, Nathan too was waiting for the deal of a lifetime. That was why, in the heat of his thoughts, plans, and meticulously calculated future profits, he would sometimes give customers the wrong change.

— Shit, he would say if, at the end of the day, money was missing from the till.

But sometimes there was too much money. Then he said nothing, only slipped the difference discreetly into his trouser pocket.

I have children, he thought.







When the right moment finally came, he felt like running home from work. On the way he did the shopping. At home he did not sit down for a moment. He helped Rywka prepare the meal. Over supper he asked the children what was happening at school. Another matter that he had no patience to listen. But at least he asked, Rywka thought. Then he told her to sit down and rest. He collected the plates himself, rolled up his shirtsleeves, and washed the dishes in the basin.

Only when he had finished, and the children were already asleep, did he take the dollars, rolled into a tube, out of the metal bed leg.

— I’ve got a feeling this time it’ll work. This is the one.

Rywka said nothing. But Nathan knew what she was thinking:

Why, with that last deal, didn’t it occur to you to inspect at least one candle properly?

He had not wanted to waste time; he was afraid other merchants would get there first, that someone would offer more. But most of all he was afraid that the seller, who had come all the way from Kołomyja, would find out what candles cost in Tarnów and realize he was selling his for next to nothing. Nathan had to act fast. An opportunity like that was surely never going to come again. Candles four times cheaper than those from the famous Jewish factory Pszczółka, which supplied all the churches in Tarnów! Maybe even those in Kraków too. That was what he had heard, though he did not know whether it was true or just advertising. Then again, he had also heard that the owner, Mr. Szpilman, quietly gave money to the socialists. But that, in turn, might have been a smear spread by Pszczółka’s competitors.







At the moment of paying, Nathan even felt, for an instant, like a swindler. Maybe he ought to add a little more for that wretched seller from Kołomyja, or at least invite him to dinner? But the feeling at once gave way to regret that the man had no more goods to sell. So perhaps not after all.

That was how Nathan answered, in his own mind, the question Rywka had not asked.

They sat opposite each other at the slightly wobbly kitchen table; the floor was uneven and the little bit of wood must have slipped out from under one leg again. As usual, Nathan tried to nudge it back with his shoe. It did not work, so he bent down, found the chip, and wedged it back in place. Tomorrow morning, when it was light, he would finally clean it and glue it down, he decided.

They both knew that none of his explanations would convince her, and perhaps that was why they spoke to each other without words. Or perhaps they simply did not want to wake the children. They only looked at each other, but for a moment Nathan still had the feeling that in Rywka’s light eyes, instead of the one candle burning on the table, he could see the thousand he had bought without wicks.

What good was it that the moment he realized, he had run straight to the station and spent his last money on a platform ticket? The train, with the merchant and Nathan’s still faintly purple dollars, had long since left for Kołomyja. Nathan stood for a long time among the people on the platform, repeating incomprehensible American words, before he admitted to Rywka what had happened. And that evening, even with the window shut, on Goldhammera Street you could hear his children crying.







After that he took to his bed and did not go to work for a week. At the slaughterhouse Rywka said the same thing she told the children:

— Nathan has stomach catarrh.

This time it will be different, his look was saying now.

It did not impress Rywka that his eyes had lit up again, the way they had when she first met him. Sometimes she only wondered where that energy of his came from, coming and going in waves. Once she even asked Nathan.

— I brought it from America, he said.

Then she thought of the sea, which he had told her about after he came back to Tarnów. It too was torn by waves; there were tides and ebbs. Rarely was it still—like her.

— More likely you caught it at sea, she said with a smile.

In any case, she decidedly preferred stories about the sea and the great ships to stories about New York. They soothed her. She liked picturing them before she fell asleep.


RYWKA

Nathan had promised many times to take her to the sea, but that was before marriage and children. So far she had never been farther than Kraków.

Of course, when she was young she had been offered a trip to America. What girl hadn’t? A pleasant, graying man with a signet ring on his little finger stopped her in the street. A Jew, of course. He introduced himself as a businessman, the owner of a thriving factory in Nebraska, and “above all an inconsolable widower who has returned to Europe in search of love.”







That search even took him to her parents. Rywka’s father threatened to go to the police. It wasn’t for nothing that Galicia was then called “Scandalicia.” Everyone knew what it was really about and who they were really looking for. All the same, two of her friends from Nisko, Nesa Schnur and Lila Flaum, agreed to go. Or perhaps their parents talked them into it and pushed them out of the house? She heard they ended up in Argentina or Brazil. She never saw them again.

In Nisko people said that pimps called pretty girls “silver teaspoons,” “bales of silk,” and “Smyrna carpets.” Less attractive ones they called “barrels of flour,” and ugly ones “sacks of potatoes.” She still remembered those names. Maybe because the girls used them on one another. She also wondered which category they had put her in.

She knew she was too short to be a silver teaspoon. But she had dark lashes around light eyes, thick black hair, and a trim waist. So she was hardly a barrel of flour, let alone a sack of potatoes.

Now, on Goldhammera Street, those times seemed unreal to her. She had married, moved to Tarnów, borne children. Much had changed in her life. But in the world? You still heard of pimps trading in “tender meat.” All in all, not so many years had passed. It still happened that by mistake she would begin a letter with the date eighteen hundred and… Somehow she could not switch over to the twentieth century.

Maybe because she rarely read newspapers. Who had time for that with six children?







In fact she had given birth seven times. The first son, Kuba, was born too early, without eyelashes or nails. She reproached herself for having needlessly helped Nathan paint and fix up their first flat, even smaller than the one on Goldhammera Street, while she was pregnant. Once she had even slipped and fallen outside the entrance. She should have stayed in bed. Anyway, it no longer mattered.

It was winter; they wrapped him in their warmest quilt, but it did no good. He lived less than three weeks. There was something so unnatural about arranging the funeral of your own child, walking through the snow behind the coffin of your own son. She forced herself to cry. She could not, so she covered her face with her hands. So the others would not see that she wasn’t crying.

The little coffin was not expensive, but even so it cost them everything they had. They buried Kuba by the cemetery wall.

On the night after the funeral she woke to muffled sobbing. She pressed herself against Nathan, and only then did she begin to cry herself.

She did not know whether Nathan missed their tiny baby too. She did not know whether he too was afraid that Rywka would never get pregnant again. Like his sister-in-law Pepi, who even before leaving for America had also given birth too soon and gone to the hospital with the baby, but came out alone. From what Nathan had told her, that loss had decided their departure. Then again, everyone knew America had the best doctors for such things.







But Ben and Pepi, in letters written in an even hand on company stationery, never once mentioned a pregnancy or a child. It was in the letters going the other way—and it was possible they crossed somewhere at sea, Rywka thought—that Nathan, in his nervous hand, each time informed his brother of the birth of another Stramer and described the others. Perhaps that was exactly why they never managed to come to Tarnów. Though they were the closest family after all. Still, not once did they forget to put money in the envelope.

That was easier for Rywka to understand than for Nathan. Because what if it were the other way round? What if we were the childless ones, and instead of dollars, news of more and more American nephews and nieces were coming across the ocean? How would we feel?

So she thought of Ben and Pepi with affection. And whenever she thought of them, Kuba came back to mind.

— The first child is for God, Nathan had once said.

— Don’t talk like a rabbi, she answered.

Who would he have taken after? Would he, like the eldest, Rudek, never ask their advice about anything and spend whole days running around town? What did he actually do there, and where did he get the money he secretly slipped her for the household?

She could only guess.








RUDEK

One Sunday Nathan happened to spot him among a Polish-Jewish gang of loutish boys who, fooling around, were trailing after a regiment of Austrian soldiers marching in tight dress uniforms through Tarnów’s main streets. Possibly on the first anniversary of Emperor Franz Joseph’s death, because there was even a band playing a march. That was what Nathan had come to see. Or rather, to get a look at the violinist. At that time he was still predicting a musical career for Salek; he had only just bought him a used Höfner violin. And he wanted to see what a real violinist looked like. But why couldn’t he spot him anywhere?

Like everyone else, he found himself staring at the drum major conducting the band, at his white baton with the brass ball at the end. Now it was up, now down, now spinning on its axis, and at the finale it flew into the air.

I wonder how much they pay him for that, Nathan thought, and stood waiting: maybe for once the drum major wouldn’t catch his fancy baton.

He would have expected that sooner than he would have expected to run into Rudek here. At first he couldn’t believe that his son, together with the others, was picking cigarette butts off the ground, making insulting faces and gestures at the soldiers. And on top of that shouting across the whole of Sobieski Square:



At Ulm, at Ulm, at Austerlitz

We took it up the ass and never said a thing.



That song Nathan happened to know from his own youth. He began shoving his way toward Rudek.



For we Austrians have this custom, don’t you know:

We take it up the ass and never say a thing.









He did not let him get to the next verse. He yanked his son out of the crowd by the collar. And there, in full view of everyone, thrashed him with the American belt. A few Austrian officers with waxed little mustaches turned their heads without breaking stride; some of them raised opera glasses to their eyes.

Nathan threaded the belt back through his trouser loops and asked:

— Will we ever have it better than we did under Emperor Franz Joseph?!

And he added:

— And if I ever see you again with a cigarette in your gob, I’ll pull it out together with your lungs.

That was less than a year ago, but it had evidently done some good, because lately Rudek could be found every day by the wall of the military barracks, on the other side of Goldhammera Street, where the Austrian garrison was stationed, and the cigarettes were no longer in his mouth but in his pockets.

For a week now, after school, he had been taking Salek there with him. Did Rudek look much older, or Salek much younger, than he really was? Or perhaps the brothers gave that impression by contrast. A tall, broad-shouldered boy in a flat cap and plus-fours bent over a slight boy—squeezed into a tight school uniform with a high collar, still really a child.







Besides, more than thirty centimeters or a year and a half separated them. Salek did not play football at right wing, in a blue shirt and white shorts, like Rudek in Samson Tarnów, or in any other position on any other team. In fact he played nothing, nowhere. He had never learned to whistle through his fingers or spit properly. He splashed his uniform when they shared a bottle of lemonade outside the shop. And worst of all, under a tree he still sometimes managed to piss down his trouser legs, even though Rudek had shown him several times how to avoid it.

— You can shake for two weeks and a drop will still fall in your pants, he instructed him. — Just not on your pants.

Boxing and wrestling were much the same. Rudek made Salek practice on their younger brothers, Hesio and Nusek, again without much success. In the end he more or less taught him one blow—to ram his forehead into a nose. After that Hesio got blood on his shirt, and all three of them ended up under the American belt.

In any case, when someone bothered him, Salek at most clenched his fists in anger and windmilled his arms.

He’s not really in danger anyway, because everyone knows whose brother he is, Rudek felt a warmth inside. But he had also prepared him for the possibility that in another district they might not know, and someone might try to hit him. Then he was to say that he knew Omega, Zachar, or Bänder. There was probably no one in Tarnów who did not think of them with fear.

— No one will touch you.







He did not admit to Salek that he didn’t know them himself either. Why should others know that?

Everyone knew, however, that Omega, Zachar, and Bänder, if they were not actually in prison, could be found in the evenings in the company of prostitutes outside the Hotel Polski on Dworcowa Street. But everyone preferred to avoid that. People said they were afraid of no one.

As if by accident they would shoulder into people, shove passersby off the pavement, and knock whatever was on their heads to the ground. School caps, flat caps, hats, yarmulkes, and the fur shtreimels of the Hasidim landed in the mud and the puddles that were there in every season, even after the slightest rain.

And that was only the beginning. Then the knifemen waited for a reaction. Would the victim venture the slightest objection, even if expressed only by a look, the smallest gesture of displeasure visible in the dark only to their eyes?

— Think you’re such a tough guy?

And then at once they would come so close you could smell what they had eaten and drunk that day in the third-class restaurant at the railway station.

Of the three, only Dawid Bänder did not hit at once. First he played with the knife. And that was what people feared most, because Bänder was unpredictable. One man he would let go, another he would suddenly stick.

— Want me to circumcise you, tough guy? he was said once to have asked the terrified Prince Roman Sanguszko, whose car had broken down one night near the station.







Rudek understood the importance of such an encounter. At school they taught that the Sanguszko family were the former owners of Tarnów, and on the street that Bänder was the great-grandson of Idel Muc, the legendary chief of Tarnów’s thieves. And because Rudek was among the most diligent pupils in both places, he understood that descendants of the city’s rulers had stood face to face.

— Want me to circumcise you, little tough guy? Rudek and his brothers acted out that scene many times for fun in the yard on Goldhammera Street.

— Only not that! Mercy for His Princely Grace! Salek, in the role of Sanguszko, covered himself with his hands and fainted against the wall of the wooden privy.


SALEK

He had picked that up at the theater. For his birthday Rudek had given him a standing-room ticket. They were putting on Samson and Delilah, directed by and starring Dante Baranowski. During the performance, in the cool, half-empty Sokol hall, he could not concentrate on the actors flinging themselves about the stage, now bellowing with rage, now weeping, now fainting. In the role of Delilah he immediately recognized Jadwiga Geron, the elegant wife of his Polish teacher at the gymnasium, to whom he bowed in the school corridors. Should he now, as usual, at least bow discreetly from a distance? He did not want her to feel slighted. He also did not know whether he had the right to sit down, even though more than half the red seats were empty. Whom was he supposed to ask? Besides, all the while he was wondering whether Dante Baranowski had really been born Dante Baranowski.







Perhaps before that he too had been an ordinary Salek or Jurek. And only later, as an adult, in order to appear on a poster and go onstage, had he first changed his name to something more artistic. It made him think of Goldhammera Street, which when they moved there had still been called Zdrojowa.

And how would this sound: Dante Stramer?

After the performance Rudek was waiting outside the Sokol building.

— Rudziu, why is Baranowski called Dante? Salek asked on the way home.

— Baranowski’s mother was Italian, that’s all, and Dante is a typical Italian name.

How did he do it, know everyone and so much about them? Salek looked up at his brother in admiration.

But he too could impress Rudek.

He quickly became popular at the gymnasium, already in his first year, when during recess, at the sight of Father Wątorek, who taught Catholic religion, and Isaac Bleiweiss, who taught Mosaic religion, strolling arm in arm across the uneven courtyard, he tossed out to his classmates:

— The Old Testament is walking arm in arm with the New.

True, some claimed that someone from an upper class had made it up, but Rudek did not believe them. Their friend Romek Brandstaetter, a pupil at the same gymnasium, was after all an unimpeachable witness that Salek had said it first.

Within moments the joke had spread through the whole noisy courtyard and the brick school building. And the bolder boys began shouting it at the religion masters.

— Do you hear that, Professor? Wątorek finally asked.

And Bleiweiss replied:

— Why shouldn’t I hear it, Reverend Prelate?







All sorts of rumors circulated around the school about them. For instance, people whispered that they liked walking arm in arm so much because neither of them had a wife.

Salek did not listen or take notes in Mosaic religion. He did his mathematics homework, wrote his Polish essays, prepared for the tutoring he gave after school. And when he had finished, he looked out the window at the people passing in the street. He thought that after lessons he too would be walking there, and those who then saw him through the window would see simply a boy walking down the street. There was nothing unusual or extraordinary in that, and he himself did not know why he kept returning to the thought that in a moment he would be for others what they now were for him.

He also watched the rubbish bin at the corner across the street. He counted how many people, between one bell and the next, bent over it and put a hand inside. This was already the fourth—though less than a quarter of an hour had passed since the bell—an old woman pushing the rubbish aside, probably looking for scraps of food. Before her there had been a boy, no older than Salek, who with a quick movement gathered bent cigarette butts into the pocket of his coat. Rooks flew to the dustbin too, and they not only made the biggest noise and mess, but also gave the others a bad name, because they were the ones that threw rubbish out onto the pavement. But Salek could never count them.







The Hebrew alphabet now seemed sad to him, gloomy. It reminded him of the poorest part of Tarnów, the old Jewish quarter. The letters hung downward like the sidelocks, beards, and overlong black coats, forever muddy at the bottom, of the people from Grabówka.

Anyway, he already knew everything Bleiweiss taught. Once it had been interesting, might even have pleased him. Fairy tales for the three-year-old boy he had been when his father first led him by the hand to cheder. Not the one nearest home, where Rudek had gone the year before, but one much farther away. Salek’s legs hurt; he almost had to run to keep up with his father. He did not see the curb, stumbled, fell on his hands, but he dirtied his trousers too, and what was worse, tore them at the knee.

— Goddammit! his father cried, jerking him to his feet.

Salek burst into tears and no longer wanted to go to cheder.

— Then you’ll go to the one near home.

A year earlier Rudek had come running back from there with a fever and the resolve never to return. It took their mother a long time to pry out of him what had happened. Only when she promised not to repeat it to anyone did Rudek tell her that the melamed not only yanked ears and beat children with a reed for badly repeated Hebrew words. He had made him strip naked and stand in the corner of the room, and the other children were to come up and spit in his face. Then he had run away.

— What had you done to make him order that? she asked.

— Because I didn’t believe the Jews crossed the Red Sea dry-shod.







Without a word Salek went on after his father. All day in cheder he kept a hand on his knee and repeated passages of the Torah after the melamed along with the others.

True, the old teacher never laid a hand on the children, or even raised his voice, but he had one unchanging answer to every question and doubt:

— Little fool, it’s written so in the Pentateuch.

That was what Salek heard too when, after a year of lessons in that damp room, which was at the same time the melamed’s entire apartment, he dared ask the first question he had been carefully preparing for many days:

— Why, when God saw children sitting on the wall and laughing at the baldness of the prophet Elijah passing below, did He let two maddened she-bears out of the forest, and they tore forty-two children to pieces?

Only Rudek explained to him later that you simply had to respect your father and both grandfathers, even though under their hats they were bald.

In any case, luckily God never repeated anything equally cruel in cheder. Like the melamed, He kept silent at the sight of the oldest boys calmly playing cards, buttons, and slaps under the table. He remained equally unmoved when they stood behind the teacher and pretended to dance a waltz with him, or, stopping up their noses in disgust, took from his cupboard with two fingers his old patched long johns and socks, or imitated a dog that had mistaken the old melamed for a bitch.







Perhaps it was because the melamed was far from the power, holiness, and clarity of mind of the prophet Elijah, Salek thought. Or perhaps because he wasn’t bald; from under his yarmulke long, matted gray hair stuck out.





II


NUSEK

In Tarnów people said that the farther from Grabówka, the cleaner the air. Nusek breathed there only through his mouth. The smell of that district might remind you of the privy on Goldhammera Street, or of Friday night, when after Sabbath supper, from overeating cholent, the children could not fall asleep for a long time.

— PKP? Salek or Hesio would ask every so often.

— RKM, Rudek would answer them.

— No! if she was still awake, Rena would shout. — I won’t allow it. Go out to the yard.

At night the two-room flat on Goldhammera Street turned into a dormitory. Three beds filled the whole room, and a fourth the kitchen. In the middle stood the chairs and the table—you had to remember where they were, especially in the dark on the way to the privy. But Nusek almost every time tripped or at least knocked against something.

Rywka had done her best to give each child as much room as possible. She decided that the tallest and oldest son, Rudek, would sleep in a bed with the youngest, Nusek; beside them Salek with Hesio, only a little smaller than he was; and a little farther off, behind a screen, the girls, Rena and Wela, born last of them all. Only the dog Suchard and the cat Milka chose for themselves each evening at whose feet they would spend the night.







— What does PKP mean? Nusek asked. — What does PKP mean? he repeated, as if Rudek had not heard.

— You’ll feel it soon enough yourself.

— Why do you never tell me anything? Why?

— For the public good. Go to sleep.

— Just tell me what PKP means.

— Polish State Railways.

— But what does it really mean?

— Really. Go ask at the station.

Nusek wondered why they never took him seriously. And how many times had he begged his older brothers to take him with them to the barracks? No point even talking.

Surely they still weren’t holding against him that stupid business with the bow. Especially since he had done everything Rudek told him. He had meant well; proof enough was how hard he had tried—even too hard.

Nusek closed his eyes, but he could not fall asleep. He heard his father’s even snoring. Salek muttering something indistinct in his sleep. And the bed creaking when Rudek turned over and laid his head on their shared pillow.

He could feel his warm breath on his face.

Why do I always get the least of everything? he thought. The least room in bed, the least of the pillow, even the quilt that Rudek steals from me every night?

— Are you asleep? he heard Hesio ask.

He opened his eyes.

— What?

— Want to know what PKP and RKM mean?

— Yes. Tell me.

— And will you carry wood and coal for me for a week from the shed in the yard?







Nusek still could not fall asleep, but now for a different reason. Every so often he looked with admiration at the sleeping Rudek beside him, his mouth open. Maybe he really did deserve the whole shared pillow, even the quilt? Nusek felt like laughing at the very thought of PKP and RKM. Clever devils! That way they could say it at school in front of the teachers or at home in front of their parents. He himself would never have thought of it. You needed someone like Rudek or Salek to invent that. To realize that all you had to do was call something by a different name—and there, done.

— That head isn’t there just to carry a cap, as his father said whenever he was cooking up another deal.

But that saying did not suit his father, and not only because Nusek had never seen him in a cap. Usually, when he left the house, he took one or the other of two old, crumpled hats from the peg.

The saying suited much better the head lying beside Nusek on the pillow.

Again he remembered all the different times he had heard them say PKP and RKM. He had suspected they were vicious swearwords, or a secret code connected with cigarette dealing, girls, or at least communism. And meanwhile he kept snickering, because it sounded so elegant: PKP?—“May a gentleman fart?” And RKM—“A gentleman may, once.”

He fell asleep smiling. As always, he still wanted to catch the moment when he was falling asleep, and as always, at the very last instant, he failed.








RYWKA

At night some of them dreamed of one another. And Rywka was almost certain that sometimes they even dreamed the same dream at the same time. Evidently there were single dreams and shared dreams, she thought—like graves in a cemetery, rooms in a hotel, or cabins on the ships Nathan had told her about.

She could have sworn that once she heard the children playing football together in their sleep.

— Pass to me, Nusek pleaded.

— Hesio, to you, Rudek said. — Shoot for the top right corner.

— Goal! Salek shouted.

Rywka waited to hear what would happen next, but there was only silence.

— Nathan. She pressed herself against her husband and kissed his broad neck. — You won’t believe what I just heard.

— All the children in all the squares and yards of Tarnów are playing it, Nathan said with his eyes closed, — and there’s no need to wake me up over it.

He knew what he was talking about. The day before, on the front page of a newspaper in the kiosk, he had seen a long headline: THE EPIDEMIC OF BALL-KICKING IS SPREADING THROUGH OUR CITY ALARMINGLY. Through the dirty pane it had been especially hard for him to make out the last word. Not like the first, whose big broad letters had drawn him to the kiosk.

As usual, he had not bought the paper; he tried to read at least a few lines there on the spot.



There is not a yard here where a dozen brats, little Jews, or loafers, regardless of weather, do not begin kicking a ball through the mud. We only fear that our ladies may take to kicking too, for then the end of the world would come.









Maybe he hardly ever washes the pane on purpose, Nathan thought with approval, looking at the old toothless seller, so people won’t read for free.

You don’t understand! Rywka wanted to cry out. They were playing in their sleep!

But instead she said:

— I’m sorry. Sleep well, wake well.

In the morning she forgot all about it. She had to get everyone out of the house. To work, to school, to cheder. In the dark room she laid out the clean clothes they were to put on. In the kitchen she lit the fire in the stove. On the table she put before each of them an evenly cut slice of brown bread with butter and poured chicory coffee. Nathan, as head of the family, had the additional right to a breakfast roll.

— Daddy, lend me your little roll, Nusek said. — I’ll give it right back, just let me eat it first.

At first Nusek did not know what they were laughing at. Only after a moment did he join them, and then he himself laughed loudest of all, as if he wanted to make up for the time it had taken him to understand his own joke.

— I’ll give it right back, just let me eat it first, he repeated proudly, pointing to himself. — I’m cleverer than I thought.

Before they left, Rywka washed the children’s faces, straightened their collars, combed their hair.

Already in the doorway, to say goodbye, she first rose on tiptoe for Rudek, then bent her knees and gradually went lower, stopping in turn at Salek, Rena, and Hesio on the way down to Nusek and Wela, beside whom she squatted.







— You go down like a New York elevator. Nathan had promised not to say it anymore, but once again he could not help himself.

Rywka always kissed the children in the same place. Her favorite place. Where the forehead ends and the nose begins. She did not know what it was called, but she called it the “little saddle.”

Every morning the same. Thanks to that—as she kept telling herself and Nathan—she had no time to be bored. These mornings had sunk deep into habit. It even happened that only while eating her own slice of bread or washing the dishes did she realize Nathan and the children had already left. As though someone else that day had woken them, made sure they put on warm underwear, drank their coffee, and did not leave the house late.





III


NATHAN

— I bought it, Nathan announced in the doorway, proud as if it were Tarnów’s Nitrogen Works he meant, and not a sideboard from the neighbor on the second floor, who was selling off his last pieces of furniture before leaving for America.

— For peanuts, he added after closing the door.

That was probably what he liked best about the sideboard. And at once he sent Rudek, Salek, and Hesio upstairs.

— And Nusek? Hesio asked.

— Too small to be a porter.

Rudek was first down the stairs. As quickly as if he were carrying the piece all by himself, not with Salek and Hesio, who could barely keep up with him, gripping the oak legs tighter and tighter so the sideboard would not crash down on their brother.

After the third time they banged it into the wall, Rudek spoke in Anszel Koszcz’s style:

— Gentlemen… is this what you’re being paid for?







Salek asked if they could take a short break so he could tie his shoe. Three steps later, when Rudek set the sideboard down on the first-floor landing, his hands were trembling.

Anszel, the tallest and broadest-shouldered of all Tarnów’s porters, was their unofficial chief. He could probably, without difficulty and without help, have carried down an even heavier piece of furniture by himself. Only recently Rudek had watched him circling by the Pilzno Gate. In search of work he moved slowly, with patient step, among the stalls.

Like the Earth around the Sun, Rudek thought.

And indeed Anszel hardly ever stopped. If the porters wanted to talk to him, they had to walk alongside him, circling in his orbit. It was to him they brought everything. He was their highest authority, from whom on earth—at least on that uneven, muddy patch of earth by the Pilzno Gate—there was no appeal.

Even with the black patch over his left eye and the rope slung over his shoulder making him look like one of the sea pirates their father had frightened them with, Anszel kept order. He generally spoke calmly, even with a smile, but everybody knew what awaited a porter if, say, he stole a parcel—or, God forbid, a customer who failed to pay for a job. More than once Rudek had seen Anszel yank the metal shaft out of his handcart with one quick movement.

And how had he lost the eye? Tarnów knew so many versions. Perhaps even Anszel himself was no longer sure how it had happened, and that was why he told each person something different.

— Besides, there can be more versions than listeners, Rudek explained to his brothers. — Because some people heard a different one every time.







The version Rudek liked best was the heroic one. Years ago, on a certain Saturday in July, hooligans surrounded the Jewish quarter. They attacked people and broke into shops closed for the Sabbath. They must have planned it well, because besides clubs and iron bars they also had sacks, into which the stolen goods went.

— Their bad luck, Anszel would say, never ceasing to circle among the stalls, — was that they also attacked the porters’ families coming out of the synagogue on Fish Square.

Anszel’s men ran to the Pilzno Gate, where during the holidays they left their carts chained together on one long chain. They pulled the metal shafts from them. Jewish laborers joined the porters, carrying crowbars and planks. And at their head went Anszel.

— We drove them out of the district. We smashed the wagons they tried to flee in, and let the horses loose. We got everything back. Only I lost something, Anszel would say, pointing to the black patch.

— But you saved the city from a pogrom, people answered him with unabated admiration. Perhaps because nearby towns and little places had been less lucky.

— They only got their paws lightly slapped. Every time he recalled it, Anszel modestly shrugged shoulders as broad as the sideboard the three Stramer brothers were now carrying down, with difficulty, from the second floor to the ground floor.







At school Salek heard a somewhat less heroic version. Before an older boy repeated it to him, he asked for discretion. Earlier the same request had been made to him by his father, who in turn had been sworn to silence by one of Tarnów’s rope dealers.

— I’m telling you this only to you, Anszel was supposed to have said in a low, hushed voice to that trustworthy man walking beside him.

Before he became a porter, Anszel had spent a few years in prison after catching his wife with a lover. Only it was not clear whether he lost the eye in prison, or earlier, in the victorious fight with the lover, whom he killed.

Salek heard from Hesio yet another account of the event, one in which there was no mention of prison at all.

— As it turns out, it was the sleeping Anszel who was attacked with a knife by a jealous husband. He came home earlier than usual from his work in the slaughterhouse and found him in his wife’s arms.

The thought of a bloodbath, of a butcher’s knife driven into an eye, silenced the clamorous yard on Goldhammera Street for a moment.

True, Nathan, who through the open ground-floor window had overheard what they were talking about, declared at supper that he remembered from his youth how Anszel, dead drunk, had had a bicycle accident. He fell into a ditch. He must have landed on something there. A dry branch, a piece of wire, broken glass. The next day he woke up without an eye, covered in blood, and remembered nothing.







What made the strongest impression on the children in that story was the fact that their father knew Anszel personally.

— I’ve met many like that in my life. Nathan waved a hand.

At the same time he demonstrated to the children the blow of a Jewish porter. He called Hesio over and, unexpectedly, shoved him with his thrust-out belly. Little Hesio went flying into the wall where, luckily, they had not yet put the elegant sideboard with the horse heads projecting from it, but tears still sprang to his eyes. When Salek timidly objected that the porters by the Pilzno Gate and on Fish Square were thin and had nothing to hit with, Nathan did not even look at him, only shook his head.

— In America, he added softly, to himself.

— We’re talking about the blow of a Jewish porter in America, he repeated loudly, as if he had just realized it was not at all obvious to the rest of the family that the real porters, craftsmen, traders, and the whole belly-having rest were there, where his brother Ben had stayed. Among truly tall houses with real elevators. Truly broad, long streets with pavements, full of real shops, cafés, and customers paying for everything in real dollars.

— Even the sky is higher there, it slipped out of him once.

Rywka sometimes had the impression that Nathan had never really come back from there at all. She sensed that he often imagined how his life would have turned out across the ocean. And that it seemed truer to him than the one they had in Tarnów, which was why he neglected it.







Carefully but thoroughly, she wiped the carved wooden columns with the horse heads projecting from the very top. This elegant oak sideboard did not suit the rest of the furniture. The bench Nathan had knocked together out of three pine planks, the chest of drawers with four drawers that barely opened, the table forever wobbling, or the wardrobe standing beside it with the cracked back panel. It did not suit their cramped ground-floor flat—or rather, the flat did not suit it.
