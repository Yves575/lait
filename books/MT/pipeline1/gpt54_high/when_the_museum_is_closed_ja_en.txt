There was a goddess. In the octagonal room, deftly curving her ample body.

“Um, hello.”

“So it’s you. Welcome.”

Her voice was huskier than I’d expected—somewhere between mezzo-soprano and alto, though more alto than mezzo, if ever there were occasion for choral singing.

When I tried to hand over my letter of introduction, the curator swiftly took it from me and spread it out before the goddess. I stiffened at once. How exactly had I meant to place it in her hands? She, however, seemed not to mind in the least, and after a moment she said,

“You’re Horauchi Rika, yes? May I call you Hora?”

I nodded. The sudden announcement of what I was to be called threw me, but it wasn’t as though I had any preferred form of address. The goddess narrowed her eyes. Eyes with no pupils.

“I’d like you to call me Venus, not Aphrodite. I may be Greek in style, but I was born Roman. Though of course that, too, is the English pronunciation.”

I nodded again. Venus narrowed her eyes still more, her carved smile almost brimming over.

“Good. Tell me, am I speaking too quickly? Does this match the form you learned at university? If not, I want you to tell me frankly.”

“No... you’re very easy to understand.”

“Like a textbook example,” I added after a brief hesitation, rubbing my palms on my raincoat. I was sweating badly.

“I’m glad. Well, perhaps I am something like an example. I come from a time before Latin split into Italian and French and the rest, when no one imagined the Roman Empire itself would ever divide.”

Venus laughed aloud. Neither her bare white shoulders nor the smooth belly sheathed in a thin layer of fat moved in the slightest. Apparently marble bodies were bound by a rule: nothing below the neck moved.

That part-time job was by no means a bad proposition. It was simply the kind of work one hardly ever heard of.

The one who introduced me to it was a professor whose lecture I had taken once, and only once. For the first time in years I got off at that station, passed the rotary in a commotion like a wildfire, went through the university gates, and climbed the hill to his office in the north building, passing girls in matching pajama-like clothes. Inside, the first thing I noticed was the old yellow-green carpet. New students, perhaps. As I stood looking at the pile as it impassively swallowed the cheers spilling in from the courtyard, a letter of introduction was held out to me. An envelope with the university’s name printed on it. When I raised my head, there stood someone I recognized.

And yet, though I took it in both hands, my hands remained suspended in midair.

“I’m sorry, but I’m not confident I can do it.”

“As far as conversation goes, there is no one better suited than you. At least among people currently alive.”

It was as though the professor were speaking to someone standing behind me.

“Think of it as something like tutoring. Easier than that, really. All you have to do is talk.”

“But the other side is practically a native speaker, and besides—”

And besides, it was the talking itself. But the rest of my words fell away soundlessly before the yellow vinyl, like spray from a fountain in a park at closing time. While I kept inhaling and pursing my lips, the professor gave a few small, low hops, as if trying to shake water from his ears after some invisible splash, then turned to the mirror and began straightening his jacket. Apparently the conversation was over. With no choice, I left the university. The pajama girls were eating matching sandwiches on a bench outside the gates.

The museum was some distance away by bus. Walking from the stop, all that met the eye were the petals of white magnolias; houses and shops alike were buried in that bone-like whiteness. Checking and rechecking the map the professor had given me, I finally found the building. Its nearly windowless concrete walls and bronze doors looked plainly old, less like a place where cultural treasures and historical materials from every age and place had been gathered than like a student dorm everyone had moved out of. I passed the sign reading CLOSED TODAY, made my way around to the back entrance, took out my phone, and called the number I’d been given. The door opened on the second ring, and I stepped inside when prompted. Like arriving at a members-only party.

And yet the place did not seem to welcome visitors. The living least of all. Perhaps for the sake of preserving the exhibits, the inside was chilled like a morgue; the smell of chemicals had seeped into the floors, the walls, every surface. The chandelier enthroned beneath the atrium ceiling, and the pretty milk-glass lamps lining the hall, had all softly lowered their eyes into darkness for the day. As I passed displays of fossils and earthenware in glass cases, I noticed the racks by the front entrance were filled with notices for children’s events and neighborhood flea markets, but could such things really happen here? Could the dates and times printed on these drowsy flyers ever truly come round somewhere in the world, with children gathering to do science experiments and old sweaters and stereo equipment being sold along a shrine approach?

In that field of vision faded like an old calendar, the only thing moving was the curator walking ahead of me.

“Watch your step.”

He turned as though he had foreseen the moment I would catch my hem on the stairs. The curator, Hashibami, was strikingly handsome. I couldn’t tell how old he was, but in his white shirt and slim trousers he had all the appearance of a young man, and yet the beauty he possessed felt closer to that of a vase or a piece of lacquerware than to that of an actual person. In my pocket, I gripped the letter of introduction. I was no good with handsome men. Especially handsome men who were kind, or seemed kind.

And yet, if any scholar ever insisted that light had gravity, my left hand would be the first to help prove it. When the door at the top of the stairs opened, I found myself pulled toward an arched window. You could never have guessed it from the outside, but the center of the building had been hollowed out, opening onto trees and a small pond, and the corridor running around that modest courtyard, overlooking it, was full of a limpid brightness. Pointing to the bouquet relief carved into a pillar, Hashibami explained,

“This building was donated by a wealthy local family; it used to be their residence. The doors are closed today, but the second floor displays his collection.”

From one of the galleries, orchestral music spilled out. The oboe vaulted upward in a smooth leap; the timpani rolled like mallets digging up an old memory. When we passed a door carved with seashell patterns, I caught the lukewarm smell of the tide and heard the sound of waves. There was even a room with the hem of ivory lace showing beneath the door. A side of the museum I had never known was luxuriating in its day off.

That room was at the very end. Hashibami chose one key from a heavy ring and slid it into the lock; with a click, the door opened. Ignoring the blurred reflection of my yellow raincoat in the corridor glass, I peered into the room beyond.

“This way, please.”

So this, apparently, was my workplace. One shift a week. No experience necessary. Conversational Latin required. Duties: conversation partner to an ancient Roman statue of Venus.

I looked around the gallery again, shifting my eyes little by little toward the corners so Venus would not notice.

It was a room out of myth. Under the ceaseless light pouring in through the domed skylight, they stood on their pedestals like constellations taking shape: Hera glaring this way, Artemis with her hunting dog, Minerva in armor. Eight goddesses of Greek and Roman myth. In ancient Greek, perhaps, or perhaps some other language, those bloodless white lips idly wove a lacework of talk. And at the far edge of the shadow cast by that overlapping, loquacious pattern, in the deepest part of the octagon, she stood.

“Oh, pardon me,” Venus said. In contrast to her frozen shoulders, her jaw moved with remarkable smoothness.

“Hashibami, bring Hora a chair. She mustn’t remain standing.”

The curator brought over what looked like a guard’s chair from the corner of the gallery. When he made to return to the goddess’s side, the alto voice said at once, “Thank you. You may leave us.”

“But—” There was something oddly urgent in his Latin.

“It’s all right. If anything happens, I’ll call,” the goddess went on. “Close the door. Be sure you do.”

Hashibami gave me a brief glance, then went to the entrance, bowed, and shut the door. When his hard footsteps had receded, she said, “Sit.” I sat down, raised my face, and lowered it again at once. I was at exactly the height where my eyes met those of the slightly downcast goddess.

She was every inch the goddess of love and beauty. Upon her pedestal, her voluptuous body bent in a splendid S-curve, and she smiled with a face beautiful yet carefully stripped of everything that gives a human face its individuality. Perfectly symmetrical, with no smile lines beside the mouth and no creases at the corners of the eyes, it had in its happiness a spoonful of resignation carved there from the beginning, which made her seem all the more graceful, all the more mysterious. Her garment, its folds flowing magnificently and never to tremble, seemed to have no intention at all, as clothing, of covering or protecting the body; it concealed neither her abundant breasts nor her rounded hips, and that, together with my own body’s lack of contour, left me ill at ease. When I shifted, the goddess asked,

“Does the chair not suit you?”

“It’s fine.”

I answered by reflex, trying to produce as pleasant a smile as I could. But apparently that answer was not enough. Venus asked again, like someone checking the contents of a thief-child’s pocket.

“Hora, is that chair comfortable?”

“Yes. It’s fine.”

“Tell me—did you attend some kind of school where they teach you to end conversations in the fewest possible words? Is that chair really comfortable? For you?”

For the first time I became aware of the chair’s depth. Then of where my spine was. Then of how much my heels hovered off the floor. As though tracing an X-ray with my finger.

“The seat may be a little too high,” I said, then added, wondering if even that was too brief, “because my legs are short.”

“Thank you for telling me something about yourself. I wouldn’t know, you see. I’ve never sat in a chair. Now ring the bell there for Hashibami.”

As instructed, I went over to the curtain, found a small gold bell, and shook it timidly. Lips that had never known dryness or cracking rippled.

“I still don’t know how long your legs are, but for now, there is no need for you to speak of yourself that way.”

The goddess smiled. My body went rigid. Like a statue.

When footsteps approached and the door opened, Venus instructed the curator to bring another chair. Thanking him for coming so quickly and specifying the proper size with perfect precision, Venus seemed like an excellent boss. If she had ever worked anywhere outside the museum.

She waited a beat after the door closed again. “Now then,” she said.

“Thank you for coming today. You really are fluent in Latin. When Hashibami asked your professor, I’m told you were the very first person he recommended. He said you were extremely gifted.”

“That’s not true.” I let out a thin breath as if trying to escape her white eyes.

“I say it because it is true.” There was no hesitation in the goddess’s words. “I heard you’ve already graduated from university. Are you in graduate school now?”

“Graduate school? No, nothing like that. Right now I just, well, do little tasks, really...”

I tried to catch hold of the words. The words that ought to connect. But the more desperately I chased them, the more lightly they vanished, or else dropped away in pitiable fashion, and inside my raincoat I fell silent, half hoping I’d be fired from the job. But Venus merely murmured, “Tasks. I like that,” and began explaining the work.

What she told me about the duties and conditions was no different from what the professor had already said. My job was to talk with her. My shift would be on Mondays, the museum’s closing day; if Monday fell on a holiday and the museum opened, then the following day instead. Transportation reimbursed. Three-month probationary period.

“If you need to miss a day, it’s no problem as long as you let us know two days in advance. Of course, if it’s because of your health or the weather, same-day notice is fine.”

I nodded. For six months in my student days I had worked as a private tutor, and the family I was assigned to was fanatical about punctuality. On the day the trains stopped because of snow, I rushed to the house of the elementary-school boy I taught, changing buses in a panic, while the cold of my wet socks robbed my toes of feeling. But when I rang the bell at the appointed time, his mother informed me on the spot that since snow was such a rare treat, she wanted to let him play, and that we would reschedule the lesson. I stood there for a while watching my pupil and his pet golden retriever, both wearing matching, magnificent Montbell down jackets, tear around a silver garden that looked large enough to fit two tennis courts. Compared with that, this job seemed to offer far better conditions. At the very least, the goddess was unlikely to change her plans according to the weather. Her marble legs were not about to step suddenly down from the pedestal.

And yet I was still searching for a reason to quit this part-time job. I felt out of place. The goddess was beautiful and so exposed, while I wore more layers than necessary. Always.

I wiped the sweat beading on my forehead. At some point the yellow hood had slipped down as far as my eyes.

Suddenly Venus gave a small yawn. “Tell me, Hora,” she said, her alto voice muffled.

“Are you a sound sleeper?”

“I think... about average,” I answered, keeping my face lowered so as not to look too closely at the way her perfectly balanced mouth distorted.

“How nice. My sleep is always shallow. If I had a healthy stomach, the first thing I’d want to take would be sleeping pills. Strong enough that I’d never have to wake again.”

Only then did I notice the exhibit label.

Aphrodite of Pariano  
Roman copy of a Greek sculpture  
1st–2nd century

Two thousand years had raced by without a sound, turning into bottomless dust and the faint heat of human presence; innumerable days and nights had gone reeling past. It adorned the naked goddess more gracefully than any ornament, and at the same time wrapped her in a solitude no one could approach.

Footsteps were approaching from beyond the gallery door. “Listen,” Venus whispered, as though shielding a candle flame from something immense.

“No painting or sculpture can equal the beauty of a single night’s dream. There, you can become someone else, go somewhere else. I can escape my endlessly continuing time.”

Will you come with me too?

Those half-joking words suddenly brushed my bare skin beneath the raincoat, with nowhere for their meaning to go.

By the time Hashibami returned with the chair and the employment contract, I had somehow signed and stamped it. My wages would be paid by bank transfer on the tenth of the following month.

Oh, someone crossed.

I stopped without thinking. From the rusted stairs of the apartment building I could see a bridge, thin as a sardine’s spine, which almost no one ever used. As I watched it, the frosted-glass window behind me slid open a crack. I drew air in through the gap beneath my hood: the smell of warm cooking, and the savory promise hiding inside it.

The moment I found this apartment, I applied for it. It was old, certainly, and the two-story building stood awkwardly in a district lined with small factories, making it just about the worst place imaginable for sunlight. But the rent was cheap, and the ivy covering the white walls had, depending on how you looked at it, a certain charm. Separate bath and toilet, two-burner stove, washlet, and for some reason there was even a landline in the room.

The one thing I hadn’t expected was that it came with a landlady. With the building—or rather, with everyday life.

I ran down the stairs and opened the door. It wasn’t locked. Stepping over the cardboard boxes piled up in the entryway and pushing aside the bead curtain, I saw Seriko peering into a pot amid the lukewarm smell that hung there all year round, like onions simmering. Beside her, under the incandescent bulb, a mitten nicely browned was on the verge of turning into a small flame.
