*Kokun*, Book One: The Girl from the West







Prologue  
Blue Flower





The wind roared by her ears, whipping at her hair.

A passing shower that had fallen earlier in the afternoon still left the earth damp, and the rock she clung to with both hands and both feet was cold as ice.

“...Sister!”

A high-pitched voice came down to her, and with it a scatter of tiny flecks of mud.

In reflex, Aisha let go with one hand and, by feel, braced the heel of her younger brother’s shoe, dimly white in the dusk.

The instant she did, her body tilted, and the other hand gripping the rock nearly slipped free. Desperately she caught hold again, just barely checked herself from pitching sideways—and the moment she exhaled, her knees began to shake.

If she fell, she would die.

Aisha clenched her teeth and kept holding her brother up until he managed to plant his foot firmly in one of the hollows carved into the rock and steady himself.

Even after he was secure, she could not move for some time.

Panting hard, Aisha waited for the dizziness to pass.

Even if they made it to the top of this cliff path, there was probably no future waiting for them there. She had understood that some time ago. From above, from the top of the rocky mountain, came the overpowering smell of leather, metal, and sweat.

She had been told that few knew this path. If warriors were lying in ambush above, that meant old Uchai—who had made himself a decoy to draw the enemy off and help them escape—must have been captured almost at once.

She could not believe Jiiya had confessed anything. And yet the warriors had guessed where they were trying to flee, and had gone ahead to cut them off.

*Should I let go?*

Her brother’s mud-smeared shoe floated dimly before her eyes. Remembering the smile on his face when he had first been given those shoes, Aisha’s face twisted.





Suddenly the world grew brighter.

The clouds had broken apart in the wind. The setting sun, just before vanishing behind the mountains, cast down several shafts of light upon the rocky slope.

In that unexpected brightness, she saw that the grass rooted in a crack just above the rock she clung to was bearing a tiny blue flower. The wind worried at it so fiercely it seemed ready to tear away, but it did not tear.

The voice of the flower’s scent reached her. Faint, yet sure, its fragrance drifted on the wind like a thread. Soon enough, insects that heard that scented voice would come flying through the lulls in the gusts.

“...Sister.”

The voice came down again. On the cold wind drifted the smell of fear, like a puppy gone rigid with terror.

“Hold fast, Mircha!” Aisha shouted.

“Grip the rock and climb! If you start to slip, I’ll hold you!”

Just a little farther, and they would be on top.

Feeling from the movement above her that her brother had begun climbing again, Aisha too started upward, slowly.









Chapter One  
Encounter





I. Ritaran





The glow of the campfires, invisible until a moment ago, now stood out sharply against the blue dark.

The wind that had been flapping the tents had eased somewhat too.

White smoke rose wavering from the countless tents spread across the encampment and dissolved into the evening sky. The smell of supper being cooked drifted faintly over.

“The rank and file eat before we do.”

The low complaint came from a bearded man slouched heavily in a folding chair among a knot of hard-faced men.

Looking down at his bearded colleague Urairi, Mashu smiled faintly.

“You don’t have to sit in. I can manage the questioning alone.”

Urairi started to toss back some light remark, thought better of it, turned his eyes to the grassland, and muttered,

“...If I could, I would.”





In the evening gloom, the Tenro mountain range loomed in all its vastness. Across the grassland at its foot, a dozen or so mounted riders were advancing toward them, the torchlight they held aloft flickering in the distance.

“Oroki.”

Twisting around, Urairi looked back at the man standing behind him. At the man’s feet, the hound raised its head as if it had been the one called. Oroki laid a hand on the dog’s head.

“Yes.”

“You said the heir was about eight, and the older girl no more than fifteen or sixteen, right?”

“The boy is nine. The older girl is fifteen.”

Urairi nodded and sighed.

“They could just have left them alone.”

He tipped his head back to look at Mashu and went on.

“Don’t you think? Unless some faction meant to raise them up as figureheads, what use could descendants of a king hated by his own people and driven from the throne possibly be?

“That must be why they were left alone till now. So why has Dyukchi suddenly decided to do this now, of all times—in the middle of a campaign?”

Mashu did not answer. Keeping his eyes on the riders, he murmured,

“So they did come from that direction.”

Urairi frowned.

“What do you mean?”

“The riders. If the men who took them are returning from there, then the descendants must have been trying to flee into the Great Collapse Gorge—Toura-Ira—in the Tenro range.”

“...”

Urairi rose and came to stand beside Mashu.

“You said *so they did*. Which means you’d already guessed.”

Mashu glanced at him.

“If they were going to flee, there was nowhere else.”

“That’s not necessarily true. Weren’t they living hidden away in the forests? It’d be easier to run east through the woods than risk mountain paths.”





“They must have thought they had a better chance of surviving if they fled into Tenro, danger or no.”

“Why?”

“You called Keruan a king hated by his people. But there are people in Tenro who don’t hate him.”

Urairi’s face hardened.

“...That’s news to me. The mountain folk? Which mountain folk?”

Mashu kept his eyes on the riders, now clearly visible.

“You weren’t the only one who didn’t know. If anything, there are fewer who do know.”

Urairi narrowed his eyes.

“When did you learn that?”

Mashu looked at him and answered quietly.

“I? I’ve known since I was a child.”

One of the attendants from the great tent Dyukchi used for official business came out. Sweeping his gaze over the dusk as though peering through it, he spotted Mashu and the others and approached at a brisk pace.

“My lords Inspectors.”

Crossing both arms neatly over his chest, he bowed deeply, then said,

“I am to convey Lord Dyukchi’s words: the prisoners will be arriving shortly, and he requests that you enter the tent. That is all.”

Mashu nodded and answered briefly.

“I have heard.”

The attendant bowed once more, then led the two men to the great tent.





The imperial inspectors were the eyes and ears of the Emperor of Umar in the four vassal kingdoms under his rule.

The term *han*, used of those kingdoms, derived from the Umar word *rachi*, meaning “that which marks off a tract of land and encloses and protects it.”

Once, each of these kingdoms had been fully independent, and even now, under the rule of the Umar Empire, they retained a measure of autonomy. In return for the duty of guarding the frontiers—the *han*—of imperial territory, their rulers were granted limited authority over the lands they had once governed. But they were no longer kings of sovereign states. Dyukchi, ruler of no more than West Kantal, was not permitted to conceal anything from men like Mashu.

Leaving their armed escorts behind, Mashu and Urairi walked slowly through the blue dark toward the entrance of the great tent, bright as though a piece had been cut clean out of the night.





*





Inside, the great tent was more spacious than it had seemed from without.

The smoke-hole in the roof stood wide open, and the central hearth glowed red with flame. Lamps had been placed here and there, enough to make out, at least to a degree, the expressions of the senior retainers and clan chiefs seated along the walls.

At the far end opposite the entrance stood an altar beautifully painted in vermilion, blue, and gold. Dyukchi sat before it in a large chair, speaking in a low voice with an attendant beside him, but when Mashu and Urairi entered, he rose, inclined his head, and gestured for them to sit in the chairs near him.

Outside, the wind had been cold. Inside, the great tent was stifling. On the small tables before each man were set fruit juice to wet the throat, nuts and dried fruit to take the edge off hunger, and the like.





Not long after Mashu and Urairi had taken their seats, a bell rang to announce the arrival of the prisoners. The murmuring died away, and the great tent fell quiet.

Dyukchi sat down again and, taking a cloth from his attendant, wiped the sweat from his brow and the back of his neck.

He was a big man, but there was nothing slack about him; his skin was darkened by the sun. Everything about him was large, hands and feet included, but what stood out most were his brows and eyes. When those great staring eyes fixed on someone, anyone would feel ill at ease.

Though he came from only a collateral branch—one merely said to have some connection, somewhere, to the royal line—he was a man with a distinctive instinct for war. On the condition that he serve as a bulwark of the empire in times of crisis, he had borrowed troops from the emperor and overthrown the previous king—who had ascended the throne after King Keruan was driven out, but had failed to suppress the skirmishing among the clans—and so became ruler of West Kantal.

One clan still had not acknowledged Dyukchi, so it could not yet be said that he had all of West Kantal in his grasp. But if things continued as they were, Mashu judged, the land would be pacified before long.

The emperor wanted Dyukchi to pacify West Kantal quickly, and Mashu wanted that too.





The frame and hanging cloth at the entrance were drawn sharply back from both sides, and cold night wind rushed in.

Wrapped in that wind, two powerfully built warriors entered, each leading a captive: one a boy, the other a slender girl.

Neither was bound, but each had their dominant arm gripped by a warrior. One of the men struck an arm to his chest and said in a clear, resonant voice,

“As ordered, we have brought Keruan’s descendants.”





Dyukchi nodded, spoke words of praise to the warriors, and gestured that they might withdraw.

The warriors, however, hesitated.

“What is it? You may stand down.”

At Dyukchi’s prompting, one of them spoke.

“With respect, the older girl has shown no sign of submission. If we let go of her, there is no telling what she may do.”

At that, Dyukchi lifted his thick brows.

“I see. Very well, we’ll be on our guard. Let go.”

The warriors bowed, released their captives, and stepped back, but kept their eyes fixed on the girl, ready to seize her again at any moment.

The girl, however, did not move.

Not so much as a single brow. With black eyes still as stone, she merely stared straight at Dyukchi.

Meeting that stare, Dyukchi said,

“You are Keruan’s grandchildren, Mircha and Aisha, yes?”

The girl opened her mouth.

“I have no name to give a usurper.”

Her voice was hoarse, but steady.

Dyukchi sighed.

“So. No sign of submission indeed.”

He rose slowly and looked down at the brother and sister. Then he took up the long sword from the rack beside him and brought the end of its scabbard crashing down on the floorboards.

The senior retainers flinched. So did the siblings. The boy’s face crumpled, and he began to sob.

“Your brother is crying.”

Dyukchi kept his eyes on the girl.

“Mind your circumstances and your place when you speak. One turn in your attitude, and your brother’s head will be struck off before your eyes.”

The girl looked up at him, her face bloodless. At last she said in a faint voice,

“...That is a lie.”

Dyukchi raised his brows.

“A lie?”

The girl nodded.

“Is that truly what decides what becomes of our lives?”

Dyukchi’s eyes widened slightly.

He stared at the older girl for a while. Then one corner of his mouth twisted.

“If not your attitude, then what decides your fate?”

He might have expected her to stop and think, but she answered at once.

“Your own advantage.”





The senior retainers shifted almost imperceptibly.

Mashu let his gaze move from the girl to Dyukchi.

*Well then. What will you say to that?*





Dyukchi remained silent for a long while, staring at her. At last he sighed.

“My own advantage, is it? ...Well, you’re not wrong.”

Then he dropped heavily back into his chair.

He picked up a horn cup decorated with gold from the small table and drained the milk wine in one pull. After wiping his mouth with the cloth an attendant offered him, he used it to mop the sweat from his face and neck as well.

Then he turned back to the girl.

“You’re not wrong—but you’re not right, either. Your existence is a loss not merely to me, but to the empire as a whole.”

The boy looked up at his sister as if asking a question. She did not look at him. She only kept her eyes fixed on Dyukchi.

“You were trying to flee into the Tenro range. To be exact, into Toura-Ira, the Great Collapse Gorge.”

Seeing the expression on the older girl’s face, Dyukchi added,

“Don’t mistake me. That old man did not confess. He’s a loyal retainer indeed. He resisted harder than one would expect from a man his age, and I had already suspected he might make himself a decoy, so I split the soldiers into two groups from the start.”

The girl’s face tightened. Watching it, Dyukchi said,

“The one who told us where you were likely to run was an honored inspector of the Umar Empire.”





Urairi stirred beside Mashu. He made no move to look at him, but his surprise was plain from his whole body.

Mashu’s expression did not change. He only watched Dyukchi, and the very young girl before him.





Dyukchi spoke in an even tone.

“Your existence serves neither West Kantal nor the empire. Do you know why?”

The girl had gone white. She had probably realized by now that there was no way out anywhere.

Only Dyukchi’s voice echoed through the great tent.

“Your grandfather Keruan was a foolish man. Because of him, many of the people starved to death. Most in this land hate him from the bottom of their hearts. I am one of them.”

The girl’s eyes flickered. Her already pale face turned whiter still.

Dyukchi had more milk wine poured into his horn cup and again emptied it with a roll of his throat.

“And yet, I hear that in these borderlands there are still people who hold you in reverence.

“They’re called the Makishi—the People of the Hidden Valleys. They’re not many, merely mountain folk on the edge of the realm, and they have no ambition for supremacy in this country, so I’ve left them alone until now. But at present, their existence is troublesome. The place they control is the problem.”





From beside him, Mashu heard Urairi murmur, “So that’s it.”

The clan this expedition was meant to subdue controlled the foothills in the western reaches of the Tenro range, but its authority did not extend into the mountains.

They did not have the strength to hold out long against Dyukchi’s expeditionary force. But there remained one possibility of reversing the situation: if they could secure aid from Tatsuketsu beyond the Tenro mountains.





The Tenro mountains, which curved from the north around to the west of West Kantal, were steep and severe. There were only a few places where a large army could invade from the Tatsuketsu side, and at such points forts had been built, jointly held by imperial troops and the forces of West Kantal.

At Toura-Ira, the Great Collapse Gorge in western Tenro, there was also a mountain path by which a small force of warriors accustomed to mountain crossings could come over from Tatsuketsu—but there was no fort there.

Around Toura-Ira, there were many places where hidden cavities lay beneath the ground; step through one and it could cost you your life. Without a guide, the mountain paths could not be followed.

Unless the Makishi, who ruled the gorge and the surrounding region, chose to help them, warriors from Tatsuketsu could not descend into West Kantal on that route. And unless a large army could be committed, it would be impossible to bring the Makishi to heel, for they knew the Great Collapse Gorge like the palm of their hand. That was why neither the rulers of West Kantal nor the empire had ever regarded the region as especially important.

But if the Makishi still regarded Keruan’s descendants as the true king’s heirs, then the situation could change drastically if the hostile clan chose to raise these two as its standard.

Among the clan chiefs seated along the walls of the great tent, some wore expressions that showed they were only now beginning to understand the significance of what was happening.

Those chiefs who had joined this campaign from the southern and eastern regions of the kingdom knew little of affairs in western Tenro. Dyukchi had likely kept the details from them deliberately, so that men who had only recently submitted would not begin entertaining ideas of their own.









“You are, so to speak, an ant-hole in a levee. Ordinarily, it can be left alone. But when a storm comes, the bank gives way from that hole, and disaster spreads through the land.

“I am not a hard-hearted man. There is a part of me that feels it cruel to kill you merely because you are that man’s grandchildren. But it cannot be helped.

“There is the option of keeping you close under my hand, but that too is a somewhat dangerous course.”

At that, Dyukchi suddenly looked tired.

“So that is how it stands. If you must hate something, hate your blood and your fate.”

At a tilt of Dyukchi’s chin, the warriors from before stepped forward and reached for the siblings’ arms.

The boy shrank back in fear and stretched out a hand toward his sister. After gripping his hand tightly, the older girl looked up at Dyukchi.

There was something like hesitation on her bloodless face. Her lips parted, then closed again, as if she had changed her mind.

Dyukchi frowned.

“Is there something you wish to say?”

The older girl seemed to think for some time. Then, as though she had made up her mind, she said,

“You’ve been poisoned.”

Dyukchi knitted his brows hard and stared at her, then wiped his face and neck again with the cloth. But the sweat would not stop. Even to an onlooker, it was soaking his skin at an unnatural rate.

“What did you say?”

The girl looked at him with eyes from which no emotion could be read.

“I smell nethergrass on you. Someone has poisoned you.”

The senior retainers broke into an uproar, some half rising from their seats.

While wiping the sweat from his eyes, Dyukchi gave her a fearless smile.

“A final spiteful jab, is it? Admirable nerve—but if you’re going to lie, make it more believable.”

“I am not lying.”





“Of course you are. Poison brewed from boiled nethergrass root has neither taste nor smell.”

A look of understanding crossed the girl’s face.

“...Then think what you please.”

She sighed, drew her brother close by the shoulder, and turned her back on Dyukchi. One of the warriors hurriedly caught her arm and began leading the siblings toward the entrance of the great tent.





Mashu rose at once.

“Wait.”

Brother and sister halted and looked over. Dyukchi did too. So did all the senior retainers, their faces asking what this meant.

Mashu addressed the girl.

“Lady Aisha=Keruan—do I smell of anything to you?”

She stared at him. Her eyes were black as obsidian.

He thought perhaps she would not answer at all. Then she frowned slightly and spoke.

“...Are you a Ritaran?”

Mashu’s eyes flew wide. Something cold spread from between his brows through his whole head, and his heart began to hammer so fast it almost hurt.

His lips had gone numb. Parting them, he asked in a hoarse voice,

“Why do you think that?”

Still frowning, the girl answered,

“Because you smell of blue-scent grass.”

Mashu stared at her, stunned.

“...What’s wrong with you?”

Ignoring Urairi’s question, Mashu strode toward Dyukchi.

At close range, it was obvious at a glance that something was badly wrong. Dyukchi’s eyes would not focus.

Mashu turned to the attendant.

“Send for the healer and tell him to bring kahful! Don’t get it wrong—kahful! And bring a great deal of water. Hurry!”

Then he called to the warriors holding the siblings.

“Take those two away and confine them securely!”





A dull thud sounded, like a sack of flour hitting the floor. When Mashu looked around, Dyukchi had slipped from his chair and collapsed to the boards.

As the senior retainers leapt up and confusion broke into shouted voices all around them, Mashu went quickly to Urairi and, lowering his voice so the others would not hear, spoke in a rapid whisper.

“Stay with those two. Take the guards with you. Don’t let anyone lay a hand on them. Watch their food, even the insect-repelling smoke—make sure they aren’t poisoned. And later, send Oroki to me.”

Urairi nodded without a word and headed after the siblings. With the warriors, he left the great tent at their side.









II. Poison Without Scent





Dyukchi’s condition turned the corner at dawn the next day.

Throughout that long night, the senior retainers came and went to the tent, but Mashu allowed them no farther than the entrance, permitting them only to look inside.

Some showed anger, but since no one yet knew who had administered the poison, Mashu’s precautions were perfectly reasonable, and none tried to force the issue.

The only one he allowed inside was his subordinate Oroki. Mashu gave him detailed instructions and sent him out into the camp with the dog that followed him like a shadow.

After sitting up all night at Dyukchi’s bedside with the healer, Mashu waited until the ruler’s condition had stabilized, then spread bedding in a corner of the same tent and dozed fitfully.





He woke a little before noon. Dyukchi was still breathing as before, deeply and quietly.

Rising, Mashu stepped out of the tent and drew a long breath.

The grass smelled of rain. Come to think of it, he seemed to remember hearing a sudden downpour drum against the tent in his sleep. At this season, such rain was common in these parts.

The sky was overcast and dull, but the wind was strong, and each time the heavy clouds were swept swiftly apart, sunlight broke through and struck the Tenro mountains with startling brightness.

Preparations for the midday meal must have begun; amid the smell of smoke came the toasty aroma of *tapa*, thin cakes of rice flour, baking.

Hearing footsteps in the grass, Mashu turned and saw Oroki approaching with the dog.

“Well?”

When he asked, Oroki answered in a low voice,

“Exactly as you surmised.”

“There was?”

“There was.”

After hearing the details of the report, Mashu smiled.

“You’ve done well. Get some rest.”

Oroki bowed. His heels rang as he turned smartly away. Mashu called after him.

“And see that your partner gets a good meal too.”

Oroki looked back, one corner of his mouth softening slightly, and nodded. The dog, as if understanding the remark was meant for it, wagged its tail lazily.

Drawing one more deep breath, Mashu turned his back on them.

*...Now then,* he said to himself.

*Another task begins here.*

Stepping from the bright outdoors back into the tent, he was momentarily dazzled.

As his eyes adjusted to the dimness, he made out Dyukchi lying on bedding beside the hearth, with the healer attending him. The smell of medicinal broth drifted through the smoke.





As Mashu approached the bedside, the sound of his steps must have woken Dyukchi. He opened his eyes a little and made a motion asking for water.

The healer lifted Dyukchi’s head and supported him, then held a bowl of water to his lips. Dyukchi drank noisily.

Seeing him swallow without choking, the healer’s face relaxed in relief.

After coughing two or three times as if trying to clear the phlegm from his throat, Dyukchi raised his face and looked at Mashu. Strength had returned to his eyes.

“The thing I was given...” he said in a rasping voice. “Was it truly nethergrass?”

Mashu glanced at the healer. The middle-aged man spoke.

“I cannot be certain, but I believe so. The symptoms that appeared in your body were extremely close to those of nethergrass poisoning. And the antidotal decoction used in such cases proved highly effective.”

Dyukchi said sourly,

“But nethergrass is supposed to have neither smell nor taste.”

The healer nodded.

“It does not.”

Dyukchi turned his eyes back to Mashu.

“Then why did that girl say she smelled nethergrass in my sweat?”

How he ought to answer that question—Mashu had thought about it all through the sleepless night, and every answer that came to mind had left him with a lingering hesitation.

But now that he had actually been asked, he felt none at all about speaking the answer he carried in his head.

Mashu turned to the healer.

“Lord Dyukchi looks stable enough. Can he do without you for a little while?”

The healer went to Dyukchi’s side on his knees and took his pulse, then nodded.

“For a short while, I believe he can.”

“Then take some rest. If anything changes, I’ll send for you at once.”

The healer bowed and withdrew from the tent.

Mashu waited until even the sound of his footsteps had faded before turning back to Dyukchi.





“As for the answer to what you asked—”

“...”

“I think the girl really may have smelled nethergrass. She must simply have a keener nose than most.”

A hard light entered Dyukchi’s large eyes.

“You send everyone away, and this is what you tell me? What I wanted to know was why she chose to say that needless extra thing. The girl knew I had been poisoned. Which means she’s connected to the one who poisoned me...”

Mashu slowly shook his head. Dyukchi scowled irritably.

“You mean to say I’m wrong? In what way?”

“The girl is not connected to the poisoner.”

“How can you say that so certainly?”

“For the very reason that troubles you—because she told you that you had been poisoned.”

“...”

“That truly was a needless thing to say. If she had been on the poisoner’s side, and believed your death would save her and her brother, she would never have said it.”

Mashu let out a small sigh.

“She carried herself bravely, but she’s still only fifteen. When she realized that if she kept silent, a man would die, she likely blurted it out before she could stop herself.”

“A man who meant to have her killed.”

“Even so, there are people who cannot stand by and let another human being die.”

“But nethergrass...”

Cutting across him, Mashu said,

“You are too fixated on the fact that nethergrass itself is odorless and tasteless. The signs of poisoning show themselves in many ways. I too had noticed how heavily you were sweating. The girl, standing directly in front of you, would have seen it clearly.”

“...”

“A king lives under constant threat of assassination, and so he comes to know its methods well. She is King Keruan’s granddaughter. She was likely raised with a thorough education in all the ways such things are done.

“That is why, from your abnormal sweating and the unsteadiness of your gaze, she inferred that you had been given nethergrass.”

Dyukchi looked half convinced, but something still troubled him, and he started to speak.

Before he could, Mashu went on.

“Besides, the one who tried to have you poisoned probably knew nothing of the value of Keruan’s descendants.”

Dyukchi’s eyes widened.

“You know who did it?”

Mashu nodded.

“The head of the Rima clan.”

Dyukchi’s face tightened.

“Rariha? What proof?”

“One of his servants was carrying a cloth for polishing cups. I’m told that servant was assigned to polish the vessels set out in the great tent last night.”

Dyukchi sighed and shook his head.

“If that is why you suspect him, you’re mistaken. I wash my own vessels, polish them myself, and keep them where I can see them. That man could not have smeared poison on them.”

Mashu smiled.

“What he smeared on the vessels was not poison.”

“...?”

“He coated everyone’s cups with kahful sap.”

Calmly, Mashu explained the method of the attempted poisoning.

“You fear poison, and so you drink only milk wine poured from the same container as everyone else’s. If poison were mixed into the wine itself, Rariha would have to drink it too.

“He might have chosen to kill everyone in the great tent and let himself alone survive. But even if that succeeded in removing you, he would earn the hatred of every other clan.”

A look of shock spread over Dyukchi’s face.

“I see! He turned the fact that I polish only my own cup separately to his advantage.”

Mashu nodded.

“Yes. Nethergrass poison is weak against kahful sap. Touch it with the sap and it loses its toxicity. The antidote the healer gave you was also made from kahful sap.”

“He... coated his own vessels, and everyone else’s, beforehand.”

“Exactly. My man spent the whole night searching for whoever had a cup-cloth that smelled of kahful sap.”

Mashu smiled a little.

“With a dog, not his own nose.”

Dyukchi grunted and lowered his head for a while, thinking.

Stroking his chin, Mashu said,

“In the present circumstances, punishing Rariha will be difficult. We’ll have to think through the aftermath carefully before we act.”

“That too, yes,” Dyukchi muttered. Then he raised his face and looked at Mashu. “But there’s also the matter of what to do with that girl.”

“Because she saved your life?”

Dyukchi nodded.

“If only the girl, at least...”

Mashu shook his head.

“That won’t do. If the brother is killed, that girl will direct fierce hatred at you for the rest of her life. It will only leave trouble behind.”

Seeing the shadow that crossed Dyukchi’s face, Mashu said,

“If you mean to show mercy, then let the execution be by poison instead of beheading.”

Dyukchi drew his brows together.

“That would be crueler.”

“No. It only depends on the poison you choose. There are poisons that let one go as though falling asleep, without pain.”

Dyukchi looked at Mashu with a complicated expression. Mashu only met his gaze in silence.









III. Frostgrass





Aisha and her younger brother Mircha were being confined in a tent pitched on the edge of the encampment.

It stood by itself out on the grassland, away from the other tents, so anyone approaching would be easy to spot.

The inside and outside of the tent were under strict guard by the men who had accompanied Mashu, and even meals and changes of clothes were not passed on to the siblings until the guards had inspected them.

Once, early in the afternoon of the day Dyukchi recovered, Oroki came to the tent, called Urairi outside, whispered something to him, and then went off somewhere with three of the guards. Other than that, there was little movement in or out.

Besides the smoke-hole in the roof, the tent had two small windows for air. When the sounds of drilling and other noises drifted over from the encampment, the boy would sometimes come to the window and peer out. The sister never once approached either opening. She only sat inside, passing the time with embroidery on a piece of cloth someone had given her for that purpose.

By the time night had fully fallen and the soldiers had finished their supper, a late evening meal was brought to the siblings.

Behind the servants carrying it came Mashu and two of Dyukchi’s attendants.

The guards lifted the entrance flap and whispered something inside, and Urairi came out. When he noticed Mashu and the attendants behind the supper servants, his face tightened.

Mashu raised a finger to his lips. Urairi signaled with his eyes that he understood.





A servant carrying a large tray of food ducked and entered the tent, while Mashu and Dyukchi’s attendants each moved to one of the windows.

Night had fallen completely, and the camp lights were far away. From inside the brightly lit tent, the faces of those outside would be invisible unless they came very close. Even if the siblings noticed shadows there, the guards had already stood outside those windows many times before. They would have no reason to suspect anything was different tonight.

The meal was finer than what had been served to the soldiers: roast mutton, a vegetable stew, fruit. The *tapa* had been spread with curds and dusted with precious sugar. Naturally, milk wine had been set beside it as well.

The boy must have been hungry. When the food was laid out before him, his face lit up with pleasure. The sister, by contrast, had not raised her head even before the servant came in.

Seeing the way she sat, Mashu narrowed his eyes.

Her shoulders were tight. She was tense.

*Has she noticed?*

The boy reached first for the flatbread and stuffed his cheeks with the *tapa*, softened with melted curds and sugar.

The sister too began to eat quietly. Like her brother, she started with the flatbread, then ate the mutton and the stew slowly, savoring them. She did not leave even the fruit.

After trying a little of everything, the boy reached for the milk wine.

At once, the sister stopped eating and looked at him.





*...Will she stop him?*

Without realizing it, Mashu leaned forward and fixed his eyes on her profile.

Her hand trembled faintly. Her slender neck was stiff with tension, the tendons standing out.

The boy took the milk-wine jar by its handles in both hands and put it to his mouth.

She did not stop him. She only watched him intently.

He drank in great, happy gulps, then set the jug on the floor and reached for the fruit that remained.

And just then, his head wavered, and he started to pitch forward.

The sister shot out a hand and caught him. She laid him down, drew his head onto her lap.

Something glinted on her cheek.

Bent over him as if embracing his limp head, she remained like that a moment. Then she straightened and reached for the empty jug from which he had drunk.

Almost before Mashu could register the movement, she hurled it with all her strength.

The jug struck below the window with a dull crash, and Mashu jerked back instinctively.

Inside, Urairi and the others sprang up in alarm, but she did not so much as glance at them. Seizing her own milk-wine jug, she turned her face toward the window where Mashu stood and, without taking her eyes away, put it to her lips and drank, her throat working.

Her black eyes blazed with a terrible light as they fixed on him.

Mashu tightened his belly and returned her stare.

*Yes. I’m the one who poisoned you.*

Something hot surged up from the bottom of his chest, and his whole body began to tremble.

*You caught the scent of the fingers that pinched the poison into the drink—and that their owner was here as well.*

Before he knew it, Mashu had begun to smile. His whole body burned.

He watched intently as the strength went out of the girl’s eyes and she toppled forward.









*





Dyukchi emerged from his tent, attended by an aide.

A large woolen cloth had been spread on the grass before the tent. By the wavering torchlight of the soldiers, the bodies of the girl and boy laid upon it showed dimly pale.

Even in the dark, their faces looked white, and somehow smaller than they had in life.

Dyukchi stood looking down at them for some time. At last he crouched, put a hand to the boy’s mouth, and confirmed there was no breath.

He did the same with the girl, then touched her cheek lightly and drew back his hand in surprise.

“She’s very cold. It can’t have been that long since they died, can it?”

From beyond the bodies, Mashu answered in a quiet voice.

“Because I used frostgrass.”

Dyukchi nodded, rose, and sighed. Then he turned to the soldiers.

“Wrap them in cloth and bury them.”

That was all he said. Then he turned his back on the bodies and went into the tent.

In West Kantal, the bodies of children were buried beneath a yugi tree.

According to tradition, the yugi tree had sprung from the body of a mother who, in her grief after losing a child, had thrown herself into a river, and whose body had drifted ashore. It was said that if the dead were buried at its roots, the spirit of the yugi tree would gently guide the souls of those who died young to the heavens.





By Dyukchi’s order, soldiers had been searching for a yugi tree since evening, and they had already found a suitable burial place and returned. The two bodies were therefore quickly loaded onto a wagon, carried across the grassland and into a wood along a narrow river, and buried in the pit that had been dug beneath a yugi tree.

The soldiers too were somebody’s children, and some were parents themselves. They did not toss the bodies into the pit. They wrapped them in wool so they would not be cold, laid them gently at the bottom, and covered them with earth so that beasts would not dig them up. Then they bowed deeply and returned to the encampment.





The instant the sound of their horses’ hooves had faded, three shadows leapt out from among the dark trees and began furiously digging up the grave.

They lifted the two bodies from the pit and laid them on the grass beside it, then hurriedly stripped away the cloths that covered them from head to foot.

Next they wrapped the bodies carefully in different cloths, and two of the men each took one—sister and brother—and disappeared into the depths of the forest.

The remaining man put the original wrappings back into the bottom of the pit, arranged them a little, then covered them with earth again, restoring the grave to its former shape.

The moon, beginning its descent, glimmered faintly on the tips of the trees.









IV. The Voice of Scent





A toasty smell drifted to her.

*...Jiiya is baking tapa.*

She was just thinking that she ought to get up soon and wake Mircha too when the terrible memory came back in a flash like lightning, and a sharp pain struck her chest. It spread from chest to throat to head like bitter water.

Aisha opened her mouth and sucked in air.

Her throat whistled like a flute, and at once the smell of baking flatbread, the smell of smoke, the smell of two men and a dog all rushed into her nose.

Within that flood of scent was Mircha’s. Their arms seemed to be touching; she could feel his warmth as well.

*Mircha... he’s alive!*

She tried to open her eyes and look at him, but the world was spinning so violently that she shut them again and waited for the dizziness to subside.

As she heard a man’s voice remark that she seemed to be awake, she desperately tried to make sense of what had happened.

*I drank milk wine with frostgrass in it...*

She had watched Mircha drink down the milk wine laced with the deadly poison frostgrass, and then had drunk her own as well.

*Then why?*

Even with her eyes closed, she felt one of the men rise and approach. She did not need to see to know which one it was.

“You are awake, are you not, Lady Aisha=Keruan?”

Aisha opened her eyes slowly and looked at the man. She was still a little dizzy, but even so, his face came clear.

A sun-browned, hard-featured face. Black eyes fixed on her.

“...Why,” she murmured.

Perhaps he noticed how hoarse her voice was, because he turned and told someone behind him to bring water.

When a flask was brought, he slipped a hand gently beneath her head, supported her, and slowly raised her up. It was a broad, thick, warm hand.





The moment she took a sip from the wooden bowl, she smelled old cedar. The water must have been carried in a barrel. Even so, it tasted wonderful against her parched throat.

When she finished and returned the bowl, the man took it from her without a word.

Aisha looked at him.

Each time he moved, the scent of blue-scent grass grew stronger. He must keep it tucked inside his robe all the time. Mingled with his own smell, it had changed somewhat, but there was no mistaking what it was.

“Why...”

She was about to ask again, but the man shook his head.

“There’s no time to explain now. I’ll explain tonight. Until then, rest here.”

When she tried to look toward her brother, the man rose and said,

“Your brother is safe as well. He woke a little while ago and has gone back to sleep. I had him wrapped in blankets, so he should not catch cold. You too should warm yourself. I’m leaving one of my men behind as a guard, so if anything happens, follow his instructions.”

With that, he strode back to the fire, returned the wooden bowl to the man sitting beside it, plucked a piece of flatbread from the plate and put it in his mouth, then walked off into the trees.

Beside the man tending the cooking fire was a dog. Still lying down, it was watching her.

“If you feel well enough to sit up, would you like to come warm yourself by the fire and have some flatbread?” the man asked. He had a lean face, but there was something sharp about him, almost like a hunter.





Aisha put a hand to the grass and slowly got to her feet. For a moment the dizziness came again, but it quickly passed.

When she sat beside the fire, the warmth seeped into her skin, and only then did she realize how chilled her body had been.

*Frostgrass freezes the body.*

Anyone who ate it by mistake was supposed to grow cold in moments and die as though falling asleep, before there was even time to suffer.

*Why am I alive?*

The man deftly peeled away a thin *tapa* clinging to a small pot, browned and fragrant, and laid it on a wooden plate. Then he spread it generously with curds, drizzled honey over it, and handed it to her.

The instant she felt the warmth of the plate in her palms, something hard that had encased her heart tore open, and the sensation of being alive spread through her whole body.





She had thought there was no road left. That this was the end of her life.

She had imagined being beheaded, so when she learned the execution would be by frostgrass, she had thought that at least she would die more easily than by the sword. While she waited for it, she had been frightened enough to make her bones tremble. But when the moment itself came, and she watched her brother lift the poisoned milk wine to his lips, the fear was already gone. Everything in the world had gone black. She and her brother and everything else had been shut away inside a pitch-dark void.

Only when she caught the scent of that man’s fingers in the milk wine did a searing anger shoot through the space between her eyes.

That they should have to die because of their blood—and the bitterness of leaving this world unable to right that wrong—erupted into rage at something she could not even name, and burned through her.









She slowly lifted the warm flatbread from the plate, folded it to an easy size, and put it in her mouth. The rich saltiness of the curds and the sweetness of the honey mingled and spread across her tongue.

It was a taste she knew well.

The flatbreads she had been given since her capture had carried the scent of ingredients she had never known before. But this was the familiar flatbread she had always eaten. As that smell filled the back of her nose, tears welled up.

Lowering her head so the man would not see, Aisha bit into the warm, fragrant bread.

The dog lumbered to its feet and came over. Its cold nose pressed against her elbow.
