GALLIMARD





Cool rain on blue lawn. Wet summer grass, smells of black earth. Always those August showers on the cropped stalks, burned gold. Heavy drops stream down the pane, winding, snaking, intertwining in long ribbons of liquid light. How many afternoons spent behind the curtain’s gauzy veil, tracing with a finger their path, nervous and languid all at once. Little wisps of hair curl around the cheeks, and you marvel that they can be so white when you are still so young, haloed in ether beneath the window. And suddenly the gaze falls from the window to the hand parting the curtain, and the hand is old, so old.



There are places that harpoon you. That wind their mesh around your dreams, that set their claws just tight enough to let you grow, but leave in your flesh the bruise of their grip.

There are doors whose sound, when you push them open, is like time crying out, still shattering oblivion.

There are staircases whose steps you would so dearly love to climb again, just once, letting the cold smoothness of the banister run through your palm.





That is the House.



By contrast, there are places that send the clammy discomfort of estrangement sliding down the nape of your neck. Places that do not resonate inside you. Places imposed on you, forced on you to endure—it’s temporary, it’s transitional, there’s no choice. When the world grows tired of you, they offer you a television by the bed, a bedside table for your glasses case, yellow wallpaper. Yellow is cheerful, and anyway everything you need is here, we’ll take care of you. They make us trade the House for a room in a medically sanctioned dying-house. That yellow wallpaper—a sledgehammer blow to the nape of my old neck.



Anyway, the only thing I love anymore is the armchair. It’s the only piece of furniture I managed to bring with me. It has always been an old person’s chair: first my great-grandfather’s, whom I never knew, then Great-Aunt Babel’s, when she came to visit us. I understand why people grow fond of it as they age; you recognize yourself in its dust, in its softness. With the tip of my nail, I pick at the scuffs in its dusty-rose velvet.

The comfort of settling your arms in the intimacy of the armrests. The fabric slides close against you like a second skin. It is like finding again the embrace of a lover all syrupy sweetness, one who would have remained far more constant than any man I could have known.





On reflection, I was the only one who truly loved the House. Even my great-great-grandfather, who desired it, imagined it, built it, did not love it as much as I did. I loved it enough to spend my whole life there. To give up my studies in the City, because I suffered too much away from the woods of my childhood. To say no to Oktav, when he held out a little ring to me on Black Bridge one Wednesday afternoon, because he would never have wanted us to live in the House. He wanted us to move into a large apartment in the old town center, not far from the school where he would have taught and where our future children would have learned to surpass us. I loved the House enough to desire nothing else, in all my life, but to stay there, nestled in the hollow of familiar things, letting time burnish me exactly as it did the banister of the spiral staircase.



I still close my eyes whenever I want to remember it. I do that every day, every waking minute perhaps. I strive, impatiently, even violently, to recreate every room, every corner. I cling to the details: the shape of the light switches, the sound of doorknobs turning, the fine layer of dust on the yellow lightbulbs. I want to see everything again, feel everything again. I want to hollow out the space of the present and bring back, with sheer frantic remembering, the places I loved so much, knew by heart, walked all my life, and which, now that I am no longer there, are fading, coming apart.



The House as I left it, four months ago, perhaps five—that I remember. I remember it a little too well, in fact. The slow decay of deserted rooms, dulled floorboards, cold corridors where the paint peels—yes, that. The broken pane in the stained-glass window, through which dead leaves came blowing, whirling faintly across the hall floor like an exhausted couple waltzing at the end of the night—I can still see that, and very clearly. And the cast-iron radiators, faded white, icy in the rooms no one lived in anymore, the bricked-up fireplaces, the sheets over the furniture in bedrooms where no one slept any longer. That was how it was, in the end.



I want to drive that image of the House away. Rekindle the colors, throw wide the bright windows to the intoxicating morning air, wax the staircase, clean the immense dining-room table. Lay the table for fifteen people, as in the great days when everyone, without exception, came.



I want to see again the tall House with its white wooden siding, rising up when you came back from the garden, nestled among the great blue spruces and the maples, with the grass breaking into emerald sprays along the flight of porch steps. I want to see again the facade with its narrow windows, the veranda jutting out on the left, the steep high roof bristling with chimney stacks and immaculate white gables, with the attic’s bull’s-eye window perched like the round gold eye of a reassuring giant, all ringed with carved wood. I want to see again the strange pointed roofs capping the bay windows, and in winter fringed with icicles. I want to see again the windows sparkling in the cool air, caressed by the branches of trees that grew too close to the House whenever a light breeze blew. I want to see again the great House, with the carved wooden columns framing the front door, coated in a warm varnish the color of hard caramel, and the stained glass of entwined flowers that, when the sun shone through it, filtered shards of color into the hall. Painted with immense trellises of tropical foliage, the hall gleamed a gentle blue. There the spiral staircase rose in a whirl of coppery wood.



Everything stood open; the smells from the kitchen circulated freely and flooded the whole House. Voices rumbled from the drawing room to the attic like the murmur of a reassuring torrent running through the walls. Somewhere at the back of my ear, I can still hear it. The floorboards creak and breathe out scents of pine, and even our lightest childish steps make the thick planks groan. We run up the stairs, because it is more fun to arrive dizzy. The corridor on the first floor leads to Uncle’s and Aunt’s old rooms, the ones from when they were little, which they still use whenever they come to see us with the cousins. They sleep directly across from Harriett’s room and mine. It’s easier for games: they only have to cross the corridor and whisper the password at the door so we’ll know they aren’t bothersome grown-ups come trampling everything with their long clumsy legs. At the very end of the corridor is the bathroom, the green one, with its window that sticks a little, but through which you can see a bit of orchard. Opposite is Great-Aunt Babel’s room, when she comes and sets down her trunks exuding camphor. When everyone goes away again, we have almost the whole floor to ourselves. On the second floor we can hardly play at all; that is the parents’ floor, the serious floor, the one with the two most spacious bathrooms. Louisa, lucky thing, even has one of her own, with a little ceramic bathtub, behind her wardrobe. Because she is the eldest of the girls, she has the biggest room, and it is hers alone. Still, I wouldn’t trade my room for hers for anything in the world, because if I did, Harriett and I would never be able to make nearly so much noise, given how close it is to our parents’ room. That is also where the cubbyhole is, which we don’t even use anymore for hide-and-seek: everyone knows perfectly well it’s the first place people think of when they need to vanish from the world. The best place to hide is, of course, the attic. It is not as big as it was when Little Father was a child because since then they split it in two to make Klaus’s room. I like Klaus’s room. It smells of boy, because he hardly ever opens the window. Since the grown-ups never go up there, we’re left in peace. You can hear every creak of the roof on stormy evenings, and there is something reassuring in that garret, warm as a wooden cocoon suspended above the forest.





But the images go by in faded colors, drained of life. The faces are blurred, and that is the most painful thing, realizing that the image of my family, young and alive, is lost forever. I am lost as well. Who was I, at eight years old? Now that old age is breaking my back and wrenching my fingers, I can feel how much I would have been irritated, as a child, by the presence of the person I am today, encumbered by what is no more. I would have hated the puppet theater I put up in my mind to replay the dead images over and over. Who were we, in the woods and in the bedroom, in the kitchen where the soup is steaming? I make puppets dance whose faces are fading away. No matter: there comes a moment when certain people are so familiar to us that we no longer need their physical presence for them to be there. It is only of the people one has grown up with that one can truly say one knows them. Our own particular becoming was faintly tinted by that of others, like water when a single drop of syrup falls into it, enough to color the whole glass a pale mint. I know exactly what Little Father or Louisa would have replied to such-and-such a remark; I can still see Little Mother’s smile when she watched us dance on spring evenings. Then it seems to me that, for a time, I live again, in this body of today, this troublesome creaking body, the days that came to an end and the days that followed them, in the hollow of the House.





I want to remember the glorious tangle of children’s voices, Little Father’s cool, reticent tenderness, Little Mother’s bursts of inspiration when she would suddenly rise from the table, seize the brushes from the pot on the sideboard, and rush to her studio in a corner of the veranda. I want to remember when Klaus, Louisa, and Harriett were still at the House, Klaus already on the brink of adolescence. He was handsome, that prodigy of an older brother, brilliant, funny, indecently gifted. I’d like to grumble again when he ran through his scales at six in the morning and the sweet, rasping lament of his trumpet woke the whole family. I want to find Louisa again, the prettiest of girls, as if she had absorbed all our mother’s radiance and left none for Harriett or me. I remember that when I went by, dragging my feet over the boards on the second floor, past Louisa’s half-open door, she was never on her bed or in her armchair, but always at the mirror in her dressing room, losing her eyes in her own, watching for the flecks of gold deep in the iris.



What wouldn’t I give to find again the room with the two little beds side by side, Harriett’s and mine, with the lamp between them, cause of so many quarrels between us—me always wanting to read later than she did! Her peevish baby face hidden under the quilt, yelling that I was preventing her from getting her eight hours of sleep… “Then you’ll just have to get up later,” I would crow, slowly turning the pages of my novel to drive her mad. “You know Klaus is going to wake us again with his damned trumpet, so turn it off!” she would protest, indignant, tears brimming in her eyes, thumping the pillow. “I’ll turn it off when I’ve finished my chapter.” “Let me see how far along you are.” I would show her a fake page. “See, nearly done,” I’d assure her. She would start hissing like a snake—I was lying, I was the worst of sisters, just wait till she told Louisa… She would let the threat hang and shoot me a furious look from under the covers. Harriett was always appealing to our older sister to settle our disputes, because she was absolutely partial and absolutely unjust. She always took the side of our youngest, who had adorable dark curls Louisa loved to arrange, whereas she said my mop was “bewildering.” But since Harriett was far from docile, Louisa usually left the room slamming the door, then shut herself away in hers to dream of great chandeliers and dinners with friends.





Even the House of my adolescence—I would like to see that again. The House that haunted me every evening in my student room and made me hate the College, curse my studies. They had torn me away from my home, my life, my walks in the woods and my afternoons spent reading, curled on the too-hard window ledge. But the triumph of Friday evenings! When I would rush onto the train, fling myself into my seat, jiggle with impatience, my lips stretched in an irrepressible smile, while the inexorable machine carried me away from the towers of the City! Then I loved the arrival at the station, though it seemed endless, and the moment when I saw Little Father by the open trunk waiting for my suitcase, with Harriett already in the car, since she finished earlier on Fridays.



“Mrs. Aberfletch? Can you hear me? I’m putting your tray on the desk; you can eat whenever you like.”

And there she is, that pleasant nurse, come to dissipate everything in her rose perfume. To hell with her white coat and her insolent youth, her clear voice, a living girl’s voice, that goes home at night. She must spend the whole year waiting for summer, that season of the liquid body, of blond evenings.





Summer





I loved nothing so much as summers at the House. Everything shone then, in the damp languor of the holidays, which seemed endless, stretched out by the long days of delicious boredom. At the first heat of June, everything began to glitter, to overflow with life. The maples and the spruces seemed swollen with incandescent sap; the grass, an insolent green, was crossed by broad swathes of sunlight.





Everyone came back from the City, flowed back toward the familiar countryside and the forests haloed in lustral shade. It was enough to stand at the edge of the woods to feel the wind scented with resin breathe out its murmur. When we were children, my father’s brother and sister, Uncle Bertie and Aunt Hilde, came every summer with their families. The joy when someone announced, “The cousins are arriving tonight!” The whole day would take on an impatient bustle that had us hopping in circles in the drawing room, and, scolded by Little Father, we would race back upstairs stifling nervous laughter. The afternoon passed in a flurry of preparations; I loved helping the grown-ups, with meticulous attention. I made sure, like a little despot, that the attic was aired, the toy chests opened, the sheets carefully folded on the beds. I hurtled down the spiral staircase, waxed for the occasion, I spun through the hall cut through by the stained glass’s shattered light. I went into the kitchen twenty times, bent over the stove, inhaled the aromas as I lifted, with a slightly trembling hand, the lids of the soup tureens. I knew that the nearly ready food was the signal: the cousins would soon be there, and before long we would all be sitting down together in an unbearable din of loud laughter, letting loose the frenzy of a long day of waiting.





The evening the cousins arrived, at the beginning of each summer, was always the same. They were usually worn out by the road they had endured all day, the cramped closeness of the cars, the hot breath of the asphalt, the sun reflected off gleaming bodywork. Even so, we sat down at table eagerly. When everything seemed to waltz with light and laughter and aromas, when our stories were constantly being interrupted by a dish going by, or by another story bursting out from the far end of the table, and when I tipped back in my chair to steal glances at those we had not seen since Christmas, I felt immersed in a bath of liquid joy, golden and sparkling, scented by the honey smell of Uncle Bertie’s cigars or the thyme dissolving in the chicken juices. My gaze went round the table and slid mechanically over the familiar faces, only to catch on the cousins’, always prettier from year to year. I was fascinated above all by their noses, small and round, which came from Bertie’s in-laws. He himself had not escaped the family nose, long and hooked, with the light sliding over it as over a blade. Amelia and Magda had taken everything from their mother, a sort of blond, full-bodied grace, with skin all pink beneath the neck, skin like fresh flowers beaded with water in crystal vases. Always sitting side by side, passing the pitcher carefully when it came to them, they laughed quite openly at all Klaus’s or Harriett’s clowning. Each summer, as soon as the two cousins got out of the car and set foot on the porch steps, they and Louisa sized one another up. In those first moments of their reunion, you never knew whether Louisa and they were about to tear strips off one another or trade some barb among pretty girls; but no, they fell into each other’s arms and went laughing into the House, trailing a scent of precious oils in their wake. Harriett and I spent little time with the cousins, who had Louisa’s temperament and were her age. We were the little nuisances, noisy, a bit alarming, I think. Klaus showed no particular interest in the cousins, and sat at table beside Aleksander, Aunt Hilde’s cherished son. Aleksander combined the traditional Aberfletch nose with a steely gaze that contrasted with the murky-pond depth of our own eyes. His hair was a little ashier and paler than ours, and his very upright head caught the reflections of the dining-room lamp, which cast its warm halo over the whole table. Aleksander and Klaus did not get on especially well, but, being the oldest of the children, they had learned to remain together, in a sort of wary fraternity halfway between competition and solidarity. They competed in dexterity whenever they poured water, each trying to see who could raise the carafe highest without the thin stream swerving from its course. We clapped our hands, staring at the clouded water quickly filling the glass, and screamed when it came close to overflowing.



The adults were nervous; they sent us to bed early, telling us not to “carry on till all hours like last year.” Then the cousins and we would exchange a gleeful glance, our complicity suddenly revived by that reminder of a shared past. In our eyes, for a brief instant, memories drowned in summer light flickered by, quivering with laughter and skinned knees. We escorted the cousins to their room, dancing as we went, wildly excited to have them back with us, and Klaus would rumble his fine voice in the middle of breaking and play the grown-up, saying, “I’m going up to my room, be good, children.” Aleksander grumbled a little, because he was the same age as Klaus and yet had to share a room with the two cousins, whom he cheerfully ignored most of the time. Then Louisa and Klaus disappeared again into the dimness of the staircase, and Harriett, the cousins, and I remained in the dark corridor, the neutral ground between our two rooms. Through the half-open door we were pleased to see that their suitcases had been carried up as if by magic by some devoted relation, and were resting on their respective beds in the lamp’s yellow halo. That room stayed empty all year, its bed frames bare and cold, except when the cousins came. Then great cool sheets were spread for them, still scented with the sprigs of lavender scattered in the wardrobes. The cousins always had tired eyes that first evening, but they seemed truly happy to be back. Harriett and I stayed in their doorway, watching them rush to the window as if to make sure the view had not changed since last summer. Then, satisfied, they rubbed their eyes as they yawned and wished us good night. I took Harriett by the shoulder to force her back into our room, because she was much too worked up to sleep, and wanted right away to play hide-and-seek in the dark, or spy on the grown-ups, or catch Klaus in his attic by creeping very softly up the stairs so they would not creak. I told her we had all the evenings of the whole summer ahead of us to play, and she followed me with a resignation tinged with pleasure.





In bed, lying there with my eyes wide open, my whole being turned toward the ceiling drowned in blue shadow, I felt the weight of the two floors above me, and I sensed the bustling of the adults below, clearing the table, with the warm rumble of their husky voices. I listened to the sounds of the night preparing itself: Louisa upstairs walking briskly from her dressing room to her bed, the cousins on the other side of the corridor putting their things away in the wardrobes, arriving, settling in, taking possession again of the room they found every summer. I felt surrounded by the people I loved, as if I were part of a whole and the House, at last full of this great family sharing my blood, were truly alive. I knew it was not such a terrible thing if we were going to bed a little early today, because there would be other evenings. Harriett’s little rasping voice always ended by rising in the silence, and she would venture, “It’s nice the cousins are here.” On other nights, I would have told her to be quiet, because I didn’t feel like talking. But that night I was curled in my sheets, and outside the summer wind blew softly against the shutters. So I would simply answer, “Yes, it really is.” We talked about what we would do tomorrow. In the almost complete darkness of the room, all I could make out of Harriett was a little dark mass on the pillow swollen with pale shadow, enormous beside her head. And it was my favorite moment of the day, full of promises, plans, ideas made wilder still because they were already entangled with a half-consciousness full of dreams. We slipped into silence like a spark across the surface of the pond, and the waves of sleep closed over our eyes.





We let the days flow by like a stream of liquid light. It was the precious time of elastic hours, evanescent mornings, endless afternoons.



Mornings were always each to their own. You scarcely noticed that the House was inhabited by anyone besides us, everything so silent, with the doors of the late sleepers closed, the drawing room empty but still smelling of Bertie’s honey cigars.



I always woke before Harriett, because my bed was closest to the window, and a shaft of white sunlight always filtered through the crack in the shutters, cutting a warm spindle across my sheets. I stayed for a few minutes in the warm bedclothes, but I could hear the birds outside, the muddled buzzing of wasps knocking against the window frame, and suddenly I would be seized by a wild desire to look at the garden, to see the morning lawn spread glittering before my eyes, and to feel the glorious breeze billow my dress. I did not open the shutters so as not to wake Harriett, and my eyes, accustomed to the faded dimness, could make out without too much trouble the obstacles between my bed and the door. I slipped slowly out of the room, taking care not to make the floorboards creak too much. My toes first met the firmness of the boards, then the slightly rough rug tickling the soles of my feet. I carefully avoided the jutting corners of our two beds, the toy chest, the wardrobe, and cast an anxious glance toward the little bundle curled beneath her flowered quilt to make sure she had not moved. Then I set one hand on the handle, the other on the bolt, and pulled with a sharp jerk, so the door would not squeal—for a badly oiled door always makes more noise when you open it slowly. The morning light flooding the corridor made me sway for a moment on my feet. The row of closed doors was intimidating, and kept me from knowing whether I was the first one up or not. Still a little dizzy, I went down the stairs. There again, you had to avoid the groan of the wood, control where you placed your weight, favor certain steps over others, and so take giant strides, clutching the banister so as not to lose your balance. It was the first adventure of the day, repeated every morning.



When I crossed the deserted hall, the foliage painted there unfurled in frightening fashion, because the morning light cast a cold sheen over it, and the painted flowers seemed blurred, like old stars in the process of disappearing. The floor tiles were always icy, and I hurried into the kitchen. There, the clock clicked, and it was the only time I ever noticed it, because the rest of the time its mechanical pulse was drowned out by the noises of the House, the laughter, the voices, the discordant clatter of saucepans knocking together as they were put away. The kitchen was cool; the windows and shutters on the garden level had been left open so that the night air could circulate. In the morning, we drove out the bark-colored moths that had slipped in to curl themselves into the warmth of the walls. They fluttered clumsily, and their trembling wings beat against the panes without finding the way out.

I closed the windows again, biting my lip when they slammed a little too loudly. The dishes by the sink had dried, and a puddle of cold water trickled, drop by drop, from the edge to the drain. I loved the kitchen windows, with their little white-framed panes looking out onto the waking orchard. I hoisted myself onto the counter cluttered with jars and spatulas and pressed my nose to the glass. Through the mist of my breath I watched the vague shapes of the fruit-laden trees. The leaves, a timid emerald, quivered in the sun like little fish with green veins.



I went through the drawing room and library, deserted, and was already sighing with boredom, telling myself that adults were really too lazy, sleeping like that while the day unrolled without waiting for us. Suddenly I hear movement above. The sound of light slippers makes the floorboards creak; then the game is to guess who it is. If someone comes down the stairs fast, it’s Harriett or Klaus. The cousins come pelting down quickly too, but not as quickly as we do, especially the first few days: they have to get used again to those treacherous steps in their gleaming wood. A heavy, slow tread, that’s Uncle Bertie. Bertie, in the morning, smells extraordinarily good, because he always washes before coming downstairs, and once I saw him pressing great splashes of cologne onto his freshly shaved neck with the flat of his hand. Then the skin under his chin is red and gleaming, and his wake blooms with tall grasses swaying in the wind.
