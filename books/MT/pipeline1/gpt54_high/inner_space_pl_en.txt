Transcript of Questioning

LAMAR: Will you need a Bible?

HUNT: No, thank you.

LAMAR: Please raise your right hand. Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?

HUNT: I do.

LAMAR: I understand you have information relevant to this committee?

HUNT: That’s right.

June 2021

Nate Hunt was waiting for his wife. Lucy was late.

“Dad, I’m bored,” groaned Eliza, their daughter. Nate had her on his lap. Her hair had gone static from rubbing against his sweater and kept tickling his nose.

“Yes, I know,” he sighed. “You’ve said that already.”

Nate was bored too. All these ceremonies, tours, and press conferences seemed never-ending. At the same time, he was terrified. It might have seemed as if those two emotional states canceled each other out, but they didn’t. He had been through this before, eight years earlier. Only then he had been here alone. He hadn’t had to rehearse in his head what he would say to a child if something went wrong. He still couldn’t find the right words. It was possible there simply weren’t any.

“When’s Mom coming?” the girl asked.

“Soon,” he said.

“But exactly how soon?”

“I don’t know, sweetheart.”

“*Ty khochesh konfetu, rebyonka?*” The woman in the chair beside them rustled a bag of hard candies and smiled encouragingly. But Eliza was in no mood to talk to strangers. She turned away from the woman and burrowed into her father.

“*Prostite, ona sonnaya.*” Nate smiled apologetically. He had learned Russian more or less by accident. He had quizzed Lucy so many times before her exams that some of it had stuck. His favorite word was *pochemuchka*—someone who asks too many questions. It fit Eliza to a tee.

The woman nodded in understanding; she had probably been up before dawn too. Wonder how many times she’s done this, Nate thought. Third? Fourth? She didn’t look nervous at all. Quite the opposite: the broad smile never left her face. Maybe because, besides the candy, her purse also held a pack of tranquilizers.

“Let’s at least play a game,” Eliza said.

“Sure,” Nate agreed. “How about I Spy?”

“Okay. You go first.”

Nate looked around the room. It resembled a classroom or a dentist’s waiting room: harsh fluorescent light, beige walls decorated with faded posters, rows of plastic chairs, spindly plants on the windowsill. Except that a thick sheet of plexiglass ran down the middle of the room. On the other side stood a long table, and on it three microphones, each labeled: Cyrillic on top, Latin letters underneath. Anton Kovalyov. Lev Zaitsev. Lucy Poplaski.

“I spy something beginning with m,” Nate whispered.

“A map?” Eliza guessed.

“No.”

“A man?”

He shook his head.

“A microphone?”

“Yes. Your turn.”

The girl started fidgeting, paying no attention at all to the fact that she was digging first an elbow, then a knee into her father’s stomach. The people gathered in the room—members of other families, officials, journalists—waved at her and smiled. But Eliza ignored them completely; she was focused on the game. She took any kind of competition extremely seriously. Once, at a school event, she had come second in a sack race and cried for the rest of the day. “Eliza is a very ambitious little girl,” her teacher had told them on the way out, in a tone suggesting perhaps too ambitious. “She gets that from her mother,” Nate had replied without a second’s thought.

“I spy something beginning with r,” she said.

“A radiator?”

“No.”

“Hmm...” Nate rubbed his chin. “A rubber plant?”

“No.”

“A row of chairs?”

“No.” The girl shook her head with obvious satisfaction.

“Then I give up.”

“A rocket!” Eliza crowed.

Nate looked out the window. It rose toward the sky from a vast expanse of sun-scorched grass—haloed in milky-white vapor, visible through the lattice of scaffolding. It did not belong in this landscape: not with the faded Kazakh steppe, the crumbling, peeling buildings, the howling stray dogs. It looked like the remnant of some extinct alien civilization. In a way, that was exactly what it was: the Baikonur Cosmodrome had, after all, been built by the Soviet Union. Two factors had favored this particular location. First, the latitude made it easier to reach the desired orbit. Second, if something went wrong—if, for example, the rocket exploded just after launch—the risk of flaming pieces of metal falling on someone’s head was relatively small. As far as the eye could see, there was nothing but grass.

Nate had seen such an explosion. He had been six years old, visiting family in Florida, and as it happened the repeatedly postponed launch of the space shuttle *Challenger* was coming up. His parents had taken him to the Kennedy Space Center so he could see the launch with his own eyes, especially since Christa McAuliffe was on the crew, an ordinary teacher who was supposed to teach a lesson from space. He remembered it with a child’s memory, as if he had had a camera with him and taken random snapshots: badly framed, out of focus, overexposed by emotion. He had been given a paper cap with the NASA logo, but the brim kept slipping over his eyes, which made him very unhappy. They found seats in the stands directly behind a man in a cowboy hat. The man blocked everything, so little Nate was now thoroughly offended with the whole world. His father lifted him onto his shoulders so he could see better, ignoring the protests of the people in the row behind them. Then there was a blinding flash and a roar, hot air in his hair, vibrations spreading through his whole body until his teeth rattled and his skin tingled. The rocket climbed higher and higher, drowning out the cheers, belching fire, faster and faster, until it turned into a tiny dot in the sky. And then that dot flared and turned into several smaller dots. Some of them began to fall, while others traced fanciful loops through the air. For a split second Nate thought that was how it was supposed to happen, that they were space fireworks, like on the Fourth of July, and he was delighted. The joy did not last long, and what came after—sobbing, screaming, chaos—haunted his dreams for years afterward. Even so, he too had tied his life to space. The vastness fascinated him, terrified him, drew him in.

A murmur passed through the room, camera shutters chattering in bursts. At last, Nate exhaled. The door on the other side of the glass opened and the Soyuz crew came in—Lev, Anton, and Lucy. It was his wife the lenses turned toward. First იმიტომ because she was undeniably more photogenic than the Russians. Dark curly hair pulled into a ponytail, a broad smile, a freckled upturned nose—she looked like a nice girl next door, someone you could become friends with in five minutes. Second, because she was one of the last American women who would fly to space from Kazakhstan. After more than a decade-long break, the United States finally had its own way of getting to orbit again: Dragon capsules, built in the finest American tradition by an eccentric billionaire. The first flights had been successful, and NASA was already negotiating a new, larger contract that was supposed to free the Americans entirely from dependence on Russian help. The clock was ticking.

“*Uvazhayemyye kollegi, dorogiye gosti...*” began Roscosmos director Yuri Rybkin, a large gray-haired man in a cream blazer.

“What’s that man saying?” Eliza asked.

“Nothing interesting.”

“But what?”

“That a beautiful chapter in the history of friendship between our nations is drawing to a close, but a new one is beginning.”

“What does that mean?”

That soon the Russians would no longer be able to charge ninety million dollars for a seat on a Soyuz, Nate thought. That without those regular infusions of cash Roscosmos would be on the brink of bankruptcy. That beneath the declarations of willingness to continue cooperating lurked envy, grievance, and resentment.

“That we’ll keep flying into space together,” he said instead, “but separately.”

The Russian official finished his speech, and the microphone passed to NASA’s representative, associate administrator for crewed missions Steve Ayers—a stocky, balding man in thick-lensed glasses. More lofty platitudes, wishes for a successful mission, agency doublespeak. At last it was time to say goodbye to family: forced intimacy before the cameras, among strangers, through plexiglass. Nate sat down with his daughter opposite Lucy. He wanted to tell her so many things. That he was damn proud of her. That he would miss her. That he was happy for her. That he was afraid for her. But he couldn’t make himself do it, not in this crowd.

“Hey.” She spoke first. “How’s your morning been?”

So: banalities, empty words. But empty words can be filled with something; they can carry things wholly unrelated to their literal meaning. The timbre of a voice, a pause, a glance—in that way they told each other everything that could not be said, everything they would not even have known how to name, regardless of semantics.

“Fine,” Nate said. “Right, Eliza? Fine?”

“When are you coming back?” the girl asked. The question was also an accusation.

“You know that. In six months.”

“You won’t be here for my birthday.”

“I will, sweetheart,” Lucy said with a sad smile. “More than once, in fact.”

“What?”

“Remember? The station goes around the Earth once every ninety minutes. If your party lasts three hours, I’ll pass over the house twice while it’s going on. And I’ll be thinking about you then. Okay?”

“That’s not the same.”

Lucy pressed her hand to the glass. A poor substitute for real contact, imposed by quarantine.

“I know, sweetheart.”

“Then why are you going up there?”

Lucy didn’t have time to answer. The goodbyes were over. The next time they were supposed to see one another would be in half a year. Assuming everything went well.

The Orthodox priest’s gold-threaded vestment glittered in the flashbulbs. The aspergillum swept a broad arc through the air, and holy water ran down Lucy’s cheeks like tears. Another tradition, another superstition. Every stage of flight preparation—and there were many—was tied, for the Russians, to some ritual, and after each mission another got added; there was more folklore here than at a village wedding. Every participant in a flight must lay flowers at Yuri Gagarin’s monument. Hoist their country’s flag. Plant a sapling in Cosmonauts’ Alley. Sign the door of the hotel in Baikonur. On the eve of launch, watch *White Sun of the Desert*, a hit Soviet film from the seventies. And of course there was the mandatory blessing from a clergyman, regardless of personal preference or faith, and not once but twice. As if the Russians believed a successful mission depended not on the laws of nature or mathematical equations but on the will of capricious heavens that had to be placated with weeks of ceremony.

Lucy boarded the bus that would take the crew to the launchpad. She was already wearing her Sokol suit: custom-made, fitted with dozens of laces, buttons, and snaps, a veritable space corset. Putting it on took nearly two hours and required the help of several technicians, and because it was fully airtight, each crew member had also been given a portable fan—a little blue suitcase—so they wouldn’t boil alive.

When the bus set off, when Lucy was finally, if only for a moment, out of range of cameras and microphones, she rested her forehead against the window and closed her eyes. She had gotten up at four in the morning, it was nearing six in the evening, and she still had a three-hour flight ahead of her to the International Space Station in a capsule the size of an elevator—so every chance to rest had to be seized. The Russians, for their part, were already asleep.

She, however, could not sleep. Her mind kept returning to Eliza’s question. Why? What for? Of course she had answered it thousands of times already: first in the endless selection interviews, then in press interviews, public appearances, and private conversations. But then she had said what was expected of her, what she was supposed to say, what other astronauts had been repeating for decades: that in this way she would serve science, that space exploration was humanity’s most important mission, that she wanted to be an inspiration to little girls all over the world, and so on and so forth. Only those were lies. If someone had given her truth serum—if such a thing existed—her answer would have been less exalted. Why? Because nothing gives you a rush, nothing is as much fun, as flying at thirteen thousand kilometers an hour. Because it’s an incredible adventure. Because others couldn’t, and I could. Because it proves I’m the best.

All of which made the reproach in her daughter’s voice hurt even more. It wasn’t really about the birthday. Lucy was almost never home. She had spent the last four years living out of suitcases, flying between Washington, Houston, Toulouse, Moscow, and Cologne—endless training, courses, preparations, exams. Every hour, every minute of the mission had to be planned and rehearsed over and over at least a year in advance. Lucy had to know every safety procedure by heart, memorize every instruction and technical diagram cold, know the name of every screw, switch, and wire, in both English and Russian. If she were truly doing all this for the good of humanity, maybe she would have been able to justify it to herself, to quiet her guilt. But she wasn’t. She wondered whether her male colleagues had thoughts like these too, whether they too mourned the first steps and first words they had missed, or whether this was simply women’s lot: to feel guilty about their own ambition.

Something else was gnawing at her too. The day before, she had met with Associate Administrator Ayers. It hadn’t been planned, it wasn’t on any schedule, and the words spoken there would be found nowhere in the press reports. Rising tensions. Conflict at the highest levels. Fear of escalation. It was hard to believe something like that could happen in the twenty-first century, and in blood-soaked Europe at that. But Ayers had been entirely serious. Storm clouds were gathering on the horizon.

The International Space Station was a child of the optimism of the nineties. Russians and Americans, freed from the logic of the Cold War, no longer had to race one another among the stars, so they joined forces for the good of humanity. Together—with Europe and Japan participating—they built a laboratory orbiting the Earth for the modest sum of a hundred and twenty billion dollars. A symbol of the new age, of shared dreams, of the end of history. Of course behind the lofty slogans lay pragmatic calculations. The Americans wanted access to Soviet technology; the Russians needed money to keep their space program alive amid the chaos of perestroika, and no one but the Americans was then capable of putting up the sums required. Everyone got what they wanted, and everyone was satisfied.

But now, more than twenty years later, the numbers no longer added up. First, technically speaking, Roscosmos no longer had anything to offer the Americans: it still relied on Soviet solutions from the sixties. Second, a new superpower had emerged—China—ready to put money into the Russian agency, and without tedious questions about human rights, democracy, or freedom of the press. In other words, the marriage of convenience had begun to fall apart, but the cosmic house still had to be shared.

The bus stopped in the middle of nowhere, several kilometers from the rocket; the chirr of crickets carried across the steppe. Right. Time for the last tradition. She stood up, and Anton ushered her down the aisle with an exaggeratedly gallant gesture. Broad-shouldered, square-jawed, with short blond hair, he was actually handsome. Only the teeth yellowed by cigarettes spoiled the effect. They had served together on the station when she flew there the first time, eight years earlier. They knew each other well. That was exactly why she avoided him.

“Ladies to the left, gentlemen to the right,” Anton ordered when they stepped onto the hot asphalt. Lev came right behind him: a head shorter, self-contained, with a mustache as bushy as his eyebrows. Lucy had met him during training—and liked him. He was a man of the old school, sincere and devoted to the cause. He deeply believed the chasm between East and West could be bridged, or at least that from the perspective of orbit the division ceased to make sense. He was the embodiment of the Soviet ideal: hardworking, rational, convinced that the future could be better than the past. This was his last mission, his farewell to space. He looked as though the sadness of it was already upon him.

A moment later snaps clicked free, zippers hissed. The Sokol suits, so carefully sealed less than an hour earlier by a swarm of technicians in latex gloves, cotton caps, and plastic overshoes, before the eyes of families and journalists, were unsealed again. Of course everyone, including those same technicians, knew that this was exactly what would happen. Because on April 12, 1961, in precisely this spot, Yuri Gagarin had asked the driver to stop the bus. The man who was about to cast off the shackles of gravity first had to deal with the most earthly of needs: he had to pee. And since he then flew and came back in one piece, every subsequent cosmonaut had made the same stop ever since—just in case. As usual, the men had it easier. The women, to satisfy the demands of tradition, brought along urine collected earlier in a plastic bottle. Lucy poured its contents over the rear tire, then flicked off the last drops with a sharp snap of the wrist she had copied from the priest who had blessed them earlier.

From the loudspeakers roared the official cosmonauts’ anthem, *Trava u doma*, heralding that the crew were already in the rocket and less than two hours remained before launch. Steve Ayers wondered how many times he had heard that plaintive song, how many times he had stood on the viewing terrace at the Baikonur Cosmodrome. The twentieth? Twenty-fifth? He had no idea. He had seen off so many missions here that they blurred together; his passport was full of Kazakh customs stamps. But this moment, this final stretch, still sent adrenaline surging through him. In a moment they would send people into space. It was impossible to get used to that; it never stopped being astonishing. A few more photographs, speeches, a teleconference with mission control in Moscow, and the officials went out onto the viewing terrace. It was nearing eight in the evening.

“One thing ends, another begins.” Director Rybkin leaned on the railing. A leaflet with the day’s program, folded in four, stuck out of his back pocket. Rybkin came from the military, and it showed that he had not served behind a desk. His eyes gave him away: alert, focused, always moving, trained to spot enemies and sniff out traps. The habit might yet prove useful. His predecessor had been dismissed less than a year earlier amid scandal, on corruption charges that threatened him with a long stay in the gulag. No one had bothered to wonder whether he was guilty or not; one look at his much younger wife’s jewelry was enough to form an opinion. More puzzling was why the prosecutors had noticed him only now. In other words: how had he disappointed his patrons? Which expectations had he failed to meet? What had changed, in the meantime, behind the thick walls of the Kremlin?

“It doesn’t have to end,” Ayers replied. They were speaking English; it was easier that way.

“Oh? So you do want to buy a seat on another flight?”

“If you lower the price below the competition...”

“Right... the free market.” Rybkin grimaced. “Unfortunately, I don’t think that’ll be possible.”

“There are other projects.”

“For example?”

Ayers raised his eyes meaningfully to the sky. The sun had already set; the moon showed through the clouds. The last time a human being had stood on its surface was more than fifty years ago. There had been many plans to return, but what was lacking was enthusiasm, money—and a good reason. During his long career at NASA, Ayers had more than once watched lunar-return projects fed into the shredder. Just when he had begun to lose hope that anything would come of it, that people would once again travel beyond Earth orbit in his lifetime, the situation had changed completely. SpaceX—a private company, a start-up that industry veterans had laughed at only a dozen or so years earlier—had shown that it was possible to fly more cheaply, more often, and farther than anyone had dared dream. What was more, advances in nuclear fusion had made the long-cherished dream of using the moon’s immense reserves of helium-3 as a revolutionary new fuel begin to look attainable. America was looking at the night sky with growing interest. And not only America.

“We’ll find another partner.” Rybkin stepped back from the railing and clasped his hands behind his back.

“China?”

“Would you object?”

“China won’t be a partner for you.”

“And were you?”

Ayers did not answer at once. He felt that from now on he would have to weigh every word.

“I’d like to think so,” he said.

The Russian turned his gaze toward the rocket. Bathed in floodlights amid the night-black steppe, it looked like an enormous monument raised in honor of—what? Human curiosity? Power? Arrogance?

“Do you know how much we pay Kazakhstan to use Baikonur?” Rybkin asked after a moment.

“The figures are publicly available.”

“Say it out loud. Let’s remind ourselves.”

“One hundred and fifteen million a year.”

“But one hundred and fifteen million what? Rubles, perhaps?”

“No. Dollars.”

“Dollars, exactly.” The Russian nodded. “Is that a lot of money or a little?”

“For me? A lot.”

“Exactly. And how much money did Kazakhstan put into building this cosmodrome, which it now graciously lets us use? Are those figures publicly available too, Mr. Ayers?”

“You’re trying to draw me into a political discussion. And I—”

“Just tell me: how much?”

Ayers looked Rybkin in the eye. He was trying to understand where the man was going with this, what he wanted. Was this only about old grievances, or something more?

“Kazakhstan was part of the Soviet Union at the time,” the American said diplomatically.

“Ah, exactly. And what happened that the Soviet Union fell apart?”

“I don’t think that’s relevant to our conversation.”

“It’s relevant to every conversation.”

“Let’s say there are differing opinions.”

“Yes, very different ones,” Rybkin agreed. “Mine is that our partner had a hand in it. The same partner who has now taken half my budget.”

“Private companies are simply cheaper. That’s not our fault.”

“Ah, feels like the nineties all over again,” the Russian snorted. “Maybe now you’ll tell me about the invisible hand of the market too?”

Oh boy, Ayers thought, time to change the subject.

“I have the impression our conversation is no longer moving in a constructive direction,” he said with the warm smile that only an American can paste onto his face.

“Really?” Rybkin did not let go. “Because in my view some very concrete conclusions are emerging from it.”

“*Zazhiganiye... Predvaritelnaya...*” crackled the loudspeakers. The gantries leaned away from the rocket, the umbilicals fell away; it looked as though a flower were opening its petals. “*Desyat'! Devyat'! Vosem'!*”

“And what conclusions might those be?” the American asked.

“*Sem'! Shest'! Pyat'!*”

“That all this”—Rybkin pointed first at himself, then at Ayers—“was a mistake.”

Their faces were flooded with the light of flames.

Just above Lucy’s head dangled a plush dog. It had a black nose, pink fur, and huge eyes. It weighed exactly twenty-three grams and was an important piece of Soyuz equipment. It reminded her of the souvenir Nate had once brought back from a conference in Edinburgh. It was a framed stone with the label “Scottish weather station.” If the stone is warm, the sun is shining. If it’s wet, it’s raining. If you can’t see it, it’s foggy. The dog’s instructions would read as follows. If the dog isn’t moving, you haven’t launched yet. If it starts trembling, the engines are on. If it starts bouncing, you’ve left the ground. If it’s floating in midair, you’ve reached orbit. If it’s on fire, something has gone very wrong.

The toy began to shake. So did her hands, legs, stomach, tongue, eyes, brain—the vibrations spread through the whole capsule. Lucy sat, or rather lay, on the left side, curled up with her knees pressed almost to her chin, wedged into a cavity made from a plaster cast of her body; to endure several hours in that position, everyone swallowed a horse dose of painkillers as a precaution. Every cubic centimeter had to be used inside the cramped Soyuz cabin, which was why above her head, sheltered under a plexiglass bubble, the bulging luggage compartments were stuffed to the gills. Packing them was the responsibility of the capsule commander, Anton. If the weight were distributed unevenly, if there were even half a kilogram more on one side than the other, the vehicle would veer off course. And when you were traveling through the vastness of space, that was never good news.

“Launch vehicle readings nominal,” came a voice through the headset.

Lucy felt an enormous force pressing her into the seat, squeezing the air from her lungs. Beneath her, forty thousand kilograms of rocket fuel were burning. She glanced at the screen: the g-force was at two g, two and a half, three, three and a half... She wanted to laugh, cry, shout with joy—at last, after eight years of waiting, work, sacrifices, her own and others’, she was leaving Earth again. But she smothered it inside herself as deftly and impassively as if she were flipping a lever on the control panel or pressing a button. For an astronaut, that skill mattered just as much as knowledge of orbital mechanics or first-aid techniques. Lucy was always being watched, assessed, evaluated; even now, in this moment, there was a camera in front of her face transmitting her image to mission control centers in Houston, Moscow, Toulouse, and Tsukuba. She knew that although on this flight she was really more passenger than pilot—the craft was under Anton and Lev’s control, and even they had little to do for now, since the onboard computer handled most tasks—she had to look serious and focused, otherwise she would end up on the radar of the psychologists overseeing the crew. So she followed the readouts on the screens, checking off one item after another on the checklist.

“First-stage separation,” Anton said.

It felt as though someone had suddenly eased off the gas. The rocket jettisoned its tail, along with the empty tanks and four engines, each the size of a truck. They would soon burn up in the atmosphere, like sacrifices offered to gravity.

“Launch escape system separation.”

There was a bang. The spike concealing the motors that, in the event of an accident, would instantly yank the capsule free of the rest of the rocket sprang away. The most dangerous phase of the flight was behind them now. They could breathe.

“Fairing jettison.”

The capsule flooded with light. Until now the windows had been covered by metal panels. Lucy looked outside. Half the window was filled with the black abyss, stars shining in it—seemingly the same stars she saw in the night sky, yet not the same at all: without the veiling shroud of the atmosphere, their light was much sharper, much more distinct. She felt as if she were at the optometrist’s, trying lenses, and had finally found the right pair, the ones that showed the world as it really was. Below, Earth slid past. It was night over Asia; city lights were burning. She had missed that sight. Every day. Every moment.

“Still impressive, huh?” Anton said.

“It is.”

“Second-stage separation,” Lev read from the computer prompt. Of the soaring tower that had lifted off from Baikonur, nothing remained now but the capsule. “Third-stage ignition... initiated...”

“Just like that view from Cupola,” Anton went on. There was mockery in his voice. A challenge.

“Speed thirteen thousand seven hundred and two miles per hour,” Lucy said, ignoring the bait.

“We were over Turkmenistan then too.”

“Solar array deployment...” Lucy flicked the appropriate switch. “Initiated...”

“Those lights over there are Ashgabat... and there, Bishkek...”

“Attention,” came through their headsets. Flight controllers from Moscow. “Loss of signal in thirty seconds. Communications will resume in approximately twenty minutes. Begin preparations for the first delta-v maneuver.”

“Copy.”

Silence fell in the cabin. For exactly half a minute. The bitter words spoken afterward were not entered into the official record.

The Baikonur Cosmodrome began to empty out. The loudspeakers fell silent, the floodlights illuminating the pad went dark. The officials got into the limousines waiting for them, the journalists folded up their tripods and slipped their cameras into their cases. Nate wondered what demand there would be for their footage, who would want to air it and—more importantly—watch it. A trip to the moon or Mars could still fire the imagination, and people still got excited by the feuds of billionaires zipping around in orbit, but flights to the International Space Station had become routine. News of yet another launch was, of course, followed by a faithful circle of enthusiasts, but it no longer made the front pages—not even the second or third.

Nor was that surprising. The ISS was, after all, a laboratory—one that flew, certainly, but still a laboratory—and how excited can you get about pipetting, even in zero gravity. NASA’s press office understood that perfectly well. So it did what it could to squeeze some enthusiasm out of the station: astronauts recorded videos explaining how the space toilet worked (look, you strap your feet in with these straps, and you pee into this funnel...), spun like tops, squeezed juice from tubes and chased droplets drifting away from them, veritable circus performers of microgravity. But after a while even that wore thin. And so something that only half a century earlier had scarcely been a dream—really more a fantasy than a dream—had already had time to fold itself into daily life, to melt noiselessly into the background. Such is the fate of every breakthrough, every technology: we are capable of getting used to anything. Perhaps that is the key to our success, the reason an animal made for life on the grassy savanna, a two-legged ape, has not yet gone mad in this world of glass, wires, and screens. Or perhaps rather: has not gone completely mad.

“Dad, are we going?” Eliza asked. The girl was tired. A taxi was waiting downstairs.

“One more minute.”

Nate lifted his eyes to the sky, to the place where only moments earlier the fire-breathing engines had flickered. There was no trace of the Soyuz now; it had dissolved among the stars, one dot among thousands. He took a deep breath—he could smell exhaust and hot asphalt—then slowly let the air out of his lungs. The fear that had been eating at him for weeks, fastening onto every thought and seeping into every dream, had finally eased a little. But it had not disappeared, because it never did: it was ready to come back at the first opportunity, at the slightest trigger, like a spider sensing even the faintest tremor in the web stretched inside his head. That was how he imagined it: thousands of silver threads strung inside his skull, and among them gleaming black eyes that never blinked.

That was why Nate was glad to be going home, back to ordinary life, to the familiar routes between house and school, store and gas station, park and library. He needed routine to tame reality, to cope with it. That, perhaps, was what most set him and Lucy apart. She was always hungry for new experiences; he avoided them, even when it came to something seemingly pleasant and simple. A weekend out of town, say. Sounds great—but what if the weather turns, or they run out of gas, or get a flat, or Eliza gets sick, or they arrive and something is wrong with the reservation? Of course, in the end they went—because he wanted to go, or at least knew he ought to want to—but the questions never disappeared. So Nate drew up ever more intricate contingency plans in his head, branching like the Amazon delta, and undertook elaborate preventive measures. He filled up the day before, checked the tire pressure and the oil, made doubly sure he had packed the rain jackets and that the hotel room was booked for the correct date. Nate did not go on vacation so much as take part in a kind of three-person military exercise in which, as chief of staff, he had to keep checking maps, receiving reports, and identifying potential threats. Under such conditions, relaxation was obviously hard to come by. He admired the scenery only after coming home, in photographs.

Lucy was his total opposite. Always composed, always in control. For her, a new problem was an interesting challenge, a puzzle to solve. Running late for the airport and stuck in traffic? Great—time to work out an alternate route. A deadline for an article is a few hours away and the computer freezes? Excellent opportunity to expand one’s knowledge of IT. You couldn’t stump her, couldn’t throw her off.

They had met fourteen years earlier at the International Conference on Planetary Science. He, a geophysicist, was then a member of the team working on a probe that was supposed to go to Titan (it never did; the money ran out). She, still an astrophysicist at the time, was presenting the results of a spectroscopic analysis of the planets in the Gliese 581 system in the constellation Libra. He had noticed her because her poster, instead of being mounted on a folding stand, was hanging from an improvised contraption made from a broom handle, a straightened wire hanger, and thread from the hotel sewing kit. It turned out the airline had lost her luggage, so she had had to build a new stand out of whatever she had at hand, and in under an hour. They talked for a while, he made a joke, she laughed. He asked if she had any plans for the evening. She didn’t. Sometimes—rarely, but still—he regretted not asking what her plans were for the next ten years as well. Who knows: maybe then they wouldn’t have gone out for drinks, wouldn’t have kissed in the hotel elevator, wouldn’t have spent the night in his room—in short, there would have been none of that chain of cause and effect that had led Nate here, to the cosmodrome in Kazakhstan. Partners of astronauts had it hard. Partners with a temperament like his had it especially hard.

“Nate!” Someone put a hand on his shoulder. Associate Administrator Ayers. Though this was only the third time in their lives they had seen each other, he sounded as though he were addressing a best friend. You could tell he had spent his life in politics. He had a reputation as a player as skillful as he was ruthless. “Will you be at the farewell dinner?”

“Sorry, not this time. The little one has to go to bed.”

“I understand. If you need anything...”

“Thank you, we have everything we need.”

“Excellent... In that case, we’ll all be seeing each other here again in six months?”

If everything goes right, said the voice in the back of Nate’s head. If Lucy’s capsule doesn’t enter the atmosphere at too steep an angle and burn up. If it doesn’t come in too shallow and skip off the planet’s cloak of gas like a stone skimming a lake. If the heat shield doesn’t fail. If the cabin doesn’t decompress. If the parachute opens. If they don’t go into a spin that produces lethal g-forces. If they don’t smash into the Kazakh steppe.

“Exactly,” he said, forcing himself to smile.

The bolts clanked free and the hatch began to open. The Soyuz filled with the deafening roar of fans and the characteristic smells Lucy remembered from last time: sweat, heated metal, stale plastic. In the open hatchway above them, the faces of the station crew appeared. Anton came out toward them first—a light push, and he was already drifting toward the opening, as though swimming through the air. Lucy swept the hair floating in front of her face aside and tied it back tightly with an elastic. She was ready; she could hardly wait. Now Lev—one flick of the hand and he was up. Her turn. She stood on the seat, bent her knees slightly, and flew after the others.

Although she had been here before, although she had done hundreds of parabolic flights to accustom her body to weightlessness, her head still spun and her stomach clenched. Up, down—suddenly those words had lost their meaning, become useless. She felt as though she had found herself inside an optical illusion in which nothing was where it ought to be, one that tangled the senses more and more the longer you looked at it. Lucy felt sour saliva rise in her throat. Don’t puke, she thought, just don’t puke. Someone was already hugging her, patting her on the back. She nearly banged her head on a camera lens. They were in the Russian segment, in the Rassvet module, so it was cramped, too cramped for two people, let alone nine. They clustered together like fish in a net.

Lucy recognized three of the astronauts they were relieving; their Soyuz would be returning to Earth in a few days: Igor Volkov, Kate Hortchak, Francesco Russo. A moment later Dragon pilot Ezra Wendley was greeting her. Red-haired, muscular, white as a sheet, he had once been military, and he looked the part. To her left she caught sight of Lafayette Summers, a perpetually smiling botanist, an African American from Louisiana. Last in line—though it was hard to call it a line, since everyone was moving; it would have been easier for her to plan a mating move in three-dimensional chess than to say who was where at any given moment, whose hand that was and whose leg—was Devaki Anand, an American of Indian descent. Lucy knew her the least well. She was the only one in the group who was not a professional astronaut but a space tourist. Of course that did not mean she got to sip cocktails from a glass with a little paper umbrella; NASA had put that crew member to work too. How much she had paid for her stay on the station was a secret, but it was undoubtedly a seven-figure sum. For Devaki, who had made billions in Silicon Valley, such an expense was hardly a problem.

Lucy dreamed of going straight to her cabin—grand word; in reality it was a compartment smaller than a broom closet—getting undressed (though no one said it out loud, astronauts made the trip in diapers, officially and euphemistically called “high-absorbency garments”), and washing up (that is: wiping her entire body with disposable wet wipes, because there were no showers; slow-floating water plus microgravity equaled a guaranteed short circuit). But there was one more item on the program: the press conference to cap the flight. They moved into Nauka, one of the station’s oldest—and newest—modules. Oldest, because construction on it had begun back in the nineties. Newest, because the Russians had only managed to finish it and send it into space at the beginning of this year. What they had not managed was docking it safely: when Nauka was attached, its previously malfunctioning main engine unexpectedly fired, pushing the whole station off orbit, which came within a hair of disaster. Inside, the module looked like any other: cables, diodes, electronics protruding from every surface, everywhere.

“Okay, let’s line up...” Volkov, the station’s current commander, took charge. “The new arrivals in front... the rest in back...”

It took them a moment to get into position. To avoid confusing viewers on Earth, they all adopted the same orientation, as though they were standing on a floor, though half of them might just as well have been hanging upside down. Everyone grabbed hold of something—those who had just arrived instinctively used their hands; the rest, already used to microgravity, hooked their feet under the restraints instead. It was more comfortable that way. Three, two, one: the little light by the camera’s glass eye turned red.

“ISS, this is Moscow,” a controller called. “Not too cramped in there?”

“We’re managing somehow,” Volkov replied with a smile. Like the other Russians, he wore his hair cut short, and his neck was red with razor burn; shaving in space was especially unpleasant. Above his head, a gold Orthodox cross gleamed where it had been fixed to the wall.

“Excellent. Soyuz, how was the flight?”

“No difficulties whatsoever,” Anton said.
