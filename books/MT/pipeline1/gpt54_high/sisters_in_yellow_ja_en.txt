Chapter One  
Reunion





1





I had thought that no matter where I ended up living from here on, no matter how old I got, no matter what became of me, I would never forget her.

And yet just now, until I happened by chance on a small article online and saw her name, I hadn’t even realized that I had forgotten everything—not only ever having thought that, but her name, her very existence, the time we spent together, and what we did there. All of it.

Yoshikawa Kimiko.

For an instant it crossed my mind that it might be somebody else with the same name, but I knew instinctively that the person in the article was that Kimiko-san.





<On December 23, the first hearing was held at Tokyo District Court in the case of Kimiko Yoshikawa (60), unemployed, of Shinjuku Ward, Tokyo, who has been charged with assault causing injury, intimidation, and unlawful confinement for allegedly confining a woman in her twenties from Ichikawa, Chiba Prefecture, inside an apartment in Shinjuku Ward for a year and three months beginning last May, assaulting her and inflicting serious injuries. When asked to enter a plea, Yoshikawa stated, “I will remain silent,” while her defense counsel argued she was not guilty.

According to the indictment, beginning around February 2018, Yoshikawa allegedly confined the woman, who was living with her, in a room in an apartment in Shinjuku Ward for one year and three months, assaulted her, and caused serious injuries requiring one month to heal.

In its opening statement, the prosecution explained that in 2017 the victim, who had no fixed address, began living with Yoshikawa at her home. At first the two lived together without incident, but Yoshikawa then began controlling the victim’s belongings and friendships and monitoring her movements. Prosecutors alleged that by repeatedly threatening her with statements such as, “Even if you go outside, you won’t be able to survive anyway,” Yoshikawa instilled fear in the woman and deprived her of the will to escape. They further stated that Yoshikawa assaulted her on multiple occasions, unlawfully confined her, and kept her under her control, making her obey as she pleased. The case came to light after the woman escaped on her own and reported it.>










After reading the article three times over, I let out a breath that seemed to come up in a lump from deep in my chest. My heart was pounding hard enough that I could feel a faint tremor in my fingertips. It was Kimiko-san. There was no mistake. That Kimiko-san had been arrested.

When I searched for Yoshikawa Kimiko, only one more article with similar content came up, plus another tiny report just a few lines long. Both were minor items. After that there was nothing but pages about name divination, lucky stroke counts, and recommended names for baby girls. It seemed there was no information online about Kimiko-san herself apart from the case I had just seen.

I tried to sort out what I ought to be thinking about, and where to begin. I went back to the first article and checked the date again.

It had been published on January 10, 2020—about three months ago. And it said the incident had occurred last year, in May 2019.

But no matter how carefully I read it, I couldn’t quite make sense of what those dates actually meant. I understood that three months had passed since the first hearing, but I had no idea what state the victim or the other people involved were in now, or how the case and the trial might unfold from here. What was happening to Kimiko-san now? What would happen to her from here on? Where was I even supposed to find that out?

I couldn’t imagine at all how police questioning worked, or detention centers, or the order of things and the rules surrounding them. All I could picture were the pitiful stock images I’d picked up from TV dramas and news programs: drab gray little rooms, handcuffs, expressionless judges, courtroom sketches.

And Kimiko-san’s face.

Kimiko-san, with whom I’d lived for several years some twenty years ago, when I was still young. The article said she was sixty. That Kimiko-san, sixty? I couldn’t believe it. If I thought about it normally, of course that much time really had passed, and I myself was forty now, so it was only natural. Even so, the age printed beneath Kimiko-san’s name simply refused to feel like a real number.







I closed my eyes and told myself I was fine. Obviously, I had nothing to do with this case. There was nothing to worry about. For twenty years I hadn’t known what Kimiko-san was doing, I hadn’t been in contact with her, and in any sense at all, we were no longer connected. A long time had passed since then. Everything had gone by. It was over. So far, apart from this case—apart from the confinement case Kimiko-san was said to have caused last year—nothing else had become a public issue. At least, nothing was online. It’s fine, I told myself again and again.

When I lifted my face from the phone screen, the room was filled with the dark blue of evening, which until then I hadn’t even noticed drawing in, and the shadows of everything had thickened. On the low table sat the plate of spaghetti with retort-pouch meat sauce I had put out for myself to eat. But with the sense of night slowly coming on, it somehow no longer looked like food.







That night I woke over and over, and morning came with almost no sleep at all.

The curtain lit by the spring morning light looked like a huge blank sheet of drawing paper. When I shut my eyes against the brightness, colors swam and vanished—dark blue, deep red, yellow—and there Kimiko-san’s face rose in my mind.

Gathering up her thick black hair, kinky and long enough to reach the middle of her back, Kimiko-san had said with a cheerful laugh, There’s so much hair on my head, you wouldn’t even notice if there were a whole black cat curled up in it. I laughed too, and everybody else laughed. An old house. Each room was tiny, crowded and cluttered with things, but the entrance was always clean. We were allowed only two pairs of shoes each, and there was a rule that because the entrance was where good fortune came in and the toilet was where bad things went out, both always had to be kept clean.







I shut my eyes and rolled over, trying to shake off what was surfacing in my mind. But things I shouldn’t have remembered anymore, things I hadn’t remembered in so long, came to me one after another as if hand in hand. The creak of the hallway, sagging in places, turned into our laughter; the grain of the ceiling I used to stare at before falling asleep became someone’s cigarette smoke, as though whispering to me.

The cosmetics left scattered in front of the mirror, the clothes and underwear stuffed tight into the color-box shelves in the closet, the cups of instant noodles piled in a basket in the cramped kitchen—all of it rose in my mind. More than anything, they brought back the smell of those days we lived there.

After lying there worrying for about thirty minutes with the covers pulled up around me, I sent a message to the group chat for my part-time job.

<Good morning, this is Ito. I’ve had a bit of a cough since yesterday. I don’t have a fever, but just to be safe, I’d appreciate it if I could take the day off today. I’m sorry for the trouble, and thank you.>

A reply came almost immediately from the person in charge of scheduling.

<Understood. We’ll be deciding our policy going forward at the start of the week, so I’ll let everyone know then. Since times are what they are, I’ll report it to headquarters too, just in case. Take care!>

<Thank you! I think it’s probably just a regular cold, but if I happen to develop a fever or anything like that, I’ll contact you right away. Thank you again!>







Until the middle of last month, there had still been a general atmosphere of half believing and half not when it came to the virus. People made a lot of noise about it, but in the end it was the same as influenza, plenty of them said, so there was no need to be that afraid, and masks were useless anyway. There was a floating feeling in the air, as if anxiety and a strange excitement had mixed together, but even so it still felt, somehow, within the range of ordinary life.

But the reports from overseas gradually grew more frightening, and from around the end of last month into the beginning of this one, rumors began to spread that Japan too would finally go into lockdown—or maybe not.

Then, five days ago, the state of emergency was declared, and the tension that had been building and building suddenly burst all at once. It wasn’t only in the news anymore: there was actual panic-buying at the supermarket in my neighborhood, and masks, disinfectant, and toilet paper vanished wholesale from the drugstores; people disappeared too, and at my workplace we ended up scrambling to respond. I worked as a sales clerk at a deli in the shopping street within walking distance of my apartment, one of a chain of prepared-food shops operated by a major supermarket with branches all over Tokyo.







It was a small shop, with about thirty dishes of side items and salads lined up on counters and in chilled cases, and customers would pick what they wanted from among them and we would pack it into bento containers. Since all we did was sell what arrived finished from the central kitchen each morning, there wasn’t even a kitchen on-site, and with four people inside the place already felt full. For the three years since I’d started working there, it had been the same menu over and over, and on top of that there were lots of regulars who came every day, so I sometimes wondered how they didn’t get sick of it—but maybe that sameness gave people a sense of security, because the shop did well, and at lunchtime and in the evenings there was always a line. But by the end of last month, customers had finally dropped off sharply, and things quickly started to go wrong. When customers did come in, they got into arguments with each other over whether or not to wear masks, and we even started getting complaint calls saying our infection-control measures weren’t sufficient.

Lying there, I thought that maybe it hadn’t been a good idea to say in the Line chat that I had a cough. Why had I lied about that? Why had I gone out of my way, at a time like this, to say I had a cough? What exactly had I been thinking? I didn’t know myself.







After a while I picked up my phone, went back to the site with the article about Kimiko-san, and read it again from the beginning, slowly, taking my time. My mood darkened each time, and my arms and legs felt heavy. Whatever reason I had given, I was glad after all that I had taken the day off. Even if the job was mostly just standing there, there was no way I could have worked in that state.

I got out of bed, took some barley tea from the refrigerator and drank it, then pulled a box down from the shelf in the closet.

It was a slightly oversized shoebox, the corners crushed and the lid a little torn, and inside were old letters, planners, notebooks, and things like that.

The shoebox, worn and so faded that its original deep navy looked completely washed out, had once held a pair of high heels my mother bought somewhere long ago. I remember her happily trying on those pure-white heels in the room, and being so delighted by them that she sat down on the tatami still wearing them and ate instant ramen. I asked her for the empty box and started using it to keep stickers in, and little freebies from manga magazines, and the tiny notes I exchanged with friends at school. After that it became a habit to put in it whatever I felt like saving at the time. I’d moved from place to place, leaving things behind and losing them, and before I knew it only this shoebox remained. Even so, under ordinary circumstances I would never have opened it or even picked it up. Looking at it now, though it was mine and I had somehow brought it all the way here without ever throwing it out, I felt as though I were looking at somebody’s remains.







When I opened the lid, I saw tucked into one corner a small navy flip phone and its charger. I was the one who had been looking for it, and yet the instant I found it, my heart gave a jolt. I plugged the charger into the phone, not knowing whether it still worked, connected it to the outlet, and after waiting about thirty minutes, pressed and held the power button. The little screen slowly brightened, as if breathing back to life, and a startup tone sounded.

I had changed my own number and wiped everything clean so no one could contact me anymore, and yet I had kept these old numbers—people I’d known in the past—without deleting them. Had I sensed somewhere deep down, without even knowing it, that one day I might need them like this?

There were only seventeen entries in the address book.

Under K I saw Kimiko-san’s name. I selected the one just above it: Kato Ran. Then I went down to M. Momoko. Tamamori Momoko. I copied both numbers into the memo app on my current phone.

I had no idea where Kato Ran and Tamamori Momoko were now, or what had become of them.

The last time I’d seen them was when the two of them left that house—back when we were about twenty, or around that age. Since then I’d never contacted either Ran or Momoko. If I hadn’t happened to see the article about Kimiko-san yesterday, maybe I would never have remembered them at all.







The time all of us spent together in that house came back to me like footage stitched together without any logic.

Voices and expressions replayed themselves, going suddenly sharp, then blurring again. I didn’t think these numbers would still connect, and I didn’t want to contact either of them now, after all this time. But the only people I could talk to about Kimiko-san were Ran and Momoko. The only people who could share the fear I felt now were those two.

What I feared was that Kimiko-san might have talked about the past she had with us.

During questioning, all kinds of evidence from the period when we lived together might have been found in Kimiko-san’s room, and maybe, beneath the surface, an investigation had already begun. The thought made me too agitated to sit still. No one had come to me yet, but it was possible that the police had contacted Ran or Momoko first, called them in, and were already questioning them.

If I thought about it calmly, maybe what we had done would fall outside the statute of limitations. Maybe it wouldn’t count as such a serious crime. Ran and Momoko and I had all still been young, and we’d done it because Kimiko-san told us to. But then what about Kotomi-san? In the end, whose fault was it that Kotomi-san died, and why had it happened? Could we really say we had nothing to do with it?

The more I thought, the more a burden settled over me—an anxiety I could no longer distinguish from fear. It felt as though some huge iron plate were silently crushing my chest, and tears stung my eyes. What should I do? Should I pretend I’d never seen the article and say nothing, contact neither Ran nor Momoko? Or should I go to the police and tell them what I knew?







My imagination kept swelling in darker and darker directions, until it blocked out everything else. How were the two of them now? Where were they? What were they doing? Of course the numbers wouldn’t connect. But if they probably wouldn’t connect anyway, why not at least try calling? I set the phone aside and pulled the blanket over my head, as if to blot everything out. The daylight was cut off, and for a while I lay there in the lukewarm spring darkness, blinking. Then just like that, I dozed off.

I dreamed one of those elusive dreams that you know only as a nightmare. No specific event happened, no particular people appeared—there was only time there—yet why is it that with a nightmare you always know it is one? Like dark, merciless waves, it came at me without end. When I woke, my chest and back were slick with sweat. Then I called Kato Ran’s number.







After six rings, a bright voice answered, “Hellooo?” I could feel my lower jaw trembling with tension.

“Excuse me—um, is this Kato Ran’s… number?”

“Yes.”

Her voice dropped just slightly as she answered. It was Ran. My heart lurched.

“Um, it’s Hana.”

“Hana?”

“Yes, um, I’m Ito Hana, and a long time ago, we—”

“Hana…” After a short silence, Ran said, “…that Hana-chan?”

“Yes, it’s Hana. Sorry to call so suddenly.” I shifted the phone to my other hand and pressed it tight to my ear. “I honestly never thought it would go through. Sorry, calling you out of nowhere like this.”

“…What’s going on? How’d you get this number?”

“It was still in my old phone.”

“Oh.”







I heard Ran let out a small sigh.

“I’m really sorry for startling you out of nowhere.”

“No, that’s fine, but… I’m just surprised. I mean, it was so long ago.”

“I know. I’m sorry. Actually, the reason I called is Kimiko-san.”

In the background behind Ran, I could hear children making a happy racket. There were women’s voices too. Then those sounds grew more distant, and I realized Ran had moved somewhere else.

“…Kimiko-san? You mean that Kimiko-san?”

“Yes.”

“What about her?”

“Yesterday I found out about Kimiko-san’s case.”

“What’s that supposed to mean?”

“I found it online.”

“What case?”

“Kimiko-san got arrested. I was shocked too. The trial’s already started, and I thought maybe—just maybe—it could involve us too, and I wanted to talk to you, Ran-chan.”

“Wait a second,” Ran cut in. “What are you talking about? I have no idea what you mean. Why was Kimiko-san arrested? What do you mean it could involve us too? Did Kimiko-san say something?”

“No, not that. Kimiko-san was arrested for confining a girl in her apartment and injuring her. I think… she was probably doing the same kind of thing she did back then, and that’s why she got caught. Maybe the past will become an issue too, and all kinds of things might come out. I really don’t know, but it scared me.”

I forced myself to ask.

“…Nobody’s contacted you, have they? The police, or anyone like that?”

“As if they would.” Ran laughed as though the idea were ridiculous, but there was the faintest hint of unease underneath.







“I’ve been scared ever since yesterday, and I keep thinking maybe I should go to the police and tell them—”

“Huh?” Ran sounded genuinely startled. “Tell them what?”

“About the past, or what I know about Kimiko-san.”

“Come on, quit joking.” Ran lowered her voice, but her tone was firm. Just then someone called out to her, and Ran answered brightly, “Okay!”

“Listen, I’ve got people over right now.”

“Right, of course. Sorry.”

“Hana-chan, where do you live now? Tokyo?”

“Yes.”

“It might be better if we meet and talk. With corona and all… if possible, maybe it’d be better not to do this over the phone.”

“Yes, I think it’d be better if we met too… Oh, right, Momoko—Momoko was there too, wasn’t she? I don’t know if her phone still works, but we should probably contact Momoko too, right? If she can come.”

“Momoko’s impossible,” Ran said shortly.

“Why?”

“I’ll tell you when we meet. Hana-chan, anyway, don’t go to the police or anything like that, okay? Absolutely not. Got it?”

“Okay.”

We set a time and place to meet and hung up.







2





Kato Ran, whom I was seeing again for the first time in twenty years, looked as though her whole air had changed a great deal.

She was still small-framed overall, as I remembered, but she seemed somehow a size bigger, and something about her proportions felt slightly off. If she hadn’t spotted me first and raised a hand, I don’t think I would have recognized her right away, even in a café with hardly anyone in it.







Ran was wearing a loose, natural-colored cotton tunic and a large mask that covered half her face. Her hair, dyed a light brown, was tied back, and at the hairline a brushstroke of gray caught the light. Around her eyes, done in solid brown shadow and heavy mascara, the old Ran still remained.

Her narrow forehead was unchanged, and I remembered how once, late at night, Ran and Momoko and I had measured the width of our foreheads and eyes and compared them. Come to think of it, Ran had always liked makeup, and laughing at how little I knew about that kind of thing, she would try all sorts of looks on me, saying she wanted to see how much her technique could change my face, and then the three of us would howl with laughter at the result. Remembering that as I looked at the Ran in front of me, I felt the reality of how much time had passed rise up inside me, and something deep in my chest gave a small throb.

And yet we didn’t exchange any of the bright greetings people offer when they meet an old friend again—no Long time no see, no How have you been? We just ordered our drinks. Since her mouth was hidden, I couldn’t tell what expression Ran was making, but for a while we sat in silence, in an atmosphere so still that nothing seemed to stir.







After two iced coffees had been set on the table, I tried saying, “I’m glad you recognized me right away,” and Ran answered shortly, “You were standing by the entrance looking around.” She stopped fiddling with her phone and took off her mask. The inside was thickly smeared with brick-red lipstick.

“Kimiko-san,” Ran said. “What happened? What kind of thing did she do? I tried searching, but I couldn’t really make sense of it.”

Ignoring my tension entirely, she spoke as if this were simply the continuation of a conversation we’d had the other day.

“Did it make the news? Was it bad?”

“Well, not exactly the news…” I brought up the bookmarked article on my phone and handed it to her. Ran stared at the screen with a serious face, scrolled a little with one fingertip, then fixed her eyes on it again.

“How did you even find this?”

After sliding the phone back across the table to me, Ran asked.

“I was reading some big article on Yahoo or somewhere about another case, just vaguely, and then below it, more cases like it kept coming up. Similar content. Old ones too, and links to tiny local-paper articles from places I’d never even heard of. There were loads of them. I kept following those, and eventually I landed on this one.”

“Shinjuku, huh… so not that place in Sangenjaya.” Ran knit her brows. “It said Kimiko-san was sixty… which means back then she was, what, around forty? About as old as we are now.”

“Yes.”







“When you think about it now, she was completely insane.”

Ran spat the words out.

“It’s unbelievable. Forty means our age now, right? So it’s like us gathering up a bunch of twenty-year-old girls and living like that. Completely unhinged.”

She gave a small shake of her head.

“But with this thing Kimiko-san did, why are you so freaked out, Hana-chan? It has nothing to do with us, does it? And anyway, the incident itself was last year, and the trial’s already started, right?”

I nodded.

“When we were living with her—sure, I guess there were things we did that were bad. But we were twenty or whatever, and that was ages ago. I really don’t see how that could suddenly become an issue now.”

“But if, say, those stacks of cards from back then were still left in Kimiko-san’s apartment… from the police’s point of view, wouldn’t they start asking what those were? And then if Kimiko-san talked, even if they couldn’t charge us, they might at least ask us questions. There’s probably some statute of limitations, but with the things we were doing… I don’t know, I have no idea which parts of it would count as what, or how serious any of it was.”

“So because of that you were actually thinking of going to the police and saying you were connected to her back then?” Ran widened her eyes, sounding exasperated. “You’re overthinking it.”

“Am I?”

“You are. Your imagination’s running wild in a really bad direction. In the first place, what we did wasn’t even that big a deal. That’s how I see it. It’s absolutely not the kind of thing you need to torture yourself over twenty years later. Kids now do way more vicious stuff.”

“Do they?”

“They do.” Ran paused, as if thinking it over. “And besides… we were made to do it.”

“But Kotomi-san died too, and we still don’t know what really happened, and we dragged her into it, or anyway, however you want to put it, that’s what happened—”

“Kotomi-san, who was that again? Oh.” Ran gave a small nod. “Right. The hostess from that Ginza club you used to go work at.”

“Yes.”

“No, no, no. I don’t think that had anything to do with us. That was completely their own situation… We said the same thing back then too, didn’t we? Didn’t we? Sure, it was shocking, but… what, Hana-chan, do you still think it was your fault or something?”

“No.” I shook my head. “Not that.”

“That’d be ridiculous. They were all crazy.” Ran lifted her shoulders a little. “So calm down, okay? Brighten up, change gears. Don’t look like that. There’s no point worrying over something so long ago nobody even remembers it. Sure, if Kimiko-san’s name suddenly pops up like this, it’s a shock. And with her confining some girl, with the situation being so similar. I get how you feel. But it’s fine. Really. If there were any chance something bad was going to happen, the police would’ve come already. I mean, that whole thing was just a cringe chapter. Youthful stupidity, and a cringe chapter.”

“A youthful mistake… and a cringe chapter.”

“Exactly. Besides, corona’s worse, right? What about you, Hana-chan? My kids are out of school and it’s hell. Corona’s awful. What’s life like for you?”

“I’m…” I let out a small breath through my nose. “Fine. I live alone.”







Ran stirred her iced coffee round and round with her straw and nodded as if to say, I see. We were in a café in front of Jiyugaoka Station. Ran apparently lived somewhere along the Toyoko Line, and she had named this place because it was convenient—straight through on one line, and she had another errand nearby. It was a chain coffee shop with glass walls all around. Ran didn’t ask where I lived exactly, or what kind of work I did, or where I had been or how I had lived since then.

“There’s hardly anyone here. Usually it’s packed. This self-restraint stuff is brutal.”

Looking out at the street, it was true that there were only a few pedestrians, and with the heavy cloud cover that had hung there since morning, the whole neighborhood seemed sunk in darkness.

“But time really does go around, doesn’t it? We’re forty now. I never would’ve imagined I’d see you again like this, Hana-chan. When we parted, I mean, we kind of scattered.”

“Ran-chan, you never changed your number, but did Kimiko-san ever contact you after that?”

“No,” Ran said. “I can’t really remember the details anymore, and I don’t want to. But back then we slipped out in the middle of all that chaos, didn’t we? And that was it. Though if anybody had a hard time after that, it was probably you. She doted on you the most… I mean, really, no wonder Kimiko-san got arrested. She was terrifying. Way too crazy. We were young then, still kids, so we had no idea, but she was completely dangerous.”







“Hey, you said something on the phone before,” I said. “About Momoko… you said Momoko was impossible. Did something happen?”

“Oh, Momoko.” Ran gave a little shake of her head. “Remember? Momoko had that wild younger sister. Drop-dead gorgeous, and with filthy teeth. She came to the house too, didn’t she? Anyway, I heard Momoko got into some kind of fight over money with her sister and the sister’s boyfriend, and in the end she disappeared. I don’t really know. Just rumors. After we left that place, I stayed in touch with Momoko for a little while, but then suddenly she was gone.”

I sighed.

“Man, everybody around us was crazy. We just didn’t know anything.”

Ran’s voice had suddenly grown louder, and it rang out with an exaggerated force, almost as if she were practicing lines. The spacious café was sparsely occupied, the scattered customers all sitting alone in masks, and soft bossa nova music drifted from speakers somewhere in the ceiling. From the washing area behind the counter came the clatter of dishes, mixed with the staff’s voices. Other than us, no customers were talking. An elderly man sitting a little way off opened his newspaper with a loud snap, glanced over, and our eyes met. I took a sip of water and checked with my fingertips the mask hanging at my chin.







“Anyway, whatever it was, it’s over. I keep saying it, but it was a long time ago. Just the past. There’s nothing for you to worry about… Hey, Hana-chan.”

Without blinking, Ran looked straight at me.

“Seriously, absolutely no police or anything like that, okay? It’s seriously pointless. It’ll one hundred percent just make things worse. Really, Hana-chan, I’m counting on you there. We’re fine, right? I came out here today and listened to you properly. So forget it now. Seriously.”

I nodded.

“We’re really okay, right?”

“Yes.”

After that we sat in silence for a long time. Ran sighed, rounded her shoulders, and stared at a fixed point on the table. There was fatigue in her expression. I had the feeling there was something else I should be saying to Ran now that we had met again, something important. And perhaps Ran was feeling the same thing. But I didn’t know what it was, or how it could come out of my mouth in the form of the right words.

“Well, I should probably—oh, right, Hana-chan.”

As if pulling herself together, Ran straightened her back and put on her mask.

“One more thing. Could you delete my number? I’ll delete your call from my phone too.”

I couldn’t say anything and stayed silent. Ran picked up the receipt and checked it, put down her share of the bill, swung her bag onto her shoulder, and stood.

“Okay, Hana-chan, I’m going. Be careful with corona.”







Even after Ran had left the café, I couldn’t make myself stand up right away. There had to be other things I ought to be thinking about, but my mind wasn’t working properly. I had no appetite, but I hadn’t eaten since morning, so I should have been hungry, and I found myself vaguely thinking things like maybe I ought to order something.

As I looked at the little photo menu on the table, I heard a strange sound. Outside, somewhere far away, there was a noise like something enormous being rolled slowly along—I couldn’t tell whether it sounded heavy or light, but something huge all the same. After a while I realized it was thunder. The instant I looked through the glass toward the street, rain came down all at once with a bursting crack. The drops were so large it felt as though you could see each one individually, and I saw several passersby run off, shielding their heads with bags or their hands. The rain intensified still further, hammering the awnings of the shops diagonally across from us and the asphalt, splashing high and raising a faint white mist.





Chapter Two  
Money Luck





1





The first time I met Kimiko-san, I was fifteen, and it was summer.

One morning, just after the last summer vacation of junior high had begun, I woke to find that instead of my mother, who should have been sleeping beside me, there was a woman I didn’t know.

I couldn’t see her face, and she was wearing my mother’s pajamas, but I knew at once that the woman sleeping soundly with her back to me, snoring away, was not my mother.







Propped on one elbow, I edged back a little, but then immediately decided it was nothing unusual and went back to sleep. My mother worked at a snack bar in the neighborhood, and it wasn’t the first time she had brought home one of the girls from the place or a friend and let her stay over like this.

When I woke again, the woman was gone, and on top of the futon folded neatly in three was my mother’s pajama set, carefully arranged. They were the same worn-out pajamas my mother had been wearing for years, but seeing them folded into the neat shape clothes have when they’re sold in stores felt strangely new, and I found myself staring at them for a while.

Clothes and underwear were usually hung to dry on hangers from the curtain rail by the window, then taken straight down and worn. There were always too many things in the room, and it was forever in a mess, so that one spot stood out in a soft round patch, like the blank place left when a faint black pencil smudge has been carefully erased from a notebook page.

The place where my mother and I lived was an old, tiny apartment house hidden from the main road, on the outskirts of a small town in Higashimurayama.







Between two detached houses facing the street ran an unimproved passage about three meters wide. Go down it, turn left at the back, and there was the shared entrance. The name Seifuso was still barely legible on the sign, so weathered and blackened it looked sinister, and the entrance itself resembled the mouth of some ominous cave. The corridor, lit only by a few low-wattage bulbs hanging there, was dark even at midday on the brightest days.

It was a two-story wooden building with four identical units upstairs and four downstairs, but besides us the only occupant was the middle-aged landlady, who lived in the back on the second floor. Open the flimsy wooden sliding door—a child could probably have kicked it in—and there was a tiny entryway; step up from there and you were in a kitchen the size of three tatami mats, and beyond that, two four-and-a-half-mat rooms ran back in a row. We used the shared toilet at the back of the ground floor as if it were ours alone. The building was hemmed in on all sides, so even if you opened the window all you could see was the concrete wall next door, and hardly any light came in.

We rented the two ground-floor rooms. They were the rooms immediately to the left and right of the entrance to Seifuso, and my mother and I mainly used the room on the right.







The room on the left was supposed to be my father’s place, but apart from a television, a futon, and a few clothes hanging in the closet, it had no lived-in feel at all. In the first place, my father hardly ever stayed at home.

At the time I didn’t really understand what kind of work he did. He was solidly built and always deeply sunburned, and I think he probably did day labor of one sort or another—construction work, truck driving, jobs that sometimes took him away for long stretches. (Once, on my way home from elementary school, I heard my name called from a big truck, and it was my father.) For a while there was a period when he brought home workmates dressed the same way, and they would cook hot pot or yakiniku, drink, and make a night of it, but that ended quickly.

Whenever I saw my father, as if he had suddenly remembered me, he was always in a good mood. He would give me things like badminton gear or stuffed dolls that looked as though he had won them from a claw machine, or he would come home in the middle of the night without warning and wake me up just to feed me sushi, saying it was good.

I didn’t dislike him, exactly, but because I had spent so little time with him and never knew what to talk about, I always got strangely tense when he came home. I would feel awkward and on edge, wishing he would leave soon, and then my mood would darken again at the thought that he might be able to tell I felt that way. I knew my father wasn’t what people would call an ordinary father, but even so I vaguely felt that maybe it was pitiable for a man to have his own child think of him like this, and that in turn brought on another kind of tension and self-loathing.







By the time I reached the upper grades of elementary school, my father had stopped coming home for good, and after that I never saw him again. I don’t know where he is now or what became of him. Later, I came to understand vaguely that my father had another home—or rather, other people he lived with.

Living alone with my mother was easy, if you wanted to call it that, but only because it was the same as having nothing at all. My mother liked fun, and liked drinking even though she wasn’t all that strong, and she had lots of friends, and a personality that let herself be swept along. One of her proudest stories was that after graduating from the local commercial high school and going to work full-time at a stockings factory, she had once modeled her legs in front of everyone when important people from headquarters came to inspect the place. But she stayed there only a few years. After that she drifted from one local snack bar to another with friends, and somewhere along the way I was born.

Compared with the mothers of my friends at school, my own mother looked remarkably young, and flashy, and even to me, her daughter, she didn’t feel much like what people meant by an ordinary mother. She was small, with a somewhat childish face, bright and optimistic and always laughing, but when she drank she cried almost without fail. There was never any especially clear cause or reason for it; maybe she was one of those crying drunks. For my mother, drinking and getting excited and noisy and crying all seemed almost like the same thing.







She didn’t seem attached to my father either. I never heard her complain about him or say a bad word against him, and even after he stopped coming home she didn’t seem particularly interested. Once in a while she would talk, half jokingly, about what a terrible personality her own estranged mother had had, but only when she had been drinking, gotten carried away, and turned sentimental.

Basically, when she was with me, my mother preferred not to be with just me but with somebody else there too. She often spent time aimlessly with hostesses from the place where she worked, or somebody a customer had introduced her to, or old local friends, and whenever I was alone with her I always felt just a little tense. While I was at school, she slept; she got up in the afternoon, did her makeup in the evening, went off to work, and came home late at night. Ours was basically a life of passing one another.







There was only one child in the neighborhood who came from an environment like mine, and I became close to her. Ever since elementary school, neither of us had such a thing as a curfew, and the parents of children from homes where there were places they weren’t allowed to go and fixed dinnertimes seemed not to like their kids spending too much time with us. At the very least, they were forbidden to come over to our house. There was another friend I liked fairly well who treated me normally at school but after classes always went off somewhere with a different group. Once I asked her why. “At your place,” she told me, sounding slightly awkward, “weird grown-ups are always coming and going, and it’s not a proper house, so they say I’m not supposed to go.” And there was nothing I could do about that, because it wasn’t some malicious rumor or lie. It was simply true.

By the time I got to junior high, other girls’ faces and attitudes—and lots of other things besides—began to stand out more sharply. I felt as if I could tell at a glance which girls came from normal homes and which didn’t, as though they were wearing hats of different colors. In a normal home, you do not wake in the morning to find a strange woman sleeping beside you. Nor, probably, does your mood brighten simply because a futon or a pair of pajamas has been neatly folded, or do you feel like staring at such a thing for a long time.







I folded my own futon the same way, into thirds, and laid it beside the one that had already been folded. There was no one in the room and no one in the kitchen either, so I slipped on sandals, stepped across the corridor, and opened the door to the room opposite. The television was blaring, and when I went in I saw the back of a woman lying there watching a daytime variety show. The fan we had used forever slowly turned its head from right to left, every so often giving a loud creak.

When the woman noticed me, she didn’t change position, only turned her face toward me and smiled. The way she smiled was so natural that for a second I felt she might be someone I had met before. But she was a stranger—she may have slept beside me in the same room last night, but at least as far as this went, it was the first time I had seen her face. She turned back to the screen and laughed happily along with the cheers coming from the television, her shoulders shaking slightly. I stood there on the boundary between kitchen and room, looking at the TV, the fan, and the woman.

“Wanna eat something?”

When the program ended and the commercials came on, the woman stretched and said it.

“You’re hungry too, right?”

We stood in the tiny kitchen and made packet ramen.

The woman was about a size bigger than me, and her arms and legs seemed much longer. Her long black hair, wavy and tied back in one bundle, sagged at the nape of her neck, the ends scattered across a large white T-shirt with English printed on it. I stood a little apart from her and watched the water for the two of us boil in the aluminum pot.







The woman deftly tore open the packets, loosened the noodles in the hot water, added the powdered soup, gave it a quick stir, and divided the ramen between the bowls I had set out. She tried to pick up a wonton stuck to the rim of the pot with the tips of her chopsticks, but it wouldn’t come free. “Oh well, let’s take it in there too,” she said brightly, nodding toward the room. I pulled out the bare kotatsu frame that had been propped against the wall, and we sat facing each other, slurping our ramen.

“It’s summer vacation, right? Not going anywhere?”

I made a vague sound that was neither really an answer nor not an answer. Up until a moment ago it hadn’t felt especially hot, but suddenly I felt sweat beginning to gather. I reached out, switched on the air conditioner, and hit the button for high.

“Not going anywhere with Ai-san?”

Ai was my mother’s name.

“Not really.”

“Huh. What’s your name? I’m Kimiko.”

“Hana.”

“Hana-chan, huh. Who named you? Ai-san?”

“I’m not really sure,” I said quietly.

“I see.”

“Um… you’re my mother’s friend, right?”

“Right.”

There the conversation stopped, and we ate the rest of the ramen in silence. A quiz show was playing on TV, and the old air conditioner’s motor roared. After a while, on top of that, I could hear the distant sound of an ambulance outside. It seemed to come closer for a moment, then quickly faded away again.

“Is your Hana written in kanji?”

“With the ordinary flower.”

“My Kimiko is yellow, then beautiful, then child. Kimiko.”







Again I gave a vague nod and drank some of the broth. When I lifted my face a little over the rim of the bowl, my eyes met Kimiko-san’s. She had finished before me and was leaning on one elbow at the kotatsu, and I looked away without meaning to. After that, from behind the edge of the bowl, I kept stealing glances at her face. Kimiko-san had a face unlike any woman I had ever seen before.

Of course every person’s face is different, so that should have gone without saying, but even so there was something about Kimiko-san’s face—a kind of presence—that I couldn’t have explained in any of the words I had at my disposal then. It was the face she had just after waking, and she couldn’t possibly have had any makeup on, yet her eyebrows were thick and full, the lashes under her sharp double lids were dark and long, and the space between her brows and her eyes was narrow. Her nose rose strongly from the bridge, and the hair running from her temples into her sideburns, mixed with sweat, curled into tiny frizzed spirals.

Kimiko-san’s face felt strong, more than pretty or beautiful.

It reminded me of the young, dignified pharaoh who was the hero of a girls’ manga set in both ancient Egypt and the present day, a manga that had been wildly popular when I was in elementary school and that I had been obsessed with too. As I watched Kimiko-san crack her neck and shift her posture while looking at the TV, I had the odd feeling that the pharaoh’s lines were floating somewhere diagonally above her head, and that I could see the outlines of comic panels framing her, and the thought made me a little happy.

“I suddenly feel like drinking cider. Want to go buy some?”

After carrying the bowls to the sink and coming back into the room, Kimiko-san invited me out. We went outside just as we had slept and woken.

“So even though Ai-san’s place has two rooms, you don’t sleep separately.”

“The room where we just had ramen is what we call the TV room, and the other one is the futon room, and somehow it just sort of stayed that way.”

“So like a living room.”

“Not that fancy,” I said, looking down.

“Still better than sleeping alone.”

The summer sunlight was sharp enough to make me squint, and every time I breathed I could feel the heat seeping into my skin.

The apartment house where I lived stood right behind the junior high school I attended.

If you walked about two hundred meters along the long gray wall surrounding the school buildings and turned left at the corner, you came to the main gate, and the convenience store stood directly across the road from it. We weren’t allowed to shop there in uniform, but hardly any students actually obeyed that rule. Green branches from trees planted on the school grounds thrust vividly out over the narrow sidewalk, casting thick blue shadows on the asphalt.

As we neared the main gate, I saw a small group hanging around, making noise.

Even though it was summer vacation, students still came and went for club activities, but the ones there weren’t those students. They were the ones people called delinquents.
