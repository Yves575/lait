He had barely had time to clean himself up when a man grabbed him by the shoulder and dragged him off by force.

Comforting his classmate made Valentyn feel stronger still. He opened his notebook and wrote that everything would be fine, that there was no reason to worry, and that they would surely be home before nightfall, because the bus was about to give up the ghost. To show off, though he wasn’t sure how to spell the word, he wrote that a connecting rod was about to go. Barely concealing his satisfaction when the driver let loose a stream of curses after thick smoke poured out from under the hood, he bent over a blank page and wrote in big letters: They’re fucked, we’ll be home for dinner. But on that point, Valentyn was wrong.

At daybreak, Veronika and Lilya leave their house and head for the town hall, where the Russian command has installed itself. Veronika has known famous rages, but never one like the fury that drives her now as she walks. Invading a country, bombing cities, decimating entire populations to serve the megalomania of a declining dictator was not enough for them? Now they had to kidnap children as well? Was that what these conquerors had been reduced to, these men commanded by incompetent generals, mercenaries forming the battalions of an army without honor?

The soldier on guard in front of the town hall has little courage left. He doesn’t know what the hell he’s doing in this backwater so far from home. A young man of barely twenty-five, glad to be among those who took a small town without having had to fight any serious battles, gladder still to be alive. He points out the office of his superior in charge of the occupied population. The future mayor of the town, when Ukraine is liberated, he explains.

“Excellent news, then you’ll be going home soon,” Veronika retorts coldly, drawing her daughter along with her.

They advance down the corridor with an almost military step, their heels ringing so loudly that those they pass do not dare ask where they are going. Lilya spots the commander’s office, Veronika draws a deep breath, and opens the door.

“Where is my son?” she asks as she enters the room.

The officer, who had been dozing with his head slumped on a desk, starts and stares at the two women.

“Who is your son?” he asks with a yawn.

“Valentyn Khodova!”

The man straightens up, turns with a sigh, and takes from a shelf the binder where the names of fighters captured or killed at the front are recorded.

“Don’t pretend to look for his name. He is nine years old, one of the two children you abducted yesterday at noon from their school. Are you going to pretend you know nothing about it?”

His expression changes. It was he who, a week earlier, had received instructions to carry out this operation, whose usefulness had at first seemed questionable to him. As if he didn’t have enough problems already. But the call had come straight from Moscow, more than that, from the Kremlin. Few officers of his rank were granted such an honor. The call had lasted only a few minutes, but he would never forget the soft, benevolent voice of the commissioner for children’s rights, never once flaunting the power her proximity to the president gave her. Instead of using the threatening tone his superiors had accustomed him to, she had carefully explained how important the task he had to accomplish was. What cause could be nobler than protecting orphans and minors from the dangers to which the Ukrainian rebellion exposed them? People who accuse Russia of every evil, though incapable of caring what becomes of their offspring. Saving orphans was a priority, she had repeated before hanging up.

Orphan—a detail he may perhaps have failed to specify to the unit he sent out. But then again, why would he have, since he had been told the establishment in question was an orphanage?

And yet here is a mother claiming her child; perhaps his intelligence sources are not as reliable as that. He will find the person responsible for this blunder. Then again, it seems to him that the commissioner for children’s rights also mentioned children living in precarious circumstances. Enough to reassure him as to the perfect legitimacy of his mission, since the whole town lives in precarious circumstances.

“This dilapidated building, you call that a school?” he says, snapping his ledger shut.

“You bombed us, we live most of the time without water or electricity. Are you going to reproach us for our cracked walls and shattered windows?”

“Be that as it may, children need care your institutions are incapable of providing, even less so in this region. We are showing infinite kindness and spending precious money to help them. You ought to be grateful and thank us; the government of Russia has a duty to protect minors, whatever their nationality.”

Veronika is about to ask him how many children have died under the bombs his government has dropped on hundreds of schools, maternity wards, hospitals, or public gardens, how many others are now orphans, but reason compels her to keep silent.

“My son has no need of care, and certainly not yours!” she replies in an icy voice. “The civilians your men shoot at point-blank range, on the other hand, have a great need of mine.”

“Well then, go back to your work,” the officer retorts.

“Where is my son?” Veronika asks again, ready to commit murder.

The man walks to the window and looks out onto the street.

“On his way to a reception center. Our specialists will question him and, after assessing his condition, decide what is best for him.”

“My son will not answer your questions. He cannot speak.”

“And you said he needed no care? What kind of parent are you?”

Lilya sees the fire burning in her mother’s eyes. She takes her hand to remind her that she is there beside her and takes the initiative of addressing the officer.

“We don’t want to cause you trouble, we just want my brother back, to know that he’s all right and where you’ve taken him. Please.”

The restraint of the teenager surprises the officer. He tells himself she will be easier to mollify; perhaps she will even help him persuade her mother to leave him in peace.

“Your brother is fine and we did not abduct him. We merely put him out of harm’s way, to offer him the life of a child to which he is entitled, healthy food that can no longer be found here and that he needs in order to grow, supervision that will allow him to pursue his studies properly. Even during a special operation like the one we are conducting at the moment, don’t you think?”

“A special operation?” Veronika erupts. “It’s a war you’re waging against us!”

“That lying term is punishable by eight years in prison! We are at war with no one, we are liberating Ukraine!” the officer protests, outraged.

“And in your country, is mentioning rain when it rains also punishable by prison? A storm, do you call that drizzle, mist?”

“When calm has returned and his safety is assured, we will send him back to you, I promise,” he says in a voice that makes it plain his patience is at an end.

Veronika’s audacity has stripped him of his swagger, to the point that he feels shabby; his sense of superiority is slipping through his fingers like the sand on the beaches of Odessa he used to play with as a child. His own mother must be about ten years older than this woman, and if she had found herself facing him in such circumstances, she would have smacked him and kicked him out of the room. This time he will not let himself be humiliated; he is going to show who he is.

“One more word and I’ll have you arrested!” he says, adopting a grave tone. “Your daughter needs you, and if you were no longer able to look after her, I would be obliged to place her in safety as well.”

“Where is this center?” Lilya asks.

“I don’t know yet. We have many of them; there are so many children in precarious circumstances in your country. Come back and see me in three days. I’ll know more then and I’ll tell you where your son is, perhaps I’ll even be able to give you news of him—provided that in the meantime you don’t make trouble.”

Terrified at the thought of being detained in turn, and hoping the officer will keep his promise, Lilya grabs her mother by the arm and begs her to obey. They will get nothing more today, except make their situation worse.
