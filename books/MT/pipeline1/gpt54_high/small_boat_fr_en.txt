I



“I didn’t ask you to set off,” I had said.

You’re the one who wanted it, and if you didn’t want to get wet, pal, you shouldn’t have climbed aboard. I didn’t push you into the water, and I’m not the one who came and got you from your village or your field, from your crumbling suburb, to drag you out of there and put you in your damned boat that’s taking on water, and now you’re floundering around and I’m perfectly willing to believe you’re scared, and you call me for help as if it were my fault, you ask me to save you and then you get impatient. You’re counting on me. But I never asked anything of you. So let me do my job and wait it out.

And I must have been thinking all that so hard that I said it out loud, at least the first sentence, if the recordings were to be believed—and there was no reason not to believe them, I’m willing to admit.

And while I was at it, I might as well have added sentences like “I’m not the Good Lord either,” because I must have been thinking that too, and I could have said it. I could have said, “There are guys like you everywhere tonight, forty Titanics supposedly sinking in the Channel at the same time, and I can’t deal with everyone all at once. So you’re going to have to wait, pal, or else tell the others, who are sinking too, to calm down and hang up so I can deal with you and only you. You can always ring all the others yourself, since you’ve got a mobile, all the others who every evening climb into rotten dinghies, with no compass, no signal lights, thirty of them on a floor that can barely take the weight of five, no instruments, no bearings, no knowledge of the sea, with women and children.”

But in the end what I’d said to Julien, beside me, was: “These people are unbelievable, they throw themselves into the water and then they practically start yelling at you because you’re not chucking the lifebuoy fast enough, the nerve of them.” He’d smiled, before burying himself in his book again. “More like deflated, in this case,” he’d said. And at three in the morning, that was the kind of joke that made you laugh.

That joke wasn’t recorded; I don’t think it would have looked good, and Julien can sleep easy: he’s not the one who’s going to be taken for a monster for making a joke about inflatable dinghies deflating. Anyway, on the station recordings, it’s only me you hear, or almost. Just my luck.

After that I’d gone back to my monitors, my computer, my microphone, probably telling myself that, after all, they ought to be pleased, that lot: since they wanted to go to England, there they are, in British waters, and now on a British vessel, with British survival blankets, wrapped up like sweets in gold foil, and they’ll be able to carry on their conversation in English as long as they like.

But in the end the currents had brought their bodies back into French waters.



And now they were floating on the investigator’s desk, in the offices of the maritime gendarmerie. There were twenty-seven of them, counted properly, a little girl among them, scattered among the pens, notepads, files, floating around the gendarmerie captain’s computer, and among them too the body of the one who had called me fourteen times that night and whom of course you couldn’t hear any more now. The sea was calm on the desk, no wind and no swell, and beside the bodies there were only neatly ordered papers on the surface.



While she was playing me the recordings, the gendarme kept alternately staring at me and looking at I don’t know what out of the window, from which you couldn’t see the sea, and that was just as well, because I saw the sea all the time from my signal station and, all things considered, I preferred to see, as I did today, a strip of street with a building site opposite, workers, most of them African but at least alive and not wet and frozen with cold, and no women and no children, so I was quite happy to look at that while on the recording you could hear “Please, please” and I could hear myself saying, “Calm down, rescue is on the way.”

The gendarme had her hair pulled back and tied in a severe ponytail, exactly like mine, I remember thinking, and her back very straight like mine, military in a way, shoulders like coat hooks, as Éric used to say, and perhaps even the same face as me, for all I knew, only ten years older, she in her blue gendarmerie sweater and me of course in plain clothes, wearing a ridiculous sweatshirt and a pair of trainers as if I’d just come back from my morning jog, so that I looked like a kid despite my strict ponytail, like a sullen little girl, face locked shut with two turns of the key, summoned to the headteacher’s office, and no doubt it was that ridiculous and vaguely humiliating resemblance, by virtue of which I had a caricature of myself sitting opposite me or the pitilessly dismal portrait of what I must really look like, no doubt it was that, that resemblance, which made me dislike her at once, even find her antipathetic, though in any case this was hardly a situation in which one was supposed to feel sympathy.



As she showed me into her office, she’d said, “Thank you for coming of your own accord. Up to now your director had refused to give us your details.” Then she’d added, “Your colleague, it seems, the one who was on duty with you that night, didn’t have the, the, the…” but she’d hesitated over the choice of the word that should come next; it might have been scruple, or courage, or why not moral conscience or sense of duty, but she couldn’t find the right word, so she corrected herself, saying, “Hasn’t taken this step, for the moment.”



And luckily she hadn’t asked me why I, for my part, had taken this step, as she finally called it, and why I had come like this of my own accord, before, for all I knew, the judge and the gendarmes came knocking at my door anyway and hauled me off in front of my little girl and planted me in the dock on a charge of failure to assist a person in danger or whatever else; luckily, in short, she hadn’t asked me why, that morning, after dropping Léa at my parents’, I’d got in the car and driven to the maritime gendarmerie in Cherbourg, four hours on the road from Boulogne, a tasteless coffee on the motorway whose surface seemed to have little dinghies bobbing on it but they were biscuit crumbs, and around me people silently pointing at me behind my back, a call to my parents to make sure everything was all right with the little one, anxious about not going to school, another call too to the gendarmerie to say, “I’m coming, I’m on the road,” and me quite astonished not to hear English at the other end, not to hear “Please, please” and shouting in the background, but no, simply “We’re waiting for you,” exactly what sensible shipwrecked people should have said, waiting patiently since there wasn’t much else to do apart from pray and look around to see whether there might be a boat nearby, “We’re waiting for you,” they would have said calmly, instead of piling up useless pleas, instead of failing to understand the difference between being in French waters and being in British waters, instead of calling fourteen times in two hours to say they were sinking, instead of getting on my nerves by repeating it as if I were the one who didn’t understand, but I understood perfectly well, I did, that there was water at your feet, but it was English water, not French water, even if it was just as cold, I’m willing to believe that, so look that way instead if you still know which way to look, where north is, where south is,

luckily, then, she hadn’t asked me why I had finally made up my mind, because I wouldn’t have known what to answer, in any case I don’t think I’d have used words like scruple or sense of duty, and certainly not courage, perhaps rather words like fatigue or nausea,

but more surely anger,

that is to say shame.



There was also the fact that I wanted to hear myself. That is, to hear myself rather than read myself, rather than read my words in the newspapers, hear my own voice rather than the voices of journalists on television repeating what I’d said next to expressions like Migrant Drama, Migrant Tragedy, as if every night at eight o’clock we were at the theatre putting on the same play again,

and indeed it was the same play, and me in the front row, you could say I never missed a single performance, every evening I came to watch the Migrant Drama, I had a free seat and they even paid me for it, there were those in the boxes, those in the balcony, but for me they’d installed a signal station in the front row so I could see properly, so I had the best seat in the house and a front balcony over the sea at the same time, and even if sometimes it went under a different title, sometimes Migrant Drama, sometimes Migrant Tragedy, Drama in the Channel after Drama in the Mediterranean, it was always the same play of course, and always the same character too, and at a certain point the character picks up a telephone to address the audience, it’s interactive, he calls out, it begins with “Please please,” but it isn’t really the audience he’s addressing, while in the meantime the audience gives little dismayed ohs and ahs and sometimes sighs of indignation, it’s me, me alone, he’s speaking to, and then it’s up to me to answer and answer properly otherwise the audience isn’t happy and then the sighs of indignation are for me, and the next day the theatre critics come down on me, because the way the show turns out depends on my answers and if I handle it well the Migrant Drama is a little less of a Migrant Drama, not to mention that at the same time I also have to deal with what is happening on other stages, where they’re putting on the drama of the drifting pleasure-boater or the tragedy of the capsized trawler.



So, to hear myself, to hear my voice, in that office of the maritime gendarmerie, to be really sure of what I’d said, rather than hear myself ventriloquised by some dolled-up journalist saying I’d said this and that, and among the things I’d said things that were horrible, scandalous, or simply appalling, before going on to say that I had done or not done things I should or should not have done.

But in the recordings it was mostly him you heard, I don’t know what to call him: the migrant, the man on the phone, the man who’s drowning, but his voice, I knew that voice by heart, and what it said too, because in reality it’s always the same one who calls me every night, the same voice and the same pleas, because however many times we pull that idiot out of the water, back he goes, once, ten times, a hundred times: one night we haul him out in an emergency, safe and sound, he can say thank you, and the following night it’s him calling again, because he’s back in there as if he hadn’t learned his lesson and saying “Please please,” every day we put him back on dry land, every night he starts all over again, and the feeling then is that this is how it’s going to be until Judgment Day: the same bloke, always, going back into the water and starting to sink in the middle of the Channel and waiting for someone to come and get him so he can start again the following night, and after a while it gets wearisome.



But my own voice too, all the same, although not quite my voice either, not because recordings inevitably distort timbres, but because it was my professional voice, the one that dryly handles disaster every night with sentences like “Calm down” and “Send me your location on WhatsApp” or “Rescue is on the way.” So what you heard, essentially, were sentences like that, at 2.05, at 2.36, at 3.12, at 4.22 and up to 4.32, after which there was nothing more to hear, and among those sentences there were others that apparently I ought not to have uttered, like the one that went, then, “I didn’t ask you to set off,” and the investigator asked me how that sentence was supposed to be understood.



So I told the investigator that that sentence, since apparently it was a problem, although I still didn’t know whether they were going to throw me in a cell for having stated an obvious truth, namely that it wasn’t me, all the same, who had asked them to set off, if that was one of the faults I was being blamed for but what fault exactly, that sentence, in any case, ought not in my opinion to be given excessive importance, because after all I could have said it to any irresponsible pleasure-boater, to the cretin who goes out to sea without paying any attention to the weather conditions or who is incapable of handling his stupid little sailboat properly, and ends up dead in the water like a complete chump and panics, and then the whole French Navy has to be mobilised to stop him going under. To him too, if he comes and tells me I’m not moving fast enough as if I were the one responsible for what’s happening to him, I’d say in just the same way that I’m not the one who asked him to set off, after all.



But she looked at me as if she didn’t believe me and I had my doubts too. But why?

Then she seized the chance, all too delighted to ask me with feigned innocence, “You don’t see the difference between an irresponsible pleasure-boater and a migrant dinghy?” The question was probably not especially well-meant, but in the end it was easy enough to answer, because my job consisted precisely in not making that distinction, which is what I said then, and I ought to be congratulated for not making it, that distinction: we come to everyone’s aid, we don’t make distinctions. Was I supposed to be more eager for one than for the other?

But of course that wasn’t what she meant, because I seemed to think it was the same thing, some yokel who takes to the sea in his leisure boat and people fleeing misery and war, as if they’d all set off in the same boat for a fishing trip. The distinction to be made was not between one drowning man and another drowning man, but between the reasons, the causes that had put him in such a situation. The distinction to be made is why people leave, and that explains why people set out thirty to a nutshell and not alone on a First 24. So the problem, then, would indeed be that I didn’t make the distinction, that I didn’t seem to understand that the situations were different, that people don’t leave in the same way and above all not for the same reasons and therefore not under the same conditions either.



And yet the insinuating question implied that, on the contrary, I was making a distinction all right, only not in the right way, namely that in the end I’d have been quicker to save some parvenu wanting to show off on his floating palace on a night of high winds than to mobilise rescue for a handful of Iraqi Kurds who kept insisting, night after night, on paddling about in the Channel. In other words I wasn’t making the distinction when I should have been, and when I shouldn’t have been making one I was, and all that could only be explained by one and the same reason, and since sooner or later she was going to ask me whether I had something against migrants, or whether I thought a migrant’s life was worth less than that of a pleasure-boater or a fishing skipper, then, to head off that sort of question, I said that I had no opinions about migrants, no more opinions about migrants than about migration policy or the right to asylum, about North-South relations, about the problems, the solutions, about the misery of the world, about injustice, because having opinions about migrants was not what was asked of me.



No, I don’t have opinions about migrants, I think I have even fewer than other people, and anyway I’m asked not to have any at all and that suits me very well. If anyone has opinions about migrants, it would be my ex, for example, Léa’s father, he has them, and besides they’re pretty much the same as the baker on the corner and the cashiers in the supermarket next door, the ones I chat to now and then when I’m off for a week, pretty much the same, in fact, as half of Calais, Boulogne or Dunkirk. And I heard Éric’s opinions about migrants all the more because I did that job, at the CROSS, because he not only couldn’t see why we at the CROSS went to so much trouble to help those parasites, but why we didn’t just chuck them straight into the drink the moment we saw one, with a good whack from the oar as well if possible just to make quite sure they didn’t come back up.



It had started with all the Calais business and the Jungle, because Éric was from there, from Calais, and in the end he simply couldn’t stand the idea that I did that job, helping trawlers or cargo ships yes, migrants no, and every time I got home I’d get it, and how many did you haul out of the drink this time, and how much is all this bullshit costing us, and why don’t you shove them into English waters and let them sort themselves out since they’re so desperate to go there, and why, once you’ve fished them out, don’t you send them straight back to Africa, and don’t you think we’ve got anything better to do than fuss over their little aches and pains, let them fend for themselves or stay home, etc. I’d heard all that a thousand times, until, for reasons that had nothing to do with any of this, I asked him to leave, once and for all, but I wasn’t there to tell the story of my life with Léa’s father and the way we had ended up separating.
