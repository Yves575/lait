I





He has set down his weapon and, with difficulty, pulls off his boots, whose smell (excrement, stale sweat) adds still more to his exhaustion. The fingers working at the frayed laces are dry twigs, lightly singed here and there; his nails are the same color as the boots, and later he will have to scrape them with the tip of the knife to get the filth out, mud, dried blood, but later, he has no strength for it now; two toes, flesh and earth, stick out of the sock, great smeared worms crawling from a dark trunk, knotted at the ankle.

All at once he wonders, as he does every morning, every evening, why these boots stink of shit, it is inexplicable,

you can rinse them in every puddle you come across, scrub them against the rasping clumps of grass, nothing helps,

and yet there are not that many dogs or wild beasts, not that many, up here among these rocky heights dusted with holm oaks, pines, and thornbushes where the rain leaves a thin pale mud and a smell of flint, no shit, and it would be easy for him to believe that the whole country reeks, from the sea, the hills of orange trees and then of olive trees all the way to the innermost depths of the mountains, of these mountains, even himself, his own smell, not the smell of the shoes, but he cannot bring himself to it and flings the boots against the lip of the ravine that hides him from the path, a little higher up the slope.



He lies on his back on the gravel, sighs, the sky is violet, the last light of sunset illuminates the racing clouds from below, a canvas, a screen for fireworks. Spring is almost here and with it come the rains, often torrential, that turn the mountains into cans riddled with bullets, vomiting from the slightest hollow a powerful spring, when the air smells of thyme and fruit-tree blossom, white flakes strewn between the low walls by the violence of the downpour. It would be just the devil if it started raining now. At the same time it would wash the boots. The boots, the fatigues, the socks, both pairs he owns equally stiff, cardboard-hard, tattered. Betrayal begins with the body,

how long since you washed?

Four days walking near the ridgelines to avoid the villages,

the last water you splashed over yourself smelled of petrol and left your skin greasy,

you are far from purity, alone under the sky eyeing the comets.

Hunger forces him to sit up and swallow, without pleasure, three army biscuits, the last ones, brown hard slabs, no doubt a mixture of sawdust and old mare’s glue; for a moment he curses the war and the soldiers,

you are still one of them, you still carry weapons, ammunition, and memories of war,

you could hide the rifle and the cartridges in a corner and become a beggar, leave the knife too, beggars do not carry daggers,

leave the shit-smelling boots and go barefoot,

leave the jacket the color of misery and go bare-chested,

when the meal is over he drinks the dregs of his canteen and makes a game of pissing as far as he can toward the valley.



He lies down again, this time right against the rock face, the bottom of the pack under his head; he is invisible in the shadow, never mind the little creatures (red spiders, tiny scorpions, centipedes with teeth sharp as remorse) that will scamper across his chest, slide over his nearly shaven skull, stroll across his beard as rough as a bramble patch. The rifle against him, the butt under his shoulder, the barrel toward his feet. Wrapped in the piece of tarred canvas that serves as both blanket and roof.

The mountain rustles; a little wind rounds the peaks, drops into the hollow and trembles among the shrubs; the cries of the stars are glacial. There are no clouds left, it will not rain tonight.

Angel, my holy guardian, protector of my soul and my body, forgive me all the sins committed this day and deliver me from the works of the enemy, despite the warmth of prayer the night remains a beast fed on anguish, a beast with breath of blood, of cities whose ruins are haunted by mothers brandishing the mutilated corpses of their children before disheveled hyenas who will torture them, then leave them naked, defiled, their nipples torn off with teeth before the eyes of their brothers, raped in turn with clubs, terror spread over the country, plague, hatred, and the night, this night that always wraps itself around you to drive you toward cowardice and betrayal. Toward flight and desertion. How much longer will he have to walk? The border is only a few days from here, beyond the mountains that will soon become red-earth hills planted with olive trees. It will be hard to hide. Many villages, towns, peasants, soldiers,

you know these parts,

you are at home here,

no one will help a deserter,

tomorrow you will reach the house in the mountain,

the hut, the hovel, you will take refuge there for a while,

the hut will protect you with its childhood,

there memories will caress you,

sometimes sleep comes by surprise like a sniper’s bullet.





II





More than twenty years ago, on September 11, 2001, near Potsdam on the Havel, aboard that cruise vessel, a little river steamer grandly christened Beethoven, summer seemed to falter.

The willows were still green, the days still warm, but an icy mist rose from the river before dawn and immense clouds slid over us from the distant Baltic Sea.

Our floating hotel had left Köpenick, east of Berlin, very early on the morning of Monday the 10th. Maja was still alert, sprightly. She would go up to the top deck for a walk, a promenade between the showers, the deck chairs, and the deck games. The green domes and golden spire of Berlin Cathedral captivated her from afar as we passed. She imagined, she said, all those little golden angels leaving their prison of stone and flying off in a cloud of acanthus leaves blown by the sun.

At times the water of the Spree was a dark, matte blue, at others a glowing green. In the preceding weeks all Germany had been shaken by storms whose convulsions had swollen even the Havel and the Spree, usually rather low at that late point in summer.

We sailed through the eddies.



I remember the confluence of the Spree, the wooded islets, the salty light dusting the tall black poplars, and the muddy flow of the canal that the ship’s wake mingled with the river’s waxy waters.

Maja and I sat side by side, each in a canvas chair, in the sun on deck, at the back, at the stern, as one ought to say, and we watched everything flee: the landscape broadened as though the ship’s prow were opening up the green substance of the foliage.



We were celebrating, a few months late, the tenth anniversary of the refounding of the Institute by Paul while also paying tribute to the founder himself. Or, more precisely, we were celebrating the tenth anniversary of the “unification” of the Institute, in the spring of 1991, and the fortieth anniversary of its creation in 1961. But first and foremost it was a celebration of Paul’s work. I think no one was missing—among the old guard, those from the East, all of them were there; the new members, the colleagues from Berlin and elsewhere, almost all had come. A few, including Linden Pawley, Robert Kant, and several French researchers, had even come from abroad. This floating conference was called the Paul Heudeber Days; there were to be two sessions a day, on number theory and algebraic topology, and a session on the history of mathematics in which I was to take part.

The only absentee was Paul himself.

Maja had just celebrated her eighty-third birthday.

Maja drank liters of tea.

Maja was cheerful and sad and silent and talkative.

We all knew she had no business being there, aboard the Beethoven for a mathematics colloquium; we all knew she was indispensable.

Let us strip away the possessive: naked love.

It has grown in absence and in the night: the lack of you is a source. A body, a ring—you are the seal on all things, unique. Your distance brings infinity nearer. You alone allow me to hide from time, from evil, from the currents of melancholy. I wonder what has become of my youth when I hear it cry out.

I stop my ears with learned calculations.

I race down surfaces no foot has ever trod.

I remember September 1938. Fire smoldered in iron; our fire in irons.

We stood facing the ruins to come.

We held on, suspended from one another by the force of memory.

As we hold firm today, in fear and hope, before the world ahead of us.

Irina has just turned seventeen, scarcely the blink of an eyelid to a star.

I long for you to come back this way.

I will make concessions; I will come visit you in the West.

I read your beautiful piece, in that horrible newspaper, on the Prague affair.

Paul





Most people traveling by train prefer to sit facing the direction of travel.

A historian is a traveler who chooses not to sit facing the direction of travel.

A historian of science is a historian who, seated facing backward and, unlike most historians, does not look out the window.

A historian of mathematics is a historian of science who, seated facing backward, eyes closed, seeks to prove that the Arabs invented trains.

No one laughed.



It must be said that I was the only historian at the colloquium. All the others were mathematicians, physicists, or, worse still, logicians, male and female alike. All of them seated facing forward. Looking toward innovation, invention, discovery. I was the only one interested less in tomorrow’s glorious proofs and inventions than in the cloying meanders of the past. Meanders of the past that cast their light into the furthest reaches of the future, and I could feel, during that session of the Paul Heudeber Days on the Havel, that this audience of learned women and men would listen to my paper on Nasiruddin Tusi and irrational numbers only with the respect the occasion required, full of consideration for me and for my mother, who despite her great age would not miss a word of the talks, between two walks on deck.

Maja was at the origin of the idea for this river colloquium; if I remember rightly, Jürgen Thiele, the secretary-general, had proposed “an afternoon excursion on the Spree or the Havel” as the conclusion to the Days, which were initially to take place at the Institute in Berlin; she had made a face, the Spree or the Havel, that’s still Berlin at best, Brandenburg at worst, why not the Danube, and Jürgen Thiele had widened his eyes, the Danube, but that’s very far away, and I imagine Maja had started laughing, all right then, the Havel, but at least let the whole colloquium be on a boat, and Jürgen Thiele was terribly embarrassed (he explained all this to me later), because he wanted to refuse my mother nothing for those commemorative days but his means were limited—this whole business of a floating colloquium still struck him as absurd, an old person’s whim.



Nonetheless Thiele was surprised to receive two letters on the same day, a few weeks before the call for participation in the Days was published: one informing him that the mathematics faculty of the University of Potsdam proposed to co-organize the Paul Heudeber Days with our Institute, and another that the Georg Cantor Foundation was granting, though Thiele had requested nothing at all, an enormous subsidy for the conference, making it possible to hold it on the water, however aberrant the idea still seemed to him, though he said nothing.

Paul’s tragic death a few years earlier had aroused a burning emotion in the scientific community; everyone was ready to take part and even if most of the organizers (Jürgen Thiele first among them, I thought) did not know why Maja wanted it so badly, nobody wished to disappoint her. Those two letters arrived at just the right moment and Jürgen could only suspect, no doubt rightly, that Maja had taken up her pen or picked up her telephone: though in theory retired from politics since the federal elections of 1998, she still had the power to draw “benevolent attention” to projects in the cradle. The money from the Georg Cantor Foundation was welcome; Jürgen Thiele got in touch, as co-organizer, with the University of Potsdam, which was celebrating its tenth anniversary and whose founding Paul had helped bring about: many of the mathematics teachers there had been his students.



The Paul Heudeber Days would therefore take place on the Havel, aboard a cruise ship capable of accommodating, in its conference room, the fifty or so delegates, while the participants who were not Berliners were for the most part lodged in a hotel opposite Peacock Island, technically therefore in Wannsee—a hotel with the name of some medieval or Alpine inn, The White Owl, an inn which Maja assured me (I wondered where she could possibly get such certainty from) had existed since at least the sixteenth century, though its present building—Doric columns supporting a monumental balcony, windows with green shutters, climbing roses, as in a fairy tale, softening the façade with their innumerable very dark red flowers shading toward black—had been rebuilt by Karl Schinkel during the first third of the nineteenth century. The White Owl stood lost in the middle of the forest, on the edge of the immense lake crossed by the Havel. Only the keynote speakers and other VIPs of the colloquium were housed on the Beethoven, since there were few cabins; the daytime cruises, on the other hand, were open to everyone: Potsdam-Elbe on Wednesday, the actual day of homage itself, centered on Paul’s work, then Peacock Island-Köpenick via Spandau on Thursday to bring the festivities to a close. Only a few prestigious guests had arrived on Sunday to take advantage of the boat’s “positioning” from Köpenick to Wannsee and thus an extra day of cruising through Berlin on Monday.



Jürgen Thiele was full of empathy, disorder, and good will. If he was still secretary-general of the Institute, he now carried out the task only out of loyalty to Paul, whose student he had been thirty years earlier; he readily admitted that he was tired of organizing, arranging, setting things in order—putting on a Christmas lunch panics me, he confessed. So imagine a colloquium with fifty people! The University of Potsdam had assigned him a co-organizer, a young doctoral student in number theory named Alma Sejdić who was trying, in her thesis, to prove a corollary to Paul’s first conjecture. The addition proved as disastrous as it was hilarious: instead of adding up, those two forces seemed either to combine uselessly or to cancel each other out. Things forgotten were forgotten twice over, blunders repeated in duplicate. It was like a drawing traced by two ballpoint pens tied together with a rubber band, parallels never meeting, despite all their efforts, constrained by Euclid himself.

Jürgen Thiele had had to deploy all his diplomacy so as not to offend the University of Potsdam, which could not understand why it should help finance, only a few kilometers from its own premises, the rental of a luxurious cruise ship—but Jürgen Thiele had pulled the Georg Cantor Foundation subsidy from his sleeve and everyone had found the idea of a floating conference exhilarating.

And so, after a few months of this chaotic ballet, Maja and I boarded, as planned, on Monday, September 10, in Köpenick, in the company of Linden Pawley, whose flight from New York had landed at Tegel that very morning, the inevitable Robert Kant of Cambridge, and Jürgen Thiele—there were indeed five luxurious cabins prepared for us.







III





Every morning since he left, the cold wakes him shortly before dawn. He shivers. No sudden movement, so that the dew, black beads on the canvas, will not run off. Patiently, by folding his shelter into a gutter, he manages to fill his canteen with a few centiliters and drink that icy sweat of dawn, which will be his only breakfast.

He sets off, once his recalcitrant feet are wrapped in that knitted sponge of a miserable green, still damp, in the direction of destiny, northward, for one has to call debacle and oblivion something. Once again he hesitates over abandoning the rifle, it is heavy and its strap awkward, too short since he cut it to make himself a belt, with that knife still so sharp, itself too the mark of a dangerous solitude, drunk on blood, he thinks no more, he is already walking while the first rays of the sun probe the shadows of the rocks. Those spurs of light bring to life the sparrows, the warblers, the tits, whose wingbeats follow in the wake of the morning song.



If he thinks so much about birds, if their presence and their song keep him so taut, it is because they sharpen his hunger—it would be so easy to crouch in wait, nose to the wind, with the rifle, wait for one of those little birds to give itself away, bring it down and then eat it, but the power of a weapon of war would leave nothing but feathers, the shot would echo far through the heights and even if a big pheasant or a partridge strayed into his line of sight, it would have to be cooked, and he has no wish to interrupt his march for long, to expose himself by fire or smoke.

He has resolved to reach the house.

You would find it even on a moonless night,

the hut,

the path goes on into the day among the holm oaks, scattered by the drought; a few lentisks shelter among the rocks, releasing as the walker passes their apothecary scent, scent of a forgotten pharmacy; he searches with his eyes for the fresh wild savory that spring multiplies in the mountain, so that he may chew a bunch of it for a long time, bitter, sour, peppery—strawberry-tree fruit still survive the winter like forgotten Christmas decorations, red and rough, they taste of stale strawberries, the blandness of oblivion.

These fruits are tiny stars, planets within reach of the hand,

little moons reddened by desire and the Evil One,

with every step the sun kindles the petals of the dogwood flowers, their vivid yellow softened by no leaf, on their still bare branches the first fissure in winter opens as if by magic.

He walks like the last man, in the mountain’s obsessive rustling.

He envies the black specks of planes or distant birds of prey.







He sits there, ass on a stone, numbing himself with memory—one of those blue-gray rocks close to the surface, quick to warm in the sun and smelling of metal and flint, as smooth as they are hard: was there some first tremor, some hoarse wind, a premise of the logic of brutality, some bellow before the sovereign rut of war, it seems to him not,

surprise is what sat you there,

soon the black snakes will come out of their holes and the males will go looking for females,

he loosens his boots, unbuckles the straps and pulls them off. The leather is eaten away by wear, water, and cold. The smell of shit has not left him. His hands are rough; the white of his palm is starred with darker calluses, stiffened from gripping wooden handles too hard. His tobacco-stained fingers end in yellowed nails furrowed with dark grime, the veins show at the thumb and along the wrist; his cheeks are rough with an obstinate beard, his hair is greasy and stuck in clumps, matted into duller locks by dried blood,

you will reach the house before night,



the house, the hut, the hovel—it lies very deep in his memories and his hopes. Cairn of the land of childhood. High enough in the mountain that no one ventures there. Hidden enough from the world of the mountain that he can shut himself away there. For a time. The roof is partly collapsed perhaps, the round cypress beams, still gleaming, left bare, without tiles, among the uneven stones. The door so low. The porch in front, its wooden braces recalling the Father’s arms, its two roughly squared stone posts the columns of a brutal God’s temple. The rough-stone façade without plaster. The covering of old yellow clay tiles,

you will be able to carve faces in the beams with the knife as before,

you are hungry enough to frighten,

you are hungry to the roots of your hair,

to imagine the little hearth beneath the hut’s porch and a fowl crackling there on the embers, writhing with furious pain,

you are thirsty,

he drains his metal canteen. The beautiful March sun turns orange. A wind blows from the sea,

you go on,

you must go on even if you stagger a little, clumsy with dizziness. He lets thoughts escape as soon as they are born. He drives them out through his feet, sends them away by walking. He passes his thoughts down into his boots, which scatter them among the stones. Then inner silence, until the return of hunger, the great fixed star.

The treachery of illusion, the scent of the coming spring.

The sea, hemming its violet plains with white.

So high in the mountain the sea is only a threatening line, a horizon of sorrow.



His feverishness drives it farther away: the more he walks, the farther away the house recedes.

You crunch too much,

you will have to beware of the scree overlooking the hut,

lie stretched out in the sunset and watch for unknown movements—dogs abandoned and gone feral in the war, deserters, villagers, distant cousins, all cut off from their relics, on the path to the hermitage, to escape suffering, to put an end to the long Lent of blood,

spring suddenly takes his breath away. A spring of wingbeats, rock flowers, thornbushes, white and blue rosemary, the hum of wing-cases—the path he had been following has dropped a few dozen meters toward the sea; he strips off his clothes stiff as cardboard with filth, stained with grease and dried blood, and finds himself bare-chested, by turns licked by the breeze and blinded by the power of the sun whose burn he feels on his shoulders, on the long scar slashing across his back, before the cloth of the pack covers it again. Tired of the too-short strap he takes his weapon in his arms like a hunter, his left hand around the forestock, his right around the grip as one clasps a bird by the neck, with force and nonchalance; the bolt is open, he sees the brass of a casing gleam in the magazine, once again he wants to rid himself of the accursed object,

it weighs more in your arms than a child,

you ought to abandon it, hide it there in a clump of bushes, a few hours’ walk from the hut,

he works the well-oiled bolt, impossible to get rid of it,

destiny ahead of you and all these things, the remains, the traces, and the great mourning of the future,

you will be what the Lord wishes,

strength or forgiveness, nothing, like that yellow arachnid beneath your boot, crushed despite its power of death, crushed despite its sting, all that one does not know of oneself, we bend beneath yesterday’s world, we bend beneath our faults, we bend beneath the prospect of tomorrow, our Father give us this day our daily oblivion, in the too many footsteps that sand away the soul, meter after meter, road after road, path after path, this sudden emotion comes from the nearness—one day’s walk—of the village below, halfway down the slope, where the orange trees little by little invade the plains, where the olive trees grow discreet on terraces with stone walls, among houses with soft arches, broken vaults among the loquats so green, brightening with orange fruit in June, among the noble fig trees bowed by the years whose figs hum with insects in autumn, just as the vine arbor shaded the terrace in front of his father’s house, where they pressed a wine that quickly stung the tongue, violet, cloudy, intoxicating—the green demijohns swathed in straw piled up in the darkest, coolest storage places until they were cleaned in September to receive the new vintage, and the red and black clouds of tannin clinging beneath their glass shoulders were scrubbed away with a metal brush,

you are going to have to hide, they are surely looking for you,

you must meet no one, keep hidden from men and beasts, from shepherds, from dogs, swallow your own name,



the nearer your steps bring you to the hut, to the house in the mountain, the greater the danger grows, in the village everyone knows, no doubt, rumors swell like the war itself, everyone knows, or thinks they know,

the afternoon swells like thirst and reddens like hunger.

He stops in the shade of a holm oak. He sits on a root. The sun pours over the valley before him. He dreams of rain. He shakes his canteen over his tongue once again. He loosens his boots, hesitates to take them off, he is so tired he will not put them back on if he removes them. The smell seems for a moment to have vanished but comes back, stronger still, out of nowhere,

you stink of blood and shit,

you stink of sleep and hunger,

a child would kill you with one punch,

he counts the days since leaving the town. Since fleeing the barracks. Four days since he sent the vehicle into the ravine,

you have covered nearly a hundred kilometers on foot in the mountains,

the holm oak’s root is hard under your ass,

your drawn-up knees hurt,

he leans back against the black trunk, stretches out his legs, his eyes on the valley (almond trees, hazels, prickly pears) that he knows so well. He has plowed these terraces, weeded around the feet of the trees, removed the innumerable stones. The sun he knows. The fringe of sea beyond the hills he knows. The fear he carries with him.



At the next bend in the path, once he has passed the old stream reservoir, dried up now, he will be two hours’ walk from the house. He will reach it nearly an hour before sunset,

you know where you’ll hole up,

behind the big rock and make sure, without being seen, that no one is hanging around the hut. Behind the rock and watch. Watch the last insects in the dusk. Listen to the birds and the stones in the dusk.

He takes out the knife. The blade is as gray as it is blue. He dreams of a hare, springing from a hollow, suddenly within dagger range. He cuts a cross into the tree root. A fine short cross. A sign. He would have been capable of drinking that hare’s warm blood if it had appeared,

you are as feverish as these parts in your memory,

for hours he has been searching with his eyes for an orange tree, or even a lemon tree with a few forgotten fruits still hanging on its branches. Opposite the hut stands an immense lemon tree planted by his grandfather that bears—or rather bore, it has been so long since he last saw it—dozens of yellow, juicy fruits with thick rinds, which leave on the hands a smell of linen and flowers, a fragrance of purity, purity pleases the Lord,

there is an orange tree too, wreaths were woven from its blossoms for weddings,

you are the least pure of creatures,



he finds the strength to set off again, knees aching, thighs hard as stone, feet flayed; the farther the war recedes, the more his body comes apart, an old mechanism that habit used to keep running. He is almost incapable of climbing the few kilometers that still separate him from the hut, the house, the violet mists and hollows of cloud. It is his rifle that carries him and guides him, the oversized needle of a magic compass, the rod of a dowser of death,

you walk badly, you stagger, you make too much noise,

he brushes away the tiny flies that pursue him only to catch up with him again and again. The sun burns his skin, made fragile by the cold of war, he is a lizard reanimated by heat; everything in him is stretched between fear and exhaustion.

His steps suddenly set off, a few meters away, the flight of a pigeon—stones rolling, branches trembling, sounds of wings. He closes the bolt to ready the rifle and shoulders it—he does not fire,

you are too near the villages, you must not attract the attention of a shepherd passing this way,

he watches the bird vanish behind a clump of holm oaks to rejoin its mate,

those birds always go in pairs,



they are the mountain’s inseparables, the inevitable companions of spring, along with the nightingales. He clicks on the safety. At the top of the pass between those two hills blotched with rock the hut will come into view. He watches the clouds suddenly mass, gray above the line of the sea. A cloud veils the sun. The wind turns the drops of sweat on his shoulders and chest into so many icy pins. He had forgotten how deft the cold is—it forces him to stop and put back on his jacket, stiffened by all the fluids clogging its fibers, stiffened too with pain and dread,

you stink of the slaughterhouse, that is the smell you give off, of entrails and a jet of water on blackened tiles,

a smell of meat,

he passes his left hand over his face, his beard is rough as bark. The disappearance of the sun means the return of altitude as much as shadow: he shivers. Behind him, a little lower down, a cottony mist spreads between two folds of hills, white fog on red earth, the sea has disappeared. Steel gnaws at the horizon. He throws his last strength at the stones to cross them, at the slopes to climb them. The pass bellows, the pass freezes his face. The wind presses against his face and shoulders. He clings to his rifle and leans forward. From one imbalance to the next he reaches the shelter of a rock, a few dozen meters below. He leans against it,

the house is below you on your right,



he watches, the roof of tiles more yellow than red is there, a single-pitched roof braced at the back against the mountain, he catches sight of the porch, the short chimney, the partition wall of rubble stone, the low walls around the abandoned garden, not an animal in sight, far off a bird of prey circles, a tiny solitary speck in the sky gone milky, the pen to the right of the garden is empty, the great almond tree before the house has no leaves yet, the lemon tree is green with that grave citrus green, eternal, a sepulchral green with yellow flashes in the wavering light of the absent sun, no smoke rises from the chimney, in the air there floats a smell of thyme and snow,

if you had binoculars you would look for traces,

signs of someone’s presence, shepherds, peasants, refugees, creatures, angels, demons,

there is only the short hesitant plain breaking toward the sea, only the wind can be heard crossing the stone walls, his back against the rock, his hands around his knees, the rifle to his right, the pack at his feet like a motionless dog, he waits, he waits the span of time he had planned, the two hours left before black night, reassured by the presence of the hut, by the lemons on the lemon tree, by the old orange tree, invisible to him, beside the hazel beyond the hut to the right of the wall,

all urgency abolished by the sudden presence of childhood,

on swift wings,

backward,

you wait for the apparition, Lord, your invisible face, you wait for Sirius, you wait for Orion, you wait for your face, Lord,

your ass is frozen by the scraps of winter the mountain still keeps,



the mountain keeps winter, orange trees and lemon trees keep winter—the fruits of December still hang on the branches when the flowers open in April, he strains his eyes into the gathering dusk, he strains his gaze, he sees nothing, not a movement, not a shadow except that of the almond tree growing, that of the hovel growing, a blackbird sings in the evening, a blackbird, Lord, one of your creatures sings your glory, all creatures sing your glory, hope infuses his chest, it is the presence of the hovel and the blackbird’s voice,

hope, Lord, is born of You,

you are about to find a little childhood and rest again in the hovel,

the hovel where you went with your father, where your father went with his father, went to till, went to gather, went to till,

the house is before his eyes and there is no movement, no presence, it is night now, or almost, the bird’s voice has fallen silent, he is going to go down to the hut, the hovel, the house, whatever name one gives it,

you limp, you stumble, the pack and the rifle weigh heavy,

the path unwinds like a snake along the mountainside, there is no longer a single star in the sky and the wind is still at war.
