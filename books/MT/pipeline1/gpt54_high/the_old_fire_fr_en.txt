6 NOVEMBER


Because of the rain, I missed the sign for the village. It had blurred the hollows, erased the ruts; in the end I was inching forward blind before pulling onto the verge. All that water pounding on the bonnet. The storm began yesterday. I haven’t seen a soul since I came off the motorway. Even though the radio was advising people not to drive, I had no choice. It’s five o’clock, an anthracite sky. I couldn’t work out how to tilt the seat. I wait, sitting very straight, numbed by the racket. At least the van looks solid. It looks like a road-maintenance vehicle, plasma-orange. At the rental office I insisted on practicality.



An hour passes. At last the torrents ease. I start up again. The GPS drives me deeper into the forest. Before long, neither rain nor light can pierce the roof of leaves. I switch on full beam. The steering wheel is sticky. I crawl along for miles, making out the path between the tracks under the brambles, until I emerge at the foot of a steep slope. A little higher up, the gate stands open. For the first time, I repeat my father’s gestures. I shift into first, accelerate; the wheels spin on the loose stone but keep their grip, and I cut the engine in front of the house. The automatic light comes on. A rabbit darts away.

The house looks worn out, the roof slumped over the brickwork like a giant being smothered by ivy. A car is parked beneath the hazel tree. Ferns are prising apart the front steps. Through the window I can make out light. I press myself against the peephole, then step back at once. I wasn’t expecting my sister’s face, huge forehead, brows pulled wide apart, fish eyes—my sister bloated by that lens my father claimed to have installed backwards on purpose. According to him, we had nothing to fear and nothing to hide, our riches were within us, and the whole world ought to know that the most beautiful people lived here.



— Hi.

My voice comes out louder than I expected. Véra answers with a smile too wide for her mouth. She takes the suitcase from my hands and sets it down at the foot of the stairs in the kitchen. I find again the stone floor, the wooden furniture, the bathroom door in the shadow of the fireplace. I had never known it like this, the hearth stuffed with books. Above the table, a birdcage has replaced the light fitting. Cheeses are piled up behind the bars.

Véra points to the stairs, then to herself at the worktop: I’m to settle in while she finishes getting dinner ready. I remember her as a slob. I compliment her. She types on her phone and shows me the screen:

“It’s to welcome you properly.”

I answer, a little sharply, that we’re sisters and this is my home too; let’s skip that kind of politeness. She turns on the gas with a hurried movement. I can’t help adding:

— Especially since we’re not keeping anything.

The staircase whispers under my socks. I have to be careful not to slip. My parents’ bedroom door is ajar. I stay on the threshold, in the draught from the badly insulated French window. Black parquet. In the middle of the room, the big bed, the naked mattress, no sheets or blanket. I still wonder how my parents could sleep with nothing behind their heads. I close the door again, vaguely relieved. I don’t know what I had dreaded more, sleeping in that bed, or sharing our room with my sister now that we’re adults.



Her sweet perfume catches in my throat. She has kept our bunk beds. Tonight the sight of them saddens me. The wrought iron looks too thin to hold us. The chest of drawers and the desk are where they always were, as plump as before, painted salmon. I make sure I can get online. I’m going to spend nine days here; I need to be able to communicate with my colleagues. The signal shows only one bar. Sometimes it disappears.

Leaning out of the window, I catch sight of the van. Its orange colour makes me laugh. It looks like a big bumblebee. It looks as out of place as my sister and I do, together again for the first time since my father died, five years ago.

Véra has poured wine into the crystal glasses. Unnerved by the ritual, I say I don’t drink. She raises her eyebrows, pours the wine back into the bottle, some spills, I wipe it up with my jumper then take it off, I’m hot. The bamboo crockery feels alien to me. Proudly, Véra shows me the cheese wrapper decorated with chestnuts, then points to the fireplace: it’s a smoked cheese. I push away the thought that it’s made with raw milk. She has prepared an endive salad with figs and walnuts. I ask whether she has thought about how we should proceed over the next few days. I haven’t, I’ve been very busy. She taps out:

“Congratulations on your award.”



I murmur that it’s kind.

I don’t know what she knows about the films I’ve written. The latest has just won an award at an Italian festival; I couldn’t go, and anyway I hadn’t invited her.

— Have you heard from Octave? I ask, in a voice I want to sound neutral.

She nods, of course, points to the figs, the walnuts, they’re from him... I cut her off. As far as I’m concerned, I say, there’s nothing I want to keep. She can do her sorting; we’ll take the rest to the tip. Her fingers tighten around her phone. With her chin she indicates the wardrobe, the kitchen, the bathroom. I roll my eyes—surely we’re not going to rummage through all that? Her face lights up in the glow of her screen:

“As you like.”

I soften. It’s just that I have work. I’m behind. I’m going to have to shut myself away to write. With a simplicity that disconcerts me, she shows me the screen again:

“As you like.”



Then she asks what’s new with me. I mention my latest commission, the adaptation of Georges Perec’s novel W, or the Memory of Childhood. Véra punctuates my sentences with smiles. I feign nonchalance as I mention the prestige of the production, the fame of the actors being considered, of my co-screenwriters. We have to create six episodes. Shooting is planned for two years’ time. Véra applauds. I qualify it. Adapting this text isn’t simple. And I’m only writing the dialogue. I start to tell her about Perec; she nods vigorously, she knows, she has read A Void.

— You read?

She makes it clear that’s obvious.

— I don’t know, my father...

Silence.

— I mean Dad. He never told me.

Still smiling, Véra serves me the last figs. I was fifteen, Véra twelve, when I left for the United States. I was supposed to stay there through high school, with a host family. Véra had stopped speaking long before that. She was learning to read and write, but I hadn’t imagined she’d come this far.



— And you, are you all right? I ask, realising I haven’t asked her a single question since I arrived.

Our last exchanges were last year, when she moved to Périgueux. Until then she had lived here with my father, even after his death. I helped from a distance. The studio was already furnished; she didn’t want to take anything from our childhood home without my agreement. Now that the sale has gone through, Véra was counting on me to come and empty it.

I read over her shoulder. She makes an irritated gesture; I have to give her time. I apologise and help myself to more salad.

Following a re-parcelling of the land, our house is no longer classed as an outlying farm of the neighbouring château, the Pigeon Froid, owned by Octave’s family. Safety standards have changed. To comply with them, we would have to redo the roof, the heating, the electrical system. We don’t have the money, so we accepted an offer from a campsite that is going to demolish it. Octave wants our stones for the restoration of the dovecote.

Véra shows me the screen. Shop work bored her. She’s training in flower stabilisation.

— Stabilisation?

“I make flowers that don’t wilt thanks to chemistry.”

I put on an interested expression.

— And does it work?



“That depends on the flowers.”

I clarify: I meant as a business. She shrugs:

“People don’t want the hassle any more.”

We clear the table.

— It’s true flowers don’t grow in November, I end up saying flatly.

Véra doesn’t stay up long. I remain in the living room, night seeping into me. The floor lamps cast warm half-shadows, but I’m not at ease. There are no curtains at the windows. In the pane above the sofa, ringed with black, I can see my reflection, while the fridge purrs and the smell of the cheese Véra has put back in the cage hangs in the air. I feel oppressed by the fireplace stuffed with books, by the dozens of Van Gogh sunflowers pinned up everywhere. My father collected theatre and art-exhibition posters. He never went to see them and didn’t care about the artists’ names, but chose the most colourful images to plaster the house with.

I feel weary at the thought of the great clear-out. If we set fire to the books, only the stones would remain; that would be simplest, since they are all we have been asked to preserve.



I put off sorting my emails till tomorrow and spend the evening drifting around my messaging apps, anxious over Irvin’s silence. It’s nearly evening in New York. He’s had time to write. I’m tempted to wait for him to make the first move, think myself childish, wish him good night, tell him I arrived safely. I hesitate. Add that I miss him.

The bathroom is just as I remember it. It looks like a cave, floor and walls of rough stone. Véra has put out a towel for me, neatly folded on the sideboard. It has always disgusted me with its wormholes, even if they’ve been treated. She has taken off her jewellery. Ethnic style, feathers, shells. I open a drawer. It is full of amber, necklaces, brooches. I push the wet bathmat aside with my foot, step into the shower cubicle and wash Véra’s hairs down the drain. I hope I don’t clog it.



I stay under the scalding water for a long time. My hair still bears the mark of the elastic; it dried that way while my hormones were dropping. I don’t know when my periods will come back. I turn towards the stone. I still have the reflex, when I’m naked, of looking for something to lean on, of not lowering my eyes to this lower belly Irvin claims he never sees as swollen. According to him, it’s all in my head. He wants to reassure me. It isn’t his fault. He didn’t see it drain down my legs, turn the water red, then disappear down the pipe. He knows nothing about my body.

The more I try not to make noise, the more the stairs creak. The bedroom is bathed in the greenish light of our phones charging. Only Véra’s head sticks out above the blanket, her hands domed on her chest. Her clothes are in a heap on the chest of drawers. The ladder squeaks, the sheets rustle. Rocked by the smell of washing powder, I fall asleep at once.







7 NOVEMBER


I sort by category. Things to destroy, things to give away. I laugh to find myself following influencers’ tidying advice. Cold light comes through the window. The spiders scuttle away. They don’t spin webs but cling in the corners, around the oven. I get rid of the dead ones in the bottoms of saucepans. I started with the kitchen, which seemed the most neutral room. Into a large bin bag I throw expired food. Mustard, tomato paste. A jar of white stuff, duck fat. A whole shelf is devoted to cheese. The fridge seems to shrink back under my onslaught. The freezer compartment needs defrosting. The plastic cracks. In the vegetable drawer, potatoes have sprouted. Véra has only come back once a month since she moved out. The smell of cheese turns my stomach. My movements are quick. Mouldy jam, leftover butter, wilted herbs.

Véra is dealing with our room. I feel uneasy knowing she’s nearby. I listen out for her. A little while ago she came downstairs to make coffee; I was sniffing a jar of compote, she asked if I wanted some too, and not knowing whether she meant the compote or the coffee, I dropped the jar into the bin like a rabbit caught in headlights throwing itself under the wheels.

Véra is getting through things too. At this rate, I’m afraid we’ll have too much time on our hands. What will I do with her for another six days? I try to slow down.



The muddle in the kitchen looks like Véra. I prefer it to the neatness of the living room when I arrived. Oil has run. I clean the greasy bottoms, the sticky clumps. As I run the cloth along the back of a cupboard, I feel the panel move. I press harder. It comes loose, comes away easily. It was covering a space the size of an apple crate, hollowed into the cool stone. It is full of packets of sugar and strong spirits. I call Véra. She too had no idea this recess existed. Does she think our father... ? She shakes her head, probably not. It’s true I never saw him drink.

— And Mum?

Véra stays thoughtful. I pick up a bottle.

Quince liqueur. I want to throw it away; she stops me, miming the two of us drinking straight from the bottle. I shake my head but set it back and close the hiding place. At the bottom of the sink, bits of jam mingle with the greyish water. I have to plunge in my whole arm to fish them out.

Véra seems impressed by my sorting. She looks at me, tugs at her jumper and points to the stairs: I should take a look at my clothes.

— I’m giving them away.

She insists. I can feel her watching me go upstairs. That look weighs on me, gives importance to a gesture that, to my mind, has none.



My side of the chest of drawers has stayed just as it was before I went to the States, a few jumpers fewer. Even so, I try on a pair of jeans. For fun. My father had mended a hole by sticking a flower-patterned patch over it with a saucepan of boiling water. I’m surprised to find I’ve grown slimmer. Though I’ve put on weight these past few months, Irvin likes it, which reassures and alarms me at once. I put the jeans in a plastic bag, do the same with the flannel trousers—a fabric I never wore again—cotton pyjamas, T-shirts and woollen jumpers. I start rummaging with the growing impression that I am looking for something.
