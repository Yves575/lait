Ice





We do not freeze.





I


WARSAW




“If a soldier fires a shot from a rifle, then at that very moment the wound the bullet will drill into the enemy some time later begins to exist; if someone takes a dose of an incurable poison, then at that very moment the final mortal process begins to exist, though its moment may not come for another few days. The ancients were right to say: we died the moment we were born. Over the further course of the bullet and the poison (and, in part, of living ourselves out) we have no power.”





CHAPTER ONE





Of the Thousand-Rouble Son




On 14 July 1924, when the officials from the Ministry of Winter came for me, that evening, on the eve of the Siberiad, only then did I begin to suspect that I did not exist.

Under the eiderdown, under three blankets and an old gabardine coat, in flannel drawers and a wool sweater, in socks pulled over socks—only my feet stuck out from beneath the bedding—after a dozen hours of sleep, finally thawed out, curled almost into a ball, my head jammed under a pillow in a thick case, so that even sounds reached me softened already, warmed, dipped in wax, like ants mired in resin, and so they forced their way inward slowly and with great labor, through sleep and through pillow, millimeter by millimeter, word by word:

“Gospodin Venedikt Yeroslavsky.”

“Him.”

“Asleep?”

“Asleep, Ivan Ivanovich.”

A voice and a voice, the first low and hoarse, the second low and sing-song; before I raised the blanket and an eyelid, I could already see them leaning over me, the hoarse one by my head, the sing-song one by my feet, my tsarist angels.



“We have awakened young master Venedikt,” Ivan declared when I raised the other eyelid. He nodded to Mrs. Biernat; the landlady meekly left the room.

Ivan drew a stool toward him and sat down, knees pressed together, a black bowler with a narrow brim resting on them. His high father-killer collar, white as snow in the noonday sun, hurt my eyes, that white collar and the white office cuffs, dazzling against the solid black of their clothes. I blinked.

“Permit us, Venedikt Filippovich.”

They did permit themselves. The other man perched at the foot of the bed, dragging the eiderdown down with his weight until I had to let go of it; seizing the blankets in turn, I hauled myself upright on the pallet, and in so doing I uncovered my back as well, cold air pressing under my sweater and drawers, and I shivered, fully awake.

I threw the coat over my shoulders and drew my knees up under my chin.

They looked at me in amusement.

“How’s your health?”

I cleared my throat. Night phlegm had gathered there, a caustic acid distilled from everything in my stomach, from garlic sausage, gherkins, whatever else we’d taken on board yesterday, from warm cornelian liqueur and cigarettes, cigarettes by the dozen. I leaned toward the wall and hawked into the spittoon. It doubled me over. Bent over, I coughed heavily for a long while. I wiped my mouth on the torn sleeve of my coat.

“Horse’s.”

“That’s good, that’s good, we were afraid you wouldn’t get out of bed.”

I stood up. My wallet lay on the windowsill, wedged behind a flowerpot with a dead geranium. I took out my papers and held them under Ivan’s nose. He did not even glance.



“But really, gospodin Yeroslavsky! Do we look like beat cops of some kind!” He drew himself up on that stool still straighter; I’d thought that impossible, but he drew himself up still more, and now the walls seemed crooked, the wardrobe hunchbacked, the doorframe scoliotic; offended, the official raised his chin and thrust out his chest. “We very politely ask you to come with us to Miodowa, for a little tea and sweets; the commissioner always has sorbets, little cakes, cream horns sent over, straight from Semadeni’s, a veritable debauch of the palate, if I may so express myself, eh, Kirill?”

“You may, Ivan Ivanovich, most certainly,” sang Kirill.

Ivan Ivanovich had great bushy moustaches, heavily pomaded and curled upward; Kirill, by contrast, was shaved smooth all over. Ivan took a watch from his waistcoat pocket on a tangled chain and announced that it was five to five, Commissioner Preiss thought highly of punctuality, and wasn’t he due out to supper? He was meeting the major general at the French Café.



Kirill offered Ivan snuff, Ivan offered Kirill a cigarette, and they watched me get dressed. I splashed myself with icy water from the basin. The stove tiles were cold. I turned up the wick in the lamp. The room’s only window looked onto a cramped courtyard, and the panes were so overgrown with dirt and frost that even at noon very little sunlight seeped through. When I shaved—when I still shaved—I had to set the lamp before the mirror and turn the flame all the way up. Zyga gave up the razor as soon as he arrived in Warsaw; he grew a beard fit for a village priest. I glanced at his bedding on the far side of the stove. He had lectures on Mondays; he’d probably risen at dawn. On Zygmunt’s bed lay the officials’ black fur coats, their gloves, cane, and scarf. The table, after all, was crowded to the edge with dirty dishes, bottles (empty), books, journals, notebooks; Zyga dried his socks and underwear there, hanging them over the edge of the tabletop and pinning them down with anatomy atlases and Latin dictionaries. And in the middle of the table, on top of a much-thumbed, greasy copy of Riemann’s Über die Hypothesen welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen and a heap of yellowing Kuryer Warszawski issues kept for kindling, for patching up cracks pried apart by frost and drying boots, and also for wrapping sandwiches—there rose a double row of candles and stubs, the ruins of a stearin Parthenon. Against the wall opposite the stove, meanwhile, stood neat stacks of hardbound volumes, arranged by size and thickness, and by frequency of use. Hanging above them on the smoke-blackened wall, the little plaque of Our Lady of Ostra Brama—the only remnant left by the previous tenants, whom Mrs. Biernat had thrown out onto the street for “improper conduct”—had blackened completely and now looked more like a piece of medieval armor for Lilliputians. Ivan stared at it for a long time, in intense concentration, rigid on his stool, his left hand holding the cigarette out to one side at a forty-five-degree angle, his right resting on his thigh beside the bowler, frowning and wrinkling his nose, bristling his moustache—and then I understood that he was almost blind, a chancery myope; on the bridge of his nose and under his eye sockets he had the marks of pince-nez, and without them he had to rely on Kirill. They had come straight in from the frost and Ivan must have taken his glasses off. My own eyes sometimes water in here. The air inside the building is thick, heavy, rubbed through with every odor human and animal bodies can produce; no one opens the windows, doors are shut at once and rags stuffed into the cracks above the thresholds so the heat won’t escape from the building—for fuel has to be paid for, after all, and anyone who had enough money for coal wouldn’t be nesting in such dark back-court tenements in the first place, where the air is thick, heavy, you breathe it as though you were drinking water spat out by your neighbor and his dog, every breath you take has already passed a million times through the consumptive lungs of peasants, Jews, carters, butchers, and whores, coughed up from black throats it comes back to you again and again, strained through their spit and mucus, passed through their moldy, louse-ridden, suppurating bodies, they’ve coughed it, blown it, vomited it straight into your mouth, you have to swallow, you have to breathe, breathe!



“Ex-excuse me.”

Luckily the privy at the end of the corridor was not occupied just then. I vomited into the hole from which an icy stench blew into my face. Cockroaches crawled out from under the shit-smeared seat. I crushed them with my thumb when they came up under my chin.

When I came back into the corridor, I saw Kirill standing in the doorway—he had his eye on me, he was standing guard in case I bolted from them into the frost in drawers and a sweater. I smiled in understanding. He handed me a handkerchief and pointed to my left cheek. I wiped it. When I tried to give the handkerchief back, he stepped away. I smiled a second time. I have a wide mouth; it smiles very easily.

I put on the only outfit I had fit to go out in, namely the black suit in which I had sat my final exams; if not for the layers of underwear underneath, it would now have hung on me like it was hanging on a skeleton. The officials watched while I laced my boots, while I buttoned my waistcoat, while I wrestled with the stiff celluloid collar pinned to the last cotton shirt I had. I took my documents and what cash remained, three roubles and forty-two kopecks—a bribe from that would be scarcely more than symbolic, but in an office a man with empty pockets feels naked. As for the old sheepskin coat, though, there was nothing I could do about it: patches, stains, crooked seams, and no other to wear. They watched in silence as I forced my arms into the uneven sleeves, the left one longer. I smiled apologetically. Kirill licked his pencil and carefully wrote something down on his cuff.



We went out. Mrs. Biernat had evidently been spying through a crack in the door—for she immediately appeared beside the officials, flushed and gabbling, to lead them back down from the second floor and through the two well-courtyards to the main gate, where Walenty the porter, adjusting his cap with the brass badge and slipping his pipe into his pocket, hurriedly swept snow from the pavement and helped the officials into the sleigh, taking the gentlemen by the elbows so they wouldn’t slip on the ice-glazed sidewalk, while Mrs. Biernat, with them already seated and wrapping their legs in blankets, showered them with torrents of complaints about malicious tenants, about gangs of Powiśle thieves who broke into houses even in broad daylight, and about the cruel frosts that warped windows damp on the inside and burst pipes in the walls, for no plumbing or sewerage could long endure underground; and in the end she warmly assured them that she had long suspected me of various offenses and abominations, and would certainly have informed the proper authorities if not for the thousand and one other cares piled upon her head—until the driver, up on his box behind Kirill’s back, cracked his whip and the horses jerked the sleigh to the left, forcing the woman to step back, and so we set off for the Warsaw delegation of the Ministry of Winter, to the former Palace of the Bishops of Kraków, 5 Miodowa Street, corner of Senatorska.



Before we turned from Koszykowa into Marszałkowska, snow began to sift down; I pulled my cap over my ears. The officials in their voluminous furs and bowlers like nutshells, seated on the low benches of the sleigh, Ivan beside me, Kirill with his back to the driver, reminded me of beetles I had seen in one of Zygmunt’s textbooks: fat oval torsos, short legs, small heads, everything glossy black, enclosed in the geometric symmetry of ellipses and circles. A shape so close to the ideal sphere cuts itself out from the world. They stared ahead with expressionless eyes, mouths clenched and chins raised high by their stiff collars, submitting inertly to the sleigh’s motion. I had thought I might learn something from them along the way. I had thought they would begin hinting for gratuities in exchange for good will, for not being too hasty or too diligent. They said nothing. I would ask them—how? about what? They would pretend not to hear. Flakes of sticky snow swirled between us. I tucked my cold hands into the sleeves of my sheepskin.



The French Café was lit up, electric light streaming through the great windows knitting woolly halos around the silhouettes of passersby. The summer sun ought still to have been high in the sky, but as usual heavy clouds hung over the city, and even the streetlamps had been lit—very tall ones, with spiraled tops. We turned north. From Ostrowski’s pastry shop, at the corner of Piękna, girls in red coats and white capes with little hoods came running out; for a moment their laughter pierced the street noise. I remembered the unfinished letter to Miss Julia and her last shouted question. Beside Ostrowski’s, at Wedel’s, Fredek, Kiwajs, and I used to arrange our card evenings. Just there, beyond the Sokół cinema, at Kalka’s, the Dear Prince rented a room for night sessions. If I lifted my head and looked left, over Ivan’s bowler, I would see the second-floor window of the house at number 71, the window Fredek fell from.



At the corner with Nowogrodzka a fat cow hung frozen to a streetlamp, a tendon of dark ice joining it to the top of a four-story façade. The cow must have come from the last drove headed to the slaughterhouse in Ochota; the wintermen had not yet chopped it down. Far down the street, above the roof of the Sphinx building, there loomed a bluish-black nest of ice, a great clot of frozen matter hard as diamond, joined by a web of icy threads, icicles, spans, and columns to the buildings on both sides of Marszałkowska and Złota—to buildings, lamps, stumps of frozen trees, balcony railings, bay windows, the spires of domes and turrets, parapets and chimneys. The Sphinx cinema had of course long been closed; no lights burned on the upper floors.

The sleigh slowed after we passed Nowogrodzka. The driver pointed with his whip. The cart ahead of us was climbing onto the pavement. Kirill looked back over his shoulder. I leaned to the right. At the intersection with Jerozolimskie Avenue two policemen stood, using whistles and shouts to clear traffic from the middle of the street—a lute was freezing its way across overhead.



For several minutes we were stuck in the jam it had caused. Usually lutes move above the rooftops, coming down to ground level less often in cities. Even from that distance I thought I could feel waves of cold flowing off it. I shivered and instinctively tucked my chin into the collar of my sheepskin. The officials of the Ministry of Winter exchanged glances. Ivan looked at his watch. On the other side of the street, behind an advertising pillar plastered with posters promoting a wrestling match at the circus on Okólnik, a man dressed in the English fashion was setting up an archaic camera to take a photograph of the lute; the photograph would almost certainly never appear in the papers anyway, confiscated by the men from Miodowa. Ivan and Kirill did not even glance at him.



The lute was exceptionally lively; before dusk it should manage to get to the other side of Marszałkowska, overnight it would climb above the roofs, by Friday it might reach the nest above the cinema. When last year a frostling crossed from Praga to the Castle by way of Alexandrine Bridge, the bridge was closed for nearly two months. Meanwhile this glacius—wait a quarter of an hour and I might have made out its movement, the way it froze from place to place, shifting in ice, by ice, from ice to ice, the way first one crystalline thread snapped behind it, then another, and a bluish-white crumble sifted slowly down, one minute, kshtr, two minutes, kshtr, the wind carrying off the lightest particles with the snow, while most of it froze into the black sheet hardening the street mud in the lute’s wake, ice of ice; and this trail of rough frost, like the track of a slug’s slime, stretched for dozens of meters east along Jerozolimskie, over pavement and hotel façade alike. The rest the wintermen had already chipped away, or it had thawed off by itself; yesterday afternoon the thermometer at Szniccer’s showed five degrees above zero.



The lute moved neither in a straight line nor at a constant height above the cobbles (they freeze in beneath the ground as well). Three or four hours ago, judging by the shattered architecture of ice, the lute had begun changing trajectory: until then it had moved barely a meter above the middle of the street, but then, three hours ago, it had turned in a steep parabola upward, beyond the tops of the streetlamps and the crowns of frozen trees. I could see the row of slender stalagmites it had left behind; they gleamed with reflected lamplight, with the colors of neon signs, with the light pouring through windows and shopfronts. The row broke off above the tram rails—the lute hung there with its whole weight on the starry web of frost-strings stretched out horizontally and rising upward toward the façades of the corner buildings. One could have walked under it, if anyone mad enough could be found.



Ivan nodded to Kirill, and Kirill clambered out of the sleigh with a reluctant grimace on his face, reddened by the biting frost. Perhaps I’d get lucky, I thought, perhaps we’d be delayed, Commissioner Preiss would already have gone out to the supper he’d arranged with the major general, they’d send me away from Miodowa with nothing. Thank You, God, for that freak of an icicle. I shifted on the bench, bracing my shoulder against the side of the sleigh. A newsboy came running up—“Hirohito defeated!” “Special Express, Mierzov triumphant!”—I shook my head. Whenever there’s a stoppage in the center, crowds form at once, street vendors appear, sellers of cigarettes and holy water, holy fire. The policemen were driving pedestrians away from the lute, but they could not possibly keep watch over everyone. A gang of urchins sneaked up from the side of Briesemeister’s restaurant. The boldest, with his face wrapped in a scarf and thick shapeless gloves on his hands, ran to within a dozen paces of the lute and threw a cat at it. The tomcat flew in a high arc, legs splayed, yowling fit to split the sky... and then the terrible cry stopped short. It struck the lute already perhaps dead, then slowly rolled off it into the snow, frozen to the bone: an ice sculpture of a cat with limbs spread wide and tail straight as wire. The boys ran off, howling with delight. A Jew with sidelocks shook his fist after them from the doorway of Epstein’s jewelry shop, cursing viciously in Yiddish.



Meanwhile Kirill had reached the older policeman and, catching him by the elbow so he would not run off in pursuit of the street boys, began persuading him of something in a lowered voice, though with substantial help from the sweeping gestures of his other hand. The constable kept turning his head away, shrugging, scratching his skull. The younger of the two policemen shouted at his companion, move, help! On the Avenue two sleighs had locked runners, causing an even greater commotion, carts were mounting the sidewalks, pedestrians, cursing in Polish, Russian, German, and Yiddish, fled from wheels and hooves, outside the wine shop a matron the size of a Gdańsk wardrobe slipped on the frozen mud, three gentlemen were trying to lift her, a fat officer hurried to help, and so the four of them hoisted with all their might, on one—down she went—on two—down she went—on three—half the street was already splitting with laughter, while the woman, red as a cherry, squealed shrilly, flailing her thick little legs in dainty boots... No wonder that only at the sound of tearing metal and cracking wood did we look back toward the intersection. An automobile had collided with a coalman’s wagon; one horse had gone down, one wheel had come off. Trapped inside the closed body of the car, the motorist began leaning on his alarm horn; to top it all something went off under the bonnet, as though a double-barreled shotgun had been fired. That was too much for the gray hitched to the sleigh beside it. Startled, it lunged forward, straight toward the lute. The driver caught the reins, but the horse itself must have felt what wall of frost it had run into—for it kicked even more violently sideways, spinning the sleigh in place. Had a runner caught on the curb? Had the gray slipped on the black sheet of ice? I was already standing in the ministry sleigh, Ivan and I watching the accident over the line of vehicles in front of us, but it was all happening too fast, too unexpectedly, too much motion, shouting, lights, and shadows. The gray went down, the sleigh it was pulling went down with it, its load spilled, a dozen or so bulging bottles in baskets packed with sawdust, the baskets and bottles rolling into the middle of the intersection, some of them must have broken, for a gleaming celadon liquid spread over the ice—kerosene, I thought—and then flame was already bursting up, from what, an electrical spark from the automobile, a dropped cigarette, the strike of a shod hoof on the stones, I don’t know. Blue fire danced across the full width of the puddle, high, higher still, a meter, a meter and a half up—almost reaching the lute frozen into the overhead web.



The photographer, hunched over his camera, slowly, methodically exposed plate after plate. And what would he see on them afterward, what would remain on glass and print itself onto paper: snow—snow—pale halos of streetlamps—dark mud, dark cobbles, dark sky—the gray façades of tenement houses in the perspective of the city’s broad ravine—in the foreground the chaos of angular vehicles blocked in the jam—and from among them and from among human figures bursts the blaze of pure fire, so bright that in that spot the sheet seems altogether blank—and above it, above the flame of white whiter than white, in the heart of the hanging arabesque of ice stretches the lute, the lute, a massive thunderbolt of frost, a frost-star, a living hearth of cold, lute, lute, lute above the fur hats of ladies, lute above the caps and bowlers of men, lute above horse heads and carriage hoods, lute above the neon signs of cafés and salons, shops and hotels, pastry shops and fruit stores, lute above Marszałkowska and Jerozolimskie Avenue, lute above Warsaw, lute above the Russian Empire.

Later, as we drove by the Saxon Palace, and along Królewska, past the Garden dead beneath many years of frozen ground and the colonnade hung with icicles, past the tower and the cathedral on Saxon Square buried beneath snowy overhangs, toward Krakowskie Przedmieście, that image—the afterimage of an image and of an imagining—came back to me again and again, an importunate memory of unclear meaning, a sight seen and not understood.



The officials exchanged gruff remarks under their breath, the driver shouted at inattentive pedestrians; the snowfall had eased, but it was growing colder and colder, my breath froze on my lips and hung before my face as a white cloud, the sweating horses moved in clouds of clammy vapor—the Royal Castle was drawing nearer and nearer. Before we turned into Miodowa I saw it above Sigismund’s Column: the Castle enclosed in a mass of shadowy ice—and the great nest of lutes above it. The violet-black coagulation reached halfway over the roofs of the Old Town. On clear days you can see waves of frost standing in the air around the Great Tower. No scale exists on thermometers to measure that cold. Gendarmes stand guard by the fires on the edge of Castle Square. When a lute freezes out of the nest, they close the streets. The governor-general had once established a cordon here of dragoons from the Fourteenth Little Russian Regiment, but the regiment had meanwhile been dispatched to the Japanese front.

The roof of the Palace of the Bishops of Kraków, however, remained free of any growth of ice. In the wing on the Senatorska side there were still elegant shops—electric floodlights illuminated the advertisements of Nikolai Shelekhov’s Exclusive Delicatessen and the teas of Sergei Bazylovich Perlov’s Moscow Trading House—but the main wing on Miodowa, beneath its rococo crowning and among pilasters with Corinthian capitals, belonged to the Ministry of Winter. Above both carriage entrances hung black double-headed eagles beneath the Romanov crowns, inlaid with onyx tungetite.



We drove into the inner courtyard, the runners screeching on the cobbles. The officials got out first, Ivan vanishing at once through the door as he set his pince-nez on his nose; Kirill stood on the steps, before the threshold, and looked back at me. I opened my mouth. He raised an eyebrow. I lowered my gaze. We went in.

A porter took my sheepskin and cap, and the doorman slid a great ledger toward me in which I had to sign in two places; the pen kept slipping from my numb fingers, perhaps he might sign for the gracious gentleman, no, I, I myself. Illiterate commoners, too, visit the corridors of Winter’s high command.

Everything shone here with cleanliness: marble, parquet, glass and crystal and iridescent winter-iron. Kirill led me up the main staircase, through two secretariats. On the walls, beneath portraits of Nicholas II Alexandrovich and Pyotr Rappacki, hung sunny landscapes of steppe and forest, of springtime Saint Petersburg and summer Moscow, from the days when spring and summer had still been permitted entry there. The staff did not raise their heads from their desks, but I saw councillors, clerks, office men, and copyists following me with sidelong glances, then exchanging crooked looks among themselves. When does office time end? The Ministry of Winter never sleeps.



Extraordinary Commissioner Preiss W. W. occupied a spacious office with an antique stove and an unused fireplace; the tall windows looked out onto Miodowa and Castle Square. When I entered, brushing past Ivan in the doorway, who had evidently already announced me, the commissioner was fussing about the samovar with his back turned. He himself had the shape of a samovar, a bulging, pear-shaped torso and a small bald head. He moved with frenetic energy, his hands fluttered above the table, his feet never stopped dancing, a little step left, a little step right—I was sure he was humming under his breath, that he was smiling to himself, that merry little eyes were peering out at the world from his ruddy face, that no furrow troubled the smooth brow of the commissioner of Winter. Meanwhile, because he did not turn around, I stood by the door with my hands clasped behind my back and let the warm air fill my lungs, wash over my skin, melt the blood in my veins that had stiffened with cold. It was almost hot in the office; the great painted majolica stove never cooled for a moment, and by the end the panes had steamed over so completely that through them I saw chiefly blurred rainbows of streetlights, strangely spreading and merging on the glass. It is a matter of great political importance that there must never be cold in the Ministry of Winter.

“Well now, why aren’t you sitting down, Venedikt Filippovich? Sit, sit.”

Ruddy face, merry little eyes.

I sat.



With a loud sigh he dropped into his chair behind the desk, cupping a steaming glass of tea in both hands. (He did not offer me any.) He had not been in office here long; the desk was not his, he looked behind it like a child playing at ministers, he would certainly have it replaced. They must have sent him only just now, sent him, the tsar’s extraordinary commissioner—from where? Petersburg, Moscow, Yekaterinburg, Siberia?

I drew a deeper breath.

“If Your Nobility will permit... am I under arrest?”

“Under arrest? Under arrest? How could such a thought have entered your head?”

“Your officials—”

“My officials!”

“If I had received a summons, I would certainly have come of my own—”

“Did they not invite you politely, Mr. Gierosławski?” At last he pronounced the name correctly.

“I thought—”

“My God! Under arrest!”

He was out of breath.

I laced my fingers together on my knee. It was worse than I had thought. They were not going to throw me in jail. A high tsarist official wanted to talk to me.

He began taking papers from the desk. He took out a thick wad of roubles. He took out seals. Sweat ran over me beneath my underwear.

“Yeees.” Preiss slurped his tea loudly. “Please accept my condolences.”

“I beg your pardon?”

“Your mother died last year, did she not?”

“Yes, in April.”

“You were left alone. That is sad. A man without family is like... well, on his own. That’s bad, very bad.” He turned a page, slurped, turned the next.

“I have a brother,” I muttered.

“Yes, yes, a brother at the other end of the world. Where was it he went, Brazil?”

“Peru.”

“Peru! And what does he do there?”

“He builds churches.”

“Churches! I’m sure he writes often.”

“Well, more often than I do.”



“That’s nice. He misses you.”

“Mhm.”

“And you don’t?”

“Him?”

“Your family. When did you last hear from your father?” Page, page, slurp.

Father. I knew it. What else could this be about?

“We do not correspond, if that’s what you’re asking.”

“That’s disastrous, disastrous. Doesn’t it concern you whether he’s even alive?”

“Is he?”

“Ah! Whether Filip Filippovich Gierosławski is alive! Is he alive! Is he alive!” He all but sprang from behind his operatic desk. By the wall stood a great globe on a delicate frame of wet winter-iron, and on the wall hung a map of Asia and Europe; he set the globe spinning, slapped the map with the back of his hand.

When he looked at me again, not the slightest trace of his recent joviality remained on his puffy face; dark stones watched me with clinical attention.

“Whether he is alive,” he whispered.

He lifted yellowing papers from the desk.

“Filip Gierosławski, son of Filip, born in the year eighteen hundred and seventy-eight in Wilkówka, in the Kingdom of Prussia, in East Prussia, Lidzbark district, a Russian subject since the year nineteen hundred and five, husband of Eulalia, father of Bolesław, Benedykt, and Emilia, sentenced in the year nineteen hundred and seven to death for participation in a plot against the life of His Imperial Majesty and in armed rebellion; well then, by way of clemency the sentence was commuted to fifteen years of katorga, with deprivation of estate rights and sequestration of property. In the year nineteen hundred and seventeen the remainder of his sentence was remitted, with an order confining his residence to the boundaries of the Amur and Irkutsk governor-generalship. He didn’t write? Never?”

“To Mother. Perhaps. At first.”

“But now? Lately? Since seventeen. At all?”

I shrugged.



“You yourselves surely know very well when and to whom he writes.”

“Don’t be insolent, young man!”

I smiled faintly.

“I beg your pardon.”

He studied me for a long while. On a finger of his left hand he wore a ring with some dark stone set in precious tungetite, engraved with Winter’s emblem; he tapped the ring against the desk, tok, tokk, the even-numbered blows were stronger.

“You finished the Imperial University. What are you doing now?”

“I was preparing for my rigorosum—”

“What do you live on?”

“I give mathematics lessons.”

“And do you earn much from these lessons?”

Since I was already smiling, all I could do was lower my eyes to my clenched hands.

“A lot, not a lot.”

“You are a frequent guest of moneylenders; all the Jews on Nalewki know you. You owe Abiezer Blumstein alone more than three hundred roubles. Three hundred roubles! Is that true?”

“Once Your Nobility tells me what matter I am being questioned about, it will be easier for me to confess.”

Tok, tokk, tok, tokk.

“Or have you really committed some crime, that you’re sweating here from fear, eh?”

“Would Your Nobility deign to open the windows.”

He stood over me; he didn’t even have to bend especially low to speak straight into my ear, first a whisper, then a growling soldier’s tone, finally almost a shout.



“You’re a gambler, my Benedykt, a card addict. What you win, you lose—what you earn, you lose—what you borrow, you lose—what you beg from your friends, you lose—you have no friends anymore—you have nothing anymore, and that too you lose, you lose everything. Twenty-one, baccarat, zimucha, poker, any way is good enough. Once you won half a sawmill—you lost it that same night. You have to lose, you can’t rise from the table till you’ve lost so completely that nobody wants to play with you anymore. Nobody wants to play with you anymore, Venedikt Filippovich. Nobody wants to lend to you anymore. You’ve pawned yourself two years in advance. Bolesław doesn’t write to you; it’s you who write to him, begging for money, but he sends no more. You don’t write to your father because your father has no money. You wanted to get married, but your would-be father-in-law set dogs on you after you pawned and lost your fiancée’s dowry. If only you’d put a bullet in your brain, as befits a nobleman, but you won’t, ptui, and what kind of nobleman are you, scum!”

I smiled apologetically.

Commissioner Preiss caught his breath, then patted me on the shoulder like a friend.



“Well now, don’t be afraid, we know our way around scum too. Never mind that your own father matters to you no more than the gout of the Emperor of Japan—for a thousand roubles matter to you! True? A thousand roubles matter to you very much indeed. We’ll give you a thousand, and then perhaps a second, if you do well. You’ll go visit your father.”

Here he fell silent, evidently waiting for my reply. Since it did not come, he went back behind the desk, back to the steaming tea (it had cooled a little by now, so he slurped it longer and louder), back to papers and seals. With a heavy pen he signed a document, struck it with one seal, adjusted another; satisfied, he rubbed his hands together and leaned back in his leather chair.

“Here you have your passport and a referral to our delegation in Irkutsk, they’ll take care of you on the spot. They bought you a ticket at once; tomorrow you leave for Moscow, otherwise you won’t make that Siberian Express—today is the first of July, your ticket is for the fifth, departure at ten in the evening from Yaroslav Station, you’ll be in Irkutsk on the eleventh, and there they’ll put you on the Zimna for Kezhma. Here’s your thousand, sign the receipt. Buy yourself some clothes, so you look like a human being! And if you get the idea of taking the money and losing it all—then lose it, for all I care, just so long as you get to Baikal. Well then, sign!”



A thousand roubles. What did they want me to do there? Draw from Father the names he hadn’t betrayed at his trial? Only why was the Ministry of Winter turning to me about it, and not Internal Affairs?

“I’ll go,” I said. “I’ll visit my father. And then what? That’s all?”

“You’ll talk to him.”

“I’ll talk.”

“And once you’ve talked, well, that will already be good.”

“I don’t understand what Your Nobility—”

“He wasn’t sending letters, and naturally that didn’t strike you as odd.” Commissioner Preiss opened a folder bound in cloth and tortoiseshell. “Irkutsk writes us... He was a geologist, wasn’t he?”

“I beg your pardon?”

“Filip Filippovich studied geology. He did not cease taking an interest in it in Siberia either. They report to me here... He was very close from the beginning, his company was taken into the second or third expedition that went there in the spring of nineteen ten. Most of them died of frostbite. Or froze where they stood. He survived. Then he came back. To them. I don’t believe it myself, but that’s what they write me here. They gave the order, they gave the money, so I’m sending a man. Your father talks with the lutes.”





Of What Cannot Be Spoken




My life is governed by the principle of shame.

One comes to know the world, one comes to know the language used to describe the world, but one does not come to know oneself. Most people—almost everyone, I think—will never learn, before they die, a language in which they might describe themselves.



When I say of someone that he is a coward, that means I consider him to behave like a coward—it means nothing more, because of course I cannot peer into the depths of his soul and discover whether he is a coward. Yet I cannot use that language to describe myself: within the compass of my experience I remain the only person for whom his words, deeds, omissions are merely a pale and fundamentally accidental reflection of what lies beneath them, what is their cause and source. I experience the existence of that source directly, whereas of some of my own behavior I am wholly unaware, and all of it I perceive incompletely, crookedly. We are always the last to find out what kind of idiot we have made of ourselves in company. We know the intentions behind our actions better than the actions themselves. We know better what we meant to say than what we actually said. We know who we want to be—we do not know who we are.

A language for describing our behavior exists because many people experience that reality and can discuss among themselves someone’s ostentatious politeness or someone’s faux pas. A language for describing myself does not exist, because no one but me experiences that reality.



It would be a language for a single user, a language unsayable, unwritable. Everyone must create it for himself. Most people—almost everyone, I am convinced—never bring themselves to do it before they die. At most they inwardly repeat other people’s descriptions of their own person, expressed in that derivative language—in a language of the second order—or imagine what they would say about themselves in it if they were granted the chance to look at themselves from the outside.

To say anything at all about themselves, they must make themselves strangers to themselves.

My life is governed by the principle of shame. I have no better words in which to tell that truth.

A beggar holds out his hand in supplication, I have money, I can give him some, even five, even two, even one kopeck, no one else is looking, there are only the two of us, the beggar and I—I give him nothing, I turn away and walk off quickly, hunching my head into my shoulders.

Who broke the jam jar? Mother raises her voice. Who broke it? Not I, and I have no idea whether Bolek is to blame or Emilka, but Mother asks again, and again, and then I point firmly at Bolek. Him.

Laughter in company, all of us mocking a mutual acquaintance who is of course not here with us now, one after another recalling his embarrassments and weaknesses. I rise, I do not laugh, brows drawn together and lips pressed tight, I rise and—aren’t you ashamed!



A beauty presses herself on me, spills over my shoulders, slides down onto my chest, onto my lap, as the cab slows and stops before the gate of the house where I rent a room with Kiwajs, Kiwajs is off with distant family in the provinces, the room is empty, the drunken girl giggles, biting at the button of my frock coat—the cabman turns around, winks at me, shall I help milord? I look at him in terror. I thrust a crumpled rouble into his hand. Take the whore wherever she wants! I throw the girl off me, leap out of the cab, and run into the depths of the dark gateway.

In the woods beyond my grandfather’s village, in the hollow of an uprooted tree above the stream, lies the carcass of a roe deer. Half eaten by predators and scavengers, beneath a black canopy of flies. I tell no one, I come every morning and every evening, I prod it with a stick, turn it over, watch the insects swarm over it anew, watch the flesh change color and dark fluids run out of the body, slowly soaking into the earth. Where do I spend so much time, Grandfather asks. I lie. He can see I am lying. Where do I go, he asks. I keep silent. He beats me with his belt. I cry. I do not tell the truth, I say nothing. All summer long I roam the forest looking for dead animals. I carry a thick stick with me, like a club. I beat carrion with that stick in a dull obsession until the rot comes away from the bones. Take that! take that! take that!



Eighteen and seventy-four! Eighteen and seventy-four! The fairground barker is shouting himself hoarse, proclaiming the winning numbers. We all bought tickets as we came into the fair. Laughing, red from the frost and the plum brandy, we now search our pockets for the little slips. I find mine, take it out. Eighteen and seventy-four. Pretending great disappointment and rage, they throw their tickets into the mud, curse their bad luck, and jeer at the fool who never comes to collect his prize. I tear up the winning ticket and do what they do.

At night, when no one can see me, I practice terrible grimaces, bulging eyes and bared teeth, too wild to mean anything in the language of human physiognomy; terrible grimaces, and also the absolute deadness of the face, the immobility of the tiniest muscles of the skull, which in daylight and among people I can never achieve, then I have no power over the contortions of my face. At night, when I sleep, I see beneath my eyelids a diagram, like something from one of Zyga’s yellowed plates, an anatomical scheme of that betrayal: dozens of hard twine-strings threaded under the skin of cheeks, brows, chin, lips; and the other ends of those strings are held by the people around me, in their hands, between their teeth, tied round watch chains and cufflinks, on wedding rings, cane handles and pipe stems; some have them sewn into their own facial muscles, or even into their hearts, straight through chest and breastbone. Then the diagram comes alive—it is day—there is movement—people—I—

I smile—I smile—I smile.

…



I recall behaviors, I use the second language...

I must become a stranger to myself.

Benedykt Gierosławski was a good child. Who would not want such a child? He was well-behaved—he always obeyed his parents, obeyed his elders, did not speak unless spoken to, did not get into mischief, did not fight with other children, said his prayers before bed, got the best marks at school. Other boys roamed the town and spent their time in the streets—he read books. Other boys peeped at girls and teased their sisters—he taught his little sister her letters. He washed behind his ears. He bowed to neighbors and acquaintances. He did not pick his nose, he did not point. He did not fall ill. He caused his parents no trouble. Who would not want such a child?

When he turned seventeen and entered the Imperial University, he met others who had been good children.

They recognized one another by their smiles.

Was it already too late? Could I have gone back—back to what, to whom, to which me? There is no moment in the past that, if miraculously changed, would reverse the course of my life, no catastrophe that befell me and made me other than I should have been; there is nothing of the sort I can point to.



In fact it is exactly the reverse: however far back I reach in memory, I always find beneath the thin skin of my words and deeds the same principle, the muscle of unease, hope, and revulsion tensed in the same direction when I was three, thirteen, and twenty-three. Not a feeling, something else, not a thought—it flees, slips away, slides out of reach, immaterial, indescribable—stop! look me in the eyes!—you, you—I will call you Shame, though you are not it, I will call you Shame, because there are no better words in human language.
