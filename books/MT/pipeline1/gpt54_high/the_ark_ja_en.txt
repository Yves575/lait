1 The Ark

I

Leaving the walking path near the highway, the seven of us pushed into a mixed wood. We made our way up the mountain path, stepping over rotten logs and fallen leaves, then crossed a field thick with dead grass somewhere or other.

By the time we reached an old-looking wooden bridge spanning a ravine about ten meters deep, the sun had disappeared behind the mountain ridge.

Ryuhei grabbed the railing, a crude thing made of lashed logs, and shook it with one thick arm.

When the bridge answered with a long, complaining creak, his deeply carved, wrestler-like face twisted. He turned to Yuya beside him.

“Hey, we’re seriously crossing this? You never said anything about that. Isn’t this thing gonna give way?”

“No, come on, it’ll be fine. I crossed it before. It wasn’t that dangerous. Totally manageable. See?”

Yuya stepped onto the bridge, spread both arms, and rocked his body from side to side.

There was no other way. The six of us had no choice but to follow him.

Once we were across, I pulled my phone from the pocket of my windbreaker and checked the time: 4:48 p.m.

Seeing the phone in my hand, Hana, in flashy fluorescent hiking gear, quickened her pace and came up beside me. Holding up her own phone in her right hand, she asked,

“Shuichi, you getting any signal?”

“No. Been out of range for about an hour now.”

“Thought so. Same here. So basically we’re not getting back to the villa tonight, right?”

No one answered. We all knew that was impossible.

Beyond the bridge, the land opened into a rough field hemmed in by steep mountains. A few hundred steps farther on, Yuya shouted,

“There! There it is! I can see it! Just a little more. It’s right there.”

There was relief in his voice. He had spent all this time walking under a barrage of dissatisfaction and distrust from behind. Even so, there was still no sign of anything that looked like the entrance to a building.

This had started earlier that morning. After all of us had gone out on the lake and played around in boats, Yuya had said this:

“There’s this really interesting place you can walk to from here. Want to check it out? Deep in the mountains there’s this huge underground structure. Sounds like it was used for some pretty sketchy stuff a long time ago, but I’m guessing by now nobody even knows it exists.”

Since the day before, we had all been gathered at a villa in Nagano owned by Yuya’s father.

Yuya was a friend from college, and he had been the one to propose getting everyone together. It was a small reunion of the six of us who used to hang out back then.

It had been two years since I’d seen any of them. Yuya used to have bleached blond hair and black piercings; now he’d stopped dyeing his hair and only the piercings remained, and I still wasn’t used to the change.

I had my own concerns, so I’d brought my cousin along. That made seven of us staying at the villa.

An underground structure in the mountains. Even hearing it described that way, none of us quite knew what to make of it.

An underground structure—apparently an enormous one, at that. Who would build such a troublesome thing in the middle of the mountains, and for what? It was hard to believe, but Yuya said he’d seen it himself about six months earlier.

It sounded intriguing, and if it wasn’t far, we figured we might as well go.

But contrary to what we’d been told, no matter how long we walked, we never reached it. Yuya, who had made it sound like a casual twenty- or thirty-minute stroll, spent the entire time frowning anxiously at the map on his phone.

Naturally, the underground structure itself wasn’t on any map. Yuya said that when he had visited before, he’d marked the location in a map app, but apparently the point he had saved was badly off. By the time we finally figured out where it was after getting lost, dusk was already coming on.

“So, Yuya? This underground structure of yours—you mean we’re staying there, right? There’s no time to go back now. Is that okay? Didn’t you say it was a pretty dangerous place?”

“I’m saying it might’ve been used for dangerous stuff a long time ago. A long time ago. Spending one night there will be fine. It’s not like anybody’s there. It’s basically just like poking around an abandoned ruin.”

Ryuhei and Yuya kept walking on ahead, leaving the rest of us about ten meters behind.

It had been like that the whole way here. Ryuhei, talking as if he represented all of us, had kept up a steady stream of complaints to our guide, Yuya.

Watching him, Mai, walking to my right, gave me a troubled smile—or maybe a placating one. In the gathering dusk, her pale face only made her long-lashed eyes stand out more clearly.

I started to answer, but if I spoke, Ryuhei would hear, and I couldn’t bring myself to do it. Mai didn’t seem to expect a reply anyway; before he could notice, she turned away.

When I looked back, Sayaka, who had fallen a little behind, came hurrying up to us at a light run. Her warm brown hair was tied in a bun, and there was sweat on her forehead.

She finally gave voice to something she had apparently been worrying about all along.

“Um, what about bathrooms in this underground place? And for sleeping—are we talking about using our backpacks as pillows and lying on the floor? Is everyone really okay with that?”

Yuya had never mentioned anything like that. We hadn’t come intending to spend the night.

My cousin Shotaro, walking a step ahead of me, answered her.

“I wouldn’t expect much. The building probably has a history. Still, it should be better than sleeping out in the open. Since it’s underground, it shouldn’t get all that cold.”

“I guess so. Right. It gets really cold at night, doesn’t it?”

Sayaka politely agreed with him.

I had only introduced my college friends to my cousin the day before. Shotaro was fitting in with them even better than I’d expected.

Five years ago, Shotaro inherited a considerable estate from our aunt, and ever since then he had gone without a regular job, drifting from one thing to another—travel for a while, an intense interest in geological research for a while. But it wasn’t exactly that he meant to live grandly by simply eating through the inheritance. Sometimes he would go abroad with about a million yen and come back with several times that amount.

He was my cousin, so I had known him longer than any of my friends. Even though he was the person I trusted most, he was still the one whose full nature I understood least.

The reason I had brought Shotaro with me was that I had sensed the possibility of conflict in this gathering. He had already been interested in the geography of the area, which made him easy to invite.

So far, nothing like the trouble I had worried about had happened. Instead, we had ended up heading for a mysteriously unsettling underground structure. It was reassuring to have him with us—someone who seemed capable of sorting things out if anything went wrong.

II

In the middle of that wasteland ringed by rugged mountains, Yuya suddenly stopped. Pointing at the ground, he shouted,

“Found it! Look, this is it. The entrance.”

He crouched, thrust a hand into the dead grass, and lifted a lid like a manhole cover, about eighty centimeters across.

When I peered inside, I saw a vertical shaft plunging straight down into the earth. Its sides were concrete, with iron bars embedded in the wall as ladder rungs.

“So this is where we go in—”

“What? No way. Creepy. It’s so narrow.”

Hana shone her phone light down the shaft. It wasn’t bright enough to show the bottom.

I felt the same. Of course, in a place this deep in the mountains, something mine-like was to be expected. But from the way Yuya had talked about it, I had imagined something a little more civilized.

“Yeah, okay, the entrance does look like this, but once you’re inside, it’s fine. It’s seriously huge. Goes down three basement levels. We can make it through one night.”

Hana and the others were plainly hesitant, and I wasn’t eager either.

Ryuhei was the first to move.

“Whatever. I’ll go. We can get through here like this, right?”

Trying not to let his backpack scrape, he climbed down the ladder into the earth. After checking the expressions of the three women, Yuya followed after him, unwilling to let Ryuhei go on ahead alone.

After a round of whispered “What do we do?” and “Who goes first?”, Hana, Sayaka, and Mai climbed down one after another. Shotaro and I brought up the rear.

After descending seven or eight meters, my feet touched the ground.

From there, a tunnel-like horizontal passage stretched ahead. It was wide enough to walk through without stooping.

It sloped gently downward. We went on, holding our phone lights up.

A little way in, there was a huge boulder lying in the middle of the passage.

It was far too large to move by hand. For some reason, it had been wrapped over and over in heavy chain.

“What’s this? Did they try to dig it out and give up?”

“Who knows,” said Shotaro, in a tone that suggested he did.

We squeezed past the boulder and came to an iron door.

Just before it, the natural rock underfoot gave way to a filthy plank floor. Beyond this point the place was unmistakably man-made.

Yuya opened the door and shone his light inside.

“Whoa. Damn, you’re right. This is something.”

Ryuhei’s voice was hard to read—whether impressed or unnerved.

Beyond the door was a broad corridor. The ceiling was low. A little farther on, the corridor bent out of sight, but from the way the sound of the iron door opening echoed through the darkness, it was obvious how large the underground structure was.

“Wow. It smells moldy,” Mai murmured.

A sour smell hung in the air. The dampness was like the air deep inside a sunless forest, with a faint chemical taint mixed in.

“What about lighting in here? There’s no power coming in, right?”

“No, but there was this huge generator. It looked like it might still work. If not, I guess we’ll have to do our best with our phone lights. I brought a portable battery, anyway.”

Ryuhei and Yuya passed through the iron door and stepped into the pitch-dark corridor.

All of us carrying lights, we followed them timidly in a line like glowworm larvae.

The floor was laid with old, cheap vinyl building material. Doors lined both walls like a hotel hallway.

Before the corridor turned left, Yuya pointed to a door on the right. A plaque on it read 107.

“The generator’s in here. It didn’t look broken or anything, but…”

He turned the knob and directed his light into the room.

It seemed to be some kind of machine room. Black cables ran all over the walls. They converged at the back of the room and fed into a generator.

It was a self-contained power unit about the size of a bathtub, like the emergency generator I’d seen at the hospital where I once worked part-time. An exhaust pipe rose from it and disappeared through the ceiling. The machine itself looked as though it had been made before I was born, but the several LP gas cylinders connected to it looked comparatively new.

I checked the gauge on one of them. There was still gas left.

Yuya and Ryuhei ran their hands over the generator, trying to figure out how to start it.

When Shotaro saw that neither of them knew what they were doing, he spoke up quietly.

“For now, check that the cylinder and hose are properly connected. Then there should be an engine switch, and after that a starter handle you pull.”

Yuya did as he was told, and a motorcycle-like engine sound filled the room.

The next instant, the fluorescent lights in the ceiling flickered. Then, from the machine room out into the corridor, a bluish white light filled the underground structure.

“Thank God. It really would’ve been rough without lights.”

Sayaka looked around at all our faces as she said it.

With the mood eased by relief, we filed out of the cramped machine room.

There were countless mysteries in this underground structure. Part of me wanted to explore it now that it was lit, but for the moment fatigue outweighed curiosity.

Leading all of us along, Yuya went back a little toward the entrance and opened the door to room 106, opposite the machine room.

“This is the dining hall. Want to take a little break?”

It was a large room. Long tables stood in the narrow, deep space, with chairs packed tightly along them. It was a dining hall that could seat several dozen people.

Hana pulled out the nearest chair.

“Ugh, gross. Is this actually okay?”

It was the cheap sort of chair you’d find in a school. Left underground for who knew how long, the backrest was black with mold and starting to rot.

After brushing off the seat, Hana lowered herself onto it gingerly. It held.

Looking more closely, I saw that the table tops too were cheap veneered plywood. They were worn down as well, and looked dangerous enough to stand on.

At the back of the dining hall there was a sink. When I turned the faucet, it first gave a choking, gurgling sound, then spat out reddish-black water. After I let it run for a while, it gradually turned clear.

“Huh,” I murmured without thinking. “The water still works.”

There was a cupboard above the sink. Inside it, old thick-walled plates and cups stood in great numbers.

Sayaka was kneeling by the wall, fiddling with something.

“Oh, wow. The outlets work too. Look.”

She had plugged her phone charger into an outlet connected to exposed wiring.

For a while we stayed in the dining hall, stretching and yawning.

There was hardly any conversation. It was the kind of time when you simply let the fatigue drain out of your body, like when you finally reach the mountain hut where you’ll spend the night on a long traverse. Since we had gathered the day before, this was the moment that made me nostalgic for our student days most of all.

But the room we were resting in held too ominous an air to stretch out in carefree comfort. After a while, Shotaro tapped me on the shoulder.

“Shuichi, shall we have a look around the place? It’s quite an interesting structure.”

I had just begun to think that I ought to eat something, but I was curious about the place too.

At that point Yuya, who had been looking vaguely awkward, cut in.

“Oh, Shotaro—if you want, I can show you around a bit. I looked all over the place last time.”

So he joined us, and the three of us left the dining hall and began exploring the underground structure.

Taken all together—the low ceilings, the dim fluorescent light, the dirty floors, the walls made of cheap, drab materials, the wiring spread everywhere—the place had the atmosphere of an old freighter.

Not just the atmosphere, either. Its scale and structure were ship-like too. The building had been made by welding iron plates onto a framework of steel beams. It was long and narrow and arranged on three levels, with rooms lining the corridor on both sides—some apparently storerooms, others fitted with simple steel-pipe bunk beds.

Each door bore a room number plaque, like an apartment. If you went back to the entrance and stood with your back to the iron door, the room on the right was 101. The left was 102. As you went farther down the corridor, the numbers advanced—103, 104, and so on. Every room, whether storeroom or living quarters, had a number. Every door also had an irregular gap between it and the wall. The whole place was crudely built.

Room 104, beside the dining hall, was the toilet. It had four stalls, the same sort you’d find in a public building. I had no desire to use it, but there was a shower booth attached.

The smell was trapped in there, but not strongly enough to be unbearable. Apparently, because the place had gone unused for so long, the decomposition of the waste had progressed.

“There’s no sewer line out here, right? How did they deal with this?”

“They probably collected it in a holding tank first and pumped it up to the surface,” Shotaro said, peering into one of the squat toilets. “The wastewater was probably handled the same way.”

That was enough of the toilet. We went back into the corridor.

Past room 107, the machine room, the corridor turned left. At the corner there was a metal staircase leading down to the second basement level.

We ignored the stairs for the moment and went farther down the corridor. Five meters later it bent right again. Doors lined both sides there too. Beyond the machine room, the numbering continued from 108, and the last room at the far end was 120.

On the outer wall, there were places where dark rock showed through. It had the same feel as the cave passage we had descended through from the entrance. Water seemed to be seeping in here and there; the surface was damp.

Apparently this underground structure had been made by taking a natural underground cavity, shaping it, creating levels, and setting partition walls in place until it became a building. The corridors bent at unnatural angles because the original contours of the ground had been respected.

When we reached the dead end, Shotaro said, with the air of someone who had finished looking through a museum exhibit, “Twenty rooms. It must have cost a fair bit. They really did build it. Wildly illegal construction, of course.”

“This isn’t even all of it. There’s still the second basement too.”

Yuya went on ahead and led us back to the stairs.

The second basement was laid out much the same way as the floor above. A lightning-bolt-shaped corridor ran through it, with doors stretching endlessly along both sides. This level had no large room like the dining hall, but the space beneath the toilet on the first basement was occupied by the holding tank, taking up a full room. Here too, Yuya explained, there were twenty rooms, numbered 201 to 220.

The fluorescent lights were on down here as well. But on the left after you came down the stairs—the lower-numbered side of the floor—the lights were out. The fixtures were there, so perhaps a wire had been cut somewhere. Since lights still worked at the far end, that section might have been on a different circuit.

Once we were downstairs, before we started opening doors, Shotaro asked,

“Yuya. How was it you found this place in the first place?”

“Oh—uh, about six months ago, totally by accident. I’d come way out into the mountains planning to solo camp somewhere absolutely nobody would ever come, and then I found that hatch. So I went in, and it was unbelievable.”

Standing in the middle of the corridor, Yuya spread both arms.

“What even is this place, anyway? Who built something like this out here, and why? Honestly, it feels pretty obvious it was used for some bad stuff, doesn’t it?”

Shotaro thought for a moment, then answered.

“Most likely this was a radical group’s hideout from fifty years ago.”

“Seriously? Radicals? You mean, like, seventies stuff?”

“At a glance, that’s about when it was built. There was that very large rock halfway down from the entrance, wasn’t there? The one wrapped in chain. That was clearly left there on purpose to be used as a barricade in an emergency. They would have used it to block the iron door with that rock.

“But after the radicals, it looks as though some other criminal group or something used the place too. Some of the wiring seems to have been put in comparatively recently—twenty years ago at most. Radicals wouldn’t still have been carrying on down here that late.”

I had been suspecting something along those lines myself.

If you built a structure underground, deep in the mountains, it could only be to keep it out of sight. But once someone said it outright, the place became even more unsettling.

Shotaro said brightly, “Well, let’s take a closer look.”

We tried room 208 first. It seemed to be a junk room.

We rummaged through its contents. There was no end to the odds and ends—used work gloves, rusty sickles for cutting brush, old speakers, copper piping, scraps of wood. Some things looked very old; others not so old. It all resembled the kind of rubbish piled up at a curbside dump.

“Oh?” Shotaro said with a straight face, holding up a broad-brimmed, battered straw hat for me to see. “So criminal hideouts have straw hats too.”

“Yeah. I kind of thought maybe we’d find pistols or white powder or something, but it’s not really that kind of place.”

“They’d have taken those with them when they cleared out,” Shotaro said. “Assuming the people who used this place dealt in that sort of thing at all. Though if we searched every corner, we might still turn up something.”

He tossed the straw hat carelessly onto a broken wooden crate.

Then he opened the door to room 209, diagonally opposite.

At first glance it too appeared to be a room where junk had simply been thrown together. There was less of it than in 208, but refuse-like items had been swept into the corners.

But when we turned on the room light, what we found there was nothing like the ordinary, harmless clutter from the other room. When I had heard that criminals used the place, what I had imagined was weapons or drugs. What we actually found was something darker than that.

The first thing that caught the eye was a restraint rig: a long chain fitted with handcuffs and ankle shackles. At the back stood a pitch-black iron chair with a strangely pointed seat.

There was also a club made from a thick length of wood wrapped in leather, and a device I could not guess how to use: a metal frame large enough to fit around a human head, equipped with vise-like fittings. There were rusted nails and concrete blocks as well.

Yuya, Shotaro, and I all looked at one another with the awkwardness of people who had accidentally glimpsed someone else’s secret.

Yuya walked to one corner of the room, crouched, and, without touching the implements, let out a groan.

“No way. Jesus. These are torture tools, right?”

“Sure look like it,” said Shotaro.

Apparently Yuya had not seen this room on his previous visit.

“Do you think they were actually used?”

“I mean, I dunno, but doesn’t it look like it? I’ve only ever seen this kind of stuff in museums. Hard to believe this kind of thing even exists.”

The torture implements were old and rusty. There were no traces of dried blood on them, but they were too worn to have been merely tasteless decorations. I looked around at the floor. There were torn-looking gashes in the vinyl flooring. They looked as though they might have been made by someone writhing in agony and clawing at the ground.

I had heard of cases where internal conflicts within radical groups in the seventies escalated into killings. If the history of this underground structure was what we imagined, the discovery of torture implements was not all that surprising.

All at once, the sterile underground structure seemed to exude a bloody kind of menace.

“—But there’s no proof they were actually used, right?”

“No,” said Shotaro. “And even if they were, it was a very long time ago. By now they’re historical relics.”

That seemed to reassure Yuya a little.

I too decided to lean on his words and not think too much about what might once have happened here. Torture implements had of course never had anything to do with my life until now, and no matter how things turned out, surely they never would.

After that we looked into several nearby rooms, but nothing turned up more alarming than the torture equipment.

“By the way, Yuya, you said this place has three basement levels, didn’t you? Where do you go down to the third?”

There was no staircase leading to the third basement anywhere in the corridor.

“Oh, uh, the stairs are at the very end, but for various reasons you can’t go down there anymore. We can go take a look, though. Once you see it, you’ll get it.”

Yuya went on ahead to guide us.

We headed down the corridor toward the lower-numbered rooms. The lights in that stretch of corridor were out. It wasn’t too dark to walk, but we switched on our phone lights anyway.

At the dead end we saw another iron door. It was like the one at the entrance on the first basement, but smaller and narrower.

Yuya pointed to it.

“Look, this is probably directly under the entrance we came in through.”

Tracing back the route we had taken, he was almost certainly right.

Yuya opened the iron door slowly.

Inside was a space unlike any other room we had seen. Just past the entrance it narrowed like the mouth of a bottle, and beyond that the chamber opened out, all of it bare black rock. Only the ceiling near the entrance had been boarded over and made deliberately low. Everywhere else it remained raw stone. This room alone had the look of a natural cave.

And against the wall at the back there stood something like a hoist, the kind of device one might think had been used on a sunken ship.

“What is this? Rusted as hell.”

A thick chain was wound around the hoist. Following it with our eyes, we saw that it branched, passed through pulleys, and disappeared upward through the boarded ceiling near the door, toward the first basement.

“Oh! Is that the chain that was wrapped around that huge rock?”

“Yes,” said Shotaro. “I told you the rock was a barricade.”

If the hoist were turned, the boulder would be dragged into place and the iron door on the first basement blocked shut.

“Right. Now that you say it, it’s obviously a barricade. I wasn’t really thinking. So that’s why the ceiling’s boarded over only near the entrance—to pass the chain through.”

“Yes, that’s part of it,” Shotaro said meaningfully. “There may be another reason as well.”

“Yeah, and the stairs down to the third basement are in this room. But the bottom’s no good. Here.”

Yuya pointed to the back right corner of the chamber.

A square opening had been cut into the floor. The stairs were there.

When we stepped closer and looked down, what Yuya meant became obvious at a glance.

The third basement was flooded. The water had risen to just shy of the ceiling of the floor below, up to about the fourth stair down. Crouching and stretching as far as I could, I managed to touch the black, glossy surface with my fingertips.

“Cold. Seriously? So it’s flooded.”

“It’s underground,” said Shotaro. “And it was built by amateurs, so a little flooding is only to be expected. Surrounded by natural rock like this, it’s inevitable, really. The drainage system is probably broken too. This may be why the place was abandoned.”

That made sense. I had already seen water running down the outer wall on the first basement.

“Well, so much for calling this a mansion with a pool. Honestly, this is a little scary. Doesn’t this mean the whole place is definitely going to end up underwater someday if this keeps up?”

Yuya answered.

“Yeah, in theory, but it’s gotta take a while. It doesn’t look that different from when I was here six months ago. Maybe there’s a little more water, but if the whole place goes under, wouldn’t that be like five years from now?”

That too seemed reasonable enough.

When I shone my phone light into the water, I could make out what looked like rebar and steel beams crusted with concrete, left lying around in disordered heaps on the submerged floor below.

There was nothing else to see there, so we went back into the corridor and started walking back the way we had come.

When we got near the stairs, we saw Sayaka from behind on the far side of the corridor, holding up her phone camera. Out of simple curiosity, she seemed to be taking pictures.

“Yuya, is that hatch the only way in and out of this underground structure? It seems unlikely there’d be just one.”

“No, there’s one more, technically, but you can’t use it. It’s down on the third basement. There’s a narrow shaft from there up to the surface, kind of like a dust chute, but it’s flooded, so there’s no getting through.”

“I see.”

“Oh, right. There’s something like a floor plan in the machine room. That’ll be quicker.”

The three of us went back to the machine room.

Yuya opened a drawer in the desk there. Inside was a jumble of old adhesive bandages, nail clippers, pencils, ballpoint pens, and other odds and ends.

He scooped handfuls of them out onto the desktop. At last, mixed in among them, he found a large A2 sheet folded into quarters.

“Ah, this is it. Guess I shoved it pretty far back in there last time.”

It was a diagram of the whole structure, though not quite clear enough to call a proper guide map. The paper, apparently dating back to when the building was first constructed, had yellowed badly.

Across the top of it, apparently added later in ballpoint pen, was written the word Ark.

That seemed to be the name of the place.

As we had already seen, the Ark was a long, narrow, three-level structure bent in the middle into a reverse-Z shape. According to the diagram, the third basement, unlike the first and second, was not divided into small rooms, but consisted only of several large chambers. The entrance we had come through was on the west side, and on the east side of the third basement there appeared to be another exit leading to the surface.

“This is probably some kind of emergency exit,” Yuya said. “It connects to the surface with a hatch just like the one we came in through. Actually, we passed the hatch for the emergency exit on the way here. I’m guessing nobody noticed.”

He said it had been just beyond the bridge. I certainly hadn’t noticed it, and neither, apparently, had Shotaro. By the time we passed there it had already started getting dark, so that was hardly surprising.

“Did you look down the emergency exit too, Yuya? Is this diagram accurate?”

“Yeah. I climbed down that side too, just to check. There’s something like a ladder, and I got all the way down to near the ceiling of the third basement, but the third level was full of water. So I just went back up.”

“Speaking of exits,” I said, because something had been bothering me, “what are these?”

I pointed at the desk.

There were two LCD monitors there, old fifteen-inch ones like the sort an elementary-school library might have had. Scrawled across the bezels in marker were the words Entrance and Emergency Exit.

“Oh! Yeah, that bugged me when I came before too. I think they’re probably monitors for surveillance cameras. See—Entrance and Emergency Exit. In daylight I spotted what looked like cameras near the hatches. They’re mounted up in trees. There’s one by the west entrance and one by the east emergency exit.

“So I’m guessing these show the feeds. But last time there wasn’t any electricity, so I couldn’t check.”

Talking fast, Yuya turned on the two monitors one after the other.

“Oh, looks like they’re still alive? …Whoa, they work.”

With a faint mechanical buzz, the old monitors lit up with surveillance footage.

Night had already fallen completely, so the images were unclear, like a woodcut print. The cameras, like the monitors, seemed old and were far from sharp.

Even so, it was clear they showed the dead field above ground. In both images, under the moonlight, a hatch-like shape was faintly visible near the middle. If anyone came close to either the entrance or the emergency exit, you would know at once.

Tracing the hatches on the two screens with his finger, Yuya said,

“Right, this one’s the entrance we came through. And this one’s the emergency exit by the bridge.”

“So these two are about a hundred meters apart, then.”

“Yeah, about that. East and west. Maybe it was so if one got discovered by outsiders, they could escape from the other?”

That was Yuya’s answer to Shotaro.

Installing those cameras must have taken a fair amount of effort. A little appalled, I said,

“They were pretty thorough. Seriously, what kind of people were using this place?”

“Could just as easily have been some new religious sect practicing some kind of special discipline,” Shotaro replied. “If the cameras were really for watching for intruders from outside, the placement is odd. They look more like they were meant to stop people from escaping.”

It was a fairly persuasive theory. I studied once more the word written at the top of the yellowed plan: Ark.

“This is from Noah’s Ark in the Old Testament, right?”

“I can’t think of any other source for the name.”

I remembered idly flipping through the Bible in a cultural anthropology lecture back in college. The famous story of Noah’s Ark appears almost at the very beginning of the thick Old Testament.

When the world had fallen into disorder and violence filled the earth, Noah, a righteous man, received a revelation: God had decided to destroy humankind. Noah was told to build an ark and prepare for the flood. Once the ark was finished and Noah, his family, and male and female of every living creature had entered it, the flood came upon the earth. That was the story. In the original text, it is rather spare as narrative, but in sermons, novels, and films based on it, people often mock Noah and his family for building an ark on a mountain, refusing to believe a flood will ever come.

A ship-like structure built deep in the mountains. Whether the name had been added later or not, Ark made sense. To the radicals, or the cultists, or whoever had used the place, perhaps it had been a place to await salvation.

To me, it was nothing but a bad joke. There was no salvation anywhere in this sinister underground structure. The only thing we had found was torture equipment.

Nor was there anyone in our group who was especially devout or held strong political convictions. Apparently we were not Noah’s family, but the people who would laugh at those who had built the ark.

“Huh? What are you doing?”

Hearing us talking in the machine room, Sayaka poked her head in through the still-open door.

A moment later Hana appeared as well, apparently having been looking for Sayaka.

“Oh, there you are. Sayaka, were you taking pictures or something?”

“Yes. I mean, I probably won’t ever come to a place like this again, so I thought I’d keep some as a souvenir.”

It seemed she had been peeking into different rooms and photographing them.

“Well, I don’t mind, but you should probably not put them online or anything. If whoever used this place saw them, that could get annoying.”

“Oh, true. Then I won’t. I’ll just take them.”

While Sayaka and Hana were talking, Mai and Ryuhei came in together.

Apparently the four of them had all grown curious about the interior and gone off exploring on their own. Now all seven of us ended up gathered in the machine room.

They looked surprised to see the monitors switched on.

Yuya repeated for them the same explanations he had already given Shotaro and me—about the flooding on the third basement, the emergency exit, and the surveillance cameras.

“—Okay, fine, I get it,” Hana said, cutting him off. “But actually, I kind of want to go outside for a second. My boyfriend got in touch with me earlier today, and if I don’t reply by tonight, he might come over to my place while I’m away.”

As she said it, she rubbed the phone in her hand.

Yuya scratched his head.

“I mean… signal’s probably pretty bad around here.”

“Yeah, so if it doesn’t work, I’ll give up. But would somebody come with me?”

It was pitch dark out in the mountains. Anyone would be afraid to go alone.

“In that case, want me to come?”

But Hana seemed distinctly unhappy at the thought of going outside alone with Yuya.

Picking up on that, Sayaka immediately stepped in to help.

“Then maybe I’ll go too. I probably have work messages waiting anyway. Is that okay?”

“Oh, you’ll come too, Sayaka? Thanks. That’s better.”

That settled it. Hana, Sayaka, and Yuya went out for a while.

Once the three of them had left, Ryuhei stared over my shoulder at the dark monitor images.

“Does that thing actually show anything?”

From a distance the picture was dim enough that it could have been a dead screen with a bad connection.

But before I could answer, the image changed.

“Oh. That’s Hana and the others.”

On the monitor marked Entrance, the three of them appeared climbing out through the hatch in search of signal.

The leading figure waved at the camera. That would be Yuya. The one behind in the flashy jacket was Hana. Behind her came Sayaka. Their faces were impossible to make out in the darkness, of course.

A little later, the camera feed from the emergency exit also showed the three of them walking by with phone lights in hand. They seemed to be crossing the wooden bridge and heading for higher ground to see whether they could catch a signal.

“Huh. So it really does work.”

Ryuhei sounded satisfied.

After that, he and Mai idly handled some of the things Yuya had dumped onto the desk. Before long they seemed to get bored, and Ryuhei took Mai’s hand. The two of them left the machine room.

Shotaro and I stayed where we were, staring vaguely at the monitors.

Out of sheer restlessness, I asked my cousin something pointless.

“Do you think anyone’s eaten yet?”

“Who knows.”

They had all seemed too preoccupied with looking around the place. Maybe no one had eaten after all.

A little over thirty minutes later, Yuya and the others came back. Thanks to the cameras, we knew at once that they were returning.

But there was something odd about them.

“Huh? Aren’t there more of them? …That can’t be right.”

The figures on the Entrance monitor had gone from three to six.

It was like something out of a horror movie. Shotaro and I left the machine room and went to meet the group, who had clearly run into something, at the iron door.

Yuya came in first through the iron door.

Then Hana. There was mud on her jacket; apparently she had fallen.

Then Sayaka.

And behind her, a frightened family of three.

The father had a gray-flecked crew cut, thick black-rimmed glasses, and looked to be in his fifties. The mother was slightly heavyset and wore her hair short. The son, with somewhat thick lips, looked around middle-school age.

The moment Sayaka saw our faces, she explained first thing.

“Um, these people say they got lost, and so… we had them come with us. Since there was at least someplace here with a roof. You were out mushroom-picking, right?”

The father answered,

“Yes, something like that. Sorry.”

It was mushroom season, true, but even so, they had come remarkably far into the mountains.

Not that this place was exactly ours, but in any case, we ended up inviting them into the dining hall.
