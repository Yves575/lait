To loss.

And to the birds as well.




Sweet Cherry


Dunaj howls. And they are here already, just as I remember them—hungry, restless. They shine as if their wings had been doused in gasoline; violet, blue, red, yellow iridesce over them.

The flock circles above the village.

June has taken in a great deal of sun; already at its very beginning the fruit on the cherry trees is sweet.

Grandfather—who avoids grandmother’s illness, to whom the ceremonies around illness are alien, who wants to know nothing about illness, touch nothing in illness—watches from the veranda as the starlings swoop low over the orchard.

He strikes an aluminum milk can with a metal rod. The boom rolls through the valley.

I watch him. It must be booming inside him too; his body trembles, his skin is like the skin of the men who live along the Carpathian Arc, I think. Bronze. Ebony.

The starlings take off in a surge. Flying over the house, they cast a shadow on the floor of the west room, the one the cast-iron stove warms in the cold months.

A moment later they are back above the orchard.

They’re stubborn.

Each fresh blow on the metal sets them jerking nervously. The booming keeps them in the air.

Grandfather pauses for a cigarette. They use the silence and land in the broad-crowned tree. They stuff their small bodies with fruit.

They’ve already learned to recognize silence, I say to Ann.

Yes, she says.



Ann is brightness here. She is interested in light and is light herself. This morning she arrived here, straight into warm May, from a distant country. The journey is still in her. There is still a scent in her that isn’t from here—freesia, patchouli. She stands with me on the veranda, getting her eyes used to early summer.

Her eyes follow the light.

Ann knows light.

She studies light.

She hunts light.

She reads omens in light.

I touch her at the neckline with my fingers, where constellations of moles grow.

Ann says, We have to count them.

All right, I say.

As a child I counted grandmother’s marks. She hid little from me. She let me count the dots that covered her back, and the ones around her breasts as well. She let my gaze into recesses like the shell of her ear or the crooks of her elbows.

You too are from my body, she used to say.

We move from the veranda into the west room. You can hear the tune grandfather is beating out. Grandmother watches the shadow of the flock of birds. She raises her emaciated hand as if volunteering an answer.

There, she says, pointing at the wardrobe.

She asks me to take her summer dresses out of it. It surprises me. Day to day she dresses in the pajamas I buy her at the market in the nearby town of Stary Sad. She likes the powder-mint ones.

I spread the dresses out on the sofa bed.

That one, grandmother whispers.

She has chosen the one the color of blood. I take it in my hands. Velvet.

Take it to him, she says.

I do it without a word of protest.



On the veranda grandfather is finishing off another Club—yes, out of nostalgia for what is gone, that’s what he calls every cigarette; I got it from him. He takes the dress in his hands. The dry skin of his fingers snags on the fabric. He smells the cloth.

Róża, he says.

I look at him. The last few years have dried out his body. More and more often his gaze runs away, hides, dodges. He can’t hold a look or return one. I would like to see him stronger, I think.

He turns and says, Come with me.

I go.

He heads for the workshop.

There he knocks together out of hazel something like a cross, and clothes this cross in grandmother’s blood-red dress. He studies his work. He adjusts the little foam pads that give the object an expression of surprise, as if something alive were lifting its arms.

Will it do? he asks.

It will do, I say.

I know he needs my assent. Here, in this illness, there is no room for objection.

Grandfather nods at my nodding. He takes the dummy in his hands and goes into the orchard with the dress-clad skeleton. Behind him, a procession: Dunaj the dog and Klakier the tomcat.

Grandmother watches us from the window of the west room. With her nails she scratches at the skin on her cheek. I know the impulse. It is born in her whenever she watches something. She can scratch herself all over and know nothing about the scratching.

Grandfather sets the ladder against the trunk of the cherry tree. I steady it. Carefully he places his feet on the rungs. He reaches the top. With wire he fastens the scare to a branch.

A scare against hunger, I murmur.



Grandmother’s red dress begins to move in the wind. A gust enters it and fills the place where a warm body once was. I have the impression the dress has come alive.

Spirit of the orchard, I say to Ann, who joins us.

Nothing works like a red dress, she hums.

The starlings wheel above the tree.

Back in the west room, I ask grandmother whether she remembers wearing that thing last spring. There are photographs.

She nods.

The red brought out her gray hair.

You know, she says, the illness must already have been in me then. Can you imagine it?

I nod.

With my fingertips I examine the wound she has scratched into her cheek.

For the rest of the day we take turns going to the window of the west room and looking out at the orchard. The blood-red of the dress blends with the cherries ripening in full sun. The fruit saved from the starlings’ beaks we will gather for grandmother. Grandfather will make sweet jam from them.





West Room


We freshen the room on the west side of the house for the illness. In my childhood it served us for years as a roomy cold store. We kept there the meat of pigs slaughtered for church holidays. My grandparents stored furniture there, old pictures, jars of preserves, cans of honeydew honey, boxes filled with Christmas ornaments. After the tragedy, the one from many years ago that befell my parents and my aunt, grandmother has her lair here.



Soon we’ll have radiator sections in here, grandfather says, standing in the doorway.

Yes, I nod.

Grandmother mustn’t be cold in her illness, he says.

Yes, yes, I nod.

I can still agree to the cold, but take that pink out of here, grandmother says.

Yes, yes, I nod.

Pink reminds me of meat, she says.

Yes, I nod.

Make it white in here, she asks.

I nod.

I am obedient. That is why I came back here from far away, why I left my life in the ever-bright city: to be obedient.

Obedience, I say to Ann.

Obedience, she repeats, running a hand over my hair.





Deal


We begin freshening the room. As if the illness required special means and measures from us.

We’re collaborating with it, cutting a deal, I think.

Now it has us too, I say to Ann, handing her the overalls I wore here as a teenager.

The two of us work. The illness binds us in physical labor. We need to get tired.

Grandmother’s eyes are watchful.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us and look, look.

Let’s work ourselves to death, I say.

Every little while grandmother peers into the room, nods, and looks, looks. She likes that against the white walls the plants and holy pictures will show clearly.

Make it white in here, she asks again.

The instructions are simple—white.

We scrape away the old layers of paint with a putty knife. We pass the tool back and forth when pain reaches the arms and forearms of the one who is working. I taste dust in my mouth; it grits between my teeth.



I hold the ladder Ann is standing on. She grapples with the wall. Her arms are bare, the powder settles on them. You can see her muscles working clearly. She is beautiful when she moves the putty knife. She strips away the layers of paint laid on here over the last thirty years. A great deal can be read from them. Written in them are the great occasions that required the room to be freshened: a christening, a communion, a confirmation, and now illness. I recognize every color as it comes down.

Perhaps if someone began to study them, more could be read from them still, the way changes in the environment are read from a soil profile.

The history of this house falls to the floor.

We make room for the new.

Ann is beautiful when she tightens her body, sets her thoughts in motion, talks as she works, when everything she is goes into motion.

We need what is bright, I say to Ann, stirring the paint.

Yes, she nods.

We muck about in white.

At night, while the room dries, I go in to look at the interior.

Hooo-hooo, hooo-hooo, I hoot.

Behind me I hear Ann.

Hooo-hooo, hooo-hooo, she repeats after me.

The sounds tremble in those dozen or so square meters.

We rasp from the dust. It’s in our throats. We spit white.

In the morning we show grandmother the room.

White, she says.

She presses her cool hands to our faces and strokes us the way she strokes Dunaj when he comes home after days of roaming.

Grandmother begins carrying plants into the west room.

Life, life, she says.



After two pots of calathea she weakens. She sits down on the floor and leans against the wall. Without a word we take over her work, become her hands. We carry from the hallway into the west room the plants put there for the duration of the renovation: aloe, asparagus fern, fern, geranium, Siberian ivy, Christmas cactus, philodendron, croton, dracaena, begonia, caladium.

Grandmother’s eyes are watchful.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us.

Grandmother’s eyes look, look.





Humpbacked


When the smell of paint is gone, the three of us spend a night in the west room. We still don’t know it will stay this way, that the nights to come will be shared. But not yet. For now the moon is waxing.

There it is, swelling, humpbacked, I think.

I am getting used to closeness.

We divide the bed and the sofa bed between us.

We strip off the daytime layers of clothes.

We mix our scents.

My women, I think as I fall asleep, are beautiful and smell, smell.





Throat


In the room I can hear grandmother’s breathing and Ann’s. Night has stilled the pupils that dart by day. Ever since the illness moved in with us, I am the last to fall asleep.

Only my vigil is strong, I think. No one else can keep watch this long.

In the corner the cast-iron stove gives off heat. The last days of June have brought chill here, into the valley.

It will begin any moment.

Light.



Light cuts across our faces. I look at Ann, whose face is lit by the headlights of a truck carrying cattle to slaughter. She opens her eyes. And they are deep, bottomless. They look at the waking world, and when they notice me looking, they stop.

For a moment Ann keeps watch with me.

But she cannot keep vigil the way I can.

She slips back into sleep.

And now, oh-ho, it begins. A few dozen meters from the west room, big trucks take the bend, their headlights filling the interior with light.

Light and dark.

Light and dark.

Light.

Dark.

Light.

This will last a million years.

I have time, I think.

The trucks bring animals to the slaughterhouse. I imagine the bodies of cows—warm, wet.

As a child I did not always manage to wait up for this moment in the night. Sometimes I fell asleep and it was the spectacle already under way that woke me. With it came sounds like tonight’s: throaty, drawn-out sounds that, instead of dissolving into space, can hang over the village for a long time.

The bellowing of cows.

The whining of dogs.

The hooting of the night.

And this too—the smell: manure, urine, sweat.

The bodies of the women close to me are easy to see when the headlights of the Jelcz trucks sweep slowly over them. Depending on the falling light, and on the shadows, their features are dead one moment, alive the next.

Grandmother lies on her side, her hand tucked beneath her left cheek. She looks like a child. A child, the kind they would have wanted me to bear.



But now is no time for life. Death—that occupies my mind. That is what I serve. Grandmother is my child. I am the mother of my grandmother. That is good, I think.

Ann’s body is beside me. I never stop being astonished by the porcelain of her skin, the whiteness of her hair, eyelashes, brows. There is something twinlike in us, and there is also—and we know it—something that binds us: so much in us that is prodigal.

The bodies of the women close to me react to the light; beneath their eyelids the eyeballs move.

One moment more.

They open their eyes.

One after the other.

Grandmother looks and whispers into the light: Water or fire will deal with it.

Ann, woken again, sets her bare feet on the floorboards; her nails are painted dark burgundy. Mine are the same. We paint them for each other now, no longer for our people, who are far away. They have to stay far away; I do not let them come close to the illness. I belong to the illness, not to them. Only Ann fits here. She can plunge nails painted like that into manure or compost, wallow in it.

Sisters in what decays, I think of us.

Ann pulls a pack of Clubs from the pocket of grandfather’s coat she has put on.

Come, she says to me.

I throw a blanket over myself and go. Grandmother watches the lights running across the ceiling.

It is possible, I think, that she has fallen asleep again, but left her gaze awake. She is most frightening then, when she looks in such a way that you can’t tell whose eyes they are.

We go out onto the veranda. I feel the cold.

A concert, I say to Ann.



She hands me a lit cigarette.

It’s beginning, I say.

Yes, she nods.

Oh-ho, already.

We listen.

Hooves know no rhythm, I say.

After a moment the animals begin to sing.

They’re singing, I say.

Ann presses a finger to my lips and with that gesture stops the cigarette smoke inside me.

The animals sing. Their song comes from within, from warm bellies, I think.

After a moment the dogs answer. First the wolf-spitz at Rybowicz’s below the slope on the far side of the village, then the throats of the slaughterhouse dogs begin, a moment later Dunaj joins in, after him Sołtysowa’s Samoyeds and the dogs chained beside the presbytery kennels, descendants of those restless mutts that have been crossing on the village margins for generations. They bear the names of apostles: Marek, Jan, Łukasz. There is also one black one, christened Murzyn by the village.

Strange, I think, that I can tell these howls apart, the melody of every dog. A little longer here and I’ll be able to guess what these howls are for, what purpose they serve.

Ann takes her finger from my lips.



I say: You know, I always wanted to be the bitch of this village, to have all of May to myself. To know no boundaries. I was tempted by being far from what is human. To have a dog’s body and act in keeping with it, yield to hunger, deny myself nothing. To fawn only on the chosen few, nip and bark at everything else. To be a choosy bitch. To love one human being, to have obligations toward that person, to obey. And one more thing: to disappear for many days. To have the right to it, to make use of it.

Make use of it, Ann repeats.

The barking carries and wakes more sounds.

The whole village howls.

That night this place is the deep throat of a hungry animal, I think.





Faith


At night the slaughterhouse wakes the birds. The valley pays the consequences. Time gets mixed up. Something comes loose in time. People open their eyes at the wrong hour, move through the day dazed. At noon they look into the sun and can’t make sense of where it is in the sky.

Grandfather gets mixed up worst of all. Grandfather gets up with the birds. Wakefulness catches him by surprise.

I wake up and it’s empty, he tells me when it happens.

Empty? I ask.

Empty, yes, there’s nothing to catch hold of, nothing in which to recognize yourself, he says.



He gets up in the night and walks through the house. He clambers up the ladder to the attic and paces there. Putting one foot after another soothes him. Stroking the antlers he has been collecting for decades soothes him too. When night and day get mixed up for grandfather, he can fall asleep there.

In the mornings, when I go to take my place at the desk to work on code—clean, intuitive, beautiful code, the kind that sometimes comes to me in dreams—I often find him there among the deer racks. Grandfather has a story for every set of antlers. He invents lives for those bony tissues. To make him tell such a story, you have to know how to approach him, you have to praise the collection in the attic. His stories about deer are full of animal panic, full of forest; they happen in rain and fog. Only out of weather like that do animals step from grandfather’s stories.





Rivers


Waking birds is a sin, grandmother says.

In the orchard that belongs to the slaughterhouse, for years nothing has been born. The ground there is trampled by herds of cows. Most of the animals are unloaded at the top of the hill and led straight into the concrete walls. Those for which there is no room in the cool pens are let into the orchard. They take advantage of it, empty their deep bellies, piss as if they had endless rivers inside them. Urine in such quantities burns the soil; trees have a hard time growing there.

The orchard is naked, stunted, sick.

It’s because of the damp from inside the cows, I say.

Fire or water will deal with it, grandmother says.

I believe her.



The greengage trees in that orchard haven’t borne fruit in years, she says.

I nod.





Sweet


Grandmother cannot resist living things. Anything alive she would take into the west room, invite in. She would like the living to move before her eyes, to stir, to scrabble, to flutter.

Let it parade, she says.

Let it fly, she says.

Let it be, she says.

Grandmother has adored living things forever, that is, for as long as I can remember. You could, I think, trace the generations of all that has multiplied here, in the western part of the house. And if you asked these latest generations, the living ones, where they come from, then probably, if they could talk, they would say: from the cold room of that human, that little human, Róża.

She wants, I think, for the living—especially now—to occupy her mind, generate duties, distract her from the illness.

Grandmother studies living things with curiosity. This fondness—as I call it, this weakness—as grandfather calls it, has made her useless so many times.

You were afraid to take Róża out into the fields, grandfather begins, and at once he brings back to me that childhood time.
