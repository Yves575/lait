Fame is my life—the life I knew I would have, the life I made sure I had. Was I prepared for that kind of success? Of course I was. I always thought that what awaited me was not a life but a destiny. My path would be exceptional, my trajectory unlike any other.

When people talk about fame, they think of glitter, glamour, money, devoted fans, prestige, recognition. But they should also mention the constant sense of superiority, the intoxication of wealth, the nonstop commentary, vanity, hypocrisy, impunity. Fame is a hard drug, a ferocious monster. And I went after it with my rage, my nails, my teeth.

Extreme fame unleashed the beast inside me, pitiless and cruel. I may as well say it up front: I got my hands dirty. At my level, everyone has bodies in the closet. Anyone who claims otherwise is a liar. Fame is a war prize—no one is ever ready to give it up.

At thirty-two, I am at the top of a castle I built by myself with my songs. I don’t believe in luck. I don’t believe in an influential network of friends. I don’t believe in glass ceilings. I owe my success to nothing but my talent, my character, and meritocracy. So if I could have been honest on the night of my latest awards ceremony, there is only one person I would have thanked in my long speech: myself.

Three weeks on a desert island in the middle of the Pacific Ocean. A place with no running water or electricity, with no contact of any kind with the outside world. That’s the sort of fantasy fame lets you buy. When you have everything, you have to show a little imagination with your summer vacation.



Yesterday I spent ten hours on a private jet. We landed somewhere near Tahiti or Fiji, unless it was Hawaii. I boarded a second plane, then a seaplane. An hour later, the island appeared: an atoll lost in the vastness.

Ocean as far as the eye can see, white sand, a sumptuous lagoon—I am walking through a postcard. Clouds of seabirds stop over on the rocks, the vegetation is lush, you can tell it rains often here, and the cistern that collects rainwater is full. Coconut palms, banana trees, orange trees: I’m not likely to starve. With my eyes on the open water, I search the horizon for land, for some point of anchorage to rest my gaze on. There is none.



My only shelter for the next three weeks is a hut on the beach. The place is charming, built on stilts, with a terrace facing the ocean. Its tall, sloping wooden frame supports a roof thatched with woven tropical leaves. Inside there is only one room, sparsely furnished: a single bed, a chest of drawers, a table, two chairs. The supplies are stored in a cupboard: rice, fruit, dried fish, tubers, canned goods, a hundred liters of mineral water. This year, I’m giving myself the finest of gifts: to be left the fuck alone.

I have no trouble imagining celebrities paying for this singular experience and cracking after forty-eight hours. Solitude is another kind of hell.

The sun is setting over the ocean for the first time. My suitcase lies open at the foot of my bed, the fire I lit with dead wood crackles on the beach. My guitar on my knees, I stumble over the first words of a verse, a notebook within reach. This heavenly retreat will certainly give me material for my fourth album—I’ve isolated myself on an island, not taken a vow of silence. At nightfall, the sea gives way entirely to the sky, where I discover thousands of constellations whose geometry I don’t recognize. I am somewhere in the Southern Hemisphere.



The flames throw jagged shadows over the sand, and I shiver as I roll the globe I found in the hut beneath my fingertips. It dates from the second half of the twentieth century; it shows vanished countries, acronyms from another age, and I look for borders that don’t yet exist. I run my forefinger along the latitudes and meridians, trying to locate myself on the sphere. In all likelihood, the island isn’t even on it. I am only a dot in the immensity of the Pacific—but where? The outlines of states take shape before my eyes; the nearest continental coasts are probably more than four thousand kilometers from here, somewhere in South America or Oceania.

What am I doing here? More than that, how did I end up here? To calm the vertigo rising inside me, I stroke the scars on my thighs, my scalp, my hands, my arms, my ankles. Each one traces the path of my life, each one another milestone on the way to glory.

There is a price to fame, and it gets paid every day.

Part One


Faith

My name is Cléo Louvent Johnson. My mother is French, my father American; I move effortlessly between the two languages. My mother is a statistician, my father an Egyptologist; I am shaped between numbers and hieroglyphs. I grow up in an apartment full of books in a quiet corner of Paris’s Fourteenth Arrondissement. My father, who affectionately calls me Cleopatra, sticks my drawings of pyramids and sphinxes up in the kitchen with magnets. On Saturdays, my mother takes me to the neighborhood library; on the way back, we stop to say hello to the gray herons in Parc Montsouris.



I am four years old, and my father is telling me a story in my bedroom. My favorite books are the ones he brings to life with his commentary. A specialist in Egypt, passionate about the ancient world in all its forms, he tells me about the misfortunes of Isis and Osiris, their struggle against Set, but also the wanderings of Odysseus, the childish antics of the gods of Olympus; I hang on his every word when he describes the exploits of Ramses II, Pericles, Alexander the Great. Every story becomes a pretext for detours through history and its epics, swarming with characters on extraordinary quests and marvelous animals. It is almost time to sleep; my father tucks me in before switching off the light, when suddenly he senses that something is troubling me. He presses me a little, and I finally confide: “Dad, I wish I were as famous as Céline Dion.”

I have no memory of it, but for years my parents delighted in reminding me of that anecdote, which made them laugh a great deal. I can sense tenderness in it, mingled with surprise. Where on earth did she get that from?

My parents live inside the microcosm of their academic research, their publications, their conferences. They don’t know who the celebrities of the moment are—they would be incapable of recognizing the idols of their age if they passed them in the street. Red carpets, star couples, Hollywood, private jets, celebrity magazines: it is an exotic country where they have no desire to spend a vacation.



At the school gate, my parents look older than everyone else’s. My mother explains to me that her pregnancy lasted not nine months but nine years. I will be their only child. Their difficulty in conceiving is no secret; my Wikipedia page states that I was conceived by in vitro fertilization. For some obscure reason, that fact adds to the magic surrounding my birth: did science really have to step in to create someone as exceptional as me?

I am a little girl with short hair, well-behaved and solitary, slipping plane-tree leaves into the pockets of my coat to make salads for my dolls. My parents hear me talking to myself in my room and notice that I always close the door to play.

What they don’t suspect is that their darling daughter spends her Wednesday afternoons filling notebooks trying to come up with a star’s signature. At seven, I practice signing strings of autographs as fast as I can. The following year, I stage my first interviews. The questions come naturally to me; I work out my answers methodically, in French, in English. At my feet, my stuffed hedgehog, whale, ladybug, and squirrel listen attentively. My menagerie makes a captive audience, the best I could hope for.

I also sing along with my favorite stars, starting the music on the CD player on the floor beside my desk. I know every lyric by heart; I read them off the liner notes, sitting cross-legged on the pale blue rug in my bedroom. As a teenager, the website paroles.net, with its thousands of indexed songs, will take over. I will spend hours there, spellbound.



I have explained my path to journalists so many times. I have recounted my past so many times in interviews. I have taken part in that dangerous game so many times without understanding its gravity.

I romanticized my life, embellished my answers to make myself seem more brilliant, more impressive. I cut from my story, and little by little from my memory, the episodes that didn’t fit my legend. I manufactured false memories. In search of the piquant detail or touching anecdote, I plundered other people’s pasts, my reading, films, until I no longer knew what truly belonged to me.

Memories are fragile; to convince yourself of that, all you need do is recount the same event several times. By the third or fourth telling, the past begins to recompose itself. Little by little, you change the lighting, remove certain details, heighten the dramatic force to captivate your audience. You turn the past into a story, and with each retelling it is the memory that gets damaged. There are anecdotes I have repeated so many times that I no longer know whether they are true or false.

It is tempting to rewrite your life; I did it often. The signs announcing my destiny are legion: the early interviews with my stuffed animals, my extraordinary mezzo-soprano voice, my fascination with song lyrics, my musical ear, my ease at the piano, my guitar teacher’s encouragement, my solid grounding in music theory, my passion for choir. It is so much easier to travel the road in reverse.



I begin music lessons at seven, but make no mistake. Where I come from, the conservatory is what good students do after school. My parents do not dream of making me famous by encouraging me to learn an instrument—we are not in Los Angeles. In my world, music is a bourgeois reflex, not an invitation to become a global pop star.

My desire for fame comes not from my family but from my flaws. All my life, I will seek the admiration of millions of strangers, command the respect of everyone who crosses my path. The baker, the pharmacist, my kindergarten teacher, the neighbor on the third floor. I have only one aim: that they all recognize my superiority.

People talk a great deal about celebrities’ mental disorders. How many become alcoholics, suffer from addictions, sink into depression, kill themselves? Fame drives you mad—and you have to be mad to want to be famous. Public life is a pot of honey that attracts narcissists, perverts, sociopaths. Balanced people have no need to vampirize love on that scale.

I think back to that little girl of four telling her father how sad she was not to be famous. In that thirst to be admired, congratulated, applauded, there was a need that had always exceeded normal proportions.



“When I met Cléo Louvent for the first time, I knew at once she would become famous.” In articles about me, there is always some former teacher or classmate ready to claim that my potential was obvious to everyone. In reality, no one would have bet on me. I am an ordinary child, not especially pretty, shy.

My first report cards are excellent, but I am encouraged to speak up more in class. I hide behind my grades—I owe them to my anxious perfectionism. The moment I cross something out in my school notebook, I tear out the page and start over.

At eight, I skip a grade and find myself seated beside beautiful Juliette Marchand. My new best friend is everything I am not: popular, talkative, loud. Her ease impresses me. My mother thinks she has an unfortunate tendency to make a spectacle of herself; my father feels the same aversion toward people who talk loudly or try to be the center of attention. If I behaved like Juliette at home, I would get a curt: stop trying to make yourself interesting.

Juliette spreads her affection among Lydia, Clémentine, and me. In other words, I am one of her ladies-in-waiting, and at every recess I am afraid of ceasing to be her favorite. When Juliette holds forth on the importance of kissing a boy before starting middle school, I nod silently behind her. She starts calling me Cléou; I hate it, but once again I say nothing.



One Monday in October, Juliette decides to organize a fashion show with several girls from our class. The performance will take place at her house the following Sunday, in front of her parents and their friends. We spend the week talking about it in the schoolyard. At ten years old, I swear that nothing matters more.

The big day arrives. My father drops me off at Juliette’s with my nicest clothes folded in my backpack. To guarantee my place in the show, I pretended I owned a pair of flared pants; once I get there, I act as if I forgot them. In the bathroom, I overhear Lydia saying I’m a nasty little liar: those bell-bottoms do not exist. Clémentine agrees—then adds that I have a deep voice like a boy and hair on my thighs. Juliette comes to my defense firmly: the queen protects her favorites and orders the peace kept. I swallow back my tears on the other side of the door.

Juliette orchestrates the event, chooses the order of appearance, starts the music. Her bedroom serves as backstage; we change in a rush, and each of us gets four turns. She opens the show in a tiered skirt and an orange tank top, her blond hair flowing down her back. Half an hour later, I am breathless with jealousy as she closes the show in a midnight-blue dress with thin straps. Juliette walks confidently under the applause, spins around, throws winks at the delighted adults. On Monday at recess, I steal her snack and spit in her schoolbag.



To become famous, you have to accumulate enough grudges, boil with frustration for months, stew for decades. The years spent in my best friend’s shadow are the ones that will give me the stamina to endure the years spent in the light. It is no coincidence that at the dawn of my fame, I decide to dye my hair blond like Juliette’s. I have a score to settle, and it isn’t only about hair.

At night in bed, I resent my parents for giving me that ridiculously short name. Why Cléo? I would rather have been called Juliette, Mathilde, Émeline, or Éléonore. A girl’s name, a princess’s name, pretty, important (minimum three syllables). I am jealous and petty, and I hope no one comes to Juliette’s birthday party. Or better still, that she tells me her mother has died. And then I imagine my reprisals, stroke them with my fingertips. When I am at the top, I won’t spare a glance for those who underestimated me. I won’t be content with spitting in their bags anymore; I’ll spit in their faces, reduce them to ashes.

I wish my parents had explained it sooner: the children who attract every gaze rarely go on to have the most dazzling adult lives, the most united families, the most impressive careers. The star of elementary school never becomes the global star. Since then, I’ve had occasion to observe it dozens of times: fame isn’t victory, it’s revenge.



I still have very vivid images of the summer I was fourteen: bike rides through the pines with my cousins, snacks on the beach, a rollerblading race. I go with my aunt to buy oysters at the market, I take sailing lessons, I pluck my eyebrows in secret. My diary is hidden in my underwear drawer; I suspect my cousin Violette of reading it while I’m in the shower and taking sadistic pleasure in quoting passages from it during our conversations; doubt creeps into my gaze, and she revels in it. Or so I think.

My mother’s sister owns a second home on Île d’Oléron, and I have spent part of my vacations there for as long as I can remember. The rest of the time, I stay with my grandparents in Aix-en-Provence, camp in Auvergne with my cousins and my uncle, or fly to Boston to visit my father’s family. That summer, my father joins us on Oléron for a week. One afternoon, out of curiosity, I sit down beside him on the couch while he watches the Olympic Games on television.

In front of the screen, I am first swept up by a fierce intuition, almost a revolt: I hate team sports. Handball, volleyball, basketball—making your victory depend on someone other than yourself seems insane to me. How can you not want to burn your teammate alive after a bad performance?



Swimming strikes me as a more reasonable discipline (except relays, obviously). Yesterday, a seventeen-year-old French girl caused a surprise by winning the four-hundred-meter freestyle. Our new national star is only three years older than I am, and this morning she is on every front page at the newsstand. In her interviews, I discover, fascinated, the years of sacrifice, the dawn wake-up calls, the sixteen kilometers of swimming a day, the smell of chlorine clinging to the skin to the point of nausea, the relentless work for a hundredth of a second. Everything rings true: strength of character, tolerance for pain, the need to win. I recognize in it the jagged outline of my own ambition.

I too have something to prove. I too am determined to do ten times more than everyone else. I too am ready to fight. I too am a perfectionist with an enormous capacity for work. I too want to live in a simplified world where the best wins. It’s no accident that I’m always top of my class; I have never yielded my place to anyone. I’m also the most gifted at the conservatory, the best in my piano, guitar, and singing classes. If the target is ten, I deliver a hundred. An overachiever, as my father says. Overachieve or nothing.



And yet I can’t explain this peculiarity. Why do I want so desperately to become famous? To feel special, powerful, loved? Of course I believe I’m different from other people, out of step, misunderstood—but isn’t that the most universal feeling there is when you’re growing up? So how am I supposed to interpret this rage? Did my parents not look at me enough? On the contrary, is it the effect of ambition transferred onto an only child too long awaited and too cherished? Is my thirst for fame the consequence of an innate disposition, an acquired experience, neglect, bullying, inheritance? I don’t know. I have no cause to offer, no psychological, familial, sociological, astrological, mystical, existential explanation. I can’t explain it, and yet I burn. I grew up in the posh neighborhoods of Paris, but I’m a junkyard dog.

“Swimming’s a dog’s sport,” my father remarks just as the hundred-meter backstroke begins.

A minute later, the French swimmer wins her second gold medal.

“Dad, do you think she knew she was going to win?”

“Of course. You need big dreams to do big things.”



In September, I put up the faces of my new idols on my bedroom walls: swimmers, boxers, fencers. I have found my tribe; it is made up of determined, disciplined women who resemble me. As a model for life, Céline Dion had never really worked—and besides, Juliette thinks she’s corny. I still aspire to become a singer, but I don’t recognize myself in what my favorite artists say. When artists talk about creation, it is more about passion, inspiration, and dissolute lives. I want to be a music star because I want to be a machine. And my rise will also be forged through my body.

Alongside my music lessons, I sign up for tennis with Juliette and for dance. I learn to love gesture, repetition, performance. In the evening I do planks; in the morning I step on the scale. I weigh my food scrupulously, count calories, check my protein intake. Each week I plot my weight on a graph drawn on graph paper. The moment I put back on five hundred grams, I run until exhaustion.

Even the best athletes need recovery time. So on Friday nights I rest by turning on my computer. My favorite game is called The Sims.



Charlotte, my current avatar, plays guitar and piano. She has a social life, a fiancé, a Dalmatian. Yesterday she was burgled, and she spent her evening repairing a leak in her bathroom. To develop her charisma, Charlotte talks to herself in the mirror for hours. I also impose workouts on her, a healthier diet, frequent makeovers. Because I’m not doing this blindly, just for fun. I have an objective in mind: make her a star. To have any hope of getting there, she absolutely has to become friends with other famous people and always be in a good mood.

Charlotte works hard. She runs from auditions to concerts to photo shoots. I could have chosen another career; around her, her friends are acrobats, bank robbers, dog trainers, secret agents, fighter pilots. But I won’t be satisfied until Charlotte finally becomes famous. I want a limousine to come pick her up in front of her house, strangers to ask her for autographs in town. Then and only then will I buy her a more comfortable bed, old-master paintings for her bedroom, a shower stall with brand-new silicone seals, a new oven.

As long as I stay top of my class, my parents don’t forbid me to spend so much time on my computer, but that doesn’t mean they approve. “Wouldn’t you rather read a book?”

This game goes on for several weeks. Despite her efforts, my avatar does not become famous. Failure is not an option. It never will be. I lead Charlotte into the garden and build four walls around her. She will die like a medieval recluse: walled in alive.



Juliette has been fighting with her boyfriend in the bathroom for an hour. Tonight we are celebrating my birthday in her father’s apartment on Rue Princesse. The whole high school knows that my best friend throws the best parties. Earlier, we made a chocolate cake and got her older brother to buy alcohol.

Etymology as a pickup move—no doubt about it, I’m at a party in Saint-Germain-des-Prés. And while he talks, I smile inwardly because Nathan reminds me of a stork. He has long thin legs and a tiny little head.

“Cléo is a nod to Cleopatra.”

“Oh right, your father specializes in Egypt, doesn’t he?”

“Yes. He wanted to call me Cleopatra, my mother forbade it, so they compromised on Cléo. But he calls me Cleopatra all the time anyway.”

“Cleopatra is beautiful. It must mean something.”

“Yes, I think it means ‘the father’s pride’—at least that’s what my father keeps repeating. Patros means ‘father’ in Greek.”

“But Cléo—do you know if that means something in itself? Pride?”

“I’ve never really looked.”

At the same time, Juliette comes out of the bathroom with red eyes; as I passed the door, I heard insults and sobbing. How can she waste so much time on this guy? Doesn’t she have anything better to do?

My life is an adventure film. On the evening of her fifteenth birthday, the heroine is told the secret of her origins. Your name means “fame” because your destiny is to become a global star. I grew up hating my name, never suspecting that it contained my prophecy. In all modesty, this discovery makes me understand that I am the chosen one.

For a long time I had sensed that I was different from everyone else without being able to explain that strange intuition. Tonight everything becomes obvious. It isn’t that I admire fame or fantasize about it like an insecure teenager—it’s that I’m going to do it. Fuck, I’m really going to do it.



Fame is not just a sweet childhood dream; a glorious horizon awaits me. I picture myself spinning fortune’s wheel and winning the jackpot, stepping onto the top step of the podium, reaping every honor, a laurel crown on my head. I was born into the wrong cradle, the wrong body, the wrong life. And soon it will be my turn to repair that injustice.

Without thinking, I seize Nathan’s face and kiss him. I drain my glass in one gulp, set it down on the table with a bang, then take Nathan by the hand and lead him to the bathroom. No question of being passive; I’m no longer the good little girl who keeps her mouth shut. This time, I open it wide.

In return, I very much want him to finger me, but the insolent idiot doesn’t move. The yokel. The goof. The weirdo. I finish him off and go back to dancing.

I take my place in the middle of the dance floor, haloed with glory, absolute sovereign. Eyes closed, at the center of the world, I roll my shoulders, sway my hips, the alcohol doubles my boldness, every gaze is fixed on me, I can feel them sliding over my silhouette. I dance in time, magnetic, imperial. I am the queen bee.



I leave the party without saying goodbye to anyone. It isn’t even midnight; I haven’t blown out my candles; they will go on celebrating my arrival on earth without me. Certainty. Certainty. Feeling. Joy. Peace. This night is my Night of Fire, the night of revelation—and what a flash. The drink intensifies my exaltation, the icy wind burns my face, I ride my bike up Boulevard Saint-Michel, pedaling through the night, out of the saddle to climb the hill. I pass along the Luxembourg Gardens, straight on to Avenue de l’Observatoire, my heart pounding with happiness, my birthday ought to be a public holiday. In my earphones, pure jubilation, the world-famous anthem of the World Cup victory, “Samba de Janeiro”; I don’t need to speak Portuguese to understand the euphoria, I let the percussion resound inside me. I run one red light, then a second, weave between the cars around Place Denfert-Rochereau, close my eyes, invincible, let go of the handlebars with one hand, then the other, lift my arms, fly like a bird. I ignore the drivers honking at me; you don’t kill off the main character at the beginning of the movie. In the frozen night, I shout, louder and louder, the four prophetic letters that trace my destiny: C-L-É-O.

When I get home, I spit onto the sidewalk the traces of semen still caught in my gums. My phone rings; Nathan wants to know where I disappeared to. I’m not surprised—he’s already in love. I don’t answer. What would be the point? We can’t be together—it will be too complicated when I’m famous.



The apartment is silent, my parents already in bed, even though I’m home before curfew. I drink a glass of water in the kitchen, gather a few supplies. The night ahead will be long, the longest of all.

At fifteen, I have just received irrefutable proof of my future fame; it is time to understand how my life’s mission will actually come to pass. I open a squared notebook, my handwriting hurried, broken up by arrows, underlined words, sentences in capitals, numbered ideas. Half drunk, I conduct my first skills assessment.

1. My rigor, my perfectionism, my need to be the best, my refusal of laziness and mediocrity: it all makes sense. I will be a top-tier celebrity, not some bargain-basement starlet. I do not aspire to be merely a singer, but an illustrious figure. Like Cleopatra, I am on earth to leave my mark on my era. Tutankhamun, Homer, Plato, Aristotle, Alexander the Great, Caesar, Charlemagne, Shakespeare, Napoleon. The list is short on women; I volunteer.

2. I have a dazzling voice. Hard to ignore; people compliment me as soon as I open my mouth to sing. Besides, I am not just another soprano with a smooth, high, nightingale timbre. My voice is low, husky, gravelly; it is broken, complex, distinctive—my vocal signature will be easy for the public to identify, recognizable above all others. Everyone is going to adore me.

3. I am passionate about poetry, excellent in literary subjects, capable of writing songs in French and English, which will guarantee me international reach. Pretty voices are everywhere: I am worth more than that. I will not be a mere singer of other people’s songs; I will use my pen. My destiny is to become famous by singing my own compositions.



4. Puberty is rarely a gift. For me, it is a blessing. The features of my heart-shaped face are softening, my breasts are rounding out, sport is sculpting my body. I am taller, slimmer, more graceful—the hemmed-in little girl is disappearing, making way for a star’s physique. There is still work to do: change my haircut, find the right lipstick, buy new clothes. But with all that, we’ll have the makings of a beautiful poster.



With the evidence laid out before me, the thing now is not to procrastinate. My action plan is to compose, right away, the song that will make me famous. I search for a melody in a murmur so as not to wake my parents sleeping in the next room. When I come to the chorus, I think of Céline Dion: her mother and brother wrote her first song when she was thirteen. Unfortunately, I can’t turn to my family for help. If only, failing that, they could at least have opened the doors of show business for me. I would have loved to have a mother who was a director, a father who was an actor. How can they be content with lives so tiny? No one knows them, no one will remember their names—and you do not become a millionaire hunched over your research. My parents are ordinary people, dorks, failures, hicks. On Saturday nights they are not invited to premieres: they watch a documentary on abstract impressionism or Turkish ambitions in the Mediterranean, they read on the living-room sofa, and they go to bed at eleven. I love my parents, but they are useless to me. Even so, I refuse to think like a victim. There are always more reasons to fail than to succeed. I plunge back into my song. If Thérèse and Jacques Dion managed it, so can I.



I spend the next few hours carving and polishing my song. Inspired, determined, illuminated, I spin an impossible love story. Full of that look that says I know what I want, I prepare my coup d’état. I am composing a masterpiece in my bedroom, and everyone will be stunned when they hear this miraculous anthem for the first time. “You wrote that all by yourself?” “We had no idea there was a musical genius among us!” “This song is incredible, I’m sending it to my aunt immediately, she works in television, I’m sure she can find you a producer!”

The first rays of sunlight signal the end of my Night of Fire. Footsteps in the hallway, the sound of keys, the apartment door closing with a metallic snap. Like every Sunday morning, my father goes down to the bakery to buy croissants. Meanwhile my mother is getting ready in the bathroom, listening to the radio. She hums in the shower, and I have finished my song.

I repeated so many times that I began writing songs at the age of eight that I ended up believing my own lies. The truth is more ordinary: I composed my very first song at fifteen. And however much I had been struck by the fire of inspiration, what I produced was pathetic.



When you’ve scored an overall 19.75 out of 20 on the baccalauréat, when you’ve finished top of your class at Sciences Po, you dream of a prestigious career in public service, not of appearing on television and signing autographs. At twenty, I have everything going for me, beautiful as an angel, the future opening its arms to me; and yet I feel thwarted, embittered, I have the sense of leading a life that is not mine and I resent the whole world. Everyone says I’m succeeding, but that isn’t how it feels to me. I finish the semester with one certainty: I’m pouring far too much of myself into this degree.

Obviously, I have no intention of interrupting my brilliant higher education to play opening slots in shitty bars. I deserve better than playing in dingy venues, auditioning, becoming a backing singer. But my cover as the perfect student takes up an insane amount of time; it drains me without inspiring me. Written in the evening after a day spent cramming, my songs have no poetry, no substance, no nuance. They are everything I refuse to be: mediocre.



So I remain in the shadows, preparing my grand entrance. I’m not motionless, I’m paralyzed. I wait for the right idea, I wait to write better songs, I wait until I can play piano better, I wait until I can afford a new guitar, I wait until I’ve finished my degree, I wait until I find an internship, I wait for my voice teacher’s advice, I wait until I’m thinner, I wait until I’ve bought myself a corduroy jacket, I wait till kingdom come, the Greek calends, the thirtieth of February. For the moment, I don’t need anyone to slow my progress toward glory. I do that perfectly well all by myself.

“Could you keep it down a little? This is a library.”

First warning. I snap before plunging back into my revision, and I unlock my computer with the password I’ve been using for years: Cléo_famous!

Despite my intervention, the group of four idiots sitting at my table is still just as noisy. I come here to surround myself with focused faces, not losers playing on their phones instead of studying. Second warning. I shoot them several pointed looks to make them shut up—if only my eyes could spit fire. Unfortunately, my dear tablemates go right on talking.
