For Berenice





There are thirty-two of them. Thirty-two still living in the abbey on this autumn day in 1986, at the end of a road enough to make those who travel it go pale. In a thousand years, nothing has changed. Not the steepness of the way, nor its vertigo. Thirty-two sturdy hearts—you need one when you live perched on the edge of the void—thirty-two bodies that once were sturdy too, in their youth. In a few hours, they will be one fewer.

The brothers form a circle around the one who is leaving. There have been many circles, many farewells, since the Sacra first reared its walls above them. There have been many moments of grace, of doubt, of bodies braced against the shadow as it comes. There have been and there will be other departures, so they wait patiently.

This dying man is not like the others. He is the only one here who never took vows. And yet he has been allowed to stay for forty years. Whenever there was debate, whenever questions were asked, a man in a purple robe would arrive—never the same man—to settle the matter. He stays. He belongs to the place as surely as the cloister, its columns, its Romanesque capitals, whose preservation owes a great deal to his talent. So let’s not complain: he pays his way with his work.





Only his fists protrude from the brown wool blanket, one on either side of his head, an eighty-two-year-old child caught in a nightmare. His skin is yellow, stretched to snapping point, vellum pulled taut over angles too sharp. His forehead gleams, waxed by a greasy fever. One day his strength was bound to abandon him. Too bad he never answered their questions. A man is entitled to his secrets.

Besides, they feel they know. Not everything, but the essential thing. Sometimes they disagree. To fend off boredom, they discover a gossipy fervor in themselves. He is a criminal, a defrocked priest, a political refugee. Some say he is being kept here against his will—the theory doesn’t hold; they have seen him leave, and come back—others insist he is here for his own safety. And then there is the most popular version, and the most secret, since romance is smuggled in here if it enters at all: he is here to keep watch over her. She who waits, in her marble night, a few hundred meters from the little cell. She who has been waiting for forty years. Every monk at the Sacra has seen her once. All of them would like to see her again. It would be enough to ask padre Vincenzo, the superior, for permission, but few dare. Perhaps for fear of the impious thoughts said to come to those who draw too close to her. And impious thoughts the monks already have more than enough of, pursued as they are, in the heart of darkness, by angel-faced dreams.





The dying man thrashes, opens his eyes, closes them again. One of the brothers swears he saw joy there—he is mistaken. A cool cloth is gently laid across his forehead, across his lips.

The sick man stirs again and, for once, they all agree.

He is trying to say something.





Of course I’m trying to say something. I saw man fly, faster and faster, farther and farther. I saw two wars, nations go under, I picked oranges on Sunset Boulevard—don’t you think I’ve got something to tell? Forgive me, I’m being ungrateful. You clothed me, you fed me when you had nothing, or next to nothing, after I decided to hide among you. But I kept silent too long. Shut the shutters. The light hurts me.

He is agitated. Shut the shutters, brother, the light seems to trouble him.

The shadows keeping watch over me in silhouette against a Piedmont sun, the voices growing muffled as sleep comes on. It all happened so fast. Barely a week ago they were still seeing me in the kitchen garden, or on a ladder; there was always something to repair. Slowed by age, yes, but since no one would have bet on me at birth, it was enough to command admiration. Then one morning I couldn’t get up. I read it in their faces: my turn had come, they would soon ring the passing bell and carry me to the little garden facing the mountain, where poppies grow over centuries of abbots, illuminators, cantors, and sacristans.

He is at the very end.





The shutters creak. I’ve been here forty years, they’ve always creaked. Darkness at last. Darkness like in the cinema—which I saw being born. An empty horizon, nothing at first. A blinding plain that, by dint of staring at it, my memory peoples with shadows, with silhouettes that become cities, forests, men and beasts. They come forward, set up camp at the front of the stage, my actors. I recognize some of them; they haven’t changed. Sublime and ridiculous, cast in the same crucible, inseparable. The coinage of tragedy is a rare alloy of gold and tawdriness.

It is only a matter of hours now.

A matter of hours? Don’t make me laugh. I’ve been dead a long time.

Another fresh compress. He seems calmer.

But since when can’t the dead tell their story?





Il Francese. I always hated that nickname, though I’ve had far worse. All my joys, all my dramas are Italian. I come from a land where beauty is always under siege. Let it fall asleep for five minutes and ugliness will slit its throat without mercy. Geniuses spring up here like weeds. We sing as we kill, we draw as we deceive, we let dogs piss on church walls. It is no accident that an Italian, Mercalli, gave his name to a scale of destruction, the one that measures the intensity of earthquakes. One hand tears down what the other has built, and the emotion is the same.

Italy, kingdom of marble and garbage. My country.





But the fact is, I was born in France in 1904. My parents had left Liguria in search of fortune fifteen years earlier, barely married. Instead of fortune, they were called Ritals, spat on, mocked for the way they rolled their r’s—as if the word roll itself didn’t begin with an r. My father barely escaped the racist riots of Aigues-Mortes in 1893; two of his friends never made it out: brave Luciano and old Salvatore. They were never mentioned again without those adjectives.

Some families forbade their children to speak the language of the old country, so as not to “sound like Ritals.” They scrubbed them raw with Marseille soap in the hope of whitening them a little. Not in the Vitaliani household. We spoke Italian, ate Italian. We thought in Italian—that is to say, in bursts of superlatives, with Death often invoked, tears in abundance, hands almost never still. We cursed the way other people passed the salt. Our family was a circus, and we were proud of it.





In 1914, the French state, which had shown so little zeal in protecting Luciano, Salvatore, and the others, declared that my father was beyond the shadow of a doubt a good Frenchman, fit for conscription—especially since a clerk, by mistake or for amusement, had made him ten years younger when copying out his birth certificate. He left looking grim, with no flower in his rifle barrel. His own father had lost his life in the Expedition of the Thousand in 1860. Nonno Carlo had conquered Sicily with Garibaldi. It wasn’t a Bourbon bullet that killed him, but a prostitute from the port of Marsala whose hygiene was doubtful, a detail the family preferred to pass over in silence. He was dead all the same, and the message was clear: war killed.





It killed my father. One day a gendarme came to the workshop above which we lived in the Maurienne valley. My mother opened it every day in case an order came in that her husband could fill when he returned; one day or another they would have to start cutting stone again, restoring gargoyles, hollowing out fountains. The gendarme arranged his face as the occasion required, looked even sorrier when he saw me, cleared his throat, explained that there had been a shell, and that was that. When my mother, very dignified, asked him when the body would be sent home, he stammered and explained that there had been horses on the battlefield, and other soldiers, and that shells did damage and, as a result, one couldn’t always tell who was who, or even what was man and what was horse. My mother thought he was going to burst into tears, had to offer him a glass of Amaro Braulio—I never saw a Frenchman drink it without making a face—and did not cry herself until long hours later.

Of course I don’t remember all that, or I remember it badly. I know the facts; I restore them with a little color, colors now slipping through my fingers in the cell I have occupied for forty years on Mount Pirchiriano. Even now—or at least a few days ago, when I was still capable of it—I speak French badly. No one has called me Francese since 1946.





A few days after the gendarme’s visit, my mother explained that in France she would not be able to give me the education I needed. Already her belly was rounding with a brother or sister—who was never born, or in any case not alive—and she drowned me in kisses while telling me she was sending me away for my own good, sending me back home because she believed in me, because she saw my love of stone despite my young age, because she knew I was meant for great things, and because she had given me a name for that.





Of the two burdens of my existence, my given name was probably the lighter to bear. I hated it fiercely all the same.

My mother often went down to the workshop to watch her husband at work. She realized she was pregnant when she felt me twitch at a blow of the chisel. Until that moment she had spared herself nothing, helping my father move enormous blocks, which may perhaps explain what came next.

“He’ll be a sculptor,” she announced.

My father grumbled, told her it was a rotten trade in which hands, back, and eyes wore out far faster than stone, and that if you weren’t Michelangelo you might as well spare yourself the trouble.

My mother nodded, and decided to give me a head start.

My name is Michelangelo Vitaliani.









I discovered my country in October 1916, in the company of a drunk and a butterfly. The drunk had known my father, had avoided conscription thanks to the condition of his liver, but the way things were going suggested his cirrhosis would not protect him much longer. They were drafting boys, old men, the lame. The newspapers claimed we were winning, that the Boche would soon be history. In our community, the news of Italy’s having joined the Allies the year before had been greeted like a promise of victory. Those coming back from the front were singing a different tune—those, at least, who still felt like singing. Engineer Carmone, who like the other Ritals had scraped salt at Aigues-Mortes and then opened a grocery in Savoy, where he drank a good part of his wine stock himself, had therefore decided to go home. If he had to croak, he might as well do it in his own country, lips reddened by Montepulciano to wash down the fear.

His country, his, was the Abruzzi. He was kind, and agreed to drop me off with Zio Alberto on the way. He did it partly because he felt a little sorry for me and also, I think, because of my mother’s eyes. Mothers’ eyes are often something, but my mother’s irises were a strange blue, almost violet. They had started more than one brawl, until my father put a stop to it all. A stonecutter has dangerous hands—I should know. The competition backed down quickly.





My mother shed big violet tears on the platform at Modane station. My uncle Alberto, a sculptor too, was going to take care of me. She swore she would join me soon, as soon as she had sold the workshop and made a little money. A matter of weeks, a few months at most—it took her twenty years. The train snorted, spat black smoke whose taste I can still feel, and carried off the tipsy engineer and her only son.

Whatever anyone may say, at twelve grief doesn’t last very long. I had no idea what this lurching train was taking me toward, but I knew I had never taken a train before—or didn’t remember doing so. Excitement quickly gave way to unease. Everything was going too fast. The moment I fixed on a detail, a fir tree, a house, it vanished at once. A landscape is not meant to move. I began to feel ill, wanted to confide in the engineer, but he was snoring with his mouth open.





Luckily, there was the butterfly. It got on at Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne and settled on the window, between the mountains slipping past and me. After a brief struggle with the glass, it gave up and did not move again. It was not a beautiful butterfly, one of those glories of color and gold I would see later in springtime. Just a mediocre butterfly, gray, a little bluish if you squinted hard enough, a moth dazed by daylight. For a moment I thought of torturing it, as boys my age do, then realized that by fixing my gaze on it, the only still thing in a world gone mad, my nausea was fading. The butterfly stayed there for hours, sent by some friendly power to reassure me, and it may have been my very first intimation that nothing is ever really what it seems, that a butterfly is not just a butterfly but a story, something enormous crouched inside a tiny space—something the first atomic bomb would confirm a few decades later and, perhaps more than that, what I am leaving behind as I die in the foundations of the most beautiful abbey in the country.





When Engineer Carmone woke up, he laid out his plan to me, because he had one. He was a communist. Did I know what that was? I had heard the insult more than once in the community back in France; people were always wondering whether so-and-so was one. I answered, “Pfff, of course. It’s a man who likes men.”

The engineer burst out laughing. In a way, yes, a communist was a man who liked men. “Besides, there’s no wrong way to like men, do you understand that?” I had never seen him so serious.





The Carmone family owned a plot of land in the province of L’Aquila, on which geography had inflicted two injustices. First, it was the only province in the Abruzzi with no access to the sea. Second, earthquakes ravaged it at regular intervals, like the Liguria of my ancestors—except that bitch of a Liguria, she at least had the sea.

His land had a pleasant view over Lake Scanno. The engineer intended to build there a tower mounted on a gigantic ball bearing and house the local proletarians in it, all for a moderate rent that would allow him to live decently—especially since, as a good communist, he was reserving the top floor for himself. Thanks to two teams of horses relieving each other every twelve hours, the building would turn on itself over the course of the day. Its inhabitants would thus enjoy, without exception, with no profiteers and no exploited, a view of the lake once a day. Perhaps one day electricity would replace the horses, though Carmone admitted it would probably never reach so far. But he liked to dream.

The bearings would also have the advantage, if an earthquake struck, of decoupling the structure from the ground. In the event of a grade XII earthquake on the Mercalli scale—it was he who taught me that name—his building would have a thirty percent greater chance of surviving than an ordinary one. Thirty percent doesn’t sound like much, but given that grade XII was not exactly a joke, he explained, rolling his eyes wide, it was enormous.

I drifted into a half-sleep, my eyes fixed on my butterfly, and we entered Italy while the engineer tenderly discoursed to me on devastation.









Italy and I embraced like old friends the first time we met. In my haste to get off the train at Turin station, I tripped on the step and landed spread-eagled on the platform. I lay there for a moment, without the least thought of crying, with the beatitude of a priest at his ordination. Italy smelled of gunflint. Italy smelled of war.

The engineer decided to take a cab. It was more expensive than walking, but my mother had entrusted him with money in an envelope and, just as wine was meant to be drunk, he declared, money was meant to be spent; in fact, let’s buy ourselves a little quarter-liter of red from the Po before we set off, if you don’t mind.

I didn’t mind, amazed by what I was discovering: soldiers on leave, soldiers leaving for the front, porters, train drivers, and a whole crowd of shady people whose function or ambitions seemed mysterious to the child I was. I had never seen shady people in my life. I had the impression they returned my insistent stares benevolently, as if to say, you’re one of us. Perhaps they were only staring at the blue lump swelling in the middle of my forehead. I moved through a forest of legs, blissful, spellbound by other smells: creosote and leather, metal and artillery, scents of shadow and battlefields. And there was the noise, a forge’s uproar. Things grated, squealed, collided, a musique concrète played by illiterates, far from the halls where bored notables would one day crowd together to pretend to appreciate it.





Without knowing it, I had arrived in the very heart of Futurism. The world was nothing but speed: the speed of footsteps, trains, bullets, shifts of fortune or alliance. And yet all those men, that whole mass of them, seemed to be digging in their heels. Bodies exulted, pressed toward railcars, trenches, a barbed-wire horizon. But something, between two movements, two surges, was screaming I want to live a little longer.

Later, when my career took off, a collector proudly showed me his latest acquisition, the Futurist painting Revolt by Luigi Russolo. It was in Rome, around the turn of the thirties, I think. The man considered himself an enlightened amateur, passionate about abstract art. He was an idiot. Unless you were there that day, at Porta Nuova station, no one can understand that work. No one can understand that there is nothing abstract about it. It is a figurative painting. Russolo painted what was exploding in our faces.

No twelve-year-old puts it that way, obviously. At the time I did nothing more than look around me with my eyes wide open while the engineer refreshed himself in a dive at the end of the platform. But I saw all of it. One more sign, if another were needed, that I was not quite like everyone else.









We left the station under a light snowfall. We had barely stepped outside when a carabiniere blocked our way and asked to see my papers. Not those of my companion, only mine. His fingers numbed by the cold and the little red from the Po, Engineer Carmone handed him my pass. The other man looked at me suspiciously, an expression he must have put on each morning to go to work and put away at night, unless he had been born with it.

“So you’re a little Francese?”

I didn’t like being called French. I liked being called little even less.

“Little Francese yourself, cazzino.”

The carabiniere nearly choked. Cazzino was the favorite insult of the backyards where I had grown up, and carabinieri do not choose a profession that comes with such handsome uniforms merely so people can insult the size of their virility.

Like the engineer he was, Carmone pulled my mother’s envelope from his pocket and greased the gears that had seized up. We were soon able to move on. I refused to get into a cab and pointed to a tram. Carmone grumbled, consulted a map, asked a few questions, and determined that the tram would let us off not too far from where we needed to go.

With my backside on a wooden bench, I crossed the first big city of my life. I was happy. I had lost my father, I had no idea when I would see my mother again, but yes, I was happy, drunk on everything that still lay ahead of me, that great mass of future to climb, to carve to my own measure.

“Tell me, Signor Carmone?”
“Yes?”
“What is electricity?”





He stared at me in astonishment, seeming to remember that I had spent the first decade of my life in a village in Savoy I had never left.

“That, my boy.”

He pointed to a streetlamp surmounted by a beautiful golden globe.

“So it’s like a candle?”
“Yes, but one that never goes out. It’s electrons moving between two pieces of carbon.”
“What’s an electron? Some kind of fairy?”
“No, it’s science.”
“What’s science?”

The flakes wheeled, light as a girl’s dress. The engineer answered my questions without impatience or condescension. We soon passed an immense building under construction: the Lingotto, where a few years later Fiat cars would climb, by way of a helical ramp, to the roof where they would take their first turns after assembly—a mechanical Sacra di San Michele. The outskirts thinned out, roads gave way to tracks, and the tram stopped in what looked like a field. We had to walk the last three kilometers. I am grateful to that man, Carmone, for having taken me so far, despite the cold, despite the times. We walked through the mud, and I imagined that already, in his memory, my mother’s eyes were beginning to pale, to seem less violet. But he brought me unfailingly to Zio Alberto’s door.





We had to wrench at the bell and hammer several times on the knocker before Alberto deigned to open, wearing a dirty undershirt. The same murky eyes as the engineer, veined with red: the two men shared an immoderate love of the grape. My mother had written to announce my arrival, so there was not much to explain.

“Here is your new apprentice, Michelangelo, the son of Antonella Vitaliani. Your nephew.”

“I don’t like being called Michelangelo.”

Zio Alberto lowered his eyes to me. I thought he was about to ask how I preferred to be called, to which I would have answered “Mimo.” The nickname my parents had always used for me, the nickname I would still be called for another seventy years.

“I don’t want him,” Alberto said.

Once again, I had forgotten a detail. Because yes, it is only a detail.

“I don’t understand. I thought Antonell... that Mrs. Vitaliani had written to you, and that it had all been agreed.”

“She wrote to me. But I don’t want an apprentice like that.”

“And why not?”

“Because nobody told me he was a midget.”









C’è un piccolo problema, said old Rosa, the neighbor who delivered my mother one stormy night. The stove cracked and popped, whipped up by a contrary wind; a hellish draft reddened the walls. A few matrons from the neighborhood, come to help with the event, curious to glimpse that firm flesh their husbands fantasized about, had long since fled, crossing themselves and muttering il diavolo. Old Rosa, fearless, went on humming, sponging, encouraging. Cholera, cold, plain bad luck, a knife that might not have been drawn if less had been drunk—these had taken from her children, friends, husbands. She was old, she was ugly, she had nothing to lose. So the devil left her alone; he knew how to recognize trouble. He had easier prey.

C’è un piccolo problema, then, she said, wrenching me from Antonella Vitaliani’s womb. Everything hinged on that word, piccolo. It was obvious to anyone who saw me that I would remain more or less piccolo all my life. Rosa laid me on my exhausted mother. My father came up the stairs four at a time. Rosa said later that he had frowned when he saw me, looked around as if searching for something else, his real son rather than this rough draft, then nodded, I see, so that’s how it is, the way he would when his tool struck a hidden flaw at the heart of a block of stone and several weeks’ work burst into dust. You can’t hold it against the stone.





It was on stone, in fact, that my difference was blamed. My mother had not known how to rest, carrying enormous blocks in the workshop, enough to shame the local strongmen. Poor Mimo, the neighbors said, had suffered for it. Achondroplasia, they would say later. I would be described as a person of short stature, which, frankly, was no better than Zio Alberto’s “midget.” I would be told that my height did not define me. If that were true, why speak of my height? I have never heard anyone described as a “person of average height.”

I never held it against my parents. If stone made me what I am, if some black magic was at work, it also gave me richly for what it took away. Stone always spoke to me—all stone: limestone, metamorphic rock, even gravestones, the ones I would soon lie on to listen to the stories of recumbent effigies.

“This wasn’t what had been agreed,” the engineer murmured, tapping his lips with a gloved finger. “This is awkward.”

It was snowing hard now. Zio Alberto shrugged and went to slam the door in our faces. The engineer blocked it with his foot. He took my mother’s envelope from the inside pocket of his old fur coat and held it out to my uncle. It contained almost all the Vitalianis’ savings. Years of exile, labor, skin burned by sun and salt, of starting over, years of marble under the nails, with now and then an ounce of the tenderness that had seen me born. That was why those dirty, crumpled banknotes were precious. Why Zio Alberto opened the door a little wider.





“This sum was for the boy. I mean, Mimo,” he corrected himself, blushing. “If Mimo agrees to give it to you, he would no longer be an apprentice, but a partner.”

Zio Alberto nodded slowly.

“Hmm. A partner.”

He was still hesitating. Carmone waited as long as possible, then sighed and drew a leather case from his pack. Everything about the engineer celebrated wear, patching, an aesthetic of time passing. But the leather of the case, new and supple, still seemed to quiver with the passions of the animal it had clothed. Carmone passed a cracked glove over it, opened it, and reluctantly took out a pipe.

“It’s a pipe I acquired at great expense. Carved from the rootstock of a briar on which the Hero of the Two Worlds, Garibaldi himself, is said to have sat during his noble and unsuccessful attempt to bring Rome into our beautiful kingdom.”





I had seen dozens of those pipes, sold in Aigues-Mortes to gullible Frenchmen. I had no idea how this one had ended up in Carmone’s hands, how he had let himself be taken in. I felt a little ashamed for him, and for Italy in general. He was a naïve, generous man. The gesture cost him something, and I know he made it sincerely, to help me, not because he was in a hurry to get home or afraid of burdening himself with a twelve-year-old boy of unusual proportions. Alberto accepted. They sealed the deal with a shot of rotgut whose sourness stung the air inside the hovel. Then Carmone got to his feet, had one last for the road, and soon his wavering figure was receding through the snow.

He turned one last time, hand raised in the yellow phosphorescence of a dying world, and smiled at me. The Abruzzi were far away, he was no longer young, the times were hard. I never went to Lake Scanno later on, for fear of discovering that there was not, and never had been, any tower there mounted on a ball bearing.





I owe a great deal to the women they call “fallen,” and my uncle Alberto was the son of one of them. A courageous girl who lay beneath men in the port of Genoa without anger or shame. She was the only person my uncle ever spoke of with respect, with a fervor bordering on veneration. But the saint of the alleyways was far away. And since Alberto could neither read nor write, his mother became more mythological with each passing day. As for me, I wrote rather well, which delighted my uncle when he realized it.





My uncle Alberto was not my uncle. We did not share so much as an atom of blood. I never managed to clear the matter up completely, but his grandfather apparently owed mine a debt, an unpaid loan whose moral burden passed from generation to generation. In his own perverse way, Alberto was honest. At my mother’s request, he had agreed to take me in. He owned a small workshop on the outskirts of Turin. Since he was single and little inclined toward luxury, a few jobs here and there were enough to meet his needs, or had been until I arrived. Because the war—an enterprise of progress much praised by the hotheads of the day, who did not care for the term “hothead” and preferred “poet” or “philosopher”—the war, then, had popularized materials cheaper than stone, lighter, easy to produce and easy to work. Steel was Alberto’s worst enemy, and he insulted it even in his sleep. He hated it even more than the Austro-Hungarians or the Germans. A Crucco, as they called the Boches there, could still be granted extenuating circumstances. Their cooking, their ridiculous spiked helmets—they had reasons to be angry. But nobody in his right mind built with steel, and we’d see who laughed last when it all came crashing down. Alberto hadn’t understood that everything had already come crashing down. To his credit, steel had played no small part in that; it made fine cannons.





Alberto looked old, but he wasn’t. At thirty-five, he lived alone in a room adjoining his workshop. His bachelorhood surprised people, especially since after a shower, washed free of marble dust and dressed in his only suit, he didn’t look bad at all. He always went to the same brothel in Turin, where he treated the girls with legendary respect. The expression “to do it like Alberto” was popular in the city’s southern districts in the early twenties, between the Lingotto and San Salvario, before dying out when Alberto moved away, taking his marbles and his slave with him—that is to say, me. Partner: I still laugh about it.

I’ve often been asked what part he played in what followed. If by “what followed” you mean my career, none. If, on the other hand, you mean my last work, a few chips of him are no doubt in it. No, not chips—fragments. I wouldn’t want anyone to think he ever had any sparkle. Zio Alberto was a bastard. Not a monster, just a pathetic man, which comes to the same thing. I think of him now without hatred, but without sorrow.





For nearly a year I lived in that man’s shadow. I cooked, I cleaned. I carried, I delivered. A hundred times I almost got run over by a tram, knocked down by a horse, beaten by some fellow who had mocked my height and to whom I replied that at least my size was no problem downstairs, preferably in front of his girlfriend. Engineer Carmone would have been delighted to find the atmosphere in our neighborhood so electric. Every interaction was a potential lightning strike, a displacement of electrons whose effects one never knew in advance. We were at war with the Germans, the Austro-Hungarians, our governments, our neighbors—which is another way of saying we were at war with ourselves. One man wanted war, another peace, tempers rose, and the one who wanted peace ended up throwing the first punch.

Zio Alberto forbade me to touch his tools. Once he caught me correcting a little holy-water stoup commissioned from him by the neighboring parish of Beata Vergine delle Grazie. Alberto got monumentally drunk once or twice a week, and the latest binge had left its mark. The stoup was crude, insulting, the kind of thing a twelve-year-old could have done better, and one did, while the other slept off his wine. Alberto woke up and caught me red-handed, chisel in hand. He examined my work in astonishment, then rained blows on me while insulting me in a language I didn’t understand, a Genoese patois. Then he fell asleep again. When he opened his eyes and found me stiff and sore, covered in bruises, he pretended not to know what had happened. He went straight to his stoup, observed that he was not displeased with his work, and magnanimously announced that he would deliver it himself.





Alberto regularly dictated a letter to his mother, and at the same time allowed me to write one to mine—he generously paid for the stamp. Antonella did not always answer, perpetually on the road, chasing whatever job would let her hold on one more week, then another. I missed her violet eyes. My father, the man who had guided my first clumsy strokes, the man who had taught me the difference between tooth chisel, riffler, and broad chisel, was fading.

Work became scarcer over the course of 1917, Alberto’s moods darker, his binges more violent. Columns of soldiers sometimes marched against a backdrop of twilight; the newspapers spoke of nothing but her—war, war—but all we felt from it was a vague malaise, a sense of dissociation from our surroundings, of never quite being in the right place. Over there, some filthy beast was savaging the horizon. But we led an almost normal existence, the life of men safely in the rear, which lent everything we ate a faint taste of guilt. At least until August 22, when bread began to run short, and then there was nothing to eat at all. Turin exploded. Lenin’s name appeared on the city walls, barricades rose, and on the morning of the twenty-fourth a revolutionary even stopped me in the street to tell me to be careful, that their barricades were electrified—which told me more surely than anything else that the world was changing. The man called me “comrade” and slapped me on the back. I saw women face down sheepish soldiers on the barricades, climb armored vehicles, thrust out furious, triumphant chests the soldiers did not dare fire on. Not right away, anyway.





The uprising lasted three days. No one could agree on anything except that everyone had had enough of the war. The government finally brought everyone into agreement with machine guns; fifty dead cooled tempers. I hid out in the workshop. One evening, calm had only just returned, and a little bread with it, when Zio Alberto came home in a better mood than usual. He pretended to slap me, chuckled when he saw me dive under the table, then ordered me to take up the pen and dictated a letter to his mother. He smelled of the swill served on the corner.

Mammina,

I got the money order you sent. Thanks to it, I’ll be able to buy the little workshop I told you about at Christmas. It’s in Liguria, so closer to you. There ain’t no more work in Turin. But over there they got a castle that always needs repairs, and a church the authorities, they care a lot about it, so there’s work. I sold up here, cheap but never mind, I just signed with that rat Lorenzo, and soon I’m leaving with that little shit Mimo. I’ll write to you from Pietra d’Alba, your son who loves you.

“And give me a fine signature, pezzo di merda,” Zio Alberto concluded. “One that shows I’ve made something of myself.”





When I think back on that time, it’s strange: I was not unhappy. I was alone, I had nothing and no one, they were turning forests inside out in northern Europe, sowing them with flesh larded with metal, along with a few shells that would explode years later in the faces of innocent walkers, they were inventing a desolation enough to make Mercalli blanch—he who had given his poor scale only twelve degrees. But I was not unhappy. I noted as much every evening when I prayed to a private pantheon of idols that changed throughout my life and later even included opera singers and football players. Perhaps because I was young, my days were beautiful. Only now do I grasp what the beauty of day owes to the presentiment of night.





The abbot leaves his office and begins descending the Staircase of the Dead, aptly named. In a few moments he will go to the bedside of the man dying in the annex. The brothers have sent word that the hour is near. He will place the bread of life upon his lips.





Padre Vincenzo crosses the church without a glance at its frescoes, passes through the Zodiac Portal, and emerges onto the terraces at the summit of Mount Pirchiriano, from which the abbey looks out over Piedmont. Before him lie the ruins of a tower. According to legend, a young peasant girl, fair Alda, once took flight from it to escape enemy soldiers, aided by Saint Michael. Vanitas vanitatis: she wanted to repeat the feat before the villagers, just to impress them, and smashed herself on the rocks below. As part of the tower that bears her name would do in the fourteenth century, brought down by one of the many earthquakes that regularly shake the region.

Farther on, a few steps descend into the ground, blocked by a chain and a sign reading NO ENTRY. The abbot steps over it with agility creditable for his age. It is not the way to the annex where the dying man awaits him. Before joining him, the priest wants to see her, her. She who sometimes makes his sleep uneasy, because he fears an intrusion, or worse. One never knows what may happen, as on that occasion fifteen years earlier when fra Bartolomeo caught someone just in front of the last grille that protected her. The man, an American, had tried to pass himself off as a lost visitor. The abbot had smelled the lie at once; he knew its odor by heart, it was the smell of confessionals. No tourist could descend so deep into the foundations of the Sacra di San Michele by accident. No, the man was there because he had heard the rumor.





The abbot had been right. Five years later, the same man came back with proper authorization signed by some bigwig in the Vatican. So they opened the door for him, and the list of those who had contemplated her grew a little longer. Leonard B. Williams: that was the name of this professor from Stanford University in California. Williams had devoted his life to the Sacra’s captive, trying to pierce her mystery. He had published a monograph on her, a few articles, then silence. His work, brilliant though it was, slept on forgotten shelves. The Vatican had played it well, opening that door as if they had nothing to hide. For years, calm returned. But for the past few months the monks had been reporting tourists who were no tourists at all, snoops. You could spot them a mile away. The pressure was building again.

For long minutes the abbot descends, finding his way unerringly through the maze of corridors. He could find the way in total darkness, he has walked it so often. A little jingle accompanies him—the sound of the ring of keys in his hand. Those damned keys. There is one for every door in the abbey, sometimes two, as if behind every door some mystery throbbed. As though the mystery that gathers them all there, the Eucharist, were not enough.





He is nearly there. He smells the earth, the damp, the scent of billions of granite atoms crushed beneath their own weight, and even a little of the greenery of the surrounding slopes. At last, the grille. The old one has been replaced; it now has a five-point lock. The remote-control unit doesn’t work on the first try; padre Vincenzo keeps jabbing its rubber buttons—every time it’s the same, some progress this is, it’s 1986 and they still can’t produce a remote control that works? He checks himself. Lord, forgive my impatience.

At last the red light goes out; the alarm is disabled. The final corridor is watched by two state-of-the-art cameras, no bigger than shoeboxes. It is impossible to enter without alerting someone. And even if an intruder managed it, what then? He would not leave with her. It had taken ten men to bring her down here.

Padre Vincenzo shivers. Theft is not what should be feared. He has not forgotten that madman Laszlo Toth. He reproaches himself again—“madman” is uncharitable, let us say “unstable.” They had come close to disaster. But he does not want to think of Laszlo at this moment, of the Hungarian’s sinister face and blazing eyes. Disaster had been avoided.





She is kept locked away for protection. The irony is not lost on the abbot. She is there, don’t worry, she is doing wonderfully, except that no one has the right to see her. No one apart from him, the padre, the monks who request permission, the few cardinals who shut her away there forty years ago and are still alive, probably a few bureaucrats too. Thirty people in the world at most. And of course her creator, who had his own key. He came and went as he pleased to tend to her and wash her regularly. Because yes, she has to be washed.

The abbot opens the last two locks. He always begins with the upper one, a tic that perhaps betrays a form of nervousness. He would like to be rid of it and promises himself—as he did on his previous visit—that next time he will begin with the lower one. The door opens soundlessly—the locksmith who boasted of the hinges’ quality was not lying.





He does not turn on the light. The original fluorescent tubes were replaced, at the same time as the grille, with gentler lighting, and so much the better—those fluorescents brutalized her. But he prefers to see her in the dark. The abbot steps forward and touches her with the tips of his fingers, out of habit. She is a little taller than he is. In the center of a round room, a primitive sanctuary with Romanesque vaulting, she stands slightly bent on her pedestal, lost in a dream of stone. The only light comes from the corridor, carving out two faces, the break of a wrist. The abbot knows every detail of the statue sleeping in the shadows, having scrutinized it until his eyes wore out.

She is kept locked away for protection.

The abbot suspects that those who put her there were trying to protect themselves.





The city of Savona had given Italy two popes, Sixtus IV and Julius II. Pietra d’Alba, barely thirty kilometers farther north, very nearly gave it a third. I think I was partly responsible for that failure.

I would have laughed if anyone had told me, on the morning of December 10, 1917, that the history of the papacy would be nudged off course by the boy dragging his feet behind Zio Alberto. We had been traveling for three days, almost without stopping. The whole country hung on news from the front, after the drubbing the Austro-Hungarians had given us at Caporetto. They said the lines had stabilized not far from Venice. They also said the opposite: that the enemy was going to come down on us and slit our throats in our sleep or, worse, force us to eat cabbage.





Pietra d’Alba appeared, cut from the light of dawn, on its rocky crag. Its position, I understood an hour later, was an illusion. Pietra was not perched on a spur but set on the edge of a plateau. Really on the edge, meaning that between the village wall and the lip of the cliff there was a passage barely wide enough for two men abreast. Then fifty meters of emptiness, or rather of pure air, laden with the scents of resin and thyme.

You had to cross the entire village to discover what had made its reputation: an immense plateau that rolled away toward Piedmont, a piece of Tuscany shifted there by the whims of geology. To the west as to the east, Liguria kept watch and reminded it not to get too comfortable. This was mountain country, slopes covered in forest of a green almost as black as the beasts that prowled there. Pietra d’Alba was beautiful in its faintly pink stone—thousands of dawns had become embedded in it.
