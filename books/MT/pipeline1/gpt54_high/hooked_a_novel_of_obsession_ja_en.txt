I want to swim, she thought.

She wanted to strip off her shirt, skirt, and underwear and leave them in a heap at her feet, feel the softness of water and the refraction of light against her bare skin, and go on and on through a soundless space wherever her heart led her. As the temperature of the water settled into her skin, the boundary between herself and the world would grow indistinct; weight, age, sex would all lose their meaning. If she tried to speak, every word would turn into bubbles, rising higher and higher, blurring into some white beyond before finally disappearing. The hot season was almost over. Why, then, did she feel this way?

Of course. The office before her in the early morning looked very much like an indoor pool with no one in it.

The desks stood in neat rows, as though divided by lane ropes; the still, pale-blue emptiness of the deserted room suggested the surface of water. Chlorine and printer toner were alike, too, in that both had that sharp smell that made you feel you were on the verge of remembering something. Looking for a time and place where she could work without anyone bothering her, she had ended up, after all, in this office where she spent most of her day. It was a place where she was supposed to display the most capable side of herself, and yet right now, if she felt like it, she could shout utter nonsense at the top of her lungs or break into a ridiculous dance. Of course she wouldn’t.

She was not, in any circumstance, the sort of person who raised her voice or danced.

The Food Business Sales Department, which occupied half of the nineteenth floor of Nakamaru Trading’s Otemachi headquarters—the biggest trading company in Japan—was completely empty between six and seven in the morning. It was the sort of section you would expect in a trading company, responsible for importing food from overseas and selling it to firms in Japan.

The brand-new air before her, unstained by anything yet, soothed the mind and body of Eriko Shimura, who always wanted everything brought to a conclusion. As the first person in, it was her duty to turn on the copier.

Ever since she was a girl, she had liked getting out in front of things. Her father, a senior employee at the same company, had taught her that “making the first move” was one of the essential qualities of a trading-company man. Her parents, with whom she lived in a condominium in Setagaya, seemed unhappy that their only daughter was never at the breakfast table, but now that she was in her eighth year at the company, they no longer said anything.

Even at thirty, Eriko had no intention of leaving home. Without her mother’s support, it would be impossible to keep herself this well groomed, this healthy, and working. And if she did move out, her mother too would probably feel stifled, left alone day after day with her taciturn husband.

No matter how early she came in, she could keep her time alone for less than forty minutes. Most of her coworkers arrived a full hour before the official start time of eight-thirty; it was that kind of workplace, where people thought nothing of checking email with convenience-store food in hand. Weaving her way between the more than fifty desks, Eriko sank her TOD’S pumps into the carpet one after the other—the shoes her mother had polished so thoroughly they reflected even the outlines of the fluorescent lights overhead—and headed straight for her station. Half the Food Business Sales Department were full-time employees, but among them the only women were Eriko and the section chief, Sayako Yotsuba, who had joined ten years before her. By contrast, all the temporary and contract staff were women, but they never came in until just before work started.

The convenience-store bag containing her breakfast and a copy of the Nikkei rustled against her beige pleated skirt. She sat down at her desk and started up her computer. The startup sound rang out in a low note strong enough to make the floor by her feet tremble, then slowly spread in an invisible arc through the whole room.

Only at this moment was she permitted to do nothing, think nothing. It might even have been the most peaceful time of her day. While she waited for her tidy desktop to appear, she savored this tiny freedom. Now that her annual income had long since passed ten million yen, Eriko could say with confidence that the most valuable thing in the world was time. Given enough time, most things could somehow be managed. Because everyone lived in such a rush, in the modern world anything that clearly had a great deal of time poured into it—a plan, a product, a work—was valued more highly for that alone. Proper table manners, letters that ran to more than three pages and opened with gentle seasonal greetings, makeup so light it looked like bare skin, well-maintained leather goods—all of them were admired because they commanded respect for the time that had gone into them.

To make space for breakfast, she first took out an alcohol wipe and scrubbed down the desk with little squeaking strokes. She carefully wiped the crevices of the keyboard and the mouthpiece of the phone as well.

People could call her neurotic if they liked, but unless she did this, she couldn’t feel that the little piece of this too-large room assigned to her was really her own. She broke into the pile of routed documents stacked high on her desk and skimmed them one by one at speed. With nobody coming and going and phones not ringing off the hook, she worked several times faster than she did during office hours. Time could be made, if you knew how. That was the very first thing her direct supervisor had taught her after she entered the workforce. A few minutes later, when the pile had finally disappeared, Eriko, with an almost irresistible urge to rub her hands together, at last pulled the convenience-store bag into the center of the desk.

A carton of café au lait. And a packaged sweet bun: a new product called karinto melon bread. She had checked two convenience stores near her house that morning and found both sold out; she had finally managed to get hold of this precious item at a shop in the subway station. She knew perfectly well there was no chance it contained anything good for her body, and yet she still turned it over to check the ingredients list. It might have been an occupational hazard.

Mitsuzaki, the major bread manufacturer, was a client of the grains division. Artificial sweeteners, preservatives, colorings. Even at her age, she still felt a faintly illicit pleasure in secretly putting something in her mouth that her natural-food-loving mother would disapprove of. All this for just ninety-eight yen. Back in junior high and high school, some of her classmates used to buy sweet buns like this at the school store and fill their stomachs with them. She had been grateful, of course, for her mother’s homemade lunches, but Eriko had envied those huge, luridly colored breads with an intensity that surprised her. It had seemed to her that inside the bodies of girls her own age, so easily satisfied by cheap sweets, there spread some easygoing, expansive inner self.

She pulled out the long row of bookmarked sites, moved the cursor onto the URL of her favorite blog, and with a click dove into a time that belonged only to the blogger and herself. As her eyes chased the words across the screen, she bit into the melon bread, nearly as big as her face. The cookie crust shattered with a crunch, and butter, brown sugar, and melon flavor spilled out. Just as the blogger had said, the balance of salt and sweetness was not bad at all, but it tasted unmistakably cheap. Even so, eaten together with the words flowing across the liquid-crystal screen, it tasted delicious in a way that seemed to soak right through her.



“The idea of putting karinto and melon bread together is just idiotic, right? I found it at the convenience store and cracked up. But when you chew that greasy, crunchy dough, with its faint mitarashi-ish soy-sauce flavor, this fresh melon scent suddenly opens up, like coming out of a tunnel. I’m addicted right now. Idiot flavor. Idiot price. If you just call this kind of ‘idiot food’ lunch, a lot of things get easier. There’s something soothing about feeling like you’re not taking life too seriously. Not that I recommend it, though.”



Not taking life too seriously. What a lovely phrase, she thought. To Eriko, life had always been something to confront in earnest, something to grapple with. She was sitting there in a daze, buoyed by a faint sense of accomplishment and the weight of stale oil spreading through her chest, when a voice dropped from above.

“You’re eating the kind of thing high-school girls eat, Shimura. You’ll get fat.”

Whatever the situation, Yasuyuki Sugishita from the seafood team had a way of speaking that first made the other person stop and check whether they were at fault. While you were involuntarily turning back on yourself, he would go right on pressing his own pace. That manner of his seemed to fend off criticism as well; though he wasn’t particularly a perfectionist, Eriko had never once seen him scolded or warned by a superior. In a sales department full of jockish types, Sugishita stood out: a thin, spare body; features that were handsome but set in a somewhat long face; a narrow chin; thin lips with little color in them. He also had the unusual background of having studied architecture in graduate school, so although he and Eriko had entered the company in the same year, he was two years older.

“Morning. Oh, this bread? It was on a blog. I finally managed to get one.”

Disappointed that her time alone had been cut short earlier than she’d expected, Eriko impulsively pointed to the computer screen. Sugishita planted a hand on her desk and leaned in. Too close, she thought—but not enough to make her actually pull back. The smell of the hair product he used gave a sharp flick to her nose.

“Huh. A housewife blog? What’s a single woman like you find interesting about that?”

Ohyo’s Diary of a Good-for-Nothing Housewife was a blog Eriko had read almost every day for the last two years. The top page had a plain design. The entries were long and sparing with line breaks. The occasional photos were taken on a cellphone and, frankly, weren’t very good. Even so, it always ranked within the top thirty on a certain site’s housewife-blog popularity list. Perhaps what distinguished it was the total lack of “antis,” something housewife blogs usually attracted in abundance.

“It is a housewife blog, but it’s not, you know, housewife-housewife. It has this perfect looseness to it. Her diet and her housekeeping are honestly pretty slapdash, but somehow there’s still this sense of cleanliness—or maybe restraint. And the fact that she’s my age makes me feel close to her.”

“Ohyo” lived in a Tokyo apartment with her husband, who managed a supermarket. Apparently they had no plans to have children for the time being. They didn’t seem especially well-off, but there was no sign she worked part-time. Despite being a full-time housewife, she cooked some days and not others. It was common for her to meet her husband at a family restaurant or a conveyor-belt sushi place and have dinner there. He didn’t seem to reproach her for it, and the two of them appeared to get along extremely well. That said, she never went out of her way to show off how close they were. Eriko even liked her sense in calling her husband not “my hubby” or by some pet name, but “the Dark Lord.”

“We do want kids, but we never get as far as making one! Sex isn’t such a big deal once it gets going, but getting to the point of doing it is such a pain, right? Hey, Dark Lord, are you reading this?”

She would toss off something that close to the edge as if it were nothing.

And when she wrote flat out that, as a matter of principle, she never collected points at supermarkets or drugstores and never used coupons, it had been exhilarating.

“Those things are stealing our precious time (angry). Even if you patiently build them up, in the end what are you saving—five hundred yen or so? If you think about the hassle of digging your point card out of your wallet and handing it over every single time you go to the register, it’s way cleaner to just do away with all of them. If you added up all the time that hassle takes over a lifetime, it might amount to ten hours or something. We’re not rich or anything, so maybe I’m losing a ridiculous amount of money, but I can’t be bothered anymore, so whateverrr. The moment I threw them all out—wow, my wallet got insanely light! Light and breezy~”

Eriko had always hated the way her mother’s wallet bulged with point cards and coupons. Her father had become president of a Nakamaru Trading subsidiary fairly early on, and they lived a reasonably comfortable life; even so, it saddened her to see her mother allowing sellers to lead her around by the nose for the sake of a mere hundred or two hundred yen. On the surface her mother seemed to go at her own pace, but in fact she was weak against pressure and unable to insist on her own will. She was the kind of person who couldn’t throw away things she’d been given and kept them forever. Perhaps that irritated Eriko all the more because it was a trait she shared. Ohyo probably knew instinctively that time ranked above money. That was why she could live her own time so freely, unbound by anything.

“What’s so interesting about a diary like this? It’s got nothing to do with your life. It’s kind of housewifey.”

For some reason, the fact that Sugishita seemed entirely unmoved gave her the faintest sting, and she spoke more quickly.

“No, it’s not like that. Usually housewife blogs have this tendency to show off—how happy they are, how properly they keep house, how good they are at cooking. There’s this really strong desire to be understood. But she…”

The phone on the desk rang, interrupting the conversation. When Eriko picked up, it was a tuna supplier in California. At this hour, calls from America always came in waves.

“May I have your name, please? When shall I have him return your call?”

After telling the caller the person in charge was away from his desk, Eriko turned back to Sugishita.

It frustrated her that she couldn’t explain it well. What she liked was the way Ohyo, while seeming a little absentminded, had such a sharp way of thinking. Her words flowed pleasantly through Eriko’s chest. Her language was gentle and yet intelligent, and never hurt anyone. She lived in sheer laziness, and yet didn’t seem ashamed of it, nor did she try to force some meaning onto it. She showed no sign of panic about not having children. More than anything, her writing was full of turns of phrase that stayed with you and offhand little ideas that made you want to imitate them.

On muggy days, for instance, she would do the housework in a swimsuit, then pull a one-piece dress over her head and head straight for the ward pool. At the library, she borrowed almost nothing but books by Sen Ishida. There was a listless coffee shop in her neighborhood, clearly being kept afloat as some kind of tax dodge, and she went there again and again for no reason but to read every volume of Kiriko Kubo’s Bucket de Gohan, deliberately ordering menu items that took extra work to prepare. She and her husband went out to karaoke and sang English songs one after another. They ordered pizza and watched whatever Hollywood movie happened to be on TV. They hopped from convenience store to convenience store looking for Gari-Gari-kun Rich Corn Potage.

Something about the way she spent whole days like a cat—doing nothing in particular, asking no meaning of it, making no plans, just passing the time as she pleased—drew Eriko in. When had she herself last lived like that? It was so long ago she could no longer remember. Simply reading the blog loosened, if only slightly, the nerves stretched taut by nightly dinners and market research.

Whatever the situation, Eriko always made a point of not wasting time. Even away from work, she put effort into beauty, research, and study; naturally she valued rest and sleep as well. She was confident that everything she did was connected to some kind of gain. She had no doubt that the self she was today was superior to the self of yesterday. Even so, perhaps ever since she turned thirty, there were moments when the back of her nose would sting sharply for no reason. At times she felt as though there wasn’t enough oxygen, as though she could flail both arms and touch nothing at all. She would have to take a deep breath and tell herself over and over that it was all right, then count off one by one her strengths, her growth, her contributions to the company. When even that wasn’t enough to calm her, she found herself behaving with unnecessary humility so as to draw praise from someone, and only then did she recover her equilibrium. Even so, no matter how she struggled, every time a day ended and morning came again she could feel another little bit of possibility wearing away, course correction becoming harder and harder. Was this impatience something that came with age? In a few more years, would she simply get used to it? Whenever she caught a trace of a similar impatience in another woman, she couldn’t stop relief and a twisted sort of pleasure from seeping out.

She felt no such impatience in Ohyo.

More than anything, what drew her in was the fact that the blog contained almost no one but the husband; female friends never appeared at all. In a field of housewife blogs that tended to boast about one’s network, it stood out. Secretly, Eriko hoped that Ohyo might know how to tame the same sort of inner knot that she herself carried.

It comforted her that even a woman like Ohyo had no friends.

Wasn’t it possible that even someone with such miraculously balanced charm, someone who never made other people uncomfortable, might fail to make friends over the slightest mistiming? Was having no female friends really such a shameful defect? Was it a defect at all? Might it not simply be one variation among many in human relationships?

“I’m a thirty-year-old housewife living in a shabby apartment in Tokyo with my husband. My favorite food in the world is engawa at Nikkori Sushi, the all-98-yen conveyor-belt place near us.”

Sugishita, who had seemed uninterested until then, finally leaned in when he read Ohyo’s profile.

“Huh. So her favorite thing is cheap conveyor-belt-sushi engawa. If she knows that the engawa at those dirt-cheap places comes not from hirame but from ohyo—halibut—and picked that handle, that’s some sharp irony.”

Halibut could reach more than two hundred kilos and nearly three meters long, which meant the engawa cut from them was huge. Apparently they thrashed so violently when they were hauled out of the water in Alaska that people used shotguns to finish them off. Eriko felt oddly as though she herself had just been praised.

“Come to think of it, Sugishita—you were handling Russian halibut last year, right? Weren’t you doing business with Center Village Holdings too?”

That major restaurant chain owned Nikkori Sushi, if she remembered correctly. At the sly look that appeared on Sugishita’s face, Eriko clapped her hands without thinking.

“Wow… that’s amazing. It all connects. So Ohyo’s favorite food is part of your work.”

Moved, Eriko looked around the office where they stood alone. These moments—when following numbers and goods suddenly made the world feel smaller, almost something she could grasp in her own hand—were what she loved best. At some point the sun had climbed high, and late-August light slanted into the room. The carpet, whitened and fuzzy from frequent cleaning, had begun to give off that dusty smell of sun-warmed fabric.

When you worked at a trading company, the everyday scenery around you could sometimes take on unexpected depth. This café au lait, this bread, this newspaper—where their ingredients had been born and raised, how they had circulated, been processed, and come to exist here now. If she narrowed her eyes, it was as though each one opened into its own story. Both Eriko and Sugishita possessed that kind of second sight.

Japan consumed roughly 6.52 million tons of seafood. Forty percent of it was imported. Among that were countless fish with no proper name as food—in other words, species treated as substitutes or sold under false labels. Ever since being assigned to the seafood team, Eriko had become able to see through the true identity of processed fish almost at once, no matter what the label said.

“You do feel a little guilty toward consumers, don’t you? ‘Engawa’ is the name of the cut, so it’s not exactly deception, but still.”

“But if you think about it calmly, there’s no way actual hirame or karei engawa could cost under a hundred yen. Consumers sort of know that.”

“At this point it’s less a food fish than some kind of alien. Scary.”

“And everyone’s happily eating alien nigiri and saying it’s delicious, so I guess everyone’s happy. The ‘fish’ in a fish burger, the ‘white fish’ in white-fish meunière—if you really start asking what kind of fish it is and where it came from, there’s no end to it. There are a lot of things in this world people genuinely don’t need to know.”

Sugishita talked like that, always with a detached edge, but once he was in an actual business meeting he could be astonishingly charming, all smooth words and easy affability. He gave off a slightly high-strung impression, but in fact he was the son of a kimono shop in Kawagoe that had been in the family for three generations.

“You’re one to talk, Shimura. Starting this month, Nile perch is yours, right? A woman this into conveyor-belt sushi and family restaurants—one day she might wind up eating fish you sold.”

Eriko flipped through the file of materials she had inherited from her predecessor. Nile perch was a freshwater fish of the perch order. It could grow as large as two meters and two hundred kilos. It was native to the African continent and lived in lakes and rivers. Despite its mild, bland flavor, it was a ferocious carnivore. When it was introduced into Lake Victoria, after all, it drove more than two hundred species of endemic small cichlids to extinction. Its light, unobtrusive taste had made it easy for Japanese consumers to accept as well, and in the nineties it had even been marketed as “sea bass” or “white sea bass,” but these days ninety percent of it was exported to Europe and America, and it was rarely seen in Japan. Eriko had spent the last several months trying to reopen sales channels and put facilities in order on the ground. The more she learned about Nile perch, the more helplessly drawn to it she felt.

“Nile perch is just incredibly hardy.”

“Ugh. It’s a carnivore, right? No telling what it’s been eating. Creepy, creepy. Oh, right. You’re supposed to watch that documentary Darwin’s Nightmare. Apparently it shows how Nile perch wrecks the ecosystem.”

“I keep meaning to watch it someday, but I never have time. Besides, until this next negotiation is over, it might be information I don’t really want in my head… If people hadn’t released it into the lake, Nile perch might have gone its whole life without ever realizing how vicious it was. It’s kind of sad. Oh, and apparently it’s popular as an aquarium fish too. Silver, really beautiful. It has this sort of lonely face…”

“You can see one at that new aquarium in Shinjuku, right? Want to go sometime?”

At the mumbled invitation, Eriko said neither yes nor no, and instead pretended to be absorbed in the blog again. She knew perfectly well that Sugishita felt a faint sort of attraction toward her. Apparently he had imposed on himself some private rule that if he ever put it plainly into words, he would lose, and the resulting mixture of desire and embarrassment gave his face a strained look. These little digs were probably his way of expressing affection. Perhaps unwilling to admit he had just been evaded, he hurriedly dragged the conversation back to where it had been.

“Well, maybe she’s fine as long as you only read the blog. But if you actually met in person, there’s no way it would work.”

“That’s not true. I feel like Ohyo and I could be good friends.”

“Come on. You don’t even have any women friends.”

It felt as if her fingertips had suddenly gone cold. No matter how hard she tried, no matter how wholly she concentrated every nerve on that single point, she still didn’t look like a woman who had female friends. Faced with that unalterable fact, her heart pounded audibly. In contrast to the shock his words gave her, there was something faintly sticky and sexual in the way he said them. When men saw a woman who couldn’t enter a female pack, they felt a small stir of desire. There was even a trace of pride in him, as though without dirtying his own hands he had found some major flaw in the enemy camp. Pretending not to notice, she made her voice as carefree as possible.

“Well, that’s true enough. Since I started working, I’ve drifted away from the group I used to hang out with in college. And even if I get invited to girls’ nights, I never have time to go.”

Taking the point on board first and thereby preserving the upper hand was a tactic she had learned in business. No such group existed, and she had never once been invited to a girls’ night. At times, the brazenness and childishness of grown women blithely calling themselves “girls” struck Eriko as dazzling.

“You don’t go to lunch with the temp girls or with your intake-year friends in other departments, either.”

He never said it outright, but Sugishita seemed to be secretly involved with the temp employee Maori Takasugi. That might have been why he knew so much about the social lives of the women in the office.

“Oh, that. My intake-year friends are never free at the same time, and eating with the temp girls gets complicated. A lot of them want to keep it under a thousand yen, so you can’t just casually invite them.”

She could hardly say, Because they never invite me, and made an effort to sound cheerful.

“Honestly, maybe I’ve always been bad at girl-group stuff. You know—this sounds silly to say about myself, but I’m kind of the all-wound-up honor-student type, right? I was always the one people pushed class-rep jobs and organizer roles onto, and before I knew it I was alone. I never really understood those invisible rules girls have, and I guess somehow I always ended up sticking out without realizing it.”

Even she could hear how fast she was talking. She prayed. Please, don’t ask anything more. Beyond this point was unknown territory. She wasn’t confident she could keep from letting something slip.

“Well, a beautiful woman like you, who can do everything without even trying, is bound to make people jealous. Women are scary that way—so full of envy.”

Sugishita’s eyes had narrowed fondly. Did he think he was complimenting her? He was probably comparing her with Maori Takasugi. Maori Takasugi, who wasn’t very good at her job, who never stopped chattering, and whose plump body carried soft rolls of fat. Sugishita was probably toying with her. And yet Maori was always surrounded by the other women in the office, and Eriko envied her for it.

Without trying. Sugishita had surely never once thought about how much time and money went into this spotless skin, this glossy hair. Suddenly Eriko found herself wondering what kind of skin lotion Ohyo used. Some cheap thing sold at a discount bin in the drugstore? Or perhaps, unexpectedly, she was devoted to organic cosmetics. Eriko wanted to try the same one. As she dug back through old entries, her lips began to move.

“Come to think of it, I think this Ohyo probably lives near me.”

“What? Seriously?”

“Look—this organic café she mentions here, called Giselle? It’s one station over from me, and I know it well because my mother helped get it started years ago. And anyway, all of Ohyo’s favorite fast-food places and chain restaurants are right around my nearest station. I mean, they’re the kind of places you can find anywhere, but still.”

“I don’t know, getting that familiar with that kind of thing is a little creepy. From her point of view, you’d be kind of like a stalker.”

The word was so far from anything she had ever imagined that Eriko turned and stared at Sugishita in shock.

“What? That’s ridiculous. I’m just reading her blog. I only happened to realize we might live near each other.”

“Still, you copy what she eats, you keep track of her daily life, and now you think you live nearby. Even if you don’t mean anything by it, from her side it’d be a little gross. Don’t take it too far.”

He cut himself off there and turned on his heel, heading back to his own desk. Employees had begun appearing here and there. Did he come in this early because he wanted to be alone with her before Maori Takasugi got there? Was it conceit to think so? It had been a year since she’d broken up with her last boyfriend, but she still didn’t feel lonely. Someday she wanted to marry, but right now going back and forth between home and the office took everything she had.

Had Ohyo ever imagined an office lady in an early-morning office, biting into the same bread while reading her blog? Even so, Eriko had a feeling that somewhere, somehow, Ohyo must already sense someone like her out there. On the surface her blog affected a casual air, but perhaps it contained messages for many people. You’re fine as you are. Let your shoulders relax. It’s okay to be alone.

As though cherishing another self in the mirror, Eriko carefully reread Ohyo’s entries.

Shoko Maruo stopped updating her blog on her cellphone and looked up.

That laundry looks like it’s about to fall, she thought, gazing at the denim flapping from the balcony of the apartment building across from her. Must belong to someone young. There was no way Shoko, now thirty, could pull off skinnies like that. She used to stand in department stores in slim pants so tight they seemed to cut off her circulation. Being on her feet for more than ten hours a day was normal. There were months when she got only two days off. What would the woman she had been back then think if she saw the life she led now? She might envy it, surprisingly enough.

In any case, she might have come a little too early. The organic café Giselle was one of her favorite places, a fifteen-minute walk from the apartment she shared with her husband. Perhaps because it was Saturday, she noticed several women dining alone, much like herself. She popped the last of the food remaining on her large plate into her mouth—konnyaku and chicken liver dressed with mustard mayonnaise, coleslaw with bitter melon, brown-rice rice balls with sesame and yukari, omelet with aonori—and polished everything off at a speed that was ridiculous even to her. It had been ages since she herself had eaten such a balanced meal with so many vegetables, and she certainly hadn’t made one for Kensuke. Once again, she thought what a failure she was as a housewife. Tonight, at the very least, she should simmer some hijiki. No… just imagining it was a pain, so maybe she should have them wrap something up here. She didn’t want to wash dishes. More than anything, Shoko hated doing anything with water.

She had met Kensuke, now the manager of a supermarket specializing in imported foods, through a part-time job, and married him three years ago. As a rule they tried to live on his salary alone and to be frugal, but that didn’t mean she wanted thrift to cramp the way they lived. A little waste here and there was nothing; she could cover it by dipping into the savings she’d built up while single. Lately she was even making a tiny bit of income from affiliate ads on her blog. No wedding ceremony, no rings, no overseas trips. Of course no house, either. Instead of indulging in big luxuries, they had agreed not to be stingy about small things. Better to eat out somewhere cheap than fight over dishwashing. It was a promise they had made when they filed their marriage papers.

Truth be told, Doutor or a family restaurant would have been fine today too, but since the person she was meeting was, after all, one of those media types, she had named the most stylish café she knew. The wood-toned interior always calmed her. Hashimoto, the part-time worker who came to clear away her plate, suddenly bent down close.

“Shoko, thanks for writing about our new fall menu on your blog.”

“Whoa, too close, too close. Don’t startle me like that.”

His breath on her ear tickled. Twenty-five-year-old Hashimoto was the same age as her younger brother, which made him easy to talk to. When she gave him a light smack, she felt beneath his shirt biceps far firmer than his lanky frame suggested. Their hardness almost bounced her hand away—a world apart from her husband’s upper arms, lately soft and squishy again as he’d started putting weight back on.

“On the days you mention us on your blog, we get a few more customers than usual.”

For someone who preferred family restaurants and fast food, Giselle was one of Shoko’s very few regular independent places. Earlier that year, she had taken a short-term part-time job there just over the New Year, and since then she had come to know the staff; even after quitting, she still came by as a customer once or twice a month. She had turned down their invitation to work there as a contract employee. The moment it became a workplace, she knew for a fact the human relationships would grow annoying, and she’d lose the ability to come and use it this casually.

Shoko lowered her eyes to her cellphone again. Just following the comments on her own blog was enough to make time fly. Almost none of them condemned her sloppiness. If anything, most were supportive—This makes me feel relieved; It cheers me up and makes me feel like it’s okay to be like this. Many people even posted links to products or shops they recommended. She was happy, as if she had finally found a place where she belonged, but when people said such kind things to her, she couldn’t help feeling, a little, that she was deceiving her readers. Even so, the comfort of favorable comments had become addictive, and she found herself rereading them for hours. It was a lot like soaking in the low-temperature carbonated bath at a super sento. When there was too little stress, it became hard to know when to get out.

Whether online or off, she wanted to secure as many places where she could feel at ease, and as many people she could exchange smiles with, as possible. Eight years after moving to Tokyo, she still hadn’t gotten used to the city. When she had been working, she’d been too busy to go out and have fun; after her health broke down, she had turned into a complete homebody. Just as the doorbell rang, a woman in a suit, probably in her forties, appeared. She spotted Shoko at once and strode toward her.

“Are you Ohyo… Shoko Maruo? My name is Satoko Hanai, from the Third Books Editorial Department at Shumeisha. It’s a pleasure to meet you.”

Shoko hurriedly rose, bowed, and accepted the business card. Satoko Hanai had regular features and a decent enough figure, but her skin was dry and the circles under her eyes stood out, so there was no shaking the overall impression that she was tired and worn.

“Well, I’m surprised. You’re much cuter and more charming than I’d imagined, Ms. Ohyo.”

By not going so far as to call her beautiful, she showed the caution of an elite professional. So she’s sizing me up. Shoko could feel a sensor, long dormant, click into action. She knew perfectly well that a much-washed T-shirt, chinos Kensuke had worn back when he was thinner, and almost no makeup did not add up to an outfit appropriate for a first meeting. Since quitting the apparel company, she had almost entirely lost interest in clothes, which she had once been so obsessed with buying. To begin with, she had never been especially striking. Her body had no real curves, her eyes were single-lidded, and freckles stood out on her pale skin. The shape of her lips meant that when she smiled, her gums showed, and that had always been a complex of hers.

And yet, for some reason, a certain kind of woman seemed almost to have agreed among themselves to detest her.

She’s pandering to men.

That was their charge. But she wasn’t. It was only that the ordinary consideration and tact women probably extended to other women without thinking came easily to her when the other person was a man. In front of women, moments often came when she didn’t know what to say; with the opposite sex, she somehow knew by instinct what to do. Maybe it had something to do with the fact that her parents had divorced and she had been raised by her father, surrounded by brothers. She was not especially calculating, and she didn’t have all that much romantic experience either. It always struck her as unfair to be judged that way simply because she felt at ease around men. Out of caution, she had made a habit of behaving more roughly than necessary in front of other women.

After ordering coffee, Satoko Hanai arranged several books and magazines on the table—housewives’ magazines and books based on housewife blogs, all published by Shumeisha, she explained—and launched into one explanation after another. To be honest, Shoko was not very enthusiastic. She had always been bad at having expectations placed on her. Just reading the humid, overheated prose of housewife bloggers, forever showing off how happy they were, exhausted her. She was the sort of person who wanted to avoid anything troublesome if at all possible. Unfazed by the look on Shoko’s face, Satoko rattled on in a rapid stream.

“At present, housewife bloggers are divided into all sorts of niches, but your selling point, Ms. Ohyo, is your naturalness. What’s so wonderful is that there’s none of this you should be like this, you ought to live like that. At a time when all kinds of values are supposedly accepted, there are many housewives who feel unmoored. And not just housewives—I think many people empathize with your steady, unforced way of moving through life at your own pace. You have a quality that isn’t pushy, and one that even single working women wouldn’t find off-putting.”

“Uh… but if a diary like that got turned into a book, who would even read it…?”

“Exactly. So rather than publishing the diary as it is, what would you think of reworking it into essays? We’d love to have you write a guide to living, as an ordinary woman who goes through life without trying too hard.”
