In the Place Beyond the Shell





Standing in the shadow of the deserted station building, I kept waiting for a visitor whose face had half vanished. Even when I dredged through memory and somehow pieced together the image of that face, it would immediately collapse like water. Even so, I gathered the fragments, pressed them back inside the outline, and made a patched-together portrait. There was a timidness to the repetition, akin to probing an aching tooth with one’s tongue.

At the beginning of July, the sunlight had grown whiter, pouring out heat without stint. Even as it sharpened every color, it was at the same time reducing everything to black and white. In the station square, glowing white, there were no shadows. On the benches people sat at measured distances from one another, like statues that would not melt even in this heat. In contrast to those figures pinned motionless by the sun was the black dog, darting about. He clung to the clear water rising from the plaza. The cobblestones were pierced with fountain jets, and from them water burst forth and stopped again with no regular pattern. The slender columns, rising to about the height of a child, bloomed and quietly vanished like glass plants in a rapid cycle, leaving behind dark wet circles. Delighted, the dog twisted his body against the shining water-snakes, white and transparent. Each time the water disappeared, he sprang toward another jet. He alone was enjoying even this heavy sunlight without a care.

Beside the white-scorched cobbled square in front of Göttingen station was a forest of tangled bicycles. Bikes had been shoved intricately into every gap in the racks, and the metal of their frames gave off a light that would not melt even in the sun. Even now, three years after coming here, I still did not understand by what means such complicated three-dimensionality managed to keep its balance. Even as I watched, a man braked hard, twisted the bicycle he had been riding into a seam in that metallic forest, and came hurrying toward the station building. Even the gesture of putting on his mask as he ran was smooth, and he was sucked in through the doors. With one bicycle’s worth of complexity added, the forest had already turned into such a mass of things that it had almost lost any meaning as bicycle. In the light skimming its surface there rose another image—a lump of objects whose meaning had come undone—and it began to throb, imitating toothache.

After checking the time, I peered in through the main entrance of the station. Perhaps a train had just arrived; the hall, almost empty a moment before, had somehow filled with people, and rich color crowded into the white space. Yet as they passed through the doors and moved away into the white sunlight, the travelers who had appeared lost their color again. When I called the dog’s name, he came at once to my feet, though not without reluctance at leaving off his play with the water. I clipped the leash to his collar and stepped inside the station. In the white hall, the black of his wet fur grew heavier still, vivid enough to seem almost to drive a stake into the white, and I stood there taking my bearings from that color.

The flow of people thinned, voices ceased, even the announcements of arriving and departing trains held their tongues, and for a brief while the hall filled with blankness. I stepped out from that soundless white and moved a little farther down the passage. Since the station’s front and rear exits were joined by a single straight corridor, and only a few glass elevators interrupted the line of sight, I could see faintly all the way to the doors at the far end. Standing at one end of the quiet passage and casting my gaze toward the back, I saw there a vivid green, framed by the doorway like a painting. Its clarity did nothing to shorten the distance between us. As if seen through the wrong end of a telescope, it merely flickered in my sight as a fragment of a distant world. When I brought it into focus, a human figure slowly took shape. In that corridor, where perspective seemed warped, Nonomiya stood against the green. Perhaps he had noticed me; the monotonous rattle of suitcase wheels began to draw nearer. As the distance between us dwindled, I could feel time, already far receded, and memory, left standing still, beginning to move again, imitating the squeak of those wheels. His tall, lean figure seemed to let the oppressive heat slide past. The balance of his broad forehead and the large eyes beneath it had something of medieval sculpture. Perhaps because he stood against greenery trembling in the sun, I found myself suddenly thinking of a saint depicted in stained glass high in a church window.

But there memory ceased to function properly. Nonomiya had come close enough that we ought to speak, yet his mouth was covered by a white mask, and everything below his nose blurred into the color of the cloth. In the white shadow that blurred outlines, I could hardly make out the movement of his mouth or the stir of words. The same was true of me; this thing, hot and obstructive to breathing, was, just then, more welcome than anything. I too was hiding behind what covered my mouth, keeping words in reserve. But once we moved away from the station entrance, we would have to take it off, and then, to join time back together, we would have to let words show themselves. Nonomiya stepped out of the impression of a saint shut up in glass and at last removed his mask outside the station. His mouth started to form what looked like It’s been a long time, but then, as if the length of the severed time between us had occurred to him, it never became voice, and his mouth softened into a small, vague smile. Even so, at the sight of that slight but careful bow, memory came apart in pieces, together with the pain of something catching inside me, and the words I had prepared vanished. This was how he greeted people, my cobbled-together, broken memory cried out. More than his face or his figure, it was that gesture that tried to bridge the nine years. At the same time, I felt relief at the heft of his presence, too solid for the sunlight to shine through. Even the dead have shadow pooling at their feet. I returned the bow in place of words. We both smiled wryly at the clumsy mirroring of it, but once our lips had moved even a little, the starting point of conversation settled itself naturally. “Long trip, wasn’t it,” I said. Nonomiya nodded, taking in all the meanings those words might bear, and formed an easy smile. “I’m a little tired.” In a calm tone he spoke of the delayed train and the missed connection, of asking a station employee about other trains and having trouble catching the answer because the man spoke so fast and his mask muffled his voice, and of the impression made by the old fortress he had seen from the train window. To judge from the way he spoke and carried himself, he seemed nothing more than an ordinary traveler. Everything I had variously imagined from what his friend Sawada had told me melted white before this sense of reality, and I could do no more than nod along like a puppet. Then, over our lopsided conversation, silence dropped with an audible snap, as though the cord suspending a curtain had broken.

“There’s so much green, and yet no cicadas.”

Without caring in the least that the conversation had broken off, Nonomiya murmured this, narrowed his eyes against the light, and seemed to search for the voices that scorch summer. His body, where time had stopped, received the heavy white light, but his gaze was not quite fastened to the waking world. In search of the cicadas that would fill that dense blankness, his eyes were turned toward some distant time.

The dog had drawn a little apart and was watching Nonomiya. His body language was easy to read: his tail, white only at the tip, was hooked in midair at an in-between height, sketching a sign of suspension. Without straying from his own orbit, the dog waited for the other to approach. He probably had no notion that Nonomiya was a ghost. His manner toward strangers was always the same; he seemed to make no distinction there between the living and the dead. Relieved by that, I urged Nonomiya on and started walking toward the street that led to the old town. From a distance, I and the dog and Nonomiya too would probably have looked as though we were losing our color, dissolving and vanishing into the white. That white blurred the perspective of memory. But it could not fill the rupture of time or of distance.







Göttingen is a city in which the seams of time do not stand out. One can slip easily from one time into another, moving through layers of memory. Like many German cities, it has as its core an old town thick with history, and around that core the city spills outward in all four directions, as though covering it over. The old town, circular in shape, is encircled by the traces of the old city wall. The wall itself is long gone; in its place a thin ring of woods surrounds the center, stitched through with footpaths packed down by generations of walkers. You can cross the old town from edge to edge in about half an hour, a size easy to grasp on foot. If you trace the streets set into it, you begin to notice that time there is layered in a kind of polyhedral structure.

I had heard, again through Sawada, where Nonomiya would be staying. It was north of the old town, near the science campus of the University of Göttingen. “So it’s better to go by bus,” I said, and Nonomiya nodded, and we turned toward the broad road that cuts off the belt of woods marking the old wall. There was a bus stop in front of the station as well, but no direct bus from there. So we decided to head for one inside the old town instead.

I had learned of Nonomiya’s arrival about two weeks earlier. Until the day he came, we never exchanged messages directly; instead we relied on Sawada’s careful and meticulous mediation. Because of that, I could not shake the feeling that I was here somehow as Sawada’s stand-in in the flesh.

Dragging our shadows behind us, we had scarcely entered the old town when we came upon a golden sphere, glinting dully in the sunlight. On the sidewalk facing a hotel of pale stone walls, it swelled atop the stalk of a dark bronze plate like an overblown bud. Nonomiya’s gaze slowly traced its outline. “This is the sun.” My own voice sounded farther away than I had expected, and I nearly lost my sense of where I stood.





In Göttingen there is a scale model of the solar system built into the town, known as the “Planet Path” (Planetenweg). In a place overlaid with old memories, the time of this model is still shallow, but it has already settled into the city and become one of its symbols. The sun model placed on Goethe Street, which runs straight east from the station, marks the starting point, and from there the planetary models, from Mercury to Neptune, are arranged one after another along the streets. Because their positions follow a scale of one to two billion, the distances between them are irregular, and unless you are paying attention, you pass them by. From Mercury to Saturn they lie within the old town, but Uranus and Neptune stand quietly in the residential district beyond it. Neptune, the edge of the solar system, sits just at the boundary with the woods. The city had found a deft way of laying the distances between the planets metaphorically over place.

I have heard that there are around fifty such Planet Paths in Germany alone, but sometimes I wonder what reason there can be for placing a miniature of the solar system on the earth. Is it because the journey into space, source of so many near-future fantasies, has receded farther and farther away? Or because we now have no choice but to keep our feet on the ground and turn our attention to the earth, making do with a substitute? Whatever the reason for its installation, it serves lovers of walking and jogging as a perfect marker of distance run and of turning points. They move nearer to the sun and farther from it, the planets in the corners of their eyes.

The bronze sun and eight planets look at first glance like a guide meant for travelers. Besides a simple map of the Planet Path winding through the city, each display gives general information about the planet in question—its size and period, the number of its moons, its distance from the sun. There is also a scale model of the planet itself. The sun at the starting point is represented by a large metal sphere whose surface shines a muted gold, its unstable balance supported by a bronze plate. Like a balloon swollen by sunlight, it seemed, if one stared at it long enough, as though it might simply float softly up into the sky. The presence of the planets, by contrast, was modest. In the upper part of each bronze plate there was a little viewing window, and inside it the corresponding planet object had been set according to scale. But in the case of Mercury through Mars, this meant no more than a metal speck the size of a pinhole embedded in thick glass. Peer through glass scored with fine scratches, and the too-small planet slips into the warped scenery. Only with the planets beyond Mars did they acquire a visible existence as objects.

The intervals from the sun to Mars are short. Nonomiya and I walked with the dog from the sun to Mars, about as far as the middle of Goethe Street. Each time we came upon a bronze plate, Nonomiya stopped, so the rhythm of our walking—and the conversation paced to it—kept catching. Since leaving the station we had hardly spoken. I knew the reason was the nine years between us, but it was beginning to feel as though the Planet Path itself were impeding our conversation. And yet some part of me welcomed that. These bronze markers filled the gaps in a conversation that hovered close to silence.

Once we crossed the river that ran through the town, the street changed its name to Prinzen Street. Goethe became prince, but the atmosphere of the street changed very little. For a while after that there were no more bronze plates in sight, and at last, flustered by the blankness of words, I found myself giving a rambling tour of the city. On our left were an antiquarian bookshop, a pizza place, a music shop, a stationery store; on our right, I pointed out the silent presence of the library. Eventually, near a flower shop and another secondhand bookshop on the main street that runs north and south through the old town, we came at last upon bronze Jupiter.







The dog I had brought with me when I went to meet Nonomiya was Agata’s. She was my housemate, and for the past year we had shared a rented place. She had studied economics at a university in Hamburg, then after some time working in an office had come to Göttingen to study information science.

Her dog was about the size of a beagle, with a somewhat long coat patterned in black and white, the colors unmixed. Most of his face and back were black, while his belly, legs, chest, throat, and part of his tail were white. He had a long muzzle and an oddly indeterminate face, as if some other animal had been superimposed on it.

When people spoke of him, everyone except his owner Agata usually called him not by his name but “the truffle dog.” By then that name sat more naturally on our ears and tongues than Hector, his actual name. A “truffle dog” is, literally, a dog with a nose for finding truffles. Through training, it learns the scent of that special fungus and acquires the skill of sniffing out its fragrance hidden under the soil. Agata’s dog had gotten his mushroom nose from her mother, his former owner. She had lived with him in a small town far south of Göttingen and freely enjoyed her walks through the woods as a hobby. But a year and a half earlier, Agata told me, she had died of breast cancer.

Most of the stories about the truffle dog, too, had been handed down from Agata’s mother. The small town where she had lived also ran almost directly into the woods, and there, it seemed, fragrant mushrooms lay quietly hidden. One day in late summer three years earlier, during the daily walk they always took through the forest, Hector had sniffed out a black truffle. He worked his long nose, suddenly plunged into the brush, and began scraping at the earth with his nose pressed to the ground. When Agata’s mother watched to see what he was doing, a black, round lump came up from the soil. Small, but densely heavy, it was black with a rich scent. She had slipped it into the pocket of her jacket as if nothing were amiss and taken it home. Strictly speaking, wild truffles are protected in Germany and may not be harvested. One, and they’ll overlook it, she had laughed. But she did not leave that discovery a mere accident.

Seeing the dog rub his nose against the pocket still fragrant with mushroom, her mother repeatedly held the truffle to his nose, weaving the scent into his memory. The forest plays an array of smells: the scent of water held in green things; the mold under leaves and the rot of fallen ones; the earth loosening in the warmth of the sun; the cool bluish-green smell of cypress; the dry fragrance of deciduous trees layered deep with autumn color. Into that was newly woven the hue of truffle scent as well. In the dog’s olfactory field, the forest must appear limpid and clear. But just as one cannot read in detail every color woven into an Impressionist painting, it remains a secret between the forest and the dog. From that time on, Hector became a truffle dog, though he never found more than three black mushrooms. “He’s such a courteous dog with the forest,” Agata’s mother would often say with a smile, burying her hand in his black, soft, smoky fur.

After her mother’s death, the truffle dog was taken in by Agata in Göttingen, and he adapted to his new surroundings. This city, too, is wrapped in woods. The traces of the old city wall surrounding the old town make a perfect walking route, and Agata circles that ring of woods with the truffle dog. Whenever I saw her with him bounding at her feet, I thought of a planet in orbit with a satellite in tow. On weekends they often went farther afield, entering the forest beyond the green where Neptune’s bronze plate stood. Agata did not seem to expect much from mushrooms, but she often joked, “If we find one, we’ll share the secret.” Once she showed me a photograph of a truffle her mother had found in the year she died. Small and wrapped in a hard black skin, it looked very much like a star that had fallen from the sky and burned out.

The story of the truffle dog had been repeated so often that the taste and smell of the black spherical mushroom had already been woven into the story itself. Through that anecdote, my own nose and mouth would try of their own accord to conjure the impression of something meteor-like and unknown. The name truffle had taken on a life of its own. It was like a ghost haunting taste and smell.

From the townscape to the dog: never quite finding an orbit, I went on speaking little stories about truffles, as though playing a game of association. Taking advantage of the quiet way Nonomiya inclined his ear toward me, I let words spill from my mouth in imitation of Agata’s way of telling them. What my quickened tongue spun was no true telling at all, only a counterfeit tale in the guise of one. It was a bad habit of mine. To avoid the central subject, I fled into talk of animals as a bridge in conversation. I had brought the truffle dog precisely as emergency treatment for the moment our talk began to fray. When at last words ran out, both Nonomiya and I fell silent and pretended to be distracted by the dog’s behavior. He walked at a rhythm that did not interfere with our steps, but he could not loosen the snag in our words. With those nine years always in view, I was still doing nothing but circle them endlessly. Was Nonomiya too? However intently I listened to the silence, I could not measure the depth of his quiet.







When we reached the bronze plaque for Jupiter, we turned left and headed north through the town. Weender Street, which divides the old town east from west, admits no cars except postal vans, garbage trucks, and police vehicles; only the feet of pedestrians strike the cobbles.

Once we left Jupiter behind, we left the Planet Path as well. Only when we reached Jupiter did the scale model in the viewing window finally achieve any real presence, and Nonomiya lightly traced its outline with a fingertip. A faint creak passed through my memory: when describing a work of art, he had always done this unconsciously. He would explain the content of a painting or sculpture in words while moving his finger over the surface of a desk or through the air, tracing outlines and arrangements—as if his fingers too were trying to inscribe memory. “We’re heading for the bus stop, so unfortunately Jupiter is as far as we go.” “Is Saturn far from here?” “Farther on ahead. It’s near the edge of the old town, so you should look for it another time.” Nonomiya let his finger leave Jupiter at once and stepped without hesitation into the bustle of the town, full of sounds of words unfamiliar to his ears.

The crowds on Weender Street were beginning to return, but people were walking with distance in mind, and there were more people moving alone. “For such a main street, it isn’t very crowded,” Nonomiya said quietly, letting his gaze travel over the street. When I had been in touch with Sawada, I had explained the state of the city, but I did not know how much of that had reached Nonomiya. Since March, the shadow cast by the unfamiliar word corona had gone on turning into something monstrous whenever one took one’s eyes off it. “There was an outbreak last month, and things went backward again. People will be out more soon enough,” I said, and he nodded. Just when it seemed the shutdown was loosening, the sudden leap in numbers had visibly altered the air of the city. The tension was easing again, but I still could not shake the sensation of standing blankly before a door shut right in front of my nose. That abrupt turn from relaxation back to tension had cracked reality badly somewhere, and even now something like jet lag made this place opaque. I ought to have known the fragility of reality well enough, and yet I must still have believed without condition that its foundations were stable. Perhaps because Sawada had already explained a certain amount, Nonomiya did not ask questions, but only looked around the town as he walked from sunlight into shadow. Without trying to explain further this unreal reality that had gone on for months, I confined myself to telling him, simply, the rules for living under it. What he needed, perhaps, was not words but a sensory way of shortening the distance to reality. How would he accustom himself to the time lag of nine years? What did this place look like through his eyes—this quiet life warped as if it belonged to the other shore?

Partway along, the truffle dog nudged my leg with his nose to complain of thirst. I took a lukewarm bottle of water and a small bowl from my bag and poured some out for him, as he pressed me on. The sound of the water rang out louder than I expected, and I flinched a little and glanced at Nonomiya. He was distracted, looking up at the buildings that lined the street. Their facades were covered with faces, all of them gazing down at him and meeting his eyes. The face of a man with a strange smile, the face of an indignant devil—these took on a kind of masklike effect, stuck fast to the city and casting their stare. Buildings with faces, houses with physiognomies: these too were among the things that still lay beyond the reach of my senses. Once one grows used to them, do they become part of one’s own time, no different from the faces of family? Or are they things from beyond the boundary, treated like troublesome lodgers, like house-haunting ghosts?
