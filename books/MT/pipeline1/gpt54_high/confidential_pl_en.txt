The Hubbub of the Past



Unless you know how to get ahead of the course of events and, by conjuring the future, write a good ending. You don’t—I know what I’m talking about. You’re mired, your legs tangled in dried roots. The drought does you no favours; what you really need is months of downpour, so the roots turn slippery and you can scramble out faster. In the meantime, I urge you to practise saying goodbye to memories. You’ve been late for years; you have to come to terms with linear time. Memory loops are the preserve of the very old, whom the passing years rob of retrospection. Time to set time free. Do it slowly; the body can play tricks, and abrupt change may send it into shock. Turn the circle into a timeline, not a line segment—that sounds like some strange geometry problem.

I can’t promise that once time finally starts moving forward, life will brighten. Everything has its drawbacks and few advantages. Lift your head out of the hubbub of the past. That’s where you’re from, but there’s no one left there now except you. Look around and throw out the things your children won’t want to inherit. Do the same in your head, though that’s much harder. Tell what you can in elegies. Sometimes that works; it lets you hold the subject at a safe distance. Safe, meaning the kind of distance that opens you up to thinking about your own life. Have you ever thought about that? Are you living well? Whose life are you living? It’s no longer about whether they would be proud of you. That’s over. Start thinking about tomorrow today.





The English




They were already very close; all they had to do now was leave the square and walk another two hundred metres.

“If he starts screaming for the whole street to hear again, this time you’ll go up without me,” she said.

Ever since the boys were born, Sunday lunches at her in-laws’ had become obligatory. Luckily not every week, but at least once a month. The same script played out every single time.

As they came round the corner, they looked at the same spot—the second-floor balcony. He was already there, waiting, though it was only quarter to two. You could see him from far off, a redheaded old man gesticulating energetically. The closer they got, the clearer it was that he was doing more than merely flinging his arms about.

“How old are you, for God’s sake?! Can’t you agree on a time and keep it?! I’ve been standing here for fifteen minutes, dying of fear something’s happened to you! How dare you put me through this?!”

“I mean it. I’m not putting up with this any longer.”

“Mum, come on, don’t worry. Grandpa’s just like that.”

She looked at her husband. He spread his hands helplessly and smiled at her kindly, lifting his bushy eyebrows. She didn’t want to come across as hysterical, but she’d had enough of these balcony scenes long ago.

“He acts like a lunatic. Maybe this time you could actually say something to him?” she asked, though she knew perfectly well what the answer would be.

“What am I supposed to say to him? He’s telling the truth—he’s dying of fear, and there’s nothing we can do about it. Let’s agree that when he dies, we’ll stop coming.” He looked at her with a warm smile.

“Do you think Grandpa’s going to die today?” one of the boys asked.

“I hope not yet.”

“And you, Mum? Would you like Grandpa to die today?”

“I’d rather not. We’ll have more than enough excitement as it is.”

Ignoring the ever-louder shouts coming from the balcony, they reached the stairwell, passed the letterboxes and started up the stairs. They never took the lift; it was so small there was no chance all four of them would fit.

Grandpa was waiting in the open doorway. First he bent down to the older grandson, hugged him too hard and a little too long.

“My firstborn, my firstborn. Let me look at you. My, don’t you look clever. Who would have thought you’d ever make it into the world!”

Then it was the younger one’s turn. He hugged him too hard as well.

“You’ve got black curls; maybe they’ll lighten. Good thing you’re not ginger.”

Without taking his eyes off the boys, he embraced his son, then finally greeted his daughter-in-law.

“You’ve borne me beautiful grandsons. Come in, come in.”

“Dad, could you spare us the shouting from the balcony next time? We arranged for two o’clock, we got here fifteen minutes early, and you’re yelling that we’re late?”

“Everything’s fine, don’t worry. I was afraid a car had hit you, or some other misfortune... The important thing is that we’re all alive and together. By the way, I wonder whether I’ll live to see great-grandchildren. Well, boys, can I count on you?”

“No knowing whether you’ll live that long,” said the younger grandson.

“Well now, aren’t you funny,” Grandpa said with a smile.

After the greetings they went into the dining room, where a large round table stood. White tablecloth, plates with a gold border and silver cutlery—everything had already been laid. At the table sat an old woman, tiny and shrivelled. She had had Alzheimer’s for years and didn’t react to the noise filling the room.

“Boys, say hello to Grandma,” their father said.

They didn’t like going near her; they didn’t understand what was happening to her. She rarely spoke, and never in Polish. They would go up, kiss her quickly on the cheek and sit down in their places. Sometimes the faintest smile would pass over her wrinkled face; it was hard to say whether she was aware of it.

Lunch was cooked by Grandpa’s housekeeper. She would set the pots and pans on the windowsill and leave, despite his sincere invitations to sit down at the table with everyone else. Chicken broth with homemade noodles, minced-meat cutlets, mashed potatoes, and lettuce dressed with cream, which nobody but Grandpa ate. The youngest grandson bolted down his whole meal so he could get a second helping of soup before it disappeared back into the kitchen. Then he politely asked whether he might leave the table and lay down on the floor, where he played with his toy cars.

The rhythm of these lunches never changed. The whole thing lasted about an hour and a half; then Grandpa took a nap, and they left, with no one trying to stop them.

“The bodybuilder from Dachau lifted a brick and got winded,” the younger grandson said one Sunday, lying on the floor with his belly full of soup.

“Oh God!” Grandma groaned—in Polish.

Silence fell, and the parents’ eyes turned to the older son.

“I didn’t teach him that!”

“My friends at school did,” replied the youngest member of the family, utterly unruffled.

“Why did you decide to share that with us just now?” his father asked.

“I remembered it.”

“Well, listen to him—he remembered! And do you know that some people never managed to forget?” Grandpa asked, upset.

“I do.”

“And how do you know that, if I may ask?”

“From you. You talk about it all the time. I don’t understand why you’re always talking about Jews.”

“Because we are Jews,” Grandpa replied.

“All of us?”

Grandpa looked around and smiled.

“Yes, all of us.”

“Me too?”

“You too.”

The boy went back to his toy cars for a moment.

“I’d rather be English,” he said after a long silence.

“Shall I tell you something?” Grandpa asked.

“Go on.”

“I’d rather be English too.”

Grandpa reached for his glass of tea, but by then it had cooled enough to lose his interest. He got up from the table and went to take a nap.





I Tell Everyone




He was the prankster sort. He loved medicine and women, in exactly that order. Half the town loved him back—the half he treated. The other half split between those he drove up the wall and those who were indifferent. He was red-haired and freckled—in other words, hot-blooded. He could punch someone in the face out of nowhere, drink half a litre, and carry on as if nothing had happened.

He had managed to live into the second half of the twentieth century and decided to make use of the chance: to live, without giving up pleasure. He considered his ration of dramas used up. His wife took charge of raising their child; he got involved only when a situation called for a doctor. He thought a father’s lot was thankless, like in that joke about the little boy who wouldn’t eat. The whole family worried: he was skinny, he ate nothing. At last the father couldn’t stand it any longer and asked what the boy would most like to eat. The little boy thought it over and said: shit. What do you mean, shit? Just shit. Shit on a plate. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, then he got up and went to the bathroom. He came back with a plate of shit. Put it in front of the boy. The boy looked at it and said he wanted fried shit. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, then he got up and went into the kitchen. He came back and put it in front of the boy. The boy looked at it and said he wanted Daddy to taste it. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother. He tasted it, said it was excellent, and urged the boy to start eating at last. I’m not eating it, because Dad ate the best bit.

The household ran, the son grew like a weed, and the red-haired doctor kept meeting new women and spending blissful moments with them. When friends asked how he was doing, he told the joke about the Jew who went to a priest to confess. The priest asks whether the penitent is Catholic. He isn’t. Then why has he come? Because it matters to him very much. The priest goes into the confessional, and the Jew starts telling him about all the women he’s recently slept with. This blonde one, that one with curly hair, and another had such tiny breasts... Why did you choose me, of all people, to tell me this? the priest asks. I tell everyone.

His wife pretended not to hear. After what they had been through, she knew life could bring graver problems. Sometimes she would complain to a friend, and once the friend got home she would ask her husband to have a word with the doctor. They would arrange to meet, go out for vodka, and the family friend would gently reveal the mission he’d been sent on. The redhead would look at him and say that at just this moment he’d remembered a joke about a tax collector who comes to the Jewish community. What do you do with the wax from melted-down candles? We collect it and make new candles from it. I see. And what do you do with the crumbs from matzah? We make delicious matzah balls out of them. I see. And when you circumcise little boys, what do you do with those, well, you know? Ah, those. We collect them, send them to the tax office, and they send us back pricks like you.

He put a stop to such friendly impulses in an instant. If the other man persisted, he would call the waitress over and ask her to open a window. Nothing to be afraid of, he’d say, my friend isn’t much of an eagle, he won’t fly out.

His son married a tiny brunette, a graduate of the Academy of Fine Arts. True, the doctor had had other plans for him, but he accepted it and stopped looking for the ideal match.

The red-haired doctor’s wife began to suffer from rapidly advancing Alzheimer’s. He took excellent care of her. He brought a young woman from the provinces into the house. She cooked, looked after the sick wife, and got on very well with the doctor too.

To his grandsons he became a marvellous doctor and an odd grandfather who had his flashes. Once a year he took them shopping. To everyone’s surprise, he was capable of buying a billiard table or a bicycle. Sometimes he took them to a restaurant and, after ordering, quickly disappeared. Only years later did the boys understand that the main reason for those outings was by no means a wish to spend time with them, but a waitress in a short skirt. He didn’t know how to be a grandfather, but he was proud that the family was growing.

A year after his wife’s death, he invited the older grandson to lunch. After they had chosen what to eat, he did not get up from the table as he usually did. While they were waiting for the food, Grandpa remembered a joke about Rosenzweig, who spends the whole night pacing round the house. Around five in the morning his furious wife gets up and asks him what he’s pacing for. I’m pacing because I can’t sleep, Rosenzweig replies. Why can’t you sleep? Because I owe Hozenduft money. A lot? A lot. Then call him and tell him you’re not going to pay him back. The husband thinks for a moment and rings. Rosenzweig speaking. Yes, I know what time it is. I only wanted to tell you I’m not paying the money back, good night. And he puts the receiver down. And now what? he asks his wife. Go to sleep. Now he’s the one not sleeping.

The grandson, though trained in the art of the joke from childhood, cannot understand why Grandpa is telling him this now. A pretty waitress brings the food. Grandpa smiles at her, but stays at the table.

“Listen, there’s a Jewish law that says a healthy man shouldn’t be alone. It also says that when a wife dies, her husband may marry her sister. Your grandmother’s sister died back then, when everyone else did.”

What now? the grandson thinks. Grandpa takes a deeper breath and announces that he’s getting married. He adds at once that his son will not be pleased with his choice of wife.

“How do you know?” the grandson asks.

“I’ve got a feeling. People rarely like their stepmothers. My half-sister couldn’t stand my mother.”

“Did she have reason to?” the grandson asks.

“Oh, she had every reason!”

The grandson worries that he has been deputised to break this strange news to his father. He can feel that it will be neither easy nor pleasant. Relief comes unexpectedly.

“But let’s not say anything to your father for now...” Grandpa decrees, then lets his voice trail off. “A second wife, one for old age, you choose without thinking about anyone else. You look for someone who will understand you. Even if you know she speaks before she thinks, that she’s difficult, that she hurts everyone around her. You go through with it because you want to drown out loneliness. You think you’ll deal with the mismatch later.”

The grandson is in his teens and senses that later may be too late, but these are adult matters, after all. He does not yet know that in his family the stepmother type will prove hereditary.







Underwater Odyssey




A lie doesn’t have short legs. His father always said it did, but he had no idea how wrong he was. It rises to the surface whenever too little attention is paid to hiding it.

When he was little, he didn’t lie—they said he made up beautiful stories. By the time he became a teenager, they called it lying. He didn’t think much about it; he ran his life to suit himself.

He forged his value system to fit the needs of the moment.

The most important rule: calm parents, calm life. There were gaps in the system, but over time it became an integral part of the cycle: keep his parents calm between parent-teacher meetings; survive their return from a meeting with dignity and hope for a better tomorrow. Find solace in the hush after a domestic row, and don’t take reproaches or anger personally. After a few days, give them something to lift their hearts; it didn’t have to be true, it just had to work for the time being. Remember that “calm parents, calm life” was the priority, and that the best moments came between parent-teacher meetings. Don’t charge recklessly, but don’t give them a walkover either.

He was a cheerful child, the first in his family to be born after the war. He sensed that they had little tolerance for unreliability. Quite naturally, he developed a modus operandi. You could say he took his cues from nature. He flowed like a stream—towards the point of least resistance, not where he was expected to go. A principle as simple as it was frowned upon.

The door opened and a shaft of light fell into the cinema.

“Is there anyone here by the name of—” and then came his surname.

He thought he must have misheard.
