PROLOGUE

The swallowed slip of paper lodges in the esophagus, somewhere near the heart. It soaks in saliva. The black ink, specially prepared, slowly dissolves, and the letters lose their shape. Inside the human body a word splits in two: into substance and essence. When the first vanishes, the second, left without form, lets itself be absorbed by the tissues of the body, for essence is forever in search of a material bearer—even if that should become the cause of many misfortunes.

Jenta wakes, though she was already almost dead. She feels it clearly now; it is like pain, like the current of a river, a trembling, a pressure, a movement.

Around her heart a faint vibration returns; the heart beats weakly but evenly, sure of itself. Warmth flows once more into Jenta’s dried-up, bony chest. Jenta blinks and with difficulty raises her eyelids. She sees the worried face of Elisza Szor bent over her. She tries to smile at him, but she does not command her face to that degree. Elisza Szor’s brows are knit; he looks at her reproachfully. His lips move, but no sound reaches Jenta’s ears. Hands appear from somewhere—the old Szor’s large hands, reaching for her neck and slipping under the blanket. Awkwardly, Szor tries to turn her limp body onto its side and look beneath it, at the sheet. No, Jenta does not feel his efforts; she feels only warmth and the presence of the bearded, sweating man.

Then suddenly, as if from some blow, Jenta sees everything from above—herself, and Szor’s balding crown, for in his struggle with her body his cap has fallen off.

And from then on, that is how it is—Jenta sees everything.



I. THE BOOK OF FOG

1

1752, Rohatyn

It is late October, early morning. The dean stands on the porch of the rectory waiting for the carriage. He is used to rising at dawn, but this time he feels half awake and scarcely knows how he came to be here—alone before a sea of fog. He does not remember getting up or dressing, or whether he has eaten yet. He looks in surprise at his stout boots protruding from beneath his cassock, at the somewhat frayed skirts of his faded wool cloak, and at the gloves he is holding in his hand. He slips on the left one; inside it seems warm and fits his hand perfectly, as if hand and glove had known each other for years. He breathes more easily. He touches the bag slung over his shoulder, mechanically feeling the rectangular edges, hard and raised like scars under the skin, and slowly remembers what is inside—the heavy, familiar, pleasing shape. Something good, something that brought him here, some words, some signs—all of it deeply bound up with his life. Ah yes, now he knows what is there, and that knowledge begins little by little to warm his body, and the fog seems somehow more transparent for it. Behind him is the dark mouth of the doorway, one half closed; the cold must already have set in, perhaps even a frost has spoiled the plums in the orchard. Above the door an indistinct inscription—he sees it without looking, because of course he knows what is written there, after all he commissioned it himself; two craftsmen from Podhajce spent an entire week carving the letters into the wood, for he had ordered them made decorative:

TODAY WHAT WAS, TOMORROW AFTER IT.

WHAT HAS FLED, WE CAИNOT CATCH

In the word CANNOT, much to his annoyance, the N is written backwards, like its mirror image.

Irritated by this for the Lord knows which time, the priest jerks his head sharply—and that wakes him completely. That reversed letter is an И... What carelessness! One must watch their hands all the time, keep an eye on them at every step. And because those craftsmen were Jews, they made the inscription somehow Jewish too: the letters are too curly, too wavering. One of them even argued that such an N was possible, prettier even, because the diagonal ran from bottom to top and from left to right, the Christian way, whereas the other way round would be the Jewish way. This petty irritation restores his senses, and now Father Benedykt Chmielowski, dean of Rohatyn, sees where that feeling had come from, that he was still asleep—he is standing in a fog the color of his bed linen, grayish; a dirty off-white, immense reserves of gray that are the world’s lining. The fog is motionless, tightly filling the whole yard; behind it loom the familiar shapes of the great pear tree, the wall, and farther off the wicker cart. It is an ordinary sky-cloud that has fallen to earth and stuck to it belly-down. He read about that yesterday in Comenius.

Now he hears the familiar creaking and rumbling that on every journey unfailingly puts him into a state of creative meditation. Only after the sound do Roszko, leading the horse by the bridle, and the dean’s chaise emerge from the fog. At the sight of it the priest feels a surge of energy; he strikes his palm with the glove and clambers up onto the seat. Roszko, taciturn as ever, adjusts the harness and throws the priest a long look. The fog has grayed his face and makes him seem older than ever, as though he had aged overnight, though he is a young fellow.

At last they set off, but it is as though they were standing still; only the swaying of the vehicle and the soothing creak bear witness to movement. They have traveled this road so many times, for so many years, that there is no longer any need to look at the scenery, and no landmarks will be needed. The priest knows they have now reached the road that runs along the edge of the forest, and along it they will go as far as the crossroads, where the roadside shrine stands, one he himself had erected years earlier, when he took over the parish in Firlejów. For a long time he had wondered whom to put in that shrine, and Saint Benedict, his patron, came to mind, or Onuphrius the hermit, whom a palm tree miraculously fed with dates in the desert, while every eighth day angels brought him Christ’s Body from heaven. Firlejów too was meant to be such a desert for the priest when he came here after years spent educating Dymitr, the son of His Excellency Lord Jabłonowski. But after reflecting on the matter, the priest decided that the shrine was not to be built for him and the satisfaction of his own vanity, but for the common people, so that at the meeting of the roads they might have somewhere to rest and from there lift their thoughts to heaven. So upon a whitewashed masonry pedestal there came to stand Our Lady, Queen of the World, with a crown on her head. Beneath her small pointed slipper a serpent writhed.

But today she too is fading into the fog—the shrine and the crossroads as well. Only the tops of the trees can be seen, a sign that the fog is beginning to settle.

“Look, sir, Kaśka won’t go on,” Roszko says gloomily when the chaise stops. He climbs down from the box and crosses himself several times with broad sweeps.

Then he bends down and peers into the fog as if looking into water. Beneath his best red coat, though somewhat faded now, his shirt shows.

“I don’t know where to drive,” he says.

“What do you mean, you don’t know? We’re already on the Rohatyn road,” the priest says in surprise.

And yet! He gets down from the chaise and follows the servant; helplessly they circle the carriage, straining their eyes into the whiteness. They think they see something, but eyes that cannot fasten on anything begin to play tricks. Fancy such a thing happening to them! Why, it is like getting lost in one’s own pocket.

“Hush!” says the priest suddenly, raising a finger and listening. And indeed, somewhere to the left, out of the banks of fog, comes the faint sound of water.

“Let us follow that sound. Water is flowing,” the priest decides.

Now they will creep very slowly along the river called the Gniła Lipa. The water will guide them.

Soon the priest relaxes in the chaise, stretches out his legs, and lets his eyes wander over the sea of fog. At once he falls into his traveling reverie, for a man thinks best in motion. Slowly, reluctantly, the mechanism of his mind stirs to life; gears and cogs catch, setting the driving wheels in motion, just like the clock that stands in the hall of his rectory, bought in Lwów; he paid dearly for it. In a moment it will strike bim-bom. Could the world have begun in just such a fog, he wonders. After all, the Jewish historian Josephus Flavius maintains that the world was created in autumn, during the autumnal aequinoctium. One might think so because there were fruits in paradise; if an apple hung on the tree, it must have been autumn... There is some reason in that. But at once another thought comes to him: what kind of argument is that? Could not almighty God create such miserable fruits specially, in any season of the year whatsoever?

When they reach the main road leading to Rohatyn, they join the stream of pedestrians, riders, and wagons of every kind that emerge from the fog like little bread figures shaped at Christmas. It is Wednesday, market day in Rohatyn; peasants’ carts go by loaded with sacks of grain, cages of poultry, every sort of farm produce. Among them briskly stride traders with every possible sort of ware—their stalls, cleverly folded for the moment, can be carried on their backs like shoulder-yokes, and in a moment they will become tables covered with gaudy fabrics, wooden toys, eggs bought up in the villages for a quarter of their price... The peasants are also leading goats and cows for sale—the animals, frightened by the uproar, dig in their heels in the puddles. A wagon races past them under a torn tarpaulin, full of noisy Jews converging on the fair in Rohatyn from the whole surrounding area, and behind them forces its way a rich coach which, in the fog and the crush on the road, has a hard time preserving its dignity—the pale lacquered doors are black with mud, and the coachman in his blue cape looks none too pleased; clearly he had not expected such confusion and is now desperately scanning for a chance to get off this devilish road.

Roszko is stubborn and will not let himself be pushed into the field; he keeps to the right side and, driving with one wheel on the grass and the other on the road, moves forward briskly. His long, melancholy face flushes, and some damned grimace comes over it. The priest glances at him and is reminded of an engraving he was looking at only yesterday—there were fiends on it, and they wore exactly the same grimaces Roszko does now.

“Make way for the reverend sir! Gee up! Move aside, good people!” Roszko shouts.

The first buildings rise before them all at once, without warning. The fog must alter one’s sense of distance, because even Kaśka seems startled by it. Suddenly she shies, jerking the shafts, and if not for Roszko’s firm reaction and his whip, she would overturn the chaise. Perhaps Kaśka was frightened by the sparks flying from the forge fire, or by the restlessness of the horses waiting their turn to be shod...

Farther on there is an inn, miserable and poor, like a village hut. The sweep of a well sticks up over it like a gallows, thrusting through the fog, its end vanishing somewhere high above. The priest sees that a dusty coach has stopped there; the exhausted coachman has lowered his head almost to his knees and does not jump down from the box, nor does anyone come out from inside. But already a tall, thin Jew is standing before it, and beside him little girls with tangled hair. That is all the dean sees, because the fog devours every passing sight; it disappears somewhere, soaking away like a melted snowflake.

Here is Rohatyn.

It begins with clay hovels roofed with straw thatch, which seem to press the buildings down into the earth; but the closer one comes to the market square, the more slender the houses grow, the lighter the thatch becomes, until at last it gives way to wooden shingles on little town houses of clay, unfired brick. There are also the parish church, the Dominican monastery, Saint Barbara’s church by the market square, and farther on two synagogues and five Orthodox churches. Around the square little houses stand like mushrooms, and in each some trade. The tailor, the rope-maker, the furrier, all Jews, and beside them a baker named Bochenek, which always pleases the dean, because it reveals some hidden order that might be made more visible and consistent, and then people would live more virtuously. Next to that the establishment of the swordsmith called Luba; its façade stands out for its affluence, the walls freshly painted blue, and above the entrance hangs a great rusty sword—plainly this Luba is a good craftsman and his customers have deep pockets. Farther on the saddler, who has set a wooden trestle before his door, and on it a beautiful saddle, with stirrups perhaps silvered, they shine so brightly.

Everywhere there is the cloying smell of malt, permeating every article set out for sale. A man could eat his fill on it, as on bread. In Babińce, on the outskirts of Rohatyn, there are several small breweries; it is from there that this filling smell spreads through the whole district. Many stalls here sell beer, and the better shops have both vodka and mead in stock, mostly trójniak. The store of the Jewish merchant Wakszul offers wine, true Hungarian and true Rhine wine, and a somewhat tart one brought here all the way from Wallachia.

The priest moves along the stalls constructed out of every possible material—boards, pieces of coarse-woven cloth, wicker baskets, even leaves. Some good woman in a white kerchief is selling pumpkins from a cart, and their bright orange color draws the children. Beside her another extols little rounds of cheese laid out on horseradish leaves. Farther on stand many other market women who trade because they have been widowed or have drunkard husbands: sellers of oil, sellers of salt, sellers of linen. He usually buys from this woman with the meat pies, and now he sends her a pleasant smile. Behind her are two stalls decorated with a green bough, which means that freshly brewed beer is sold there. And here is the rich stand of Armenian merchants—beautiful light fabrics, knives in ornate sheaths, and then vizina, dried fish, whose faint odor seeps into the wool Turkish rugs. Farther on, from a box hung from his thin shoulders, a man in a dusty caftan sells eggs packed by the dozen in baskets woven of grass. Another offers eggs in lots of sixty, in large baskets, at a competitive, almost wholesale price. Bagels hang all over the baker’s stall—one has fallen into the mud, and now a little dog is eating it with relish.

They trade in whatever they can. Also in flowered fabrics, kerchiefs and shawls straight from the bazaar in Istanbul, children’s shoes, fruit, nuts. A man by a fence has a plow and nails in every size, from pinlike ones to great big ones for building a house. Beside him a buxom woman in a starched cap has laid out clappers for the night watchmen: small ones—whose sound is more like the night music of crickets than a summons to sleep—and large ones, which, on the contrary, would wake the dead.

How many times the Jews had been forbidden to trade in things connected with the Church. Priests and rabbis alike had thundered over it, but to no avail. So here there are beautiful prayer books, with ribbon markers between the pages, with marvelously silvered letters embossed on the covers that, when one runs a fingertip over them, seem warm and alive. A clean, almost elegant man in a fur cap holds them like relics—wrapped in thin paper, creamy tissue, so that this dirty, foggy day should not stain their innocent Christian pages, fragrant with printer’s ink. He also has wax candles, and even holy pictures with haloes.

The priest approaches one of the traveling booksellers, hoping to find something in Latin. But all the books are Jewish, and beside them lie things whose purpose the priest does not know.

The farther the eye strays down the side streets, the more misery comes out, like a dirty toe from a torn shoe; coarse, quiet poverty bent to the ground. These are no longer shops, no longer stalls, but doghouses cobbled together from thin boards scavenged from rubbish heaps. In one of them a cobbler repairs shoes that have already been sewn, resoled, and patched many times over. In another, hung all about with iron pots, sits a tinker. His face is thin and hollow, his cap pulled down over a forehead full of brown blotches. The dean would be afraid to have pots mended by him, lest some terrible illness pass to others with the touch of that poor wretch’s fingers. Beside him an old man sharpens knives and every kind of sickle and scythe. His workshop consists of a stone wheel slung around his neck. When he gets something to sharpen, he sets a primitive wooden frame on the ground—a few leather straps turn it into a simple machine whose hand-driven wheel laps at the metal edges. Sometimes the machine throws off a few genuine sparks, which fall into the mud, much to the delight of the filthy, scabby children. He will earn pennies at his trade. At least, with the help of that wheel he could drown himself in the river; that is one more advantage of the profession.

Women in rags gather shavings and dung from the street for fuel. Beneath those rags it is hard to tell whether this is Jewish poverty or Orthodox or Catholic. Yes, poverty has neither faith nor nationality.

*Si est, ubi est?* the priest asks himself, thinking of paradise. Certainly not here in Rohatyn, nor, it seems to him, anywhere on Podolian soil. And if anyone thinks it is better in the large cities, he is badly mistaken. It is true the priest has never in his life got as far as Warsaw or Kraków, but he knows one thing and another from the stories of the Bernardine Pikulski, who has traveled more than he, or from what he overhears here and there in manor houses.

Paradise—that is, the garden of delights—was removed by God to a beautiful and unknown place. And as it is written in the *Arca Noe*, paradise is somewhere in the land of the Armenians, high in the mountains, while Brunus claims it is *sub polo antarctico*, beneath the South Pole. The sign of paradise’s nearness is four rivers: Gebon, Philon, Euphrates, and Tygris. And there are authors who, unable to find a place for paradise on earth, locate it in the air, some fifteen cubits above the mountains. But that strikes the priest as rather foolish. How would that work? Would those living on earth beneath paradise see it from below? Would they be looking at the saints’ heels?

On the other hand, one cannot agree with those who try to spread the false notion that the sacred text about paradise is meant to have only mystical significance, that is, to be taken in a spiritual or allegorical sense. The priest—not only because he is a priest, but out of his own deep conviction—holds that Holy Scripture must be taken literally.

He knows almost everything about paradise, for only last week he was finishing the chapter of his highly ambitious book, a chapter compiled from all the books he has in Firlejów, and he has one hundred and thirty of them. For some of them he rode even to Lwów, even to Lublin.

There is the corner house, modest enough—this is where he is going. Father Pikulski advised him so. The low double doors stand wide open; from within comes a spicy smell unlike anything amid all this stench of horse dung and autumn damp, and another sharp scent as well, already known to the dean—coffee. The priest does not drink coffee, but he really must make better acquaintance with it.

The priest looks back, searching for Roszko; he sees him sorting through sheepskin coats with gloomy concentration, and beyond him the whole market absorbed in itself. No one is looking at the priest; all are engrossed in bargaining. Noise and uproar.

Above the entrance to the building there is a rather clumsily executed sign:

SZOR STORE OF GOODS

Then come Hebrew letters. By the door hangs a metal plaque, and beside it some marks, and the priest remembers Athanasius Kircher saying in his book that when a Jewish wife lies in childbed and they fear a witch, they write on the walls the words: “Adam, Hawa. Huc – Lilit,” which is to say: “Adam and Eve, come here, and you, Lilith—that is, witch—be gone.” This must be that. Evidently a child was born here not long ago.

The priest steps over the high threshold and is at once engulfed in warm spicy fragrance. It takes a moment for his eyes to get used to the dark, for the only light admitted here comes through a small window, and even that is blocked by flowerpots.

Behind the counter stands a gangling youth whose mustache has only just begun to sprout; he has full lips that first tremble slightly at the sight of the priest, then try to shape themselves into some word. He is completely taken aback.

“What is your name, boy?” the priest asks boldly, to show how sure of himself he feels in this low dark shop and to encourage the youth to talk, but the other does not answer. “*Quod tibi nomen est?*” he repeats formally, but the Latin that was supposed to serve understanding suddenly sounds too solemn, as though the priest had come here to perform an exorcism, like Christ in Saint Luke’s Gospel, who puts the same question to the possessed man. But the boy only opens his eyes even wider and repeats “bh, bh,” and then suddenly darts behind the shelves, knocking a braid of garlic hanging from a nail.

The priest has behaved foolishly; he should not have expected anyone here to speak Latin. He looks himself over critically—beneath his cloak protrude the black hair-cloth buttons of his cassock. That is probably what frightened the boy, he thinks—the cassock. He smiles to himself and is reminded of the biblical Jeremiah, who likewise nearly lost his head and stammered: *Aaa, Domine Deus ecce nescio loqui!*—Lord God, behold, I do not know how to speak.

From then on the priest calls the boy Jeremiah in his thoughts. He does not know what to do, now that the lad has vanished so suddenly. So he looks around the shop, buttoning his cloak. It was Father Pikulski who urged him to come here; he obeyed, but now it no longer seems to him such a good idea.

No one comes in from outside, for which the priest thanks God inwardly. It would not be an ordinary sight—a Catholic priest, the dean of Rohatyn, in a Jew’s shop, waiting to be attended to like some burgher-woman. Father Pikulski had advised him to go to Rabbi Dubs in Lwów; he himself had gone there and learned much from him. And the priest did go, but old Dubs had probably already had enough of Catholic priests questioning him about books. He was unpleasantly surprised by the request, and the thing that interested Father Benedykt most he did not have—or pretended not to have. He put on a polite expression and shook his head, clicking his tongue. And when the priest asked who might be able to help him, Dubs flapped his hands and, turning his head as if someone were standing behind him, made it plain that he did not know, and if he did know, he still would not say. Later Father Pikulski explained to the dean that it had to do with Jewish heresies, and although they boast that among them there are no heresies, it seems that for this one they make an exception and sincerely hate it, without mincing words.

At last Pikulski advised him to go to Szor. The large house with a shop by the market square. Yet he gave the priest a sidelong look as he said it, ironic—or so it seemed to the priest. Perhaps he should have handled these Jewish books through Pikulski? Though the dean does not much like him. He would not have had to stand here now, ashamed and sweating. But there is a great deal of contrariness in the priest, so he went himself. And there was still something else, not very rational—a little play on words had slipped into the whole matter; who would believe such things can affect the world. The priest had been working diligently over a certain passage in Kircher where the great ox Szorobor was mentioned. And perhaps the likeness of the words drew him here—Szor and Szorobor. Strange are the Lord’s decrees.

But where are those famous books, where is the figure who inspires fearful respect? The shop looks like an ordinary stall, though its owner is supposedly descended from the famous rabbi, the much-revered sage Zalman Naftali Szor. And here there is garlic, herbs, pots of spices, jars and little jars, and in them spices of every kind—pounded, ground, or still in their natural form, like those vanilla pods, clove buds, whole nutmegs. On the shelves, laid on hay, are bolts of fabric as well—silk perhaps, and satin, very bright, drawing the eye. The priest wonders whether he might not need something, but his attention is already caught by the clumsy label on a large dark-green jar: “Herba the.” He already knows what he will ask for when someone finally comes out to him—for a little of that herb that puts him in a better mood, which in the dean’s case means that he can work without fatigue. And it helps his digestion too. He would also buy some cloves to spice his evening mulled wine. The last nights have been so cold that his frozen feet would not let him concentrate on writing. He looks around for a bench, and then everything happens at once: from behind the shelves emerges a sturdy bearded man in a long wool gown, beneath which protrude Turkish boots with pointed toes. A thin dark-blue cloak is thrown over his shoulders. He squints as if he had just come up from a well. From behind his back peer out that same Jeremiah, who had been frightened before, and two more faces very like Jeremiah’s, curious and rosy. And from the other side, in the doorway from the market square, a small breathless boy—or rather a young man, for a fair little goatee has sprouted thickly on his chin—has just come to a stop. He leans against the doorframe and pants; plainly he ran here as fast as he could. He drills the dean with his eyes and smiles roguishly, showing healthy, wide-spaced teeth. The priest is not sure it is not a mocking smile. He prefers the dignified figure in the cloak, and it is to him that he addresses himself with extraordinary politeness:

“Pray forgive this intrusion...”

The other looks at him tensely, but after a moment the expression on his face slowly changes. Something like a smile appears on it. The dean suddenly understands that the man does not understand him, so now he begins differently, in Latin, joyfully certain he has found one of his own kind.

The Jew slowly shifts his gaze to the boy in the doorway, the breathless one, and the latter boldly comes inside, tugging down his dark-cloth jacket.

“I’ll translate,” he announces in an unexpectedly low voice with a soft Ruthenian sing-song, and, pointing a finger at the dean, says with emotion that this is a real priest, a true priest indeed.

It had not occurred to the priest that an interpreter would be needed; somehow he had not thought of it. He is embarrassed and does not know how to get out of this, because the whole matter, meant to be delicate, suddenly becomes public and in a moment will draw the whole fair in here. He would much rather step back out into the cool fog smelling of horse dung. He begins to feel hemmed in in this low room, in air thick with the smell of spices; besides, someone from the street is already peering in curiously to see what is happening here.

“I should like a word with the esteemed Elisza Szor, if he permits,” he says. “In private.”

The Jews are surprised. They exchange a few remarks among themselves, Jeremiah disappears, and only after a long moment of unbearable silence returns. Evidently the priest has been granted permission, and now they lead him behind the shelves. There are whispers, the light patter of children’s feet, muffled giggles—as though behind the thin walls there were crowds of other people, now peering through cracks in the wooden partitions at the dean of Rohatyn making his way through the recesses of a Jewish house. And it turns out too that the little shop by the market square is only the outpost of a much more elaborate structure, something like a beehive: rooms, passageways, little staircases. The whole house is larger and built around an inner courtyard, which the priest catches only a glimpse of through a small window in the room where they stop for a moment.

“I’m Hryćko,” says the boy with the little beard as they go along. The priest realizes that if he wanted to turn back, he would not even know how to get out of this beehive house. The thought makes him sweat, and then one of the doors opens with a creak and in it stands a slender man in the prime of life, with a pale, smooth, impenetrable face, a gray beard, a robe falling below the knees, and on his feet woolen stockings and black slippers.

“This is Rabbi Elisza Szor himself,” Hryćko whispers with feeling.

The room is small, low, and very plainly furnished. In the middle stands a broad table, on it an open book, and beside it in several stacks others—the priest’s eyes roam greedily over their spines, trying to read the titles. The priest knows very little about Jews in general, and of the Jews of Rohatyn he knows only them by sight.

It suddenly seems pleasing to the priest that both of them are small men. In the presence of tall ones he always feels somehow disconcerted. They stand facing one another, and for a moment the priest has the impression that the other man too takes satisfaction in the resemblance. The Jew sits down softly, smiles, and gestures toward a bench for the priest.

“With your permission, and in these extraordinary circumstances, I come to you entirely incognito, having heard much of your great wisdom and erudition...”

Hryćko stops him halfway through and asks the priest:

“In-co-gnito?”

“Why yes, which is to say I supplicate for discretion.”

“And what’s that? Sup-pli-cate? Dis-cre-tion?”

The priest falls silent, unpleasantly surprised. Some interpreter he has got, he sees; the fellow does not understand him. So how are they supposed to talk to each other? In Chinese? He will have to try to speak plainly.

“I ask that this remain secret, for I do not conceal that I am the dean of Rohatyn, a Catholic priest. But above all, an author.” He emphasizes the word “author” by lifting a finger. “And today I would prefer to speak here not as a clergyman, but precisely as an author, who labors diligently on a certain opusculum...”

“O-pus-cu-lum?” Hryćko’s doubtful voice pounces.

“...a small work.”

“Ah. Forgive me, Father, I’m unlearned in Polish, only in the ordinary language people speak. I know only what I’ve overheard around horses.”

“Around horses?” the priest asks in extreme surprise, annoyed with the poor interpreter.

“Well, I work with horses. Trade.”

Hryćko talks with the help of his hands. Szor looks at him with dark, unreadable eyes, and it occurs to the priest that perhaps he is dealing with a blind man.

“Having already read several hundred authors from cover to cover,” the priest continues, “borrowing here, buying there, I feel that many books have escaped me, and I have no access to them at all.”

Now he breaks off and waits for the other to speak, but Szor only nods with an ingratiating smile from which nothing follows.

“And since I heard that you have here quite a fine library, not wishing for anything in the world to incommode...” he corrects himself at once, reluctantly, “...to trouble or inconvenience, I gathered my courage, contrary to custom but for the good of others, to come here and...”

He falls silent, for the door flies open and into this low little room, without any announcement at all, walks a woman. Behind her, half-visible in the dark, faces peer in whispering. A small child whimpers for a moment and then suddenly falls silent, as if everything were to concentrate now on the woman: bareheaded, framed by thick curls, she strides in boldly, her gaze fixed somewhere straight ahead, not looking at the men at all; on a tray she carries a jug and dried fruit. She is wearing a wide flowered dress and over it an embroidered apron. Her pointed shoes tap on the floor. She is slight but shapely, and her figure catches the eye. Behind her patters a little girl carrying two glasses. She looks at the priest with such terror that, not watching where she is going, she runs into the woman ahead of her and falls. The glasses roll across the floor; fortunately they are thick. The woman pays the child no attention, but throws the priest one glance, quick and brazen. Dark, shadowed eyes flash, large and somehow bottomless, and her shockingly white skin flushes in an instant. The dean, who has no dealings with young women, is taken aback by this sudden intrusion; he swallows. The woman noisily sets down the jug and plate and the glasses picked up from the floor, and again looking straight ahead, goes out. The door slams. Hryćko, the interpreter, also seems discomfited. Elisza Szor, meanwhile, springs up, picks up the child, settles her on his knees, but the little girl wriggles free and vanishes after her mother.

The priest would lay his head on it that the whole entrance of the woman and child had been only so they could get a look at him. Really now! A priest in a Jewish house! Exotic as a salamander. And so what? Does not a Jewish doctor treat me? Are not my medicines ground for me by a Jew as well? And the matter of books too is, in a way, a matter of hygiene.

“Books,” says the priest, pointing at the spines of the folios and Elzevirs lying on the table. On each of them, in golden paint, two signs have been written, which the priest takes for the owner’s initials; he can recognize Hebrew letters.

He reaches for his ticket for this journey among the people of Israel and carefully lays before Szor the book he has brought with him. He smiles triumphantly, for this is Athanasius Kircher’s *Turris Babel*, a great work both in content and in sheer size, and the priest risked much hauling it here. What if it had fallen into that stinking Rohatyn mud? What if some cutthroat had snatched it from him at the market? Without it the dean would not be who he is, but would become some narrow parish priest, some Jesuit tutor in a nobleman’s house, some vain official of the Church with rings on his fingers and no liking for the world.

He pushes the book closer to Szor, as if presenting him with his wife. Now he taps the wooden boards lightly.

“I have more. But Kircher is the best.” He opens it at random, and they look at a drawing of the Earth, shown as a globe with the long, slender cone of the Tower of Babel upon it.

“Kircher proves that the Tower of Babel whose description we have in the Bible could not have been as high as it is represented. A tower reaching all the way to the sphere of the Moon would disturb the whole order of the cosmos. Its base, resting on the globe of the earth, would have to be enormous. It would block the sun, with catastrophic consequences for all creation. Men would have to use up all the wood and clay on Earth...”

The priest feels as though he were uttering heresy, and in fact he does not know why he is saying this to a silent Jew. He wants the other man to take him for a friend, not an enemy. But will that be possible? Perhaps it is possible to understand one another, even though one knows none of the other’s languages or customs, knows nothing of one another, of one another’s things and objects, smiles, hand gestures making signs, nothing; perhaps one can understand by means of books? Is that not the only possible road? If people read the same books, they would live in the same world, whereas as it is they live in different ones, like those Chinese Kircher wrote about. And there are also those—whole multitudes—who do not read at all; their minds are asleep, their thoughts simple, animal-like, like those peasants with empty eyes. If he, the priest, were king, he would decree that one day of corvée labor be set aside for reading; he would drive the whole peasant estate to books, and the Commonwealth would look different at once. Perhaps it is even a matter of the alphabet—that there is not just one, but many, and each builds thought differently. Alphabets are like bricks—from some, fired and smooth, cathedrals are built; from others, clayey and rough, ordinary houses. And though the Latin alphabet is certainly the most perfect, Szor, it seems, does not know Latin. So he points with a finger at one engraving, then another, and another, and sees the other man bend over them with growing interest, until at last he produces a pair of spectacles neatly framed in metal wire—Father Chmielowski himself would like very much to have such a pair; he must ask where to order them. The interpreter too proves interested, and so the three of them bend over the engraving.

The priest glances at them with satisfaction, pleased that he has caught them both on the hook, and sees gold and chestnut hairs in the Jew’s dark beard.

“We might exchange books,” he proposes.

He says that in his library in Firlejów he also has two more works by the great Kircher, *Arca Noe* and *Mundus subterraneus*, locked away, too precious to be reached for every day. He knows there are other titles as well, but he knows them only from references here and there. And he has gathered many other thinkers of bygone times too, including—he adds, to ingratiate himself—the Jewish historiographer Josephus.

They pour him compote from the jug and push toward him a plate of dried figs and dates. The priest puts them into his mouth devoutly; he has not eaten them in a long while—the unearthly sweetness immediately improves his mood. He understands that he ought now to lay out his business, high time indeed, so he swallows the sweets and comes to the point; before he has finished, however, he already knows he has spoken too soon and will get nowhere here.

Perhaps he knows it from the sudden change in Hryćko’s behavior. He would lay his head on it that the boy is adding something of his own to what he translates. He just does not know whether these are warnings, or on the contrary something meant to support the priest. Elisza Szor draws back slightly in his chair, tips his head backward, and half closes his eyes, as though consulting his inner darkness.

This lasts until the moment when, against his will, the priest exchanges a glance of understanding with the young interpreter.

“The rabbi is listening to the voices of the elders,” the interpreter whispers, and the priest nods as if he understands, though he does not. Perhaps this Jew really does have some magical contact with little demons of various kinds; there are plenty of such beings among the Jews, all those lamias and Liliths. Szor’s hesitation and his closed eyes convince the priest that it would have been better had he not come here at all. The situation is delicate and unusual. Let him not expose himself to disgrace.

Szor rises and turns toward the wall, lowers his head, and stands that way for a moment. The priest begins to grow impatient—is this a sign that he is to leave? Hryćko too has closed his eyes, and his long youthful lashes now cast shadows on cheeks covered with soft down. Could they have dozed off? The priest clears his throat softly; their silence has stripped him of what remained of his self-assurance. He already regrets having come.

Suddenly Szor, as if nothing were the matter, goes to the cabinets and opens one of them. Reverently he takes out a thick folio, marked with the same symbols as all the other books, and lays it on the table before the priest. He opens the book from the back and turns a page. The priest sees a beautifully executed title page...

“*Sefer ha-Zohar*,” Szor says reverently, and puts the book back into the cabinet.

“Who would read that to the priest...” Hryćko says consolingly.

The priest leaves on Szor’s table two volumes of his *New Athens* as encouragement for a future exchange. He taps them with his forefinger, then points to himself—right at the middle of his chest: I wrote this. They ought to read it, if they knew the language. They would learn much about the world. He waits for Szor’s reaction, but Szor only raises his brows slightly.

Father Chmielowski and Hryćko go out together into the cold, unpleasant air. Hryćko chatters on about something, while the priest studies him closely—his young face with the first signs of future beard growth, his long, curling lashes that give him a somewhat childish look, and finally his peasant clothes.

“Are you a Jew?”

“Eh, no...” Hryćko answers, shrugging. “I’m from here, from Rohatyn, from that house there. More or less Orthodox.”

“Then how do you know their speech?”

Hryćko draws nearer and walks almost shoulder to shoulder with the priest, clearly encouraged to this familiarity. He says that his father and mother died in the plague in 1746. They had done business with the Szors; his father was a craftsman, tanned hides, and when he died, Szor stood up for Hryćko, his grandmother, and his younger brother, Ołeś, paid off the father’s debts, and took the three of them under his protection. And so they live as neighbors now; he has more dealings with Jews than with his own people, and somehow he does not know when he came to understand their language, and speaks it like his own, fluently, which often comes in handy in various matters and dealings, because Jews, especially the older ones, are reluctant to use Polish and Ruthenian. Jews are not as people say they are, and the Szors least of all. There are many of them, and their house is warm and hospitable; they will always give you something to eat and a glass of vodka when it’s cold. Now Hryćko is learning his father’s trade, so that he can take over the tanning after him; there will always be a demand for that.

“And have you no Christian kin?”

“I do, but they live far away and don’t trouble much about us. Oh, and that’s my brother, Ołeś.” A little boy of perhaps eight comes running up to them, freckled all over. “Please don’t trouble yourself over us for nothing, reverend sir,” Hryćko says cheerfully. “God made man with eyes in front, not in the back of his head, which means a man should concern himself with what will be, not with what has been.”

The priest does indeed take this for proof of God’s thoughtfulness, though he cannot remember where in Scripture it is said.

“Learn their language from them, then you’ll translate those books.”

“Oh no, sir, books don’t draw me at all. Reading bores me. I’d rather go into trade; that’s what I like. Best of all horses. Or like the Szors: vodka and beer.”

“Ah, you’ve gone to the bad with them already...” says the priest.

“And what of it? Are those wares any worse than others? People need drink, for life is hard.”
