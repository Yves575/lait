Prologue


To See Nothing Anymore


Everything went dark. It was like a garment of mourning. And then, here and there, scintillations, like the blotches the sun leaves when you stare at it in vain behind tightly shut eyelids, just as one clenches a fist to resist pain or emotion.

Of course, she had not described it that way at all. In the mouth of a fresh, anxious ten-year-old girl, distress comes out bluntly, without flourish or lyricism.

— Mom, everything’s black!

Mona had blurted out the words in a strangled voice. A complaint? Yes, but not only that. In spite of herself, she had let a note of shame slip into it, and whenever her mother caught that note, she took it seriously. For if there was one thing Mona never feigned, it was shame. The moment it nestled in a word, an attitude, an intonation, the die was cast: some unpleasant truth had entered.

— Mom, everything’s black!

Mona was blind.





The effect seemed to have no cause. Nothing in particular had happened; she had been dutifully doing her math, a pen in her right hand, a notebook pinned beneath her left palm, at the corner of the table where her mother was studding a fat roast with garlic. Mona was delicately taking off a pendant that bothered her because it kept swinging over her exercise page, and because she had picked up the bad habit of hunching over when she wrote. She felt a heavy shadow fall over both her eyes, as though they were being punished for being so blue, so large, so pure. The shadow did not come from outside, as shadows ordinarily do when night falls or the lights in a theater are dimmed; it seized her sight from within her own body, from the inside. Some opaque layer had insinuated itself into her and cut her off from the polygons drawn in her school notebook, from the brown wooden table, from the roast farther away, from her mother in a white apron, from the tiled kitchen, from her father sitting in the next room, from their apartment in Montreuil, from the gray autumn sky above the streets, from the whole world. By some spell, the child was plunging into darkness.

Feverish, Mona’s mother telephoned the family doctor. She gave a confused description of the glaze over her daughter’s eyes and added, because the doctor asked her, that Mona did not seem to be suffering from any speech disturbance or paralysis.

— It sounds like a TIA, he blurted, unwilling to say more.





He ordered large doses of aspirin at once and, above all, Mona’s immediate transfer to the Hôtel-Dieu hospital, where he would call a colleague so she could be seen right away. He had not thought of him by chance: he was a marvelous pediatrician, also reputed to be a very good ophthalmologist and, incidentally, a gifted hypnotherapist. Normally, he concluded, the blindness should not last more than ten minutes, and he hung up. A good quarter of an hour had already gone by since the first cry of alarm.

In the car, the little girl was crying, striking her temples. Her mother held her by the elbows, but deep down she too would have liked to strike that small round fragile head, the way one bangs on a broken machine in the foolish hope of making it start again. Her father, at the wheel of his rattling old Volkswagen, wished he could catch whatever affliction his little girl had fallen victim to. He was angry, convinced that something had happened in the kitchen and was being kept from him. He kept turning over an inventory of every possible cause, from a burst of steam to a bad fall. But no—Mona said it a hundred times:

— It just came on by itself!

And her father would not believe it:

— You don’t just go blind like that!





And yet you do: you can also go blind “just like that”—Mona was proof. And today that someone was Mona, ten years old, with tears of fear pouring down her face—tears she perhaps hoped would wash away the soot that had clung to her eyes, on that October Sunday, as evening fell. No sooner had she reached the entrance of the hospital beside Notre-Dame, on the Île de la Cité, than she broke off her sobs and froze:

— Mom, Dad, it’s coming back!

Standing in the street, where a cold wind was blowing, she rocked her head backward and forward to help her perception flow back. Like a shade being raised, the veil obstructing her eyes was lifting. Lines reappeared, then the contours of faces, the relief of nearby objects, the texture of walls and every shade of color, from the brightest to the darkest. The child recovered her mother’s slight outline, her long swanlike neck and frail arms, and that of her father, more solidly built. At last, in the distance, she made out a gray pigeon taking flight, which filled her with joy. Blindness had seized Mona, then let her go. It had passed through her, the way a bullet tears the skin and comes out on the other side of the body—painful, certainly, but still leaving the organism to heal itself. “A miracle,” thought her father, who scrupulously counted how long the attack had lasted: sixty-three minutes.





In the ophthalmology department at the Hôtel-Dieu, there was no question of letting the little girl leave before they had run a battery of tests, provided a diagnosis, and written prescriptions. The anguish had certainly been postponed, but not entirely dispelled. A nurse directed them to a room on the first floor of the building. It was the office of the pediatrician alerted by the family doctor. Dr. Van Orst was mixed-race, and prematurely bald. His large, radiant white coat contrasted with the sickly green of the walls. His immense smile, which furrowed his face with small jovial wrinkles, made him likable; he was nonetheless the repository of many dramas. He stepped forward.

— How old are you? he asked in a voice roughened by tobacco.









Mona was ten. She was the only child of two parents who loved each other. Camille, her mother, was nearing forty. She was short, wore her hair cropped and tousled, and still had in her voice a distant trace of working-class Paris cheek. Her charm lay in what her man called her “slightly ramshackle” side, though it was supported by a formidable determination: in her, anarchy was forever braided with authority. She worked at a temp agency, a good employee—committed, conscientious, zealous. In the mornings, at least. Afternoons were another matter. She wore herself out doing volunteer work. Every cause was good enough for her, from lonely old people to abused animals. As for Paul, he was past fifty-seven. Camille was his second wife. The first had run off with his best friend. He wore a tie to distract from the frayed collars of his shirts and worked away as a small-time junk dealer, with a particular passion for 1950s American culture: jukeboxes, pinball machines, posters... And since it had all begun in his adolescence with a collection of heart-shaped key chains, he owned an impressive array of them, which he had no wish to sell and which, in any case, would have interested no one. With the rise of the internet, his shop, tucked away in Montreuil, had very nearly closed its doors. So he too had gone online, put his expertise to use with a website he updated constantly and translated into English. Despite a business sense close to nonexistent, he could count on a clientele of attentive collectors who regularly saved him from ruin. The previous summer he had repaired a 1955 edition of the Gottlieb Wishing Well pinball machine and made the handsome sum of ten thousand euros from it. A lifesaving transaction after months of scarcity... And then, once again, nothing. The crisis, people told him. Every day at the shop Paul drained a bottle of red wine and then jammed it, like a trophy, onto one of those hedgehog-shaped bottle-driers immortalized by Marcel Duchamp. He raised his glass alone, without finding a way to resent anyone. In his head he toasted Mona. To her health.









While a nurse led the child through the labyrinth of the hospital for various tests, Dr. Van Orst, settled deep in an enormous armchair, gave Paul and Camille an initial diagnosis:

— TIA, or transient ischemic attack.

That meant blood flow had temporarily ceased to supply the organs, and the cause of the malfunction would now have to be determined. But Mona’s case, he went on, puzzled him: on the one hand, the attack, extremely rare in a girl of her age, seemed violent, since both eyes had been affected and it had lasted more than an hour; on the other, it had left her ability to move and speak entirely untouched. The MRI would no doubt say more. They had to prepare for the worst, he added, looking uncomfortable.





Mona had to lie down on a table inside a horrible machine and submit to it docilely, without moving. They asked her to take off her pendant. She refused. It was a fine length of fishing line weighted with a tiny shell that had belonged to her grandmother and brought her luck. She had always worn it, and her adored Dadé wore the same one too. The two charms connected them to each other, she thought, and she did not want to feel torn away from her grandfather. Since the pendant contained no metal, they let her keep it. Then her head, her pretty head haloed with chestnut hair, half long and lit with tawny glints, and blessed with an adorable round mouth, was enclosed in a monstrous casing filled with the racket of a factory. For the fifteen minutes the torture lasted, Mona sang songs to herself without stopping, to hold out, to inject a little good cheer and life into that coffin. She sang herself a slightly mawkish lullaby her mother used to hum when tucking her in, long ago; she sang herself a pop song, one of those tunes that loop endlessly in supermarkets and whose video, full of slick-haired boys, she liked; she sang herself nagging advertising jingles; she also sang herself “Une souris verte,” remembering the day she had screamed the words to annoy her father, unsuccessfully.





The MRI results came in. Dr. Van Orst called Camille and Paul in and hastened to reassure them. There was nothing. Strictly nothing. On the cross-sectional images, the anatomy of the brain showed only uniform areas. No tumor, at least. More tests were run, endlessly. All through the night: from the very depths of her pupils to her inner ear, and through blood, bones, muscles, arteries. Nothing there either. The calm after the storm. Had there ever even been a storm?

The face of a clock forgotten in a corridor of the Hôtel-Dieu showed five in the morning. A nursery-rhyme image came into Camille’s mind: it was as if, she confided to her husband, exhausted, some evil being had stolen Mona’s two eyes before giving them back. As if he had got the wrong victim, Paul added. Or as if he had sent a sign, a warning, and was preparing to repeat his crime, they both thought in silence.





In the schoolyard, the bell rang. The stream of children, shepherded by Mme Hadji, made its way up to the second floor. The teacher warned her CM2 pupils that they would not be seeing Mona, their classmate, again until school resumed after the All Saints’ break. She herself had just been informed by Camille, who on the phone had explained all—or almost all—of the infernal night, without concealing how serious things were. Naturally, the children had questions. Had Mona been allowed to go on vacation a week before everyone else?

— She’s a little sick, was all the teacher offered, not very satisfied with the phrase herself.

— A little sick? Lucky her! cried Diego, in the third row, and his shrill voice met with the approval of the whole class.





Most children imagine illness as an open sesame to freedom...

At the back of the room, right beside the curtains dusted with chalk, Lili and Jade, Mona’s two best friends, who knew every corner of her bedroom, were even more envious. Ah, how they would have loved to be with her! “A little sick”? Fine, thought Lili, but she was surely spending her days in her father’s junk shop. And Jade, her eyes turned toward Mona’s empty place, delightedly imagined herself there with her, inventing all sorts of games and stories in that modest little shop full of old objects that smelled of America—a whole jumble of bright, amusing, mysterious things that set children dreaming. But Lili protested:

— No, no, when she’s sick it’s her grandpa Dadé who comes to look after her, and he scares me.

Jade gave a forced mocking laugh to show that she, nothing frightened her, and certainly not Mona’s grandfather. Yet deep down the little girl had to admit it: yes, in front of that old man, immense, skinny, scarred, speaking in a deep, mineral voice, she too was not exactly at ease...





— Hello, Dad, it’s me.

It was noon when Camille, her limbs numb, finally decided to call her father. Henry Vuillemin refused to use a cell phone; he invariably answered calls on his landline with a terse, searing little “Yes?” that left little room for enthusiasm. His daughter hated the ritual and regretted every time the days when her mother, still alive, used to pick up the receiver. She drew out the syllables.

— Dad, I have to tell you: something terrible happened last night.

She told him everything, in order, trying to master her emotion.

— Well? Henry asked, with a touch of impatience.





But Camille had held back her tears so hard as she spoke that a gigantic sob shook her body and choked her: she was unable to answer.

— Darling, well? her father pressed.

That unexpected darling gave her a rush of oxygen; she caught her breath and whispered:

— Nothing! For the moment, nothing. I think she’s all right.

Then Henry let out a long sigh of relief, tipped back his neck, threw back his head, and studied in the moldings on his ceiling the cheerful motifs of plump fruit, scrollwork, and spring flowers.

— Let me say a word to her.

But Mona, curled up in an armchair in the living room under a russet throw, had dozed off.

The poet Ovid described the moment when consciousness falls asleep as entry into an immense cave sheltering the god Sleep, languid and indolent. He imagined a cavern inaccessible to Phoebus, lord of the Sun. Mona had learned from her grandfather that, on the human scale, no journey is more regular than the one toward those mysterious and shifting lands... It was therefore wise not to neglect those territories through which one passes ceaselessly, all through life.









In the days that followed, Dr. Van Orst, at the Hôtel-Dieu, ordered further tests. They still revealed no particular anomaly. The explanation for those sixty-three minutes of blindness stubbornly refused to emerge, to the point that the doctor now balked at using the term “transient ischemic attack,” which presupposed a vascular deficiency he was no longer altogether sure of. In the absence of a clear diagnosis, he suggested to Mona—and to her parents—that they resort to hypnosis. The idea was met with dumb astonishment from Paul. As for the little girl, she was not at all sure she knew what the word meant. She associated it with the “scarf game” she had vaguely heard about at school, which terrified her. To correct this mistaken notion, Van Orst explained that by putting Mona into a hypnotic state, he could place her temporarily under his influence. Such an experience would then allow him to go back in time and guide her toward that original moment when her sight had gone out, make her relive it and, potentially, identify the cause. Paul objected angrily. It was out of the question; it was dangerous. Van Orst did not insist: for hypnosis to work effectively on a child, the child must surrender in complete trust. Yet between Mona’s prejudice and her father’s angry overreaction, the ground was now mined. Camille, for her part, said nothing.

So Van Orst prescribed the young patient a conventional course of treatment: weekly blood tests and blood-pressure checks, visits to the ophthalmologist, ten days of convalescence. He instructed Paul and Camille to watch for “any appearance of subjective signs of a symptomatic nature,” which meant they had to pay extremely close attention to their daughter’s sensations. On that score, he suggested consulting a child psychiatrist.

— It’s everyday prophylaxis rather than therapy properly speaking, he assured them.





Paul and Camille took in his recommendations only confusedly, but in truth there was just one question gnawing at them: “Is Mona in danger, in the long run, of losing her sight?” Curiously, Dr. Van Orst never once mentioned the threat of a final relapse and, despite the dread it inspired in them, the parents preferred to avoid the subject. They even told themselves that, after all, if the doctor was skirting it, then there was no need to bring it up.

Henry Vuillemin raised it head-on with his daughter. He was not the sort to run from questions, even when they opened onto abysses. Ordinarily sparing with phone calls, except when he wanted to hear Mona’s voice, he multiplied them that week. In a warm, impassioned voice, he badgered Camille: yes or no, was his darling granddaughter, the treasure of his life, going to become blind? Henry also insisted on seeing Mona, and Camille could hardly refuse him. She suggested that he come on All Saints’ Sunday, exactly one week after the attack of blindness. Paul, who could guess the drift of the conversation, secretly resigned himself and nearly tossed back a glass of rough Burgundy in one gulp. In front of his father-in-law, he felt miserably stupid. Mona, on the other hand, when she heard the news, stamped with impatience.





This grandfather, so full of years and strength, she loved. She also loved seeing how everyone who crossed his path let themselves be charmed by his endless silhouette and his heavy glasses with thick, almost square frames. In his company, she felt protected. And carried away. Henry had always made a point of speaking to her as he would have spoken to an adult. It was she who demanded that inequality and savored it, amused by it. She was never afraid of not understanding and laughed at mistakes and misunderstandings. For she watched her own language in return, and it was obviously more a game than anything at stake.

Henry had no wish to make a little performing monkey of her. He did not want to be one of those parody grandfathers who hunt down the mistakes of the young only to correct them in a pedantic tone. It was not in his nature. He had never made her do her homework, never meddled in report cards. Besides, he adored the way Mona spoke. More than that, he was fascinated by the turns her sentences took. Why? There he had no key. He could not understand it. For as long as he could remember, he had been obsessed, haunted by something in her childish language. Was it an extra element, or some indefinable lack? A quality or a defect? The impression was all the more troubling because it was not new: Mona’s “little music” had always harbored an enigma Henry was fully determined one day to uncover, by dint of listening.





Camille sometimes admitted her astonishment at a relationship she judged “too good to be true,” but she recognized that it worked beautifully and that her daughter was happy in it. And then Henry, who readily quoted Victor Hugo’s The Art of Being a Grandfather, reminded anyone who cared to listen of one of the cardinal principles of transmission: it matters little if one does not immediately understand everything someone says, as though every new word already had to be a full-grown tree in the immense orchard of the brain. The blossoming would come on the appointed day, provided furrows had been drawn and seeds planted.

In Henry Vuillemin, those furrows and seeds took the form of a stream of rich, assured words that caught hold of you from the very first intonation and never let go; of speech very simple, yet of such breadth that it was exhilarating; of a storyteller’s cadence that often quickened, then slowed, then colored itself with gentle emotion. It was a steamroller of worldly experience and tranquil erudition.

Thus, with this “Dadé,” the relationship was of a special kind. Sometimes between grandparents and grandchildren there forms a miraculous bond, due to the fact that, by a sort of existential curve, the elders return, from the heights of old age, to the feelings of their earliest youth and grasp, better than anyone, the springtime of life.





Henry Vuillemin lived in a beautiful apartment on Avenue Ledru-Rollin, just above the Bistro du Peintre, a narrow, wood-paneled establishment imitating the Art Nouveau style. He went down there every morning and had his habits there: a coffee and a croissant, the reading of the national papers, an exchange here and there with whoever happened to be in among the customers and with the waiters on break. He felt himself to belong to an older world and, as a ritual, walked very slowly to the Place de la Bastille, liked examining furniture in the windows on the Rue du Faubourg-Saint-Antoine, went back up toward the Place de la République along the median of Boulevard Richard-Lenoir, then cut across to Boulevard Voltaire. Late in the afternoon, at home, he pored over the art books piled to the ceiling. Henry, who stood one centimeter taller than General de Gaulle, reached for the most inaccessible ones without stepstool or ladder and, by a curious chance, they were regularly the very ones that attracted him most. His memory was prodigious, though one had to distinguish between his tendency to speak about what he knew and his personal memories, which he protected beneath layers of reserve. Mona knew the rule. The only taboo with her grandfather was Colette Vuillemin, who had left him a widower seven years earlier. Taking her cue from her father, Camille never mentioned her either. However much the child tried from time to time to open a breach, she came up against leaden silence. Colette was not spoken of. Ever. The sole exception to the taboo was that Henry kept around his neck the charm in homage to his dead wife. It was a pretty little cerith shell strung on fishing line, which he had picked up with her on the Côte d’Azur in the summer of 1963—he no longer knew exactly what day, but remembered the canicular heat and having sworn Colette a great many things. Mona, as we have said, wore the same pendant, inherited from her grandmother.





We all have our ways of swearing. Henry Vuillemin swore on “all that is beautiful on earth.” The expression surprised Mona, and whenever she heard it she automatically shrugged with a small, confused laugh: all that was beautiful on earth was at once a little everything and a little nothing. And then she wondered whether he, her revered grandfather, was part of it. Henry had plainly been an attractive young man and remained imposing, charming, spectacular. His octogenarian face, gaunt and sharp, radiated a vigor and intelligence of extraordinary seductiveness. But he was scarred. A scar tore through the right side of his face, beginning below the cheekbone and rising to the eyebrow. The wound must have been very painful. It had torn away not only a furrow of skin but a piece of the cornea. It was a war souvenir. A horrible one: on September 17, 1982, during a photo report in Lebanon for Agence France-Presse, a Phalangist militiaman had slashed him with a knife to stop him from going farther. He had been approaching the Shatila camp. Word had it that massacres were being committed there, that Palestinian refugees were being executed arbitrarily, without trial, in reprisal for the assassination of President Bachir Gemayel. He had wanted to verify it, to bear witness. They had barred his way with inhuman violence. Henry had lost a great deal of blood and the use of his eye. That infirmity, combined with his height and, over the years, an ever more pronounced thinness, had given his appearance something supernatural. The handsome reporter who had looked like Eddie Constantine had become a figure of legend.









On All Saints’ Day, Mona was in good shape. Her parents had done their best to make the leaden atmosphere of November cheerful. Jade and Lili, the two friends, had come by to watch an installment of Toy Story, the animated film in which toys come to life. They had engaged in some gentle horseplay. Jade especially. She was a pretty, mischievous little girl, with very fine Eurasian eyes, dusky skin, and hair combed to perfection. Yet she was animated by a surprising passion: making faces. She knew how to transform her harmonious face into a mobile, deranged theater through which odd and burlesque expressions passed like actors in a frenzy. Mona always demanded more, delighted.

At seven o’clock, the intercom buzzed. Paul pursed his lips and raised his eyebrows. Camille pressed the button.

— Dad?

It was indeed him, arriving to the minute. Paul, after greeting him, went off to take Jade and Lili back to their respective parents and, for the duration of that trip, Mona, her mother, and her grandfather found themselves alone in the apartment. After an irrepressible burst of joy, the little girl, who had taken care not to tell her two friends about her mishap, launched into a detailed account of her sixty-three minutes of ordeal and of what she had endured at the hospital. Camille did not interrupt her.





Meanwhile, while he listened to Mona talk and talk on, Henry was examining with wholly clinical detachment the places where the child lived. Even her room, for all the enchanting kitsch of the décor, struck him as terribly sad. That wallpaper patterned with garlands of flowers, those trinkets encrusted with glitter in the shape of hearts or animals, those pink or brown stuffed toys, those grotesque posters of stars barely out of adolescence, those plastic jewels, that furniture imitating that of cartoon princesses... The candy-bright colors of all that clutter caught him in the throat. In that whole ensemble dripping with bad taste, there were only two breaches through which some beauty showed. A sturdy American industrial lamp from the 1950s, with an articulated arm, which Paul had hunted down, given to Mona, and fixed to her little writing desk. And then, above the bed, under glass, an exhibition poster reproducing a painting. Colors of extraordinary subtlety, cool in tone, crackled there. They outlined a nude woman in profile, leaning forward, seated on a stool draped in white cloth, her left ankle resting on her right knee. In one corner one could read: “Musée d’Orsay Paris – Georges Seurat (1859–1891).”





Despite those exceptions, Henry was forced to make the dismal observation that childhood is, for reasons of convenience, steeped for the most part in futile and ugly objects. Mona was no exception to the rule. Beauty, true artistic beauty, entered her daily life only clandestinely. That was perfectly normal, Henry noted: the refinement of taste, the shaping of sensibility would come later. Except that—and the thought constricted his throat—Mona had come very close to losing her sight, and if her eyes were to go out forever in the coming days, weeks, or months, she would carry with her into the far reaches of memory only the recollection of gaudy, empty things. A whole life in the dark, forced to make do mentally with the worst the world produces, with memory offering no way out? It was impossible. It was terrifying.

Much to his daughter’s annoyance, Henry remained taciturn and distant throughout dinner. When at last Mona went off to bed, Camille, with a resolute air, turned up Coltrane’s saxophone on an old chrome jukebox, to cover their voices and make sure the little girl would hear nothing.

— Dad. For the moment Mona seems to be digesting... (she hesitated over the words) ...what’s just happened. But the doctor recommends follow-up with a child psychiatrist. It may feel strange to her, and I was wondering whether you could take her to the appointment, so that she feels reassured...

— A shrink? Is that really what’s going to stop her going blind?

— That’s not the question, Dad!

— I think it is, and it will remain so as long as you don’t dare ask it of the doctor! Doctor what’s-his-name again?

— His name is Van Orst, and he’s very good, Paul put in awkwardly, trying to join the conversation.





— Dad, wait, Camille went on. Listen to me. Paul and I are going to do everything to make sure nothing happens to Mona, do you hear? But she’s ten years old and we can’t pretend she’s been through nothing. The doctor says her psychological balance is a priority. So I’m only asking whether you’d be willing to take care of that, because I know Mona will trust you. Do you hear me, Dad?

Henry heard her perfectly well. But at that precise instant an Apollonian idea flared in his mind, in a fraction of a second, and he kept it jealously to himself. He would not take his granddaughter to a child psychiatrist, no... He would instead put her through a cure of a wholly different kind, one capable of counteracting the ugliness on which her young life was being fed.

Mona, who trusted him completely, who placed in him a confidence she granted no other adult, would have to accompany him where what the world has produced that is most beautiful and most human is preserved: she would have to accompany him into the museums. If, by some misfortune, Mona were one day to become blind forever, she would at least possess a kind of reservoir deep in her brain from which to draw visual splendors. So the grandfather had his plan... Once a week, according to an unchanging ritual, he would take Mona by the hand and lead her to contemplate a work—one work only—first in a long silence, so that the infinite delight of colors and lines might penetrate his granddaughter’s mind, then in words, so that she might go beyond visual enchantment and understand how artists speak to us of life, and how much they illuminate it.





For his little Mona, then, he imagined something better than medicine. They would go first to the Louvre, then to the Musée d’Orsay, and finally to Beaubourg. There, yes, there, in those places devoted to preserving what humanity had produced that was boldest and most beautiful, he would find a tonic for his granddaughter. Henry was not one of those art lovers content, apart from the world, with the sheen of flesh painted by Raphael or the cadence of a line traced in Degas’s charcoals. He loved the almost incendiary qualities of works of art. Sometimes he said, “Art is pyrotechnics or it’s hot air.” And he loved it when, in its totality or through some detail, a painting, a sculpture, or a photograph could fan our sense of existence.

At the very moment Camille was asking for his help, Henry was assailed by hundreds of images: the rocky forms behind the Mona Lisa, the monkey carved on the reverse of Michelangelo’s Dying Slave, the alarmed expression of the blond-curled child at the right of The Oath of the Horatii; the strange gelatinous loins of Goya’s Lamb; then the clods of earth in Rosa Bonheur’s Ploughing in the Nivernais; the butterfly-shaped signature Whistler uses in the portrait of his mother; the tottering little apse of Van Gogh’s church... Or else Kandinsky’s colors, Picasso’s fractures, or Soulages’s outrenoir. All of it surged up like so many signs calling him, asking to be seen, heard, understood, loved. Like a backfire against the ashes threatening Mona’s eyes.





Henry broke into a broad smile:

— Agreed, I’ll take Mona with me every Wednesday afternoon. From now on, I—and I alone—will handle this psychological follow-up. It will be our business, the two of us. Is that agreed?

— You’re going to find someone good, Dad? You’ll ask your old friends for advice?

— Is it agreed in principle? I’m taking care of it, with no questions and no interference from anyone.

— But you’re not going to pick a child psychiatrist at random, are you? Be very careful, do you hear me?

— Do you trust me, my dear?

— Yes, Paul answered firmly, cutting off any hesitation from Camille. Mona admires and respects you, and she loves you as she loves no one else, so yes, we trust you.

At her husband’s firm words, Camille nodded gently without adding anything. Henry felt his good eye crossed by a slightly moist gleam. Coltrane’s saxophone made the walls ripple. Mona, in her room, was asleep, watched over by Georges Seurat.

Sandro Botticelli


Learn to Receive




The great glass pyramid delighted Mona. Planted insolently in the middle of the Louvre’s stone pavilions, its ethereal shape, its transparency, the way it caught the cold November sun enchanted her. Her grandfather was not saying much. She could see perfectly well, though, that he was in an excellent mood, because he held her little hand in his with the assured tenderness of happy people and swung their arms loosely. His cheerfulness, though silent, radiated in a childlike way.

— The pyramid is so beautiful, Dadé! It looks like a big Chinese hat, Mona declared, threading her way through clusters of tourists in the forecourt.





Henry looked at her and mixed into his smile a sort of dubious pout. It gave him such a strange expression that the little girl snickered. They entered the glass structure, went through security, rode down an escalator, found themselves in the immense hall so little different from those of train stations or airports, and made for the Denon wing. The bustle around them was suffocating. Suffocating, yes, because most of the visitors who make up the crowd in a great museum do not know what they want to do; they create a general drift, infuse the air with that stagnant, hesitant, even slightly murky atmosphere peculiar to such places when they become victims of their own success.

In the midst of the din, Henry bent his immense, skinny legs to speak to his granddaughter eye to eye. He did this whenever he had something truly important to say to her. His mineral voice, pure and grave, rose above the surrounding clamor. It seemed to reduce to silence the vain babble and wearying outbursts of the whole universe.

— Mona, every week the two of us will go and look at one work—one work only, no more—at the museum. These people around us would like to swallow everything in a single gulp, and they get lost because they don’t know how to pace their desires. We will be much wiser, much more sensible. We will look at a single work, first without saying a word, for long minutes, and then afterward we’ll talk about it.

— Really? I thought we were going to the doctor. She had wanted to say “the child psychiatrist,” but she was not quite sure of the term.

— Tell me, Mona, do you want to go to the psychiatrist afterward? Is that important to you?

— Like I want to! Anything rather than that!





— Then listen to me carefully, my dear. There’ll be no need if you look attentively at what we’re going to see.

— Really? And is it bad not to go to the... doctor?

— No, it’s not bad. I swear it on all that is beautiful on earth.





After threading their way through a maze of staircases, Henry and Mona found themselves in a modest-sized room through which many people passed but where hardly anyone bothered to stop and look at the work enthroned there. Henry let go of his granddaughter’s hand and said to her with infinite gentleness:

— Now look, Mona. Take all the time you need to look, to really look.

And Mona stood there, intimidated, before a very damaged painting, badly cracked in several places, with bits missing, a painting that at first glance gave off the feeling of an altered and distant past. Henry too contemplated it, but what he was really watching was his granddaughter, sensing her wavering, her perplexity. There she was, knitting her brows, then stifling a slightly embarrassed laugh. He knew that even before a Renaissance masterpiece, a ten-year-old girl, however lively, curious, sensitive, and clever, could not immediately fall into ecstasy. He knew that, contrary to a common idea, one needs time to enter into the depth of art, that it is an arduous exercise and not an easy transport. He also knew that Mona, because it was he who was asking it of her, would play along and that, despite her embarrassment, she would scrutinize with the promised attention the forms, the colors, the texture.





The image was composed simply enough. At the far left, one could make out a fountain, before which, like a frieze, stood four young women with long, curly hair, astonishingly alike. They were holding one another by the arm, intertwining as if they formed a human garland, its rhythm given by the variety of their garments: green and mauve for the first, white for the second, pink for the third, yellow-orange for the fourth. This multicolored procession gave the impression of moving forward and, facing it, on the right side of the work, isolated against a neutral background, stood a fifth woman, young, extremely beautiful, wearing a magnificent pendant and a purple dress. She too seemed animated by a forward movement, as though she were going out to meet the procession. In fact she was stretching toward it a kind of cloth, into which one of the figures—the one in pink—was delicately placing something. What? Impossible to say. The object had been effaced. There was also, in the foreground, in one corner, a little blond-haired boy in profile, faintly smiling. The setting was entirely spare: only a truncated, very faded column on the right answered the fountain on the left, closing the scene.



Mona played along. But after six minutes it was already too much. Six minutes in front of a faded old image was an unusual and painful ordeal. So she turned toward her grandfather and started the conversation with an insolence only she could allow herself:

— Dadé, your painting is really battered! Next to it, your face looks brand-new...





Henry looked at the work and all the damage that scarred it. He bent down on his knees.

— You’d do better to listen to me instead of talking nonsense... A “painting,” you say! Wrong already! First of all, Mona, this is not a “painting.” It’s what’s called a “fresco.” Do you know what a fresco is?

— Yes, I think so... but I forgot!

— A fresco is a painting done on a wall, and it’s very fragile, because if the wall gets damaged—and walls crumble a great deal over time—well, then the painting gets damaged too...

— Why did the artist paint on that wall? Because it’s the Louvre?

— Not at all. It’s true that an artist might want to make a fresco at the Louvre, because the Louvre is the greatest museum on the planet and it would be natural for a painter to want to place his work there directly, so that it would be like the palace’s skin. But you see, Mona, the Louvre hasn’t always been a museum. It’s only been one for about two hundred years. Before that, it was a palace, where kings and the court lived. When the artist made this fresco, around 1485, he was not painting it for the walls of the Louvre but for those of a villa in Florence.

— Florence? (She absently fingered the pendant around her neck.) To me that sounds like the name of one of your old fiancées, before Grandma, right?





— It doesn’t ring a bell, but it’s not impossible! Now listen. Florence is an Italian city. In Tuscany, to be exact. And it is the cradle of what we call the Renaissance. In the fifteenth century—the Quattrocento, as the Italians say—Florence experienced an extraordinary effervescence. There were about a hundred thousand inhabitants, and the city was prosperous, thanks to trade and banking. And, you see, religious orders, senior political figures, and even private citizens from the upper reaches of society wanted to put their wealth to use and display their prestige by supporting the creation of artists of their own time. They were what we call great patrons. So painters, sculptors, and architects took advantage of their trust and the means they were given to create paintings, statues, and buildings of incredible beauty.

— I bet they were made of gold...

— Not exactly. It’s true that in the Middle Ages there were many beautiful paintings lavishly covered in gold leaf. That gave the object value, and besides it symbolized divine light! But in the Renaissance, painting gradually gives up the flashy effect of gilding in order to render reality as we see it, with its landscapes, the singularity of faces, animals, the movement of beings and things, of the sky and the sea.

— So people start loving nature, is that it?

— That’s exactly it: they begin to love nature. But you know, when we speak of nature, we don’t mean only what grows on the earth.

— Oh? What else do we mean?

— We also mean, more abstractly, human nature. Human nature is what we are deep down, with our zones of shadow and light, our faults and our qualities, our fears and our hopes. Now, you see, it is precisely this human nature that the artist is trying to improve.





— And how does he do that?

— If you tend your garden, you do nature good. You allow it to flourish. This fresco is trying to do human nature good by telling it something very simple, but essential, and something you must remember forever, Mona.

But to provoke the old man, Mona plugged her ears and shut her eyes, as if she wanted neither to hear nor to see anything he might say. After a few seconds, she discreetly opened one eyelid to watch his reaction. He was smiling casually. So she stopped her little game and summoned all her attention. For she felt that her grandfather, after those long minutes of silence, contemplation, and talk, after that little journey through the damaged image spread before her eyes, was finally going to reveal one of those secrets that stay engraved deep in the heart.

Henry motioned for her to direct her gaze toward the somewhat faded area where there seemed to be an object the young woman on the right was taking in her hands. The little girl obeyed.

— The procession of those four women on the left consists of Venus and the Three Graces. They are generous divinities. And they are offering a gift—we don’t know what kind, because some of the paint is missing—to a young girl. The Three Graces are what are called allegories, Mona: they don’t exist in real life and you will never meet them, but they stand for important values. They are said to represent the three stages that make us sociable and hospitable—that is to say, truly human. And this fresco shows how crucial those three stages are; it seeks to root them in each of us.





— Three stages? What stages?

— The first is knowing how to give, the third is knowing how to give back. And in between them there is one without which nothing is possible, one that is like a keystone, a keystone that supports all of human nature.

— Which one, Dadé?

— Look: what is the young girl on the right doing?

— You told me: she’s lucky because she’s getting a present...

— Exactly, Mona. She is receiving a gift. And that is what is absolutely fundamental. Knowing how to receive. What this fresco is saying is that one must learn to receive, that human nature, in order to be capable of great and beautiful things, must be ready to welcome: to welcome the kindness of others, their desire to give pleasure, to welcome what it does not yet have and what it is not yet. There will always be time afterward for the one who receives to give in return, but in order to give in return—that is to say, to give again—it is indispensable first to have been capable of receiving. Do you understand, Mona?

— Your story’s complicated, but yes, I think I understand...

— I’m certain you do! And you see, if these ladies are so beautiful, with this drawing so supple and graceful, this unbroken line that admits no jolt, no doubt, it is in order to express the importance of that continuity, of that chain that must link human beings to one another and improve their nature: give, receive, give back; give, receive, give back; give, receive, give back...





Mona no longer knew what to say. Above all, she did not want to disappoint her grandfather. She had already used humor during their conversation and now kept silent so as not to add anything too naive, knowing full well that he was speaking to her and bringing her into this immense museum so that she might become a little more adult. For the moment, she felt only a tug inside herself, because this summons to grow up, this intoxicating exploration of a new world, had extraordinary magnetic power, all the more because the summons came from Henry, whom she adored. And yet, in the deepest part of her soul, there already existed the terrible premonition that what one gives back, one never gets again. And that regret—keen, though still very distant—for a childhood vanished forever twisted her heart.

— Shall we go, Dadé? Come on, you old rascal?

— Come on, Mona! Off we go!

Henry took her hand again in his, and they left the Louvre at a slow pace, without saying a word. Outside, night was beginning to fall. Henry knew perfectly well the disturbance that had just made his granddaughter waver. But he categorically refused to spare people on the pretext of securing only good moments, full and seductive, in their company. No: he knew very well that existence is worthwhile only on condition that one accept its roughnesses, and that those roughnesses, once sifted by time, reveal a precious and fertile matter, a substance both beautiful and useful, which allows life truly to be life.





Besides, by the miracle of childhood, Mona’s disturbance did not last long: as she walked along briskly, she began to hum. Henry never interrupted her at such moments, which he found incredibly touching. Then suddenly, as they approached her home, Mona stopped, overtaken by the shared lie they had agreed upon in order to avoid the sessions with the child psychiatrist. She opened her large blue eyes and turned her mischievous little head, laughing at the trick they were playing on her parents.

— Dadé, what do I say if Mom and Dad ask me the name of the doctor I went to see?

— Tell them his name is Dr. Botticelli.

The All Saints’ vacation passed quickly and Mona went back to school. Camille arrived early, around eight o’clock, under the wan awning that sheltered them from an unpleasant autumn rain. She handed her daughter over to Mme Hadji, quickly explaining how the convalescence and the medical follow-up were going, including, among other things, the weekly Wednesday consultations with a child psychiatrist. She insisted: the teacher was certainly to keep an eye on Mona, but without showing her any special consideration, nothing that would set her apart from her classmates.
