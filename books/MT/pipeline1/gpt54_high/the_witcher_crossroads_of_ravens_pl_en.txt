Kaedwen is a land wedged in between the Dragon Mountains to the north, the Blue Mountains to the east, and impassable forests to the west. There rule the hereditary kings of the House of Topp. Their original capital was ancient Ban Ard, but in the year 1130 post Resurrectionem King Dagread granted that stronghold to the mages for a school and moved the capital to Ard Carraigh, which lies in the middle of the realm.

The other principal strongholds of Kaedwen are Ban Fearg, Daevon, Ban Fillim, and Ban Gleann.

The arms of the Topps and of the whole land have from time immemorial been the unicorn—d’or, licorne effrayée de sable.

Around the royal lands of Kaedwen lie the border territories belonging to the commonwealth, called marches, or marchiae. These are entrusted to margraves, that is to say counts, hereditary or appointed by the king. The name comes from this: those lands are as it were the vanguard, and by pressing ever farther on they are to carry conquest forward and wrest new lands for Kaedwen from the elves, push back the borders, and set their signs, id est marks, ever farther afield. Those marches are: the Western, the Upper, the Lakeland, and the Lower.

Baldwin Adovardo, Regni Caedvenie Nova Descriptio









Chapter One


Despite his sincerest efforts—and for rather important reasons—Geralt was utterly unable to concentrate on the reeve’s prattle. His whole attention was taken up by the large stuffed crow on the reeve’s table. The crow, fixing the witcher with one glass eye, stood on a base of clay painted green, both its legs embedded in it. So the crow, despite its utterly lifelike appearance, could not possibly have been alive, that admitted of no doubt. Why then, Geralt could not stop wondering, had the crow already winked its glass eye at him several times? Could it be magic? Probably not, because his witcher medallion had not stirred or vibrated, not once, not in the slightest. So then was it a hallucination? A delusion? Brought on, say, by the fact that he had been hit on the head a few times?

—I’ll repeat the question, —reeve Bulava repeated the question. —I’ll repeat it, though I’m not in the habit of repeating myself.

Reeve Bulava had already assured Geralt several times that he was not in the habit of repeating himself. Even so, he kept doing it all the time. Apparently he liked it, though he wasn’t in the habit of it.

—I’ll repeat my question: what was it really all about? What did you have against that deserter, to hack him to pieces so horribly? Some old score? Because I simply don’t believe, you see, that it was over that peasant and his daughter’s maidenly honor. That you rushed to the rescue. Like some fucking knight-errant.





The crow gave him another sidelong look. Geralt moved his hands, tied behind his back, trying to get the blood circulating. The rope was cutting painfully into his wrists. Behind him he could hear the heavy breathing of a village brute. The brute stood right behind him, and Geralt was sure he was only waiting for a pretext to punch him in the ear again.

Reeve Bulava huffed, leaned back in his chair, and thrust out his belly and velvet doublet. Geralt stared at the front of the doublet and could tell what the reeve had eaten today, yesterday, and the day before. And that at least one of those meals had been in tomato sauce.

—I’d thought, —said the reeve at last, —that I’d never have to lay eyes on one of you witchers. No witcher’s been seen for years. They said that after ninety-four hardly any of you got out alive, there in the mountains. Then word came that the rest had died off too, of hunger or pestilence. And now look at that, one turns up in my very village. And the first thing he does is commit murder. And when he’s caught in the act, he’s got the cheek to invoke some fucking ukases.

—By virtue of the personal ukase of the year one thousand one hundred and fifty, —Geralt croaked, clearing his throat, —issued by Dagread, King of Kaedwen and the frontier Marches, primo: witchers are permitted freely to practice their profession within the territories of the Kingdom and the Marches and are exempt from the jurisdiction of local authorities—

—First primo, —Bulava cut in sharply, —it’ll soon be half a century since Dagread turned to dust, and with him his personal autocratic ukases. Second primo, no king’s exempting me from under anything, because the king’s in Ard Carraigh, far from here, and here local authority rules. Meaning me. And third primo, brother, it’s not for practicing your profession that you were arrested, but for killing a man. Catching werewolves and killing leshens, that’s witcher business. But cutting down people—no king ever gave you leave for that.

—I acted in defense—

—Daryl!

The brute obediently punched Geralt, this time in the nape of the neck.

—Your repeating yourself, —the reeve said, looking at the rafters, —is irritating. Do you know what irritation can lead to? Even in a calm man like me?

The crow gave him another glass-eyed look. Geralt kept silent.

—You, —said Bulava at last, —are no witcher. You’re defective. In need of repair. You should be sent back to that mountain den of yours people talk about. I don’t know how things are done up there among you. Maybe a botched piece like you gets taken apart into components, to be used in producing new, better witchers. Because that’s how it’s done up there, isn’t it? Witchers are made out of assorted human bits, stitched together or glued, or however it goes. People say all sorts. So as not to waste words... I’ll dispatch you, failed witcher, back to the mountains beyond the Gwenllech. In a week.

Geralt said nothing.





—Won’t you even ask why in a week? —The reeve bared yellow teeth. —You like invoking ukases and laws. Well, I’m all for the law too. And the law here says that outsiders aren’t allowed to carry weapons in this district. And you came in armed.

Geralt wanted to object that he hadn’t come in, he’d been dragged in. But he didn’t get the chance.

—The punishment, —Bulava pronounced, —is twenty lashes. The sentence will be carried out by Daryl here, and he has a heavy hand. You won’t be on your feet before a week is out. Come now, take him. Out to the yard with him, tie him to the post—

—Easy, easy, —said a man in a brown cloak, heavily dirtied at the hem, as he entered the room and stopped the thugs. —What’s the rush to the post and whip, Bulava? You want to maim my witcher for me? No chance of that. I need the witcher whole and sound at the works.

—And what’s this— —the reeve planted his fists on his hips —you meddling in my punishments for, Blaufall? It’s bad enough I have to put up with you forever taking my villagers off for corvée. But don’t meddle in my jurisdiction. It’s none of your business, my jurisdiction. A crime must be punished—

—Bah, no crime, —Blaufall cut in. —There’s no offense here at all, only self-defense and helping folk in need. Don’t make faces, don’t make faces, I’ve got a witness. Come along, goodman. There now, don’t be afraid. Tell it. What happened, and how.

Geralt recognized the peasant. The very same one he had saved yesterday from being robbed, and who had run off into the forest instead of thanking him. The father of the little girl he remembered stripped to her shift.

—I testify... —the peasant wheezed, pointing a finger at Geralt. —I give my word as how that young man hurried to my rescue from bandits... Saved my property... Saved my daughter from dishonor... Delivered the innocent maid from robbers’ claws...

—And that deserter, —Blaufall prompted, —set on him with an axe, and the young man only defended himself. Self-defense! Confirm it, goodman, that’s how it was.

—That’s how it was... Right so! Master reeve, that young man ain’t guilty! Master reeve! Release him, I beg you. And here... Please, take this... By way of, hmm... Perhaps there were expenses, or losses... I stand ready to make good...

Bowing obsequiously, the peasant handed the reeve a purse. Bulava quickly slipped it into the pocket of his baggy breeches, so deftly the purse did not even jingle.

—Self-defense! —he snorted. —Cut a man to ribbons with a sword. Innocent young man... I’d just—

They went out into the yard. The thugs shoved Geralt ahead of them without untying his hands.

—And you, Blaufall, —asked the reeve, —what’s got you in such a hurry you even dragged in a witness for me? Need that witcher so badly?

—What, you don’t know? We’re laying a road, the Great Road, from Ard Carraigh through the woods all the way to Hengfors it’s meant to run. And not just any road, not some track, but a proper highway, dry and level, laid with logs and fascines so wagons and teams can pass. It’s important, the Great Road; brisk trade between our lands and the North’ll go that way. The king himself’s said to have ordered haste. And there are monsters in the woods and on the marshes, every so often a worker gets killed, cut down or carried off by some monster...

—Since when do you care about workers? You always used to say one worker didn’t matter, there’d be another...

—Fuck the workers, most of them are corvée anyway. But every now and then a monster kills me a foreman, and that throws the whole job into chaos, the whole work front goes to hell. Ah, what’s the use. I’m telling you, I need the witcher. If I miss the deadline, not only’ll the bonus go to the devil, they may even send an inspection. And an inspection...

—Always finds something, —Bulava nodded in understanding. —Materials sold off on the side, padded cost estimates, or—

—Don’t change the subject, —Blaufall grimaced. —And release the witcher at once, no delay, I’m taking him straight to the works... Oh, blast it... What’s going on?

—The lads from the garrison. —The reeve shaded his eyes with his hand. —Captain Carleton’s men.





A dozen or so horsemen galloped into the yard, raising dust and scattering chickens. Armed men. Dressed colorfully, brightly, and rather raggedly. Only the two in front were better turned out. The commander, a moustached man in a moose-hide jerkin, with a gilt pendant and a hat stuck with ostrich plumes. And a long-haired elf with a headband, in the green uniform of a scout.

—Captain Reisz Carleton, —Bulava greeted him, going out to meet him. —Welcome, welcome. To what do I owe the honor?

Captain Reisz Carleton bent in the saddle and spat forcefully. Then he signaled to the scout. The elf rode up to the post with the crossbeam and deftly flung a rope with a noose over it.

—Oho. —Bulava planted his hands on his hips and checked that his thugs were standing behind him. —Looks like the captain’s come to my village for a hanging. Ah, and I can even see whose lot it is for the noose today. I see, I see, those two there in bonds... So the captain bagged deserters from his garrison! Those same ones that were out in the woods assaulting peasants and girls round here?

—Those, —Captain Reisz Carleton twirled his moustache, —I’ve no intention of hanging. They’ll both run the gauntlet, down a lane of rods. So they remember. That’s all. I’ve too few men to hang them for every little thing. And too few to let some stray kill them with impunity.

The captain straightened in the saddle and raised his voice, speaking not only to the reeve but to the village thugs, Blaufall and his men, and the little crowd of peasants already gathering.

—What am I supposed to punish my soldiers for? For what? For leaving post without leave? For wanting to fuck a girl? Why, we’re in that garrison like at the end of the world, like in exile, like we’ve been sent there for punishment. You get neither beer nor women there... Why wonder if the lads slip off now and then and grab some—

—Because what the hell, —Reisz Carleton raised his voice, —are women doing wandering through the woods? And this goodman here, what, did he have to take his young lady by that road of all roads? Couldn’t she have stayed home? So why blame the lads for wanting to... I don’t approve of it! I don’t approve, but I understand! Master Aelvarr? Ready there?

—Ready, captain.

—Then hand over that witcher, Bulava. He killed one of my soldiers; he goes to the rope. An example has to be made. And don’t cut him down, reeve, let him hang, as a warning to others.

Blaufall stepped forward, looked as though he meant to speak, then thought better of it. The thugs seized Geralt, but did so uncertainly. As it turned out, with good reason.

All at once it grew strangely quiet. As though something cold had passed over the yard.

Into the yard, from behind the barns, there stepped very slowly a black horse, black as pitch. It bore a rider. White-haired, in a black leather jerkin studded at the shoulders with silver rivets. Over the rider’s right shoulder rose the hilts of two swords.

Very slowly, almost gracefully, the black horse passed the peasants and the reeve. Then it stopped before Captain Carleton’s horsemen.

For a moment there was silence. Then the black horse tossed its head. The bit-rings jingled.





—Reeve Bulava, —said the white-haired rider into the silence, —will release the young witcher at once. He will return his horse, his weapons, and his belongings. At once.

—Yes... —the reeve coughed. —Yes indeed, Master Holt. At once...

—Captain Carleton. —The rider inclined his head slightly. —Greetings.

—Master witcher Preston Holt. —Reisz Carleton touched the brim of his hat. —Greetings.

—Captain, —the rider raised his voice, —be so good as to remove from here that elf, his rope, and the rest of your men. You are no longer needed here. Today’s lynching is canceled.

—Indeed? —The captain drew himself up in the saddle, laying a hand on the hilt of his sword. —You’re that sure of yourself, witcher?

—Yes. I am that sure. Farewell. Master reeve, is the lad released? His things returned?

—You bastard! —shouted one of Carleton’s horsemen, wrenching his sword from its sheath and spurring his mount. —I’ll—

He did not finish. The rider called Preston Holt raised a hand and made a short gesture. The air howled and whistled; the peasants clapped their hands over their ears. The horseman screamed, flew from the saddle as if shot from a sling, and came down hard and limp directly beneath the hooves of his comrades. Their horses shied, snorted, stamped, tossed their heads; one reared. The fallen man’s horse bolted between the huts, bucking and laking out with its hind legs.

It grew very quiet.

—Anyone else? —Preston Holt raised his gloved hand. —Anyone eager to stand up to me? Play the hero? No? That’s what I thought. Farewell, gentlemen soldiers. Is the young witcher in the saddle yet?

—He is, —Geralt answered.

—Then on the road. Follow me.









The Upper March has set the sign of its border upon the river Gwenllech. It is well known that the ambition of the margraves there is to make further incursions into the valleys of the Dragon Mountains, wherefore they are wont to call their domain cis-montane, as though it should soon be enlarged by a transmontane part, when the elves have been driven deeper into the mountains. Yet the years pass and nothing of the kind occurs.

Baldwin Adovardo, Regni Caedvenie Nova Descriptio





Chapter Two


The Kingdom of Kaedwen was known throughout the inhabited world for its cold and capricious weather. Shut off to the north by the barrier of the Dragon Mountains, and to the east by the mighty massif of the Blue Mountains, the land suffered from the frequent and unpredictable shifting of air masses, which resulted in long and severe winters, cold springs, and short, rainy summers. As for autumn, that varied—sometimes sunny, warm, and pleasant, and sometimes not at all.

Now, in the month of March, called Birke by the elves, snow still lingered here and there in ravines and windthrows, lay in white patches in hollows on the bare uplands. Yellowing sheets of ice still covered some puddles and ditches. The sun might seem to warm a little, but when the wind blew from the mountains it burned with frost no worse than in January.





Geralt had set out from Kaer Morhen the day before the Equinox. That was the witchers’ custom. Experience had shown that after winter monsters were at their hungriest and so aggressive that people in villages and settlements were willing to fork out to hire a witcher, even though by the hungry gap they had already eaten through their stores and were practically without means to live. But Geralt had had no chance to be hired. Things had turned out as they had turned out: barely two days’ ride from the mountains and bang, a peasant and his daughter, marauders, a bald thug with rotten teeth, slash, hack, and there you were. He found himself hauled before reeve Bulava of the village of Neuhold, and from that judgment seat, and then from the threat of lynching at the hands of soldiers from the nearby garrison, he had been rescued by that strange white-haired fellow with two swords on his back and a black horse under him, whose trail Geralt was now following.

—I suggest, —the strange fellow turned in his saddle, —that we ride together for a while. Captain Carleton may still want to hang you; it was plain he very much wanted to. He isn’t quite stupid enough to go after me, but you, alone, may be an easy target. So if my company doesn’t bother you...

—Not at all, —Geralt assured him hastily, urging on his buckskin mare. —Gladly... I... I’m—

—I know who you are. Did your hair go white from the mutations? From the Changes? Loss of pigment, like mine?

—Well... Yes. But how—

—How do I know who you are? Because I keep track of what goes on there at your Keep. And word reached me that some wunderkind had trained there, called Geralt, and that before long he was to take the Path.

—But Vesemir—

—Never mentioned me? The name Preston Holt never slipped out? I’ll explain: Vesemir and I have for some time moved in different orbits, so to speak. If you know what I mean.

Geralt did not really know what an orbit was, but he nodded with a knowing expression.

For some time they rode in silence. Side by side.

—So you’ve set out from Kaer Morhen on the Path, —Preston Holt said at last. —Your beginning may not have been the best, but that’s how beginnings are. I’ve no intention of scolding you; quite the contrary, I looked at the corpse of that marauder, and your cuts must be judged impeccable. Perhaps unnecessary, perhaps unconsidered, perhaps inelegant—but on the whole impeccable.

They fell silent again and watched a herd of cattle driven out onto the pasture, and a little herdboy running from cow to cow to warm his frozen feet in fresh, warm cow dung. The dung probably warmed them only moderately, but the running certainly did.

—They’re driving the cows out, —Holt observed, —though the grass is barely poking up. Which means the season has begun and you’ll find work without much trouble, Geralt. Villages will soon be willing to pay for protection for shepherds and livestock. Let’s hurry the horses, there, into the birches, along the bank of the race.

—The bank of what?

—The race. This ditch here is a drainage race, a channel by which water was once carried off from a mine. We are, as you doubtless know, in the part of the Kingdom of Kaedwen called the Upper March. The wealth of the Upper March is minerals: chiefly salt, but also silver, nickel, zinc, lead, lapis lazuli, and suchlike. At least that was once the case. Now most of the minerals seem to have begun to run short. Nothing lasts forever.

Geralt made no comment.

—Do you see that hill ahead of us? It’s called Podkurek; that’s how it appears on official maps. And the name comes from this: perhaps a hundred years ago a peasant called Podkurek happened to dig up there a lump of silver the size of a good cabbage. A mine sprang up here at once, biting into the mountainside. Large quantities of silver and galena, lead ore, were extracted here. But the deeper the miners dug, the worse trouble they had with water. There are more drainage races like this one; you’ll see them. In the end the cost of drainage made the whole enterprise unprofitable. The miners moved elsewhere. They left behind a labyrinth of corridors and workings, partly flooded. And now for the best part: the abandoned, flooded mine was occupied and taken over by wandering zatrawce. You do know what zatrawce are, I trust?

—Zatrawce, —Geralt recited, drawing breath, —are small creatures resembling dog-faced apes. They live underground in darkness, in packs. In a pack they are dangerous—

—Dangerous as hell, —Preston Holt cut off the recitation. —And they quite often make trouble for amateur miners who come here to burrow in Podkurek’s slopes in search of the silver that can still be dug up here. Ah, and there’s an illustration of what I’m saying: what’s gleaming over there is wagon-canvas and tents. We’ll ride straight into the camp of those brave diggers. The first this spring.

The brave diggers first greeted them with a delegation armed with shovels and clubs. The threatening expressions of the greeters sent a simple message: clear off, strangers, we were here first. But the expressions quickly softened—inspection showed that the newcomers were not unwelcome competitors. And on faces still hostile only a moment before there now appeared outright joy.

—Sweet gods, thank the gods! —cried the chief digger, hiding behind his back the mattock he had been brandishing warningly a moment earlier. —Thanks be to the gods! Sweet gods, why, it’s the noble witcher himself! And when word reached us you were in the area, we were already meaning to send for you! And meanwhile you come to us straight from the heavens, straight from the heavens!

—That’s a habit of mine. —Preston Holt straightened in the saddle. —To fall from the sky upon those in need. For I am a witcher.

By now several lamenting women had joined the diggers. It was growing louder and more confused. With gesture and voice Preston Holt demanded order. It took a while before it finally became possible to make out what the matter was and what the diggers and their women actually wanted.

—We need a witcher, a witcher! —the chief digger shouted, waving his mattock. —Them underground creatures that live under the hill carried off our boy! Attacked him, carried him off, dragged him into the abyss! Who’s to save him now if not you?

—It isn’t even two weeks, —said Holt, —since I warned you to keep away from the shaft and the adit. What? Didn’t I say it? To dig on the other side of the hill? You say the zatrawce carried off the boy? How old? Five? How long ago? Ah, the day before yesterday? You weren’t in any hurry to send for me, then. Dismount, Geralt.

Preston Holt dismounted from his horse. Geralt could not help noticing it was not easy for him. And that he limped badly on his left leg. With a gesture he drove off the women crowding around him, wailing. He spoke for a moment with the elder digger, then tugged Geralt by the sleeve.

—Well then, young witcher, —he said. —Time to help people in need, harmed by monsters. Because we have just been hired.

—If the zatrawce carried off the boy the day before yesterday, —Geralt muttered, —there’s little chance he’s still—

—Alive? Yes, that is doubtful. But perhaps at least we’ll find... Hmm... Give the mother the chance to bury what remains... What are these hesitations, young Geralt? Just now you fling yourself without a thought into defending a girl’s honor and slash a man to death, and now you hesitate?

—Did I say anything? —Geralt shrugged. —Or that I’m hesitating? I’m not hesitating.







On the mountainside there still survived the remains of wooden structures, doubtless drainage gear. There a shaft yawned, partly collapsed, opening on darkness.

—What remains of the mine, —explained Holt, —is the shaft and the adit, there farther on. Both lead to the upper workings. The workings twist because they follow the vein, which has been completely exhausted. From there an incline runs down to the lower workings, even more winding, with numerous branches. Below that there’s yet another adit, but completely flooded. The zatrawce are holed up in that lower tunnel. There may also be holes there, links to natural caves.

—So this is the plan: with my leg I won’t be going down, so I shall go to the adit and make a racket there, draw the zatrawce onto me. Then all hope lies in you. You’ll go down the shaft to the gallery, from there by the incline below. There you have a chance of finding... what needs to be found. With a bit of luck. Which I wish you, lad. See you above.

—Maybe first, —Geralt ventured, —we should scope things out—

—Do what? —Holt grimaced. —Ah, I understand. There’s no point in scoping anything out. Drink an elixir, take your medallion in hand, and get down the shaft.

—With all due respect, —the chief digger standing nearby blurted out, —but that stripling’s awful young-like... Can he manage? We thought it’d be you, Master Holt, as’d go down. Your own honored person...

Preston Holt turned and looked at him. The digger shrank, mumbled, and did not finish.







The gallery was high; Geralt could walk fully upright, and there were still several handspans to the roof. Water dripped everywhere from the walls; apart from the sound of falling drops nothing could be heard. He quickened his step. He wanted to reach the incline as fast as possible, before Holt, as promised, made a commotion by the adit.

In the walls of the workings were deep cavities, traces of excavated ore deposits. In one such cavity he noticed a strange brick structure—or rather what remained of one. He could not know that these were the remnants of an ancient chapel.

Miners, as Geralt did not know either, held to belief in the gods longer than any other social group. Working under constant threat, they had to believe some providence watched over them, and that prayers offered up to it would ensure their safety. As is easy to guess, experience soon proved that prayers were no use at all, that cave-ins and methane explosions happened just as often to the pious as to unbelievers. But the miners went on believing all the same, built chapels, burned candles, and prayed. For a long time. But not forever. Reason, as usual, won in the end.

The corridor suddenly began to slope downward; he was already on the incline. He pricked up his ears, but still heard nothing except dripping water.

He seemed already to be close to the lower gallery when it began.

A large stone whistled out of the darkness and grazed his hair. More followed; some of them hit. One struck Geralt in the head and made him reel, and the zatrawce flung themselves at him from all sides, howling and barking wildly, clawing and biting. There was no counting how many there were; they darted around too fast. Geralt snatched his sword from its scabbard, only to lose it at once—two creatures pinned him, a third struck the weapon from his hand with a blow of a great rock. The creature raised the captured sword high and roared in triumph, opening its dog’s maw as wide as it would go. The triumph was premature—Geralt flung off both attackers, seized a stone and hurled it, striking the zatrawiec straight in the teeth. The creature dropped the sword; Geralt leapt and caught it before it fell, hacked down the monster struggling with the stone wedged in its jaws. With two quick cuts he laid open two more. Then he turned and bolted toward the shaft. Behind him broke out wild barking and more stones came flying—again, some of them well aimed. One struck him in the back of the head—there was a flash, a spray of stars lit up the workings. Another hit him in the small of the back and nearly knocked him down. Twice the zatrawce caught him, sinking their teeth into his calves; the leather gaiters saved him from serious injury. One leapt and bit him painfully above the knee, where the gaiter did not protect him. Geralt did not slow to kill it.





Under a hail of stones he reached the shaft, climbed the remains of the ladder, burst out onto the surface, and there fell and lay. For some time.

—Well, well, —he heard. —You came out. And without major injuries, even. Blood’s flowing from a few places, I see. But it isn’t pouring anywhere, and that’s something already. I’m full of admiration.

Preston Holt stood over him, gnawing at the leg of a roast chicken.

—Oh, come on, —Geralt groaned, still flat on the ground. —Oh, come on! You were supposed to be at the adit... Make noise... Draw them away...

—Really? —Holt threw away the bone. —Ah yes. Forgive me, I completely forgot.

Geralt cursed. With his mouth still close to the ground.

—I’ll tell you more. —Holt licked his fingers. —They won’t pay us anything. Because the boy turned up. Came back. He’d simply wandered off somewhere, and the diggers, as usual, blamed the zatrawce. Get up, young Geralt. Witcher Geralt. Let me help you. Can you walk? Then let’s go. As I said, no pay. But they’ll feed us and let us spend the night. The girls will dress your wounds. If you ask nicely, perhaps one of them will be kind to you.

They walked toward the camp and the steaming pots. Witcher Geralt had difficulty putting one foot before the other.

Round-faced girls bandaged him in several places. And fed him. And the diggers let them stay the night. Holt in a tent, Geralt on a wagon.

One of the girls came to Geralt in the night and was kind. But only kind, only a little, nothing more. And right after that she went away.







At dawn Geralt clambered down from the wagon and began saddling his mare, still hissing with pain. Holt caught him at it.

—What’s this hurry? —He rubbed an eye. —Wait. They’ll give us breakfast, then we’ll ride on.

—Oh, come on, —Geralt grated out. —Maybe I’m not at all sure I want to go anywhere with you. Maybe I’d rather go on alone.

Holt leaned against the trunk of a birch and looked at the sky. Clear, without a single cloud.

—I fully understand, —he said, —your attitude. But I had to—and I emphasize had to—put you through a preliminary test, to see what you were like in action.

—I might not have come out of there.

—But you did.

—Not thanks to your help. So now—

—I ask you, —Holt interrupted, —to keep me company on horseback at least until noon. Which, reckoning roughly and taking the time of day into account, translates into five or six leagues. That time and that distance should suffice, I think, for you to cool off and look at the world more soberly. And then I shall have a proposal for you.

—What sort of —Geralt narrowed his eyes —proposal?

—Six leagues. At noon.

It was indeed close to noon when the sky suddenly blackened with wings, rang with the beating of feathers and one vast croaking. From the ground and from the branches of the surrounding trees dozens, if not hundreds, of black birds took flight.





—Ravens, —Geralt sighed. —So many ravens! It can’t be! Ravens never fly in flocks! Never!

—Undoubtedly, —Holt agreed. —So many ravens at once is unheard of; I’m surprised myself. Undoubtedly we are dealing with something out of the ordinary. And we also happen to be in a rather out-of-the-ordinary place. If you’ve noticed.

—A crossroads. —Geralt looked around. —Where roads part.

—A crossroads. A symbolic place. Four roads, in the four directions of the world. A place of choice and decision. Which you are now to make, Geralt. Witcher Geralt.

The ravens settled on the higher branches of the trees. Croaking, they watched the riders.

—Three of the roads, including the one we came by, are your roads as a solitary witcher, the fate you chose when you set out on the Path from Kaer Morhen. If you take one of those three roads, if that is your decision, we shall part. But if you choose the fourth road, you will hear my proposal.

The ravens croaked.

—I am, as you have doubtless noticed, on in years. I’ll add that I am very much on in years. You’d be surprised how very. I am also—and that too is plain, impossible to hide—a cripple. My days of witcher glory are already a song of the past. I no longer take the Path with swords; the light of my blades, if I may put it that way, will no longer disperse the darkness. But the darkness exists, monsters still go bump in the night. You can stand against them and defeat them. People threatened by monsters await your help.





—True, here I am waxing poetic and you’re waiting for the proposal. Here it is: I propose a partnership. I was known here in Kaedwen and still am; I never lacked for clients, and even now people ask me for help I can no longer give them. But you can. I have watched you and I say this: be my successor, Geralt. Instead of wandering hungry from road to road, live with me. Make use of my reputation, and you will not lack for work. And after work you will have somewhere to return to. And somewhere to winter. As for me... it will make me happy that someone continues my work. And also, I won’t hide it, that someone helps keep me in my old age.

—You needn’t decide at once. For now it will be enough if you go on not alone, but with me. Keep me company a while longer. Agreed?

—Well, I don’t know... Agreed.

—Then on the road. Let us leave this crossroads to the ravens.

The ravens saw them off with croaking.

—Don’t take offense, —Holt turned in the saddle, —but I insist that in my presence you try to speak more or less correctly. In particular, don’t say “scope things out” and “oh, come on.”









The Western March lies upon the river Buina, and on the west is buttressed by the Pustul Mountains. It was formerly the margraves’ intent to keep shifting the border farther and farther west, but all came to naught, for there the kingdom of Hengfors marked out its eastern boundary. There was contention over it and bloodshed threatened. Hengfors, though small, had a mighty protector in Kovir, wherefore the margraves were obliged to lower their ears and consent to peace.

That peace, concluded in the year 1225 post Resurrectionem and called the Hołopole Peace, established the border between the two kingdoms upon the brook Braa and thereby fixed the western limit of the March as its non plus ultra.

Chapter Three


As usual, the first to notice them were the cats and the children. The cats, of which there were any number at the edge of town, gave way reluctantly and, as they moved off, turned their heads and hissed. The children fled into the houses crying and wailing, abandoning whatever they had been playing with, chiefly lumps of dried mud.

Apart from the cats and children, none of the inhabitants of the little town of Spynham paid the incoming witchers the slightest attention. You would have thought riders in black cloaks with swords on their backs entered Spynham so regularly they had altogether ceased to be of interest.

Preston Holt knew the place and knew in which stable to leave the horses. Then they went on foot along the muddy street, scattering more cats and more snot-nosed brats.

—Good sir, —called a beggar woman squatting by a wall, a baby on her lap. —Good sir, spare a coin... For milk for the child...

Before Holt could react, Geralt fished a mark from his purse and tossed it to the old woman; she thanked him effusively. Holt said nothing for some fifty paces, then stopped.

—You are not a great lord, —he said through clenched teeth, looking Geralt in the eye. —You are, however, a great fool. For a mark one can buy the milk of twelve cows. You, as you’ll soon find out, they’ll want to pay little more than that for killing a monster. And killing a monster takes somewhat more effort than whining for alms and baring rotten teeth. And the child was probably borrowed from a crony sitting in the tavern waiting for a customer so she can suck him off in the privy for half a mark. I warn you, don’t come down this street again; there’ll be great laughter if you show up here once more.

For a moment Geralt felt like standing up to Holt and snapping back at him, telling him to get off his back and mind his own business. He did not do it. Somehow, by instinct, he had acknowledged Holt as higher in rank, almost as a master. Perhaps because Holt strongly resembled Vesemir. In manner and in speech. Physically he was very different.

Ahead of them shone the lime-whitened colonnades of a temple beneath which squatted a whole army of beggars, male and female. The temple had stood closed and abandoned for years, but the beggars still squatted there. And held out their hands for alms. Out of habit.

Holt prudently drew Geralt over to the opposite side of the street.

—Do you know why they call us witchers? —he asked. —Because we are the children of witches.

—Oh, come on... —Geralt stumbled. —Meaning, what a load of nonsense. Witchers from witches. Give it a rest.





—It may strike you as funny, but this happens to be true. The first witchers were the children of women with uncontrolled magical gifts, called witches. They weren’t right in the head and were often used by lusty young men as sexual toys. The children, the result of such amusements, were abandoned. Or left on doorsteps. And from orphanages and refuges they sometimes found their way into witcher schools.

—Sure. You made that up. It wasn’t like that.

—It was. All of us witchers descend from feeble-minded girls. Doesn’t that amuse you?

—Not in the least. Because that isn’t how it was at all.

—It was, it was. But long ago! Nowadays you won’t find any witches beneath temples. The sorcerers exterminated them all. Ha! Nothing lasts forever.

The closer they came to the market square, the more crowded it grew. For Geralt this was something new. He was not used to crowds and felt bad in them. The noise unnerved him. He was beginning to run short of breath, and the stench was getting worse and worse. It smelled of smoke, scorching fat, rotting fruit, dung, and the devil knew what else.

On the square they had to make their way through a line of stalls and a crowd of buyers. Here too nothing but novelties and surprises awaited Geralt. And finally wonder. He had never in his life suspected that there were so many crafts in the world, or that such an abundance of wares was offered for sale. And that so many people were willing to buy them. Straps and leather goods, glazed and unglazed clay pots, fur caps, short sheepskin coats, rawhide shoes, embroidered kerchiefs, copper pans, rakes, pitchforks, hoe-handles—and bagels, bagels, bagels.





Holt paid not the slightest attention to the marvels on the stalls. At one point, however, he stopped shouldering through the crowd and caught Geralt by the sleeve.

—Eyes down, —he hissed. —Eyes down, don’t stare.

—Why?

—Sorceresses.

Geralt obediently looked away. Reluctantly. The two women at the stall with amber jewelry drew the eye almost magnetically. By their rich dress. And by beauty like something out of a painting.

—They, —Holt explained once they had gone on, —take ordinary people for cattle, and they hate witchers. They’re quick to pick a quarrel; they may take staring as a provocation. Better too that they not sense our medallions.

Holt stopped before a substantial house above whose door a signboard had been nailed. The picture on the sign, by no means badly done, showed a mermaid with a generous bosom. The inscription beneath the mermaid read: “Lorelei.”

Holt seized the knocker and rapped energetically. Very energetically.

The massive door opened, and in it stood a broad-shouldered brute with a jaw like a loaf of bread. For a long moment the brute measured the witchers with his gaze. Then he stepped back and invitingly made way.

Holt, plainly a man familiar with the place, took both swords from his back without being asked and handed them to the brute. Geralt hastened to follow his example.





Disarmed, they entered an anteroom lit by several lamps. Geralt had difficulty drawing breath; the place was thick with the scent of perfume and incense. On the wall hung another painted mermaid. Equally full-bosomed. There was no inscription.

—Master Preston Holt, —said the woman who entered.

—Madam Pampinea Monteforte. —Holt bowed. Geralt bowed too. And shut the mouth he had opened a moment before. In admiration.

Untutored in the art of ladies’ dressmaking, Geralt could not know that the materials of Pampinea Monteforte’s black gown were transparent chiffon, muslin, and crepe. Nor did he know that it took great skill to make a dress that concealed by revealing. And vice versa.

Geralt also did not know what the quintessence of femininity was.

But what did it matter that he did not know, when he could see it.
