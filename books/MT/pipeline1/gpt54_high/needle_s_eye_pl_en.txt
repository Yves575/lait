Chapter 1

It was as I say. He tripped over his cane. I don’t know why he looked back. No one was coming down behind him. Of course I recognized him. Even though he had changed beyond recognition—after so many years it’s hard not to. But I gave no sign that I knew him. Maybe that was why he thought I didn’t recognize him. I pretended he was just one of those forever coming down or going up those stairs. I even wondered whether I shouldn’t warn him not to go down into that old wild green valley, because it no longer existed. Old people like visiting places that aren’t there, and no one knows whether they ever were. I don’t understand what longing draws them so strongly. Unfortunately, old-age longings can be dangerous. If only one could reach that valley on the flat, perhaps I’d have understood him. But you can’t reach any valley on the flat; that’s the nature of valleys. To go down those stairs at his age, with a cane, when the legs no longer obey and the eyes no longer see in accord—not only the world, but each step set before them—is no different from imagining in youth that instead of arms you have wings and can fly down into that valley.

I had been expecting not him, and then unexpectedly there he was. A trembling came over me; I shrank into myself, and at that moment I wanted to be neither here nor anywhere else. Feeling for the first step with his cane, he set one foot on it uncertainly, then dared to place the other. With the same effort he went down to the next, and the next. Helpless but stubborn, I thought. Why are old people so stubborn? Can they not forgive those who will go on living after them?

He paused for shorter or longer moments on every step, looking down at his feet, as if unsure whether these were indeed the stairs he had always gone down and up, or whether memory was betraying him. At his age it happens that memory exempts itself from remembering, since it burdens a man with duties he is already tired enough of, after so many years of living. He felt out each step below with his cane, making sure it was safe to descend. First he put his left foot on it, then his right. And with the same caution, lowering his right foot beside the left, he went down to the next step. The cane he held in his right hand struck me as unsure of itself; it trembled whenever he touched the step with it.

I was afraid his head might start spinning, the way he kept looking down at his feet on every step. I wondered whether to move out of his way, or let him go first when he reached the Needle’s Eye.

He was not surprised when he saw me; perhaps he had even expected to find me here before he got here himself from the park through town. He stopped on the step above me and, without looking me in the eye, said:

— I went to the park as well, but for the last time now. I don’t know whether you remember that she sometimes came home from school through the park. I sat down on a bench and decided to wait. A Gypsy passed by with a child at her breast. Did she stop, do you think, the way she did then? Not a chance. She didn’t even ask whether I wanted my fortune told, even though the baby whimpered as she passed me. She must have decided that the future lay not ahead of me but behind me, and she had no years to add to mine, how could she. I had been sitting there a long time, and was just about to get up and go through town, because perhaps she too would come back through town today, if she wanted to buy something, when some old woman sat down beside me, asking whether she might.

“— Unless you’re waiting for someone?”

“— No, please do,” I said. “Once I used to wait, but that was long ago.”

“— Long ago, not so long ago—if you’ve loved, you go on waiting.”

“— And did you love?”

“— What a question. Is there anyone who has never loved? You’ve lived quite a number of years, I can see, as I have, and you don’t know that? I still love him to this day, though by now probably for nothing. What pains me is that I never told him.”

“— And why didn’t you?”

“— I was ill, and the doctors didn’t give me much time, so I didn’t want to burden him with my love. Especially since he left after finishing school. I didn’t even know where, and by now he may not even be alive. Would you tell her, if you weren’t sure you yourself would go on living?”

“— I don’t know, especially after so many years. Maybe I too wouldn’t want to burden her with the guilt of leaving my love behind, if it were her turn to die.”

“— Does that mean you loved someone?”

“— I still love her, though I too am not sure she’s alive. But even if she were, I don’t think I’d tell her now either, if we met, because she wouldn’t know me, nor I her. We are no longer the same as we were in youth.”

“— If you love, you’re always the same. If only a person knew that when young, he would live differently. As it is, he wastes many things. Most often love. Well, I must be going. I have to feed the cats.”

“— Do you have many?”

“— It varies. As many as turn up.”

“— I’ll see you home.”

“— No, thank you. I don’t live far. And where are you going? Maybe I should see you home. You’ve got a cane, and I, thank God, am still on my own two feet.”

“— I’ll manage. I’ve nowhere near as far to go as you.”

He tested the next step with his cane, but as if he didn’t trust it, he moved the tip back and forth across the step, left and right, before setting his foot down. I wanted to ask him why, in that case, he was going down into that old wild green valley, but he forestalled me:

— I feel sorry for you, that you still have to go on living. But perhaps you’ll have better luck. I wish you that. Though in my opinion, wishes have never yet done anyone any good. — He went down to the next step, testing it the same way with his cane. — The truth is, every life is a repeating of someone else’s life. The past is ahead of us, you must know that; we trail along behind it. For who could ever keep pace with his own life? — He stopped on the next step. — Ah, how many feet must have passed this way. Have you noticed how worn these steps are? It takes centuries. Who knows whether they aren’t still passing this way. Maybe I’ll go with them. What, does that surprise you? It’s always more heartening in a crowd. — And feeling for the step below with his cane, he swayed, though it was as if someone steadied him. — When I was in high school... Though what am I telling you for? You’ll live through it yourself, because you have to. Otherwise you could never ask yourself whether it was worth it. All the questions we ask ourselves in life come down to that one: was it worth it. Well, not at your age. Only after years, only after years. Now you wouldn’t know what answer to give yourself. Just remember that youth can deceive for a very long time. Don’t let it fool you. One day it may suddenly turn out that you were young many years ago. Unfortunately. Forgive these bitter words, but they’re all I have left. In return I give you my life. It may come in useful when emptiness engulfs you. Emptiness is the worst thing that can happen to us. And the only remedy for it is to live in someone else’s life. Besides, no one begins his own life with himself. You don’t understand that yet. But in time, when you begin visiting it... I’d like to know what your impressions will be. Mine were better and worse, as with life. Once I ran away from my own childhood, but perhaps you will never want to leave yours. It happens. Let me tell you, though, it will do you no good. Life is wandering after yourself with no hope that you will ever find yourself. But enough, or they’ll pass me by. And there are still so many steps.

So I moved aside for him. And then the cane fell from his hand and rolled downward, and he lurched and went down after it. He fell all the way to the road at the foot of the stairs, striking his head on the cobbles. As it happened, no one was coming down or going up, so no one saw. I flung myself after him. I tried to lift him in some mindless hope that perhaps he was alive. But he was heavy. Tall in life, and in death limp as well, which made him heavier still. Then I heard someone’s voice above me:

— Leave him. The ambulance is already on its way.

So I laid his bloodied head back on the cobbles, and only then did I see that several people were standing nearby. Where they had suddenly come from I don’t know. Maybe it is like that at every accident, people seem to sprout from the ground or fly down from the sky, but while he was falling down those stairs, I could have sworn there wasn’t a living soul. So there is no point listening to people, because no one saw that he had tripped over his cane. They can say whatever they like, and each will say something different anyway, as always when no one wants to be a witness. And as it turned out afterward, no one did. The man who had told me to leave him was already telling of a similar accident, pointing to a house not far away:

— There, in that house. Two friends, knew nothing about guns, found a revolver from the war. And because it was rusty, even the trigger wouldn’t budge, so one of them started banging the barrel on the table. Well, it went off into the other one’s belly. He collapsed, and the first one started lifting him up, may even have straightened him out. The doctor later said that if he hadn’t lifted him, he’d have lived.

At that moment his words were cut short by the ambulance arriving with siren blaring. Two orderlies jumped out with a stretcher, and a doctor, who pulled back the dead man’s eyelids, then, just in case, put a stethoscope to his chest and pronounced him dead. Right after the ambulance, also with its siren on, the militia arrived. Two uniformed men got out of the car, one officer with stars, the other younger, with bars. After them, from the back seat, a third man clambered out, so fat he looked almost ridiculous in uniform.

The officer began asking the people standing around the body who had seen what and how it had happened, while the younger one with the bars wrote things down, though he had nothing to write, because though they all talked over one another, no one had seen a thing. Someone had only seen me running down the stairs after the body was already lying on the cobbles. And the one who had forbidden me to lift him had arrived just as I was trying to do so, and said:

— Leave him.

And he had even begun saying, “There, in that house...,” when the officer cut him off:

— Did anyone know him?

Someone started wondering whether, if he’d seen him alive, he might have recognized him, at which the officer waved a hand.

— Search his pockets, he said.

The fat one tried to bend over the body, but couldn’t manage it, so he knelt down and pulled a wallet from the inside pocket of the man’s jacket, handed it to the younger one, and he, without looking inside, passed it to the officer. The wallet had many compartments, but apart from money and a photograph of some young girl there was nothing in it, not even a receipt, say, from a laundry.

— No identity card? the officer said in surprise. Search him again, thoroughly. Maybe it’s somewhere. — It’s not? he said, even more surprised.

— A citizen without an identity card is as good as nonexistent, said the fat one.

— There’s a body, so there ought to be an ID as well, said the younger one with the bars. Without an ID it’s as if the body itself didn’t exist. And how are we supposed to conduct an investigation?

— Maybe it fell out and it’s under the body. Lift him, ordered the officer.

The orderlies lifted the body, but beneath it there was only a broad pool of blood.

— Put him back. The way he was.

— And the girl in the photograph? The lower-ranking one peered into the open wallet in the officer’s hands. Pretty girl.

The officer nodded.

— Maybe it’s his daughter. Someone might recognize her more readily. Shall I show it around? He took the photograph from the officer and held it under everyone’s eyes.

They shrugged and said they didn’t know her. Only one person remarked:

— It must be old. Scalloped edges. They don’t make them like that anymore. I’ve got similar ones from my grandparents.

— And you? he turned to me. Do you recognize her?

Yes, it was she. I recognized her. But I did not admit it. And so that not even the shadow of suspicion would fall on me, I drew the officer’s attention to the cane:

— Maybe someone would recognize him by the cane. Canes grow attached to people. And almost everyone carries a different one. One of the teachers at the high school...

The officer did not let me finish. He looked at me distrustfully, positively pinned me with his stare.

— We know well enough what we should identify people by. Leave that to us. — And he ordered: — Just in case, look for that cane, put the money into safekeeping, and outline the body. Who knows, maybe he’s some tourist; he may have left his identity card with the guide, in case he got lost.

They outlined the body in chalk, the ambulance drove away with it, and the younger man and the fat one began looking for the cane. That outline of his body on the cobbles in white chalk, with the bloodstain where he had struck his head, seemed to me as if it were still breathing with his breath, as if it were even whispering something in his own words, and it was more harrowing than when the body itself had lain there, before the orderlies took it away.

— We’ll have to drive around town and question every place where a coach is parked, the officer ordered, when despite searching both on the stairs and all around, they still hadn’t found the cane. — It’s Saturday, so they’ve all come pouring in today. They park below the castle, they park by the granary, they park in the New Town, and wherever they park, question them. Luckily there’s only one hotel; question them there too. And question the private lodgings, get a list from the town hall, they have to register after all. — He took off his cap and scratched his head, as if his thoughts had started itching. — It would help to put up a notice too, saying: male, aged—except we don’t know how old he was; we found no ID. Damn, we didn’t even bring a camera, we could have taken his picture.

— How were we supposed to know he’d killed himself? the fat one cut in. There was a report of an accident, and with an accident he might just as easily have broken an arm or a leg. Haven’t enough arms and legs been broken on those godforsaken stairs?

— We could put up a photograph, said the one with the bars. Only not here; better in town, otherwise people will be afraid to come this way later, thinking the place is haunted. Though with a photograph of a corpse, who’d admit to knowing him. Better the daughter’s. Someone would be more likely to recognize the daughter.

— And how do you know it’s a daughter and not a granddaughter? the officer snapped at him. And the other actually blinked, as if that had not occurred to him.

— So it appears that in you we have our only witness, the officer said to me. And as if hesitating, he added: — Perhaps even the only lead from which the investigation can proceed, since we have neither the dead man’s identity card nor a photograph nor any other witnesses. You’ll come with us; we’ll take down a statement.

At the station he sat me at a desk opposite him, asked whether I would have some tea, and picked up the phone.

— Two teas, he ordered. Do you take sugar? In that case I’ll have mine sweet today as well. And sugar. Ah yes, and send Bronka in to type.

A woman thin as a rail, who turned out to be the cleaning lady, brought in two glasses of tea, sugar in a metal tin that had once held hard sweets, and a single teaspoon. He sweetened his, stirred it, and then handed the spoon to me.

— I think I’ll have to bring a teaspoon from home. These aluminium ones break, and there aren’t many left in the whole station, he said, as if apologizing for the one spoon.

Bronka came in too, threw me a contemptuous look, sat down at the typewriter and spread some glossy magazine out in front of her.

— Before you’ve finished your tea, I’ll have time to see what’s in fashion for autumn.

— Type. What’s tea got to do with an interrogation?

— How should I know.

He waited until I had at least taken the first sip; he himself was on his third by then, I think. Then he pushed himself back a little from the desk in his chair, settled his back against it, laced his hands together, clearly relaxing, and I think he even stretched his legs out under the desk, because he touched me.

— Well then, speak.

— What am I supposed to say?

— What you know.

— I know nothing more than what I’ve already said.

— You know, you know. We know very well that you know. Everyone who is questioned says the same thing: name, surname, date of birth, and that he knows nothing more. Experience teaches us, however, that a man himself doesn’t know what he knows unless he’s helped. There are various forms of such help. I, however, use ordinary conversation, you might say, of an enlightening kind. I proceed from the assumption that everyone has to discover for himself what he knows. So?

Apparently I still had understood nothing, because I decided to remind him what he had told me when he brought me to the station:

— Excuse me, but this is some misunderstanding. I was only supposed to be a witness.

— True, I don’t deny it, you are still a witness. Only the only one. We have no other witnesses with whom we could confront you in order to confirm your credibility. And when there is nothing certain, the question arises whether witness is your only role in this event, or whether some other role might not be ascribed to you as well. I don’t yet know what, but we must try to clarify it. The two of us. So I would like you to understand that I have to question you in different roles. The boundaries between roles are fluid, besides. Circumstances set them. To use a comparison, a liquid may pass either into a gaseous state or into a solid one, depending on the temperature. Water, steam, ice. The same, and not the same. We cannot allow ourselves helplessness. That would disqualify us. We would be unnecessary. So sometimes we have to probe to the point of pain. Truth, as you know, commands the highest price and does not go down even after years. So I would not advise counting on that. Truth never becomes time-barred, no matter how long a case may drag on. If only we had at least a second witness. But in your case that could only be the deceased. Unfortunately we cannot question him. Though it would be interesting to know what he might have said. We can only conjecture. And that is not so groundless. Conjecture is usually where the search for a trail begins, because hardly anyone is caught in the act. Consider, for example, all those infidelities, husbands’, wives’. Rarely does one catch the other, as they say, in flagrante. Most infidelities begin with conjecture. And only conjecture tells you to look for proof. So it cannot be ruled out that the deceased would testify against you, just as you testify that he himself was to blame, because he tripped over his cane. Only where is that cane? We looked for it, you saw. If it were there, we would have found it. A cane has no wings to fly away. — He laughed, but with such a laugh that it chilled me. — All right, we’ll look again. In the meantime, tell me which hand he was holding it in, left or right, when, as you say, he tripped over it. You don’t remember. Hard luck. Are you surprised that such a detail may matter? Let me tell you: a detail can tell us more than we suspect. The merest detail can overturn all testimony, contradict all witnesses. A detail is a cosmos. The world may seem large, but it is details that decide that someone met someone here and not there, that an apple fell from an apple tree and Newton discovered the law of gravity, that the bacteria Fleming was cultivating were unexpectedly killed by some mold and penicillin was thus discovered. And so on, and so on. Typed that, Bronka? Then throw that sheet away.

He drew the questioning out, and I must say that at times he surprised me with his perceptiveness:

— You said no one was coming down behind him, yes? And yet he looked back. Maybe the sun struck him like that—it’s sharp today—and unable to see anything, he turned his head, and then perhaps you, let us say, wanted to help him down onto the lower step?

More than once he would break off in mid-sentence and go out, as if giving me time to think. Or he would sink into thought, then tell Bronka to cross something out, though it seemed important to me. Apparently his thoughts were following some other track. And sometimes it was as if he wanted to soften the interrogation and give it a tone closer to confession, because he let drop something about himself: that he had two children, a son and a daughter, by his first wife, who had died, and that he had had to bring them up on his own.

— And you know how it is. Duty’s duty.

He had married a second time, and now they were expecting a child again. Sometimes he asked me questions that on the face of it had nothing to do with the case:

— Father and mother alive? You’re young, but things happen. I remember hardly anything of my mother; she died of tuberculosis. And my father passed away two years ago.

And suddenly he caught me off guard again:

— When did you take your school-leaving exams? And why didn’t you go on to university?

— They didn’t admit me.

— What, you failed the exam?

— I passed it.

— How’s that? You passed and they didn’t admit you? That too would bear looking into. Interesting. And what do you do now?

— I work.

— Where?

— At the fruit-and-vegetable processing works. Jams, marmalades, juices.

— We know that place. We conducted an investigation there: five freight cars of cherries stood unloaded in barrels on a siding till they rotted. And where do you live?

— With my parents.

— And what did you come here for? Had you arranged to meet him?

— Meet whom?

— Why, the deceased.

— I didn’t know him.

— You didn’t know him, and yet you recognized him. That’s what you said, that you recognized him. Or perhaps he only thought you didn’t recognize him. Well, we can’t ask him. What puzzles me, though, is that if this was a chance meeting, why did you happen to meet in the Needle’s Eye, and not on the street, in the park, in a café, a restaurant, anywhere at all, as happens with chance meetings? Think about that. I’ll be right back. — And he went out.

Oh, he wouldn’t be easy, I thought. And I could hardly tell him that I had been waiting for her, and he had turned up unexpectedly instead. I had even thought he was no longer alive, because a year had passed since, according to the Gypsy’s prophecy, he ought to have been dead. At once he would have asked: and who is she? Name, surname, where does she live? And did she come? What do you mean, you knew she wouldn’t come and you were waiting for her? You wait for someone who may be late or may even not come that day because something unforeseen detained them, but who will come another time.

Before long he came back, and already from the doorway threw me an impatient question:

— And have you something to tell me? Let us assume, however, that you didn’t know him. We’ll follow another track. You saw that he was going down, yes? Going down where?

— Down.

— Obviously one goes down, not up. — He sat behind the desk, staring into my eyes with a strange intensity. — Down, you say? And how was he supposed to get back up, since he could barely get down? Did that not occur to you? At his age, with a cane.

— I don’t know. Maybe he had no intention of coming back up.

— He had no intention, you say. And how do you know he had no intention?

— I’m only supposing.

— Then I must inform you that your suppositions too may constitute evidence. And we may use them against you, regardless of the fact that you are supposing in favor of your own innocence. Everything has two sides, every thing, every thought, every word. Nothing is unambiguous, however much you may think so. Unambiguity is an illusion. The very fact that you are only supposing betrays you; it means that something lies behind it. And what matters most is what lies behind a thing. True, you have protected yourself with that “maybe.” But sometimes people throw in a “maybe,” though they don’t do that without reason either. Words rule us, you should know that. You don’t need a school-leaving certificate for that. Sometimes a man would rather not say a certain word, and yet it says itself. So then? Because something here doesn’t add up. — He fell silent, as if waiting for my answer.

What troubled me was that he stayed silent so long, staring at me and saying nothing. In those eyes fixed on me, it seemed to me, I saw a question he wanted to ask, but did not know whether he himself might not get entangled in something if he did. I was afraid of that question and waited tensely for him to ask it. He did not. He only cleared his throat:

— Hmm... — And for a long time that was the only sound to come out of his mouth. After a while he cleared his throat again. — Hmm...

That “hmm” made me shrink into myself. Who knew what might be hidden in such an “hmm”—even an accusation for which there was as yet no proof, but in which there already sounded the conviction that proof would be found, sooner or later. Later it might even be easier to find. Investigative techniques would improve, methods of examination would be refined, new apparatus would appear, apparatus that would read on its own, without asking questions. And without Bronka, because there would be no need to write anything down; the suspect would simply appear on a screen. If he had a soul, you would see the soul too, plain as day. Patience—what recedes also comes closer. Meanwhile there was no harm in dreaming. In that time the suspect too would gain years, experience more than one thing, understand more than one thing; perhaps one day he would even come of his own free will and confess, and there would be no need to arrest him, go anywhere, track anything. One would sit behind a desk and receive the guilty as one receives people filing applications for relief or housing. If only one could get at consciences, because as things stand consciences rarely open of themselves, and if they do, only in old age. Perhaps instead of militia there will be only offices, bureaus, departments, and salaries will go up as well. The militia gets on poorly, especially with the young. The young are the hardest material for interrogation—they twist, hedge, deny; codes and paragraphs mean nothing to them; they imagine youth permits everything. They think nothing of the militia. Well, they’ll see, when we become the world’s signpost.

At that moment an officer one star above him came in and laid a note on the desk.

— Have you got him in hand yet? he asked, glancing at me.

— Almost.

— What, that tough? Maybe hand him over to Edek.

— No, I’ll manage.

— As you like.

After he left, he cast a glance at the note the other had brought him, put it aside with suppressed fury, and turned that fury on Bronka:

— Put that magazine away, Bronka! Type!

— I’ve been typing all along, Bronka said indignantly. Only there’s nothing to type.

He bored the same fury into me with his eyes, as though expecting that at least my hands would begin to shake.

— Let’s get back to the cane.

— “Let’s get back to the cane,” I’ve typed, Bronka repeated.

— Stop, for Christ’s sake, reading out what you type! — Oh, he was furious now. — Well then, what did the cane look like?

— What did the cane look like? Bronka again.

— So what did the cane look like?

— Ordinary. Like a cane.

— Precisely. What color was it?

— Black, I think.

— “I think,” or black?

— It may well have been black.

— “May well have been” is not an answer. You people don’t seem to understand that you’re being questioned in connection with—who knows?—perhaps even a murder. You might just as easily have tripped him as he might have tripped over his cane. Because so far we haven’t found the cane.

— Black, I said, slightly unnerved. I felt as though he were slowly beginning to get at my thoughts.

— Good, so now we have the color. What else?

— Black farther down too.

— Are you making fun of me? And I’m trying to give you a chance.

No, I was not making fun of him; I wouldn’t have dared. Maybe I had answered stupidly, but that was from the sudden confusion in my head, what with his switching to “you people” and what that might mean.

— And the handle?

— What about the handle?

— What shape was it? They come in all sorts. We had one fellow here, young too, like you, who walked with a cane whose handle was shaped like a snake’s head.

— No, semicircular, smooth.

— And what else did it have?

— A rubber tip at the end.

— Which means he couldn’t have slipped, because it held him. And he fell?

— Maybe precisely because it held him and he wanted to step lower. And he tripped.

He looked at me as if thinking, you’re a sly one, brother, but you won’t lead me astray.

— All right. Let’s leave the cane until we find it.

He began drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desk, as if considering whether after all he should not pass me on to that Edek the officer above him by one star had suggested, the one who had brought him the note. I didn’t know who Edek was, a name like any other, but the very idea of being passed on to some Edek struck me as threatening. Until then he had been mild, at times even friendly; I had even had the impression it pained him to have to question me. And fear swept over me. True, he had not agreed to Edek, but perhaps there was some Edek inside him too, since he had said he would manage.

— You people do know, I hope, that a confession is a mitigating circumstance, he said, with weariness in his voice. I think he even yawned.

— But what am I supposed to confess to?

— That’s not my affair. I’m not the one who’s supposed to confess. Truth be told, everyone would have something to confess to. Even if we hauled in the first random people off the street for questioning. And maybe one day that’s what we’ll do. For now our range of operations is limited.

He stopped drumming with the fingers of his right hand on the desk and shifted to the left; as if with a fresh surge of energy he drummed louder and faster, and my fear rose into my throat.

— Ah yes, I forgot to say: on that cane, just below the handle, there was a gold plaque. And on it an engraved inscription...

— Gold, you say, he cut me off. He picked up the phone. Send Fatso in. — There, you see. You have to dig until it hurts. — He stopped drumming his fingers, and even seemed to smile at me. — That would settle the matter for us.

The fat one appeared in the doorway.

— What is it, chief?

— Go look for that cane. There’s a gold plaque on it.

— If it’s gold, there’s no point looking, you won’t find it now. — And he began spinning out his doubts: — Gold is valuable these days. Trade in it is forbidden, so naturally it flourishes. How many gold rubles and dollars have we confiscated already, and they still go on selling. How many have we put away, and what of it? They even steal in churches and melt it down into rubles and dollars.

— Don’t give me that crap. Go. That’s an order.

— Yes, sir. — The fat one clicked his heels so hard he nearly lost his balance. Evidently his body was too heavy for his skinny legs.

— I don’t smoke, otherwise I’d gladly light one, he said, looking slightly deflated. He took out a comb and ran it through his hair. It was thick and black, though already greying at the temples. — I used to smoke. But first my wife would say, quit, the children are small; then the second, quit, I’m pregnant, do you want the child born crippled? Well then, we’ll wait till they bring that cane in. One must spare no time in getting at the truth.

And time dragged on endlessly, not only for me but for him as well, because once and then again he went out, leaving me alone with Bronka, who, each time he left, took the opportunity to leaf through that women’s magazine, and did not say so much as half a word. I asked her if she knew how long this might take.

— Maybe, maybe, she said without taking her eyes from the magazine. If they don’t finish tonight, they’ll start again in the morning. I’ve only filled one page so far. With Edek I’d have five already. Oh, Edek doesn’t pussyfoot around. He’ll hit, he’ll squeeze, if someone isn’t telling the truth. And nobody does. You’re dodging too, the way I listen to you. And there’s something off in everything you say. Edek would squeeze it out of you. Edek can bend horseshoes. He lifts me like a feather, right up to the ceiling. With Edek a person doesn’t get bored when he’s questioning you. Next to Edek nearly everyone here is a dwarf. Everyone spills the lot to Edek. That’s how you get to know life. You won’t read in a book what you find out with Edek. No one says as much in confession as they tell Edek. If Edek got hold of you, you’d find out more than one thing about yourself too. Edek wouldn’t let you sit there the whole time, like this one. Though he’s a good man and no fool, but half the time he doesn’t know himself what he wants. He questions you and questions you, and there’s nothing to type. And he gave you tea. Edek wouldn’t give you a drop of water. And he kept calling you “sir” till you got on his nerves. With Edek the interrogation would be over by now and I could go home. But with this one, who knows. He sent them off to look for the cane, but what’s a cane going to tell him? It’s getting dark. If you’d confess, maybe he’d finally get his promotion, because he’s been waiting a long time for them to add another star. He’d have a bigger salary. Two children from the first wife to bring up, and a third on the way with this one. And it’d be easier for you too, if you got it off your chest.

But what was he supposed to confess to, to make it easier? Nothing had ever been easy for him again from the day he let the Gypsy tell his fortune. She had foretold a long life, but that didn’t matter to him at all; what hit him was hearing that she would not be coming back this way through the park from school today, that she would go through town because she had to buy something, hooks or snap-fasteners, and he almost flew off the bench.

The park was usually empty at that hour, and besides it was small, one of those inner-city parks, and badly thinned out by the almost apocalyptic storm that had fallen on the town a few years earlier, and which people still remembered with horror. So it was no longer as popular as it had once been. There was nowhere in it to hide from human eyes, so hardly anyone arranged meetings there now. The young preferred to go to the copse by the river, and the old were kept from walking and sitting on the benches by their grief for the park as it had been. Once, at that time of year, the trees rang with birdsong; now only something now and then disturbed the silence, so he was sure he would hear her footsteps when she drew near.

He half closed his eyes against the glaring sun, for in that silence no one could have escaped him. He heard every footstep from far off and did not need to open his eyes each time to tell whether it was she or not. Besides, he knew her footsteps by heart, as though they only lightly touched the ground; one might have thought she wore sandals, though she always wore raised heels, she could not abide flat shoes. She seemed to place her feet toe-first, not heel-first; he would have known those footsteps even in starless dark.

So he gave himself over to imagination beneath those half-closed lids, especially since he did not expect her until the last class was over. He had that one class’s head start on her, having slipped away from Latin so as to be there waiting for her in the park when she came back, and more often than not she did come back through the park. More than once he slipped away, not only from Latin, which he disliked, but from physics and mathematics too, though in those subjects he was one of the best pupils. When he left, as she did, after the last bell, sometimes he lost her in the pupils streaming out from several classes at once. There were several parallel classes, and each was large, so when lessons ended a crowd poured out. And by the time he found her, she was almost always already walking with someone. Oh yes, there were many of them, older boys too, waiting for the chance at least to walk that stretch with her or even see her all the way home.

What he might have dreamed of is hardly worth recalling, since dreams at that age are commonplace enough and universally known, so anyone can draw on his own. Suddenly, however, he heard footsteps approaching, or rather the soft rustle of bare feet. No, those were not her footsteps; he would have recognized them at once. These were unhurried, stealthy rather, so he would not have let go of his dreams if, close by, almost within arm’s reach, a child had not suddenly whimpered. So he flung his eyes open, so violently that the brightness of the sun blinded him, and in that brightness he saw before him a Gypsy with an infant at her breast.

She was right in front of the bench on which he sat, and plainly would have gone on, for nothing in her footsteps suggested that she meant to stop beside him. Evidently experience told her there was no point telling the fortune of someone like him; she would not squeeze so much as a single zloty out of him. If he had any money, he was saving it for the pictures or the circus. Only what girl would let him invite her to the pictures or the circus? The prettier ones looked around for boys with a future. And what future could he have? Even if he lived a long time, did long life guarantee a future? Pale, worn, as if sunken in on himself, perhaps even ill, only not yet aware of it. The only thing she might have foretold for him was a long life, because that can be verified only by living it.

So perhaps it was no accident that just as she drew level with him and was about to take the next step, her baby whimpered because the nipple slipped from its mouth. She pressed it back in and stifled the crying. But she had to stop for a moment, and then her eyes met his. Only briefly, because his gaze chilled her and she lowered her eyes to the head of the sucking child, even stroked it tenderly. She had practice in subduing human distrust, even contempt, but his eyes seemed older than his years. A sad light shone from them. Never before had she seen so much suffering in young eyes; often you do not see that much even in old ones. Proof enough that he had opened them only when he heard her baby cry. Could it be he had inherited that suffering from someone? Each of us, after all, is an unending inheritance. But since she had stopped beside him, she could not leave without a word. Her baby’s cry had already been that first word to him. So, as if continuing it, she said:

— Let me tell your fortune, and I’ll tell you whether she’ll come back this way.

And without waiting for his consent, she sat down beside him on the bench. With a practiced movement she drew her right breast from under her blouse and shifted the infant to it. Torn away from sucking, it whimpered again, but had no time to burst into tears, because it already had the other nipple in its mouth.

— When something hurts, the best thing is to have your fortune told. No wise man has yet been found who doesn’t believe things will get better. Fortunes know how to console, and they take hope from no one. They can even change more than one thing. And all his life is still ahead of him. If he would only give something for a rattle, I’d tell him his whole life.

And without waiting for his consent or refusal, she slipped her left arm free of the shawl that, tied around her neck and waist, supported the little one she carried at her breast.

— Give me your hand, she said. Not that one, the other.

And she drew that hand onto her lap, took it in both of hers, turned it palm upward, and began running her forefinger over it.

— He’s got a long life-line. Oh yes, long. From here to here. — But he did not even look to see how long. — He’ll live and live. — She named the number of years, which happened to fall exactly last year. — There now, from here to here. If he had a bigger hand, he’d live longer still. But even so it’s a long time. He’ll live his fill. — She must have noticed the distrust in his eyes, because she closed that hand up and almost fearfully whispered: — There’s another line here too, the heart-line. A Gypsy doesn’t make mistakes. A Gypsy always tells the truth.

But that second line did not concern him either. A longer or shorter life meant nothing to him. He was immersed in that one moment for which he had been waiting there in the park by the main avenue. He would have given the longest life for that moment. There are moments that, like sun in a lens, focus an entire life into a single point, burning a mark into us forever. Who knows whether it is not for such moments alone that one lives, rather than for so many years.

He scarcely listened to what she was prophesying, for in his thoughts he was already walking out of school with her after the last class, through the doors of the building, then through the gate from the schoolyard, crossing to the other side of the street, walking a little way along the pavement, then turning toward the church, already by the church, in a moment entering the park, already in the park, so that any second her footsteps coming toward him should reach him. He already thought he could hear them, still very faint, she must be somewhere at the start of the side path, but they were her footsteps, how could he fail to know them, when suddenly the Gypsy’s words hit him like a cudgel to the head:

— She won’t be coming back this way today. She meant to, but remembered she had to buy something, hooks, snap-fasteners, and she’ll go through town.

She had not finished when he snatched his hand from hers, sprang up, and ran toward town.

— And won’t he give something for the rattle? she called after him, so that the nipple slipped from the baby’s mouth, for it had burst out crying.

He turned back, fished a coin out of his pocket and threw it into her lap.

— That won’t buy a rattle! she shouted, disappointed. Curse him!

If the Gypsy had spoken the truth, that she would be coming back through town, then the road to her house led down a flight of stairs through the Needle’s Eye into that old wild green valley, separating the hill on which the town stood from the opposite hill, much lower, where her house was.
