1.

Boarding House for Gentlemen

The view is obscured by billows of steam from the locomotive, now drifting across the platform. We must peer out from beneath them if we are to see everything, allow ourselves for a moment to be blinded by the gray haze, until the sight that emerges from this trial is sharp, piercing, all-seeing.

Then we shall see the platform slabs, squares threaded through with blades of frail little plants—an expanse that at any cost wants to preserve order and symmetry.

In a moment a left shoe appears on them, brown, leather, no longer quite new, and at once the other joins it, the right; this one seems even more worn—the toe is a little scuffed, and in several places the leather shows paler spots. The shoes stand there for a moment, undecided, but then the left one moves forward. The motion briefly reveals a black cotton sock beneath the trouser leg. The black is repeated in the skirts of an unbuttoned wool coat; the day is warm. A brown leather suitcase is held by a small hand, pale and bloodless; under the weight the veins stand out upon it, tracing themselves back to their source somewhere deep in the innards of the sleeve. Beneath the coat a flannel jacket flashes, not of the best quality and, besides, somewhat rumpled by the long journey. On it are visible tiny pale specks of some indeterminate grime—the world’s scales. The white collar of the shirt, one of the detachable kind, has evidently been changed quite recently, for its whiteness is fresher than that of the shirt itself and contrasts with the traveler’s sallow face. Pale eyes, brows, and lashes make that face seem unhealthy. The whole figure, against the intensely red western sky, gives the disturbing impression of having come here, to these melancholy mountains, from the hereafter.

The traveler walks toward the main hall of the station, surprisingly large for this mountain district, along with the other arrivals; he differs from them in that he walks slowly, even reluctantly, and that no one greets him, no one has come to meet him. He sets his suitcase down on the worn tiled floor and pulls lined gloves onto his hands. One of them, the right, its palm curled into a little trumpet, rises after a moment toward his mouth to receive a parade of short, dry coughs.

The young man hunches over and searches in his pocket for a handkerchief. For a moment his fingers touch the place where, beneath the cloth of his coat, his passport lies hidden. If we focus our attention for a moment, we can make out the fanciful handwriting of some Galician official who carefully filled in the document’s blanks: Mieczysław Wojnicz, Catholic, student at Lwów Polytechnic, born 1889, eyes blue, height متوسط, face long, hair fair.

This Wojnicz is now crossing the main hall of the station in Dittersbach, near Waldenburg, moving uncertainly through the gloomy high-ceilinged room where echo must surely live up among the highest cornices, and he feels someone’s eyes scrutinizing him from behind the ticket windows in the waiting room. He checks the hour on the great clock—late, the last train from Breslau. He hesitates for a moment and then steps out in front of the station building, only to be gathered at once into the broad arms of the ragged, uneven line of the mountain horizon.

It is mid-September, yet here, the newcomer notes with surprise, summer has long been over and the first fallen leaves lie on the ground. The last few days must have been rainy, because a light mist still almost completely fills the landscape, sparing only the dark lines of the streams. He can feel in his lungs that he is high up; that is good for his body, worn out by illness. Wojnicz stands on the station steps, eyeing with suspicion his shoes on their thin leather soles—he will have to think about winter boots. In Lwów asters and zinnias were still in bloom, and no one was thinking of autumn at all. Here, by contrast, the high horizon makes everything darker, and the colors seem more vivid, almost vulgar. At this moment he is overtaken by a familiar melancholy, proper to people convinced of their own imminent death. He feels that the world around him is scenery painted on a paper screen, that he could push a finger into this monumental landscape and bore a hole in it leading straight into nothingness. And that nothingness would begin pouring out through it like a flood, and would reach him too in the end, seize him by the throat. He has to shake his head to rid himself of the image. The image breaks into droplets and falls onto the leaves. Fortunately, an ungainly vehicle resembling a trap comes trundling toward him along the road. In it sits a thin, freckled boy in strange clothes. He is wearing something like a military jacket of hard-to-place provenance—for it resembles neither a Prussian uniform, which here would have made sense, nor any other—and a military field cap cocked rakishly on his head. Without a word he stops before Wojnicz and, muttering something under his breath, takes his luggage.

“How do you do, my good man?” Wojnicz asks politely in schoolroom German, but waits in vain for an answer; the other pulls his cap down over his eyes and impatiently points to a seat in the trap.

And at once they set off. First through the town over cobblestones, then along a road that leads them, in the gathering dark, on a winding track through the forest between steep mountain slopes. They are accompanied constantly by the murmur of a nearby stream and by its smell, which always unsettles Wojnicz so: the scent of damp undergrowth, rotting leaves, stones forever wet, water. He tries to ask the driver something that might establish contact—how long they will be traveling, how he recognized him at the station, what his name is—but the boy does not even turn around and remains silent. A gas lamp mounted to the boy’s right half-lights his face, which in profile resembles the snout of a mountain rodent, a marmot, and Wojnicz concludes that the driver must be either deaf or brazenly rude.

At last, after some three-quarters of an hour, they emerge from the shadow of the forest and drive into a surprisingly flat valley, an unexpected plateau here among wooded mountains. The sky is going out, but one can still see that piercing high horizon, which to every newcomer from the lowlands seems to come right up to the throat.

“Görbersdorf,” the driver says suddenly in a boyish, unexpectedly high voice.

But Wojnicz sees nothing, nothing except a dense wall of darkness tearing itself away in whole sheets from the mountainsides. Only when his eyes have adjusted to it does a viaduct suddenly emerge before them, under which they pass into the village, and behind it the enormous mass of a red-brick building, and then at once other, smaller buildings, and a street, and even two gas lamps. The brick building proves to be a colossus, growing before his eyes, and the motion of the vehicle draws rows of lit windows out of the darkness. Their light is a dirty yellow. Wojnicz cannot take his eyes off this unexpected triumphant sight and keeps looking back at it long afterward, until it sinks into the dark like a huge steamer.

The trap now turns into a narrow side lane along the stream, passes over a little bridge where the wheels raise a clatter like gunfire. At last it stops before a fairly large wooden building of peculiar architecture, suggestive of a house made of matches—so many verandas, little balconies, and terraces there are. A pleasant light burns in the windows. Beneath the first-floor windows there is a beautiful inscription in blackletter cut from thick sheet metal:

Gästehaus für Herren

Wojnicz climbs down from the trap with relief and draws deep into his lungs this new air, said to cure the gravest conditions. Perhaps he does so too soon, because at once he is seized by a fit of coughing so violent that he has to lean against the railing of the bridge. And then, coughing, he feels the chill and unpleasant sliminess of rotting wood, and his good first impression evaporates. He cannot stop the violent spasms of his diaphragm, and a terrible fear comes over him—that in a moment he will suffocate, that this will be the final attack. He tries to drive the panic away, as Dr. Sokołowski advised him, to think of a meadow full of flowers, of warm sunshine. He tries very hard, though his eyes are watering and his face is suffused with blood. It seems to him he is about to cough up his soul.

But then he feels a grip on his shoulder—and a tall, well-built, graying man offers him his hand. Through his tears Wojnicz catches sight of the man’s pink, healthy face.

“There now, sir. Let’s pull ourselves together,” the man says with confidence and a broad smile, so that the guest, scarcely alive from coughing, feels like clinging to him and letting himself be led to bed like a child. Yes. Exactly. A child. A bed. With some embarrassment he flings his arms around the man’s neck and lets himself be guided through a hall that smells of spruce smoke and up soft stairs carpeted with a runner. All of it summons some distant association with wrestling, with men’s sport, where hard bodies press against one another, rub, strike, but not in order to hurt each other—quite the contrary, in fact, to show tenderness and attachment under the pretext of struggle. He yields to the strong hands, lets himself be led into a room upstairs, seated on the bed, stripped of coat and sweater.

Wilhelm Opitz—for that is how the man introduces himself, touching his chest with a finger—covers him with a woolen throw and receives from some hands that appear for a moment in the crack of the door a mug of hot, savory broth. As Mieczysław drinks in little sips, Wilhelm Opitz raises a finger (at this moment Wojnicz realizes how essential a part of Wilhelm that finger is) and says in soft, slightly comic German:

“I wrote Professor Sokołowski to make you stop in Breslau. Too long and tiring a journey. I told him.”

The broth spreads pleasant warmth through Wojnicz’s body, and the poor fellow does not even know when he falls asleep. We stay with him a moment longer, listening to his calm breathing, glad that his lungs have quieted down.

Our attention is now drawn to a line of light, thin as a blade, falling into the room from the corridor and coming to rest on the porcelain chamber pot beneath the bed. We are drawn to the cracks between the floorboards—and disappear there.

At a quarter to seven the sound of a bugle woke Wojnicz, and for a long moment he could not tell where he was. The melody was unmistakably being played out of tune, which amused him and put him in a good mood. It seemed familiar to him, but in the way of things so simple they are almost ingenious. They seem as though they always were and always will be.

Mieczysław Wojnicz was made up of afflictions, and the person best informed about them was not even he himself but his father, January Wojnicz, a retired civil servant and landowner. He administered those afflictions with great expertise, gravity, and tact, treating the property entrusted to him in the form of his son with great responsibility and—naturally—love, though one stripped of all sentimentality and all those “womanish feelings” he so detested.

One of those afflictions, in the shaping of which the father himself had had some part, was the son’s exaggerated fear of being watched. So the young Wojnicz devoted much attention to other people’s gaze, checking whether it might be following him from behind a corner, from some nook, through a window where the curtain had been bent back, through a keyhole. The father’s caution and suspiciousness had turned into the son’s obsession. He felt as if another person’s gaze were something sticky that fastened to him like the soft, hideous mouth of a leech. That was why in every room where he was to spend the night he always carefully inspected the window curtains, plugged the keyhole with a ball of paper, checked for possible holes in the wall, cracks between the boards, even looked behind pictures. In boarding houses and hotels, spying is not so far-fetched after all—once, when he and his father were staying at a hotel in Warsaw during one of those health visits to a specialist, young Wojnicz discovered an actual little peephole in the wall, clumsily disguised by the rich pattern of the wallpaper, and of course sealed it with a pellet of bread; and when in the morning he tried to determine from where and by whom the guests might be watched, he discovered that behind the wall there was a service stairway used by the hotel staff. And yet! So it was not an obsession. People spy on each other. They adore it, watching a person when he does not know it. Judging, comparing. A person being watched like that becomes defenseless, becomes an unsuspecting, helpless victim.

When he woke, he immediately set about writing a message to his father, to reassure him. It was only a matter of a few simple words, and yet it did not go well; his hand was numb and weak. So he focused all his attention on his hand as it guided the point of the pencil across the cream paper of a leather-bound notebook. This movement fascinates us; we like it. It recalls the same twisting lines and spiral ornaments that earthworms burrow underground and bark beetles gnaw into tree trunks. Wojnicz sat in bed, propped up against two enormous pillows. In front of him he had a clever contrivance—something like a table without legs. Its underside was a cushion stuffed with peas—so that it settled easily on the writer’s knees.

First two digits appeared, forming “13,” then a straight line and a cross, “IX,” and after it four more digits making up “1913.” Next, out of the flourishes there emerged the word “Görbersdorf,” gone over several times to darken it. The umlaut was treated with particular reverence. After that the pencil moved across the paper evenly and steadily. The graphite rasped, the paper yielded beneath the rounded shapes of the letters.

The room was modest, but furnished comfortably. Two windows looked out onto the street and the little river before the house, though the view was obscured by crocheted thread curtains. Under one of the windows stood a round table and two comfortable upholstered chairs, already a little worn, almost armchairs—a pleasant corner for reading, if anyone had wanted one. To the left of the door stood the bed with its beautifully ornamented wooden headboard, and beside it a wardrobe. The dressing table had been placed to the right of the door. The walls were covered with fabric wallpaper in broad pale-blue stripes, which made the room seem taller and more spacious than it really was. On the wall hung prints from exotic regions: a herd of hares and a pack of hyenas.

In Polish, Mieczysław Wojnicz briefly described his impressions of the journey, converting one thousand nine hundred feet into meters (it came to almost five hundred), and entered the figures on a sketch map that illustrated his journey from Lwów all the way here. The brief comments concerned mainly the meals on the road. Beside the place marked “Wrocław/Breslau” he wrote: “Yellow pumpkin soup, then purée with cracklings, cabbage, and a cutlet exactly like our breaded pork cutlet. For dessert vanilla pudding with meringue and blackberry compote, very tasty.” Beneath that he added: “Payment: 5 marks.” He had promised his father that every day he would write him a few words, preferably about his health, but in truth he did not know what he felt, so he preferred to send recipes or geographical information to Lwów.

There came a soft knock at the door, and before he could say come in, a leather boot slipped into the gap between door and frame and gently pushed it open; after it appeared the black folds of a skirt, the lace of an apron, and a breakfast tray, which was quickly set down on the little table. The boot, the lace, and the apron vanished as quickly as they had appeared, and the bewildered Wojnicz only had time to pull the throw up over himself and mumble some greeting and thanks. He was so hungry that only the food interested him.

He would describe it at once in his notebook: two hard-boiled eggs in lovely faience eggcups, covered with little caps in the shape of hens; slices of smoked sheep’s cheese garnished with parsley; a ball of the yellowest butter served on a horseradish leaf; a bowl of fragrant lard with a small spreading knife; sliced radish; a basket of rolls of various kinds, light and dark; apricot jam in a glass dish; a mug of thick cocoa and a little pot of coffee.

After the full stop, the notebook snapped shut, and Wojnicz ate with gusto everything on the tray. Strengthened by the meal, he got up. Throwing the blanket around his shoulders, he padded over to his suitcase, from which he took a neatly folded set of underwear, and then set about his ablutions. And when he was drying his face with a towel permeated with the omnipresent smell of conifers that filled the boarding house, there returned vividly before his eyes the image of their country house and of the linen dried in winter in the attic, which Gliceria carried up there in pails when it was pouring outside. Back came the image of the attic, always full of dust, and the view from its little bull’s-eye windows—the fields and the small park—and the bitter, gentian-like smell of the rotting stalks of tomatoes, corn, pole beans. And by some law of synesthesia not wholly comprehensible, that image translated itself into bodily sensation: the roughness of clothes, the stiffness of collars, the angularity of freshly ironed trousers, the pressure of a hard leather belt. And it was precisely there, in the attic, whenever he could, whenever he was alone and for a moment far from his father’s drill, that he stripped naked and wrapped himself in a satin tablecloth edged with soft fringe, and felt how deliciously that fringe brushed his calves and thighs, and thought how wonderful it would be if they, like the ancient Greeks, could go about in such tablecloth chitons. And now, remembering that satin toga, he dressed and rejoiced that at last he felt strong and rested.

We watch as clothes appear layer by layer on his slender body until at last his figure, utterly unlike the one from yesterday, coughing and gray-faced, stands with a hand on the latch, eyes closed, imagining how it would look in the eyes of someone who might be watching it now. He looks well—a slim young man with fair hair and delicate features, in gray trousers and a brown wool jacket. After a moment he opens the door decisively.

No, we do not consider this an obsession, merely a harmless hypersensitivity at most. People ought to get used to being observed.

Wojnicz came downstairs at about ten, for he had an appointment for examinations at the Kurhaus.

Throughout the house, because of the small windows set sparsely in the walls, a half-light prevailed—typical of mountain architecture. There stood an oval table covered with a thick patterned cloth, a sofa and several chairs, and against the wall a piano, whose infrequent use was attested by a few finger marks on the gleaming lid and a sheaf of yellowing sheet music. A small hanging shelf beside it was full of books about the region, the surrounding ski routes, and local landmarks. In an enormous glass-fronted cabinet a beautiful porcelain service gleamed white, with cobalt-blue sentimental scenes—shepherds and sheep.

“Gemütlich,” Mieczysław whispered to himself, pleased to have remembered a certain German word he was especially fond of. His own language lacked such a word. Cozy? Pleasant?

And Dr. Sokołowski’s words came back to him too, from the time when he had begun treating him and struggling with his apathy—that one must make life appetizing. Yes, appetizing, Wojnicz thought, that was a better word than gemütlich, because it applied not only to space but to everything else as well—to someone’s voice, to a way of speaking, of settling into an armchair, of tying a scarf at the throat, to the way biscuits are arranged on a saucer. He ran a finger over the table covered in soft olive-green plush, and only after a moment did he notice with alarm the thin man sitting in an armchair by the window, with pronounced birdlike features and wire-rim spectacles on a prominent nose. He was enveloped in a cloud of cigarette smoke. Wojnicz’s hand sprang from the plush as if scalded and disappeared, embarrassed, into the clasp of the other. The man, equally disconcerted at this discovery of his solitude, stood up and introduced himself rather formally, in German, with a strange Silesian accent:

“Walter Frommer. From Breslau.”

Wojnicz slowly and distinctly pronounced his own first and last name, no doubt hoping that the other would remember them at once. They talked for a while, and Frommer informed him that he came to Görbersdorf for treatment regularly; he had been here, on and off, for three years already. Sometimes he went back to Breslau for a short while, but there he immediately got worse again.

“You see, the city of Breslau lies on the water. In spring swarms of mosquitoes hang over the buildings, small but vicious, and people suffer from rheumatism. In summer you can’t sit in the garden, which is why government officials stay there only for a short span of years and then go back to better places. Breslau is a stopover city”—a sadness entered his voice, as though he pitied the city. “It’s because of all that water, it creeps in everywhere... I bear it badly...” He began to cough. “There, you see, the very thought of it makes me cough.”

Wojnicz let his gaze escape toward the window, past which a cheerful party was just passing, breaking into laughter every few moments. He thought those people were laughing in Polish, though he could not really explain the impression to himself. From a distance one could not hear the words.

“Are you preparing to move into the Kurhaus as well?” he asked Frommer.

He thought the question might bring at least the faintest smile to the other man’s face, but he took it seriously.

“God forbid,” he protested. “There are too many people there. You can’t see anything from there. You won’t find out or learn anything. Life in a crowd is worse than prison.”

Well then, Wojnicz had probably formed his opinion of Walter Frommer now—an oddball.

Both seemed equally shy, because for a while they stood facing one another in awkward silence, each waiting for the other to utter some conventional phrase. They were rescued from this stalemate by the host, Wilhelm Opitz.

“I hope I’m not interrupting an animated conversation,” he said, and for a moment Wojnicz wondered whether Wilhelm was mocking them or simply that inattentive. But he took him firmly by the arm and led him toward the exit.

“Forgive me, but I must place the young man under Dr. Semperweiß’s watchful eye. Our guest arrived here in a deplorable state.”

Frommer mumbled something indistinctly, returned to his place by the window, and sat down in exactly the same position as before. As though he were employed here full-time as a smoking piece of furniture.

“Doctor Frommer is a little strange, but he’s a decent man. Like everyone in my boarding house,” Wilhelm said in that dialect increasingly pleasant to Wojnicz’s ear, once they were standing on the steps in front of the house. “The boy will take you to Doctor Semperweiß. Be careful with him—he doesn’t like people from the East. In fact, he doesn’t like anyone. It’s a great loss that there’s no one here like Doctor Brehmer,” he added thoughtfully, when a moment later they were both standing by the bridge.

Wojnicz watched as the mist formed strange streamers now and flowed upward like smoke.

“Do you perhaps know Doctor Sokołowski?” he asked.

Wilhelm’s face brightened and came alive.

“Of course I knew him when I was a child. He was friends with my father, who worked for him. We all work at the Kurhäuser here. How is he?”

Well, Wojnicz did not know exactly. He knew only that he worked at the Warsaw clinic, that he sometimes lectured in Lwów. His father had taken him for consultations when Sokołowski happened to be in that city. And thanks to him he was here now.

“Is he still so thin?” Willi asked.

Thin? No, not thin. Professor Sokołowski was a stocky, stout man. But Wojnicz did not have to answer the surprising question, because out of the bands of mist emerged Rajmund, yesterday’s driver, a lanky boy in his teens, whom Wilhelm greeted in a rather peculiar way: with a light smack on the head. The boy accepted it as an entirely natural, friendly gesture.

The two of them now walked down along the stream toward the center of the village. Rajmund was talking animatedly about something, but in such a strange dialect that Wojnicz understood very little. He watched with interest the handsome houses along the road and the workers repairing the electric lines. Rajmund asked whether Mieczysław knew what electricity was.

Then they bowed to two elderly women in wide skirts sitting on a bench in front of one of the houses.

“Frau Weber and Frau Brecht,” said Rajmund with an ironic smile, and this much, at least, Wojnicz understood.

A moment later the boy proudly pointed out Doctor Brehmer’s sanatorium, the same building Wojnicz had seen the evening before, though now it seemed even more immense, especially since the mist had almost vanished and the September sun shone generously somewhere high above the valley.

Rajmund vanished as soon as he had led Wojnicz to the right door in a broad corridor. Now Mieczysław was taken over by a nurse whose eyes were underlined by red puffiness. A brief polite smile exposed for a moment her large yellowed teeth, whose color harmonized with the worn gilt of the watch on a chain pinned to her apron. Above the pocket her first and last name had been embroidered: Sydonia Patek.

Wojnicz had to sit out his time in the doctor’s waiting room, because the doctor had not yet returned from his rounds. His fingers therefore reached for the illustrated magazines laid out for patients, but his eyes found no calm in them; they could not focus on the blackletter. To his surprise, however, Wojnicz found a Polish brochure, and at once, miraculously, his gaze relaxed upon the words of his native language:

In Prussian Silesia, a quarter of a mile from the Bohemian border, eleven miles southwest of Breslau, in a long valley stretching from east to west between the Riesengebirge and the Adlergebirge, there lies, in the Waldenburg district on the river Steine, the charming village of Görbersdorf, famous these many decades as a mountain climatic resort for chest patients.

The elevation of Görbersdorf is 570 meters above sea level, in the zone which medical science calls “free of consumption.” The mountains surrounding it reach 900 meters. They shelter the village and its curative establishments from the winds, which arrive here weakened; hence in Görbersdorf there reigns a stillness of air such as is seldom found in any valley.

He read no further, but folded the brochure in half and slipped it into his pocket. Now his attention was drawn to a glazed display case in which stood a human torso made of wood—without head, arms, or legs, with chest and belly open, displaying internal organs painted in various colors. Wojnicz went up to the wooden torso to get a closer look at the lungs. They were smooth and clean, polished, shining with varnish. They resembled fleshy petals of some monstrous flower, or fungi growing on tree bark. How wonderfully they were fitted to the size of the chest, how they had reconciled their airy nature with the cage of ribs. He examined them carefully, trying to peer under the pointed tip where they met the other, convoluted organs in their different colors. All the same, he was disappointed; perhaps he had expected something new, something he did not already know. A solution to the mystery. Why he was ill. And why others were not.

When he returned to his place, a familiar unease came over him, that agitation which always brought the same bodily reaction—sweats. He would have to undress and expose his body to a stranger’s gaze. And then panic: how would he hide his shameful affliction from the doctor? What would he have to say to avoid touching on all those matters so sensitive to him? How was he to slip away? He had practiced this so many times.

When the doctor burst into the waiting room, he did not even look at him—he crossed it with a quick stride, only the skirts of his white coat streaming behind him. With a movement of the hand he signaled for the patient to get up. Mieczysław followed him almost at a trot into a large consulting room with an enormous window, full of glass-fronted cabinets, assorted medical apparatuses, and strange chairs. It did not particularly surprise Wojnicz that leaning against the doctor’s desk was a rifle—large, not at all a hunting gun, rather a Winchester, perhaps, with a beautifully polished stock. Without turning around, the doctor told him to sit down, and in this way Wojnicz felt safely hidden behind the desk, as if in a trench.

He handed the doctor the letter of recommendation from Professor Sokołowski, but the doctor only cast a glance at it, plainly more interested in the body seated before him. The young man felt uncomfortable, and the reason was the gaze with which the other man regarded him. As though he did not see Mieczysław Wojnicz, the patient from distant Lwów, but only the body itself, something object-like and mechanical. First, without embarrassment, he pulled down Wojnicz’s lower eyelid, scrutinizing the color of the mucous membrane and the eyeball. Then he let his gaze travel from chin to temples; finally he told him to undress to the waist, looked critically at his chest, and began pressing the patient’s nipples with a finger.

“Slightly enlarged, and the lymph nodes as well,” he said. “Has it always been like this?”

“For several years,” the intimidated Wojnicz replied.

The doctor took him by the chin and drew a finger across the two-day, uneven, very scant stubble. He carefully felt the lymph nodes, after which his bold fingers began to percuss the back, drawing from it a dull sound, a booming as though from underground. He did it very meticulously, centimeter by centimeter, like a sapper looking for a hidden bomb. All this went on for half an hour, until at last the doctor sighed and told him to dress. Only then did he reach for the letter. And, looking over the metal rims of his spectacles, he said:

“Phthisis.” It sounded as though he had whistled. “Tuberculosis, consumption, and nowadays, fashionably, Morbus Koch. You know all this, young man, don’t you?”

Wojnicz fastened the buttons of his shirt and nodded.

“Not very advanced, frankly speaking. Oh, something small, a grain of something. ‘Phthisis’ means disintegration, you know?” He pronounced the word, Zerfall, with obvious pleasure, stressing the r. “But here we know how to handle disintegration.”

“Yes, Doctor Brehmer’s method...” Wojnicz began, but the doctor, impatient, stood up and waved a raised hand.

“Oh yes. Brehmer noticed that this going off to Italy with tuberculosis makes not the slightest sense. Only mountain air truly heals. Air like ours here. Have you seen?” The doctor went to the window and for a brief moment fell into thought. “We are here in a cauldron”—and as he said this, he made circular, exaggeratedly sweeping movements with his arms, as if he wished to impress the nature of the phenomenon more forcefully on his listener. “Beneath us lies a great underground lake, which makes it warmer here than elsewhere. This air is rich in oxygen, but there is no wind. Pulmonary diseases and epidemics were never known among the local population, would you believe it? No one here ever suffered from the lungs. And furthermore, the altitude remains within the necessary limits for the treatment of pulmonary diseases, because it does not over-accelerate the work of the heart, as happens in places lying above nine hundred meters above sea level. Fir forest grows here, saturating the air with ozone, and ozone plays a key role in renewing the blood and the entire organism. Breathing itself will stop the process of disintegration in your young lungs. Think of it that way. Imagine that with every breath pure light flows into your lungs.” The doctor looked at Mieczysław through lenses that enlarged his dark eyes in a disturbing way. “And besides that, we have other attractions here. You need only submit, surrender to the discipline of treatment. Feel as if you were in the army.”

He went to the window and, with a movement of the head, indicated the patients strolling in the park.

“Those are your comrades-in-arms.”

Wojnicz suddenly realized that he was not going to like this doctor. He remembered the gentle, kind Dr. Sokołowski.

“That is clear to me, doctor,” he replied, tugging down the cuffs of his shirt. “I would only like to know whether I have any chance.”

“Of course you have a chance. Otherwise you would not have come here at all, young man. You would not have dared come here if you had not felt you had a chance. You would have gone on quietly being ill in the East. It’s flat there, isn’t it?”

Wojnicz was now to hear many interesting things about the brilliant Doctor Brehmer, who had bought the village of Görbersdorf and the entire surrounding area, more than a hundred hectares of forest and land in all, in order to found this sanatorium. Brehmer had long ago noticed that autopsy findings and examinations of living patients suffering from tuberculosis always showed a disproportion between heart and lungs—the lungs were relatively large, while the heart was small, with thin, flaccid, weak walls. Earlier no one had paid any special attention to this relation, and no one had thought to connect this disproportion between the organs of the chest with the etiology of tuberculosis. And yet it seemed obvious that a small, weak heart led to a slowing of blood circulation, and thus to a chronic insufficiency of blood supply to the lungs and pulmonary epithelium. Tuberculosis followed from that. Besides, Brehmer had studied the geographical distribution of the disease, and this strengthened his conviction as to the aforesaid etiology. From travelers’ reports it emerged that there were places and regions where tuberculosis did not occur: higher mountains in all climate zones, Iceland, the Faroe Islands, the steppes of Kirghizia.

On the one hand, the special features of the high-mountain climate were decisive. Lower atmospheric pressure causes the organism to react with increased cardiac activity and an accelerated pulse, because in this way it defends itself against oxygen deprivation—and this leads to an acceleration of metabolism and a rise in body temperature. Secondly, lifestyle and diet were important: plenty of food, especially fat, koumiss with alcohol content, heavy physical labor.

The quickened pulse and accelerated heartbeat cause the heart muscle to increase in volume, and a strong musculature develops in that organ; often among the inhabitants of the aforementioned regions there is even hypertrophy of the heart, thus a phenomenon exactly opposite to what is found in tuberculosis patients.

“My dear young man,” Doctor Semperweiß concluded his lecture, “that is our entire prescription. In Central Europe the zone free of consumption begins at roughly four hundred and fifty meters above sea level. Add to that the constant supervision of a physician, who regulates the diet. And also exercise in the fresh air. Nature herself heals us.”

Doctor Semperweiß drew out a sheet of paper and wrote down the recommendations point by point, commenting on them aloud in a voice already a little bored:

“At least six weeks, preferably several months. Walks individually prescribed for the patient, necessarily on routes of varying incline; along the way there must be benches at short intervals, so that one does not grow too tired. Moderate cold-water treatment. And that is precisely what will help you. Moderation in medicines. In the event of strong coughing reflexes, I recommend suppressing the cough as far as possible, and when it cannot be suppressed, taking little sips of cold water or soda water with hot milk. Should bleeding from the lungs occur, God forbid, we apply ice packs to the heart and lungs and administer morphine injections. In severe attacks, with shortness of breath and weakness, we first give a strong stimulant, for example champagne. Yes, do not be afraid of champagne or of alcohol in general. But absolutely in small quantities. Drunkenness is strictly forbidden! In the case of fever, the temperature is first taken every two hours in order to confirm it. Night sweats are effectively combated by drinking milk in the evening with two or three teaspoons of cognac or liqueur. Sister Sydonia Patek will explain and show you everything.”

As he delivered this whole speech, he was writing down the recommendations, and Wojnicz admired the fact that he could do both things at once.

“You are staying with Herr Opitz, yes? Every day you will come to the Kurhaus for treatments and outdoor rest, and as soon as a place opens up in the sanatorium, I shall let you know; everything here is fluid. Fluid,” he stressed. “For the time being Herr Opitz’s boarding house is just as good for your health as we are, or Doctor Römpler’s sanatorium, and the daily short walks will put color in your cheeks.”

The doctor rose briskly and handed Wojnicz the sheet of recommendations. And that was that—he had been admitted.

Now he sat in the waiting room while the ugly nurse prepared his treatment diary and other necessary documents. He drew from his pocket the leaflet, folded in quarters, and finished reading it:

In general it must be admitted that, from the curative point of view, residence in such places as Meran in Tyrol, Görbersdorf in Silesia, or Davos in Switzerland, laid out after the model of Görbersdorf, has hitherto been accounted the most effective cure. Dr. Römpler’s sanatorium, founded in 1875, lies almost directly at the foot of the mountains and consists of a suitable number of buildings in the form of elegant villas. A water conduit 1,140 meters in length brings crystal-clear mountain spring water down from the mountain at whose foot the sanatorium lies, gushing directly from porphyry rock, into the elegantly appointed bathing rooms of the shower building.

Patients do not lack occupations and amusements. The cure itself, dinners, etc., occupy a large part of the day, while the charming environs nearest Görbersdorf offer a great many excursions. The aim of the cure is that the patient should strive to combat his disease himself. He must be made resistant through the strengthening of the organism. In this way the development of the disease will first be halted, then the suffering will gradually subside and health begin to return. Through continual gymnastics the afflicted lungs learn to function properly, and the fresh mountain air stimulates the action of the heart. The results of the Görbersdorf cure may be counted among the most favorable. Nearly 75% of patients recover.

How wonderful it would be to believe that he belonged to that seventy-five percent.

2.

Schwärmerei

Wojnicz was returning to the boarding house with the small notebook in which the history of his treatment was henceforth to be recorded, and he was considering what Doctor Semperweiß had told him. The most important thing in the cure was the regimen. One rose early, very early. Temperature taken. Entered in the notebook. Before breakfast, eaten between seven and eight, gymnastics were obligatory; after breakfast, a walk and, along the way, perhaps baths by Father Kneipp’s method, as well as treatments. Walks along a prescribed route. Second breakfast at ten—always fresh bread with butter and milk. Outdoor rest on one of the many terraces. Lunch between half past twelve and half past one (soup with meat, a hearty meat dish with vegetables, then dessert and compote; on Sundays, instead of compote, something sweet is served, cake or a sweet flour dish). After lunch, coffee in the winter garden or in the pavilions, without fail. Then outdoor rest again, and another walk, but the afternoon route had to differ from the earlier one. Afternoon tea around four to half past four, and supper at seven—hot meat with potatoes, and absolutely a glass of milk with it. In the evening the thermometer again, and a few sentences in the notebook about one’s condition. Plenty of sleep. No excitement. Good hearty food. Lots of meat, milk, and sheep’s cheese. Wojnicz decided on breakfasts and lunches at the Kurhaus; suppers he would eat at the Boarding House for Gentlemen. That was what he had been advised. Once he moved into the Kurhaus, he would take the rest of his meals there. Guests were summoned to meals by bugle call.

He was bursting with excitement and good intentions; it was that kind of euphoria that comes when one begins something new, something that irrevocably heralds a new beginning, when one cuts oneself off from the old and lets what was sink into oblivion. Now even the stern and ironic Semperweiß seemed to him a herald of change.

As he walked, he tried to remember how the houses and boarding houses were laid out. He looked at the somewhat bizarre building of the astronomical observatory, where Doctor Brehmer was said to study the influence of the cosmos and the weather on the treatment of tuberculosis. Then he reached the mighty edifice of Villa Rosa and turned back.

The full, golden September sun was shining. Mieczysław Wojnicz aimed his steps at the centers of the large flat stones with which the road was paved.

The two elderly women were still sitting on the bench in front of the house, silently shelling broad beans, snapping dry, crackling pods. One of the beans suddenly sprang from one of their wrinkled hands and landed right in front of his shoe. He carefully picked it up between two fingers and wanted to return it to its owners, but they, for some sudden reason unknown to him, sprang up from the bench and, taking their bowls and baskets, disappeared into the house. Only their black glossy skirts flashed in the sun. Well then, nothing had happened. Wojnicz rubbed the brown broad bean against his sleeve; it seemed perfect. He tossed it in the air and caught it. Not knowing what to do with it, he slipped it into his pocket.

He found the boarding house door standing wide open, which surprised him, and then at once saw a prayer book lying on the ground, flung straight into a puddle. Its cream pages had already soaked through with dirty water. He picked up the prayer book and went inside, filled with sudden unease.

The sitting room downstairs was empty—presumably all the residents of the boarding house were still away at treatments. He laid the mud-spattered prayer book on the table and was just about to head upstairs when his attention was caught by the half-open dining-room door, and beyond it a pair of shoes—on the table—somehow familiar. Without thinking, as if hypnotized, he went to the door and pushed it open to get a closer look.

The shoes belonged to the lower end of an oblong bundle of uncertain shape, which in the end turned out to be a human body. It lay in the dining room on the table where meals were eaten. It seemed to be well wrapped in folds of cloth—Wojnicz had the impression it had on countless skirts, chemises, corsets, shawls. Wojnicz had never seen a woman from so close up, and so still; they were always flitting past, always in motion. One could not fix one’s attention on them and notice every detail. But now he had such a body before him, and there was no doubt it was a dead one. He looked at the black lace-up boots protruding from beneath the skirts and petticoats. The latter were edged with some sort of embroidery, though the lace had already been washed thin, its borders somewhat frayed. The bootlaces had been tied carefully in double bows—strange that someone who by afternoon was dead had laced her boots so precisely that very morning. The outer skirt, made of slightly lustrous material in thin black-and-gray stripes, lay neatly. Higher up there was something like a close-fitting jacket of dark, almost black cloth, fastened with round buttons like the ones Polish priests have on their cassocks. From beneath that garment there protruded a white blouse, rather open, with one button torn off so that only the thread remained; the collar of that blouse had now been pulled high, right up under the chin, but not carefully enough, for Mieczysław caught sight of a livid blue-red stripe on the neck, shocking against the white skin.

At last he had to do it—look higher, at the face. To his horror he saw eyes half closed, and beneath the lashes a thin strip of gleaming eyeball. The twisted head was turned toward him, as though it wished to confess something. On the lips, narrow and already somewhat bluish, he noticed the hint of a smile—it struck him as wholly out of place, almost ironic. The tips of the teeth protruded beneath the upper lip, quite dry. And one more thing—the face was covered in fine pale down, like fluff.

He stood petrified, hardly breathing.

In fact he recognized at once that this was the woman who had brought him breakfast that morning. Back then he had remembered only the boot that had opened the door. And the ample shapes squeezed by a corset. Nothing more. Only here, after death, could one see the woman whole.

“She hanged herself,” said Willi Opitz, standing in the doorway.

Wojnicz started, frightened by the host’s low, resonant voice. Opitz said it in a tone as though he were noting some disgraceful negligence, some event not to be tolerated. But his voice trembled.

“Don’t upset yourself. The men from the mortuary will be here in a moment and take the body away. Rajmund ran for them.”

Wojnicz did not know what to say. His tongue was dry as chips of wood, and he felt a band tightening around his throat.

“When did it happen?” was all he asked.

“When? Just now—an hour ago. I went up to her when she didn’t come down to take the vegetables from the delivery man. She was hanging there. I cut her down. Go to your room, boy. Ah, here they are from the mortuary already.”

“She brought me breakfast this morning,” said Wojnicz, and despite himself the agitation could be heard in his voice. “That’s your maid, isn’t it?”

“Ah, no, no. That’s my wife.”

Opitz waved a hand as though shooing away a wasp and opened the door to the grim workers from the mortuary, who began conversing with him in a low dialect. Mieczysław withdrew from the dining room—going quickly upstairs, he heard their muffled voices, but did not understand what they were saying. The whole conversation seemed to him the muttering of people who did not need words in order to understand one another.

Wojnicz sat down in a russet armchair adorned with a crocheted headrest. He was shaken. Strange—he had not thought that nice Willi Opitz had a wife. He ought to have known that men usually have wives of one sort or another, who, not always clearly and distinctly visible, support the family enterprise from the kitchen or the laundry. Preoccupied with himself, his arrival, and his illness, he had not even noticed her. And now she was dead.
