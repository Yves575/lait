You were the one who told me about that town.

That summer evening, breathing in the sweet smell of grass, we made our way upstream along the river. Several times we crossed the little falls made by the sand-control barriers, and every so often we stopped to watch the slender silver fish swimming in the pools. We had both been barefoot for some time already. The clear water washed our ankles with its chill, and the fine sand on the riverbed wrapped itself around our feet—like soft clouds in a dream. I was seventeen, and you were a year younger.

You had carelessly shoved your red low-heeled sandals into a yellow vinyl shoulder bag and kept walking a little ahead of me, from sandbar to sandbar. Wet blades of grass clung to your wet calves, making lovely green punctuation marks. I carried my worn white sneakers in both hands.

As if tired from walking, you sat down casually in the summer grass and looked up at the sky without a word. Two small birds flew swiftly across the sky side by side, crying sharply. In the silence, the first hint of blue dusk began to gather around us. When I sat down beside you, I felt something strange. It was as if thousands of invisible threads were delicately binding your body to my heart. The briefest flutter of your eyelids, the faintest tremor of your lips, was enough to shake me.

At such an hour, neither you nor I had a name. Summer twilight by the river, when we were seventeen and sixteen; a vividly colored feeling on the grass—that was all there was. Before long, stars would begin flickering above us one by one, but the stars had no names either. Side by side on the grass by the river, we sat in a world without names.

“The town is surrounded by high walls,” you began.

You drew the words up out of the depths of silence, like someone diving naked into the deep sea to retrieve a pearl.

“It isn’t a very large town. But it isn’t so small you can take everything in at a glance, either.”

It was the second time you had spoken of the town. That was how the town acquired the high walls that enclosed it.

As you went on talking, the town acquired a beautiful river and three stone bridges—the East Bridge, the Old Bridge, and the West Bridge. It acquired a library and a watchtower, an abandoned foundry and plain communal housing. In the pale light of a summer evening near dusk, you and I leaned close together and looked at that town. At times we viewed it from a distant hilltop, narrowing our eyes; at other times from so near at hand we could almost touch it, our eyes opened wide.

“The real me is living inside that town surrounded by high walls,” you said.

“Then the you sitting here in front of me now isn’t the real you?” I asked, naturally enough.

“No. The me who is here now isn’t the real me. She’s only a stand-in. Nothing more than a shifting shadow.”

I thought about that. A shifting shadow? But for the moment I held my judgment in reserve.

“So what is the real you doing in that town?”

“I work in the library,” you answered quietly. “My hours are from around five in the evening until around ten at night.”

“Around?”

“In that place, all times are only approximate. There’s a tall clock tower in the central square, but it has no hands.”

I pictured a clock tower with no hands. “And can anyone go into the library?”

“No. It isn’t open to just anyone. You need special qualifications to enter. But you can go in. You have the qualifications.”

“What kind of qualifications?”

You smiled softly, but did not answer.

“But if I go there, I can meet the real you?”

“If you can find the town. And if...”

You broke off there and colored faintly. But I could hear the words you had left unspoken.

And if you truly want the real me...

Those were the words you had deliberately chosen not to say. I put an arm gently around your shoulders. You were wearing a pale green sleeveless dress. Your cheek rested against my shoulder. But on that summer evening, what I held in my arms was not the real you. As you had said, it was only the shadowy surrogate that stood in for you.

The real you were inside the town enclosed by high walls. There was a beautiful island in the river thick with willows, several low hills, and quiet single-horned beasts everywhere. The people lived in old communal dwellings and led simple but comfortable lives. The beasts liked to eat the leaves and nuts of the trees growing in the town, but in the long winters, when the snow piled up, many of them died of cold and hunger.

How fiercely I longed to enter that town. To meet the real you there.

“The town is surrounded by high walls, and getting inside is very difficult,” you said. “Getting out is harder still.”

“How do I get in?”

“You only have to wish for it. But truly wishing for something isn’t so easy. It may take time. In the meantime, you may have to cast various things away—things that matter to you. But don’t give up. No matter how long it takes, the town won’t disappear.”

I imagined meeting the real you inside that town. I pictured the vast apple orchard flourishing outside it, the three stone bridges spanning the river, the cries of invisible night birds. And the small old library where the real you worked.

“There will always be a place there waiting for you,” you said.

“A place for me?”

“Yes. There is one vacant position in the town. You’re going to fill it.”

What kind of position could that be?

“You’re going to become a Dream Reader,” you whispered, as if confiding an important secret.

I couldn’t help laughing. “Look, I can hardly remember my own dreams. It would be awfully hard for someone like me to become a Dream Reader.”

“No. A Dream Reader doesn’t have to dream. In the library stacks, all you have to do is read the many old dreams gathered there. But not everyone can do that.”

“But I can?”

You nodded. “Yes, you can. You have the qualifications. And the me who is there will help you with that work. I’ll be by your side every night.”

“So I’ll be a Dream Reader, reading old dreams every night in the library stacks of the town. And you’ll always be beside me. The real you.” I repeated the facts aloud.

In my arms, your bare shoulder above the green dress trembled slightly. Then suddenly stiffened. “Yes. But there’s one thing I want you to remember. Even if I meet you in that town, the me who is there won’t remember a single thing about you.”

Why?

“Don’t you understand why?”

I did understand. Yes—the shoulder I was gently holding was no more than your stand-in. The real you lived in that town, far away and mysterious, enclosed by high walls.

And yet the shoulder beneath my hand was so smooth and warm that I could think of it only as the shoulder of the real you.

In the actual world, you and I lived some distance apart. Not so far that it felt remote, but not close enough that either of us could simply decide on a whim to go and see the other. If I changed trains twice and traveled for an hour and a half, I could reach the city where you lived. And neither your city nor mine was surrounded by high walls. So of course we were free to come and go.

I lived in a quiet suburban neighborhood near the sea, while you lived in the center of a much larger, livelier city. That summer, I was in my final year of high school, and you were in your second. I went to the local public high school; you went to a private girls’ school in your city. For various reasons, we actually saw each other only once or twice a month. We more or less took turns: I visited the city where you lived, and you came to mine. When I visited your city, we went either to a small park near your house or to the public botanical garden. The garden charged admission, but beside the greenhouse there was a café that was never very crowded, and it became our favorite place. There we ordered coffee and apple tart—a small extravagance—and lost ourselves in quiet conversation.

When you came to my town, we almost always took walks by the river or along the shore. There was no river near your house in the middle of the city, and of course no sea, so when you came to my town, the first thing you wanted was to see one or the other. That abundance of natural water drew you.

“Something about looking at water calms me,” you said. “I like listening to the sound it makes.”

We had met the previous autumn through a certain turn of events, and by then had been close for about eight months. Whenever we met, we embraced where people were least likely to see us and gently pressed our lips together. But we went no further than that. One reason was simply that we didn’t have the time. Another, more practical one, was that we couldn’t find an appropriate place to become more deeply intimate. But more than either of those, the larger reason was probably that we were so absorbed in talking to each other, so intent on making the most of our time. Neither of us had ever before met anyone to whom we could speak so freely and naturally, saying exactly what we felt and thought. To encounter someone like that seemed almost miraculous. So whenever we saw each other, once or twice a month, we simply talked and talked, forgetting the passage of time. No matter how long we talked, we never ran out of things to say, and when the time came to part and we said good-bye at the station gates, we always had the feeling we had left many important things unsaid.

Of course it wasn’t that I felt no physical desire. When a healthy seventeen-year-old boy stood before a beautiful sixteen-year-old girl with the lovely swell of her breasts, especially when he put his arms around her supple body, there was no way he could help being stirred by sexual desire. But instinctively I felt that sort of thing could wait until later. For the time being, what I needed was to see you once or twice a month, to take long walks with you, to talk frankly about all sorts of things. To exchange intimate knowledge of ourselves, to come to know each other more deeply. And to embrace somewhere in the shade of a tree, to kiss. Into hours as lovely as that, I didn’t want to hurriedly drag in anything else. If I did, something important there might be damaged, and we might never be able to return to what we had before. Let the physical side wait until later, I thought. Or rather, my intuition told me so.

But what in the world had we talked about there, with our foreheads nearly touching? By now I can’t remember. We must have talked about too much for me to identify each subject one by one. But once you began telling me about the special town surrounded by high walls, that came to occupy the greater part of our conversation.

Mostly you spoke about how the town was constituted, while I asked practical questions about it and you supplied the answers. In that way, the town’s concrete details were settled and recorded. The town was originally something you had created—or something that had existed inside you from before. But when it came to raising it up into something visible, something describable in words, I think I helped no little amount. You spoke, and I wrote it down. Much as the philosophers and religious teachers of antiquity had behind them faithful, meticulous recorders—or people called apostles. As a capable scribe, or a faithful apostle, I even made a small notebook solely for recording it. That summer, the two of us were completely engrossed in that shared labor.

In autumn, the beasts’ bodies are covered with radiant golden fur in preparation for the coming cold. The single horns growing from their foreheads are sharp and white. They wash their hooves in the chilly river water, gently stretch out their necks to devour red berries, and chew the leaves of broom.

It is a beautiful season.

I stand in a watchtower built into the wall and wait for the evening horn. Shortly before sunset, the horn is sounded once long and three times short. That is the rule. The soft note of the horn slips through the stone-paved streets in the gathering dusk. Perhaps for hundreds of years—or perhaps far longer—it has been repeated unchanged. Its sound has sunk deep into the gaps between the stone walls of the houses, and into the stone statues lined up along the hedges in the square.

When the horn sounds through the town, the beasts raise their heads toward some ancient memory. One stops chewing leaves, another stops tapping its hooves against the pavement, another wakes from a nap in the last patch of sunlight, and each lifts its head to exactly the same angle.

Everything is fixed for an instant, like statuary. The only thing moving is their soft golden fur stirring in the wind. And yet what are they looking at? With their necks bent in one direction and their eyes fixed on empty space, the beasts do not move in the slightest. They only listen to the horn.

When the horn’s last note is swallowed into the air and disappears, they gather their forelegs under them and stand, or stretch to set themselves right, and then almost all at once they begin to walk. The momentary spell is broken, and for a while afterward the town’s streets are ruled by the sound of their hooves beating the ground.

The line of beasts moves through the winding cobbled streets. No one takes the lead. No one guides the column. The beasts lower their eyes and simply move on down a river of silence, their shoulders swaying minutely from side to side. Even so, between each one there seems to be woven an intricate bond impossible to deny.

After watching them several times, I come to understand that the route they follow and the speed at which they move appear to be exactly fixed. Gathering companions into the herd here and there, they cross the Old Bridge with its gentle arch and walk to the square with its pointed spire—the clock on the tower there has indeed lost both its hands, just as you said. There they are joined by small groups that had been down on the river island eating the green grass. Then they proceed upstream along the road by the river, pass through the factory district beside the dry canal that runs north, and gather up another band that had been searching for nuts in the woods. After that they turn west, pass beneath the foundry’s roofed passageway, and climb the long stairs that run along the northern hill.

There is only one gate in the wall surrounding the town. Opening and closing it is the Gatekeeper’s duty. It is a heavy, sturdy-looking gate, crisscrossed with thick iron plates. Yet the Gatekeeper opens and closes it with ease. No one but him is permitted to lay a hand on it.

The Gatekeeper is a large, powerfully built man, but one absolutely devoted to his work. His pointed head is clean-shaven, and his face is smooth as well. Every morning he boils water in a large pot and carefully shaves both head and face with a big sharp straight razor. His age is impossible to guess. One of his duties is to sound the horn that gathers the beasts, morning and evening. He climbs the tower in front of the gatehouse, some two meters high, and blows the horn up toward the sky. How can such a soft, lustrous sound emerge from a man who looks so rough, almost coarse? Every time I hear it, I wonder.

Once he has sent every last beast out beyond the wall at dusk, he pushes the heavy gate shut again and finally drops the great lock into place, with a dry, chilly clack.

Outside the north gate there is a place for the beasts. There they sleep, mate, and bear their young. There are woods and thickets there, and a small river runs through it. That place too is enclosed by a wall. It is only a low wall, a little over a meter high, and yet for some reason the beasts cannot get over it. Or else they do not try.

There are six watchtowers in the wall on either side of the gate. Anyone can climb them by way of old wooden spiral staircases. From the towers, the beasts’ habitat can be seen in its entirety. But ordinarily no one ever goes up there. The town’s inhabitants seem to have no interest at all in the beasts’ lives.

And yet, I am told, for one week at the beginning of spring people willingly climb the wall’s watchtowers to watch the beasts fight. At that season the beasts become more savage than one would ever imagine from their usual appearance, and the males, forgetting even to eat, battle with all their strength over the females. Growling, they try to drive their sharp single horns into their rivals’ throats or bellies.

Only during that one week of mating do the beasts not enter the town. The Gatekeeper shuts the gate so that the townspeople will not be endangered. Accordingly, the horn is not sounded morning or evening during that time. No small number of beasts are gravely wounded in the fighting, and some lose their lives. Out of the red blood spilled onto the ground, a new order and new life are born—just as the green branches of the willow all break into bud at once in early spring.

The beasts live within a cycle and an order of their own, beyond our knowing. Everything repeats itself regularly, and that order is paid for with their own blood. When that savage week passes and the soft rains of April wash the shed blood away, the beasts gradually return to their former calm and gentle selves.

But I have not actually witnessed such a scene with my own eyes. I have only heard about it from you.

In autumn, the beasts remain crouched where they are, their golden coats shining in the evening sun, waiting in silence for the sound of the horn to be swallowed by the air. Their number is surely no fewer than a thousand.

That is how a day in the town comes to an end. Days pass, seasons change. But days and seasons are only provisional things. The town’s true time lies elsewhere.

Neither of us ever visited the other’s house. We never met each other’s families, and we never introduced each other to our friends. Put simply, we didn’t want anyone—anyone at all in the world—to intrude on us. You and I were perfectly fulfilled simply spending time together; we didn’t want to add anything else. And even in the literal, physical sense, there was no room to add anything. As I said before, we had mountains to talk about, and the time we could spend together was limited.

You said almost nothing about your family. All I knew were a few fragmented facts. Your father had been a local civil servant, but when you were eleven he was forced to resign because of some blunder, and now he worked as a clerk at a cram school. I didn’t know what kind of “blunder” it had been. But it seemed to have been the sort of thing you did not want to put into words. Your mother had died of internal cancer when you were three, so you had almost no memory of her. You couldn’t even remember her face. When you were five, your father remarried, and the following year your younger sister was born. So your present mother was your stepmother, but once—and only once—you said something to the effect that, more than you did toward your father, you might still be able to feel “a little affection” for her. It was like a casual footnote printed in tiny type in the corner of a page. As for your sister, six years younger than you, I learned no more than this: because she was allergic to cat hair, your family couldn’t keep a cat.

When you were a child, the only person you could feel a wholehearted, natural affection for was your maternal grandmother. Whenever you had the chance, you took the train by yourself to visit her house in the neighboring ward. During school vacations, you sometimes stayed there for several days. Your grandmother loved you unconditionally. Out of her meager income, she would buy you little things as well. But each time you went to see her, you noticed a disapproving look come over your stepmother’s face. She never said anything, but little by little you stopped visiting as often. That grandmother too had died suddenly of heart disease a few years before.

You told me these things little by little, in fragments. As if drawing out, piece by piece, something worn and tattered from the pocket of an old coat.

There is one other thing I still remember well: whenever you talked to me about your family, for some reason you always stared fixedly at the palm of your hand. As though, in order to follow the thread of the story, it were indispensable to decipher the lines there with the greatest care.

As for me, there was almost nothing I had to tell you about my own family. My parents were perfectly ordinary. My father worked for a pharmaceutical company, and my mother was a housewife. They behaved like ordinary parents and spoke like ordinary parents. We kept one old black cat. I had little to say about school life either. My grades weren’t bad, but neither was I so outstanding as to attract notice. The place at school where I felt most at ease was the library. I liked sitting there alone, reading books and letting time pass in my imagination. I had already read most of the books there that I wanted to read.

I remember very well the first time I met you. It was at the awards ceremony for the High School Essay Contest. The winners from first through fifth place had been invited. You and I had placed third and fourth, and our seats happened to be next to each other. It was autumn. I was then a second-year high school student, and you were still in your first year. The ceremony was a tedious affair, so in the intervals we talked a little in low voices. You were wearing your school uniform: a navy blazer and matching navy pleated skirt, a white blouse with a ribbon, white socks, and black slip-on shoes. The socks were immaculately white, and the shoes were polished without a speck on them—as if seven kindly dwarfs had carefully shined them before dawn.

It wasn’t that I was particularly gifted at writing. I had loved reading books since I was little and picked one up whenever I had spare time, but I had always thought I had no talent for writing myself. Then, because everyone in my class had been made to write an essay during Japanese class for the contest, mine was selected and sent to the judges, survived the final screening, and unexpectedly ended up winning a place near the top. To be honest, I couldn’t understand what was supposed to be so exceptional about what I had written. Even rereading it, it seemed to me no more than a plain, unremarkable composition. Still, if several judges had read it and decided it merited a prize, there must have been something in it. My homeroom teacher, a woman, was delighted that I had won. Never in my life had a teacher shown such favorable feeling toward anything I had done. So I made no unnecessary remarks and gratefully accepted the prize.

The essay contest was held every autumn across the district, with a different theme each year. That year’s theme was “My Friend.” Unfortunately, I couldn’t think of a single “friend” I wanted to write about over five sheets of manuscript paper, so I wrote about the cat we kept at home. I wrote about how that elderly female cat and I got along, how we lived together, how we communicated our feelings to one another—within limits, of course. When it came to that cat, I had plenty to say. She was a very intelligent cat, and full of character. There were probably several cat lovers among the judges. Most cat lovers naturally feel affection and sympathy toward other cat lovers.

You wrote about your maternal grandmother: about the bond between a lonely old woman and a lonely girl, and the small, genuine set of values that had taken shape between them. It was a charming, moving essay. Several times better than mine. I couldn’t understand why mine had won third place and yours fourth. I told you so honestly. Smiling, you said that on the contrary, you thought what I had written was several times better than yours. Really, you added. I’m not lying.

“Your cat sounds wonderful,” you said.

“She’s really smart,” I said.

You smiled.

“Do you have a cat?” I asked.

You shook your head. “My sister is allergic to cat hair.”

That was the first tiny piece of personal information I learned about you: your sister was allergic to cat hair.

You were a very beautiful girl. At least that was how you appeared to me. You were small, with a rather round face and slender, beautiful fingers. Your hair was short, with straight black bangs falling across your forehead like carefully shaded darkness. Your nose was straight and small, and your eyes very large. By ordinary standards, perhaps the balance between the two was off, but for some reason it was precisely that slight mismatch that drew me. Your pale pink lips were small and thin, always dutifully closed, as if hiding several important secrets within.

The five of us prizewinners went up onstage in turn and solemnly received certificates and commemorative medals. The tall girl who had won first place gave a short acceptance speech. The extra prize was a fountain pen—the pen manufacturer was sponsoring the contest. I would go on to use that fountain pen for many years. Shortly before the long, tedious ceremony ended, I took out my notebook, wrote my name and address on one of the memo pages in ballpoint pen, tore it out, and secretly handed it to you.

“If you’d like, would you write me a letter sometime?” I said in a dry, stiff voice.

I’m not ordinarily the sort to do anything so bold. By nature I’m shy—and of course timid as well. But the thought that we might part there and never see each other again struck me as terribly wrong, utterly unfair. So I gathered my courage and acted.

You accepted the slip of paper with a slightly surprised expression, folded it neatly into quarters, and put it into the breast pocket of your blazer, above the gentle, mysterious swell of your chest. Then you touched your bangs and blushed a little.

“I’d like to read more of what you write,” I said, like someone trying out a clumsy excuse after opening the wrong door.

“I’d very much like to read a letter from you too,” you said, nodding several times in a small way, as if to encourage me.

Your letter reached me a week later. It was a lovely letter. I must have read it at least twenty times. Then I sat down at my desk and used the brand-new fountain pen I had just won as a prize to write a long reply. That is how we began exchanging letters, and how our relationship—ours alone—began.

Were we lovers? Could we really be called that so simply? I don’t know. But for close to a year, at least during that period, our hearts were joined, purely and completely. And before long we brought into being, and came to share, a secret world for the two of us alone—the strange town surrounded by high walls.

It was on the evening of my third day in the town that I pushed open the door of that building.

It was an old stone building with no distinguishing features. After walking east for a while along the road by the river, past the central square facing the Old Bridge, you came to it. There was no sign at the entrance to indicate it was a library. Only a brass plate bearing the number 16 had been unceremoniously affixed there. The plate was tarnished, the numerals hard to read.

The heavy wooden door opened inward with a deep creak, and beyond it was a dim square room. No one was there. The ceiling was high, the light from the lamp on the wall was weak, and the air smelled faintly of someone’s dried sweat. It was the kind of dimness in which everything seemed lightly veiled, about to dissolve into molecules and be sucked away somewhere. The worn cedar floorboards gave off sharp sounds here and there underfoot. There were two tall windows, and not a single piece of furniture.

At the far end of the room was a door. It was a simple wooden door with a small frosted-glass pane at about face level, and there too the number 16 was written in an old-fashioned decorative script. Through the frosted glass I could see a faint light. I knocked twice lightly and waited, but there was no answer. No footsteps either. After a moment I steadied my breathing, turned the tarnished brass knob, and gently pushed the door open. It creaked, as if warning the place: someone has come.

Beyond it was another square room, perhaps five meters by five, and here too there was no one. The ceiling was lower than in the first room. There were no windows; the room was enclosed by plaster walls. No paintings, no photographs, no posters, no calendar, and of course no clock—only bare blank walls. There was one rough wooden bench, two small chairs, a table, and a wooden coat rack. No coat hung on the rack. In the center of the room stood an old-fashioned wood stove, rust blooming on it, glowing red with fire, and on top of it a large black kettle steamed. At the far end was what seemed to be a circulation desk, and on the desk a ledger lay open. It looked as if whoever had been working there had been interrupted in the middle of it by some sudden errand. Presumably that person—presumably the librarian—would return before long.

Behind the counter there was a dark-colored door that seemed to lead to the stacks. If so, this really must be the “library.” Though not a single book was visible, the place still retained something unmistakably library-like. That particular air possessed by libraries everywhere in the world, whether large or small, old or new.

I took off my heavy coat and hung it on the rack, sat down on the hard wooden bench, and warmed my hands by the stove while waiting for someone to appear. There was not the slightest sound. A silence like the bottom of deep water. I tried clearing my throat once, but it didn’t sound like throat-clearing at all.

It was about fifteen minutes later, I think, that you opened the door to the stacks and appeared from within. There was no clock, so I can’t be sure. Seeing me on the bench, you stiffened for an instant, your eyes opening wide. Then you let out one slow breath and said, “I’m sorry to have kept you waiting. I didn’t know someone was here.”

I couldn’t find the proper words to say, and only nodded several times in silence. Your voice did not sound like your voice. It was different from the one I remembered. Or perhaps in this room all sounds and voices resonated differently than they normally do.

At that moment the kettle lid suddenly rattled and gave a small shiver, like an animal waking.

“And what is it you require?” you asked.

I was looking for old dreams.

“Old dreams,” you said, and looked at me, your small thin lips pressed straight together. Of course you did not remember me.

“But as you know,” you said, “only Dream Readers are permitted to touch old dreams.”

Without a word I removed my dark-green glasses and let you see my eyes. The unmistakable eyes of a Dream Reader—eyes that cannot endure the bright light of day.

“I understand. You have the qualifications,” you said, lowering your gaze slightly. Perhaps the nature of my eyes had unsettled you. But there was no help for it. In order to enter this town, I had had to alter my eyes in that way.

“Will you begin work today?” you asked.

I nodded. “I still don’t know whether I’ll be able to read them properly. But even if it’s only little by little, I’ll have to get used to it.”

The room remained silent. Even the kettle had gone still again. Excusing yourself, you quickly finished the ledger work you had left half-done. I watched you from the bench. Outwardly, you had not changed at all. You were exactly as you had been that summer evening. I remembered the vivid red sandals you had been wearing. And the grasshopper that had suddenly leapt up from the grass nearby.

“Have we perhaps met somewhere before?” I asked without thinking, though I knew it was a useless question.

You lifted your eyes from the ledger and stared at my face for a moment, holding a pencil in your left hand—yes, you were left-handed, here as in the other town—then shook your head.

“No, I don’t believe we have,” you answered. The reason you spoke so politely was probably that although you were still sixteen, I was no longer seventeen. To you, I was now a man much older than that. It couldn’t be helped, and yet the passage of time pierced my heart.

After finishing the record you had been working on, you closed the ledger and put it away on the shelf behind you, then made me an herbal tea. You took the kettle from the stove and carefully mixed the hot water with crushed herbs to brew a dark green liquid. Then you poured it into a large ceramic cup and set it before me. It was a special drink provided for the Dream Reader, and preparing it was one of your duties.

I drank the herbal tea slowly. It had a thick, peculiar bitterness and was by no means easy to drink. But its restorative power soothed my still-injured eyes and calmed my mind. That was what it was for. You watched me from across the table. Perhaps you were worried whether I liked the tea you had made. I gave you a small nod, as if to say, It’s all right. At that, a smile of relief appeared at your lips. A familiar smile. I had not seen it for a very long time.

The room was warm and quiet. Even without a clock, time passed soundlessly, like a slender cat padding along the top of a wall.

It wasn’t that we exchanged letters all that often. About once every two weeks, more or less. But each letter was fairly long. And generally speaking, I think your letters tended to be somewhat longer than mine. Not that the length of our letters carried any particular significance in our exchange.

I still have every one of your letters with me, but because I never kept copies of the ones I sent, I can’t clearly remember the specific things I wrote in them. In any case, there can’t have been anything especially remarkable in them. Mostly I wrote about daily life and the small things that happened around me. I also wrote about the books I read, the music I listened to, the films I saw. I wrote about things that happened at school. Since I belonged to the swim team—not because I had wanted to, and I could hardly be called a dedicated athlete—I suppose I wrote about practice too. When you were the one I was writing to, I could write naturally about anything. I could express what I thought and felt almost uncannily well. It was the first time in my life I had ever written so fluently. As I said before, until then I had believed I was poor at writing. You must have drawn that ability out of me from somewhere deep inside. You were always delighted by the little bits of humor in my letters.

“That’s probably what my life lacks most,” you once said.

“Like some vitamin or other?” I said.

“Yes. Like some vitamin or other,” you said, nodding firmly.

I was crazy about you, and when I was awake I was probably thinking about you almost all the time. Most likely in dreams as well. But in my letters I tried to restrain myself from confessing those feelings head-on. I resolved, as far as possible, to write only about practical, concrete things. At the time, I suppose I wanted to cling to a world I could actually touch with my own hands—if possible with a little humor woven into it. Because once I began writing directly about inward stirrings of the heart, about love or being in love, I had the feeling I would only drive myself further and further into a blind alley.

Your letters, by contrast, contained less about concrete things around you than mine did, and more about inward thoughts. Or dreams you had seen, or little pieces of short fiction. Several of those dreams in particular left a deep impression on me. You dreamed often, and could remember the details vividly, as if recalling something that had actually happened. That was hard for me to believe. I myself hardly ever dreamed, and even if I did, I could almost never remember what was in them. The instant I woke in the morning, they all came undone and were sucked away somewhere. Even when I occasionally woke in the middle of the night from a vivid dream—which was rare—I simply fell asleep again at once, and by morning remembered nothing.

When I told you this, you said:

“In my case, I keep a notebook and pencil by my pillow, and the moment I wake up I write down the dream I had that night. Even if I’m busy and pressed for time. Especially if I wake in the middle of the night from a particularly vivid dream—no matter how sleepy I am, I write down the details as fully as I can then and there. Those are often important dreams, and they tell me many important things.”

“Many important things?” I asked.

“Things about me that I don’t know myself,” you answered.

For you, dreams existed on almost the same level as actual events in the real world, and were not things that could simply be forgotten or disappear. Dreams were like a precious inner wellspring, telling you many things.

“That’s something you get by training. If you make the effort, I’m sure you’ll be able to remember your dreams in detail too. So try. I’d really like to know what kind of dreams you have.”

“All right,” I said. “I’ll try.”

But although I made some effort—if not so much as keeping a notebook and pencil by my pillow—I simply couldn’t take an interest in my own dreams. The dreams I had were too scattered, too incoherent, too generally incomprehensible. The words spoken there were indistinct, and the scenes I saw contained hardly anything that looked like a plot. Sometimes they contained troubling things one could never tell another person. Rather than involve myself with such dreams, I preferred to listen closely to the long, colorful dreams you had seen.

Sometimes I turned up in your dreams. Hearing that made me tremendously happy. After all, it meant that in some form I had been allowed to participate in the imagined world inside you. And you too seemed pleased when I appeared in your dreams. In most cases, though, the me in your dreams had no very important meaning, only the role of a supporting character in the drama.

Didn’t you ever have more explicit dreams—the sort that would be awkward to talk about in front of me, the kind I often had and that sometimes, despite myself, even stained my underwear? Were you really telling me honestly about every dream you saw? Those were the things I always found myself wondering as I listened to your dream stories.

You seemed to talk frankly and without concealment about all sorts of things. But no one can know whether that is really true. It seems to me that there is no one in this world who carries no secret in the heart. That is something people need in order to survive here.

Isn’t it?

“If anything perfect exists in this world, it is this wall. No one can get over it. No one can destroy it,” the Gatekeeper declared.

At first glance, the wall looked like nothing more than an old brick wall. It seemed as though the next strong storm or earthquake could easily bring it down. How could such a thing be called perfect? When I said that, the Gatekeeper looked as though I had spoken ill of his family for no reason at all. Then he seized me by the elbow and led me over to the wall.

“Take a close look,” he said. “You see there’s no mortar between the bricks, don’t you? And each brick is a little different in shape from the next. Yet every one fits together so tightly there isn’t room for a single hair.”

He was right.

“Scratch it with this knife,” the Gatekeeper said. He took a work knife from his jacket pocket, snapped the blade open, and handed it to me. The knife looked old, but the blade had been sharpened with great care. “You won’t leave so much as a mark.”

He was right about that too. The tip of the blade merely made a dry scraping sound. Not a single white line appeared on the brick.

“You see now? Storms, earthquakes, cannon fire—nothing can break this wall. Nothing can damage it. It never could before, and it never will.”

He pressed one palm against the wall and tucked in his chin with a proud look, as though posing for a commemorative photograph.

No, I murmured inwardly, nothing in this world is perfect. Anything that has form must contain some weakness somewhere, some blind spot. But I did not say it aloud.

“Who built this wall?” I asked.

“No one built it,” was the Gatekeeper’s unshakable view. “It was here from the beginning.”

By the end of the first week, I had taken up several old dreams you had selected for me and tried to read them. But those old dreams told me nothing meaningful. All I heard was vague muttering, and all I saw were blurred fragments of images. It was like being made to listen to a tape—or watch a film—spliced together at random and run backward.

In the library stacks, countless old dreams were lined up in place of books. It seemed no one had touched them for many long years; each was faintly dusted over with white. Old dreams were shaped like eggs, though each differed in size and hue, as if they had been laid by many different kinds of animals. Yet they were not precisely egg-shaped. When one was taken down and examined closely, it became clear that the lower half bulged more than the upper, and the balance of weight was oddly uneven. But that very irregularity let them sit securely, so that even without support they did not roll off the shelf.

Their surface was hard and smooth like marble. Yet they did not have marble’s weight. I could not tell what substance they were made of, or how strong it was. If one fell to the floor, would it crack? In any case, they had to be handled with the greatest care, like the eggs of some rare living creature.

There was not a single book in the library—not one. Once, no doubt, books had stood in rows on these shelves and the people of the town came here in search of knowledge and pleasure, as they would at any ordinary library. Something of that atmosphere still lingered faintly, like a scent left behind. But at some point all the books must have been removed from the shelves, and old dreams placed there in their stead.

Apparently there was no Dream Reader besides me. At least for the moment, I seemed to be the only Dream Reader in the town. Had there been another Dream Reader before me? Perhaps. Judging by how minutely the rules and procedures concerning Dream Readers had been devised and maintained, there probably had.

Your duties in the library were to protect the old dreams lined up there and manage them properly. You chose which dreams were to be read, and recorded in the ledger that they had been read. Before evening fell, you opened the library door, lit the lamps, and in the cold season kindled a fire in the stove. You made sure there was always enough rapeseed oil and firewood. And for the Dream Reader—for me—you prepared the dark green herbal tea that soothed my eyes and calmed my mind.

With a large white cloth, you carefully wiped away the white dust that had settled on an old dream and placed it on the desk before me. I removed my green glasses and laid both hands on the old dream’s surface, cupping it in my palms. After about five minutes, the old dream gradually woke from its deep sleep and its surface began to glow faintly. A pleasant, natural warmth came into both my hands. Then the dream began to spin itself out—tentatively at first, then with a certain ardor, like silk unwinding from a cocoon. It had something to tell. It must have been waiting patiently on the shelf for the time to come out of its shell.

But the voice in which it spoke was too faint, and I could not fully make out the words. The images it projected never acquired clear enough outlines; they simply faded, collapsed, and were swallowed by the air. Or perhaps that was not the dream’s fault but mine: perhaps my new eyes were still not functioning properly. Perhaps my powers of understanding as a Dream Reader had not yet developed.

And then the time came when the library had to be closed. There was no clock anywhere, but as that hour approached, you somehow knew it naturally.

“How is it? Is the work going well?”

“Little by little,” I answered. “But even reading one leaves me exhausted. Maybe I’m doing something wrong.”

“There is no need to worry,” you said, turning the knob to close the stove’s air vent. You blew out the lamps one by one, then sat across the table from me and looked directly into my face. When you looked at me so straight on, I became flustered. “There is no need to hurry. There is all the time in the world here.”

Following the prescribed steps exactly, one by one, you closed the library. You did it with a serious expression, without haste, with steady calm. As far as I could tell, the order of the tasks was never reversed. Watching you, I found myself wondering whether it was really necessary to lock up the library so rigorously. In this quiet, gentle town, who would break into the library in the middle of the night to steal or destroy old dreams?

“Would it be all right if I walked you home?” On the third night, when we stepped outside the building, I finally summoned the courage to ask.

You turned back and looked at my face, your eyes opening wide. In your black pupils a single white star from the sky was reflected. You seemed unable to understand what my offer meant. Why should you need me to escort you home?
