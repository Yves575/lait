1





“A blue tiger came in this morning,” Kaede’s grandfather said.

“How on earth did it turn the doorknob? Clever thing.”

He seemed more astonished that the tiger had opened the front door and let itself in than by the fact that it had entered his study—or even by the fact that its body was covered in blue fur.

“Well, good thing it didn’t bite you.”

Kaede made a point of cracking the joke lightly.

Inside, though, she felt a small pang of disappointment. He was awake for once, and still it was this sort of talk again.

She came about once a week, but her grandfather was usually asleep.

And even on the rare occasions when he was awake, all he ever talked about were his visual hallucinations.

He would keep it up until she left, and no real conversation was possible.

Even so, Kaede listened obediently to the “blue tiger story,” nodding again and again.

Because the time she spent in her grandfather’s house—also her mother’s family home—felt irreplaceable.

“And then,” her grandfather said, imitating the tiger’s gait, its forelegs crossing as it walked, “when it left, it gave me the most blissful smile.”

“The tiger smiled?”

Ah... here we go again, she thought with a wry inward smile.

It was a story about a hallucination, something impossible in real life, and yet here she was listening as though she meant it.

That was how it always went. She would start out only pretending to listen intently, but his way of telling things was so skillful that before she knew it she had been drawn into his world.

And today, of all days, she could almost imagine a blue tiger actually leaping out of an illustration in one of the books on the shelves.





Having talked himself out, he must have been satisfied.

Her grandfather’s eyelids slowly drifted shut.





All day long he sat in the electric reclining chair in this room.

They had chosen a larger model to suit his tall, spare frame, but it had proved even more comfortable than expected, and the fact that he now scarcely left it at all had been a major miscalculation.

On the side table beside him stood the wooden cane he needed whenever he moved about.

Still, the care manager who had recommended it had complained to Kaede more than once, with a sigh: he uses it when he goes to the toilet, but when he picks out a book from the shelves he can’t be bothered at all, and I’m worried he’ll fall.

(So he still loves books. But probably the contents...)

Probably very little of what he read was going into his head anymore. The thought crossed her mind, swift and sad.

The book-crammed study smelled of stale ink.

It reminded Kaede of the old-book district in Jinbocho, which she loved.

When she looked up, the dappled sunlight from the window had turned into a camouflage pattern across her grandfather’s sleeping face.

The high bridge of his nose and the lines etched at the corners of his eyes cast intricate shadows over a face strangely free of age spots, though he was seventy-one.

Compared with years before, the flesh had fallen away from his jaw and cheeks, but that only served to make the depth of his features stand out more sharply.

His long, thick hair, parted from the center of his broad forehead, was perhaps seventy percent white, the rest black, the gradation between the two giving him the sculpted solidity of an emperor stamped on an ancient Roman coin.

Even allowing for a granddaughter’s bias, he seemed magnificently handsome.

(He must have been popular. Surely.)

Kaede gently pulled the blanket that had slipped down back up to his thin neck.





By the time she had finished cleaning and carefully sprayed the room with the soap-scented antibacterial mist, making sure not to let it touch the books on the shelves, it was already nearly time for the physical therapist to come for his rehabilitation.

The spray was not there only to keep the room clean.

Her grandfather frequently saw tiny bugs in his hallucinations, mosquito-like things.

When that happened, the spray could also serve as a makeshift “insecticide.”

(See you, Grandpa.)

(I’ll come again.)

Beside the study door stood a dressing table that had effectively come down to them from her late grandmother.

Not age-related deterioration, perhaps, but age-related evolution.

The grain of the wood seemed to have had time itself worked into it like layers of makeup in complex shades, giving it a depth of character all its own.

Kaede took a hairbrush from one of the drawers, quickly tidied her hair, and composed her face in the mirror.

(Smile.)

The study door, once a heavy oak one, had already been remodeled into a sliding door in preparation for the day when her grandfather might have no choice but to rely on a wheelchair.

Sliding it open as quietly as she could, Kaede left her grandfather’s house in Himonya.





2





On the way home, swaying on the Tokyu Toyoko Line, she turned to the train window and saw reflected there a face with no expression at all.

She had taken the trouble to put on a smile, but by now not even a trace of it remained.

The sky had already begun to fade, like lips brushed with a pale lipstick.

It was early autumn, and the great thunderheads of summer were gone; clouds of all sorts were scattered across the evening.

A memory of her grandfather suddenly passed through Kaede’s mind.









Twenty-three years earlier. Kaede, age four.

Sitting in her grandfather’s lap as he sat cross-legged on the veranda, she gazed up at a sky dyed red with sunset.

Her grandfather lowered his clear, intelligent eyes to her.

“Kaede. What do those clouds over there look like to you? Use all of them and make a single story.”

Looking back now, it was a rakugo-style three-topic tale.

Perhaps he had been trying to set the wings of her imagination fluttering—

perhaps it was his own form of aesthetic education.

Kaede answered without a moment’s hesitation.

“That cloud is little grandpa. And that one is flat grandpa. And, um... the biggest cloud is a grandpa fatter than you.”

“That won’t let you make a story,” he said, but his face broke into a grin.

And then, astonishingly enough, as though it couldn’t be helped, he improvised a fairy tale on the spot in Kaede’s stead, titled “The Three Grandpas.”

She no longer remembered the details very well.

But she did remember the ending: “Fat Grandpa,” who loved to eat, accidentally took all the cold medicine in the world, mistaking it for sugar, and though everyone made fun of him terribly for it, he ended up living longer than anyone else on earth.

It was probably meant as a lesson for Kaede, who hated bitter powdered medicine.

But his way of telling it was so extraordinarily funny that she had clapped her hands in delight.

“Look, Kaede.”

When she looked up, only the “big cloud”—“Fat Grandpa”—remained; “Little Grandpa” and “Flat Grandpa” had, quite literally, vanished into thin air.

Just like the ending of the story.





Dumbfounded, Kaede had looked back and forth again and again between her grandfather and “Fat Grandpa” in the sky.

Thinking back on it now, he must have been secretly checking the clouds as he spun the tale.

And if “Little Grandpa” or “Flat Grandpa” had been the one left at the end, the whole course of the story would surely have changed.

“Hey, Grandpa. Tell Kaede another story. Or else—”

Little Kaede looked up and tugged on the hair growing out of the mole on his Adam’s apple.

It came out with surprising ease, and she remembered finding that so irresistibly funny that she burst out laughing.

Maybe, Kaede thought now, back then—

(maybe I pulled the stopper out of Grandpa’s intelligence.)





It was only half a year ago that something about her grandfather had begun to seem visibly wrong.

When she went walking with him, his stride was plainly shorter.

“Grandpa, maybe you’re fatter than you look. Your legs aren’t keeping up.”

He tilted his head and gave a wry little smile.

“Old age, I suppose.”

At first Kaede thought it was weight gain, or simply aging—

or rather, she tried to think that.

But after that, the decline was rapid.

When he drank his beloved coffee, the hand holding the cup trembled violently.

Whenever she visited, he was dozing in the chair in his study.

He had grown habitually stooped, and whatever he did, his movements were slow.

No—or rather, more than any of that.

More than anything else, Kaede knew she would never forget the shock of that day.

Her smartphone rang in the middle of the night.

She rubbed the sleep from her eyes and answered, and the voice on the other end—a young man, by the sound of him—introduced himself awkwardly as a member of the ambulance crew.

Then, repeatedly faltering as if reluctant to say it, he went on.





“Are you Ms. Kaede? Ah—yes, I see. Well, your name was on the emergency contact memo posted on the wall here, so that’s why I’m calling. The thing is, your grandfather, who is here with us, dialed 119. And, um—well...”

“What happened?”

“He says, ‘Kaede is lying here covered in blood.’”





At the clinic he usually went to, they told them it was probably Parkinson’s disease, but they couldn’t say for sure, so he ought to go to a larger hospital.

At the university hospital he underwent detailed tests, including a CT scan.

The result—the young doctor announced it matter-of-factly, with her grandfather sound asleep in the chair beside him.

“It’s dementia with Lewy bodies.”





That her grandfather, who had been so brilliant, had developed dementia at an age barely past seventy—

Kaede could not possibly accept that at once.

But when she began looking things up herself on the internet and in materials she ordered, every one of his symptoms matched the disease exactly.

For the first time she learned that the number of people with dementia in Japan alone was said to exceed six million—and that dementia, far from being a single thing, actually came in several different forms.

Broadly speaking, what people call dementia can be divided into three major types.

The most common, accounting for roughly seventy percent of all patients, is Alzheimer’s dementia, which apparently develops when a kind of protein called amyloid beta accumulates in the brain.

This was probably the form most people pictured first when they heard the word dementia.

The next most common was vascular dementia, caused by the aftereffects of cerebral infarction or stroke, and this accounted for about twenty percent of cases overall.





Both of those forms often involve memory impairment—repeating the same story again and again—as well as disorientation, in which one’s sense of time and place grows vague, or wandering outside.

And then there was the diagnosis her grandfather had received: dementia with Lewy bodies—also called DLB, from the initials of the English name.

Since the name itself was only coined in 1995, it was, in the long history of human illness, one of the more recently identified diseases.

In recent years it had been attracting attention as “the third dementia,” and apparently research into its pathology was advancing rapidly, not only in clinical settings but in trials as well.

In the brains and brainstems of DLB patients, there always appeared tiny deep-crimson structures like little fried eggs—Lewy bodies.

And these “little fried eggs” caused Parkinsonian symptoms such as trembling limbs and difficulty walking; REM sleep behavior disorder, marked by loud talking in one’s sleep; a state of excessive daytime drowsiness; and impairment of spatial perception, the inability to judge distance properly.

But—

the most distinctive symptom of DLB, the one unlike anything else, was above all visual hallucination.

For some patients the hallucinations were black-and-white, for others in color, but what they all had in common was this: they were vivid, graphic, sharply defined.

A person might wake in the morning, open their eyes, and find ten expressionless people standing silently in the room, staring at them so intently it felt as though they would bore holes through them.

Or there might be an enormous snake coiled sluggishly on the dining table.

A girl with braided pigtails might trail behind them all day, wherever they went.





Thoroughly unrealistic hallucinations were not unusual either.

A pig walking on two legs, briskly crossing one’s field of vision.

Fairies gracefully bouncing over a plate.

And the blue tiger her grandfather too had seen—

Strangely, in many cases there was no accompanying auditory hallucination.

The creatures that stirred within such visions were, after all, only visual phantoms; they did not speak to the patient.

But of the information human beings receive from the outside world through the five senses, sight alone accounts for a full ninety percent.

Which meant that for most DLB patients, those creatures were unmistakably real.

If there was one proverb they most wanted to quote, it might be “Seeing is believing.”

After all, they could see it there before their eyes.

However much those around them tried to deny its existence, how could it be easy to make them accept that it “wasn’t there,” that it “didn’t exist”?

And yet when people around them carelessly said, “There’s no such thing here,” or “That can’t be there,” or “Pull yourself together,” the patient would sometimes grow angry.

That was why people said caring for someone with DLB was difficult.

When Kaede read a caregiving handbook, she found this written there:





“When a patient tells you, ‘I can see a big bug,’ or ‘I’m scared,’ do not deny it by saying, ‘It’s only your imagination,’ or push them away by saying, ‘You’re sick, so please don’t make trouble.’ Instead, clap your hands and say gently, ‘See? Now it’s gone, isn’t it? You’re all right now.’ It can also be effective to change the subject.”—





That was probably how it had to be, she supposed.





And besides, she had wanted above all to avoid any situation in which she might quarrel with her grandfather, who had never once gotten angry with her.

That was why Kaede had avoided talking at length about his illness, and why, in front of him, she had consistently avoided denying the reality of what he saw.

To begin with, making a patient aware that he had dementia was probably close to impossible; and even if it were possible, it seemed too cruel.

But even so.

There was a strange discomfort in her own thinking and behavior, as though a division that ought to come out even were somehow leaving a remainder.

It felt a little different from the escapist thought—or rather wish—that a man like her grandfather could not possibly have dementia, or that he could not possibly go on losing his intellect like this.

(Yes. Something is different—)

But what exactly that unease was—

for now, Kaede still could not explain it in any concrete way.





3





Fifteen minutes by bus from Gumyoji Station.

When she returned to her one-room apartment, a book had arrived.

It was a collection of criticism by Takeshi Setogawa, the literary critic who had loved mysteries beyond measure.

On the colophon it said: “First edition, first printing, April 1, 1998.”

If Kaede remembered correctly, Setogawa had died not long afterward, at the young age of fifty.

Which meant that this book was, in effect, his final work.

Raised from childhood under her grandfather’s influence, Kaede had become a complete mystery obsessive. Before long novels alone no longer satisfied her, and she had even begun reading the Setogawa criticism on her grandfather’s shelves.

And what she felt then was not so much surprise as exasperation.





Setogawa took all kinds of works up for discussion and spoke of their charms from his own unfettered angle, but sometimes—no, almost without exception—his essays were more interesting than the books themselves.

In his serial column “Pilgrimage to Masterpieces,” for instance, he singled out the representative works of the three grand masters of orthodox detective fiction—Ellery Queen, Agatha Christie, and John Dickson Carr—and thoroughly pilloried them with lines like, “Are they really such masterpieces?”

His arguments were more logical and more thrilling than the originals, and impeccable as reasoning besides—but whether Setogawa knew it or not, his overflowing love for those writers seeped through between the lines, and every time she read him, Kaede found herself strangely warmed.

The god who had made her love old foreign mysteries—

(Setogawa Takeshi.)

The mere act of murmuring the name inside herself made her heart leap.

Around 1970, when he was young, Setogawa had been a central figure in the legendary university circle known as the Waseda Mystery Club, which produced a great many mystery writers and critics.

There had once been a café called Mon Chéri in Nishi-Waseda, where students from the Waseda Mystery Club gathered day after day, arguing mysteries so fervently that foam flew from the corners of their mouths, and at the center of the circle there was always, apparently, Setogawa’s smiling face, thick-browed and sharply featured.

And her grandfather too, she had heard, had been one of the principal members of the Waseda—

Coffee suited orthodox detective fiction beautifully.





The tall signboard, red with psychedelic white lettering spelling out “Mon Chéri—Coffee Specialists,” must have given the impression of shutting out anyone who was not a “mystery specialist.”

A rich, bitter coffee, its foam swirling like some bottomless enigma.

A yellow-tiled outer wall that called Leroux’s The Mystery of the Yellow Room to mind.

The sound of the troupe’s shoes from the little theater on the second floor—was that Chesterton’s “The Queer Feet,” perhaps, or Rampo’s The Stalker in the Attic?

Now that Mon Chéri no longer existed, there was nothing to do but let imagination spread its wings.

And yet, with Setogawa or her grandfather as narrator, surely that café had once radiated a heat and brilliance like Tokiwa-so in the world of manga, or Liangshan Marsh in Water Margin.

(I wish I could have heard the two of them talking mysteries.)

Precisely because the place no longer existed, Kaede’s daydreams only swelled.

Sadly, Mon Chéri was gone.

But books still remained.

She liked having the books she loved near at hand.

And besides, the books at her grandfather’s house had all been carefully preserved in translucent glassine covers, with not a single folded corner in any page, so borrowing them somehow made her hesitate.

And so Kaede had decided to buy all of Setogawa’s essay collections in one go.

(Good. It’s practically like new. It even still has the obi.)





She was delighted by the condition of the book.

Honestly, she would have preferred to collect them all new.

But this final volume was already out of print, so she had had no choice but to buy it through an online used-book shop.

Now she finally had every one of his books.

(What does it say about a twenty-seven-year-old woman that she gets this excited about completing a set like this?)

Aware of the smile spreading over her face, she stood there and idly flipped through the pages.

Then—

four small slips of paper fluttered out from between them and fell onto the carpet like ginkgo leaves.

(Hm. What are these?)

Kaede carefully picked them up and laid them out on the table.

Then she stared at the four rectangular scraps, each a different size, and thought.

(Too many to be bookmarks.)

(But)

(too heavy to be sticky notes—)

They were clippings from newspapers and magazines.

And every one of them was an obituary reporting Setogawa’s death.





4





Taking advantage of the holiday, Kaede went to Himonya in Meguro for the first time in three days.

In a quiet residential neighborhood not far from Himonya Hachimangu, the local tutelary shrine—

there stood her grandfather’s house, tucked away in a corner: a little two-story wooden home, already starting to run to seed.

From the token garden, cherry and fatsia branches reached beyond the fence.





On the wooden nameplate affixed to the gatepost, her grandfather’s surname had been written in bold, vivid strokes of ink.

It was the handwriting she had known all her life.

A nameplate, people said, was the face of a house.

If the exterior still retained any dignity, if it still wore a certain taut presence, perhaps it was thanks to the elegance of the calligraphy on that nameplate.

But once inside the gate, that atmosphere was abruptly spoiled.

There had once been round stepping stones laid here and there to mark the way to the front door, but since her grandfather had developed dementia, they had been replaced by a plain little concrete path devoid of all charm.

When she turned the knob of the front door, which had not yet been remodeled, the fake-soap smell of disinfectant hit her nose at once.

“Are you the helper—”

Kaede was about to call out, but stopped herself immediately.

There were no shoes by the entryway that looked like they belonged to a visiting caregiver.

The home-help worker must have finished cleaning and laundry and just left.

Along the hallway walls, still-new handrails had been installed in several places.

For her grandfather, whose footing was uncertain, multiple rails had become essential for moving around inside the house.

When applying for subsidies to purchase welfare equipment of this sort, the procedures—though differing from municipality to municipality—were often cumbersome and time-consuming.

And so, in reality, many people like her grandfather ended up having to pay for much of it themselves.

She entered the living room to the left of the hallway.





Her eyes happened to fall on the great central pillar, which still barely retained a sheen, and she saw many pencil lines drawn horizontally across the wood.

They were marks from measuring the heights of her mother when she was little, and of Kaede, the only grandchild.

The numbers and dates written beside them had almost faded away, but here too one could see the beauty of her grandfather’s hand.

And yet when she saw the post of one of the handrails driven right through those letters as if piercing them, her chest tightened.

Glancing toward the window, she noticed several white T-shirts hanging indoors to dry.

(Oh no. The helper slipped up.)

If there was a DLB patient in the house, it was best not to hang clothes indoors if it could be helped.

Patients could mistake hanging clothes for people.

White T-shirts in particular often became, for DLB patients, a “white canvas” onto which intense hallucinations were projected.

For the same reason, she had been told it was better not to leave portraits or family photographs where patients could see them, and she herself had once hurriedly put away the framed photos that had stood on the table, hiding them somewhere deep in the dresser.

She had just begun hastily taking the T-shirts off their hangers when, behind her, she heard her grandfather’s voice—surprisingly steady.

“Sorry. Kanae hung those out before she left. I think there may still have been some dirt that hadn’t come out.”

Her grandfather entered the living room carrying a coffee cup and slowly lowered himself onto the bed.

The bedroom upstairs had already become little more than a storeroom, so his range of movement was now largely limited to this living room, where the bed was, and the study all the way in the back.





But looking at him today, he seemed in remarkably better shape than the last time she had come.

Large day-to-day fluctuations in condition were another major hallmark of DLB.

“No, I was only smoothing out the wrinkles,” Kaede said, covering for herself, and gave up on taking the T-shirts down.

“So it was Mom here, not the helper.”

“She seemed to have work left to do and hurried off. Too bad you just missed each other.”

Privately, Kaede was relieved.

That was better—

at least for today, when she meant to put that question to him.

To begin with, it was rare these days to see him looking this well.

Today really was her chance.

“Coffee Kanae makes is good even when it’s gone cold.”

Still smiling, her grandfather shifted himself slightly on the bed.

Then he took a sip of coffee with a hand that trembled only a little and said,

“It doesn’t look as though I’m in any danger of spilling my coffee today. Immodest though it may sound, I must be in rather excellent form. Which is why I’d like to venture a guess. It’s nothing more than intuition, really.”

He touched the coffee to his lips once more, then looked straight at Kaede.

“You have something important to say to me, don’t you? I can tell by your face.”





Kaede felt suddenly close to tears.

That “boku” of his—his own particular way of saying “I.”

That bright, dark-eyed, gentle gaze.

It was as though the grandfather of those days had come back.

Perhaps because he was not in one of his somnolent spells, his speech was perfectly clear as well.

Come to think of it, in the past six months, out of concern for his health, she had hardly had a serious conversation with him at all.

If she was going to ask, it had to be now.

Working up her courage, Kaede began, “Yes, actually...”

“There’s something I want to ask you, Grandpa.”

“What is it?”

“Grandpa...”

She struggled with all her might not to cry.

“Grandpa... don’t you know you’re ill? Don’t you know that what you keep seeing isn’t real, that they’re hallucinations?”

No good.

Her voice was shaking.

“But you don’t want to worry me...”

Tears came after all.

Though she had sworn to herself she would not cry.

“You don’t want to worry me, so you’re pretending not to know, aren’t you?”

Keeping the same soft smile, her grandfather took another sip of coffee.

Then he carefully set the cup down on the dining table beside the bed.

“Yes. Just as you say, Kaede. I am unmistakably a patient with dementia with Lewy bodies.”





Her intuition had been right after all.

Her grandfather’s black eyes, and the irises within them, were delicate as glasswork and deep enough to draw one in.

Yes—

the light of intelligence still burned there, unchanged from long ago.

And that, precisely, was the source of the unease Kaede herself had not fully understood.





In the past two days, as she had looked more deeply into DLB, she had learned a great many things.

Even among patients with the same diagnosis, the degree to which memory and spatial perception declined could vary greatly depending on where in the brain the Lewy bodies appeared.





Some patients were always frightened by their hallucinations, while others grew accustomed to them with surprising ease.

Symptoms differed from person to person; the variations, it seemed, were endless.

When the balance of various medications—including dopaminergic drugs—worked just right, there were many cases in which the hallucinations vanished as though “the fog had lifted.”

And in fact, depending on the day, a patient might show no sign at all of diminished intellect.

What had surprised Kaede most of all was learning that there were many patients who clearly understood that what they were seeing was not reality but a product of their illness.

Some remarkably positive people even looked forward to the hallucinations that appeared every morning when they woke, and had made a hobby of sketching them afterward.

The scientific understanding of DLB was still incomplete, which made misunderstandings easy.

Even in the medical field, apparently, there were by no means few doctors who, seeing only the force of a patient’s hallucinations on the surface, rushed to conclude that “the dementia is progressing.”

The onset of DLB did not necessarily mean a waning of intellect.

When Kaede learned this, that strange feeling of unease seemed to clear away, just as if “the fog had lifted.”









“Apart from these Parkinsonian symptoms,” her grandfather said, glancing at his faintly trembling hand, “I had noticed quite some time ago that my mental state was very far indeed from what one would call normal. Let me see—for instance, if I happened to look at the wall behind that bookshelf, I would see the entire surface covered in intricate carvings, as though some master shrine carpenter had carved them into a portable shrine. But when I touched it, there was no sensation of carving at all. The wall was perfectly smooth. So then—which should I trust, sight or touch? It is simply impossible for someone to have elaborately carved the whole wall overnight without my noticing. And besides, no one in the world has any motive to sneak into an old man’s room merely to carve his wall. Which means—sadly—that the sense to be trusted is touch. Turn that around, and it means my sight is not to be trusted in the least.”

Kaede had no answer. She could only listen to his confession.

“So then, what was the cause of this strange condition? My computer was broken and unusable. I thought of searching on my smartphone, but as you can see, my hands are altogether unreliable. Or rather, ever since I dialed 119 over ‘Kaede’s corpse,’ Kanae confiscated my smartphone, so there was no searching to be done in any case.”

Her grandfather puckered his handsome lips with a trace of mischief.





“So I begged the helper to call me a care taxi, went to the library, and looked into it there. Following lines of print makes my eyes blur and brings on sleep almost at once, so it took the better part of a day... but I became sure of what disease I had. Ah yes—you know the phrase ‘the little grey cells,’ don’t you?”

With a self-mocking smile he quoted the favorite saying of the Belgian detective Hercule Poirot.

“In my case, then, deep orange Lewy bodies are spread across the surface of my brain, so I suppose I am the owner of ‘the scarlet brain cells.’”

“Then...” Kaede asked.

She could hear how hoarse her own voice was.

“Why did you keep telling me about the hallucinations? Why tell me those stories again and again?”

“Well...”

For the first time, her grandfather hesitated a little.

“When I tell those stories in particular, Kaede, your face changes over and over. You look startled, or you smile. Above all, you answer me aloud. And when that happens, I can feel certain of your reality.”

“What...? What do you mean? I’m always here beside you.”





“Perhaps this will make it clearer. Once before, I spoke to you at great length, frankly and without reserve, about what was likely to be the not-very-long remainder of my life—how shall I put it? I dislike the phrase ‘end-of-life planning’; familiar though it is, it strikes me as rather insensitive, so let us say instead that I spoke to you about how I intended to conduct the rest of my days. At the time I was vain enough to believe I was in nearly perfect form. I even thought that if I were going to tell you, it must be now. And so yes... I talked for a good hour, I suppose. But then, for some reason, the Kaede who listened in complete silence, her face expressionless the whole while...”

Her grandfather broke off once, then lowered his eyes.

“...suddenly vanished from in front of me. That Kaede was a hallucination.”

Perhaps it was the coffee, roasted a shade too dark.

For just an instant, something like bitterness crossed his face.

“Can anything be more miserable, more sad, than that? From that point on, I resolved never to speak of my illness unless Kaede herself brought it up. Even if I were taken for an old man with dementia, incapable of a proper conversation—that, I thought, could not be helped.”





(Grandpa.)

Once more she said it inwardly.

(Grandpa.)

Her grandfather, who had been unable—or had chosen not—to speak to his only granddaughter about how he wished to handle the end of his life.

Why had she not realized his anguish sooner?

He definitely saw hallucinations.

Frequently, at that.

He was afflicted as well by lapses of memory and, at times, disturbances of consciousness of various kinds.

Because of his Parkinsonian symptoms, his movements were excruciatingly slow.

And yet—

there had not been the slightest decline in his fundamental intelligence.









5





It must have been the hour when the children from the nearby mission kindergarten were being picked up.

From outside the house came the sound of children singing what seemed to be a nursery song.

Their off-key voices only made them sound more adorable.

Her grandfather’s cheeks loosened of their own accord.

Autumn days fall like buckets in a well—

and yet there still seemed to be some time before dark.

“Actually, there’s something I want to show you, Grandpa.”

Kaede took Setogawa’s essay collection from her black bag.

If her grandfather had been asleep in his chair as usual, she had meant to drape the freshly laundered blanket she had brought over him and sit beside him reading quietly before going home.

But—

as he was now, perhaps...

Putting on the rimless reading glasses he drew from the pocket of his gown, and holding the book a little farther from him even so, her grandfather said with deep feeling,

“Why, it’s Setogawa-senpai’s final book. You needn’t have bought it when I could have given you mine.”

(I could never take it. Not a book that’s been cherished so lovingly.)

Kaede smiled to herself.

“If I remember right, it’s been out of print for years. Well done finding it.”

“These days there are online used-book shops, and even pretty rare books can sometimes be bought surprisingly easily. And actually—this was tucked inside.”

Opening the book, Kaede laid the four obituary clippings out on the table once more.





“Setogawa, Active in Mystery and Film Criticism, Dies”

“Setogawa Dead: A Gifted Talent Mourned”

“The Age of Multilayered Criticism: What Setogawa Left Behind”

“Setogawa on the Happy Rendezvous of Mystery and Film”









“Yes. I read them all at the time.”

Her grandfather spoke in a lonely voice after no more than a glance at the headlines.

“I think two other papers ran articles as well. Naturally, I clipped every one.”

“I see.”

Once again Kaede was struck by his memory.

Because of the illness, memories of very recent events could vanish cleanly away; but when it came to the past, perhaps those drawers in his mind could still be opened at will.

“And, Grandpa—the real puzzle starts here. It struck me as one of those so-called ‘mysteries in everyday life.’”

“I see,” her grandfather said with a nod. “In other words, the mystery is this: who, and for what purpose, slipped these four obituary clippings into the book?”

“Exactly. To begin with, there are too many for bookmarks, don’t you think? But if they were just meant as placeholders, obituaries make the atmosphere much too heavy.”

“Very Harry Kemelman.”

As he removed his glasses, her grandfather named the old mystery writer.

Kemelman’s masterpiece The Nine Mile Walk is a supremely logical mystery in which, from a single line overheard in a pub—“A nine-mile walk is no joke, especially in the rain”—the speaker reconstructs in one swift sweep the full outline of a murder committed the previous day.

At that moment, abruptly, her grandfather asked,

“Kaede. Would you give me a cigarette?”









Perhaps because of the neat cadence of the words, the request sounded almost like an incantation.

Kaede brought him the blue packet from the drawer of the dressing table in the study.

Gauloises, the French cigarette.

Not especially expensive, perhaps, but not the sort of thing one could buy just anywhere either.

Whenever Kaede wandered through the used bookshops in Jinbocho, she usually bought it at a tiny sundries shop known only to those in the know.

“I’d be grateful if you’d light it for me. Yes, that’s fine. My hands shake, you see. When I’m alone, I try not to ‘drink’ them.”

Her grandfather did not say he “smoked” cigarettes. He said he “drank” them.

Perhaps it was a remnant from a time before the present antismoking age, when cigarettes had been recognized, like alcohol, as an ordinary pleasure of life.

Even in his youth he had never indulged in more than a few a week, and these days he “drank” them only rarely.

For that very reason, Kaede thought she could leave him at least this small pleasure.

Once he had taken the cigarette, a look of quiet rapture came over his face.

Kaede did not dislike the smell of Gauloises, but afraid it might cling to the T-shirts still hanging up, she opened the window a crack.

Her grandfather slowly let the bluish smoke curl upward and said, in a clearer voice than before, “Now then—”

It was almost as if the cigarette had switched on a booster to his intellect.

“What sort of story would you spin from these materials?”









Kaede’s heart began to pound.

Her grandfather had always called hypotheses “stories.”

He had really come back.

That grandfather.

“This is the story I came up with.”

Trying as best she could to sound calm, Kaede laid out the hypothesis she had thought through so carefully.

“Story One. The person who tucked the obituaries into the book was the previous owner. He—or she—wanted other Setogawa fans to share in the emptiness left by Setogawa’s death, and deliberately put the clippings inside.”

She glanced at her grandfather. He was nodding as though she had said exactly what he was hoping to hear.

Telling a story in front of him always made her tense.

And yet—

(and yet, I’m happy.)

“Um, Story Two.”

Coming back to herself, Kaede went on.

“The person who tucked the obituaries into the book worked at the used-book shop. He—or she—was a Setogawa fan. Then, after who knows how many years, an order came in for one of Setogawa’s out-of-print books. That made them happy, and as a kind of present for an unknown fellow enthusiast—that is, me—they put the clippings inside.”

Her throat was dry, perhaps from intellectual excitement.

“What do you think? Those are the two stories I came up with.”

Her grandfather answered,

“Hmm. Not bad. Each of them hangs together reasonably well, and neither is so strained as to be sheer special pleading. But both contain a major contradiction.”

“I see...”

Kaede bit her lip.

“Listen carefully. The contradiction in Story One is this: would someone who was enough of a Setogawa fan to keep obituary clippings really sell a beloved book? Especially this one, which was his final work. Any person of ordinary sensibility would keep the articles with the book as part of their own treasured collection.”

Kaede had no choice but to nod.

“You’re right. From a book lover’s point of view, it’s hard to believe.”

“Story Two is emotionally more satisfying than Story One. But the contradiction remains. If someone at the shop inserted the clippings out of simple goodwill... why not include even a brief note? If they took the trouble to tuck the articles inside so carefully, then surely they might also have written something like: ‘As a fellow enthusiast, I was very glad to receive your order. I hope you’ll forgive my liberty in including these articles on Setogawa’s death as a small token of remembrance.’ Why, then, did he—or she—spare themselves even that little effort? In short—”





Her grandfather said it flatly.

“Both Story One and Story Two are fundamentally broken. There must be some other Story X.”





“Then...”

Kaede asked in a rasping voice,

“are you saying you can spin that Story X?”

Without answering, her grandfather held the shortening Gauloises between thumb and forefinger as though reluctant to let it go, and drew one last breath of smoke from it.

His eyes slowly—

but unmistakably—

narrowed.

Kaede worried he might be about to fall asleep.

But that fear proved needless.

He opened his eyes clearly and said, “An image came to me just now.

“I’m afraid the former owner of the book—a man—is already dead.”

“What?”

“Why, look. There’s a man standing right there with a perfectly peaceful face.”

A hallucination.

And yet this hallucination was grounded in unmistakable logic—

Kaede felt that instinctively.

“Story X goes like this. During his lifetime, he tucked the obituary clippings for his beloved Takeshi Setogawa inside a book he treasured, out of mourning. But—after his death, his wife, having no idea they were such a treasure to him, sold the book along with a number of others to a used-book shop while sorting through his things.”

(The truth—)

She could not help feeling that it was.

Was this not a perfect “story,” one with no contradiction in it at all, one that settled cleanly and naturally into place?





“But,” Kaede pressed, “how do you know the former owner was a man? It could just as easily have been a woman.”

“No,” her grandfather said lightly. “When a spouse dies, it’s women who manage to keep their grief in check and act calmly. Men are hopeless. As a matter of fact, even I...”

He lowered his gaze.

“When my wife died before me, I couldn’t do a thing.”

For an instant the image of her late grandmother’s round, soft face passed through Kaede’s mind.

For a while, neither of them spoke.
