I



I had no belongings at all, not even a coat. They said it wasn’t necessary. They let me keep my black sweater: that would pass. The shirt I fought for. I said I’d break myself of it gradually. Right there in the passageway under the ship’s belly, where we stood being jostled, Abs held out his hand to me with a knowing smile.

“Just be careful…”

I remembered that too. I didn’t crush his fingers. I was perfectly calm. He wanted to say something more. I spared him by turning away as though I hadn’t noticed and going up the steps inside. The stewardess led me between the rows of seats all the way to the front.

I didn’t want a private cabin. I wondered whether they had warned her. The seat unfolded noiselessly. She adjusted the back, smiled at me, and left. I sat down. The cushions were immensely soft, as everywhere. The backs so high I could barely see the other passengers. I no longer resisted the gaudiness of women’s clothes, but the men I still suspected, senselessly, of being in costume, and I still secretly hoped I might see some dressed normally—a pitiful reflex. People settled quickly. No one had any luggage.

Not even briefcases or bundles. The women didn’t either. There seemed to be more of them. In front of me were two mixed-race women in parrot-bright fur jackets, all fluffed up like feathers; evidently that sort of bird fashion was in.

Farther on, some married couple with a child. After the garish selenophores of the platforms and tunnels, after the unbearably shrill, self-luminous vegetation of the streets, the light from the concave ceiling was almost a mere glow. I put my hands on my knees, because somehow they were in the way. Everyone was seated now. Eight rows of gray seats, a waft of fir, the hush of dying conversations. I waited for an announcement of departure, some signal, an order to fasten belts, but nothing came. Across the matte ceiling indistinct shadows began to run from front to back, like birds cut out of paper. What the devil is it with these birds, I thought helplessly. Maybe it meant something? I was stiff with watchfulness, afraid of doing something wrong.

It had been like that for four days now. From the very first moment. I was constantly lagging behind everything that happened, and the continual effort to understand the simplest conversation, the simplest situation, turned that tension into a feeling hideously close to despair. I was sure the others felt the same, but we never spoke of it, not even when we were alone. We only joked about our strength, about that surplus of power still left in us; though in fact you really had to watch yourself—at first, if I tried to get up, I would shoot toward the ceiling, and everything I picked up felt like paper, hollow. But I learned to control my own body quickly.

By now I no longer crushed anyone’s hand when I shook it. That was easy. Unfortunately, it was the least important thing.

My neighbor on the left, thickset, tanned, with excessively shiny eyes—contact lenses perhaps—suddenly vanished, because his seat swelled at the sides; they rose and joined over him, forming something like an egg-shaped cocoon. Several more people disappeared into such cabins. They looked like bloated sarcophagi. What did they do in there? But I kept running into things like that, and I tried not to stare unless they concerned me directly. Oddly enough, I was rather indifferent to the people who gaped at us once they learned what sort we were. Their stupefaction mattered little to me, though I saw at once that there was not a trace of admiration in it. It was rather the people who looked after us—the Adapt staff—who stirred my dislike. Most of all, probably, Dr. Abs, because he treated me as a doctor treats an abnormal patient, pretending—and doing it quite well, too—that he was dealing with someone perfectly ordinary. And when even that was no longer possible, he joked. I had had enough of his directness and joviality. Ask any passerby—so at least I thought—and he would have taken me or Olaf for someone like himself; it wasn’t so much that we ourselves seemed uncanny as our past fate: that was what was extraordinary. Dr. Abs, on the other hand, like every Adapt worker, knew better—that we really were different. And that difference was no distinction, only an obstacle to understanding, to the simplest exchange of words—hell, even to opening a door, since door handles had ceased to exist, I couldn’t remember whether fifty or sixty years ago.

The takeoff came unexpectedly. Gravity did not change by a hair, no sound penetrated the sealed interior, the shadows kept gliding across the ceiling—perhaps long habit, some old instinct, told me at a certain moment that we were in space, because it was certainty, not conjecture.

But something else occupied me. I lay there half reclining, legs stretched out, motionless. They had let me have my way too easily. Even Oswamm had not opposed my decision very strongly. The counterarguments I heard from him and from Abs could have convinced no one—I could have thought up better ones myself. They stood firm on only one point: each of us had to fly separately. They didn’t even hold it against me that I had stirred Olaf to rebellion—because if not for me, he would certainly have agreed to stay longer. That was puzzling. I had expected complications, something that at the last moment would thwart my plan, but nothing happened, and here I was, flying. This last journey was to be over in fifteen minutes.

Plainly enough, what I had devised, and the way I had stood before them to force an early departure, had not surprised them. They must have had that sort of reaction catalogued already; it was a behavioral stereotype proper to hotheads like me, complete with its serial number on their psychotechnical charts. They had let me fly—why? Because experience told them I wouldn’t manage? But how could that be, when the whole “independent” expedition consisted in flying from one station to another, where somebody from Earth’s Adapt was supposed to be waiting for me, and all I had to do was find the man at the agreed place?

Something had happened. Raised voices reached me. I leaned out of my seat. Several rows ahead some woman had shoved the stewardess, who, with a slowed, mechanical movement, as though under the force of that push—not really so strong, after all—was moving backward between the seats, and the woman repeated:

“I won’t allow it! I won’t let it touch me!”

I couldn’t see the face of the one shouting. Her companion was tugging at her arm, saying something soothing. What did the scene mean? The other passengers paid it no attention. Once again I was overcome by a feeling of unbelievable alienness. I looked up at the stewardess, who had stopped beside me and was smiling as before. It wasn’t the mere outward smile of obligatory politeness, hiding annoyance at the incident. She wasn’t pretending to be calm; she really was calm.

“Would you like something to drink? prum, extran, morr, cider?”

A melodious voice. I shook my head.

I wanted to say something pleasant to her, but managed only a stereotyped question.

“When do we land?”

“In six minutes. Would you like something to eat? You needn’t hurry. You can stay aboard after landing.”

“No, thank you.”

She went away. In the air, right before my face, against the back of the seat ahead, the word STRATO flared up as though written with the glowing end of a cigarette. I bent forward to see how it had been made, and started. The back of my seat followed my shoulders and clasped them flexibly. I already knew that the furniture anticipated every change of posture, yet I kept forgetting it. It wasn’t pleasant—as though someone were tracking my every movement.

I tried to go back to my former position, too abruptly apparently. The seat misunderstood me and almost unfolded into a bed. I sprang up. What idiocy! More self-control.

At last I sat down. The pink letters of STRATO wavered and flowed into others: TERMINAL. No jolt, no warning, no whistle. Nothing. A distant sound rang out, like a posthorn; four oval doors at the ends of the aisles opened, and into the cabin rushed a muffled, all-pervading roar, like the sea. The voices of the passengers getting to their feet were swallowed in it without a trace. I stayed seated while they filed out, rows of figures flickering against the outer lights in green, lilac, purple—a veritable masked ball. They were all gone now. I stood up. I tugged my sweater down automatically. It somehow felt foolish to have empty hands. A cooler draft came through the open doors. I turned around. The stewardess was standing by the partition, not touching it with her back. The same serene smile still rested on her face, directed at the empty rows of seats, which were now beginning slowly to curl inward, to fold up like fleshy flowers, some faster, some a little slower—that was the only movement in that long-drawn noise flowing through the oval openings and filling everything, a noise that brought the open sea to mind. “I won’t let it touch me!” Suddenly I found something unpleasant in her smile. From the doorway I said:

“Goodbye…”

“At your service.”

The meaning of those words, so strange in the mouth of a young pretty woman, did not strike me at once, because I heard them when I was already turned away, leaning out through the doorway. I wanted to set my foot on the step, but there was no step. Between the metal hull and the edge of the platform a gap a meter wide yawned. Losing my balance, unprepared for such a trap, I made an awkward leap, and already in midair I felt a current of invisible force catching me from below, so that I floated across the void and was set down softly on the white surface, which gave elastically underfoot. I must have had a rather foolish expression while flying across—I felt several amused glances, or at least it seemed so to me. I turned quickly and walked along the platform. The projectile in which I had arrived rested in a deep cradle, separated from the platform edge by an unguarded chasm. As if by accident I drifted close to that emptiness and again felt the invisible springiness that would not let me step beyond the white brink. I wanted to look for the source of that peculiar force, but suddenly it was as if I came to myself: I was on Earth.

The wave of people swept me up; pushed along, I moved forward with the crowd. It took me a while before I really grasped the vastness of the hall. Was it even one hall? There were no walls: a white, gleaming explosion of incredible wings arrested overhead, and between them columns built not of any material but of vertiginous motion. Giant fountains of some liquid denser than water, rushing upward and lit from within by colored floodlights? No: glass vertical tunnels up which processions of blurred vehicles shot. I no longer knew. Ceaselessly pushed and jostled in the swarming haste of the crowd, I tried to make my way into some empty place, but there were no empty places here. Being a head taller than those around me, I could see that the emptied projectile was moving away—or rather no, it was we who were gliding forward with the entire platform. Lights blazed down from above, in which the crowd sparkled and shimmered. Now the surface on which we stood, packed together, began to rise, and I saw below, already far away, double white strips crammed with people, with black gaps yawning along inert hulls—for there were dozens of ships like ours. The moving platform curved, accelerated, slid under higher levels. Flapping, whipping the hair of those standing there with the wind they raised, rounded shadows quivering with speed flew across them, along impossible viaducts unsupported by anything at all, their signal lights stretched into streaks; then the surface carrying us began to split, to fork along invisible seams, and the lane I was on passed through interiors full of standing and seated people, surrounded by countless little flashes, as though they were setting off colored fireworks.

I didn’t know where to look. In front of me stood a man in something fluffy as fur that iridesced like metal when the light touched it. He had a woman in scarlet on his arm. What she was wearing was covered in great eyes, peacock-like, and those eyes blinked. It was no illusion: the eyes of her dress really were opening and closing. The walkway I stood on behind the two of them, among a dozen other people, kept accelerating. Between planes of white-smoked glass there opened colored, lighted passageways with transparent ceilings, endlessly trodden by hundreds of feet on the next level up; the all-pervading roar spread and tightened again as the thousands of human voices and sounds, incomprehensible to me, meaningful to them, were stifled by yet another tunnel in this journey aimed God knew where; in the depths, farther off, the background was kept stitched by the constantly flying streaks of I knew not what vehicles—perhaps flying ones—because at times they slanted upward or downward, drilling into space, so that instinctively I expected some horrible collision, for I could see no guideways, no rails, if they were aerial trains. Whenever these blurred hurricanes of speed broke off even for a moment, there emerged from behind them, majestically slow, enormous planes crowded with people, like hovering piers, moving in different directions, passing one another, rising, seeming to interpenetrate through a trick of perspective. It was hard to rest one’s eyes on anything motionless, because the whole surrounding architecture seemed made almost entirely of movement, of change, and even what I had at first taken for a winged ceiling was only suspended tiers, which now yielded place to others, higher still. Suddenly, filtered through glazed roofs and those mysterious columns, reflected from silver surfaces, a heavy purple dawn entered all the folds of the space, the interiors of the passages we were crossing, the features of human faces—as though somewhere far away, in the heart of that structure many miles long, an atomic fire had flared.

The green of the constantly leaping neon lights turned dirty, the milk-white paraboloid supports pinked over. There was something catastrophic in that sudden russet suffusion of the air; that was how I took it, at least, but no one paid the slightest attention to the change, and I couldn’t even have said when it passed.

At the edges of our walkway whirling green circles appeared, like neon hoops suspended in the air; then some of the people stepped onto the approaching branch of another walkway or ramp. I noticed that one could pass harmlessly through the green lines of those lights, as though they had no substance.

For a while I let the white walkway carry me along helplessly like that, until it occurred to me that perhaps I was already outside the station, and this incredible landscape of contorted glass, forever seeming about to take flight, was in fact the city itself, while the one I had left behind existed now only in my memory.

“Excuse me…” I touched the fur-clad man’s arm. “Where are we?…”

Both of them looked at me. Their faces, when they raised them, took on an expression of surprise.

I had a faint hope it was only because of my height.

“On the poliduct,” said the man. “What styk do you have?”

I understood nothing.

“Are we… still in the station?”

“Of course…” he answered, with a certain reluctance.

“And… where is the Inner Ring?”

“You’ve missed it already. You’ll have to double back.”

“A rast from Merid would be better,” the woman put in. All the eyes of her dress seemed to be staring at me with suspicious astonishment.

“Rast?…” I repeated helplessly.

“There.” She pointed, through an approaching green circle, to an empty rise with black-and-silver striped sides, like the hull of some oddly painted ship lying on its side. I thanked her and stepped off the walkway, no doubt in the wrong place, because the momentum nearly took my legs from under me. I caught my balance, but I got so spun around that I didn’t know which way to go. While I stood wondering what to do, the place where I had got off had moved far away from the black-and-silver rise the woman had indicated, and I could no longer find it. Since most of the people near me were moving onto a ramp that led upward, I did the same. Once on it, I noticed a gigantic inscription burning motionless in the air: DUKT CENTR—the rest of the letters vanished on either side, too huge for the eye to take in. Soundlessly it bore me up to what must have been a platform a kilometer long, from which a spindle-shaped ship was just pulling away, showing its underside riddled with lights as it rose. Then again perhaps that whale-like shape was the platform itself, and I had found myself on the “rast”—there wasn’t even anyone to ask, for emptiness stretched all around. I must have taken a wrong turn. Part of my “platform” was built up with flattened structures without front walls. Going closer, I saw a kind of dimly lit low bays in which black machines lay in rows. I took them for cars. But when the two nearest slid out and, before I could jump back, shot past me, instantly picking up great speed, I saw, before they vanished in the perspective of parabolic slants, that they had no wheels, windows, or doors, streamlined like gigantic black drops. Cars or not, I thought, in any case this must be some sort of parking place? Perhaps for those very “rasts”? I decided the best thing would be to wait until someone came and go along with him, or at least find something out. But my platform, lightly raised like the wing of an impossible airplane, remained empty; only the black machines slipped singly or in groups from their metal burrows and sped away, always in the same direction. I walked right to the platform edge, until that invisible springlike force that ensured safety made itself felt again. It really hung in the air, supported by nothing. Lifting my head, I saw many others like it, likewise hovering motionless in space with their great lights extinguished; they burned on others, where ships were docking. Yet they were not rockets, not even projectiles like the one that had brought me from Luna.

I stood there for a long time, until I noticed, against the background of more halls of some kind—I didn’t know, in fact, whether they were reflections of this one or reality—fiery letters moving steadily through the air: SOAMO SOAMO SOAMO, then a pause, a blue flash, and NEONAX NEONAX NEONAX, perhaps station names, perhaps brand names. They meant nothing to me.

High time I found that fellow, I thought. I turned on my heel and, finding a walkway moving in the opposite direction, rode it down. It turned out not to be the same level, not even the same hall I had come up from: I knew that from the absence of those huge columns. Then again, perhaps the columns themselves had moved somewhere else; by now I thought anything possible.

I found myself among a whole forest of fountains; farther on I came upon a pink-and-white hall full of women.

As I passed, I idly stuck my hand into the stream of one illuminated fountain, perhaps because it was pleasant to encounter something even a little familiar. Yet I felt nothing—that fountain had no water. A moment later I thought I smelled flowers. I raised my hand to my nostrils. It smelled like a thousand cakes of toilet soap at once. Reflexively I began wiping it on my trousers. I was already standing before that hall full of women, women only. It didn’t look to me like the anteroom to lavatories, but in the end I couldn’t be sure. I preferred not to ask, so I turned back. A young man, dressed as though flowing mercury had frozen on him, puffed out—or rather foamy—at the shoulders, tight at the hips, was talking to a fair-haired girl leaning with her back against the basin of a fountain. The girl, in a light dress, perfectly ordinary, which heartened me, held a bouquet of pale pink flowers and, burying her face in them, smiled at the boy with her eyes. At the last moment, just as I stopped beside them and was already opening my mouth, I saw that she was eating the flowers—and for a split second my voice refused to obey me. She calmly chewed the delicate petals. She raised her eyes to me. They went still. But by then I was used to that. I asked where the Inner Ring was.

The boy seemed unpleasantly surprised, almost annoyed, that someone dared break into their being alone together. Evidently I had committed some impropriety. He looked upward first, then dropped his gaze, as though expecting to find some stilts explaining my height. He didn’t even answer.

“There!” cried the girl. “Rast na wuka—your rast. You’ll make it, quick!”

I set off at a run in the direction she had indicated, without knowing where, since I still had no idea what that damned rast looked like. After ten steps I saw a silvery funnel descending from above, the base of one of those huge columns that had astonished me so much before—could they be flying columns?—people were hurrying there from different directions, and suddenly I collided with someone. I didn’t even stagger; I just stopped dead, but the other man, a stocky gentleman in orange clothing, fell, and something incredible happened to him: his fur wilted before my eyes, caving in like a punctured balloon! I stood over him, stunned, incapable of stammering an apology. He got up, looked at me from under his brows, but said nothing, turned away and strode off, fiddling at his chest—and his outfit filled out and lit up again…

There was no one left where the girl had sent me. After that adventure I gave up once and for all the search for rasts, the Inner Ring, dukts, styks, and decided to get out of the station. My experience so far did not encourage me to accost passersby, so I followed at random an oblique blue arrow upward, passing without much reaction through two more inscriptions blazing in the air: LOCAL CIRCUITS. I came to an escalator, fairly crowded. The next level was toned in a muted brown, veined with golden exclamation points. Flowing descents of ceilings and hollowed walls. Corridors with no visible ceiling, their upper reaches seeming to dissolve into luminous down. As though I were drawing near some residential region, the surroundings had something of a system of gigantic hotel lobbies—windows, nickel pipes along the walls, alcoves with people at work in them, perhaps exchange counters, perhaps a post office. I kept going. I was almost certain now that I would not reach an exit this way and—judging by the length of the upward ride—that I was in the elevated part of the station; still I kept the same direction. Sudden emptiness, panels of raspberry-colored wall covering sprinkled with glittering stars, rows of doors. The nearest were not quite shut. I peered inside. At that same moment some large broad-shouldered man did the same from the opposite side. Myself, in a wall-sized mirror. I opened the door wider. Porcelain, silver pipes, nickel. Toilets.

I almost felt like laughing, but all in all I was more dazed than amused. I turned back quickly; another corridor, milk-white strips moving downward. The escalator’s handrail was soft, warm. I didn’t count the levels slipping by. More and more people. They stopped at enameled boxes sprouting from the wall every few steps; a single touch of a finger, something dropped into their hand, they pocketed it and went on. I don’t know why I did exactly what the man in loose violet clothing ahead of me did: there was a button with a little hollow for the tip of a finger, I pressed it, and straight into my waiting palm there fell a colored, half-transparent tube, warm as if heated.

I shook it, held it close to my eyes. Some sort of pills? No. A stopper? No, it had no stopper, no closure of any kind. What was it for? What did the others do? They put it into their pockets. The machine was labeled LAHGAN. I stood there; people bumped into me. And suddenly I felt like an ape handed a fountain pen or a cigarette lighter; for a fraction of a second I was seized by blind fury. I clenched my teeth, narrowed my eyes, and, slightly hunched, merged with the stream of people. The corridor widened; it was a hall now. Fiery letters: REAL AMMO REAL AMMO.

Through the current of hurrying people, above their heads, I saw a window in the distance. The first window.

Panoramic, enormous.

As though all the firmaments of night had been spread flat. To the horizon, out of a luminous mist—colored galaxies of squares, clusters of spiral lights, glows drizzling above skyscrapers, streets: the creeping, wormlike motion of luminous beads, and above that, in the verticals, the seething of neon, plumes and lightning bolts, wheels, airplanes and bottles of fire, red dandelions of signal lights on spires, instantaneous suns and hemorrhages of advertisements, violent with mechanical abruptness. I stood and looked, hearing behind me the steady murmur of hundreds of feet. Suddenly the city vanished and an enormous face appeared, ten feet high.

“We have just presented a set of newsreels from the seventies in the cycle ‘Visions of Old Capitals.’

“At present transtel is shifting its range to the studios of the cosmolites…”

I almost ran. It wasn’t a window. Some sort of television screen. I quickened my step. I had begun to sweat.

Down. Faster. Golden squares of light. Inside, crowds, foam on glasses, an almost black liquid—not beer, because it had a poisonous greenish gleam—and young people, boys and girls with their arms around one another, in groups of six and eight, blocking the full width of the passages, coming toward me, having to break apart to let me through. A spasm went through me. Before I knew it, I had stepped onto a moving walkway. Astonished eyes flashed very close by—a lovely dark girl in something that shone on her like phosphorescent metal. The fabric clung to her: she looked naked. White faces, yellow faces, a few tall Black men, but I was still the tallest. People made way for me. Overhead, behind convex panes, windswept shadows streamed by, invisible orchestras were playing, and here this peculiar promenade went on, in the dark passageways—headless female figures; the fluff covering their shoulders glowed so that only the necks thrust out from it shone like white, strange stalks, and a scattered glimmer in their hair—self-luminous powder? A narrow passage led me into an enfilade of grotesque statues—grotesque because they were moving, bustling even; something like a broad street with raised sides rang with laughter. People were amusing themselves, but what amused them so—those sculptures?

Huge figures stood in cones of spotlights; ruby light poured over them, honey-colored, thick as syrup, with an extraordinary concentration of color. I walked on helplessly, squinting, losing myself.

A steep green passage, grotesque pavilions, pagodas reached by little bridges, lots of tiny places, the sharp insistent smell of frying, rows of gas flames behind glass, the clatter of dishes, repeated metallic sounds I couldn’t understand. The crowd that had carried me here collided with another, then it grew looser; everyone was boarding a car open straight through—no, it was only transparent, as if cast of glass, even the seats seemed glass, though soft. Before I knew it I was inside—we were moving. The car raced on, people shouted over a loudspeaker repeating, “Meridional level, Meridional level, styks for Spiro, Blekkr Frosom,” the whole car seemed to be melting, pierced by shafts of light, walls flew past in streaks of flame and color, parabolic arches, white platforms, “Forteran, Forteran, styks Galee, styks of outer rasts, Makra,” muttered the loudspeaker, the car stopped and raced on again. I discovered something astonishing: you felt neither braking nor acceleration, as though inertia had been abolished. How was that possible? I tested it, bending my knees slightly, at three successive stops. Nothing on the curves either. People got out, got in, and on the front platform stood a woman with a dog. I had never seen such a dog before; it was huge, with a head like a ball, very ugly, and in its calm hazel eyes there were reflected the diminished garlands of lights racing backward. RAMBRENT RAMBRENT. White and bluish fluorescent tubes flickered, stairs of crystalline brilliance, black facades; the brilliance slowly congealed, the car stood still. I got out and stopped dead. Above the amphitheatrically sunken disk of the stop rose a familiar multilevel structure; I was still in the station, in another part of the same gigantic hall swollen with white sweeps of planes. I headed toward the edge of that geometrically exact hollow—the car had already gone—and experienced another astonishment: I was not down below, as I had thought, but high up, forty stories above the ribbons of walkways visible in the abyss, above the silver decks of platforms still gliding steadily, and among them long silent bodies were slipping in, and people were emerging from them through rows of hatches, as though these monsters, these chrome fish, were laying down at regular intervals deposits of black and colored roe. Above all that, far away, through the haze of distance, I saw golden words moving as though on an invisible wire:

GLENIANA ROON, RETURNING TODAY WITH A RECORDING OF MIMORPHIC REAL, PAYS TRIBUTE IN AN ORATORIO TO THE MEMORY OF RAPPER KERX POLITRY. TERMINAL NEWS REPORTS: TODAY IN AMMONLEE PETIFARGUE HAS ACHIEVED THE SYSTOLIZATION OF THE FIRST ENZOME; WE SHALL BROADCAST THE VOICE OF THE DISTINGUISHED GRAVITICIST AT THE TWENTY-SEVENTH HOUR. ARRAKER IN THE LEAD. ARRAKER HAS REPEATED HIS SUCCESS AS THE FIRST OBLITERATOR OF THE SEASON IN THE TRANSWAAL STADIUM.

I moved away. So even the reckoning of time had changed. Struck by the light of those gigantic letters, which sped over the sea of heads like rows of burning tightrope walkers, the metallic fabrics of women’s dresses flared with sudden little flames. I walked on without knowing it, and something kept repeating inside me: so even time has changed. That, somehow, finished me off. I saw nothing with my open eyes. I wanted only one thing: to get out, to get out of that hellish station, to find myself under the naked sky, in open space, to see the stars, to feel the wind.

I was drawn by an avenue of elongated lights; in the translucent stone of the ceiling something was written—the letters traced by a sharp flame enclosed in alabaster—TELETRANS TELEPORT TELETHON. Through pointed-arch doors—but it was some impossible arch, wrenched out of all bearings, like the negative of a rocket’s beak—I came into a hall roofed by a frozen golden conflagration. In recesses along the walls were hundreds of booths. People ran into them, darted out in haste, threw torn strips on the floor—not telegraph tape, something else, embossed with little nodules—others trampled those shreds. I wanted to leave; by mistake I stepped into a dark cubicle. Before I could back out, something buzzed, a flash went off like a camera bulb, and from a metal-rimmed slot, like a letter box, there slid out a folded sheet of shiny paper. I took it, opened it, and a human head rose out of it, with slightly crooked thin lips not quite closed, looking at me through narrowed eyes: it was my own. I folded the paper in two and the phantom vanished. I slowly parted the edges—nothing. Wider, and it appeared again, as if jumping out of nowhere: a head cut off from the body, suspended above the sheet of paper, with a not very intelligent expression. For a moment I stared at my own face—what was it, a three-dimensional photograph?

I put the sheet in my pocket and went out. The golden hell seemed to be settling over the heads of the crowd, a ceiling of fiery magma, unreal and yet breathing real flame, and no one looked at it; busy, they ran from one booth to another, green letters leapt in the depths, columns of digits slid down narrow screens, other booths with shutters instead of doors snapping up at someone’s approach; at last I found the exit.

A corridor, curved, with a sloping floor, like a theater at times; stylized shells blossomed from the walls, and overhead the words INFOR INFOR INFOR raced endlessly.

The first time I saw an infor on Luna, I took it for an artificial flower.

I brought my face close to the celadon calyx, which immediately, before I had even parted my lips, stilled in expectation.

“How do I get out?” I asked, not very coherently.

“To where?” a warm contralto answered at once.

“To the city.”

“To which district?”

“It doesn’t matter.”

“To what level?”

“Any level. I want to get out of the station!”

“Meridional, rasts: one hundred six, one seventeen zero eight, zero two. Triduct, levels AF, AG, circular myth level, twelve and sixteen, nadir level leads in every meridional direction. Central level—gliders, red local, white long-distance, A, B and W. Ulder level, direct, all eskales from the third up…” the woman’s voice recited singingly. I felt like tearing the microphone from the wall, the one that bent toward my face with such solicitude.

I went away. Idiot! Idiot!—ground away inside me with every step. EX EX EX EX repeated the inscription gliding overhead, edged with lemon mist. Could it mean exit? A way out?

The sign was enormous. EXOTAL. I entered a violent current of warm air that made my trouser legs flap. I found myself under the open sky. But the blackness of night had fled far out into space, driven back by a swarm of lights. An enormous restaurant—tables whose surfaces shone in different colors, above them faces lit from below and therefore a little eerie, full of deep shadows. Low chairs, black liquid with green foam in glasses, lanterns shedding tiny sparks—no, rather fireflies, like swarms of burning moths. The chaos of lights extinguished the stars. When I lifted my head, I saw only black emptiness. And yet, astonishingly enough, at that moment its blind presence somehow filled me with reassurance.

I stood and looked. Someone brushed against me in passing, I caught the scent of perfume, sharp and mild at once; a couple went by, the girl turned to the man, her shoulders and breasts sunk in a fluffy cloud, she slipped into his arms—they were dancing. They still dance, I thought. That’s something, at least. The couple took a few steps, a pale mercurial circle lifted her with other couples; their dark-red shadows moved beneath its huge disk, turning slowly; it rested on nothing, had not even an axle, it revolved in the music, suspended in the air. I walked among the tables. The soft plastic under my feet came to an end, giving way to rough rock. Through a curtain of light I went inside and found myself in a rock grotto. As though ten or fifty Gothic naves had been raised out of stalactites, veined deposits of pearly minerals embraced the mouths of caves; people sat in them with their legs dangling into emptiness, wavering flames burned between their knees, and below stretched the unruffled black surface of an underground lake, reflecting the converging rocks. There too, on crudely thrown-together rafts, people lay, all facing the same direction. I went down to the very edge of the water and saw on the far side, on the sand, a dancer. She seemed naked to me, but the whiteness of her body was unnatural. With small wavering steps she came down toward the water; when she was reflected in it, she suddenly spread her arms, bowed her head—that was the end, but no one applauded, the dancer remained motionless for a few seconds, then walked slowly along the bank, circling the uneven line of the shore. She was perhaps thirty paces from me when something happened to her. A moment before I had still seen her smiling, exhausted face; suddenly it was as though something covered her, her figure trembled and vanished.

“A plava for you, sir?” a polite voice said behind me.

I turned around. No one—only a streamlined table walking on absurdly bent little legs; it moved, and the goblets of sparkling liquid standing in rows on its side trays trembled. One arm politely proffered that drink to me, the other was already reaching for a plate with a hole for a finger, like a small concave palette—it was an automaton; behind a little central pane I could see the smoldering glow of its transistor heart.

I passed those insect arms, thrust out with such slavish eagerness and laden with delicacies I had scorned, and quickly left the artificial grotto, jaw clenched, as though I had suffered some incomprehensible insult. I crossed the full width of the terrace, between the S-curves of tables, under avenues of lanterns, sprinkled with the weightless dust of dying, disintegrating fireflies, black and gold. At the very edge, bordered with old stone, as though dimmed by yellowish lichen, I was struck at last by real wind, clean and cool. An empty table stood nearby. I sat down awkwardly, with my back to the people, looking into the night. Below spread an unexpected, shapeless darkness; only far, very far at its edges thin wavering lights glimmered, strangely uncertain, as if it were not electricity at all, and farther still rapiers of light rose into the sky, cold, thin—I couldn’t tell whether they were buildings or some kind of pillars; I would have taken them for searchlight beams if not for the delicate mesh that marked them—something like a glass cylinder driven base-first into the earth, reaching the clouds, filled alternately with concave and convex lenses. They must have been of unbelievable height. Around them a few lights drizzled, pulsing so that now an orange, now an almost white halo encircled them. That was all. That was what the city looked like. I tried to make out streets, to infer them, but that dark, almost dead expanse below stretched on all sides, brightened by not a single spark.

“Kol?…” I heard, perhaps not for the first time, but at first I didn’t take it as addressed to me. Before I could properly turn, the chair did it for me. A girl stood before me, perhaps twenty, in a blue that clung to her as though congealed; shoulders and breasts lost in dark-blue fluff that became more and more transparent below. Her slim beautiful belly was like sculpture in breathing metal. In her ears she wore something luminous, so large it covered the shells of them; small lips smiling uncertainly—painted, and her nostrils red inside too—I had noticed that most women wore them that way. Taking the back of the chair opposite me in both hands, she said:

“What’s up, kol!”

She sat down. I had the impression she was a little drunk.

“Boring here,” she said after a moment. “Isn’t it? Want to get together somewhere, kol?”

“I’m not kol…” I began. She rested her elbows on the table and moved her hand over the half-filled glass until the end of the gold chain wound around her fingers dipped into the liquid. She was leaning closer and closer all the while. I could feel her breath. If she was drunk, it wasn’t on alcohol.

“How not?” she said. “You are. You have to be. Everybody’s kol. Want to? Shall we get together?”

So that I might at least find out what that meant, I said:

“All right.”

She stood up. I got up too from that horribly low chair.

“How do you do that?” she asked.

“What?”

She looked at my legs.

“I thought you were standing on your toes…”

I smiled without speaking. She came up to me, took my arm, and was surprised again.

“What have you got there?”

“Where, here? Nothing.”

“You sing,” she said, and tugged me lightly along. We walked off between the tables, and I wondered what “you sing” might mean—perhaps something like “you’re putting me on”?

She led me toward a dark-gold wall where a sign glowed that looked a little like a treble clef. When we were close, the wall opened. I felt a gust of hot air.

A narrow silver escalator flowed downward. We stood side by side. She didn’t come up to my shoulder.

She had a catlike skull, black hair with a blue sheen, a profile perhaps too sharp, but she was pretty. Only those scarlet nostrils… She held me firmly with her thin hand, green nails digging into the thick cloth of my sweater. In spite of myself I smiled, with just the corners of my mouth, at the thought of where that sweater had been until now and how little it had ever had to do with women’s fingers.

Under a round vault that breathed with lights—from pink to carmine, from carmine back to pink—we emerged into the street. That is, I thought it was a street, but the darkness above us kept brightening every moment as though from instantaneous dawns. Farther on long low shapes flowed by, like cars, but I already knew there were no cars. It had to be something else. If I had been alone, I would have gone down that broad artery, because in the distance letters glowed: TO THE CENTER, but that probably did not mean the center of the city at all. In any case I let myself be led. However this adventure might end, I had found a guide, and I thought—this time without anger—of that poor fellow who now, three hours after my arrival, was certainly already looking for me through every infor in this station-city.

We passed several establishments emptying out, shop windows in which groups of mannequins kept acting out the same scene over and over, and I would gladly have stopped to see what they were doing, but the girl walked quickly, clicking her little shoes, until at the sight of a neon face with pulsing blushes, which kept licking itself with a ludicrously protruding tongue, she cried:

“Oh, bons! Want a bons?”

“Do you?”

“I think I do.”

We went into a small gleaming room. Instead of a ceiling it had long rows of burning little flames, as though gas flames; heat poured down from above—perhaps it really was gas. In the walls were small recesses with counters; when we approached one, seats slid out from both sides of the wall. It looked as though they had first grown from it undeveloped, like buds, then flattened themselves in the air and, hollowing slightly, came to rest.

We sat facing each other. The girl tapped a metal plate on the table with two fingers. A nickel paw shot out of the wall, dropped a small disk before each of us, and with two lightning-fast movements flung onto both a portion of whitish mass, which foamed, browned, and hardened, while at the same time the disk itself darkened. The girl then rolled it up—it wasn’t a disk at all—like a pancake and began to eat.

“Oh,” she said with her mouth full, “I didn’t even know how hungry I was!”

I did exactly as she did. A bons tasted like nothing I had ever eaten. It crackled under the teeth like a fresh-baked roll, but at once crumbled and melted on the tongue; the brownish filling inside was sharply seasoned.

I thought I would like bons.

“Another?” I asked when she had finished hers.

She smiled and shook her head.

As we were leaving, in the passage she put both hands for a moment into a small tiled niche—something inside it whispered. I imitated her. A tickling wind enveloped my fingers; when I took them out they were already dry and clean. After that we rode a broad escalator upward. I didn’t know whether this was still the station, but I preferred not to ask. She led me into a small cabin in the wall—it wasn’t very bright in there, and I had the impression that trains were running overhead, because the floor trembled. For a fraction of a second it went dark, something breathed deeply beneath us, as though a metal monster had let the air out of its lungs, it brightened again, and the girl pushed a door. This really did seem to be a street. There was no one else on it.

Small trimmed shrubs grew on both sides of the sidewalk; a little farther off stood closely packed flat black machines. A man stepped out of the shadows and slipped behind one of them; I didn’t see him open any door, he simply vanished suddenly, and the machine shot off with such violence that it must have flattened him against the seat. I saw no houses at all; only a roadway smooth as a tabletop, covered with bands of dull metal; at the intersections slit-like lights, orange and red, moved suspended above the paving. They looked a little like models of wartime searchlights.

“Where shall we go?” the girl asked. She was still holding my arm. She slowed her pace. A red streak passed over her face.

“Wherever you like.”

“Then let’s go to my place. No point taking a glider. It’s close.”

We walked on. Still no houses were visible ahead, and the wind coming out of the darkness from beyond the shrubs felt as though open space lay there. Around the station, in the very center? That struck me as strange. It carried a faint smell of flowers, which I drew greedily into my nostrils. Bird cherry? No, it wasn’t bird cherry.
