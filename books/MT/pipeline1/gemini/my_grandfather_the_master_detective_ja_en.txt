"A blue tiger came in this morning," Kaede’s grandfather said. 

"I wonder how it turned the knob. Clever thing, isn't it?"

He seemed more surprised by the fact that the tiger had opened the front door to get in than by the fact that it had entered his study—or that its body was covered in blue fur, for that matter. 

"Well, it's a good thing you weren't bitten," Kaede quipped. 

Inwardly, she felt a little disappointed: *He's actually awake for once, and he's talking about this again?* 

She visited him about once a week, but he was almost always asleep. 

And on the rare occasions he was awake, it was all talk of his visual hallucinations. 

He would carry on about them until Kaede had to leave, making any normal conversation impossible. 

Even so, Kaede earnestly lent an ear to the "tale of the blue tiger," nodding along repeatedly. 

Because the time she spent at her grandfather's house—which was also her own childhood home—felt irreplaceable to her. 

"And then the tiger..." he said, mimicking the way it walked with its front paws crossed. 

"When it left, it gave me a truly blissful smile." 

"The tiger smiled?"

*Ah... there I go again,* she smiled wryly to herself. 

Even though it was a hallucination, something completely impossible in reality, here she was hanging on his every word again. 

That was just it—even though she started out merely "pretending" to listen intently, her grandfather was such a masterful storyteller that she invariably found herself drawn into his world before she knew it. 

And today, she even had the illusion that a real blue tiger might leap right out of an illustration in one of the books on the shelves. 

He must have been satisfied to have told his tale. 

His eyelids slowly fluttered shut. 

Day in and day out, her grandfather sat in the electric reclining chair in this room. 

They had chosen a larger size to accommodate his tall, slender frame, but it turned out to be a major miscalculation: he found it so incredibly comfortable that he almost never left the chair. 

Leaning against the side table next to him was the wooden cane essential for his mobility. 

However, the care manager who had recommended the cane had complained to her with a sigh, "He uses it when he goes to the bathroom, but when he wants to pick a book from the shelf, he can't be bothered and won't use it at all. I worry he might fall."

*(He still loves his books. But I bet the contents...)*

A melancholic thought crossed her mind—*probably aren't registering anymore.*

The study, filled to the brim with books, carried the slightly musty scent of old ink. 

It reminded Kaede of Jimbocho, her favorite secondhand bookstore district. 

Before she knew it, the sunlight filtering through the trees outside the window had cast a camouflage pattern over her sleeping grandfather's face. 

The high bridge of his nose and the wrinkles etched at the corners of his eyes formed complex shadows across a face that, despite his seventy-one years, was somehow completely free of age spots. 

His jaw and cheeks had lost flesh compared to the past, but this only accentuated his sculpted features. 

His thick, long hair, parted down the middle of his broad forehead, was about seventy percent white, forming a gradient with the remaining black, which gave him the three-dimensional air of an emperor stamped on an ancient Roman coin. 

Even without her bias as his granddaughter, it was a magnificent, dignified face. 

*(I bet he was popular with the ladies,)* Kaede thought. 

She gently pulled the slipped blanket back up to her grandfather's thin neck. 

After finishing the cleaning, she sprayed a soap-scented antibacterial mist, being careful not to hit the books on the shelves. By then, it was already time for the physical therapist to arrive for his rehab session. 

This antibacterial spray wasn't just to keep the room clean. 

Her grandfather frequently hallucinated tiny insects, like mosquitoes. 

When that happened, the spray doubled as an impromptu "bug repellent."

*(Bye, Grandpa.)*

*(—I'll be back.)*

Beside the study door stood a vanity that had, in effect, been passed down from her late grandmother. 

You could call it not aging, but rather evolving with time. 

The accumulated years had been painted into the wood grain of the vanity like makeup in complex hues, granting it an exceptionally deep, rich character. 

Kaede took a hairbrush from the vanity drawer, quickly fixed her hair, looked in the mirror, and set her expression. 

*(Smile.)*

The study door, which had once been made of heavy solid oak, had been remodeled into a sliding door in preparation for the day her grandfather would inevitably have to rely on a wheelchair. 

Sliding the door shut silently, Kaede left her grandfather's house in Himonya. 

2

On the way back, swaying on the Toyoko Line, she turned her eyes to the train window and saw an utterly expressionless face reflected there. 

The smile she had so painstakingly put on was gone; not even a trace remained. 

The sky had already deepened into twilight, as if brushed with pale lipstick. 

With the onset of autumn, the cumulonimbus clouds had vanished, leaving various shapes of clouds scattered across the sky. 

Memories of her grandfather suddenly drifted through Kaede's mind. 

Twenty-three years ago. Kaede was four. 

Sitting in her grandfather's lap while he sat cross-legged on the veranda, she stared up at the madder-red sky. 

Her grandfather lowered his gaze, his clear eyes radiating intellect, down to his lap. 

"Kaede. What do the clouds over there look like to you? Try making up a single story using all of them."

Looking back, it was just like a *sandai-banashi*—a rakugo storytelling exercise using three random prompts. 

Was he trying to help the wings of Kaede's imagination take flight?

It was likely his own version of early emotional education. 

Kaede answered without skipping a beat. 

"That cloud is a tiny grandpa. And that cloud over there is a flat grandpa. And, umm... the biggest cloud is a grandpa who's fatter than you, Grandpa."

"You can't make a story out of that," he said, though his face broke into a broad smile. 

Then, to her surprise, he acted as if he had no choice and improvised a fairy tale titled "The Three Grandpas" in her stead. 

She didn't remember the exact details of the story. 

However, she did remember the ending: the gluttonous "fat grandpa" mistook all the cold medicine in the world for sugar and swallowed it all, and though he was relentlessly made fun of for it, he ended up living longer than anyone else in the world. 

It was probably meant as a moral lesson for Kaede, who hated taking bitter powdered medicine. 

But his storytelling was so incredibly captivating that Kaede clapped her hands in delight. 

"Look, Kaede. See?"

Looking up at the sky, only the "biggest cloud"—the "fat grandpa"—remained. The "tiny grandpa" and the "flat grandpa" had quite literally vanished like mist. 

Exactly as the story had ended. 

Dumbfounded, Kaede looked back and forth, over and over again, between her grandfather and the "fat grandpa" in the sky. 

In hindsight, her grandfather must have been secretly keeping an eye on the clouds as he spun the tale. 

If the "tiny grandpa" or the "flat grandpa" had lingered until the end, the plot would undoubtedly have taken a very different turn. 

"Hey, Grandpa. Tell Kaede another story. Or else—"

Little Kaede looked up and yanked on a hair growing out of a mole on her grandfather's Adam's apple. 

It pulled out surprisingly easily, which struck her as so absurdly funny that she burst out laughing. 

*Maybe that was the moment,* Kaede thought. 

*(Maybe I pulled the plug on Grandpa's intellect.)*

It was only six months ago that her grandfather's condition had visibly deteriorated. 

When she went for a walk with him, his stride had become noticeably shorter. 

"Grandpa, have you put on weight? Your legs can't keep up with you."

Her grandfather tilted his head and offered a self-deprecating smile, saying it was just old age. 

At first, Kaede too thought it was excess weight, or simply a symptom of aging—or rather, she tried to convince herself of that. 

But from there, the progression was rapid. 

When he drank his beloved coffee, the hand holding the cup trembled violently. 

When she visited the house, he was always dozing off in his study chair, his head bobbing. 

His posture became permanently hunched, and his movements for everything grew sluggish. 

But more than any of that. 

More than anything, Kaede would never be able to forget the shock of that one day for the rest of her life. 

Late at night, her smartphone rang. 

Rubbing the sleep from her eyes, she answered the phone to hear what sounded like a young man's voice. For some reason, he sounded hesitant as he introduced himself: "Um, I'm with the ambulance service."

Then, faltering repeatedly in an apologetic tone, he continued. 

"Am I speaking with Kaede-san? Ah, I thought so. Well, you see, your name was on the emergency contact list posted on the wall, which is why I'm calling. Actually, your grandfather... he called 119. And, well—how should I put this..."

"What happened?"

"He's saying, 'A blood-covered Kaede has collapsed here.'"

At their local clinic, they were told it might be Parkinson's disease, but since they couldn't be certain, they were advised to go to a larger hospital. 

They went to a university hospital and underwent detailed tests, including a CT scan. 

The result—the young doctor stated it matter-of-factly, ignoring her grandfather who was sleeping soundly in the chair. 

"It's Lewy body dementia."

That a grandfather so incredibly brilliant could develop dementia so soon after celebrating his seventieth birthday—

Kaede simply couldn't accept the fact right away. 

But when she did her own research online and by ordering information materials, every single one of her grandfather's symptoms perfectly matched the disease. 

She learned for the first time that there were over six million dementia patients in Japan alone, and that while it was broadly called "dementia," there were actually various types. 

Dementia is generally divided into three main categories. 

The most common, accounting for about seventy percent of patients, is "Alzheimer-type dementia," which is said to be caused by the buildup of a protein called amyloid-beta in the brain. 

When most people hear the word dementia, this is the type they immediately picture. 

The next most common is "vascular dementia," caused by the aftereffects of strokes or cerebral infarctions, accounting for roughly twenty percent of cases. 

Both types often present with memory impairment, such as repeating the same story over and over, disorientation where the sense of time and place becomes ambiguous, or wandering outside. 

And then there was what her grandfather was diagnosed with: "Lewy body dementia"—also known as DLB, an acronym for Dementia with Lewy Bodies—which accounts for about ten percent of the total. 

The disease was named in 1995, making it a relatively newly discovered illness in the long history of human ailments. 

In recent years, it has garnered attention as the "third dementia," and efforts to unravel its pathology are progressing at a rapid pace, not only in clinical settings but also in the field of clinical trials. 

In the brains and brainstems of DLB patients, tiny, deep-red structures resembling fried eggs—Lewy bodies—are invariably found. 

And it is these "tiny fried eggs" that cause Parkinsonian symptoms like trembling limbs and gait disturbances, as well as REM sleep behavior disorder, characterized by loud sleep-talking, excessive daytime sleepiness, or spatial cognitive impairment where the patient loses their sense of distance. 

But—

The most prominent and unparalleled symptom of DLB is, without a doubt, "visual hallucinations."

Although how they appear varies from patient to patient—some see in black and white, others in color—the common denominator is the fact that they see hallucinations "vividly," "distinctly," and "clearly."

For instance, the moment they wake up and open their eyes in the morning, ten people might be standing silently in the room, expressionless, staring intently at them. 

Or a giant snake might be sluggishly coiled on the dining table. 

A young girl with braided hair might follow them around all day, wherever they go. 

Completely surreal hallucinations are not uncommon either. 

A bipedal pig trotting briskly across their field of vision. 

A fairy leaping elegantly on a plate. 

And the blue tiger that her grandfather saw. 

Curiously, in most cases, these visions are not accompanied by auditory hallucinations. 

The "things writhing in the visions" are purely visual phantoms; they do not speak to the patient. 

But among the five senses, vision accounts for a full ninety percent of the information humans receive from the outside world. 

In other words, for the vast majority of DLB patients, the "writhing things" are unequivocally real. 

The proverb DLB patients might want to use most is "Seeing is believing."

After all, it is right there, vividly before their eyes. 

No matter how much those around them try to deny its existence, convincing them it is "not there" or "doesn't exist" is a monumental task. 

Yet if people thoughtlessly scold them, saying, "There's nothing there," "That's impossible," or "Get a grip," the patient will sometimes fly into a rage. 

This is the reason it is said that "caring for DLB is difficult."

When Kaede read a caregiving handbook, it said something like this:

"When a patient complains of a hallucination, saying things like, 'I see a big bug' or 'I'm scared,' do not deny it by saying, 'It's just your imagination,' or push them away by saying, 'You're sick, so stop causing trouble.' Instead, clap your hands and speak gently to them, saying, 'There, it's gone now. You're safe.' Changing the subject to something else is also effective." 

She supposed that was just how it was. 

Besides, she desperately wanted to avoid any situation where she might argue with her grandfather, who had never once gotten angry with her. 

That was precisely why Kaede had avoided talking deeply about his illness, and why she had consistently avoided denying the reality of the hallucinations he spoke of while in his presence. 

To begin with, making a patient realize they have dementia would be nearly impossible, and even if it could be done, it seemed too cruel. 

But still, she thought. 

Kaede felt a strange sense of incongruity regarding her own understanding and behavior—like a division problem that should divide evenly, yet inexplicably leaves a remainder. 

It wasn't quite a form of escapism, a refusal to believe her grandfather could ever get dementia or lose his intellect like this—or rather, it felt slightly different in nuance from mere wishful thinking. 

*(That's right. Something is off—)*

But as for what that sense of incongruity actually was, Kaede couldn't explain it in concrete terms. 

3

After a fifteen-minute bus ride from Gumyoji Station, she returned to her studio apartment to find a book had been delivered. 

It was a collection of essays by Takeshi Setogawa, a literary critic who had loved mysteries beyond measure. 

Looking at the colophon, she saw it read, "First Edition, First Printing: April 1, 1998."

If Kaede's memory served her right, he had passed away shortly after at the young age of fifty. 

In other words, this book was effectively Setogawa's posthumous work. 

Heavily influenced by her grandfather since childhood, Kaede had become a complete mystery maniac. No longer satisfied with just novels, she had started reading Setogawa's critical essays from her grandfather's bookshelf. 

When she did—she was less surprised than she was utterly astounded. 

He would put various works on the chopping block and discuss their charm with flawless, uninhibited prose from his unique perspective, but his columns were sometimes—no, almost without exception—more interesting than the novels themselves. 

For example, in a series of columns titled "A Pilgrimage of Masterpieces," he singled out the representative works of the Big Three of orthodox mystery—Ellery Queen, Agatha Christie, and John Dickson Carr—and thoroughly tore them down, asking things like, "Is this really such a masterpiece?" His critiques were more logical and thrilling than the books themselves, and were impeccably sound arguments—yet, whether Setogawa himself knew it or not, his overflowing love for them seeped through the spaces between the lines, leaving her feeling warm and fuzzy every time she read them. 

The "god" who had made Kaede fall in love with classic foreign mysteries. 

*(Setogawa, Takeshi.)*

Just whispering the name softly in her heart made Kaede's chest flutter. 

Around 1970, in his youth, Setogawa was said to have been a central figure in the "Waseda Mystery Club," a legendary university club that produced countless mystery writers and critics. 

There used to be a coffee shop called "Mon Chéri" in Nishi-Waseda, where the students of the Waseda Mystery Club would gather day after day, practically foaming at the mouth as they engaged in heated mystery discussions. And at the center of that circle, there was always the smiling face of Setogawa, with his thick eyebrows and distinct features. 

Her grandfather, too, had been one of the core members of "Waseda—". 

Orthodox mysteries go well with coffee. 

The tall signboard, featuring the words "Coffee Specialty Mon Chéri" in psychedelic white letters against a red background, apparently gave the impression of shutting out any customers who weren't "mystery specialists."

Rich, bitter coffee with mysterious, bottomless bubbles swirling within. 

A yellow-tiled exterior reminiscent of Leroux's *The Mystery of the Yellow Room*. 

The footsteps of the theater troupe members echoing from the small theater on the second floor—were they Chesterton's *The Queer Feet*, or perhaps Edogawa Ranpo's *The Stalker in the Attic*?

Now that Mon Chéri was gone, she had no choice but to stretch the wings of her imagination. 

Yet that shop, populated by storytellers like Setogawa and her grandfather, must surely have radiated the same heat and brilliance as Tokiwa-so in the manga world, or Mount Liang in *Water Margin*. 

*(I would have loved to hear the two of them discuss orthodox mysteries...)*

Because the shop no longer existed, Kaede's fantasies swelled all the more. 

Unfortunately, Mon Chéri was no more. 

But—the books remained. 

Naturally, she wanted to keep her favorite books close at hand. 

But the books at her grandfather's house were carefully preserved in semi-transparent glassine paper covers, with not a single crease on any page, which made her incredibly hesitant to borrow them. 

So, Kaede had decided to splurge and buy all of Setogawa's essay collections. 

*(Thank goodness, it's practically brand new. It even has the obi band.)*

Kaede felt happy seeing the condition of the book. 

Truth be told, she would have liked to collect them brand new. 

But since this posthumous work was already out of print, she had had no choice but to purchase it from a secondhand online bookstore. 

This completed her collection of all his works. 

*(I wonder what it says about a twenty-seven-year-old woman to be a completionist for something like this.)*

Conscious of her face breaking into a smile, she flipped through the pages while standing. 

At that moment—

Four small pieces of paper fluttered out from between the pages and drifted down to the carpet like ginkgo leaves. 

*(Hmm. What's this?)*

Kaede carefully picked up the four pieces of paper and lined them up on the table. 

She stared fixedly at the various sized rectangular papers, lost in thought. 

*(Too many to be bookmarks.)*

*(But...)*

*(Too "heavy" to be sticky notes—)*

The four pieces of paper were clippings from magazines and newspapers. 

And every single one of them was an obituary announcing Setogawa's death. 

4

Taking advantage of the public holiday, she headed to Himonya in Meguro Ward for the first time in three days. 

A residential neighborhood very close to Himonya Hachimangu, the local tutelary shrine—

Nestled quietly in the corner of it was her grandfather's house, a small, slightly run-down two-story wooden home. 

From the modest garden, cherry and Japanese aralia trees stretched their branches and leaves over the wall. 

On the wooden nameplate of the gatepost, her grandfather's surname was written in strikingly bold, brush-stroked ink. 

It was her grandfather's handwriting, familiar since childhood. 

They say a nameplate is the face of a house. 

The exterior retained a certain elegance and still wore a dignified presence, perhaps thanks to the masterful calligraphy of the nameplate. 

But upon passing through the gate, that charm was suddenly dampened. 

There used to be round stepping stones laid out like markers leading to the front door, but ever since her grandfather developed dementia, it had been turned into a dry, concrete path. 

Turning the knob of the front door, which had been put off for remodeling, the pseudo-soap scent of the antibacterial spray immediately hit her nose. 

*Is the care worker here?*—Kaede was about to call out, but stopped herself. 

There were no shoes indicating such in the entryway. 

The visiting care worker must have just left after finishing the cleaning and laundry. 

Handrails, still relatively new, were installed all along the hallway walls. 

For her grandfather, whose footing was unsteady, multiple handrails were essential for moving around the house. 

When purchasing such welfare equipment, applying for subsidies often required a highly complicated procedure and a massive amount of time, though it varied by municipality. 

As a result, the reality was that people like her grandfather ultimately had to cover most of the cost out of pocket. 

She entered the living room on the left side of the hallway. 

Glancing at the central pillar, which still barely retained its gloss, she saw several horizontal lines drawn in pencil. 

They were marks measuring the heights of her mother when she was a child, and of Kaede, his only granddaughter. 

Although the numbers and dates written beside the lines were mostly fading, they, too, showed signs of her grandfather's beautiful handwriting. 

But seeing the core of the handrail driven right into those letters made her chest ache. 

Looking toward the window, she noticed several white T-shirts hanging up to dry indoors. 

*(Oh no. The helper must have been careless.)*

When living with a DLB patient, it is best to avoid hanging laundry indoors as much as possible. 

This is because they can mistake the hanging clothes for people. 

Particularly with white T-shirts, DLB patients often project intense hallucinations onto those "white canvases."

Hearing that for similar reasons it was better to keep portraits and family photos out of a patient's sight, she had once hurriedly stashed away all the picture frames lined up on the table into the back of a dresser. 

Just as she was rushing to take the T-shirts off the hangers—

A relatively firm voice came from behind her. 

"Sorry about that, Kanae hung those up before she left. I suppose the stains hadn't completely come out."

Her grandfather, who had appeared in the living room holding a coffee cup, slowly sat down on the bed. 

Since the bedroom on the second floor had already been turned into a storage room, his range of movement was strictly limited to this living room where his bed was placed, and the study located at the very back. 

But looking at his steps today, he seemed in remarkably better shape than when she had last visited. 

Vast day-to-day fluctuations in physical condition were another major hallmark of DLB. 

"No, I was just smoothing out the wrinkles," she covered up, giving up on bringing the T-shirts in. 

"It wasn't the helper, it was Mom who was here?"

"She seemed to have work left to do, so she left in a hurry. You just missed her, unfortunately."

Inwardly, Kaede felt relieved. 

That was better—

At least for today, the day she was going to confront him with her question. 

To begin with, it was rare to see her grandfather looking this well recently. 

Today was definitely her chance. 

"The coffee Kanae brews is delicious even when it's cold."

Her grandfather shifted his hips slowly, reseating himself, keeping a smile on his face. 

Then, after sipping his coffee with a slightly trembling hand, he spoke. 

"It doesn't look like I'll spill my coffee today. I know I shouldn't say so myself, but I must be in exceptionally good form. Which is why I want to make sure of something. I suppose you could just call it intuition."

Her grandfather took another sip of coffee, then looked squarely into Kaede's face. 

"You have something important you want to talk to me about. I can tell by looking at your face."

Kaede felt like she was about to cry. 

The way he referred to himself—a first-person pronoun unique to him. 

That clear, gentle gaze with those dark eyes. 

It was as if the grandfather of her past had returned. 

Perhaps because he wasn't in a state of somnolence, his articulation was sharp. 

Thinking back, over the past six months, out of too much concern for his health, they had barely exchanged any serious conversation. 

Now was the only time to ask. 

Kaede gathered her courage and broke the ice. "That's right, actually."

"There's something I want to ask you, Grandpa."

"What is it?"

"Grandpa, you..."

She tried desperately to hold back the tears. 

"Grandpa... you know you're sick, don't you? You realize that what you're seeing isn't reality, but always an illusion, right?"

It was no use. 

Her voice was trembling. 

"But because you don't want me to worry..."

The tears came. 

She had sworn to herself she absolutely wouldn't cry. 

"Because you don't want me to worry, you're pretending you don't realize it, aren't you?"

Her grandfather kept his soft smile and took another sip of his coffee. 

Then, with careful hands, he placed the cup on the dining table next to the bed. 

"Yes, you're exactly right, Kaede. I am, without a doubt, a patient with Lewy body dementia."

Her intuition had been right. 

Her grandfather's dark eyes and the irises within them were as finely detailed as glasswork, filled with a profound depth that threatened to pull her in. 

Yes—the same light of intellect that had never changed since the old days resided right there. 

And *that* was the true nature of the incongruity that Kaede herself hadn't even recognized. 

Over the past two days, she had learned many things by researching DLB in greater detail. 

Even among DLB patients, depending on where the Lewy bodies appear, there are massive differences in the decline of memory and spatial cognitive function. 

While some patients are constantly terrified of their hallucinations, others easily get used to them. 

The severity of the symptoms varies from patient to patient, truly a case of infinite variety. 

When the balance of various medications, especially dopamine agents, works perfectly, it's apparently quite common for hallucinations to vanish "as if a fog has lifted."

Indeed, depending on their physical condition, some patients show absolutely no signs of cognitive decline. 

But what surprised Kaede most was the fact that there are many patients who clearly realize, "The images I am seeing are not real, but a product of my illness."

Among them, there are even highly positive individuals who look forward to the hallucinations that appear every morning when they wake up, making a hobby out of sketching them later. 

Scientific knowledge on DLB is still not fully compiled, making it prone to misunderstandings. 

Even in clinical settings, it is said that quite a few doctors hastily conclude, "It's the progression of dementia," just by superficially observing a patient's intense hallucinatory experiences. 

The onset of DLB does not necessarily mean a decline in intellect. 

When Kaede learned this fact, she felt that her strange sense of incongruity had vanished, truly "as if a fog had lifted."

"Leaving aside these Parkinson's symptoms," her grandfather said, looking at his slightly trembling hands. 

"I had realized quite a while ago that my mental state was far from what you would call a healthy person's. Let's see... for example, when I look at the wall of the bookshelf over there, the entire surface is covered in intricate carvings, as if a master shrine carpenter had carved them into a mikoshi. But when I touch it, there is no sensation of carving at all. The wall is perfectly smooth. So, which should I trust, my vision or my sense of touch? It's completely impossible to carve elaborate designs into the entire bookshelf wall overnight without me noticing. Besides, no one in the world has the slightest motive to go out of their way to sneak into an old man's room and carve up his bookshelf. Which means... unfortunately, the sense of touch is the one to be trusted. Conversely, it means my vision cannot be trusted at all."

Kaede could only listen to his confession, at a loss for words. 

"So, what was the cause of this abnormality? My computer was broken, so I couldn't use it. I thought about searching on my smartphone, but as you can see, my hands are completely unreliable. Or rather, ever since I saw 'Kaede's corpse' and called 119, Kanae confiscated my smartphone, so I had no way to search anyway."

He puckered his perfectly shaped lips in a mischievous pout. 

"So, I begged the helper to call a care taxi for me, went to the library, and did some research. My eyes blurred and I got sleepy just from following the letters, so it took me all day, but... I was able to confirm what kind of illness I have. Speaking of which, you know the phrase, 'little grey cells'?"

He quoted the catchphrase of the famous Belgian detective Hercule Poirot, smiling with a touch of self-deprecation. 

"Well, in my case, dark orange Lewy bodies are spreading across the surface of my brain, so I suppose that makes me the owner of 'little scarlet cells.'"

"Then," Kaede asked. 

Her voice was slightly hoarse, even to her own ears. 

"Why did you go out of your way to tell me stories about your hallucinations over and over again?"

"That's because..."

Her grandfather faltered, just for a moment. 

"It's because, especially when I talk about my hallucinations, your expressions change so rapidly, Kaede. Because you show me a surprised face, or a smile. Most of all, because you give voice to your reactions. When you do that, I can sense your existence with absolute certainty."

"Wait... what do you mean? I'm always right here beside you."

"Well, perhaps this will make you understand. Once, I spoke to you completely honestly about my plans for the time I have left, which honestly probably isn't very long... what do they call it? I won't use the term 'end-of-life planning' because, for a word so commonly heard, I find it insensitive and dislike it. But I talked to you at length about how I intend to handle my remaining days. At the time, I flattered myself into thinking my physical condition was near perfect. I even felt it was now or never if I wanted to tell you. And so... I think I spoke for a good solid hour. But then. For some reason, you sat there in silence, listening with a completely blank expression the whole time, and then..."

Her grandfather paused, lowering his gaze. 

"Suddenly, you just vanished from in front of me. That Kaede... had been a hallucination."

Or perhaps it was the overly roasted coffee. 

A fleeting look of bitterness crossed her grandfather's face. 

"Is there anything more miserable and heartbreaking than that? Ever since then, I decided in my heart that I would never speak of my illness unless you brought it up first. Even if you assumed I was just an old man with dementia who couldn't hold a normal conversation... I thought that couldn't be helped."

*(Grandpa.)*

She whispered it again in her heart. 

*(Grandpa.)*

A grandfather who couldn't—no, who *chose* not to—talk to his only granddaughter about how he wanted to spend his final days. 

Why hadn't she realized his agony sooner?

He undoubtedly saw hallucinations. 

Frequently, at that. 

He suffered from various cognitive impairments, including memory loss, at times. 

Due to Parkinson's symptoms, his movements were extremely sluggish. 

But—there was not the slightest decline in his fundamental intellect. 

5

It must have been dismissal time for the nearby Christian kindergarten. 

She could hear the voices of children passing by the house, singing what sounded like a nursery rhyme. 

The fact that they were singing off-key made it all the more adorable. 

Her grandfather's cheeks naturally relaxed as well. 

The autumn sun sinks like a rock in a well—

But they still seemed to have some time before it grew dark. 

"Actually. There's something I want you to see."

Kaede pulled Setogawa's essay collection out of her black bag. 

If her grandfather had been sleeping in his chair as usual, she had planned to drape the freshly washed blanket she brought over him and read quietly beside him before going home. 

But. 

With the way her grandfather was now, perhaps...

Her grandfather took a pair of rimless reading glasses out of his gown pocket and placed them on his high nose. Even so, he held the book a slight distance away, speaking with deep emotion. 

"Ah, Setogawa-senpai's posthumous work. You didn't have to go out and buy it when I could have just given you mine."

*(I couldn't possibly take it. A happy book that you treasure so much.)*

Kaede chuckled inwardly. 

"I'm sure this was out of print; I'm impressed you managed to get a hold of it."

"Nowadays there are secondhand online bookstores, so it's surprisingly easy to buy even quite rare books. And, well—actually, I found these slipped inside the book."

Kaede opened the book and laid the four obituary clippings out on the table once more. 

*'Active in Mystery and Film Criticism: Takeshi Setogawa Passes Away'*

*'Setogawa Passes Away: A Talent Sorely Missed'*

*'The Era of Multi-Layered Criticism: What Setogawa Left Behind'*

*'Setogawa Spoke of the Happy Rendezvous Between Mystery and Film'*

"Yes. I read all of those back then."

Glancing at the headlines, her grandfather spoke softly, with a touch of loneliness. 

"I think two other papers published articles as well. I have them all clipped in my scrapbook, of course."

"I see."

Once again, Kaede was astounded by his memory. 

Because of his illness, memories of very recent events might completely slip his mind, but when it came to the past, he could open those drawers at will. 

"So, Grandpa—the problem starts here. I think this is what you'd call an 'everyday mystery,' the kind that seems likely to happen but rarely does."

"I see," her grandfather nodded. 

"In other words, the theme of the mystery is this: 'Who on earth, and for what purpose, inserted these four obituary clippings into this book?'"

"Exactly. First of all, it's too many to be used as bookmarks, right? But don't you think obituaries are a bit too heavy to just be sticky notes?"

Taking off his glasses, her grandfather brought up the name of an old mystery writer. 
"It's just like Harry Kemelman." 

Kemelman's masterpiece, *The Nine Mile Walk*, is a quintessential logical mystery where the entire truth of a murder committed the previous day is unraveled in one fell swoop, starting from a single line spoken by a neighbor in a pub: "A nine mile walk is no joke, especially in the rain."

Just then—her grandfather made a sudden request. 

"Kaede. Could you give me a cigarette?"

Perhaps due to its rhythmic cadence, the words carried a spell-like resonance. 

Kaede brought a blue pack of cigarettes from the drawer of the study vanity. 

French cigarettes, Gauloises. 

They weren't particularly expensive, but they weren't the kind of thing you could buy just anywhere, either. 

Kaede always made a point to buy them at a little-known general store when she wandered around the Jimbocho bookstore district. 

"I'd appreciate it if you could light it for me. Yes, that's fine. My hands shake, you see. I try not to drink them when I'm alone."

Her grandfather didn't say he "smoked" cigarettes; he said he "drank" them. 

It was probably a vestige of an era when smoking wasn't frowned upon like today, but acknowledged as a normal indulgence alongside alcohol. 

Even in his youth, he had only partaken in a few cigarettes a week, and recently, it was exceptionally rare for him to "drink" one at all. 

For that very reason, Kaede felt she should at least let him keep this small pleasure. 

Upon drinking the smoke, her grandfather wore an expression of entrancement for a while. 

Kaede didn't dislike the scent of Gauloises, but fearing the smell would stick to the drying T-shirts, she cracked the window open slightly. 

Slowly puffing purple smoke, her grandfather said, "Now then—" in an even clearer tone. 

It was as if the cigarette had flipped the switch to his intellectual booster. 

"What kind of story will you weave from these materials, Kaede?"

Kaede's heart hammered in her chest. 

Her grandfather had always referred to hypotheses as "stories."

He really had returned. 

*That* grandfather of hers. 

"My story goes like this."

Striving to appear calm, Kaede presented the hypothesis she had thought long and hard about. 

"Story One. 'The person who slipped the obituaries inside was the book's original owner. Because he, or she, wanted to share the emptiness of Setogawa's passing with other fans of Setogawa, they deliberately placed the articles in the book.'"

Peeking at her grandfather's reaction, she saw him nodding in agreement. 

As always, telling a story in front of her grandfather was nerve-wracking. 

But—

*(But, I'm happy.)*

"Umm, Story Two."

Snapping back to reality, Kaede continued. 

"'The person who slipped the obituaries inside was an employee at the secondhand bookstore. He, or she, was a fan of Setogawa. Then, for the first time in decades, an order came in for Setogawa's out-of-print book. Overjoyed, he or she slipped the articles into the book as a sort of present for an unknown kindred spirit—namely, me.'"

Perhaps due to intellectual excitement, her throat was dry. 

"How's that? Those are the two stories I came up with."

Her grandfather answered. 
"Hmm. Not bad. Both have a certain logic to them, and I wouldn't go so far as to call them far-fetched. But they both contain a major contradiction."

"I see."

Kaede bit her lip. 

"Listen closely. First, the contradiction in Story One is this: Would a Setogawa fan devoted enough to hold on to obituary clippings really sell a beloved book? Furthermore, this book is his posthumous work. Anyone with normal sensibilities would keep it carefully as a part of their own collection along with the articles."

Kaede had no choice but to nod. 

"True. From a book lover's psychology, it might be hard to accept."

"Story Two feels better than Story One. But the contradiction is still undeniable. If a bookstore employee slipped the obituaries in out of goodwill... why didn't they include even a single note? If they went through the trouble of carefully inserting the clippings, wouldn't they at least write something like, 'I was delighted to find a kindred spirit. Please accept these articles reporting on Mr. Setogawa's passing as a tribute.' Why would he, or she, spare such a minor effort? In short..."

Her grandfather cut straight to the point. 

"Both Story One and Story Two are fundamentally flawed plots. There must be another Story X."

"Then," Kaede asked in a hushed voice. 

"Are you saying you can weave this Story X, Grandpa?"

Her grandfather said nothing. As if mourning the shortened Gauloise, he somehow pinched it between his thumb and index finger and puffed out the final wisp of smoke. 

His eyes slowly—but surely—narrowed. 

Kaede worried he might fall asleep. 

But her fears were unfounded. 

Her grandfather opened his eyes wide and said, "I just saw the 'picture.'"

"Unfortunately, the male original owner of the book has already passed away."

"What?"

"Because look, see? There's a man with a peaceful expression on his face standing right over there."

A hallucination. 

But this hallucination is based on crystal-clear logic—

Kaede instinctively felt this to be true. 

"The plot of Story X goes like this. While he was alive, he carefully kept the obituaries of his favorite Takeshi Setogawa tucked inside his cherished book out of deep sorrow. However—after his death, his wife, completely unaware that it was such a treasure, sold it to a secondhand bookstore along with various other books as part of sorting through his belongings."

*(That's the truth—)*

She couldn't help but think so. 

This was a flawless, completely convincing, perfect "story."

But, Kaede persisted. 

"How do you know the original owner was a man? It could have been a woman, couldn't it?"

"No," her grandfather stated simply. 

"When a spouse passes away, the one who can suppress their sorrow and act calmly is the woman. Men are utterly useless. In fact, even I..."

Her grandfather lowered his gaze. 

"When my wife passed away before me, I couldn't do a single thing."

For a brief moment, the image of her late grandmother's round, soft face flashed across Kaede's mind. 

A silence lingered for a while.
