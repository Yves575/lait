I

I had no belongings, not even a coat. They said it was unnecessary. They let me keep my black sweater: it would do. But I fought for the shirt. I said I would wean myself slowly. Right in the passageway, under the ship’s belly, where we stood being jostled about, Abs offered me his hand with a knowing smile:

“Just be careful…”





I remembered that, too. I didn’t crush his fingers. I was entirely calm. He wanted to say something else. I spared him that, turning away as if I hadn’t noticed, and went up the steps inside. The stewardess led me between the rows of seats to the very front.

I didn’t want a separate compartment. I wondered if they had warned her. The seat unfolded soundlessly. She adjusted the backrest, smiled at me, and walked away. I sat down. The cushions were cavernously soft, like everywhere else. The backrests were so high I could barely see the other passengers. I accepted the colorfulness of the women’s clothes without resistance now, but I still senselessly suspected the men of masquerading, and I still harbored a quiet hope that I would see someone dressed normally—a pathetic reflex. Everyone settled in quickly; no one had any luggage.

Not even a briefcase or a bundle. The women didn't either. There seemed to be more of them. In front of me—two mulatto women in parrot-like furs, ruffled like feathers; apparently, some sort of avian fashion was in.

Further on, a couple with a child. After the glaring selenophores of the platforms and tunnels, after the unbearably loud, self-luminous vegetation of the streets, the light from the concave ceiling was almost a mere glow. I placed my hands on my knees, because they somehow felt in the way. Everyone was seated now. Eight rows of gray seats, a fir-scented breeze, the fading murmur of conversations. I expected a takeoff announcement, some signals, an order to fasten seatbelts, but nothing happened. Across the matte ceiling, indistinct shadows began to run from front to back, like silhouettes of birds cut out of paper. *What the hell is with these birds,* I thought helplessly. *Maybe it means something?* I was rigid from hyper-vigilance, trying not to do anything inappropriate.

It had been like this for four days. From the very first moment. I was constantly lagging behind everything that happened, and the continuous effort to understand any casual conversation or situation turned this tension into a feeling foully similar to despair. I was convinced the others felt the same, but we didn’t talk about it, not even when we were left alone. We only joked about our brute strength, about this excess of power we still had; besides, you really had to watch yourself—at first, trying to stand up, I would shoot up toward the ceiling, and every object I picked up felt paper-light, hollow. But I quickly learned to control my own body.

When shaking hands, I no longer crushed anyone’s fingers. That was simple. Unfortunately, it was the least important part.





My neighbor on the left, portly, tanned, with overly shiny eyes (maybe from contact lenses), suddenly vanished, because his seat expanded at the sides, which went up and joined together, forming a sort of ovoid cocoon. A few other people disappeared into such cabins. They resembled swollen sarcophagi. What were they doing in there? But I kept encountering such things and tried not to gawk, as long as they didn’t concern me directly. Interestingly, I treated the people who stared at us wide-eyed, upon learning who we were, rather indifferently. Their sheer astonishment mattered little to me, though I instantly realized there wasn’t an ounce of admiration in it. Resentment was stirred, rather, by those who took care of us—the staff at Adapt. Most vividly, perhaps, by Dr. Abs, because he treated me like a doctor treats an abnormal patient, pretending, and quite well at that, that he was dealing with someone perfectly ordinary. When that was no longer possible—he cracked jokes. I’d had enough of his directness and joviality. If asked about it (or so I thought), any passerby would have considered Olaf or me similar to themselves—it wasn’t so much *we* who were uncanny to them, but our past fate: *that* was what was extraordinary. Dr. Abs, however, like every Adapt employee, knew better—that we really were different. This difference was not a mark of distinction, but merely an obstacle to communication, to the simplest exchange of words—hell, to opening a door, since door handles had ceased to exist, I couldn't remember if it was fifty or sixty years ago.





Takeoff happened unexpectedly. Gravity didn’t change by a hair, no sounds penetrated the hermetic interior, shadows flowed rhythmically across the ceiling—perhaps years of routine, an old instinct told me at a certain moment that we were in space, for it was a certainty, not an assumption.

But something else preoccupied me. I rested, half-lying, with my legs stretched out, motionless. They had let me have my way too easily. Even Oswamm hadn’t opposed my decision too much. The counterarguments I heard from him and Abs couldn’t convince me—I could have come up with better ones myself. They only insisted on one thing: that each of us had to fly separately. They didn’t even hold it against me that I had incited Olaf to rebel (because if it weren’t for me, he surely would have agreed to stay longer). That was puzzling. I had expected complications, something that would thwart my plan at the last minute, but nothing happened, and here I was, flying. This last journey was to end in fifteen minutes.





Clearly, what I had come up with, and the way I confronted them to fight for a premature departure, hadn’t surprised them. They must have had this type of reaction cataloged; it was a stereotypical behavior proper to daredevils like me, provided with an appropriate serial number in their psychotechnical tables. They let me fly—why? Because experience told them I wouldn't be able to cope? But how could that be, since this whole "independent" escapade consisted of a flight from one station to another, where someone from the Earth Adapt was supposed to be waiting, and all I had to do was find this man at the agreed-upon spot?

Something happened. Raised voices reached me. I leaned out of my seat. A few rows ahead of me, a woman pushed the stewardess away, who, with a slowed, automatic movement, as if under the influence of that—not really that forceful—push, was walking backward between the seats, and the woman repeated: "I won't allow it! Don't let it touch me!"

I didn't see the shouting woman's face. Her companion was pulling her by the arm, explaining something to her soothingly. What did this scene mean? The other passengers paid it no mind. For the umpteenth time, a sense of unbelievable alienness overcame me. I looked up at the stewardess, who had stopped next to me and was smiling just as before. It wasn't a purely external smile of obligatory politeness covering up nervousness over the incident. She wasn't faking calmness; she was truly calm.





"Would you like something to drink? Prum, extran, morr, cider?" A melodic voice. I shook my head.

I wanted to say something nice to her, but only managed a stereotypical question:

"When do we land?"

"In six minutes. Would you like something to eat? You don't have to rush. You can stay on after landing."

"No, thank you."





She walked away. In the air, right in front of my face, against the backrest of the seat ahead, the word STRATO lit up, as if written by the glowing tip of a cigarette. I leaned forward to see how this writing was formed, and flinched. The back of the seat followed my back and hugged it resiliently. I already knew that the furniture anticipated every change of position, but I kept forgetting. It wasn't pleasant—as if someone were tracking my every move.

I tried to return to my previous position, too energetically, it seems. The seat misunderstood and almost unfolded entirely, like a bed. I jerked upright. What idiocy! More self-control.

I finally sat down. The letters of the pink STRATO quivered and flowed into others: TERMINAL. No jolt, warning, or whistle. Nothing. A distant voice rang out, like a postilion's horn; four oval doors at the ends of the aisles slid open, and a dull, all-encompassing roar, like the sea, invaded the interior. The voices of the passengers rising from their seats drowned in it without a trace. I remained seated while they exited, rows of silhouettes flashing against the external lights in green, lilac, purple—a veritable masquerade ball. Now everyone was gone. I stood up. I mechanically pulled down my sweater. It felt somehow foolish, being empty-handed like this. A cooler breeze drafted through the open doors. I turned around. The stewardess stood by the partition wall, her back not touching it. Her face still wore the same serene smile, directed at the empty rows of seats, which now slowly began to curl up, folding in on themselves like fleshy flowers, some faster, others a bit slower—it was the only movement in this all-filling, prolonged roar flowing through the oval openings, bringing the open sea to mind. "I don't want it touching me!" Suddenly I detected something wrong with her smile. From the exit I said:





"Goodbye..."

"At your service."

The meaning of those words, so peculiar in the mouth of a pretty young woman, didn't dawn on me immediately, for they reached me as I was already turned away, leaning out the door. I went to step onto the step, but there wasn't one. Between the metal hull and the edge of the platform gaped a meter-wide crevice. Losing my balance, unprepared for such a trap, I took a clumsy leap and, already in mid-air, felt a stream of invisible force grabbing me from below, so that I floated over the void and was set down softly on a white surface that yielded elastically. I must have looked rather foolish mid-flight—I felt a few amused glances, or so it seemed to me; I quickly turned and walked along the platform. The projectile I had arrived in rested in a deep cradle, separated from the edges of the platforms by an unprotected gap. As if by accident, I approached this void and again felt the invisible resilience that prevented me from stepping beyond the white edge. I wanted to look for the source of this peculiar force, but suddenly it was as if I awoke: I was on Earth.





A wave of pedestrians swept me up: jostled, I moved forward in the crush. It took a moment before I truly took in the vastness of this hall. Was it even a single hall? No walls: a white, gleaming, suspended explosion of incredible wings high above, and between them columns built not of any material, but of dizzying motion. Giant geysers shooting upward with a liquid denser than water, illuminated from within by colored spotlights? No; vertical glass tunnels through which processions of blurred vehicles whizzed upward? I no longer knew anything. Constantly pushed, jostled in the swarming haste of the crowds, I tried to make my way to some empty space, but there were no empty spaces here. A head taller than those around me, I could see that the emptied projectile was moving away—no, it was we who were gliding forward along with the entire platform. Lights flared from on high, in which the crowd sparkled and shimmered. Now the plane on which we stood crammed together began to rise, and I saw below, already distant, double white lanes jammed with people, with the black gaps of crevices along inert hulls—for there were dozens of ships like ours—the moving platform curved, accelerated, went under higher levels. Fluttering, ruffling the hair of those standing with the wind they kicked up, streamlined shadows trembling with speed flew over them, as if on impossible viaducts devoid of any support, with their signal lights stretched into streaks of fire; then the surface carrying us began to divide, bifurcating along imperceptible seams; my lane shifted through interiors full of standing and sitting people, surrounded by a multitude of tiny flashes, as if they were busy setting off colored fireworks.





I didn't know where to look. In front of me stood a man in something fluffy like fur, which opalesced like metal when touched by light. He held a woman in scarlet by the arm. What she was wearing was covered in large eyes, like a peacock's; these eyes were blinking. It was no illusion; the eyes of her dress were genuinely opening and closing. The walkway on which I stood behind the two of them, among dozens of other people, accelerated again. Between planes of white-smoked glaze, colored, illuminated passages with transparent ceilings opened up, continuously trampled by hundreds of feet on the next, higher level; the all-encompassing roar would spread out, then constrict, whenever thousands of human voices and sounds—incomprehensible to me, meaningful to them—were muffled by yet another tunnel of this journey aimed who knows where; deep in the background, the surroundings were constantly stitched by the passing streaks of I don't know what vehicles—flying ones, perhaps—for sometimes they went diagonally up or down, boring into space, so that I reflexively expected a horrific collision, since I saw no guiderails or tracks, if these were aerial trams. When these blurred hurricanes of speed broke off even for a moment, majestically slow, massive planes full of people emerged from behind them, like flying wharves moving in different directions, passing each other, rising, seeming to interpenetrate due to an illusion of perspective. It was hard to rest the eyes on anything stationary, because the entire surrounding architecture seemed composed essentially of movement itself, of change, and even what I had initially taken for a winged ceiling was just overhanging tiers, which now gave way to others, even higher. Suddenly, filtered through the glaze of the ceilings, of those enigmatic columns, reflected off silver planes, a heavy, purple dawn entered every nook of space, the interiors of passing culverts, the features of human faces—as if far away, somewhere in the heart of the multi-mile structure, an atomic fire had ignited.

The green of the incessantly jumping neon lights turned dirty; the milkiness of the parabolic buttresses turned pink. In this sudden saturation of the air with a rusty hue there was a hint of an impending catastrophe; that's how I took it, but no one paid the slightest attention to this change, and I couldn't even say when it faded.





Whirling green circles, like neon hoops suspended in the air, appeared at the edges of our walkway; then a portion of the people stepped onto the approaching branch of another walkway or ramp; I noticed that one could pass through the green lines of those lights with impunity, as if they were immaterial.

For a time I let myself be carried along passively by the white walkway, until it occurred to me that perhaps I was already outside the station, and this unbelievable landscape of contorted glaze, seemingly always taking flight, was actually the city, and the place I had left behind existed only in my memory.

"Excuse me," I touched the arm of the man in the fur, "where are we...?"

They both looked at me. Their faces, when they raised them, took on an expression of surprise.

I had a faint hope it was only because of my height.

"On the polyduct," the man said. "What's your node?"

I understood nothing.

"Are we... still at the station?"

"Of course..." he replied with some hesitation.

"And... where is the Inner Circle?"

"You've already missed it. You have to double back."

"A rast from Merid would be better," the woman interjected. All the eyes on her dress seemed to stare at me with suspicious astonishment.

"A rast...?" I repeated helplessly.





"Oh, over there," she pointed through the oncoming green circle at an empty elevation with black-and-silver striped sides, like the hull of a peculiarly painted ship lying on its side. I thanked her and stepped off the walkway, probably in the wrong place, because the momentum swept my legs out from under me. I caught my balance, but it spun me around so that I didn't know which way to go. I wondered what to do, but during this time my transfer point had moved significantly away from the black-and-silver elevation the woman had pointed out, and I could no longer find it. Since most of the people standing near me were crossing onto an upward-sloping ramp, I did the same. Once on it, I noticed a colossal sign, burning motionlessly in the air: DUCT CENTR—the rest of the letters on both sides escaped my view due to their sheer size. It carried me soundlessly onto a platform that must have been a kilometer long, from which a spindle-shaped ship was just casting off, revealing its light-riddled belly as it rose. Then again, maybe that whale-like shape was the platform, and I had found myself on a "rast"—there wasn't even anyone to ask, as emptiness stretched all around. I must have taken a wrong turn. A part of my "platform" was built up with flattened spaces lacking front walls. Approaching, I noticed a kind of dimly lit, low stalls, in which black machines rested in rows. I took them for cars. But when the two nearest ones slid out and, before I could step back, passed me, instantly picking up tremendous speed, I saw, before they vanished down the perspective of parabolic slants, that they had no wheels, windows, or doors—streamlined, like giant black teardrops. Cars or not, I thought, in any case this must be some kind of parking lot? Maybe for these "rasts"? I decided it would be best if I waited for someone to show up and rode along with them, or at least found out something. My platform, angled slightly upward like the wing of an impossible airplane, remained empty, however; only the black machines slipped out of their metal burrows, singly or a few at a time, and raced off, always in the same direction. I walked to the very edge of the platform until that invisible, resilient force, ensuring safety, made itself known again. It really was hanging in the air, unsupported by anything. Lifting my head, I saw many similar ones, hovering motionlessly through space in the same way with their great lights extinguished; they burned on others, where ships were docking. But these were neither rockets nor even projectiles like the one that had brought me from Luna.





I stood for a long time, until I noticed, against the backdrop of some subsequent halls—I didn't know whether they were a mirror reflection of this one, or reality—fiery letters gliding rhythmically through the air: SOAMO SOAMO SOAMO, a pause, a blue flash, and NEONAX NEONAX NEONAX; maybe they were station names, maybe advertised products. They meant nothing to me.

*It's high time I found that guy,* I thought, turned on my heel, and, finding a walkway flowing in the opposite direction, rode it down. It turned out to be the wrong level and not even the hall from which I had come up: I recognized this by the absence of those colossal columns. Or perhaps they had moved off somewhere; I now considered everything possible.





I found myself amidst a whole forest of fountains; further on I stumbled upon a white-and-pink hall, full of women.

Passing by, I casually stuck my hand into the stream of an illuminated fountain, perhaps because it was pleasant to encounter something even slightly familiar. But I felt nothing; the fountain had no water. After a moment, I thought I smelled a floral scent. I brought my hand to my nostrils. It smelled like a thousand bars of toilet soap at once. Instinctively, I began wiping it on my pants. I was already standing in front of that hall, full of women, only women. It didn't look like a restroom foyer to me, but in the end, that wasn't certain. I preferred not to ask, so I turned back. A young man, dressed as if spilling mercury had solidified on him—puffy (or perhaps frothy) at the shoulders, tight at the hips—was talking to a blonde girl leaning her back against the basin of a fountain. The girl, in a light dress, quite ordinary, which filled me with cheer, held a bouquet of pale pink flowers and, burying her face in them, smiled at the boy with her eyes. At the last moment, as I stopped beside them and was already opening my mouth, I saw that she was eating those flowers—and my voice failed me for a split second. She calmly chewed the delicate petals. She raised her eyes to me. They froze. But I was already used to that. I asked where the Inner Circle was.





The boy seemed unpleasantly surprised, or even angry, that someone dared interrupt their tête-à-tête. Evidently I had committed a faux pas. He first looked up, then lowered his eyes, as if expecting to find stilts as the cause of my height. He didn't even speak.

"Oh, over there," the girl called out. "A rast to the I.C., your rast, sir, you'll make it, quickly!" I broke into a run in the indicated direction, not even knowing where—I still had no idea what this blasted rast looked like—and after ten paces I saw a silvery funnel descending from on high, the base of one of those colossal columns that had so amazed me earlier—could they be flying columns?—people were hurrying there from various sides, and suddenly I collided with someone. I didn't even stumble, just stood rooted to the spot, while the other man, a squat fellow in orange garb, fell, and something unbelievable happened to him: his fur wilted before my eyes, collapsing like a punctured balloon! I stood over him, dumbfounded, unable to stammer an apology. He got up, glared at me, but said nothing, turned and walked away with a sweeping stride, manipulating something at his chest—and his outfit filled out and began glowing anew...





There was no one left at the spot the girl had pointed out. After that adventure, I gave up for good on searching for rasts, the Inner Circle, ducts, nodes, and decided to get out of the station. My experiences so far didn't encourage pestering passersby, so I rode up an oblique blue arrow at random, passing with my body, without much sensation, through two successive signs glowing in the air: LOCAL CIRCUITS. I ended up on an escalator, quite crowded. The next level was styled in tones of muted bronze veined with golden exclamation marks. Fluid descents of ceilings and concave walls. Ceilingless corridors, their upper parts seemingly submerged in luminous down. As if I were approaching some residential areas, the surroundings had something of a system of gigantic hotel lobbies about them—small windows, nickel pipes along the walls, alcoves with people officiating at something; maybe they were currency exchanges, maybe a post office. I walked on. I was almost certain I wouldn't find the exit this way and (judging by how long the ride up had taken) that I was in the aerial part of the station, yet I maintained the same direction. An unexpected emptiness, panels of raspberry cladding with sparkling little stars, rows of doors. The nearest ones were ajar. I peeked inside. A large, broad-shouldered man did the exact same thing from the opposite side at that very moment. Myself, in a wall-to-wall mirror. I pushed the door wider. Porcelain, silver pipes, nickel. Restrooms.





I felt a little like laughing, but all in all I was mostly stupefied. I quickly turned back; another corridor, milk-white bands flowing downward. The escalator handrail was soft, warm; I didn't count the passing floors, more and more people; they stopped at enameled boxes that sprang from the wall at every step, one touch of a finger, something dropped into their hand, they put it in their pocket and walked on. I don't know why I did exactly what the man in a loose, purple outfit ahead of me did: a key with a small indentation for a fingertip, a press, and straight into my cupped hand fell a colorful, semi-transparent tube, feeling warm.

I shook it, brought it to my eyes, some pills? No. A cork? It had no cork, no closure of any kind. What was it for? What were the others doing? Putting it in their pockets. The inscription on the dispenser: LAHGAN. I stood there; people jostled me. And suddenly I felt like an ape handed a fountain pen or a lighter; for a fraction of a second, a blind rage washed over me; I clenched my jaw, narrowed my eyes, and merged, slightly hunched, into the stream of pedestrians. The corridor widened, it was already a hall. Fiery letters: REAL AMMO REAL AMMO.

Through the current of hurrying people, above their heads, I spotted a window in the distance. The first window.

Panoramic, immense.





As if all the firmaments of the night, thrown flat. To the horizon from a glowing mist—colorful galaxies of squares, clusters of spiral lights, glows drizzling over skyscrapers, streets: the crawling, worm-like movement of luminous beads, and above this, in vertical lines, a churning of neon lights, plumes and lightning bolts, wheels, planes and bottles of fire, red dandelions of signal lights on spires, momentary suns and hemorrhages of neon signs, mechanically violent. I stood and watched, hearing the rhythmic murmur of hundreds of feet behind me. Suddenly the city vanished and a giant, three-meter face appeared. "We have broadcast a set of chronicles from the seventies in the series 'visions of old capitals.'

The transtel now shifts its range to the cosmolite studios..."

I practically fled. It wasn't a window. Some kind of television. I quickened my pace. I broke a slight sweat.





Downward. Faster. Golden squares of light. Crowds inside, foam on glasses, an almost black liquid, not beer, for it had a venomous greenish gleam, and young people, boys and girls, embracing, in sixes, eights, blocking the entire width of the passages, walked toward me; they had to break their grip to let me pass. I shuddered. Without knowing when, I stepped onto a moving walkway. Astonished eyes flashed quite close—a beautiful, dark-skinned girl in something that shone on her like phosphorized metal. The fabric clung to her: she looked naked. Faces white, yellow, a few tall black men, but I was still the tallest. They parted before me. Overhead, behind convex panes, swept blown shadows, invisible orchestras played, while down here a peculiar promenade continued, in dark passages—headless figures of women; the down covering their shoulders glowed so brightly that only their leaning necks shone in it like white, strange stalks, and a scattered radiance in their hair—self-luminous powder? A narrow passage led me into an enfilade of grotesque, because moving, even bustling statues; something resembling a wide street, raised at the sides, roared with laughter, people were having fun; what amused them so much—these sculptures?





Colossal figures in cones of spotlights; light poured off them, ruby, honey-colored, thick as syrup, of unusual color concentration. I walked on passively, squinting, losing myself.

A steep, green passage, grotesque pavilions, pagodas accessed by small bridges, full of little establishments, the smell of frying, pungent, intrusive, rows of little gas flames behind glass, the clatter of glass, repeating, metallic, incomprehensible sounds. The crowd that had carried me here collided with another, then it thinned out, everyone was boarding a fully open car—no, it was just transparent, as if cast from glass, even the seats looked like glass, though soft. I didn't know when I ended up inside—we were riding. The car sped along, people shouted over a loudspeaker repeating: "Meridional level, Meridional level, nodes for Spiro, Blekkr Frosom," the whole car seemed to melt, pierced by beams of light, the walls flew by in streaks of flame and colors, parabolic arches, white platforms, "Forteran, Forteran, Galee nodes, outer rast nodes, Makra"—the loudspeaker mumbled, the car stopped and sped on; I discovered an astonishing thing: you couldn't feel the braking or accelerating, as if inertia had been abolished. How was that possible? I tested it, bending my knees slightly, at three consecutive stops. Nothing on the curves, either. People got off, got on; on the front platform stood a woman with a dog; I had never seen one like it, it was huge, with a head like a sphere, very ugly; its calm, hazel eyes reflected the shrinking garlands of lights rushing backward. RAMBRENT RAMBRENT. A flutter of white and bluish fluorescent tubes, stairs of crystalline glare, black pediments, the glare slowly turned to stone, the car stood still. I got out and was stunned. Above the ampitheatrically sunken disc of the stop rose a multi-level, familiar structure; I was still at the station, in a different part of the same gigantic hall, swollen with the white sweeps of planes. I headed toward the edge of this geometrically precise depression—the car had already departed—and experienced another shock: I wasn't at the bottom, as I had thought; I was high up, some forty stories above ribbons of walkways visible in the abyss, above silver decks of rhythmically gliding platforms, and long, silent hulks were entering between them, and people were getting out of them through rows of hatches, as if these monsters, these chrome fish, were depositing layers of black and colored roe at regular intervals. Above all this, in the distance, through the haze of space, I saw golden words moving as if on an invisible tightrope:





GLENIANA ROON, RETURNING TODAY WITH A RECORDING OF MIMORPHIC REAL, PAYS TRIBUTE IN THE ORATORIUM TO THE MEMORY OF RAPPER KERX POLITRA. THE TERMINAL DAILY REPORTS: TODAY IN AMMONLEE, PETIFARGUE BROUGHT THE FIRST ENZOM TO SYSTOLIZATION, WE WILL BROADCAST THE VOICE OF THE EMINENT GRAVISTICIAN AT TWENTY-SEVEN O'CLOCK. ARRAKER'S ADVANTAGE. ARRAKER REPEATED HIS SUCCESS AS THE FIRST OBLITERATOR OF THE SEASON IN THE TRANSVAAL STADIUM.

I walked away. So even the measurement of time had changed. Struck by the light of the colossal letters, which sped above the sea of heads like rows of flaming tightrope walkers, the metallic fabrics of the women's dresses flickered with sudden little flames. I walked, unaware of it, and something inside me kept repeating: so even time has changed. This seemed to finish me off. I saw nothing with my eyes open. I wanted only one thing: to get out of here, to escape this hellish station, to find myself under the open sky, in an open space, to see the stars, to feel the wind.





An avenue of elongated lights drew me; something was written in the translucent stone of the ceilings—a sharp little flame encased in alabaster traced the letters—TELETRANS TELEPORT TELETHON, through ogival doors (but this was some impossible, unhinged arch, like the negative of a rocket's nose cone) I entered a hall covered by a frozen golden fire. In the wall recesses—hundreds of booths; people ran into them, rushed out, threw torn strips onto the floor—not telegraph tape, it was something else, with embossed nodules—others trampled on these shreds. I wanted to leave, mistakenly entered a dark interior; before I could back out, something buzzed, a flash like a photographer's lamp, and from a metal-rimmed slit, like a letterbox, slid a small sheet of glossy paper folded in two. I took it, opened it, and a human head popped out of it, with parted, slightly twisted thin lips, looking at me with squinting eyes: it was me! I folded the paper in two and the three-dimensional phantom vanished. I slowly parted the edges, nothing, wider, it appeared again, as if jumping out of nowhere, a head severed from the torso, suspended above the sheet of paper with a not entirely intelligent expression. I stared at my own face for a moment—what was this, a three-dimensional photograph?

I put the card in my pocket and left. The golden hell seemed to descend on the heads of the crowd, a ceiling of fiery magma, unreal but breathing true conflagration, and no one looked at it; busy, they ran from one booth to another, green letters jumped in the depths, columns of numbers descended on narrow screens, other booths, instead of doors—blinds rolling up lightning-fast at someone's approach; I finally found the exit.





A corridor, curved, with a sloping floor, like sometimes in a theater; stylized conchs bloomed from the walls; overhead rushed the words INFOR INFOR INFOR, endlessly.

I first saw an infor on Luna and mistook it for an artificial flower.

I brought my face close to a pale green calyx, which immediately, before I parted my lips, froze in anticipation.

"Which way out?" I asked, somewhat dazedly.

"To where?" a warm alto replied instantly.

"To the city."

"Which district?"

"It doesn't matter."

"Which level?"

"It doesn't matter, I want to get out of the station!"

"Meridional, rasts: one hundred six, one hundred seventeen zero eight, zero two. Triduct, level AF, AG, myth level circular, twelve and sixteen, nadir level leads in every meridian dimension direction. Central level—gliders, local red, remote white, A, B and W. Ulder level, direct, all escals from the third up..." the female voice recited in a sing-song tone. I had the urge to rip the microphone from the wall, which was leaning toward my face with such solicitude.

I walked away. Idiot! Idiot!—ground inside me with every step. EX EX EX EX—repeated a sign gliding above, edged in lemon mist. Maybe that's exit?





A colossal sign. EXOTAL. I caught a violent current of warm air, making my pant legs flap in it. I found myself under the open sky. But the blackness of the night had retreated far into space, driven back by a swarm of lights. A massive restaurant—tables whose surfaces shone with different colors, above them faces lit from below, somewhat eerily, full of deep shadows. Low armchairs, black liquid with green foam in glasses, lanterns from which showered tiny sparks, no, fireflies rather, like swarms of burning moths. The chaos of lights extinguished the stars. When I raised my head, I saw only a black void. And yet, astonishingly, in that moment its blind presence filled me with a sort of comfort.

I stood and looked. Someone brushed past me, I smelled perfume, sharp and mild at the same time; a couple walked past, the girl turned to the man, her shoulders and breasts were submerged in a fluffy cloud, she stepped into his embrace, they danced. They still dance, I thought. At least there's that. The couple took a few steps, a pale, mercurial circle lifted them along with other couples, their dark red shadows moved beneath its enormous, slowly spinning disc; it rested on nothing, it didn't even have an axis, it rotated to the sound of music, suspended in the air. I walked between the tables. The soft plastic I was treading on ended, giving way to rough rock. Through a luminous curtain I stepped inside, found myself in a rocky grotto. As if ten or fifty Gothic naves raised from stalactites; veined drips of pearlescent minerals embraced the cave mouths, people sat in them, dangling their legs over the void, wavering flames burned between their knees, and below stretched the unruffled black sheet of an underground lake, reflecting the convergence of the rocks. Down there, on haphazardly lashed rafts, people also rested, all facing one direction. I went down to the water's edge and saw a dancer on the sand on the opposite shore. She seemed naked to me, but the whiteness of her body was unnatural. With small, faltering steps she ran down toward the water; as she was reflected in it, she suddenly spread her arms and bowed her head—that was the end, but no one clapped; the dancer remained motionless for a few seconds, then walked slowly along the shore, tracing the uneven shoreline. She was maybe thirty paces from me when something happened to her. A moment ago I had seen her smiling, exhausted face; suddenly something seemed to veil her, her silhouette trembled and vanished.





"A float for you, sir?" a polite voice sounded behind me. I turned—no one, just a streamlined little table striding on comically bent legs; as it moved, the glasses of bubbling liquid standing in rows on side trays trembled—one arm politely proffered the beverage, another was already reaching for a plate with a finger hole, resembling a small, concave palette—it was an automaton; I saw the glow of its transistorized heart smoldering behind a central pane.





I bypassed those obsequiously extending insect arms, laden with delicacies I spurned, and quickly left the artificial grotto, clenching my jaw as if I had suffered an incomprehensible insult. I crossed the entire width of the terrace, between serpentine rows of tables, beneath avenues of lanterns, showered by the weightless dust of disintegrating, dying fireflies, black and gold. At the very edge, ringed with stone, old, as if fogged with a yellowish lichen, a true wind finally wafted over me, clean, cool. A vacant table stood nearby. I sat down awkwardly, my back to the crowd, looking out into the night. Below stretched a darkness, formless and unexpected; only far, very far on its peripheries smoldered thin, wavering lights, peculiarly unsteady, as if it weren't electricity, and even further out, swords of light pierced the sky, cold, thin; I didn't know if they were buildings or pillars, I would have taken them for searchlight beams had it not been for a delicate mesh marking them—like a glass cylinder planted base-first into the earth, reaching the clouds, filled alternately with concave and convex lenses. They must have been impossibly tall; a bit of light drizzled around them, pulsing so that sometimes a halo of orange and sometimes an almost white glow encircled them. That was all; that was what the city looked like; I tried to find the streets, to guess at them, but this dark and seemingly dead expanse below stretched in all directions, unlit by even a single spark.





"Col...?" I heard, uttered probably not for the first time, but at first I didn't take it to mean me. Before I even turned around fully, the armchair did it for me. Before me stood a girl, maybe twenty years old, in a blue that clung to her like something congealed; her shoulders and breasts disappeared in a navy-blue fluff that grew increasingly transparent toward the bottom. Her slender, beautiful belly was like a sculpture in breathing metal. In her ears she had something glowing, so large it covered her ears; small, uncertainly smiling lips—painted, her nostrils red inside too—I noticed that was exactly how most women wore it. She gripped the back of the chair opposite me with both hands, with the words: "How are things, col!"

She sat down. I got the impression she was a bit drunk.

"It's boring here," she resumed after a moment. "No? Where should we take off to, col?"

"I'm not a col..." I began. She leaned her elbows on the table and wiggled her hand over a half-filled glass until the end of a golden chain, wrapped around her fingers, dipped into the liquid. She leaned closer and closer as she did so. I could feel her breath. If she was drunk, it wasn't on alcohol.

"What do you mean?" she said. "You are. You have to be. Everyone is a col. Do you want to? Shall we take off somewhere?" If only I knew what that meant.

"Alright," I said.

She stood up. And I stood up from that awfully low chair.

"How do you do that?" she asked.

"What?"

She looked at my legs.

"I thought you were standing on your tiptoes..."





I smiled silently. She walked up to me, took my arm, and was surprised again.

"What do you have under there?"

"Where, here? Nothing."

"You're singing," she said and pulled me slightly. We walked between the tables, and I wondered what "singing" could mean—maybe "lying"?

She led me toward a dark gold wall, to where a sign somewhat resembling a treble clef glowed on it. When we were right up against it, the wall opened. I felt a gust of hot air.





A narrow, silver escalator flowed downward. We stood side by side. She didn't even reach my shoulder. She had a feline skull, black hair with a blue sheen, a profile perhaps too sharp, but she was pretty. Only those scarlet nostrils... She held me tight with a thin hand, her green nails digging into the thick fabric of my sweater. I smiled in spite of myself, just with the corners of my lips, at the thought of where this sweater had been until now and how little it had in common with a woman's fingers.

Beneath a circular vault that breathed with lights—from pink to carmine, from carmine to pink—we exited onto the street. That is, I thought it was a street, but the darkness above us kept brightening, as if from a momentary dawn. Further on, long, low silhouettes glided by, like cars, but I already knew there were no cars. It had to be something else. Had I been alone, I would have walked down this wide artery, because letters shining in the distance read TO THE CENTER, but that probably didn't mean the city center at all. Anyway, I let myself be led. However this adventure was to end, I had found a guide, and I thought—this time without anger—of the unfortunate guy who now, three hours after my arrival, was surely searching for me via every infor in this station-city.





We passed several emptying establishments, window displays where groups of mannequins played out the exact same scene over and over, and I would have gladly stopped to see what they were doing, but the girl walked quickly, her heels clicking, until at the sight of a neon face with pulsing flushed cheeks, constantly licking its lips with a comically lolling tongue, she called out:

"Oh, bons! Want a bons?"

"Do you?" I asked.

"I think so."

We entered a small, shiny room. Instead of a ceiling, it had long rows of blazing little flames, like gas jets; heat billowed from above, it was probably real gas. In the walls were small alcoves with countertops; when we approached one, seats slid out from the walls on both sides; it looked as if they had grown out of it unexpanded first, like buds, flattened out in the air, then grew concave and became still.

We sat opposite each other; the girl tapped two fingers on the metal plate of the table, a little nickel hand popped out of the wall, tossed a small plate in front of each of us, and with two lightning-fast movements hurled a portion of a whitish mass onto both, which foamed, turned brown, and set, while the plate itself simultaneously darkened. The girl then rolled it up—it wasn't a plate at all—into the shape of a crepe and began to eat.

"Oh," she said with her mouth full, "I didn't even know how hungry I was!"





I did exactly as she did. The bons tasted like nothing I had ever eaten. It crunched between my teeth like a freshly baked roll, but immediately crumbled and melted on the tongue; the dark brown paste inside was sharply spiced. I decided I was going to like bons.

"More?" I asked when she had eaten hers. She smiled, shaking her head. On our way out, passing through the doorway, she put both hands into a small tiled niche for a moment—something hummed inside it. I imitated her. A tickling gale blew over my fingers; when I took them out, they were dry and clean. We then rode a wide escalator up. I didn't know if this was still the station, but I preferred not to ask. She led me into a small cabin in the wall—it wasn't very bright in there; I had the impression trains were running overhead, because the floor vibrated. It went dark for a fraction of a second, something breathed deeply beneath us, like a metal monster exhaling air from its lungs, it lit up, and the girl pushed the door open. This really must have been the street. We were entirely alone on it.





Small, clipped shrubs grew on both sides of the sidewalk; a little further on stood clustered flat, black machines; a man stepped out of the shadows, slipped behind one; I didn't see him open a door, he simply vanished suddenly, and the machine moved off with such momentum it must have flattened him against the seat; I didn't see any houses; only a roadway as smooth as a table, covered with strips of matte metal; at the intersections, suspended above the pavement, moved slit-like lights, orange and red; they looked a bit like models of military searchlights.

"Where should we take off to?" the girl asked. She was still holding my arm. She slowed her pace. A red streak crossed her face.

"Wherever you want."

"Then let's go to my place. It's not worth taking a glider. It's close."

We walked straight ahead. Still no houses in sight, and the wind running from the darkness, from beyond the shrubs, felt as if an open space stretched out here. Around the station, right in the center? It struck me as strange. It carried a faint scent of flowers, which I eagerly caught in my nostrils. Bird cherry? No, it wasn't bird cherry.
