Faith

My name is Cléo Louvent Johnson. My mother is French, my father American; I navigate effortlessly between the two languages. My mother is a statistician, my father an Egyptologist; I build myself between numbers and hieroglyphs. I grow up in an apartment filled with books in a quiet corner of the 14th arrondissement of Paris. My father, who affectionately nicknames me Cleopatra, sticks my drawings of pyramids and sphinxes to the kitchen fridge with magnets. On Saturdays, my mother takes me for a walk to the neighborhood library; on the way back, we stop to greet the grey herons in the Parc Montsouris.





I am four years old; my father is telling me a story in my bedroom. My favorite books are the ones he illuminates with his commentary. A specialist in Egyptology, passionate about Antiquity in all its forms, he narrates the misfortunes of Isis and Osiris, their struggle against Seth, but also the wanderings of Ulysses, the childish antics of the gods of Olympus; I hang on his every word when he details the exploits of Ramses II, Pericles, Alexander the Great. Each tale becomes an excuse for detours through History and its epics, teeming with characters on extraordinary quests and marvelous animals. It's soon time for sleep; my father tucks me in before turning off the light, when suddenly he senses I’m preoccupied. As he presses me a little, I finally confide: "Daddy, I want to be as famous as Céline Dion."

I have no memory of this, but my parents long took pleasure in reminding me of this anecdote, which made them laugh a lot. I sense a fondness mixed with surprise. Where did she get that from?

My parents live in the microcosm of their academic research, their publications, their conferences. They don't know who the trendy celebrities are—they would be incapable of recognizing the idols of their century if they passed them on the street. Red carpets, celebrity couples, Hollywood, private jets, tabloids: it’s an exotic country where they have absolutely no desire to vacation.





At the school gates, my parents look older than the others. My mother explains that her pregnancy didn't last nine months, but nine years. I will be their only child. Their difficulty conceiving isn't a secret; my Wikipedia page states that I was born via in vitro fertilization. For some obscure reason, this detail adds to the magic surrounding my birth: did science have to get involved to create a being as exceptional as me?

I am a little girl with short hair, well-behaved, solitary, who slips plane tree leaves into her coat pockets to make salad for her dolls. My parents hear me talking to myself in my bedroom, and notice that I always close the door to play.

They don't suspect that their darling child spends her Wednesday afternoons filling notebooks trying to come up with her star signature. I also sing in unison with my favorite stars, whose music I play on the CD player resting on the floor near my desk. I know all the lyrics by heart; I read them from the CD booklet, sitting cross-legged on my bedroom's light blue rug. I have explained my background to journalists so many times. I have recounted my past in interviews so many times. I have participated in this dangerous game so many times without understanding its gravity.

I have romanticized my life, embellished my answers to make myself seem more brilliant, more impressive. I have erased from my narrative, and gradually from my memory, the episodes that didn't fit my legend. I have manufactured false memories for myself. In search of a spicy detail or a moving anecdote, I have borrowed from other people's pasts, from my reading, from movies, until I forgot what truly belonged to me.

Memories are fragile; to be convinced of this, you only need to recount the same event multiple times. By the third or fourth time, the past begins to restructure itself. Slowly, you change the lighting, you remove certain details, you heighten the dramatic impact to captivate your audience. You turn the past into a story, and with each telling, it is the memory itself that you damage. There are anecdotes I have repeated so many times that I no longer know if they are true or false.

It is tempting to rewrite your life; I have done it a lot. The foreshadowing clues of my destiny are legion: the early interviews with my stuffed animals, my extraordinary mezzo-soprano voice, my fascination with lyrics, my musical ear, my aptitude for the piano, the encouragement of my guitar teacher, my solid foundation in music theory, my passion for the choir. It’s so much easier to trace the path in reverse.

My parents have no ambition to make me a celebrity by encouraging me to learn an instrument—we are not in Los Angeles. In my house, music is a bourgeois reflex, not an encouragement to become a global pop star.

My desire for fame doesn't come from my family, but from my flaws. All my life, I will seek the admiration of millions of strangers, compel the respect of everyone who crosses my path. The baker, the pharmacist, my kindergarten teacher, the third-floor neighbor. I have only one purpose: for everyone to acknowledge my superiority.

Well-balanced people don't need to feed on love on such a massive scale.
