Prologue





A flash of pain drills through my brain; a violent blow to my skull throws me off balance. I am two hundred meters above the void, scaling a rock face I know well. My nails scrape against the granite; my hands lose their grip. In that instant, I realize that it is over. Out of a taste for the challenge, out of pride perhaps, I have always climbed barehanded, without roping up. I knew that one day I would pay the price.

First, I see the sky. Then a hundred meters of rock face rush past in a few blinks of an eye. I can barely make out the silhouette disappearing behind the summit I was about to reach. The silhouette is familiar to me. It has just caused my fall into the void, and I am going to die. An oddly comforting thought washes over me: I am going to join my sister. She has been gone for twenty years; when I woke up this morning, I never imagined we would be meeting today.

My name is Marceau Miller; I am a bestselling novelist. Tomorrow, on my Wikipedia page, right beneath the word "Born," you will be able to read: "Died: May 16, 2021 (aged 40)." Ironically, I have spent years writing as if each day could be my last. I leave behind the most important manuscript of my life. As if a part of me had foreseen this moment, anticipating this unpredictable appointment with destiny. The Grim Reaper doesn't send a registered letter to warn you of the day and hour of your death. I leave it to them—to Sarah, and the others. They are free to do what they can with it. I owed them the truth.






My body floats in the air; I have no control over anything anymore. The ground is rushing toward me at terrifying speed. My heart aches for my children, Hermione and Benjamin, and for my wife, Sarah, with whom I've built everything.

I spot my pickup parked at the foot of the cliff, and I realize I’m going to crash right next to it. Nothing else crosses my mind. And yet, they say your whole life flashes before your eyes in a situation like this. We've all had that awful nightmare: you're falling into the void, with nothing left to catch you. You wake up drenched in sweat, your heart about to explode, with a lingering sense of unease that haunts you for hours.

I've always wondered if our brains disconnect before the fatal impact. In a few hundredths of a second, I will know for sure.

Fifty meters.

Ten meters.

Darkness.










PART ONE

THE FALL





Chapter 1

Saturday, May 15, 2021

The day before Marceau Miller's death





Settled in the rocking chair on the chalet's terrace, I look up from the novel that had completely absorbed me. My tea is still steaming. This is where I like to be, facing Lake Geneva—my lake, as if it belonged to me. In every story my husband writes, nothing foretells the storm about to strike his characters. It is often insidious; it hits them full force. I take a sip of my Saint James, a full-bodied black tea with chocolate notes, and set my cup back down. This is his twentieth book, and he still manages to surprise me. I don't have Marceau entirely figured out, but I have to hand him one thing: his success is well-deserved. He wields suspense like nobody else. Too much, even. Everywhere, all the time.

The roar of an engine pulls me from my book for good. A small boat is approaching the dock at the end of the garden. I serviced it last week; it's part of the fleet of boats Karen, my business partner, and I own. This little outboard is nothing more than a patched-up little tub we'd never dare offer our clients. But it brings our children absolute joy. Sometimes I take it to the agency, following the shoreline all the way to Yvoire. It takes fifteen minutes longer than driving, but it's just as practical as Marceau's old pickup for transporting gear.






Benjamin is at the helm of the outboard. At ten years old, he handles it well already, though he's approaching the dock a little too fast for my liking. Marceau is waving his arms. He must be telling him to slow down. I let out a breath I didn't realize I was holding as the boat glides smoothly alongside the dock. They've noticed me. All three of them wave broadly at me. I smile and wave back. Hermione, our eldest, stationed at the bow, leaps onto the landing and catches the mooring line her father tosses to her. I watch the maneuver. The stilted dock isn't very wide; its long wooden legs plunge into the depths, taking root in concrete blocks embedded in a mix of mud and green-tinged pebbles. In this season, when the sun is still so warm, the wooden planks aren't slippery; everything is fine. Benjamin cuts the engine and joins his father and sister.

I set down my book without even slipping in a bookmark. I've memorized the page number. 40, our age, Marceau's and mine. And on page 40, things are already going wrong for the characters. It's only the beginning, I'm sure of it.






I stand up and stretch. The shade of the pergola feels cool on this terrace built by my grandfather, a cabinetmaker—his last piece of work before passing away suddenly about ten years ago. This exterior woodwork of the chalet, partially covered in greenery, gives the place the feel of a vacation home. The garden stretches for almost eighty meters in length, bordered by a patch of wild woods and a hedge left equally untamed. In the warmer months, thanks to the proximity of the lake, the grass stays green for a long time. It was recently mowed and is edged along the shoreline by a fringe of gray sand and pebbles. I head toward the kids. Benjamin is already recounting his adventures as he runs toward me, but he’s too far away; I can't hear a thing. Hermione walks up with her father, laden with diving gear.

"I went down to five meters with Dad! I saw a whitefish this big!"

My boy's outstretched arms seem too short for the enormous fish he spotted in the lake. Marceau reaches me and presses a kiss to my forehead. His lips still carry the chill of the lake. Benjamin tugs at his sleeve, trying to get him to back up his claims about the discovery of the shimmering-scaled miracle. Hermione surprises him by jamming the cap he'd dropped behind him onto his head. At that moment, I feel the fragility of happiness—as fleeting as a fish vanishing into the deep. It makes me dizzy; I realize all that I hold most dear, and the pain I would feel if I lost any of them. Happiness extends its hand without warning. It can snatch it back just as fast.






"Sarah?"

Marceau flashes me a smile, then walks off to drop the diving gear further away. I catch his eye; I feel as if I can read my own feelings in it. But he knows the price and impermanence of these moments better than anyone. He has already pieced together so many fragments of shattered happiness. He reminds me of that Japanese art of repairing broken ceramics, kintsugi.

I can almost trace with my finger the golden scars his memories have left within him.





Chapter 2

Saturday, May 15, 2021

The day before Marceau Miller's death





The big hand on the living room clock hasn't quite reached 8:00 p.m. yet. Its familiar ticking fades, drowned out by a noise from outside. Daylight is fading; I can see the headlights of several vehicles filtering through the maples in the driveway. The low, distinctive rumble of Alexis's sports car engine rattles the glass of the antique bookcase I inherited from my grandmother Louise. Through the window, I soon spot him stepping out of his prized car, wearing a broad grin. Alexis, true to form. Benjamin is already circling the gleaming vehicle. Alexis grabs him and dangles him by the feet like a rag doll. I hear their bursts of laughter, and I smile too. Then they tear off in a wild race, vanishing toward the end of the garden, shouting.






Marceau comes down the staircase repainted in white, the dominant color of our interior. I love light. The large windows overlooking the lake and the reflection of the mountains allow us to never take our eyes off it. I laid out the ground floor of the chalet like a loft—a modern open-plan kitchen blended with antique elements, flowing into the living room and library area. A spacious living room, perhaps a bit cluttered with furniture, I'll admit, but comfortable and warm. Marceau joins me near the sofa. He takes me in his arms; I know he loves my smile. He returns it, never breaking eye contact.

"Are you ready?"

"Never really ready for these parties, you know that. I'd rather slip under the covers with you."

He melts my heart, the idiot. This is my favorite phase: between the moment he finishes one novel and disappears into a new one. This is when I get him back, when we are the closest.

"Then why are you so insistent on organizing this ritual every year for your book releases?"

"Ritual, Sarah, is a writer's key. A writer without a ritual is a dead writer."

"Have you gotten superstitious? Seriously, what are you afraid of? That it will end? That your readers won't like you anymore?"

"Second of all, pretty lady: a writer who isn't afraid is a finished writer. You always have to write with passion layered over fear."

"That's a beautiful line; you should save it for your next interview... Look, speak of the devil, here comes your editor. Early, as usual. He loves this party. And especially my fresh goat cheese empanadas. Pretty soon he won't even be able to see his belt."






Édouard Payet, with his good-natured paunch, lets his gaze wander over the chalet's facade, as if hesitating to come in. I know he's inspecting the whole house. The woodwork, the windows, the roof's support beams, the upstairs Juliet balcony, the terrace, the carelessly maintained greenery. He has always loved this place, the lair of his star author. He knocks on the door, and we go to welcome him. Trailing in the editor's wake, Freud, his Welsh Corgi, waddles into the small entrance hall I'd cleared for the occasion, removing the things Benjamin always leaves lying around. The little dog's claws skitter across the glossy hardwood. Short-legged and rather plump, barking incessantly, he takes after his master. Both of them are already eyeing the visible spread of food further back on the kitchen island. I must be dreaming: Édouard already has a handful of peanuts in his hand. And here I was wondering where I'd put the bowl. He clearly found it. In one swift motion, he tosses them into his mouth and swallows them without chewing, like pills. An ogre, but with a jovial smile and a disarming gift of gab.

"Sarah! Marceau! I look forward to this day every year. Nothing changes around here! And nor should it, that's my star writer's secret."

His hearty hug is as vigorous as ever, and I can feel the grains of peanut salt his fingers have left on my shoulder—Freud didn't have time to lick them off. Édouard leans in suddenly toward Marceau with a conspiratorial look.

"Did you get my little gift?"

I look at Marceau, who seems embarrassed. I have no idea what he's talking about.

"Listen, Édouard, it's magnificent, but... I don't really have the wrist for a watch, and... I'd hate to ruin it."

"A Breitling Aviator is made for you! You fly your old Savage Bobber almost every week. Marceau, live a little! You're a workaholic who doesn't treat himself enough. With everything you earn, you can afford it!"






"The house on the lake and my plane are enough. You know me—I don't want to be caught off guard if one day it all stops, just like that, suddenly, without warning."

"I could publish you with my eyes closed, Marceau."

"Never do that."

"Don't worry, I'm not senile yet. And if the text wasn't good, Marceau, rest assured, I have good ghostwriters."

Marceau's face changes imperceptibly. He has just slipped back into his own world.

"An accident with a text... with me, that would be a fatal crash, Édouard."

"Come on, I'm just teasing. I'm always eager to see what you're going to bring me, you know that."

I hold out the platter of empanadas to Édouard, while Marceau slips away quietly. Freud lifts his snout, waiting for crumbs, but Benjamin comes running up and distracts him from his goal. My son pets the dog's head and leads him off, calling to him happily.

Édouard wolfs down his final bite with undisguised satisfaction before heading out to the terrace with an almost ceremonious stride. The editor's ritual can begin, the one he always performs far from my living room, where I have little tolerance for those heavy plumes of smoke. From the inside pocket of his tweed jacket, he reverently extracts a Cohiba Behike 56, the Holy Grail for aficionados, housed in its Spanish cedar case.

His movements are precise, almost liturgical. First, he pulls out his Xikar pocket hygrometer to check the ideal moisture level of the wrapper leaf. Then, rolling the cigar between his expert fingers just beneath his nostrils, he inhales the scents of leather and precious wood. The guilloché silver cigar cutter—a gift from Marceau—makes a clean cut, releasing the first earthy notes. Édouard studies the cross-section attentively, like a sommelier scrutinizing the color of a vintage wine.






His S.T. Dupont lighter produces its characteristic blue flame. The toasting ritual begins: the flame delicately licks the foot of the cigar, prepping the tobacco for lighting. Édouard then slowly rotates the Cohiba above the flame in a perfectly calibrated choreography. The first puffs rise, carrying the promise of the complex aromas to come—notes of roasted coffee, bitter cocoa, and oriental spices.

A smile of contentment spreads across the editor's lips as he takes those initial drags, eyes half-closed, savoring the moment as one savors a moment of eternity. The impending success of Marceau's new novel is already mingling with the ribbons of smoke rising into the evening air, dancing above the calm waters of the lake.










I turn up the stereo; we don't make use of its power nearly often enough. The music fills the house and carries all the way down to the lake. Some nights, I love to shatter the silence, to let the chalet radiate joy and noise. I spot Karen dancing on the terrace with her daughter, Zoé, and mine. I hadn't even seen them arrive. Karen is wearing a burnt-orange jumpsuit with butterfly sleeves, loose-fitting and belted high at the waist. A light fabric with a plunging neckline that flatters her slender figure. Finished off with ankle-strap wedge heels that boost her to nearly six feet tall. In that moment, I regret not choosing my linen pants that show off my curves. Standing on my tiptoes, I kiss Karen on the cheeks and give her a wink as I brush her outfit; she smiles. She gets swept away by the girls again just as I ask her where Rollin, her husband, is. Between dance steps, she points a finger toward the garden. Further out on the lawn, Alexis is dragging along Marceau and Rollin, his best buddies of twenty years, ready to spark things up tonight. Alexis gets them to loosen up; glass in hand, they string together a series of suggestive hip swings. Rollin, massive and muscular, is as clumsy as ever, but he plays along. He is contemplative, naturally warm, the gentle dreamer of the group, and a walking encyclopedia when it comes to the flora and fauna on our excursions. A nonconformist through and through, he has never given up his freedom, scraping by with his food truck. From the porch, Édouard exhales thick plumes of smoke after taking a drag from his glowing cigar. His face is beaming, his eyes glued to Marceau. The truth is, in moments like this, I'm always afraid something will happen and shatter everything—just like in my husband's novels. There are false alarms, and there are real tragedies.






10:00 p.m. The automatic sprinklers kick on. I had forgotten about them; my heart leaps. I get hit by a few drops, and shouts of amusement and surprise erupt. Alexis holds Rollin back under the artificial rain, while Marceau sprints toward the shed to shut the system off. And while the three friends play to the gallery, my son tugs at my sleeve and pulls me beneath the fine, refreshing droplets on this warm evening that feels so much like summer. I sober up slightly; my heart is pounding, but I breathe—and I let go.

The magic ends as suddenly as it began. The sprinklers are shut off; the boys are drenched. Alexis strips off his clothes and tries to coax Rollin toward the lake. Rollin resists the tipsy troublemaker and lets him go. He's had enough, soaked from head to toe. Alexis, mocking, standing almost naked in the middle of the vast garden as night falls, caught in the beam of the chalet lights, shouts one last open invitation. Rollin shakes his head as Alexis sprints across the lawn and disappears with a dive into the pitch-black lake. Several seconds pass before he bursts from the depths with a noisy splash, grinning foolishly. The lapping of the water around him makes the surface shimmer, where the points of light from the chalet dance across the lake's immensity. In the distance, the Swiss shores map out an illuminated snake. The water's temperature is startling, I know it is. Even a few drinks in, you can't escape its icy bite. The most important thing is that it doesn't take the lives of the people I love; it already spat me back out once, just before I drowned. I owe it my status as an expert swimmer, with even a few medals to my name. It's just the lake and me, like the forests and mountains around us—always has been.

Rollin's clothes are clinging to his skin, revealing his stocky shoulders.

"I think I need a towel now."

"Idiot, that won't be enough," I tell him, laughing. "You know your way around; head to the bathroom and grab some clothes from Marceau's closet. They'll just be a bit tight on you."






Karen bursts out laughing with me as Rollin heads into the house. We've known each other a long time, through our husbands. Our friendship and the trust we have in one another allowed us to consider teaming up to start this boat rental agency, which is doing quite well now. A few meters from the shore, Alexis whistles and lobs his soaking-wet boxers toward the garden. Freud doesn't need to be asked twice; he catches the wet fabric in mid-air and brings it back to his master. Alexis wades toward the bank and walks backward out of the water, flashing his pale bare ass at us. Karen is the quickest to react:

"Alexis, the kids! Come on, you're crossing a line!"

Alexis continues his grandstanding, making his way to the dock, which he uses as a launchpad to dive back into the lake with exaggerated clumsiness. Benjamin starts taking off his clothes too, but I stop him dead in his tracks. Disappointed, he goes to the shore and cheers on Alexis, who tries to persuade me from afar. Waste of breath, boys.

Rollin reappears a little later, dry, in Marceau's ill-fitting clothes. He looks a little strange to me—is he tired? He has lost his smile and is striding over with a large towel to rescue his naked buddy, who has gotten out of the water and is starting to shiver. Alexis's anatomy doesn't warrant anything that large, but it will be more civilized if he drapes himself for the rest of the evening.






They step away together, toward the end of the garden, sheltered by the foliage at the edge of the woods, not far from the moored boat still bobbing in the wake of Alexis's antics. They seem to be deep in conversation. I hope Alexis isn't dragging Rollin into some new twisted scheme. What's special about these nights is that they seem to plunge us twenty years back in time, into the carefree days of our youth. Marceau emerges from the house in turn, looking serious, as if he were already somewhere else—a troubled expression I've seen on him from time to time. Why tonight? Will I ever get an answer to this mystery? The rare times I've tried to find out more about these feelings washing over him, I've found myself standing on the edge of a bottomless precipice. That is the limit of our relationship. An unspoken rule, a safety distance—for his own safety.





It's past midnight; the swimming episode has shifted the mood. The buffet is holding up against the final assaults. Freud's barking catches our attention. His hackles are raised, and he's facing the small woods adjoining the garden. Édouard whistles for him, to no avail. He finally walks over to fetch him. The woods around the chalet are full of nocturnal animals. The kids sometimes startle them when they venture into the trees.






Édouard returns. In his master's arms, tongue hanging out, Freud seems to have already forgotten everything. Édouard thanks me for the evening and reminds Marceau of his schedule for the next seventy-two hours, notably his TV appearance on the major literary show and a studio recording for a radio spot. The publicist will send him his updated schedule for bookstore signings. What I've learned over the years is that this job as a writer is nothing more than a form of parole. I share my husband with Édouard, the journalists, the readers... but also with a lot of fictional characters. If those characters could sometimes fade into the background and leave me a bit more of Marceau, that would suit me better. Closing his laptop just long enough for the machine to digest his work-in-progress isn't enough for me. But it isn't up to me, and I'm not entirely sure he decides it himself either.





By 1:45 a.m., everyone has gone home. Hermione and Benjamin are finally in their rooms, crushed by sleep. I knock softly on the door to Marceau's office, his sanctuary. I hear his muted voice just on the other side.

"Five more minutes, I'm finishing a message. I'll be right there."

In my head, I translate: half an hour, or an hour. I slip into the bathroom and swallow a Dafalgan. Exhaustion, alcohol—the headache won't be long now. I am exhausted but satisfied; the party was a success. As I slip under the sheets, the exhaustion and the slight wooziness from too much alcohol wash over me. I don't like the idea of falling asleep without my man's warmth beside me; I fight it. I need him. I fight it a little longer, but my consciousness unplugs.










Chapter 3

Sunday, May 16, 2021

The day of Marceau Miller's death





A blurry glow: 5:10 displayed on the alarm clock screen. Marceau isn't next to me. Damn writer. I frown, but my eyes slide shut. I drift back off.





8:15 a.m., a mild headache, I can tell it's a beautiful day through the shutters. The light is assaulting my eyes. Next to me, the sheets haven't even been pulled back. Did he sleep in his office or what? I pad softly down the hallway, careful not to wake Hermione and Benjamin. What mother doesn't protect her children's sleep? Marceau isn't in his office. I don't go in; I practically never set foot in there—he doesn't like it.

My memory struggles through the fog of waking up; I search my recollections, but nothing comes to mind. He should be here, with me. The living room is a mess; I pick up a few remnants of last night's party here and there, and peer out at the garden: no one. I step outside barefoot onto the terrace, facing the calm lake, untroubled by even a breeze. Our little speedboat is tied up, all alone, to the dock. The air temperature is mild, almost the same as inside the chalet. In the distance, in front of the shed, I can't make out our old pickup truck. I could have sworn it was parked there yesterday. I walk around the chalet, through the silence of the cool grass that absorbs my footsteps. I feel the cold blades tickling the soles of my feet. I approach the shed; it's wide open, as usual. The Savage Bobber is there, with its gleaming propeller and wide wings. I would have heard him taking off from the adjacent meadow anyway. Still no sign of the pickup. I spin around and cast a glance toward the woods and the path leading up to the road out front. My voice still weak from sleep, I call out to Marceau, but only a nearby bird answers with a flap of wings, rising high above the chalet. For a second, I envy its position, high above the world, and its vantage point over the lake and the mountains. Can it see Marceau from up there?






Back inside the house, I open the entryway closets. I find the sneakers, Marceau's sitting neatly next to mine, and above all, I check his climbing bag: it's empty. His climbing shoes and chalk bag are no longer there. I shudder, and then I remember: he got rid of them the day he promised me he'd stop free soloing, his thrill of climbing without ropes, without safety... and, in my opinion, without a conscience. Jackets and coats are hung on the pegs or hanging in the wardrobe. If he didn't take anything with him, he must be coming back soon. I send a text, casually reminding him that we need to coordinate our schedules for the coming days, between his promotional obligations for the novel and, on my end, the boat rentals that are non-stop this time of year. Not to mention that the kids can't exactly fend for themselves yet.





Noon. I get annoyed peeling potatoes for the french fries Benjamin is demanding. And for the tenth time, at least, I tell the kids that no, I don't know where their father is. When it comes to keeping promises, Marceau is the first to bail. Hermione was counting on him to set up her smart lighting system, and Benjamin for the complicated assembly of his new remote-controlled airplane. I advise them to negotiate for something in return. No new messages on my phone. I call for the third time, still no ringing: "Hello, you have reached Marceau Miller..." I hang up. My tone shifts with the fifth text.

*Where the hell are you?*










Chapter 4

Monday, May 17, 2021

The day after Marceau Miller's death





8:00 a.m.

I call for Benjamin one last time. Otherwise he's going to school without breakfast. Hermione is on edge. Asking her to clear the table is enough to send her storming off, grumbling and slamming her bedroom door. A normal Monday morning—well, almost normal. Usually, Marceau annoys Hermione before I get the chance to.

Last night, I fed them my first lie: Dad is away for his new novel's launch, I'd forgotten, he told me about it. I still haven't started making the rounds of phone calls to friends and acquaintances; I'm clinging, without really believing it, to the idea that Marceau is just going to show up with an explanation I hadn't thought of, to the idea that everything is going to go back to normal.





9:15 a.m.

A bitter taste in my throat—it's stress; my stomach can't handle it. I frantically search the upper bathroom cabinet for some medicine. I can't find anything, half of them are expired, I'll need to sort through them. It'll be fine. As soon as I hear from him, everything will go back to normal. Feverishly, I gather my things, trying not to forget anything for a workday that's already off to a bad start.






Arriving late at the agency, I'm not in the right headspace for the usual morning emergencies. Karen is already dealing with the first customers. Likely because of my dazed expression, she asks if everything is okay, and what I did with the rest of the weekend after Saturday night's party. And finally, I crack. I confess to her that I haven't heard from Marceau since the very end of that party. Does she have any idea where he might be? I read the bewilderment in her eyes. I must look like an idiot. She hesitates, searching for a reassuring explanation. Maybe it has something to do with his book release? It doesn't hold up; he would have left me a note, even if he just needed to isolate himself, as he sometimes does. I pace around the agency as if naively searching for him here too. Karen pours me some tea and checks the list of our available boats while I count them one by one, tied up at the agency's dock. On rare occasions, Marceau has borrowed one, just like Alexis or Rollin, for that matter. Karen calls Rollin, while I try my luck with Alexis. My heart races as every hold I cling to gives way. They don't know anything. I move on to his editor. Édouard can't think of anything better to do than remind me of the urgency of the TV appearance and the radio interviews in the coming days. Dead end after dead end, even after contacting acquaintances of acquaintances. My knuckles turn white from gripping my phone so tightly. An alarm goes off in my head. I can read the same, thinly veiled worry in Karen's eyes. She assures me she can run the agency by herself and practically pushes me out the door; I have better things to do, I need to find Marceau.






I've always been able to count on Karen. We understand each other perfectly—knowing how to laugh at the same things, keep unwarranted judgments at bay, put up with each other's flaws. True partners, with limitless trust. To the point where it's almost dizzying: she could take everything from me if she wanted to, and I'd keep my eyes closed. But truly: teaming up with her to set up the agency on the lake was one of my best decisions. The choice to be independent, too, and in line with my values. Finding my place, on the lake, surrounded by forests and mountains—my stronghold, my roots.

But Marceau is also my roots. Leaving without warning, without answering his phone, especially right when his new book is launching, is abnormal. Like being asked to walk forward in the dark, with nothing to hold onto. My heart leaps at every text and every call I receive—more and more of them now—from our circle of friends, as worried as I am, asking for news, sometimes suggesting ideas... And certainly forming their own idea of it, but in silence.





3:20 p.m.

I'm back from a driving trek along the shores of the lake, from Yvoire to Meillerie, all the way to the Swiss border. I stopped everywhere—the restaurants, the sports clubs, all the places Marceau frequents, some very isolated, when he wants to write far away from everything. Take action, I need to take action. I need help. I park in front of the gendarmerie in Thonon-les-Bains, the closest and largest police station around.





I have a knot in my stomach as I walk in: what the hell am I doing here? It has come to this, carrying my anxiety like luggage. They are my last resort. A young officer listens to my distress and takes note of everything I tell him. He tries to be reassuring and convinces me that there is nothing alarming for the moment. He starts asking me questions about my marriage, any conflicts, any infidelities there might have been... My face hardens. I remind him that my husband has disappeared and that I'm here precisely because I have absolutely no explanation. He asks me to calm down. Did I raise my voice?

Finally, a certain Captain Robin Delmas introduces himself. An arrogant guy who talks down to me. In his office where he receives me, I have to go over everything again, step by step, re-explaining, once more—the party, the alcohol, yes, no other substances, of course not! I'm having a hard time controlling the volume of my voice. On the verge of losing my temper, I stammer. Who does he think we are? Among celebrities, illicit substances are common, he maintains. Now I'm genuinely pissed. No, Marceau doesn't touch coke. Our first and last joints date back to high school. His condescending stare is unbearable. Marceau didn't leave a note, nothing is missing from the house? No. No warning signs, no particular changes in his habits? All these questions I've already gone over in my head. I let out yet another terse "No," short of breath. He left with the pickup, that's all.





Delmas concludes that since Marceau has been missing for less than forty-eight hours, he can't initiate a search for a forty-year-old adult in full possession of his faculties, with no specific conflicts or troubling reasons. And there you go, that's it. He stares at me, with an almost self-satisfied smirk to boot. I feel the urge to punch him rising up inside me. Clearly, I'm the only one who understands that nothing about this situation is normal. I feel like no matter what I say, he won't hear a word of it. Like hitting a brick wall, my anger rebounds right back in my face. I am escorted out; I'm supposed to keep him informed, case closed.

I walk out feeling even worse than when I arrived, having trouble breathing, with a sense of emptiness, of abandonment. Misunderstood, despised, but no less determined. He's going to be seeing me again, Delmas, he can be sure of that. My brain is running in overdrive. There have to be solutions... And then, a flash. An idea. I climb into my car and speed off.





Chapter 5

Monday, May 17, 2021

The day after Marceau Miller's death





Thonon-les-Bains is in my rearview mirror. Captain Delmas's infuriating voice is still echoing in my ears, as if he were hitched to my bumper. I want to speed up to leave him far behind me, but traffic on the main road is backed up. I turn off onto the secondary roads, closer to the lake. It won't save me any time, but at least I can do some real driving. The view of the lake reveals itself through gaps in the greenery—ash, beech, and maple trees. The lake, at least, doesn't betray me. I speed along the narrow roads between houses with tile roofs weathered by time, sometimes tinged with the soft green of moss. The homes with their timber cladding blend into the scenery. I'm finally nearing our house. A familiar landscape, at once comforting and cruelly empty. My heart beats to the rhythm of the final turns, swinging between the aching hope of finding Marceau and the icy dread of his absence.






Arriving at the chalet, I spot a vehicle parked in front of the house. The trees in the hedge barely let me make it out. My heart is pounding against my ribs. I accelerate up the dusty driveway, a mix of crushed shells and dirt where the potholes and rocks show no mercy to tires or shock absorbers. In my wake, a brown cloud rises, dusting the surrounding foliage white. Maybe Marceau has finally come home? I have no room for anger, so deeply am I consumed by anxiety. A tingling sensation runs through my body. Fear is feeding on me, as if everything is slipping through my fingers. Like a strange premonition right before something happens.

A figure suddenly steps out of the woods and waves frantically at me. I realize I'm driving way too fast. Instinctively, fueled by adrenaline, my right foot slams on the brake pedal; my tires skid for several meters. I skid to a halt next to the man who, a little shaken, waves the air and dust away from his face. It's old Reynaud. I realize then that the SUV is his. Despite my dashed hopes, seeing him here comforts me. Yves Reynaud knows me as well as Marceau does—since we were teenagers. More importantly, he's a retired gendarme, solid as a rock, who doesn't look his seventy years. We don't see each other often, but with him, it's as if time simply swallows up the gaps and the distance. He is always there, faithful, loyal. Paternal. He walks around my car and climbs into the passenger seat. The engine is idling; I can't manage to speak, my face is a wreck. He leans over and pulls me into a hug. I suddenly feel overwhelmed, biting my lip so I won't cry. On his shirt, I can smell the summer heat and the scent of the forests, just like on Marceau when he comes back from his long hikes. Reynaud was born here; this region is his. I cling to him as a godsend.

"Tell me everything from the beginning, Sarah. What's going on with Marceau?"






I see in him the concern of a friend, wanting to reassure me, ready to do anything to help, but also the instincts of the former gendarme, who is far more worried. He, at least, is listening to me. The old fox knows this area like the back of his hand. I tell the story, once again. This is always how it goes in these kinds of cases. Just the word "case" scares me. This all throws us twenty years into the past. My brain refuses to remember. I know Reynaud is thinking about it too. I pull forward more calmly and park in front of the house.

We get out; he stays right on my heels, watching me. I lead him toward the garden shed; I hadn't thought to inspect it yet.

"What are you looking for in here, Sarah?"

I open the plastic bins and see our scuba gear. I rummage through the clutter on the makeshift workbench that serves as a desk. I know Marceau comes in here from time to time. I only find papers, tools; nothing catches my eye. I slam my fist down, sending a set of screwdrivers flying. Reynaud pulls down some wooden crates resting on the upper shelves. I tell him to be careful, they're full of nails, screws, tacks. Reynaud handles them carefully; they're heavy. One of the bins is empty, but before he puts it back, I rub my fingers along the bottom: a fine film clings to the pads of my index and middle fingers. I smell it. It's chalk. The powder you coat the inside of your hands with to keep them dry and secure your grip while climbing. Reynaud catches my eye.

"He promised me he'd stop."






A sob interrupts me and stops me from going on. Reynaud takes me in his arms. Now, I want to cry, but the tears won't come, as if shedding a single tear would confirm that the worst has happened. I'm scared, as if I already know. All it took was a fine trace of powder, barely perceptible. No secret can ever stay truly hidden. I feel Reynaud's heart thudding in his chest. He places his calloused hands on my shoulders; his expression shifts. Then he lowers his gaze before speaking. I search for his eyes; he raises them to meet mine.

"Listen, Sarah, I know Marceau well, we happen to see each other from time to time."

"What do you mean? Without me? Why? He never told me about that."

I pull out of his grasp.

"We're all driven by old demons."

My blood runs cold. The door to my worst memories creaks on its hinges. Flashes make me flinch. I reject these images. This pain is like the needle of the syringe I dreaded so much as a child—I would thrash around, screaming. She was my best friend; it was twenty years ago. She was Marceau's sister. Born the same year as him, less than a year apart, we were inseparable.

I stare at Reynaud for a long time before asking him:

"You're talking about Jade, aren't you?"

"That was my investigation back then. I don't know where we screwed up..."

I feel as if the ground is dropping out from under my feet.

"Where is Marceau? Speak, damn it!"

"I don't know, Sarah, I don't know... but I've happened to spot him near..."

He lets a heavy silence fall, as if to let me digest it. But I don't want to waste time; I want to know.

"Near the damn place where Jade disappeared, up in the mountains, right?! Why stir all that up again? Do you know how much that hurts?"

It's all flooding back. I'm not over it either.










Chapter 6

Monday, May 17, 2021

The day after Marceau Miller's death





Reynaud's SUV devours the mountain road heading toward the French-Swiss border, less than forty kilometers away. On the narrow D25, between copses and forests, we pass through peaceful, flower-filled villages, where the houses with their exposed timbers and wide roofs look as if they've been there forever, like something off our grandparents' postcards. At Sciez, we continue onto the D1005, the main artery leading to Switzerland. Reynaud speeds up and passes one car after another on the stretch of road that goes through Évian. He is mute, intensely focused. At Évian, we hug the lake; on the horizon lie the snow-capped peaks of the Alps. As we progress, the hillsides, with houses clinging to them, impose their dominance. With every passing kilometer, the terrain around us transforms into mountains. We pass through Lugrin, then cross the border at Saint-Gingolph. I shoot a text to Karen, asking if she can take care of Benjamin until I get back. The exact place we are going, I haven't been back to in years. Still, Marceau and I do occasionally hike in the surrounding area. We have always shared this irrepressible need for nature, forests, mountains, and escape. But right now, the vastness surrounding us suddenly gives me vertigo.










The road gives way to an increasingly rocky path. Jostled in the cabin, I hold on tight. The tires bite into the mountain as we gain altitude; my ears pop. On a track narrowing like the source of a stream, we crawl along at walking pace, past the Planche parking area—a tiny patch of dirt and rocks, isolated in the middle of the trees, the last spot still accessible by car. Reynaud continues along the increasingly rough trail. Then, on a curve a few hundred meters from Switzerland, he cuts through the trees. Branches snap beneath the tires. He pushes the SUV's engine, staying in first gear, until we can't go any further and come to a halt. We are at an altitude of nearly 1,800 meters, about a forty-minute walk from Lake Neuteu. We exchange a look; Reynaud takes out a canteen, stuffs it into a backpack, and grabs his walking sticks. It's the first leg, the easiest one. Around us is the forest. Not just any forest—not for me, and not for Reynaud. My heart is racing; I exhale and start walking. Reynaud is already dozens of meters ahead. This ascent masked as a hike shakes me to my core. I watch where I'm stepping, avoiding the holes, the pitfalls of the mountain. I start to feel the heat in my thighs and calves. A sheen of sweat on my face. Then it appears before us: Lake Neuteu, surrounded by jagged peaks, forests as far as the eye can see, and oppressive memories. I chase away the flashes blinding my mind, but the dread persists, clings to me. The veneer of the years has never truly covered up the tragedy buried deep within my soul.





I arrive first, galvanized by fear. I've overtaken Reynaud, who has fallen behind. My gaze scans the perimeter of this isolated mountain lake like a laser. Nearly two hundred meters long and barely a hundred wide, bordered by short grass that races up toward the peaks before fading into raw rock. Completely deserted, not a soul in sight. The only truly remarkable viewpoint for a loner like Marceau looms right there, about a kilometer away. The highest rocky peak in the landscape, a monster of verticality made of stone and defiance. Not a walk in the park; more like a route for an experienced mountaineer. Reynaud arrives at my side. I think of the chalk found in the garden shed; a shiver runs down my spine. Reynaud names it for me.

"The Dent du Vélan."
