Comforting his friend gave Valentyn even more strength. He had opened his notebook and written to him that everything would be fine, there was no reason to worry, and they would surely be back before dark, since the bus was soon going to give up the ghost. To show off, without being certain how to spell the word, he had written that a connecting rod was about to give out. Without hiding his satisfaction, when the driver let out a string of curses after thick smoke billowed from the hood, he had leaned over a blank page and written in large letters: *They're screwed, we'll be home for dinner.* But on this point, Valentyn was mistaken.



As soon as morning broke, Veronika and Lilya left their house to head to the town hall, where the Russian command had set up headquarters. Veronika had known some spectacular rages, but never one like the one fueling her on the way. So invading a country, bombing cities, and decimating entire populations to serve the megalomania of a declining dictator wasn't enough for them? Now they had to kidnap kids? Was this what these conquerors had been reduced to, commanded by incompetent generals, with mercenaries forming the battalions of a dishonorable army?





The soldier standing guard outside the town hall doesn't have much bravery left. He doesn't know what the hell he's doing in this godforsaken place so far from home. A young man barely twenty-five years old, happy to be among those who took a small town without having to fight any major battles, and even happier to be alive. He points out the office of his superior in charge of the occupied population. The future mayor of the town, once Ukraine is liberated, he explains.

"Excellent news, then you'll be going home soon," Veronika retorts dryly, pulling her daughter along.

They stride down the corridor with an almost military march, their heels clicking so loudly that those who cross their path don't dare ask where they're going. Lilya spots the commander's office; Veronika takes a deep breath and opens the door.

"Where is my son?" she asks, stepping into the room.

The officer, who had been dozing, his head slumped on a desk, jumps and stares at the two women.

"Who is your son?" he asks with a yawn.

"Valentyn Khodova!"

The man sits up, sighs, turns around, and grabs a binder from a shelf containing the names of fighters captured or killed at the front.

"Don't pretend to look for his name. He's nine years old; he's one of the two children you kidnapped from their school yesterday at noon. Are you going to claim you don't know about this?"





His expression changes. He was the one who, a week earlier, had received the order to carry out this operation, the point of which had initially seemed questionable to him. As if he didn't have enough problems to deal with. But the call had come directly from Moscow—more importantly, from the Kremlin. Rare were the officers of his rank to receive such an honor. The call had only lasted a few minutes, but he would never forget the soft, kindly voice of the Presidential Commissioner for Children's Rights, paying no heed to the power conferred by her closeness to the president. Instead of using a threatening tone, which his superiors had accustomed him to, she had carefully explained how important his task was. What nobler cause than protecting orphans and minors from the dangers imposed on them by the Ukrainian rebellion? People who accuse Russia of the worst evils, yet are incapable of caring for the future of their offspring. Saving orphans was a priority, she had repeated before hanging up.

*Orphans*, a detail he had perhaps forgotten to specify to the unit he'd dispatched. But come to think of it, why would he have, since he'd been assured that the facility in question was an orphanage?

Yet this mother is demanding her child back; his intelligence sources might not be as reliable as all that. He will find the person responsible for this blunder. Thinking about it again, it seems to him that the Commissioner for Children's Rights had also mentioned vulnerable children. Enough to reassure himself as to the perfect legitimacy of his mission, since the entire town is living in precarity.





"That dilapidated building, you call that a school?" he says, snapping his ledger shut.

"You bombed us, we live most of the time without water and electricity. Are you going to reproach us for our cracked walls and blown-out windows?"

"The fact remains that children need care that your institutions cannot provide, let alone in this region. We are showing infinite kindness and spending precious money to help them. You should be grateful and thank us; the Russian government's mission is to protect minors, regardless of their nationality."

Veronika is about to ask him how many children have perished under the bombs his government has dropped on hundreds of schools, maternity wards, hospitals, or public parks, how many others are now orphans, but reason forces her to stay quiet.

"My son doesn't need care, and certainly not yours!" she replies in an icy voice. "On the other hand, the civilians your men are shooting at point-blank range are in dire need of mine."

"Well, then get back to your work," the officer shoots back.

"Where is my son?" Veronika insists again, ready to commit murder.

The man steps toward the window and looks out at the street.

"On his way to a reception center. Our specialists will question him, and after evaluating his condition, they will decide what is best for him."

"My son won't answer your questions. He can't speak."

"And you said he didn't need any care? What kind of parent are you?"

Lilya sees the fire burning in her mother's eyes. She grabs her hand to remind her that she is by her side, and takes the initiative to address the officer.

"We don't want to cause you any trouble, just find my brother, know he's okay, and find out where you've taken him. Please."





The teenager's restraint surprises the officer. He tells himself she will be an easier person to placate; perhaps she will even help him convince her mother to leave him alone.

"Your brother is fine, and we haven't kidnapped him. We've only sheltered him from the fighting, to offer him the childhood he has a right to, healthy food that can no longer be found here and which he needs to grow, an environment that allows him to properly pursue his studies. Even during a special operation like the one we're conducting right now, don't you agree?"

"A special operation?" Veronika bristles. "It's a war you're waging against us!"

"That false label is punishable by eight years in prison! We aren't waging war on anyone, we're liberating Ukraine!" the officer objects, outraged.

"And back home, is mentioning rain when it's raining also punishable by prison? Do you call a thunderstorm a drizzle, a mist?"

"When calm is restored and his safety is guaranteed, we'll send him back to you, I promise," he says in a voice that clearly suggests his patience is at an end.

Veronika's sheer nerve has made him lose his swagger, to the point that he feels pathetic; his superiority is slipping through his fingers, like the sand of the Odesa beaches he used to play with as a kid. His own mother must be a decade or so older than this woman, and if she had faced him in such a circumstance, she would have given him a hiding and kicked him out of the room by the seat of his pants. This time, he won't let himself be pushed around; he's going to show who's boss.





"One more word and I'll have you arrested!" he says, adopting a solemn tone. "Your daughter needs you, and if you were no longer able to care for her, I'd be forced to put her in a safe place, too."

"Where is this center?" asks Lilya.

"I don't know yet; we have many of them, there are so many kids in precarious situations in your country. Come back and see me in three days; I'll know more and I'll tell you where your son is—maybe I'll even be able to give you news of him, provided you don't cause any trouble in the meantime."

Terrified at the idea of being detained in turn, and hoping the officer will keep his promise, Lilya grabs her mother by the arm and begs her to obey. They won't get anything more today, except to make their situation worse.
