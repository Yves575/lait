There was a goddess. In an octagonal room, artfully contorting her voluptuous body.

"Um, hello."

"So it's you. Welcome."

Her voice was surprisingly husky. Somewhere between a mezzo-soprano and an alto, leaning toward the alto, if anything. Assuming she ever sang in a choir, that is.

When I tried to hand over the letter of introduction, the curator quickly took it and held it open before the goddess. I instantly froze. How had I been planning to hand it to a goddess in the first place? She, however, didn't seem to mind, and eventually spoke.

"Rika Horauchi, yes? May I call you Hora?"

I nodded. I was taken aback by the abrupt declaration of a nickname, but it wasn't as if I had a preferred name in mind anyway. The goddess narrowed her eyes. Her pupil-less eyes.

"I'd like you to call me Venus, not Aphrodite. I might look like a figure from Greek mythology, but I was born in Rome. Though I suppose 'Venus' is the English pronunciation."

I nodded again. Venus narrowed her eyes further, her sculpted smile practically brimming over.

"Splendid. Tell me, am I speaking too fast? Does my style match what you learned at university? If it's different, please don't hesitate to let me know."

"No... you're very easy to understand."

"Like a textbook example," I added after a moment's hesitation, wiping my palms on my raincoat. I was sweating profusely.

"I'm glad. Well, I suppose I am something of an example. I come from a time before Latin branched off into Italian and French, an era when no one could have imagined the Roman Empire splitting."





Venus laughed aloud. Her bare white shoulders and her smooth stomach, clad in a thin layer of fat, did not twitch an inch. Apparently, the rule was that her marble body could not move from the neck down.

The part-time job was by no means a bad deal. It was just a rather unheard-of line of work.

It was introduced to me by a professor whose lecture I had taken only once. Getting off at that station for the first time in a few years, I passed through a roundabout as chaotic as a forest fire, went through the campus gates, and walked up the hill, passing girls in matching pajama-like clothes. Upon entering the professor's office in the North Building, my eyes were drawn to an old, pale green carpet. As I stared at the carpet pile, which expressionlessly absorbed the cheers leaking from the courtyard—freshmen, perhaps?—a letter of introduction was held out to me. An envelope printed with the university's name. When I looked up, a familiar face stood there.

My hands took it, but remained suspended in midair.

"I'm sorry, I'm not confident I can do this."

"In terms of conversational level, there is no one more qualified than you. At least, among humans currently alive."

The professor seemed to be talking to someone behind me.

"Just think of it as a sort of tutoring. It's easier than that, really. All you have to do is talk."

"But she's practically a native speaker, and besides—"





Besides, the actual "talking" part... But the rest of my words fell soundlessly against the yellow vinyl of my raincoat. Like the spray of a park fountain at closing time. While I was still inhaling and pursing my lips, the professor began to hop lightly, as if clearing invisible water from his ears, and eventually turned to the mirror to adjust his jacket. Apparently, the conversation was over. Left with no choice, I departed the university. The girls in pajamas were eating identical sandwiches on a bench in front of the gates.

The museum was a short bus ride away. Walking from the stop, all that caught my eye were the petals of white magnolias; the houses and shops were buried in their bone-like whiteness. Checking and rechecking the map the professor had given me, I finally found the building. With its nearly windowless concrete walls and bronze doors, it looked visibly dilapidated—less a place where cultural properties and historical artifacts from all ages and cultures were gathered, and more like an abandoned student dormitory. Passing the "Closed Today" sign, I made my way to the back door, took out my smartphone, and dialed the designated number. The door opened on the second ring, and I was ushered inside. Like a members-only party.





Yet this place did not seem to welcome visitors. Especially living ones. Perhaps to preserve the exhibits, the interior was as chilly as a morgue. The smell of chemicals had seeped into the floors, the walls, every possible surface. The chandelier ensconced in the vaulted ceiling and the dainty milk-glass lamps lining the hall all kept their eyes quietly downcast in the darkness today. Walking past exhibits of fossils and pottery in glass cases, I noticed flyers for children's events and local flea markets lined up in the rack by the front entrance, but would they really happen? Would the dates written on these sleepy flyers ever truly arrive somewhere in the world, with children gathering for science experiments, or old sweaters and audio equipment being sold along a shrine's approach?

The only moving thing in this faded, calendar-like field of vision was the curator walking ahead of me.

"Watch your step."

He turned around, as if predicting I would trip over my hem on the stairs. This curator, whose name was Hashibami, had incredibly well-defined features. I couldn't tell his age, but his youthful figure in a white shirt and slim pants possessed a beauty closer to an artisanal object like a vase or lacquerware than a human being. I clutched the letter of introduction in my pocket. I was not good with handsome men. Especially handsome men who were kind, or seemed kind.





But if a scholar were to assert that light exerts a gravitational pull, my left hand would gladly volunteer to help prove it. When the door at the top of the stairs opened, I was involuntarily drawn to the arched windows. Unnoticeable from the exterior, the center of the building was hollowed out, filled with trees and a small pond; the corridor wrapping around and looking down on this modest courtyard was filled with a transparent brightness. Pointing to the bouquet reliefs on the pillars, Hashibami explained:

"This building was donated; it was originally the residence of a local wealthy man. The doors are closed today, but his collection is exhibited on the second floor."

An orchestral melody spilled out from one of the exhibition rooms. The smooth upward leaps of an oboe, a timpani roll like a wooden mallet unearthing old memories. Passing a door carved with seashell motifs, a tepid scent of the tide drifted out, and the sound of waves could be heard. There was also a room where the hem of ivory lace peeked out from the gap under the door. A side of the museum I had never known was rejoicing in its closed day.

And that room was at the very back. Hashibami selected a key from a heavy-looking bunch, inserted it, and the door opened with a click. Ignoring the yellow raincoat vaguely reflected in the corridor's glass windows, I strained my eyes toward what lay beyond the door.

"Right this way, please."

Apparently, this was my workplace. Shifts were once a week, no experience required, though conversational-level Latin was a must. The job: serving as a conversation partner for an ancient Roman statue of Venus.





I looked around the exhibition room anew. Carefully sliding my irises to the corners of my eyes, inch by inch, so Venus wouldn't notice.

It was a room straight out of a myth. With light constantly pouring in through the domed skylight, they stood on their pedestals as if suspended like constellations. Hera glaring this way, Artemis accompanied by a hound, Minerva clad in armor. Eight goddesses of Greek and Roman mythology. Was it ancient Greek, or perhaps some other language? Their bloodless white lips, left to their boredom, wove lacelike patterns in the air. And at the end of the shadows cast by the overlapping of those loquacious patterns, in the innermost recess of the octagon, she was there.

"Oh, pardon me," Venus said. In contrast to her seemingly frozen shoulders, her jaw moved quite smoothly.

"Hashibami, fetch a chair for Hora. We can't have her standing."

The curator brought over what looked like a guard's chair from the corner of the room. Then, as he tried to return to the goddess's side, the alto voice declared without a moment's delay, "Thank you, you may leave us."

"But..." His Latin had a somewhat desperate ring to it.

"It's fine, I'll call you if I need anything," the goddess continued. "And close the door. Make sure of it."

Hashibami cast a glance at me, then eventually headed for the exit, bowed, and closed the door. As his hard footsteps faded away, she said, "Sit." I sat in the chair, looked up, and quickly looked down again. It was exactly the right height to meet the eyes of the slightly downward-looking goddess.





She was undeniably the goddess of love and beauty. Arching her voluptuous body into a magnificent S-curve atop the pedestal, she smiled with a face that was beautiful, yet carefully stripped of any individuality that makes a human face distinct. Her perfectly symmetrical face, devoid of smile lines or crow's feet, had a spoonful of resignation pre-carved into its happiness, making her look all the more elegant and mystical. Her garments, whose folds flowed magnificently but would never sway, seemed utterly lacking in any resolve to cover and protect her body as clothing should; they concealed neither her ample breasts nor her rounded hips, which unsettled me. Along with my own uncurvaceous body. As I shifted slightly, the goddess asked:

"Does the chair not suit you?"

"It's fine."

I answered reflexively, trying to muster as pleasant a smile as possible. But my answer was apparently insufficient. Venus asked again, like checking the pockets of a child caught stealing:

"Hora, is that chair comfortable?"

"Yes, it's fine."

"Tell me, did you attend some sort of school for ending conversations with the absolute minimum number of words? Please, tell me: is that chair truly comfortable? For you?"

For the first time, I became aware of the depth of the chair. Then the position of my spine, and how my heels were hovering slightly off the floor. As if tracing an X-ray with my finger.

"The seat might be a bit high," I said. Then, thinking my answer was still too brief, I added, "Because my legs are short."

"Thank you for telling me about yourself. I wouldn't know, you see, having never sat in a chair myself. Now, ring that bell over there and call Hashibami."





I approached the curtains as instructed, found a small golden bell, and rang it hesitantly. Her lips, which knew neither dryness nor chapping, rippled.

"I don't yet know the length of your legs, but for now, there is no need to speak of yourself that way."

The goddess smiled. My body stiffened. Like a sculpture.

As footsteps approached and the door opened, Venus instructed the curator to bring a new chair. Thanking him for coming so promptly, and accurately specifying the dimensions of the chair she wanted, Venus acted like an exemplary boss. If she were ever to work outside a museum, that is.

Waiting a beat after the sound of the door closing, Venus spoke again. "So."

"Thank you for coming today. You really are fluent in Latin. When Hashibami asked your professor, I heard you were the very first one he recommended. He said you were exceptionally bright."

"That's not... true," I exhaled thinly, as if trying to escape her solid white eyes.

"I say it because it is." The goddess's words flowed without hesitation. "I heard you've already graduated from university, but are you in grad school now, or something?"

"Grad school? No, right now I'm just doing, well, some minor tasks, sort of..."

I tried to catch my words. The words I needed to string together. But the more desperately I chased them, the more effortlessly they vanished, or pitifully dropped away, leaving me to fall silent inside my raincoat. Half-hoping I would be fired from this part-time job. Venus, however, murmured, "Minor tasks sound nice," and began explaining the job.





The job description and conditions were no different from what I had heard from the professor. The job was to converse with her. Shifts were on Mondays, the museum's closed day. If Monday was a public holiday and the museum was open, then the shift would be the following day. Transportation expenses covered. A three-month probationary period.

"It's no problem if you need to take a day off, as long as you let us know two days in advance. Of course, depending on your health or the weather, you can even let us know on the day of."

I nodded. In my student days, I had a part-time job as a tutor for just six months. The family I was assigned to was incredibly strict about punctuality. On a day when the trains were stopped by snow, I frantically transferred between buses to get to the elementary schooler's house, losing feeling in my toes from the coldness of my wet socks. Yet when I rang the doorbell at the appointed time, his mother told me right there at the door that she wanted to reschedule the lesson, saying, "It's a rare snow day, so I want to let him play." I stood there for a while, watching the student and his pet golden retriever, clad in matching, high-quality Montbell down jackets, running around a silvery yard that looked about the size of two tennis courts. Compared to that, this job seemed to have far better conditions. At least the goddess wasn't likely to change her plans depending on the weather. Nor were her marble legs likely to suddenly step down from their pedestal.

But I was still looking for a reason to quit this job. I felt out of place. The goddess was beautiful and so exposed, whereas I was wearing too many layers. Always.





I wiped the sweat oozing on my forehead. Before I knew it, my yellow hood had slipped down over my eyes.

Suddenly, Venus let out a small yawn. "Tell me, Hora." Her alto voice was muffled.

"Are you a deep sleeper?"

"Average, I think," I answered, looking down. Trying not to look too much at how her perfectly balanced mouth contorted.

"That's nice. I'm always a light sleeper. If I had a healthy stomach and intestines, the first thing I'd want to swallow would be sleeping pills. Enough so that I'd never have to wake up again."

For the first time, I looked at her exhibit label. "Aphrodite of Pariano. Roman copy of a Greek sculpture. 1st–2nd Century." Two millennia had raced past soundlessly, turning into bottomless dust and the faint heat of human bodies, an immense number of days and nights flashing by. This passage of time adorned the naked goddess more elegantly than any ornament, and simultaneously wrapped her in an unapproachable solitude.

Footsteps were approaching from beyond the exhibition room door. "Listen," Venus lowered her voice. As if hiding a candle's flame from something massive.

"No painting or sculpture can match the beauty of a single night's dream. Because there, you can become someone else, go somewhere else. I can escape my endless flow of time."

Will you come with me? Her teasing words abruptly brushed against my bare skin. Inside my raincoat. Their meaning left with nowhere to go.





Before I knew it, I had signed and stamped my seal on the employment contract Hashibami had brought along with the chair. My wages would be paid by bank transfer on the tenth of the following month.

Ah, someone crossed it.

I stopped involuntarily. From the rusted stairs of my apartment building, I could see a bridge. A bridge like the fine bones of a sardine, which almost no one ever crossed. As I gazed at it, the frosted glass window behind me slid open a crack. I drew in air through the gap in my hood. The warm scent of cooking, and the savory premonition lurking within it.

When I found this apartment, I applied to move in immediately. It was incredibly old, and standing out of place in an area lined with small factories, this two-story building had the worst sunlight imaginable. But the rent was cheap, and the ivy covering its white walls had a certain charm, depending on how you looked at it. Separate bath and toilet, a two-burner stove, a washlet toilet, and for some reason, the room even came with a landline phone.

One unexpected thing was that it also came with a landlady. Attached to the building, or rather, to my daily life.

I ran down the stairs and opened the door. It wasn't locked. Stepping over the cardboard boxes piled in the entryway and pushing aside the bead curtain, my eyes fell on Seriko-san peering into a pot, enveloped in the lukewarm smell of boiled onions that hovered there year-round. Right next to an oven mitt that was browning fragrantly under the incandescent light, just about to burst into a small flame.
