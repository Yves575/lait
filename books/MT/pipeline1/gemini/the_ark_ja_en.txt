1 The Ark

Part 1

The seven of us veered off the walking trail near the national highway and pushed our way into the woods. Making our way along a mountain path, stepping over rotting logs and fallen leaves, we passed through an unfamiliar clearing overgrown with dry grass.

By the time we reached an old-looking wooden bridge spanning a ten-meter-deep ravine, the sun had already disappeared behind the mountains.

With his thick arms, Ryuhei shook the bridge's railing of assembled logs.

Hearing the bridge creak, he grimaced, twisting his deeply chiseled, wrestler-like face. Then he turned to Yuya beside him and said,

"Hey, are we seriously crossing this? You never said anything about this. Isn't it gonna collapse?"

"Nah, it'll be fine. I crossed it before and it wasn't that dangerous. We're totally good. Look."

Yuya took a step onto the bridge, spread his arms, and swayed his body to show us.

Besides, there was no other way. The six of us had no choice but to follow Yuya as he led the way.

Once we were across the bridge, I pulled my phone from my windbreaker pocket and checked the time: 4:48 PM.

Hana, dressed in loud neon hiking gear, saw me holding my phone and quickened her pace, coming up beside me. Holding up her own phone in her right hand, she asked,

"Hey, Shuichi, do you have a signal?"

"No, no service for about an hour now."

"Oh. Same here. So I guess this means we aren't making it back to the villa today, right?"

No one answered. Everyone knew that was impossible.

Past the bridge, the terrain opened up into a desolate field surrounded by steep mountains. After we walked a few hundred paces further, Yuya let out a shout.

"Here we go! I see it. Just a little further. It's right there."

Having been subjected to our glares of dissatisfaction and distrust the whole way, Yuya’s voice was tinged with relief. However, the rest of us still couldn't see anything resembling the entrance to a building.

This had all started that morning. After we had spent some time out on the lake in boats, Yuya brought up an idea:

"There's this super interesting place within walking distance from here. You guys wanna check it out? It's a massive underground building deep in the mountains. Looks like it was used for some sketchy stuff back in the day, but I bet nobody even knows about it anymore."

We had been staying at a villa in Nagano Prefecture owned by Yuya's father since yesterday.

Yuya was a friend from college, and he was the one who had organized the trip. It was a small reunion for the six of us who used to hang out during our student days.

It had been two years since I’d seen everyone. Yuya, who used to sport dyed blond hair and black earrings, had stopped dyeing his hair and now wore only the earrings, a look I still hadn’t quite gotten used to.

Having a few reservations about this trip, I had brought along my cousin, making seven of us staying at the villa.

An underground building deep in the mountains. Even hearing this description, none of us could quite picture it.

An underground building sounded like a massive undertaking, let alone an exceptionally large one. Who in the world would construct such a thing deep in the mountains, and what for? It was hard to believe, but Yuya claimed he had actually been there and seen it for himself about six months ago.

It sounded intriguing enough, and since he said it wasn't far, we figured we might as well go check it out.

However, contrary to what he had told us, no matter how far we walked, we never seemed to reach this underground building. Yuya, who had breezily suggested it would only be an easy twenty- or thirty-minute walk, spent the whole time anxiously glaring at the map on his phone.

Naturally, the underground building wasn't on the map. Yuya said he had dropped a pin on his map app when he'd visited before, but apparently, the location he'd saved was way off. After wandering around lost, by the time we finally figured out where it actually was, the sun was already starting to set.

"So, Yuya? You're planning for us to spend the night in that underground building, right? Since we don't have time to head back. Is that really okay? Didn't you say it was some seriously sketchy place?"

"I said it might have been used for sketchy stuff *back in the day*. Like, a long time ago. It's totally fine to just crash there for a bit. There's no way anyone's there. It's basically just urban exploration."

Ryuhei and Yuya walked on ahead, leaving the rest of us about ten meters behind.

It had been like this the whole way here. Speaking as if he represented all of us, Ryuhei had been constantly complaining to our guide, Yuya.

Observing Ryuhei’s demeanor, Mai, who was walking to my right, shot me a troubled, almost placating smile. In the gathering dusk, her long-lashed eyes stood out all the more clearly against her pale face.

I tried to respond, but the thought that Ryuhei would surely hear me kept me from speaking. Mai didn't seem to expect an answer anyway; she quickly looked away, as if trying to avoid being caught by Ryuhei.

Looking back, I saw Sayaka, who had been lagging a bit, jogging to catch up with us. Her warm brown hair was tied up in a bun, and sweat beaded her forehead.

Sayaka voiced what had apparently been bothering her for a while.

"Um, what's the bathroom situation like in this underground building? And for sleeping, are we just going to use our backpacks as pillows and lie on the floor? Is everyone okay with that?"

Yuya hadn't given us any sort of hotel brochure rundown like that. We hadn't come with the intention of staying the night.

My cousin, Shotaro, who was walking one step ahead, answered Sayaka.

"I wouldn't get your hopes up. It's probably a building with quite a bit of dark history. Still, it's definitely better than sleeping out in the open. Being underground, it probably won't be too cold, either."

"Oh, right. That makes sense. It gets pretty chilly at night, doesn't it?"

Sayaka politely agreed with Shotaro.

I had only just introduced my cousin to my college friends for the first time yesterday. Shotaro was fitting in with everyone much better than I had expected.

Five years ago, Shotaro inherited a considerable fortune from our aunt. Since then, he hadn't held a steady job, spending his days idly—traveling, dabbling in geological studies, and generally drifting along. You might think he planned to just live comfortably by eating away at his inheritance, but that didn't seem to be the case either; sometimes he'd take a million yen or so abroad and come back having multiplied it several times over.

Since he is my cousin, I've known him longer than any of my friends. He was the person I felt most comfortable around, yet I still hadn't fully grasped who he really was.

The reason I had brought Shotaro along was that I had sensed the potential for drama at this gathering. He had mentioned he was already interested in the local geography around here, which made it easy to bring him along.

So far, the kind of trouble I had been worried about hadn't occurred, but instead, we had unexpectedly ended up heading to a mysterious underground building. Having him around was reassuring; he seemed like the type who could handle things smoothly if anything went wrong.


Part 2

In the middle of a wasteland surrounded by rugged mountains, Yuya suddenly stopped. He pointed at the ground and yelled:

"Found it! Look, this is it. The entrance."

Yuya squatted down, thrust his hands into the dry grass, and lifted a manhole-like trapdoor about eighty centimeters in diameter.

Peering inside, we saw that the hole dropped vertically into the ground. Its sides were lined with concrete, and iron bars were embedded into the wall to form a ladder.

"We go in from here—"

"Huh? No way. That's scary. It's so narrow."

Hana shined her phone's flashlight down into the hole. The light wasn't strong enough, so the bottom remained invisible.

I agreed with her. Of course, given that it was deep in the mountains, it was only natural that it looked like a coal mine, but from the way Yuya had spoken about it, I had imagined a slightly more civilized structure.

"Yeah, well, sure, the entrance is like this, but once you're inside, it's fine. It's seriously huge in there. It goes down three basement levels. We can manage for one night."

Hana and the other girls were clearly hesitating, and I wasn't too keen on it either.

Ryuhei was the first to move.

"Whatever. I'll go check it out. Can I fit through like this?"

Mindful of his backpack scraping the sides, Ryuhei climbed down the ladder into the darkness. Yuya gauged the expressions of the three girls, then followed Ryuhei so as not to let him go ahead alone.

Hana, Sayaka, and Mai exchanged whispers—"What do we do?" "Who's going first?"—before taking turns descending into the hole. Shotaro and I brought up the rear.

After we climbed down the ladder for about seven or eight meters, our feet hit the ground.

From there, a horizontal, cave-like tunnel extended inward. It was quite spacious; we could walk through without having to stoop.

It sloped gently downward. We pressed on, holding up our phone flashlights.

A little further in, a massive boulder lay right in the middle of the passageway.

It was far too large to ever be moved by human strength. For some reason, it was wrapped tightly in thick chains.

"What's this? Did they try to dig it out and give up?"

"Who knows," Shotaro said meaningfully.

Slipping past the massive boulder, we saw an iron door.

Just before the iron door, the natural rock beneath our feet gave way to dirty wooden floorboards. From here on out, it was clearly a man-made structure.

Yuya opened the door and shined his light inside.

"Whoa? You weren't kidding. It's pretty amazing."

Ryuhei let out a voice that sounded either impressed or intimidated.

Beyond the door stretched a wide corridor. The ceiling was low. A short distance ahead the corridor curved, so we couldn't see all the way to the back, but the echo of the iron door opening told us that this underground building was quite large.

"Wow... It smells moldy," Mai murmured.

A sour, musty smell hung in the air. The damp air, reminiscent of the deep recesses of a sunless forest, was mixed with a faint chemical odor.

"What's the deal with the lighting in here? There's no electricity, right?"

"There isn't, but there was a massive generator. Looked like it might still work. If it doesn't, well, we'll just have to make do with our phone flashlights. I've got a power bank, anyway."

Ryuhei and Yuya ducked through the iron door and stepped into the pitch-black hallway.

With our flashlights in hand, the rest of us followed timidly, like a procession of glowing firefly larvae.

The floor was covered with old, cheap vinyl flooring. Along the walls on either side were numerous doors, much like a hotel.

Just before the hallway hooked left, Yuya pointed to a door on the right. It had a plate reading "107."

"The generator's in here. It doesn't look broken, but..."

Yuya turned the doorknob and swept his light into the room.

It appeared to be some kind of mechanical room. Black cables crawled along the walls, congregating at the back of the room where they connected to the generator.

It was a private power generator about the size of a bathtub, similar to one I had seen at a hospital where I used to work part-time. An exhaust pipe ran from the wall up through the ceiling. The machine looked like it was built before I was born, but the few LP gas cylinders set up for fuel looked new.

Checking the meters on the cylinders, I saw there was still gas left. Yuya and Ryuhei patted down the generator, trying to figure out how to start it.

Seeing that the two of them had no idea how to operate it, Shotaro spoke up modestly.

"First, make sure the cylinders and hoses are properly connected. Then flip the engine switch and pull the starter grip—I think that should do it."

Yuya did as he was told, and an engine roar, like that of a motorcycle, echoed through the room.

The next moment, the fluorescent lights on the ceiling flickered. Then, from the mechanical room out into the hallway, a pale blue light flooded the underground building.

"Thank goodness. Being down here in the dark really would have been rough," Sayaka said, looking around at everyone's faces.

In a mood of shared relief, we filed out of the cramped mechanical room.

This underground building harbored countless mysteries. Though a part of me wanted to explore the newly illuminated interior, right now my fatigue outweighed my curiosity.

Yuya led the group a little ways back toward the entrance and opened door number 106, on the side opposite the mechanical room.

"This is set up as a dining hall. Wanna take a quick break?"

It was a large room, dozens of tatami mats in size. Long tables were set up in the elongated space, with chairs lined up tightly alongside them. It was a cafeteria capable of seating dozens of people.

Hana pulled out the nearest chair.

"Ugh, so dirty. Is this safe to sit on?"

The chairs were the cheap kind you'd find in a school. Moreover, having been left underground for so long, their backrests were sprouting black mold and beginning to rot.

After brushing off the seat, Hana sat down gingerly. It didn't break.

Looking closely, the long tables had cheap veneer tops. They too were damaged, and it looked like it would be dangerous to stand on them.

There was a sink at the back of the cafeteria. When I turned the faucet, it made a gurgling noise before spitting out reddish-black water. After letting it run for a while, the water eventually ran clear.

"Wow, the plumbing works," I muttered involuntarily.

A dish cabinet hung over the sink. It was lined with a huge amount of old-looking, thick plates and cups.

Sayaka was kneeling by the wall, fiddling with something.

"Oh, cool. The outlets work too. Look."

She had plugged her phone charger into an outlet with exposed wiring.

We stayed in the dining hall for a while, stretching our bodies and yawning.

There was barely any conversation. It was a time dedicated entirely to shaking off fatigue, much like reaching a mountain hut after a long day of ridge trekking. Since we'd gathered yesterday, this was the moment that made me feel most nostalgic for our college days.

However, for a place to relax and stretch out, this rest area was brimming with a disquieting atmosphere. After a while, Shotaro tapped my shoulder.

"Shuichi, why don't we investigate the inside of the building a bit? It's a rather fascinating structure."

I was just starting to think about grabbing something to eat, but I was also curious about this underground building.

Then Yuya, who had been looking somewhat awkward, chimed in.

"Oh, Sho, I can show you around a bit, if you want? I checked out a lot of stuff last time I was here."

He decided to tag along, and we left the dining hall. The three of us set off to explore the underground building.

The low ceilings, the dim fluorescent lights, the dirty floors, the drab walls made of cheap materials, the wiring strung all over the place—taken together, the building had the atmosphere of an old cargo ship.

It wasn't just the atmosphere; the size and structure were also similar to a cargo ship. The building was constructed by welding iron plates to a grid of steel beams. It was three stories deep and narrow, with rooms flanking the hallways on both sides—some looked like storage rooms, others contained simple bunk beds made of steel pipes.

Each door bore a room number plate, like an apartment building. Returning to the entrance and standing with our backs to the iron door, the room on the right was 101. The one on the left was 102. As you proceeded down the corridor, the numbers continued: 103, 104, and so on. Whether they were storage rooms or living quarters, every room had a number regardless of its purpose. There were uneven gaps between every door and its frame. The entire construction was rough and unrefined.

Room 104, next to the dining hall, was the restroom. It had four stalls, similar to what you'd find in a public facility. Although I had no desire to use it, there was also an adjoining shower booth.

A stench lingered in the air, but not enough to be repulsive. Since it hadn't been used in a long time, the excrement seemed to have already decomposed significantly.

"There's no sewage system here, right? How did they pump it out?"

"They probably let it collect in a holding tank first, then pumped it up to the surface. The domestic wastewater likely operated on the same system."

Shotaro answered my question while peering into a squat toilet.

We'd seen enough of the restroom and returned to the hallway.

Past the mechanical room at 107, the hallway hooked to the left. At the corner was a set of iron stairs leading down to the second basement level.

We ignored the stairs for the moment and continued down the hallway. About five meters further, it hooked sharply to the right. Here too, doors lined both sides of the corridor, continuing from the mechanical room with 108, all the way to 120 at the very back.

On the outer-facing walls, dark rock faces were exposed in places. They had the same texture as the cave we had descended into from the entrance. Water seemed to be seeping through in spots, making the walls damp.

It seemed this underground building was built by shaping a natural cavern formed within the earth, creating different floor levels, setting up partition walls, and fashioning it into a building. The unnatural curves of the hallway were apparently the result of following the original topography.

When we reached the dead end, Shotaro spoke with the deep appreciation of someone who had just finished viewing a museum exhibition.

"Twenty rooms, huh? It must have cost a fortune. Well, they certainly did a good job building it. Although it's an ultra-illegal construction."

"And that's not all. There's a second basement level, too."

Yuya took the lead, heading back down the corridor toward the stairs.

The second basement level had roughly the same layout as the floor above. Doors lined the left and right sides of the lightning-bolt-shaped hallway. There were no large rooms like the dining hall down here, but the space directly beneath the first basement's restroom was taken up by the holding tank, occupying an entire room's worth of space. Yuya explained that there were twenty rooms down here as well, numbered 201 to 220.

The fluorescent lights were on in the B2 hallway as well. However, the area to the left of the stairs, where the lower-numbered rooms were located, was dark. The lighting fixtures were in place, so perhaps a wire was cut somewhere? The lights at the far end were on, so that section might have been on a different wiring circuit.

Once we had descended the stairs, before we began investigating the rooms, Shotaro asked a question.

"Yuya. How exactly did you find this place?"

"Oh, uh, I just stumbled across it about six months ago. I was trying to do some solo camping, and I wanted to go somewhere absolutely no one else would go, so I came deep into the mountains, and then I found that trapdoor. And when I went inside, it was insane."

In the middle of the hallway, Yuya spread his arms.

"What is this place, anyway? Who built this building, and why? Honestly, doesn't it feel like it was obviously used for some sketchy stuff?"

After a moment of thought, Shotaro replied.

"This is probably a fifty-year-old extremist hideout."

"Seriously? Extremists? Like from the '70s?"

"At a glance, it looks to have been built around that time. You saw that huge boulder in the entranceway, right? The one wrapped in chains. That was obviously placed there deliberately to be used as a barricade in an emergency. They'd use it to block off the iron door.

"But it seems that after those extremists, another criminal group or something used the place. There's some wiring that looks like it was installed fairly recently, maybe twenty years ago at most. The extremists wouldn't have been holding out in a place like this until then."

I had vaguely suspected as much myself.

Why build a structure deep in the mountains, and underground at that? To avoid the public eye, of course. Still, having it stated so plainly made the place feel even creepier.

Shotaro spoke brightly.

"Well, let's look around a bit more to see what we're dealing with."

We entered Room 208, which was right in front of us. It looked like a scrap storage room.

We rummaged through the room. We found an endless amount of junk: used work gloves, rusted sickles, old speakers, copper pipes, and scrap wood. Some items looked quite old; others not so much. It was nothing but the sort of garbage you'd find piled up at a neighborhood trash collection site.

"Oh? Even criminal hideouts have straw hats, it seems."

Shotaro said this with a straight face, showing me a battered, wide-brimmed straw hat.

"Yeah. I half-expected we might find a pistol or some white powder, but it doesn't really seem like that kind of place."

"They surely would have taken that stuff with them when they cleared out. Assuming the people using this place ever dealt in such things, anyway. Well, if we search every nook and cranny, we might find something."

Shotaro tossed the straw hat carelessly onto a dilapidated wooden box.

Next, we opened the door to Room 209 diagonally across the hall.

At first glance, it too was just a room where junk had been dumped. There was less of it than in the previous room, but scraps and refuse were swept into the corners like a garbage dump.

However, when we flipped the light switch, what we found inside were not the ordinary, mundane items of Room 208. When I heard that a criminal organization had used this place, my mind conjured up images of deadly weapons and drugs, but what we actually found was far more morbid.

The first thing that caught my eye was a set of restraints—handcuffs and shackles attached to a long chain. Set in the very back of the room was a pitch-black iron chair with a strangely pointed seat.

Furthermore, there were cudgels made of thick wood wrapped in leather, and a contraption—I didn't know how it was used—consisting of a metal frame large enough to fit a human head, fitted with a vise-like mechanism. There were also rusted nails and concrete blocks.

Yuya, Shotaro, and I exchanged looks with the awkwardness of people who had just peeked at someone else's dirty secret.

Yuya walked over to the corner of the room, squatted down, and let out a groan without touching the tools.

"You've gotta be kidding. This is insane. These are torture devices, aren't they?"

"They certainly look like it," Shotaro answered. It seemed Yuya hadn't looked inside this room on his previous visit.

"Do you think they were actually used?"

"I don't know, but doesn't it seem like it? I've only ever seen stuff like this in a museum. It's crazy that they actually exist."

The torture devices were old and rusted. There were no traces of dried blood left on them, but they were too badly damaged to be mere decorations in poor taste. I surveyed the floor around us. Deep gouges scarred the vinyl flooring, as if someone had clawed at it while writhing in agony.

I had heard stories about internal discord among extremist organizations in the seventies escalating into bloody purges. If the history of this underground building was what we imagined, finding torture implements here wasn't all that surprising.

A sudden, blood-soaked eeriness began to emanate from the otherwise sterile underground building.

"But, like, there's no proof these were actually used, right?"

"No. And even if they were used, it was a long time ago. They're basically historical relics now."

Hearing Shotaro say this, Yuya seemed a bit relieved.

Relying on his words, I too decided to try not to think too much about what might have happened here in the past. Naturally, torture devices had never had anything to do with my life before, and no matter what happened, they certainly never would in the future.

After that, we peeked into a few nearby rooms, but we didn't find anything more alarming than the torture devices.

"By the way, Yuya, you mentioned this place goes down to a third basement level, right? Where do we go down from?" Shotaro asked. There were no stairs leading down to the third basement visible in the hallway.

"Oh, uh, the stairs down are at the very end, but because of a few things, we can't actually go down there. Well, let's just head over. You'll see what I mean as soon as you look."

Yuya took the lead to guide us.

We proceeded down the hallway toward the lower room numbers. The lights along this part of the corridor were out. It wasn't so dark that we couldn't walk, but we turned on our phone flashlights just in case.

An iron door came into view at the dead end. It was similar to the entrance on the first basement level, but smaller and narrower.

Yuya pointed to it and said,

"Look, the entrance we came through is probably right above us."

Tracing the path we had taken to get here, he was probably right.

Yuya slowly pushed open the iron door.

It was a space unlike any of the other rooms. The area right inside the entrance narrowed like the neck of a bottle, and beyond that, the interior was exposed dark rock in every direction. The ceiling was exceptionally low and boarded up with wood only near the entrance. The rest was unworked rock; this room alone retained the appearance of a natural cave.

And installed against the back wall was a winch-like device that looked like it belonged on a sunken ship.

"What is this? It's rusted to hell."

A thick chain was wrapped around the winch. Tracing the chain, we saw it split, run through a pulley, and disappear up through the boarded ceiling near the door toward the first basement level.

"Ah! Is this the chain that was wrapped around that giant boulder?"

"That's right. I told you that boulder was a barricade."

Turning this winch would pull the giant boulder, blocking the iron door on the first basement level.

"Right, now that you mention it, it's obviously a barricade. I didn't really think too deeply about it. So that's why the ceiling is only boarded up near the entrance. To run the chain through."

"Well, that's the gist of it. Though there might be more to it than that," Shotaro said meaningfully.

"So, anyway, the stairs to the third basement are in this room. But we can't go down. Look."

Yuya pointed to the back right corner of the room.

A square hole was cut into the floor. The stairs were there.

We approached and looked down, and I understood what Yuya meant at a glance.

The third basement level was flooded. The water level came up to the fourth step down, just barely scraping the ceiling of B3. When I squatted down and stretched my hand out as far as I could, my fingertips brushed the glossy, black surface of the water.

"Cold. Damn. It's flooded."

"We're underground, after all. And since it's amateur construction, flooding is to be expected. Given that it's surrounded by natural rock, it makes perfect sense. The drainage system is probably broken, too. This might be the reason the underground building was abandoned."

Indeed, I had seen water trickling down the outer walls on the first basement level earlier.

"Yeah, we definitely can't pass this off as a 'mansion with a pool.' Honestly, it's kind of scary. I mean, doesn't this mean the whole building will inevitably go under someday? If this keeps up."

Yuya replied.

"Well, theoretically, yeah, but it'll take a really long time. The water level doesn't feel like it's changed much since I was here six months ago. Maybe it's risen a little bit. If that happens, it won't be for like five years, right?"

That was a fair point.

Shining my phone flashlight into the water, I could see what looked like rebar and steel beams, embedded in concrete, left scattered haphazardly across the third basement floor.

There was nothing else to see, so we stepped back out into the hallway and retraced our steps.

As we neared the stairs, we saw Sayaka's back on the opposite side of the hallway; she had her phone camera raised. She seemed to be taking photos out of curiosity.

"Yuya, is that hole we all came through the only entrance to this underground building? It seems unlikely there would only be one."

"No, there is one more, technically, but we can't use it. It's down on the third basement level. There's a narrow, trash chute-like shaft that goes up to the surface, but since it's flooded, it's completely impassable."

"I see."

"Oh, that reminds me, there's a sort of floor plan in the mechanical room. It'll be quicker if you just look at that."

The three of us returned to the mechanical room.

Yuya opened the drawer of a desk in the room. It was crammed with a jumble of items: old Band-Aids, nail clippers, and stationery like pencils and pens.

He pulled these items out and tossed them onto the desk. Eventually, mixed in with everything else, he found a blueprint—a large A2 sheet of paper folded into quarters.

"Ah, this is it. I must've shoved it way in the back last time I looked at it."

It was a bit inadequate to be called a proper floor plan, but it was a blueprint showing the layout of the entire building. It appeared to date back to the time of construction, as the paper was heavily yellowed.

At the top of the blueprint, scrawled in ballpoint pen as if added later, was the word "Ark." It seemed to be the name of this underground building.

The "Ark," as we had seen, was a long, narrow three-story structure that bent into a reverse Z-shape in the middle. According to the blueprint, the third basement level was not subdivided like B1 and B2; rather, it consisted of only a few large rooms. The entrance we had come through was located on the west side, and there appeared to be another exit leading to the surface on the east side of the third basement.

"This one here seems to be some kind of emergency exit. It also connects to the surface with a trapdoor just like the main entrance. Actually, we passed right by the emergency exit trapdoor on our way here. Though I doubt anyone noticed."

He said there was another trapdoor right after we crossed the bridge. I certainly hadn't noticed it, and neither had Shotaro. It was already getting dark when we passed through there, so it was only natural we'd missed it.

"Did you look inside that emergency exit, Yuya? Is it exactly as shown on the blueprint?"

"Oh, yeah. I tried climbing down from the emergency exit side, just to see. I went down a ladder and made it right to the ceiling of the third basement, but B3 was full of water. So I just climbed back up."

"Speaking of emergency exits..."

There was something that had been bothering me for a while.

"What are these?"

I pointed to the top of the desk.

Sitting there were two LCD monitors. They were old 15-inch screens, like the ones you'd find in an elementary school library. Scrawled in permanent marker on their respective bezels were the very words we were just discussing: "Entrance" and "Emergency Exit."

"Oh! I've been wondering about those since I came here last time. They seem to be security camera monitors. See how it says 'Entrance' and 'Emergency Exit'? When I looked around during the day, there was something that looked like a camera near the trapdoors. Mounted up in the trees. There's one at the west entrance and one at the east emergency exit.

"So, I think these probably show those camera feeds. But I didn't have power last time, so I couldn't check."

Chattering away, Yuya turned on the power to both monitors one after the other.

"Oh, are they alive? —Whoa, awesome, they work."

With a soft, buzzing mechanical hum, the old monitors flickered to life with the security camera footage.

Since the sun had completely set, the images were indistinct, looking almost like woodblock prints. The cameras themselves seemed as old as the monitors, and the resolution was poor.

Even so, we could tell they were showing the dry field above ground. Bathed in moonlight, a faint shape resembling a trapdoor was visible in the center of both screens. It was set up so that if anyone approached either the entrance or the emergency exit, you'd know immediately.

Tracing his finger over the trapdoors on the two screens, Yuya spoke.

"Yep, yep. This one's the entrance we came through. And this one is the emergency exit near the bridge."

"So these two spots are about a hundred meters apart, then."

"Ah, yeah. About that far. One on the east, one on the west. Maybe so that if someone from the outside finds one, they can escape from the other?"

Yuya replied to Shotaro.

Installing those cameras must have been quite a hassle. I spoke, somewhat exasperated.

"Pretty high security. Seriously, what kind of people used this place?"

"It might actually have been some new religious cult conducting special training or something. The way the cameras are set up is strange if they were meant to guard against outside intruders. They look more like they were designed to keep an eye out for escapees."

Shotaro answered.

It was a rather persuasive theory. I took another long look at the word "Ark" written at the top of the yellowed blueprint.

"Do you think this was taken from Noah's Ark in the Old Testament?"

"Well, I can't really think of any other origin for it."

I recalled flipping through the Bible during a cultural anthropology lecture in college. The famous story of Noah's Ark is found right at the very beginning of the thick Old Testament.

When the world fell into chaos and the earth was filled with violence, Noah, a righteous man, received a revelation. God had decided to destroy mankind. He told Noah to build an ark to prepare for the coming flood. When the ark was finished, Noah, his family, and a male and female of every living creature entered it, and the floodwaters ravaged the earth.—That was the story. The original text is rather dry to read as a narrative, but sermons, novels, and films based on it often depict people mocking Noah and his family for building an ark on a mountain, refusing to believe a flood is coming.

A structure built deep in the mountains, resembling a ship. It might have been an afterthought, but the name "The Ark" made sense. For those extremists or cultists, this might have been a place to await salvation.

To me, it seemed like a sick joke. I couldn't see any salvation in this unsettling underground building. The only things we'd found were torture devices.

None of the people I had come with were particularly devout or possessed strong political ideologies. It seemed our group was not Noah's family, but rather the ones mocking those who had built this place.

"Oh? What are you guys doing?"

Hearing our commotion in the mechanical room, Sayaka poked her head in through the open door.

Then, in quick succession, Hana arrived, apparently having been looking for Sayaka.

"Oh, Sayaka, there you are. Were you taking pictures or something?"

"Ah, yeah. We'll probably never come back to a place like this, so I wanted something to remember it by."

She had evidently been peeking into various rooms and snapping photos.

"That's fine and all, but you probably shouldn't post them online or anything. It could be a hassle if the people who used to use this place find out."

"Ah, good point. I won't. I'll just take them for myself."

As Sayaka and Hana were chatting, Mai and Ryuhei arrived together.

It seemed the four of them had also been curious about the interior and had been doing their own exploring. Now, all seven of us were gathered in the mechanical room.

Everyone looked surprised to see the monitors turned on.

Yuya repeated the same explanations to them that he had given Shotaro and me—about the flooded third floor, the emergency exit, and the security cameras.

"Right. I mean, I get that, but..."

Hana cut Yuya off.

"Actually, I kind of want to go outside for a bit. My boyfriend messaged me earlier today, and if I don't reply by tonight, he might show up at my house while I'm not there."

Hana said this, stroking the phone in her hand.

Yuya scratched his head.

"I mean... it's gonna be pretty tough to get a signal around here."

"Yeah, so I just want to try, and if I can't get one, I'll give up. Would someone come with me?"

It was pitch-black deep in the mountains; anyone would be scared to go walking around alone.

"In that case, want me to go with you?"

However, Hana seemed reluctant to go out with just Yuya.

Sensing this, Sayaka immediately threw her a lifeline.

"Then maybe I'll go too. I've probably got some work messages anyway. Is that okay?"

"Oh, you'll come too, Sayaka? Thanks. That'd be great."

It was settled. Hana, Sayaka, and Yuya would step outside for a bit.

After the three of them left, Ryuhei glared at the dark video feed on the monitor over my shoulder.

"Is that thing really showing anything?"

From a distance, the screen just looked like static from a bad connection.

However, before I could answer, there was movement on the monitor.

"Oh, it's Hana and the others."

The security camera for the entrance showed the three signal-seekers lifting the trapdoor and climbing out.

The figure in front waved at the camera. This was Yuya; the one following in the bright jacket was Hana. Behind them was Sayaka. Naturally, it was too dark to make out their faces.

A little later, the camera for the emergency exit showed the three of them walking with their phone flashlights in hand. They seemed to be heading across the wooden bridge toward higher ground to test for reception.

"Huh, so it does work," Ryuhei muttered, seemingly convinced.

Then he and Mai idly fingered the items Yuya had dumped on the desk. Eventually, growing bored, Ryuhei grabbed Mai's hand, and the two of them left the mechanical room.

Shotaro and I stayed in the mechanical room, staring blankly at the monitors.

At a loss for what to do, I asked my cousin a meaningless question.

"Did everyone eat something?"

"Who knows."

Everyone seemed so preoccupied with touring the underground building that they might not have eaten yet.

In about thirty minutes, Yuya and the others returned. Thanks to the security cameras, we could see them coming back right away.

However, something was off about Yuya's group.

"Huh? Aren't there more of them? —Is that even possible?"

The number of figures on the entrance monitor had grown from three to six.

It was like something out of a horror movie. Shotaro and I left the mechanical room and went to the iron door to greet the group, sensing that something had happened.

I opened the iron door. Yuya was the first to enter. Then Hana. There was mud on her jacket; it looked like she had taken a fall.

Then Sayaka.

And behind her, looking frightened, was a family of three.

The father looked to be in his fifties, with a crew cut mixed with gray hair and thick black-rimmed glasses. The mother was slightly plump with short hair. The son, who had somewhat thick lips, looked to be about middle-school age.

As soon as Sayaka saw our faces, she was the first to explain.

"Um, these people were apparently lost, so... we brought them back with us. We told them we at least had a place with a roof. You were, um, mushroom hunting, right?"

The father answered.

"Yes, something like that. We're very sorry to intrude."

Mushroom hunting... It was the season for it, but even so, they had come awfully deep into the mountains.

It wasn't exactly our home, but we decided to invite them into the dining hall anyway.
