1.

Guesthouse for Gentlemen

The view is obscured by clouds of steam from the locomotive, which now drift along the platform. One must peer out from beneath them to see everything, allow oneself to be blinded for a moment by the gray fog, until the sight that emerges after this ordeal becomes sharp, piercing, and all-seeing.

Then we will see the paving stones of the platform, the squares overgrown with blades of frail plants—a space that wishes to maintain order and symmetry at all costs.

In a moment, a left shoe appears upon them, brown, leather, not exactly new, and is soon joined by a second, the right; this one seems even more worn—the toe is somewhat scuffed, the finish revealing lighter spots in a few small places. The shoes stand undecided for a moment, but then the left one moves forward. This movement briefly exposes a black cotton sock from beneath the trouser leg. The black is repeated in the folds of his unbuttoned heavy woolen coat; the day is warm. A brown leather suitcase is gripped by a small, pale, bloodless hand; its veins have grown taut from the weight and now point back to their sources, somewhere deep within the bowels of the sleeve. A flannel jacket of rather poor quality, additionally rumpled by the long journey, peeks out from under the coat. Tiny, light specks of unspecified dirt are visible upon it—the scales of the world. The white collar of the shirt, the detachable kind, was evidently changed quite recently, for its whiteness is fresher than the whiteness of the shirt itself and contrasts with the sallow hue of the newcomer’s face. Pale eyes, brows, and lashes make this face appear unhealthy. This entire figure, set against the intensely red western sky, gives the unsettling impression of having arrived here, in these melancholic mountains, from the beyond.

The traveler walks toward the main hall of the station—surprisingly large for this mountainous region—along with the other arrivals; he differs from them, however, in that he walks unhurriedly, even reluctantly, and that no one greets him, no one has come out to meet him. He sets the suitcase down on the worn tiled floor and pulls lined gloves over his hands. One of them, the right, on a hand curled into a fist, wanders to his mouth a moment later to receive a parade of short, dry coughs.

The young man hunches over and searches his pocket for a handkerchief. For a moment, his fingers touch the spot where, in the pocket beneath the fabric of his coat, his passport is hidden. If we focus our attention for a moment, we will see the fanciful handwriting of some Galician clerk who carefully filled in the boxes of the document: Mieczysław Wojnicz, Catholic, student of the Lwów Polytechnic, born 1889, eyes blue, height average, face oblong, hair fair.

This Wojnicz now crosses the main hall of the station in Dittersbach, which lies near Waldenburg; he walks hesitantly through the gloomy, high-ceilinged space where an echo surely dwells upon the highest cornices, and feels someone’s eyes scrutinizing him closely from behind the ticket windows in the waiting room. He checks the time on the great clock—it is late, this is the last train from Breslau. He hesitates a moment, and then steps out in front of the station building, letting himself be instantly embraced by the broad arms of the uneven, jagged line of the mountain horizon.

It is mid-September, yet here, as the newcomer notes with surprise, summer has long passed, and the first fallen leaves lie on the ground. The last few days must have been rainy, for a light mist still fills the landscape almost completely, making an exception only for the dark lines of the streams. He can feel in his lungs that he is at a high altitude; this is good for his illness-weary body. Wojnicz stands on the station steps, suspiciously eyeing his footwear with its thin leather soles—he will have to think about winter boots. In Lwów, the asters and zinnias were still in bloom, and no one was thinking about autumn at all. Here, meanwhile, the high horizon makes it darker, and the colors seem more glaring, almost vulgar. At this moment, he is overcome by a familiar melancholy, particular to people convinced of their imminent death. He feels that this world around him is a backdrop painted on a paper screen, that he could poke his finger into this monumental landscape and drill a hole leading straight into nothingness. And that this nothingness would begin to pour out from there like a flood, and eventually reach him too, seizing him by the throat. He has to shake his head to rid himself of this image. The image shatters into droplets and falls onto the leaves. Fortunately, a clumsy vehicle resembling a carriage is trundling down the road toward him. In it sits a slender, freckled boy in a strange outfit. He is wearing something like a military jacket of hard-to-determine origin—as it resembles neither a Prussian uniform, which would be understandable in this place, nor any other—and a military forage cap, cocked jauntily on his head. He stops in front of Wojnicz without a word and, muttering something under his breath, takes his luggage.

“How do you do, my good man?” Wojnicz asks politely in his schoolboy German, but he waits in vain for an answer; the other pulls his cap over his eyes and impatiently points to a seat in the carriage.

And they set off immediately. First through the small town over cobblestones, then along a road that leads them in the gathering darkness down a winding trail between steep mountain slopes, through a forest. They are constantly accompanied by the murmur of a nearby flowing stream and its scent, which always unsettles Wojnicz so much: the smell of damp undergrowth, rotting leaves, perpetually wet stones, and of water. He tries to ask the driver some question that would allow them to establish contact—for instance, how long they will be driving, how he recognized him at the station, what his name is—but the boy does not even look back and remains silent. A gas lantern mounted on the boy’s right side half-illuminates his face, which in profile resembles the muzzle of a mountain rodent, a marmot, and Wojnicz deduces that the driver must be either deaf or brazenly rude.

Finally, after about three-quarters of an hour, they emerge from the shadows of the forest and drive into a surprisingly flat valley, an unexpected plateau amidst the forested mountains. The sky is fading, but you can still see that poignant high horizon, which seems to rise to the throat of every newcomer from the lowlands.

“Görbersdorf,” the driver suddenly speaks up in a boyish, unexpectedly high voice.

But Wojnicz sees nothing, nothing except a thick wall of darkness that unceremoniously peels away in whole sheets from the mountain slopes. Only when his eyes adjust to it does a viaduct suddenly emerge before them, under which they drive into the village, and beyond it, an enormous mass of a red-brick building, and soon other, smaller structures, and a street, and even two gas lanterns. The brick building turns out to be a colossus; it grows before his eyes, and the movement of the vehicle draws rows of illuminated windows out of the darkness. The light in them is a dirty yellow. Wojnicz cannot take his eyes off this unexpected, triumphant sight, and he keeps looking back at it for a long time, until it sinks into the darkness like a massive steamship.

The carriage now turns onto a narrow side lane along the stream, passing over a small bridge where the wheels raise a clatter resembling the sound of gunshots. Finally, it stops before a sizable wooden building of bizarre architecture that brings to mind a house made of matches—so numerous are its verandas, balconies, and terraces. A pleasant light burns in the windows. Below the first-floor windows is a beautiful sign cut from thick sheet metal in Schwabacher script:

*Gästehaus für Herren*

Wojnicz gets out of the carriage with relief and draws a massive gulp of this new air into his lungs, air that is said to cure the most severe conditions. Perhaps, though, he does this too soon, for he is suddenly ambushed by a coughing fit so severe that he has to lean against the railing of the bridge. And then, coughing, he feels the cold, unpleasant slipperiness of rotten wood, and the good first impression vanishes. He cannot stop the violent spasms of his diaphragm and is seized by an overwhelming terror—that he will suffocate in a moment, that this fit will be his last. He tries to push the panic away, just as Dr. Sokołowski had advised him: to think of a meadow full of flowers, of warm sun. He tries very hard, though his eyes are watering and his face is flushed with blood. He feels as though he is about to cough up his soul.

But then he feels a grip on his shoulder—whereupon a tall, well-built, graying man offers him his hand. Through his tears, Wojnicz catches sight of his pink, healthy face.

“Now, now, my good sir. Let’s pull ourselves together,” the man says with self-assurance and a broad smile, so that the guest, barely alive from coughing, has the urge to cuddle up to him and let himself be led to bed, like a child. Oh yes, exactly. A child. A bed. With a certain embarrassment, he drapes his arms around the man’s neck and lets himself be guided through a hallway smelling of spruce smoke and up the soft, carpet-lined stairs. All of this evokes some distant association with wrestling, with that manly sport where hard bodies press against one another, rub, and strike—yet not to cause harm, but quite the contrary: to show each other tenderness and affection under the pretext of a fight. He yields to the strong hands, allows himself to be led into an upstairs room, sat upon the bed, and stripped of his coat and sweater.

Wilhelm Opitz—for that is how the man introduces himself, pointing a finger at his own chest—covers him with a woolen plaid blanket and, from a pair of hands that momentarily appear in the crack of the doorway, receives a mug of hot, savory broth. As Mieczysław drinks in small sips, Wilhelm Opitz raises his finger (Wojnicz realizes at this moment what an essential part of Wilhelm this finger is) and says in soft, slightly amusing German:

“I wrote to Professor Sokołowski to have you take a break in Breslau. It is too long and tiring a journey. I told him.”

The broth spreads with a pleasant warmth through Wojnicz’s body, and the poor fellow does not even realize when he falls asleep. We accompany him for a moment longer, listening to his peaceful breathing; it pleases us that his lungs have calmed.

Our attention is now drawn to a streak of light, thin as a blade, which falls into the room from the corridor and comes to rest on a porcelain chamber pot beneath the bed. We are drawn to the cracks between the floorboards—and there we disappear.

At a quarter to seven, Wojnicz was awakened by the sound of a trumpet, as a result of which it took him a long moment to realize where he was. The melody was evidently played out of tune, which amused him and put him in a good mood. It seemed familiar, but in the way that applies to things so simple they are brilliant. It feels as if they have always existed and always will.

Mieczysław Wojnicz was composed of afflictions, which were best navigated not even by himself, but by his father, January Wojnicz, a retired civil servant and landowner. He managed these afflictions with great expertise, gravity, and tact, treating the property entrusted to him in the form of his son with profound responsibility and—it is clear—love, albeit devoid of all sentimentalism and all those “womanish emotions” he so despised.

One of these afflictions, in the shaping of which he had played a part, was his son’s exaggerated fear of being watched. Thus, the young Wojnicz paid a great deal of attention to the gaze of others, checking to see if he was being spied upon from around a corner, from a nook, through a window with a folded curtain, or through a keyhole. The father’s caution and suspicion had morphed into the son’s obsession. He felt as though someone else’s gaze was something sticky, clinging to him like the soft, hideous mouth of a leech. Therefore, in every room where he was to spend the night, he always carefully inspected the window curtains, plugged the keyhole with a wad of paper, checked for any holes in the walls or cracks between the floorboards, and even looked behind paintings. In guesthouses and hotels, spying is not all that unrealistic—once, when he and his father were staying at a hotel in Warsaw during one of those health-related visits to a specialist, young Wojnicz discovered a perfectly round hole in the wall, ineptly masked by the rich pattern of the wallpaper, and naturally plugged it with a pellet of bread; and when he tried to figure out in the morning from where and who could be observing the guests, he discovered that a service staircase used by the hotel staff lay on the other side of the wall. There you have it! So he was not obsessed. People spy on one another. They love it, looking at someone when he is unaware of it. Evaluating, comparing. Such an observed person becomes defenseless; he becomes an unwitting, powerless victim.

Upon waking, he immediately set about writing a message to his father to reassure him. It was just a matter of a few simple words, yet it was not going very well; his hand was numb and weak. Therefore, he focused all his attention on his hand, which guided the tip of the pencil across the cream-colored paper of a leather-bound notebook. This movement fascinates us; we like it. It resembles those same twisting lines and spiral ornaments that earthworms carve underground and that the bark beetle gnaws into the trunk of a tree. Wojnicz sat in the bedding, propped up against two massive pillows. Before him, he had a clever contraption—something like a table without legs. Its base was a cushion stuffed with peas, which allowed it to rest easily upon the writer’s lap.

First, two digits emerged, forming “13,” then a straight line and a cross “IX,” followed by another four digits making up “1913.” Next, from the flourishes, the word “Görbersdorf” emerged, thickened several times. The umlaut was treated with special reverence. Then the pencil moved evenly and steadily across the paper. The graphite scratched, the paper yielding beneath the rounded shapes of the letters.

The room was modest, but comfortably furnished. Two windows looked out onto the street and the stream in front of the house, though the view was obscured by crocheted thread curtains. Beneath one of the windows stood a round table and two comfortable, upholstered, slightly worn chairs, almost armchairs—a nice reading nook, should one want it. To the left of the door was a bed with a beautifully carved wooden headboard; beside it, a wardrobe. A vanity table was situated to the right of the door. The walls were covered with fabric wallpaper in broad, light-blue stripes, making the room seem taller and more spacious than it actually was. Hanging on the wall were prints from exotic lands: a herd of hares and a pack of hyenas.

Mieczysław Wojnicz briefly described his impressions of the journey in Polish, converting nineteen hundred feet into meters (it came out to nearly five hundred), and marked these numbers on a sketch of a map illustrating his journey from Lwów all the way here. Brief comments pertained mostly to the meals en route. Next to the entry “Wrocław/Breslau,” he added: “Yellow pumpkin soup, for the main course: purée with crackling, cabbage, and a cutlet exactly like our pork loin cutlet. For dessert, vanilla pudding with meringue and blackberry compote, very tasty.” Beneath this, he added: “Cost 5 marks.” He had promised his father he would write him a few words every day, ideally about his health, but in truth, he did not know how he felt, so he preferred sending recipes or geographic information back to Lwów.

There came a soft knock at the door, and before he could say “come in,” a leather boot slipped into the crack between the frame and the door, gently pushing it open. Behind it appeared the black folds of a skirt, the lace of an apron, and a breakfast tray, which was quickly set upon the table. The boots, lace, and apron vanished just as swiftly as they had appeared, and a bewildered Wojnicz only managed to pull his blanket up and mumble some greeting and thanks. He was so hungry that he was exclusively interested in the food.

He would note this down in his notebook shortly: hard-boiled eggs, two, in pretty faience cups, covered with little caps shaped like hens; slices of smoked sheep’s cheese garnished with parsley; a ball of the yellowest butter served on a horseradish leaf; a small bowl of fragrant lard with a little spreading knife; sliced radishes; a basket of various rolls, light and dark; apricot jam in a glass dish; a mug of thick cocoa, and a small jug of coffee.

After the period, the notebook snapped shut, and Wojnicz ate everything on the tray with relish. Fortified by the meal, he stood up. Draping his back in the blanket, he padded over to his suitcase, from which he extracted a neatly folded set of underclothes, and then set about his ablutions. And as he wiped his face with a towel that had soaked up the ubiquitous scent of coniferous trees in the guesthouse, the image of their country house returned vividly to his mind, along with the laundry dried in the attic during winter, which Gliceria would carry up in buckets when it was pouring outside. The image of the attic returned, always full of dust, and the view from its small dormer windows known as ox-eyes—a view of the fields and the small park, and the bitter scent of rotting stalks from tomatoes, corn, and pole beans. And by the laws of some not entirely comprehensible synesthesia, this image transferred into a bodily sensation: the roughness of clothing, the stiffness of collars, the angularity of freshly ironed trousers, and the pressure of a hard leather belt. And it was there, in the attic, whenever he could, whenever he was left alone and momentarily far from his father’s drill, that he would strip naked and wrap himself in a satin tablecloth trimmed with soft fringe, and feel how delightfully those fringes brushed his calves and thighs, and think how wonderful it would be if they, like the ancient Greeks, could walk about in such tablecloth chitons. Now, recalling that satin toga, he dressed and rejoiced that he finally felt strong and rested.

We are witnesses as clothing appears in layers on his slender body, until finally his figure—completely different from yesterday’s coughing, ashen-faced self—stands with a hand on the doorknob, eyes closed, imagining how he would look to someone who might be looking at him now. He looks good—a slim young man with fair hair and delicate features, wearing gray striped trousers and a brown woolen jacket. After a moment, he decisively opens the door.

No, we do not consider this an obsession, at most an innocent hypersensitivity. People should get used to being observed.

Wojnicz went downstairs around ten, as he had an appointment for an examination at the *Kurhaus*.

Because of the small windows, sparsely placed in the walls, a semi-darkness reigned throughout the house—typical for mountain architecture. Here stood an oval table covered with a thick patterned cloth, a sofa, and several chairs; against the wall sat an upright piano, its infrequent use evidenced by scattered fingerprints on the glossy lid and a sheaf of yellowed sheet music. A small hanging shelf nearby was full of books about the region, local ski trails, and monuments. Inside a massive glass-fronted cabinet, beautiful porcelain tableware with sentimental cobalt scenes—shepherds and sheep—gleamed white.

“*Gemütlich*,” Mieczysław whispered to himself, pleased to have recalled a certain German word he particularly liked. Such a word was missing from his own language. Cozy? Pleasant?

He also remembered the words of Dr. Sokołowski from the time he began treating him and struggling against his apathy: that life must be made appetizing. Yes, appetizing, that was a better word than “*gemütlich*,” Wojnicz thought, for it pertained not only to space, but to everything else as well—to someone’s voice, to their way of speaking, sitting in an armchair, tying a scarf around the neck, to how cookies were arranged on a saucer. He ran his finger over the table covered in soft olive-green plush, and only after a moment did he notice with a start a slender man with distinct bird-like features and wire-rimmed glasses on a prominent nose, sitting in an armchair by the window. He was shrouded in a cloud of cigarette smoke. Wojnicz’s hand sprang back from the plush as if burned and disappeared into the embrace of his other hand, embarrassed. The man, equally flustered by this intrusion upon his solitude, stood up and introduced himself rather formally, in German, with a strange Silesian accent:

“Walter Frommer. From Breslau.”

Wojnicz slowly and clearly stated his own first and last name, surely hoping the other would memorize it immediately. They spoke for a moment, and Frommer had time to inform him that he sought treatment in Görbersdorf regularly; he had been here, off and on, for three years now. Occasionally he returned to Breslau for a while, but his condition immediately worsened there.

“You see, the city of Breslau lies on the water. In the spring, swarms of mosquitoes—small, but exceptionally venomous—hang over the buildings, and people suffer from rheumatism. In the summer, it is impossible to sit in the garden, which is why state officials stay there only for a short period of a few years and then return to better places. Breslau is a transitional city—” a sadness appeared in his voice, as if he pitied the city. “It’s because of this ubiquitous water, it creeps in everywhere... I tolerate it poorly...” He began to cough. “Oh, you see, just thinking about it makes me cough.”

Wojnicz shifted his gaze toward the window, outside of which a merry company was just passing, breaking into laughter every so often. He thought these people were laughing in Polish, although he could not quite explain this impression to himself. From a distance, the words were inaudible.

“Are you, too, preparing to move to the *Kurhaus*?” he asked Frommer.

He thought this question might elicit at least a slight smile on his interlocutor’s face, but the man took it seriously.

“God forbid,” he bristled. “There are too many people there. You can’t see anything from there. You won’t learn or find out anything. Life in a crowd is worse than prison.”

Well then, Wojnicz had likely formed his opinion about Walter Frommer—an eccentric.

Both were, it seemed, equally shy, for they stood facing each other in awkward silence for a moment, each waiting for the other to utter some conventional sentence. They were rescued from this stalemate by Wilhelm Opitz, the host.

“I hope I am not interrupting a lively conversation,” he said, and Wojnicz wondered for a moment if Wilhelm was mocking them or just that oblivious. But Opitz took him firmly by the arm and led him toward the exit.

“Excuse me, but I must hand this young man over to the watchful eye of Doctor Semperweiß. Our guest arrived here in a deplorable state.”

Frommer muttered something indistinctly, returned to his place by the window, and sat in the exact same position as before. As if he worked here full-time as a smoking piece of furniture.

“Doctor Frommer is a bit strange, but he is a decent man. Like everyone in my guesthouse,” Wilhelm said in that dialect of his, growing increasingly pleasant to Wojnicz’s ear, as they stood on the steps outside the house. “The boy will take you to Doctor Semperweiß. Watch out for him, he doesn’t like people from the East. He doesn’t like anyone at all. It is a great loss that there is no one here like Doctor Brehmer,” he added thoughtfully when, a moment later, they both stood by the bridge.

Wojnicz witnessed how the mist was now forming strange bands, floating upward like smoke.

“Perhaps you know Doctor Sokołowski?” he asked.

Wilhelm’s face brightened and grew animated.

“Of course I knew him when I was a child. He was friends with my father, who worked for him. All of us here work at the *Kurhäuser*. How is he doing?”

Well, this Wojnicz did not exactly know. He only knew that he worked at a clinic in Warsaw and occasionally gave lectures in Lwów. His father took him for consultations when Sokołowski visited the city. And thanks to him, he found himself here.

“Is he still so thin?” Willi asked.

Thin? No, not thin. Professor Sokołowski is a stocky and stout man. Wojnicz did not have to answer this surprising question, however, because from the wisps of fog emerged Rajmund, yesterday’s driver, a teenage boy whom Wilhelm greeted in a rather particular manner: with a light smack on the head. The boy accepted this as a completely natural, friendly gesture.

They walked together down the stream toward the center of the village. Rajmund was recounting something with enthusiasm, but he spoke in such a strange dialect that Wojnicz understood little. He looked with interest at the beautiful houses standing along the road and the workers repairing the electrical lines. Rajmund asked if Mieczysław knew what it was—electricity.

Then they bowed to two elderly women in wide skirts sitting on a bench in front of one of the houses.

“Frau Weber and Frau Brecht,” Rajmund said with an ironic smile, and this Wojnicz understood perfectly.

A moment later, the boy pointed with pride to Dr. Brehmer’s sanatorium, the very same building Wojnicz had seen yesterday evening, though now it seemed even more massive, especially since the fog had almost vanished and, somewhere high above the valley, the September sun shone generously.

Rajmund vanished as soon as he led Wojnicz to the correct door in the wide corridor. A nurse whose eyes were underscored by red swelling now took charge of Mieczysław. A short, polite smile momentarily revealed her large, yellowed teeth, which color-coordinated with the worn gilding of the watch on a chain pinned to her apron. Above her pocket, her first and last name were embroidered: Sydonia Patek.

Wojnicz had to wait his turn in the doctor’s waiting room, as the doctor had not yet returned from his rounds. His fingers reached for the illustrated magazines laid out for the patients, but his eyes found no solace in them; they could not focus on the Schwabacher script. To his surprise, however, Wojnicz found a pamphlet in Polish, and his sight miraculously relaxed at once upon the words of his native tongue:

*In Prussian Silesia, a quarter of a mile from the Bohemian border, 11 miles southwest of Breslau, in a long valley stretching from east to west between the Riesengebirge and the Adlergebirge, lies the charming village of Görbersdorf in the Waldenburg district, situated on the Steine river, famous for decades as a mountain health resort for those with chest complaints.*

*The elevation of Görbersdorf is 570 meters above sea level, in a zone which medical science deems “free from consumption.” The surrounding mountains reach 900 meters. They shield the village and its medical establishments from the winds, which arrive here weakened; hence, there reigns in Görbersdorf a calmness of air rarely found in any valley.*

He did not read further, but folded the brochure in half and slipped it into his pocket. Now his attention was drawn to a glass display case containing a human torso made of wood—without a head, arms, or legs, its chest and abdomen open to present internal organs painted in various colors. Wojnicz walked up to the wooden trunk to examine the lungs. They were smooth and clean, polished, gleaming with varnish. They resembled the fleshy petals of a monstrous flower or mushrooms growing on tree bark. How marvelously they fit the size of the chest, how they reconciled their airy nature with the cage of ribs. He examined them closely, trying to peek beneath the pointed tip where they met other, coiled organs in assorted colors. Despite everything, however, he was disappointed; perhaps he had expected something new, something he did not yet know. A solution to the mystery. Why he was sick. And why others were not.

When he returned to his seat, he was overcome by a familiar anxiety, that irritability which always results in the same bodily reaction: sweating. He would have to strip and expose his body to a stranger’s gaze. And the panic: how would he hide his shameful condition from the doctor? What would he have to say to avoid touching upon all these sensitive issues? How to slip away? He had practiced this so many times.

When the doctor burst into the waiting room, he didn’t even look at him—he crossed it at a brisk pace, leaving only the flaps of his white coat billowing behind him. He gave a hand signal for the patient to rise. Mieczysław followed the doctor at almost a trot into a large office with an enormous window, filled with glass display cases, various medical devices, and strange examination chairs. Somehow, it didn’t surprise Wojnicz that leaning against the doctor’s desk was a rifle—a large one, not at all a hunting rifle, more likely a Winchester, with a beautifully polished stock. Without turning around, the doctor told him to sit down, and in this way, Wojnicz felt safely hidden behind the desk as if in a trench.

He handed the doctor his letter of recommendation from Professor Sokołowski, but the doctor only glanced at it, clearly more interested in the body sitting before him. The young man felt uneasy, and the cause was the gaze with which the man looked at him. As if he did not see Mieczysław Wojnicz, a patient from faraway Lwów, but rather the body itself, something object-like and mechanical. First, without any embarrassment, he pulled down Wojnicz’s lower eyelid, closely examining the color of the mucous membranes and the eyeball. Next, he ran his eyes from chin to temples; finally, he ordered him to strip to the waist, cast a critical look at his chest, and began pressing the patient’s nipples with his finger.

“Slightly enlarged, as are the lymph nodes,” he said. “Have you always had this?”

“For a few years,” replied a timid Wojnicz.

The doctor grabbed him by the chin and ran a finger over his uneven, feeble two-day stubble. He meticulously palpated the lymph nodes, and then his bold fingers tapped the back, extracting a hollow sound, a rumbling like from the underworld. He did this very carefully, centimeter by centimeter, like a sapper searching for a hidden bomb. All of this lasted half an hour, until at last the doctor sighed and told him to dress. Only now did he reach for the letter. And he spoke, peering over the metal frames of his spectacles:

“*Phthisis*.” It sounded as if he had whistled. “*Tuberculosis*, consumption, or as is fashionably said today: *Morbus Koch*. You know all this, young man, don’t you?”

Wojnicz buttoned up his shirt and nodded in agreement.

“Not very advanced, I’ll be honest. Just a little something, a grain of something. ‘*Phthisis*’ means decay, you know?” He pronounced the word “*Zerfall*” with distinct pleasure, rolling the “r.” “But here we know how to deal with decay.”

“Yes, Dr. Brehmer’s method...” Wojnicz began, but the doctor stood up impatiently and waved his raised hand.

“Oh yes. Brehmer noticed that traveling to Italy with tuberculosis makes no sense whatsoever. Only mountain air truly heals. Air like this. Have you seen?” The physician walked to the window and fell into thought for a brief moment. “We are in a basin here”—saying this, he made sweeping, exaggerated circular motions with his arms, as if to make the listener more keenly aware of the nature of this phenomenon. “Beneath us lies a vast underground lake that makes it warmer here than elsewhere. This air is rich in oxygen, but there is no wind. Pulmonary diseases and epidemics have never been known among the local populace, would you believe it? No one here has ever suffered from the lungs. Additionally, the altitude remains within the limits necessary for treating lung diseases, because, again, it does not excessively accelerate the heart rate, as happens in places lying above nine hundred meters above sea level. A fir forest grows here, saturating the air with ozone, and ozone plays a crucial role in renewing the blood and the entire organism. Breathing alone will halt the process of decay in your young lungs. Every breath is curative, consider it that way. Imagine that with every breath, pure light flows into your lungs.” The doctor looked at Mieczysław through the lenses of his glasses, which magnified his dark eyes in an unsettling manner. “And besides that, we have other attractions here. You only need to submit yourself, subject yourself to the regimen of treatment. Feel as though you are in the military.”

He walked to the window and with a nod of his head showed him the patients strolling in the park.

“Those are your comrades-in-arms.”

Wojnicz suddenly realized he would not like this doctor. He recalled the gentle, kind Dr. Sokołowski.

“That is clear to me, Doctor,” he replied, tugging at his shirt cuffs. “I would only like to know if I have a chance.”

“Of course you have a chance. Otherwise you wouldn't have come here at all, young man. You wouldn't have dared come here if you didn't feel you had a chance. You would have gone on being sick peacefully in the East. It’s flat there, isn’t it?”

Wojnicz was now to learn many interesting things about the brilliant Dr. Brehmer, who had purchased the village of Görbersdorf and the entire surrounding area, over a hundred hectares of forests and land in total, to establish this sanatorium. Brehmer had noticed long ago that the results of autopsies and examinations of living tuberculosis patients always showed a disproportion between the heart and the lungs—their lungs were relatively large, while their hearts were small, with thin, flaccid, and weak walls. Previously, no one had paid special attention to this correlation, and no one had come up with the idea of linking this disproportion of the chest organs with the etiology of tuberculosis. And it seemed obvious that a small, weak heart leads to slowed blood circulation, and thereby to chronic ischemia of the lungs and pulmonary epithelium. The consequence of this was tuberculosis. Furthermore, Brehmer studied the geographical distribution of the disease, which confirmed his conviction regarding this etiology. Travelers’ accounts indicated there are places and regions where tuberculosis does not occur: higher mountains in all climate zones, Iceland, the Faroe Islands, the steppes of Kirghizia.

On the one hand, the specific features of the high-altitude climate were decisive here. Lower atmospheric pressure causes the body to react with increased cardiac activity and an accelerated pulse, as this is how it defends itself against hypoxia—this leads to an accelerated metabolism and a rise in body temperature. Secondly, lifestyle and diet were important: plenty of food, especially fat, kumis containing alcohol, heavy physical labor.

An accelerated pulse and accelerated heart rate cause an increase in the volume of the heart muscle, and strong musculature of this organ is formed; often, among the inhabitants of the aforementioned regions, it even leads to hypertrophy of the heart—a phenomenon entirely opposite to that in tuberculosis patients.

“My dear young man,” Doctor Semperweiß concluded his lecture. “That is our entire prescription. In Central Europe, the zone free from tuberculosis begins at an altitude of roughly four hundred and fifty meters. Add to this the constant supervision of a doctor who regulates the diet. Furthermore, exercise in the fresh air. Nature herself heals us.”

Doctor Semperweiß pulled out a sheet of paper and wrote out his recommendations in bullet points, commenting on them aloud in an already slightly bored voice:

“At least six weeks, ideally a few months. Walks tailored individually to the patient, absolutely on paths with varying gradients; there must be benches at short intervals along the way so as not to tire yourself. Moderate cold-water treatments. And that is what will help you. Moderation in medications. In the event of severe coughing reflexes, I recommend suppressing the cough as much as possible, and when it cannot be suppressed, you should take small sips of cold water or soda water with hot milk. Should bleeding from the lungs occur, God forbid, we apply ice packs to the heart and lungs here, and morphine injections. During severe attacks, with shortness of breath and weakness, we first administer a strong stimulant, for example, champagne. Yes, do not be afraid of champagne and alcohol in general. But absolutely in small quantities. Drunkenness is strictly forbidden! In the case of a fever, measurements are first taken every two hours to confirm the fever. Night sweats are combatted effectively by the evening consumption of milk with two or three teaspoons of cognac or tincture. Sister Sydonia Patek will explain and show you everything.”

While delivering this entire speech, he was writing down the recommendations, and Wojnicz marveled that he could do these two things simultaneously.

“You are staying at Herr Opitz’s, aren’t you? Every day you will come to the *Kurhaus* for treatments and resting cures, and as soon as a place opens up in the sanatorium, I will let you know; everything here is fluid. Fluid,” he emphasized. “For now, Herr Opitz’s guesthouse is just as good for your health as we are or Doctor Römpler’s sanatorium, and daily short walks will put color in your cheeks.”

The doctor stood up energetically and handed Wojnicz the sheet with recommendations. And it was all over; he had been admitted.

He now sat in the waiting room while the ugly nurse prepared his treatment diary and other necessary documents. He pulled the quarter-folded slip of the pamphlet from his pocket and finished his reading:

*In general, it must be admitted that from a medical standpoint, a stay in places such as Meran in Tyrol, Görbersdorf in Silesia, or Davos in Switzerland, which was modeled after Görbersdorf, is considered the most effective cure to date. Dr. Römpler’s sanatorium, founded in 1875, lies almost directly at the foot of the mountains and consists of an appropriate number of buildings in the form of elegant villas. A waterworks 1,140 meters in length brings crystal-clear mountain spring water, gushing straight from porphyry rocks, down from the mountain at whose foot the sanatorium lies, into the elegantly appointed bathing rooms of the shower building.*

*The patients are not lacking in activities and entertainment. The cure itself, dinners, etc., take up a large part of the day, and the immediate charming surroundings of Görbersdorf offer numerous excursions. The aim of the cure is for the patient himself to strive to combat his illness. He must be made resistant by strengthening his organism. In this way, the progression of the disease will initially halt, then gradually the suffering will recede, and health will begin to return. Through constant gymnastics, the affected lungs learn to function correctly; fresh mountain air stimulates the heart's activity. The results of the Görbersdorf cure can be counted among the most favorable. Almost 75% of patients return to health.*

It would be wonderful to believe that he belonged to those seventy-five percent.

2.

Schwärmerei

Wojnicz was returning to the guesthouse with a small notebook in which his treatment history was to be recorded from now on, pondering what Doctor Semperweiß had told him. The most important thing in the cure is the regimen. You get up early, very early in the morning. Temperature measurement. Recording it in the notebook. Before breakfast, which is eaten between seven and eight, gymnastics are mandatory; after breakfast, a walk, and along the way, possible baths using Father Kneipp’s method, as well as treatments. Walks along a designated route. Second breakfast at ten—always fresh bread with butter and milk. A rest cure on one of the many terraces. Dinner between twelve-thirty and one-thirty (soup with meat, a hearty meat dish with vegetables, then dessert and compote; on Sundays, a sweet pastry or sweet flour dish is served instead of compote). After dinner, mandatory coffee in the winter garden or in the pavilions. A rest cure again, another walk, but the afternoon route must differ from the earlier one. Afternoon tea around four or four-thirty, and supper at seven—hot meat with potatoes, and absolutely a glass of milk with it. In the evening, the thermometer again and a few sentences in the notebook about how he is feeling. Plenty of sleep. No excitement. Good, hearty food. Plenty of meat, milk, and sheep's cheese. Wojnicz had decided on dinners and breakfasts in the *Kurhaus*; he was to eat his suppers at the Guesthouse for Gentlemen. This was what had been advised. When he moved to the *Kurhaus*, he would take the rest of his meals there. Guests are summoned to them by the sound of a trumpet.

He was bursting with excitement and good intentions; it was the kind of euphoria that comes when you start something new, something that irrevocably heralds a new beginning, when a man cuts himself off from the old and consigns what was to oblivion. Now, even the stern and ironic Semperweiß seemed to him a herald of change.

As he walked, he tried to memorize how the houses and guesthouses were laid out. He looked at the rather bizarre building of the astronomical observatory, where Dr. Brehmer supposedly studied the influence of the cosmos and weather on the treatment of tuberculosis. Then he reached the massive edifice of Villa Rosa and turned back.

The full, golden September sun was shining. Mieczysław Wojnicz aimed his feet at the center of the large flat stones paving the road.

The two elderly women were still sitting on the bench in front of the house, silently shelling broad beans, snapping the dry, crackling pods. One of the beans suddenly popped out of one of the wrinkled hands and landed right in front of his shoe. He picked it up carefully with two fingers and wanted to return it to the owners, but for some unknown, sudden reason, they sprang from the bench and, grabbing their bowls and baskets, vanished into the house. Only their black, shiny skirts flashed in the sun. Well, no harm done. Wojnicz wiped the brown bean against his sleeve; it seemed perfect. He tossed it in the air and caught it. Not knowing what to do with it, he slipped it into his pocket.

He found the door to the guesthouse wide open, which surprised him, and immediately afterward he saw a prayer book on the ground, thrown straight into a puddle. The cream-colored pages had already soaked up the dirty water. He picked up the prayer book and stepped inside, filled with a sudden unease.

The sitting room downstairs was empty—presumably all the residents of the guesthouse were still at their treatments. He placed the mud-stained little book on a table and was just about to head upstairs when his attention was caught by the half-open door to the dining room, and behind it, boots—on the table, seemingly familiar. Without thinking, as if hypnotized, he approached the door and pushed it open to inspect everything up close.

The boots were the bottom part of an elongated bundle of undetermined shape, which ultimately turned out to be a human body. It lay in the dining room on the table where meals were eaten. It looked well-wrapped in swathes of fabric—it seemed to Wojnicz that it was wearing a multitude of skirts, shifts, corsets, capes. Wojnicz had never seen a woman so closely and so motionless; they always scurried by, they were in motion. It was impossible to focus one’s attention on them and notice all the details. But now he had such a body in front of him, and without a doubt, it was a dead body. He stared at the black lace-up boots protruding from beneath the skirts and petticoats. The latter were trimmed with some embroidery, but the lace was already somewhat washed out, its edges slightly frayed. The bootlaces were meticulously tied in a double bow; it was strange that someone who is dead by the afternoon would have laced her boots so precisely that very morning. An overskirt, sewn from a slightly shiny fabric in thin black-and-gray stripes, lay neatly. Higher up was something like a tight jacket of dark, almost black broadcloth, fastened with round buttons, the kind Polish priests have on their cassocks. Sticking out from beneath this piece of clothing was a white shirt, rather disheveled, with a missing button of which only a thread remained; the collar of this shirt was now pulled high up under her chin, yet loosely enough that Mieczysław noticed a bluish-red welt on her neck, shocking against the white skin.

In the end, he had to do it—look higher, at the face. With horror, he saw the half-closed eyes, and beneath the lashes, a thin sliver of glistening eyeball. The twisted head was turned toward him, as if it wanted to confess something. On the lips, narrow and already slightly blue, he noticed the trace of a smile—it struck him as completely out of place, as if ironic. The tips of her teeth, completely dry, protruded from under her upper lip. And one more thing—the face was covered in fine, pale fuzz, like down.

He stood petrified, barely breathing.

Actually, he recognized right away that this was the woman who had brought him breakfast that morning. Back then, he had only remembered the boot that slid the door open. And the voluminous, corset-bound curves. Nothing more. Only here, after death, could this woman be seen in her entirety.

“She hanged herself,” said Willi Opitz, standing in the doorway.

Wojnicz jumped, startled by the host’s deep, resonant voice. Opitz announced it in a tone as if he were stating some reprehensible negligence, an unacceptable occurrence. But his voice trembled.

“Please do not upset yourself. The men from the morgue will be here in a moment to take the body. Rajmund ran to get them. Go to your room, my boy. Oh, the men from the morgue are already here.”

Wojnicz did not know what to say. His tongue had dried to a crisp; he felt a band tightening around his throat.

“When did this happen?” he only asked.

“When? Just now, well, an hour ago. I went upstairs to her when she didn’t come down to receive the vegetables from the supplier. She was hanging. I cut her down. Go to your room, boy. Oh, here they are from the morgue.”

“She brought me breakfast this morning,” Wojnicz said, and involuntarily, agitation could be heard in his voice. “That is your maid, isn’t it?”

“Ah, no, no. That is my wife.”

Opitz waved his hand as if shooing away a wasp and opened the door for the grim morgue workers, who began communicating with him in a quiet dialect. Mieczysław backed out of the dining room—hurrying upstairs, he heard their muffled voices, but he did not understand what they were saying. The entire conversation struck him as the grumbling of men who do not need words to understand each other.

Wojnicz sat down in the russet armchair decorated with a crocheted headrest. He was shaken. It was strange; it hadn’t occurred to him that the pleasant Willi Opitz had a wife. He should have known that men usually have some sort of wives who, not always clearly and distinctly visible, support the family’s business from the kitchen or the laundry room. Preoccupied with himself, his arrival, and his illness, he hadn't even noticed her. And now she was dead.
