I

He has set down his weapon and struggles to take off his heavy boots, whose smell (feces, musty sweat) adds still more to his exhaustion. His fingers on the frayed laces are dry twigs, slightly burned in places; his nails are the color of his boots, he’ll have to scrape them with the point of his knife to get the grime out—mud, dried blood—but later, he doesn't have the strength; two toes, flesh and earth, poke through the sock; they are fat, soiled worms crawling out of a dark trunk, gnarled at the ankle.

He suddenly wonders, like every morning, like every evening, why these boots stink of shit, it's inexplicable,

no matter how much you rinse them in the puddles you come across, or rub them against crisp tufts of grass, nothing works,

yet there aren't that many dogs or wild animals, not that many, up in these rocky heights sprinkled with holm oaks, pines, and thorn bushes where the rain leaves a fine clear mud and a scent of flint, no shit, and it would be easy for him to believe that the whole country reeks, from the sea, the hills of orange and then olive trees all the way to the very depths of the mountains, of these mountains, or even himself, his own smell, not the boots', but he can't bring himself to do it and chucks the heavy boots against the edge of the gully that hides him from the path, a little higher up the slope.





He lies on his back directly on the gravel, sighs, the sky is purplish, the glow of the setting sun illuminates fast-moving clouds from below, a canvas, a screen for fireworks. Spring is almost here, and with it come the often torrential rains that turn the mountains into bullet-riddled drums, disgorging a powerful spring from every hollow, when the air smells of thyme and fruit tree blossoms, white flakes scattered between the low walls by the violence of the downpour. It would be the devil's own luck if it started raining now. At the same time, it would wash his boots. The heavy boots, the fatigues, the socks, of which his two pairs are equally rigid, stiff as cardboard, falling apart. Betrayal begins with the body,

how long since you last washed?

Four days walking near the ridges to avoid the villages,

the last water you splashed on yourself smelled of gasoline and left your skin greasy,

you are far from purity, alone under the sky eyeing the comets.

Hunger forces him to sit up and joylessly swallow three military biscuits, the last ones, hard brown slabs, probably a mix of sawdust and old mare's glue; for a moment he curses the war and the soldiers,

you are still one of them, you still carry weapons, ammo, and war souvenirs,

you could hide the rifle and the cartridges in a corner and become a beggar, leave the knife too, beggars don't have daggers,

the shit-smelling boots and go barefoot,

the misery-colored jacket and go bare-chested,

his meal finished, he drinks the dregs of his canteen and plays at pissing as far as possible toward the valley.





He lies down again, this time right against the rock face, the bottom of his pack under his head; he is invisible in the shadows, too bad for the bugs (red spiders, tiny scorpions, centipedes with teeth as sharp as remorse) that will frolic across his chest, slide over his nearly shaved skull, wander through a beard as coarse as a bramble patch. The rifle against him, the buttstock under his shoulder, the barrel pointing toward his feet. Rolled up in the piece of tarpaulin that serves as his blanket and roof.

The mountain rustles; a bit of wind rounds the peaks, descends into the combe, and vibrates among the shrubs; the cries of the stars are chilling. There are no more clouds, it will not rain tonight.

Angel my holy guardian, protector of my soul and my body, forgive me all the sins committed this day and deliver me from the works of the enemy; despite the warmth of the prayer, the night remains a wild beast fed on anguish, a beast with breath smelling of blood, cities in ruins scoured by mothers brandishing the mutilated corpses of their children before disheveled hyenas who will torture them, then leave them naked, defiled, their nipples torn off by teeth before the eyes of their brothers raped in turn with truncheons, terror spread over the country, plague, hate, and night, this night that always envelops you, pushing you toward cowardice and betrayal. Toward flight and desertion. How much longer will he have to walk? The border is a few days away, beyond the mountains which will soon turn into hills of red earth, planted with olive trees. II


More than twenty years ago, on September 11, 2001, near Potsdam on the Havel, aboard that cruise ship, a small river liner christened with the beautiful, pompous name of *Beethoven*, summer seemed to waver.

The willows were still green, the days still warm, but an icy mist rose from the river before dawn and immense clouds slid over us, from the distant Baltic Sea.

Our floating hotel had left Köpenick in eastern Berlin very early in the morning, on Monday the 10th. Maja was still alert, sprightly. She would go up to the upper deck to walk, a stroll between the showers, the deckchairs, and the deck games. The green domes and the golden spire of the Berlin Cathedral captivated her, from afar, as we passed. She imagined, she said, all those little golden angels leaving their stone prison to fly off into a cloud of acanthus leaves blown by the sun.

The water of the Spree was sometimes a dark, matte blue, sometimes a glowing red-green. In the preceding weeks, all of Germany had been shaken by storms whose hiccups swelled even the Havel and the Spree, which were nevertheless usually quite low at this late stage of summer.

We sailed through the eddies.





I remember the confluence of the Spree, the wooded islets, the salty light that dusted the tall black poplars, and the muddy flow of the canal that the ship's wake mixed with the waxy waters of the river.

Maja and I were each in a canvas chair, in the sun on the deck, at the back, at the stern as one ought to say, and we watched everything slip away: the landscape widened as if the ship's bow were laying wide open the green fabric of the foliage.

We were celebrating, a few months late, the ten-year anniversary of the Institute's refounding by Paul, while also paying homage to the founder himself. Or, more precisely, we were celebrating the ten years of the Institute's "unification," in the spring of 1991, and the forty years since its creation in 1961. But it was above all a celebration of Paul's work. I believe no one was missing—among the historical members, those from the East, everyone was there; the new members, the colleagues from Berlin and elsewhere had almost all accepted the invitation. A few, including Linden Pawley, Robert Kant, and some French researchers, had even come from abroad. This floating conference was entitled the *Paul Heudeber Days*; two sessions a day were scheduled, number theory, algebraic topology, and a session on the history of mathematics in which I was to take part.

The only one absent was Paul himself.

Maja had just celebrated her eighty-third birthday.

Maja drank liters of tea.

Maja was cheerful and sad and silent and talkative.

We all knew she had no business being there, aboard the *Beethoven* for a mathematics colloquium; we all knew she was indispensable to it.

Most train passengers prefer to sit facing forward.

A historian is a passenger who chooses not to sit facing forward.

The historian of science is a historian who, sitting facing backward, looking to the rear and unlike most historians, does not look out the window.

The historian of mathematics is a historian of science who, sitting facing backward, with her eyes closed, tries to prove that the Arabs invented trains.

No one laughed.





It must be said that I was the only historian at the colloquium. All the others were mathematicians, physicists, or, worse still, logicians. All of them sitting facing forward. Looking toward innovation, invention, discovery. I was the only one who wasn't so much interested in the glorious proofs and inventions of tomorrow as in the sickly-sweet meanders of the past. Meanders of the past that cast their light into the very depths of the future, and I sensed, during that session of the *Paul Heudeber Days* on the Havel, that this audience of scholars would only listen to my presentation on Nasir al-Din al-Tusi and irrational numbers with a polite respect, full of consideration for me and for my mother, who despite her great age would not miss a crumb of the talks, between two strolls on the deck.

Maja was the origin of the idea for this riverboat colloquium; I seem to recall that Jürgen Thiele, the secretary general, had proposed "an afternoon cruise on the Spree or the Havel" to conclude the *Days*, which were initially supposed to take place at the Institute in Berlin; she had pouted, the Spree or the Havel, that's still Berlin at best, Brandenburg at worst, why not the Danube, and Jürgen Thiele's eyes had widened, the Danube, but that's very far, and I imagine Maja had started to laugh, alright, the Havel it is, but at least let the whole colloquium be on a boat and Jürgen Thiele was very embarrassed (he explained it to me later) because he didn't want to refuse my mother anything for these days of homage, but his resources were limited—this whole floating colloquium business continued to seem absurd to him, an old woman's whim.





Thiele was nevertheless surprised to receive two letters on the same day, a few weeks before the call for participation for the *Days* was published: one letter informing him that the mathematics faculty of the University of Potsdam proposed to co-organize the *Paul Heudeber Days* with our Institute, and another stating that the Georg Cantor Foundation was granting (without Thiele having requested a thing) a massive subsidy for the colloquium, which made its organization on the water possible (though still just as nonsensical, he thought without saying a word).

Paul's tragic death a few years earlier had sparked a burning emotion in the scientific community; everyone was ready to participate, and even if most of the organizers (Jürgen Thiele foremost among them, I thought) didn't know the reason for Maja's desire, no one wished to disappoint her. These two letters arrived at exactly the right time, and Jürgen could only suspect, no doubt rightly, that Maja had picked up her pen or the phone: although theoretically retired from politics since the federal elections of 1998, she still had the power to draw "benevolent attention" to the cradles of projects. The Georg Cantor Foundation's money was welcome; Jürgen Thiele got in touch, as co-organizer, with the University of Potsdam, which was celebrating its tenth anniversary, and which Paul had helped found: many of the mathematics professors had been his students.





The *Paul Heudeber Days* would therefore take place on the Havel, aboard a cruise ship capable of hosting, in its conference room, the fifty or so conference attendees; the non-Berlin participants being mostly lodged in a hotel across from Peacock Island, technically located in Wannsee—a hotel bearing the name of a medieval or Alpine inn, *The White Owl*, an inn which Maja assured me (I wondered where she could possibly get such a certainty) had existed at least since the 16th century, but whose current building—Doric columns supporting a monumental balcony, windows with green shutters, climbing roses, like in a fairy tale, softening the facade with their innumerable very dark red, almost black, flowers—had been rebuilt by Karl Schinkel during the first third of the 19th century. *The White Owl* was lost in the middle of the forest, on the shores of the immense lake that the Havel flowed through. Only the Key Speakers and other VIPs of the colloquium were housed on the *Beethoven*, as there were very few cabins; on the other hand, the daytime "sailings" were open to everyone: Potsdam-Elbe on Wednesday, the day of homage proper, centered around Paul's work, then Peacock Island-Köpenick via Spandau on Thursday to close the festivities. Only a few prestigious guests had arrived on Sunday to take advantage of the boat's "repositioning" from Köpenick to Wannsee, and therefore of an extra day cruising through Berlin on Monday.





Jürgen Thiele was full of empathy, disorder, and goodwill. Jürgen Thiele, though still secretary general of the Institute, only took on this task now out of loyalty to Paul, whose student he had been, thirty years prior; he readily admitted that he was tired of organizing, setting up, ordering—putting together a Christmas lunch panics me, he confessed. So a fifty-person colloquium, imagine! The University of Potsdam had assigned him a co-organizer, a young doctoral student in number theory named Alma Sejdić who was trying to prove a corollary of Paul's first conjecture in her thesis. This addition proved as disastrous as it was hilarious: instead of combining, these two forces seemed either to converge uselessly or to cancel each other out. Oversights were forgotten twice, blunders doubly repeated. It looked like a drawing made by two ballpoint pens tied together with a rubber band, parallel lines never meeting, despite all their efforts, constrained by Euclid himself.

Jürgen Thiele had had to mobilize all his diplomacy so as not to offend the University of Potsdam, which didn't understand why they had to fund, just a few kilometers from their campus, the rental of a luxurious cruise ship—but Jürgen Thiele had pulled the Georg Cantor Foundation's grant from his sleeve and everyone had found the idea of a floating conference thrilling.

And so, after a few months of this ballet of chaos, Maja and I embarked, as planned, on Monday, September 10th in Köpenick, in the company of Linden Pawley, whose flight from New York had landed at Tegel that very morning, the inevitable Robert Kant from Cambridge, and Jürgen Thiele—there were indeed five luxurious cabins prepared for us.





III


Every morning since his departure, the cold wakes him shortly before dawn. He shivers. No sudden movements, so that the dew, black pearls on the canvas, does not stream down. Patiently, by folding his tent into a gutter, he manages to fill his canteen with a few centiliters and to drink this icy sweat of the dawn, which will be his only morning meal.

He sets off, once his reluctant feet are wrapped in that knitted sponge of a miserable green, still damp, in the direction of destiny, toward the north, for a name must be given to the debacle and the oblivion. He hesitates once more to abandon the rifle, it is heavy and its strap is awkward, too short since he cut it to make himself a belt, along with that knife, still so sharp, it too the mark of a dangerous solitude, intoxicated with blood, he no longer thinks, he is already walking as the first rays of the sun probe the shadows of the scree. These needles of light rouse the sparrows, the warblers, the titmice, whose wingbeats trail the morning song.





If he thinks so much about the birds, if he is so on edge from their presence and their song, it is because they sharpen his hunger—it would be so easy to lie in wait, nose to the wind, with the rifle, to wait for one of these little birds to give itself away, to shoot it and then eat it, but the power of the military weapon would leave nothing but feathers, the gunshot would echo far into the heights, and even if a fat pheasant or a partridge wandered into his sights, he would have to cook it, and he does not intend to interrupt his march for long, nor expose himself with fire or smoke.

He has resolved to reach the house.

the path advances into the day among the holm oaks, scattered by the drought; a few mastic trees take shelter between the rocks, releasing to the passing walker their apothecary scent, of a forgotten pharmacy; his eyes search for the fresh, wild savory that spring multiplies in the mountain, so he can chew a sprig of it for a long time, bitter, acidic, peppery—arbutus berries still survive the winter like forgotten Christmas decorations, red and rough, they taste like overripe strawberries, the blandness of oblivion.
