I

NATHAN

It was for Rywka that he had returned from America. He used to say that for four years he had thought about this girl every day, until he finally bought a return ticket for a ship. He didn't mention that he had returned penniless, and that he’d even had to borrow the money for the ticket from his older brother. But he still liked to drop American words into the conversation that no one in the family understood.

He had brought a leather belt with a brass buckle back from America. A parting gift from his brother for the journey back to Poland. On the New York pier they had embraced, never to see each other again. You could trace on the belt, by looking at the wear on the successive holes, how his figure had changed over the years. Nathan himself couldn't believe he had once been so slim.

Like his father and grandfather, he looked like a wrestler. An old wrestler. Broad shoulders, a barely visible neck, and a chest puffed out like a rooster's. He was proud that people in town called him "Boss."

On the street, when asked how he was doing, he would answer:

"It's good, but not hopeless."

He would touch his hat and walk on.



Every few months, a letter would arrive from New York. Ben wrote that unfortunately, for the time being, he had to postpone his trip to Tarnów. He couldn't leave the stationery store right now. The school year was starting and he had too many orders. He had to fire a dishonest employee and hadn't yet found a new, trustworthy one to take her place. Soon it would be the Christian holiday of Christmas, the season for gifts, and it's not just Jews who buy from us! His wife had fallen ill again. The crisis had begun; if he left the store now, he would have nothing to return to in America. But he missed them and was planning for next year. He couldn't wait to meet Rywka, their sons, and their daughters.

*As parents, you know best what they need, so buy it for them on my behalf.*

Inside the letter—written on company letterhead (BEN STRAMER GENERAL MERCHANDISE, 33 Grand St, NY) and folded in three—he would slip green banknotes. He would also wrap them in purple tissue paper so that it couldn't be seen what was inside. The tissue paper invariably stained the bills, which emerged from the envelope green-and-purple, and Rywka had to gently wash them, then dry them over the stove in the kitchen.

Nathan gave the children only the American stamps from the envelopes. He set the dollars aside.

"I'm working all the time, so I don't have time to make money," he would explain to his wife.



And he waited. He waited, waited, waited. For a good deal to come along. A deal that would change the lives of the Stramer family. It would lift them out of their one-room apartment with a small kitchen on the ground floor at the very end of Goldhammer Street, carry them high up, and set them down only on the top floors of the tenements with colorful stained-glass windows in the stairwells, in the elegant Polish-Jewish district, ideally near a stop for the tram line of which Tarnów was so proud. In one of those spacious, multi-room, high-ceilinged apartments with a toilet and bathroom with running water, electric light, and a balcony full of flowers.

There was a brief time when it seemed this would come true. He had just bought a whole railcar of rosin on the cheap. He had been told that violinists use it to rub on their bow hair. How was he to know there were so few violinists in Tarnów? In the end, he bought a violin and bow himself and ordered Salek—who Rywka said had beautiful, long fingers—to play it.

Even though he had never been to the philharmonic or the opera, in his mind's eye he already saw his seven-year-old son there—on the stages of Kraków, or even Vienna. For Rywka, too, it was easy to imagine him in a little tuxedo, with a bow tie and a mop of curly hair. Unlike his brothers, he didn't like fighting or getting dirty.

"Here," Nathan said, handing him the instrument. "A famous violinist never did any family any harm."

And it was a known fact how much could be made from ticket sales.

But nothing came of that, either.



True, he did send him to a Polish teacher for violin lessons a few times. But did Salek actually get there? Because every time guests came over and Nathan asked him to give a "little concert," Salek would make excuses. Until half a year later, Nathan stopped asking and ordered him to "bring the violin this instant."

After the performance, he remained silent.

Only when the guests had left did he say, shaking his head on his barely visible neck:

"Goddammit!"

No one understood, so he added:

"Money down the drain."

That evening, for the first time, instead of reaching for his belt, he reached for the bow. At least it would be good for something! And indeed it was. He didn't even have to hit him hard for Salek to produce sounds similar to the ones he himself had been drawing from his violin during the "little concert" just a moment before.

At work, behind the cash register in the Jewish slaughterhouse, Nathan was also waiting for the deal of a lifetime. That was why, in the fervor of his thoughts, plans, and meticulously calculated future profits, he sometimes gave customers the wrong change.

"Shit," he would say if money was missing from the till at the end of the day.

But sometimes there was too much money. Then he would say nothing, and just discreetly slip the difference into his trouser pocket.

*I have children*, he thought.



When the right moment finally arrived, he felt like running all the way home from work. He would do the shopping on the way. At home, he wouldn't sit down for a minute. He would help Rywka prepare the food. During supper, he would ask the children how things were at school. That he lacked the patience to listen was another matter entirely. *But at least he asked*, Rywka thought. Then he would ask her to sit down and rest. He collected the plates himself, rolled up his shirt sleeves, and washed the dishes in the basin.

Only when he had finished, and the children were already asleep, would he take the rolled-up dollars out of the metal leg of the bed.

"I have a feeling it'll work out this time. That this will be it."

Rywka said nothing. But Nathan knew what she was thinking:

*Why, on your last deal, didn't it occur to you to take a proper look at even one candle?*

He hadn't wanted to waste any time; he was afraid other merchants would beat him to it, that someone would offer a higher price. But most of all, he was afraid that the seller, who had come all the way from Kołomyja, would figure out how much candles cost in Tarnów and realize he was selling his for a pittance. Nathan had to act fast. An opportunity like that surely wouldn't present itself again. Candles four times cheaper than from the famous Jewish factory Pszczółka, which supplied all the churches in Tarnów! And maybe even in Kraków. That was what he had heard, but he didn't know if it was true or just advertising. He had also heard, for that matter, that the owner, Mr. Szpilman, secretly gave money to the socialists. But that, in turn, could be a smear campaign spread by Pszczółka's competitors.



At the moment he paid, Nathan had even felt like a crook for a second. Maybe he should have thrown in something extra for this poor seller from Kołomyja, or at least invited him to dinner? But that feeling was immediately replaced by regret that the seller didn't have more goods to sell. So maybe there was no point after all.

That was how Nathan answered, in his thoughts, the question Rywka hadn't asked him.

They sat across from each other at the slightly wobbly kitchen table; the floor was uneven, and the little piece of wood must have fallen out from under the table leg again. Nathan, as usual, tried to slide it back in with his shoe. He didn't succeed, so he bent down, found the splinter, and wedged it back in place. *Tomorrow morning, when it gets light, I'll finally clean it and glue it in place*, he resolved.

They both knew that none of his explanations would convince her, and that was probably why they conversed without words. Or maybe they just didn't want to wake the children? They only looked at each other, but for a moment it still seemed to Nathan that in Rywka's light eyes, instead of the single candle burning on the table, he could see the thousand he had bought without wicks.

What did it matter that, when he realized it, he had immediately run to the train station and spent his last pennies on a platform ticket? The train carrying the merchant and Nathan's still slightly purple dollars had long since departed for Kołomyja. Nathan had stood among the people on the platform for a long time, repeating incomprehensible American words, before he admitted to Rywka what had happened. And in the evening, despite the closed window, his children's crying could be heard on Goldhammer Street.



After that, he got into bed and didn't get up for work for a week. At the slaughterhouse, Rywka said the same thing she told the children:

"Nathan is sick with the stomach flu."

*This time it will be different*, his gaze said now.

It made no impression on Rywka that his eyes were shining again, just like in the days when she first met him. Sometimes she only wondered where this energy of his, coming and going in waves, came from. She even asked Nathan once.

"I brought it back from America," he said.

She thought then of the sea he had told her about after his return to Tarnów. It too was tossed by waves, it had high tides and low tides. It rarely stood calm—like her.

"More likely you caught it at sea," she smiled.

In any case, she decidedly preferred stories about the sea and the great ships to those about New York. They calmed her. She liked to imagine them before falling asleep.


RYWKA

Nathan had promised many times to take her to the sea, but that was before their marriage and the children. Thus far, the furthest she had ever been was Kraków.

Of course, in her youth she had been offered the chance to go to America. What girl hadn't been? She had been stopped on the street by a pleasant, graying man with a signet ring on his little finger. A Jew, of course. He introduced himself as an entrepreneur, the owner of a thriving factory in Nebraska, and "above all, an inconsolable widower who has returned to Europe in search of love."



This search even led him to her parents. Rywka's father threatened to go to the police. It wasn't by chance that Galicia was called "Scandalicia" back then. Everyone knew what it was really about and who they were really looking for. Even so, two of her friends from Nisko, Nesa Schnur and Lila Flaum, decided to go. Or perhaps their parents had persuaded them and pushed them out of the house? She had heard they ended up in Argentina or Brazil. She never saw them again.

In Nisko, it was said that pimps called pretty girls "silver spoons," "bales of silk," and "Smyrna rugs." Less attractive ones were "barrels of flour," and ugly ones "sacks of potatoes." She still remembered these terms. Maybe because the girls used to call each other by them. She also wondered which category they had put her in.

She realized she was too short to be a silver spoon. But she did have dark lashes framing her light eyes, thick black hair, and a shapely waist. So she probably wasn't a barrel of flour, much less a sack of potatoes.

Now, on Goldhammer Street, those times seemed unreal to her. She had gotten married, moved to Tarnów, and given birth to children. Much had changed in her life. But had it in the world? You still heard about pimps trafficking in "tender meat." All in all, not that many years had passed. After all, she still occasionally caught herself starting a letter by mistake with the date eighteen hundred... Somehow she couldn't switch over to this twentieth century.

Maybe because she rarely read the papers. Who with six children would have time for that?



Actually, she had given birth seven times. Her first son, Kuba, was born prematurely, without eyelashes or fingernails. She reproached herself for having needlessly helped Nathan, while pregnant, paint and furnish their first apartment, which was even smaller than the one on Goldhammer Street. And once she had even slipped and fallen in front of the entrance. She should have stayed in bed. Anyway, it didn't matter anymore.

It was winter; they bundled him in the warmest featherbed, but it didn't help. He lived less than three weeks. It was so unnatural, organizing a funeral for your own child, walking through the snow behind your own son's coffin. She forced herself to cry. She couldn't, so she covered her face with her hands. So others wouldn't see that she wasn't crying.

The little coffin hadn't been expensive, but they had still spent everything they had on it. They buried Kuba by the cemetery wall.

The night after the funeral, she was awakened by muffled crying. She pressed herself against Nathan, and only then did she begin to cry herself.

She didn't know if Nathan missed their little one so much too. She didn't know if he was also afraid that Rywka wouldn't get pregnant again. Like his sister-in-law Pepi, who, even before leaving for America, had also given birth prematurely and ended up in the hospital with the baby, but had come out alone. From what Nathan said, this loss had been the deciding factor in their departure. On the other hand, it was a known fact that America had the best doctors for such matters.



But Ben and Pepi, in their letters written in even handwriting on company letterhead, never mentioned a pregnancy or a child. It was in the letters flowing in the opposite direction (and possibly passing each other somewhere at sea, Rywka thought) that Nathan, in his nervous handwriting, would inform his brother every time of the arrival of another Stramer into the world and described the rest of them. Maybe that was exactly why they never managed to come to Tarnów. Even though they were, after all, closest family. Yet not once did they forget to put money in the envelope.

This was easier for Rywka to understand than for Nathan. Because what if it were the other way around? If we were the ones left childless, and instead of dollars, news of more American nephews and nieces came flowing from across the ocean? How would we feel?

So she thought of Ben and Pepi with affection. And whenever she thought of them, she was reminded of Kuba.

"The first child is for God," Nathan had said once.

"Don't talk like a rabbi," she had replied.

Who would he have looked like? Would he, like their oldest, Rudek, never consult them on anything and run around town all day? What did he actually do, and where did he get the money he surreptitiously gave her for the household?

She could only guess.




RUDEK

One Sunday, Nathan happened to spot him among a Polish-Jewish gang of youths who, fooling around, were trailing a regiment of Austrian soldiers marching in tight full-dress uniforms down the main streets of Tarnów. Possibly on the occasion of the first anniversary of Emperor Franz Joseph's death, because there was even a band playing a march. That was what Nathan had come to see. Or rather, to take a look at the violinist. At the time he was still predicting a musical career for Salek; he had just bought him a used Höfner violin. And he wanted to see what a real violinist looked like. But why couldn't he see one anywhere?

Like everyone else, he stared at the drum major conducting the band, at his white mace with the brass ball on the end. The ball was sometimes up, sometimes down, sometimes spinning on its axis, and in the finale, flying into the air.

*I wonder how much they pay him for that*, Nathan thought, and froze in anticipation: maybe, just once, the drum major wouldn't catch his ornate stick?

He would have expected that sooner than running into Rudek here. At first he couldn't believe his son was picking up cigarette butts from the ground along with the others, making insulting faces and gestures at the soldiers. And, on top of that, bellowing at the top of his lungs across Sobieski Square:

*At Ulm, at Ulm, at Austerlitz,*
*We took it in the ass and didn't throw fits.*

This particular song Nathan happened to know from his own youth. He began shoving his way toward Rudek.

*Because we Austrians have this way,*
*We take it in the ass and have nothing to say.*



He didn't let him finish the next verses. He dragged his son out of the crowd by the collar. And in front of everyone, thrashed him with the American belt. A few Austrian officers with twirled mustaches turned around without breaking their march; some of them raised their pince-nez to their eyes.

Nathan pulled the belt back through his belt loops and asked:

"Will we ever have it better than under Emperor Franz Joseph?!"

And added:

"And if I ever see you with a cigarette in your mouth again, I'll rip it out along with your lungs."

That was less than a year ago, but it had apparently worked, because lately Rudek could be found every day by the wall of the military barracks on the other side of Goldhammer Street, where the Austrian garrison was stationed, and he kept his cigarettes not in his mouth, but in his pockets.

For a week now, he had been taking Salek there with him after school. Did Rudek look much older, or did Salek look much younger than he really was? Or maybe the brothers gave that impression by contrast. A tall, broad-shouldered boy in a flat cap and plus-fours bending over a slight boy—who was, on top of that, squeezed into a tight school uniform with a high collar—essentially still a child.



Besides, they were separated by more than thirty centimeters or a year and a half. Salek didn't play football, neither on the right wing, in a blue jersey and white shorts, like Rudek for Samson Tarnów, nor in any other position for any other team. He didn't play anywhere, or anything, at all. He hadn't learned to whistle on his fingers or spit properly. He spilled lemonade all over his uniform when they shared a bottle outside the shop. And worst of all, he still occasionally peed on his trouser legs under a tree, even though Rudek had shown him how to avoid it a couple of times.

"You can shake it for two weeks, but a drop will always fall in your pants," he instructed him. "But not *on* your pants."

It was the same with boxing and wrestling. Rudek had Salek practice on their younger brothers, Hesio and Nusek, also without much success. Finally, he more or less taught him one strike—a headbutt to the nose. It was after this that Hesio stained his shirt with blood, and it ended with the American belt for all three of them.

In any case, when someone picked on him, Salek in his anger would at most clench his fists and spin his arms like windmills.

*He's in no danger anyway, because everyone knows whose brother he is*—Rudek felt a warmth inside. But he also prepared him just in case, if in another neighborhood they didn't know and someone tried to hit Salek. He was to say that he knew Omega, Zachar, or Bänder. There was probably no one in Tarnów who didn't think of them with fear.

"No one will touch you."



He didn't admit to Salek that he didn't know them himself either. Because did others need to know that?

But everyone *did* know that Omega, Zachar, and Bänder, if they weren't currently sitting in prison, could be found in the evening in the company of prostitutes outside the Hotel Polski on Dworcowa Street. But everyone preferred to avoid this. It was said they feared no one.

Ostensibly by accident, they would bump others with their shoulders, push pedestrians off the sidewalk, and knock the headgear off their heads. School caps, flat caps, hats, yarmulkes, and the fur shtreimels of the Hasidim landed in the mud and puddles that, even after a light rain, were plentiful there at any time of year.

And that was just the beginning. Then the knifemen waited for a reaction. Would the victim muster the slightest remark, even if expressed only with a look, the tiniest gesture of displeasure, visible in the dark only to their eyes?

"You think you're a tough guy?"

And immediately they would step so close you could smell what they had eaten and drunk that day in the third-class restaurant at the train station.

Of the three of them, only Dawid Bänder didn't strike right away. First he played with his knife. And this was feared the most, because Bänder was unpredictable. He might let one guy go, and suddenly stick a knife in another.

"Shall I circumcise you, tough guy?" he had supposedly once asked a terrified Prince Roman Sanguszko, whose car had broken down near the station at night.



Rudek realized the significance of such an encounter. In school, they taught that the Sanguszko family were the former owners of Tarnów, and on the street, that Bänder was the great-grandson of Idel Muc, the legendary chieftain of Tarnów thieves. And since Rudek was one of the most diligent students in both places, he understood that descendants of the city's rulers had stood face to face.

"Shall I circumcise you, little tough guy?" Rudek and his brothers had jokingly reenacted this scene many times in the courtyard on Goldhammer Street.

"Anything but that! Mercy for His Highness!" Salek, in the role of Sanguszko, would cover himself with his arms and faint against the wall of the wooden outhouse.


SALEK

He had picked that up at the theater. As a birthday present, he had gotten a standing-room ticket from Rudek. They were playing *Samson and Delilah*, directed by and starring Dante Baranowski in the male lead. During the performance, in the chilly and half-empty Sokół hall, he couldn't concentrate on the actors throwing themselves around the stage, alternately roaring with rage, weeping, and fainting. In Delilah, he instantly recognized Jadwiga Geron, the elegant wife of his gymnasium Polish teacher, to whom he would bow in the school corridors. Should he now at least discreetly bow from a distance, as usual? He didn't want her to feel offended. He also didn't know if he had the right to sit down, even though more than half of the red seats were empty. Who could he ask? Besides, he kept wondering if Dante Baranowski had actually been born as Dante Baranowski.



Maybe before, he too had been an ordinary Salek or Jurek. And only as an adult, in order to appear on a playbill and perform on stage, he first had to change his name to one that sounded more artistic. It reminded him of Goldhammer Street, which, when they moved there, had still been called Zdrojowa.

And how would that sound: Dante Stramer?

After the performance, Rudek was waiting outside the Sokół building.

"Rudziu, why is Baranowski's name Dante?" Salek asked on the way home.

"Baranowski's mother simply happened to be Italian, and Dante is a typical Italian name."

How did he do it, that he knew everyone and so much about them? Salek looked up at his brother in admiration.

But he, too, was capable of impressing Rudek.

He had quickly gained popularity at the gymnasium, even in his first year, when during a break, at the sight of Father Wątorek, the Catholic religion teacher, and Izaak Bleiweiss, the Jewish religion teacher, strolling arm in arm across the uneven courtyard, he tossed out to his classmates:

"The Old Testament is walking arm in arm with the New."

True, some claimed someone from the upper forms had come up with it, but Rudek didn't believe them. After all, the indisputable witness to Salek's primacy was their friend, a student at the same gymnasium, Romek Brandstaetter.

A moment later, the joke had swept through the entire roaring courtyard and the brick school building. And the braver ones started shouting it in the direction of the religion teachers.

"Do you hear that, Professor?" Wątorek finally asked.

And Bleiweiss replied:

"Why shouldn't I hear it, Monsignor?"



Various rumors circulated about them around the school. For example, it was whispered that they liked to walk arm in arm so often because neither of them had a wife.

Salek neither listened nor took notes in his Mosaic religion classes. He did his math homework, wrote Polish essays, and prepared for the tutoring he gave after school. And when he finished, he looked out the window at the people walking down the street. He thought about how he, too, would walk that way after classes, and those who saw him through the window then would simply see a boy walking down the street. There was nothing unusual or extraordinary about it, and he himself didn't know why he frequently returned to the thought that in a moment he would be to others what they were to him now.

He also looked at the trash can at the intersection across the street. He counted how many people between one bell and the next bent over it and stuck their hands inside. It was already the fourth person now—even though a quarter of an hour hadn't passed since the bell—an old woman rummaging through the trash, probably looking for scraps of food. Before her had been a boy, at most his age, who with a swift motion had scooped bent cigarette butts into his coat pocket. Rooks also flew to the trash can, and they not only made the most noise and mess, but also ruined the reputation of the others by tossing trash onto the sidewalk. But these Salek was unable to count.



The Hebrew alphabet seemed sad, gloomy to him now. He associated it with the poorest part of Tarnów, the old Jewish quarter. The letters hung down like the sidelocks, beards, and overly long black coats, eternally muddy at the bottom, worn by the residents of Grabówka.

He already knew everything Bleiweiss was teaching, anyway. Once, it had been interesting, he could have liked it. Fairy tales for the three-year-old boy he was when his father first led him by the hand to the cheder. Not the one closest to home, where Rudek had gone the previous year, but much further away. Salek's legs hurt; he was practically running to keep up with his father. He didn't notice the curb, tripped, fell on his hands, but he also dirtied his pants, and on top of that, they tore at the knee.

"Goddammit!" his father jerked him upward.

Salek burst into tears; he didn't want to go to the cheder anymore.

"Or I'll send you to the one near the house."

A year earlier, Rudek had run back from there with a fever and a resolution that he would never return. It took a long time before his mother pulled out of him what had happened. Only when she promised she wouldn't repeat it to anyone did Rudek tell her that the melamed not only yanked ears and beat with a cane for badly repeated Hebrew words. He had ordered him to strip naked and stand in the corner of the room, and the other children were supposed to come up and spit in his face. That was when he ran away.

"What did he make you do that for?" she asked.

"Because I didn't believe the Jews crossed the Red Sea dry-shod."



Salek wordlessly kept following his father. All day at the cheder, he kept his hand over his knee and, together with the others, repeated fragments of the Torah after the melamed.

It was true that the old teacher never raised a hand or even a voice to the children, but on the other hand, for all questions and doubts, he always had one unchanging answer:

"Silly boy, that is how it is written in the Pentateuch."

Salek heard the same thing when, after a year of study in that damp room that was simultaneously the melamed's entire apartment, he dared to ask his first question, carefully prepared over many days:

"Why, at the sight of children sitting on a wall and laughing at the bald spot of the prophet Elijah walking beneath the wall, did God send two raging she-bears out of the forest, and they tore forty-two children to pieces?"

It was Rudek who finally explained to him that one simply had to respect one's father and both grandfathers, even if they were bald under their hats.

At any rate, fortunately, God repeated nothing quite so cruel in the cheder. Like the melamed, He remained silent at the sight of the oldest boys who peacefully played cards, buttons, and slaps under the table. He remained equally unmoved when they stood behind the teacher and pretended to dance the waltz with him, or when, with disgust, holding their noses, they used two fingers to take his old, darned long johns and socks out of his wardrobe, or imitated a dog that had mistaken the old melamed for a bitch.



*Maybe it was because the melamed was a long way from the power, holiness, and clarity of mind of the prophet Elijah*, Salek thought. *Or maybe it was because he wasn't bald; he had long, matted gray hair sticking out from under his yarmulke.*





II


NUSEK

In Tarnów, they said that the further from Grabówka, the cleaner the air. Nusek breathed only through his mouth there. The smell of that neighborhood brought to mind the outhouse on Goldhammer Street or a Friday-to-Saturday night when, after eating too much cholent at the Sabbath dinner, the children couldn't fall asleep for a long time.

"PKP?" Salek or Hesio would ask every so often.

"RKM," Rudek would answer them.

"No!" Rena would yell, if she wasn't asleep yet. "I won't allow it. Go to the courtyard."

At night, the two-room apartment on Goldhammer Street turned into a bedroom. Three beds took up the entire room, and a fourth the kitchen. In the middle stood the chairs and the table—you had to remember about them, especially in the dark, on the way to the outhouse. But Nusek stumbled, or at least bumped into something, almost every time.

Rywka had made sure that each of the children had as much space as possible. She decided that the tallest and oldest son, Rudek, would sleep in one bed with the youngest, Nusek; next to them, Salek with Hesio, who was only slightly smaller than he was; and a bit further away, behind a screen, the girls, Rena and Wela, the last to be born. Only the dog, Suchard, and the cat, Milka, chose for themselves every evening at whose feet they would spend the night.



"What does PKP mean?" asked Nusek. "What does PKP mean?" he repeated, as if he thought Rudek hadn't heard.

"You'll smell it yourself in a second."

"Why do you guys never tell me anything? Why?"

"For the public good. Go to sleep."

"Just tell me, what is PKP?"

"Polish State Railways."

"But really?"

"Really. Go ask at the station."

Nusek wondered why they never took him seriously. And how many times had he begged his older brothers to take him with them to the barracks? Don't even ask.

Surely they couldn't still be hung up on that stupid story with the bow. Especially since he had done everything Rudek had told him to. He had meant well, the best proof being that he had tried—even too hard.

Nusek closed his eyes, but he couldn't fall asleep. He could hear his father's steady snoring. Salek saying something indistinctly in his sleep. And the creaking of the bed when Rudek turned over and laid his head on their shared pillow.

He felt his warm breath on his face.

*Why do I always have the least of everything?* he thought. *Space in the bed, pillow, even the featherbed, which Rudek yanks away from me every night?*

"Are you asleep?" he heard Hesiek say.

He opened his eyes.

"Why?"

"Wanna know what PKP and RKM mean?"

"Yes. Tell me."

"Will you carry wood and coal from the shed in the courtyard for me for a week?"



Nusek still couldn't fall asleep, but for a different reason now. Every so often, he looked with admiration at Rudek, sleeping next to him with his mouth open. Maybe he really did deserve the entire shared pillow, and even the featherbed? Nusek wanted to laugh just thinking about PKP and RKM. What wise guys! That way, they could say it at school in front of the teachers, or at home in front of their parents. He never would have thought of it himself. It took someone like Rudek or Salek to come up with that. That it was enough to rename something—and it was settled.

"This head isn't just for wearing a hat," as their father would say when he was scheming his next business deal.

But that saying didn't suit his father, and not just because Nusek had never seen him in a hat. Usually, when leaving the house, he would alternately grab one of two old, crumpled hats from the rack.

That saying was definitely more fitting for the head lying next to Nusek on the pillow.

Again he remembered various situations in which he had heard them say PKP and RKM. He had suspected it was severe cursing or a secret code connected to the cigarette trade, to girls, or at least to communism. And yet—he giggled, because it sounded so elegant: PKP? *Pozwoli Kolega Pierdnąć?* Will my colleague permit a fart? And RKM – *Raz Kolega Może*. Just this once, he may.

He fell asleep smiling. As always, he wanted to catch the exact moment he fell asleep, and as always, at the very last second, he failed.




RYWKA

At night, some of them dreamt of each other. And Rywka was also almost certain that they sometimes dreamed the exact same dream at the same time. *Apparently there are individual dreams and shared ones*, she thought, *like graves in a cemetery, hotel rooms, or cabins on the ships Nathan talked about.*

She could have sworn she once heard the children playing football together in their sleep.

"Pass to me," Nusek pleaded.

"Hesio, to you," Rudek said. "Shoot for the upper right corner."

"Goal!" shouted Salek.

Rywka waited to see what would happen next, but there was only silence.

"Nathan," she pressed up against her husband and kissed him on his thick neck. "You won't believe what I just heard."

"All the children in all the squares and courtyards of Tarnów are playing it," Nathan said with his eyes closed, "and there is no need to wake me up over it."

He knew what he was talking about. The day before, on the front page of a newspaper at the kiosk, he had seen a long headline: *Epidemic of Ball-Kicking Spreading Alarmingly in Our City*. It was especially hard for him to read the last word through the dirty glass. Unlike the first word, whose large, wide letters had drawn him to the kiosk.

As usual, he didn't buy the newspaper; he tried to read at least a few lines on the spot.



*There is not a courtyard here where a dozen or so toddlers, little Jews, or vagrants won't suddenly start kicking a ball, regardless of the weather, through the mud. We only fear lest our ladies take up kicking, for then it would be the end of the world.*

*Maybe he deliberately washes his windows so rarely*, Nathan looked appreciatively at the old, toothless vendor, *so people won't read for free*.

*You don't understand!* Rywka wanted to shout. *They were playing in their sleep!*

But instead she said:

"I'm sorry. Sleep well, wake up healthy."

By morning, she had forgotten about it. She had to get everyone out of the house. To work, to school, to the cheder. In the room, in the dark, she laid out the clean clothes they were to wear. In the kitchen, she lit the fire in the stove. On the table, she placed an evenly sliced piece of whole-wheat bread with butter on everyone's plate and poured chicory coffee. Nathan, as head of the family, additionally had the right to a roll for breakfast.

"Daddy, lend me your roll," said Nusek. "I'll give it right back to you, I'll just eat it first."

At first Nusek didn't know what they were laughing at. After a moment he joined in, and even laughed the loudest himself, as if wanting to make up for the time it took him to understand his own joke.

"I'll give it right back, I'll just eat it first," he repeated proudly, pointing at himself. "I'm smarter than I thought."

Before they left, Rywka washed the children's faces, straightened their collars, combed their hair.

Already at the door, for farewell, she would first stand on her tiptoes for Rudek, and then bend her knees and gradually sink lower and lower, pausing in turn at Salek, Rena, and Hesio, on her way down to Nusek and Wela, by whom she crouched.



"You're descending like a New York elevator"—Nathan had promised never to repeat that again, but again he couldn't help himself.

Rywka always kissed the children in one spot. Her favorite. The spot where the forehead ends and the nose begins. She didn't know what it was called, but she called it the "little saddle."

Every morning the same thing. Thanks to this—as she kept telling herself and Nathan—she had no time to be bored. These mornings had become deeply ingrained as a habit. It even happened that only when she was eating her own slice of bread, or washing the dishes, did she realize that Nathan and the children had already left. As if someone else had woken them up that day, made sure they put on warm underwear, drank their coffee, and didn't leave the house late.





III


NATHAN

"I bought it," Nathan announced in the doorway, as proud as if he were talking about the Tarnów Nitrogen Works and not a sideboard from a second-floor neighbor who was selling off his last pieces of furniture before leaving for America.

"For pennies," he added, after closing the door.

That was probably what he liked best about the sideboard. And he immediately sent Rudek, Salek, and Hesio upstairs.

"What about Nusek?" asked Hesio.

"Too small to be a porter."

Rudek went down the stairs first. So fast, it was as if he were carrying the piece down by himself, and not with Salek and Hesio, who were barely keeping up with him, holding onto the oak legs ever more tightly so the sideboard wouldn't crash down on their brother.

After they banged into the wall for the third time, Rudek spoke up in the style of Anszel Koszcz:

"Gentlemen... Is that what they pay you for?"



Salek asked if they couldn't take a short break so he could tie his shoe. When, three stairs later, Rudek set the sideboard down on the first floor, his hands were shaking.

Anszel, the tallest and broadest-shouldered of all the Tarnów porters, was their informal boss. He would probably have brought even a heavier piece of furniture downstairs all by himself, without problems or helpers. Quite recently, Rudek had watched him milling around the Pilzno Gate. In search of work, he was circling slowly, with a patient step, among the stalls.

*Like the Earth around the Sun*, Rudek thought.

In fact, Anszel almost never stopped. If the porters wanted to talk to him, they had to walk alongside him, orbiting him. He was the one they turned to with everything. He was the highest authority for them, from whom on earth—at least on this uneven and muddy earth by the Pilzno Gate—there was no appeal.

Even though, with a black patch over his left eye and a rope slung over his shoulder, he resembled the sea pirates their father used to scare them with, Anszel kept order. He usually spoke calmly, even with a smile, but it was known what awaited a porter if, for example, he stole a parcel, or God forbid, a client who didn't pay for services. Rudek had more than once seen Anszel swiftly pull the metal shaft out of his handcart.

And how had he lost his eye? Tarnów knew so many versions. Perhaps even Anszel himself was no longer certain how it had happened, and that was why he told everyone a different story.

"Anyway, there might be even more versions than listeners," Rudek explained to his brothers. "Because some people heard a different one every time."



Rudek liked the heroic one best. Years ago, one Saturday in July, hooligans had surrounded the Jewish quarter. They assaulted people and broke into shops closed for the Sabbath. They must have planned it well, because aside from clubs and pieces of iron, they had sacks in which the stolen goods landed.

"It was their bad luck," Anszel would say, never ceasing to circle among the stalls, "that they also attacked the porters' families leaving the synagogue on Fish Square."

Anszel's men took off running for the Pilzno Gate, where, for the duration of the holidays, they left their carts fastened to a single long chain. They took the metal shafts out of them. Jewish workers joined the porters with crowbars and planks. And at the head walked Anszel.

"We drove them out of the quarter. We smashed the wagons they tried to escape in, and let the horses go free. We got everything back. I was the only one who lost something." Anszel pointed to his black eyepatch.

"But you saved the town from a pogrom," they replied with unfailing admiration. Perhaps because the surrounding towns and villages had been less fortunate.

"They got a little slap on the wrist." Every time at this memory, Anszel would modestly shrug his shoulders, which were as wide as the sideboard the three Stramer brothers were struggling to carry down from the second floor to the ground floor.



Salek had heard a slightly less heroic version at school. Before a schoolmate from an upper form repeated it to him, he asked for his discretion. Earlier, this friend had been asked the same thing by his father, bound in turn to silence by one of the Tarnów rope merchants.

"I'm only telling you this," Anszel was supposed to have said in a low, hushed voice to this trustworthy man walking beside him.

Before he became a porter, Anszel had spent a couple of years in prison after catching his wife with a lover. It just wasn't clear whether he had lost his eye during his stint inside, or earlier, in the victorious fight with the lover he had killed.

Salek had heard yet another account of the event from Hesio, in which there was no mention of prison at all.

"It turns out a jealous husband attacked a sleeping Anszel with a knife. He came back early from work at the slaughterhouse and found him in his wife's arms."

The thought of a bloody slaughter with a butcher's knife plunged into an eye silenced the roaring courtyard on Goldhammer Street for a moment.

True, Nathan, who had heard what they were talking about through the open ground-floor window, stated at supper that he remembered from his youth how a drunk Anszel had had a bicycle accident. He fell into a ditch. He must have impaled himself on something there. A dry branch, a piece of wire, broken glass. The next day he woke up without his eye, covered in blood, and remembered nothing.



What made the biggest impression on the children in this story was the fact that their father knew Anszel personally.

"I've met many like him in my life," Nathan waved his hand.

On this occasion, he demonstrated a Jewish porter's blow for the children. He called Hesio over and unexpectedly shoved him with his puffed-out belly. Slight Hesio flew into the wall—where, fortunately, they had not yet placed the elegant sideboard with the protruding horse heads—but tears still sprang to his eyes. When Salek timidly protested that the porters by the Pilzno Gate and in Fish Square were skinny and wouldn't have anything to strike with, Nathan didn't even look at him; he just shook his head.

"In America," he added quietly, to himself.

"I mean a Jewish porter's strike in America," he repeated loudly, as if realizing that to the rest of the family it wasn't at all obvious that the real porters, artisans, merchants, and all the rest of the big-bellied lot were over there, where his brother Ben had stayed. Among truly tall buildings with real elevators. Truly wide, long streets with sidewalks, full of real shops, cafes, and customers paying for everything in the most real of dollars.

"Over there, even the sky is higher," had slipped out of him once.

Rywka sometimes had the impression that Nathan hadn't really returned from there at all. She guessed that he often imagined how his life across the ocean would have unfolded. And that it seemed more real to him than their life in Tarnów, which, as a result, he neglected.



Gently, but thoroughly, she wiped down the carved wooden columns with protruding horse heads at the very top. This elegant oak sideboard didn't fit with the rest of the furniture. With the bench Nathan had cobbled together out of three pine boards, with the chest of drawers with its four barely opening drawers, with the eternally limping table or the wardrobe standing next to it with a cracked back panel. It didn't belong in their cramped ground-floor apartment, or rather, the apartment didn't belong with it.
