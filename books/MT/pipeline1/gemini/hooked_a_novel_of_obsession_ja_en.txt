1

*I want to go swimming*, she thought.

Kicking off her shirt, skirt, and underwear at her feet, she wanted to feel the softness of the water and the refraction of light against her bare skin, drifting endlessly through that silent space, following wherever her heart led. As the temperature of the water blended with her skin, the boundary between herself and the world would blur, and weight, age, and gender would lose all meaning. If she tried to speak, the words would turn into bubbles, rising higher and higher, bleeding into the white distance before eventually vanishing. The hot season was coming to an end. Why was she thinking such things?

Ah, that was it. The early morning office stretching out before her looked exactly like a deserted indoor pool.

Desks lined up in orderly rows like lane ropes, a hushed, pale-blue, empty space that called to mind the surface of the water. Chlorine and ink toner also smelled strikingly similar in that they were both pungent odors that almost triggered a memory. Looking for a time and place where she could be alone to work completely undisturbed, she had ultimately ended up right back here, at the office where she spent the majority of her day. This was a space where she had to show herself at her most competent, yet right now, she could scream nonsense at the top of her lungs or break into a ridiculous dance, and it wouldn't matter. Not that she would ever do such a thing, of course.

Regardless of the situation, she was not the sort of person to shout or dance. 

The Food Products Sales Division, which occupied half of the nineteenth floor of the Otemachi headquarters of Nakamaru Shoji, Japan’s largest trading firm, was completely deserted between the hours of six and seven in the morning. It was a department that handled the quintessential trading company operations: importing food from overseas and selling it to domestic corporations.

The brand-new, untainted air spreading out before her settled Eriko Shimura's mind and body, which were constantly seeking conclusions. As was her duty as the first to arrive, she turned on the copy machine. 

Ever since she was a girl, she had liked to stay one step ahead in everything. This was because her father, a senior executive at the same company, had taught her that "striking first" was one of the most vital qualities of a trading company man. Her parents, whom she lived with in a Setagaya condominium, seemed dissatisfied that their only daughter was absent from the breakfast table, but now that she was in her eighth year at the company, they no longer said a word.

Now thirty, Eriko still had no intention of moving out of her family home. It would be impossible to work while maintaining this level of immaculate grooming and physical well-being without her mother's support. Besides, if she left home, her mother would surely suffocate from the daily routine of being entirely alone with her taciturn father.

No matter how early she arrived, she could maintain her solitary time for barely forty minutes. It was the kind of workplace where the vast majority of her colleagues routinely showed up an hour before the official eight-thirty start time, checking emails with convenience store food in hand. Weaving through the fifty-odd desks, she made a beeline for her station, alternately sinking her TOD'S pumps—polished by her mother to the point where they reflected the very outlines of the fluorescent lights—into the carpet. Half of the Food Products Sales Division were full-time permanent employees, but of those, only two were women: Eriko and her senior by ten years, Chief Sayako Yotsuba. The temporary and contract employees, on the other hand, were all women, but they invariably arrived right before the start of the workday.

The convenience store bag holding today’s breakfast and the Nikkei newspaper rustled crisply against her beige pleated skirt. She sat at her desk and booted up her computer. The low startup chime resonated deeply enough to vibrate at her feet, drawing an invisible arc as it slowly radiated across the surrounding space.

For this moment alone, she was permitted to think of nothing and do nothing. It was perhaps the most peaceful moment of her day. While waiting for her organized desktop to appear, she savored this modest freedom. Now that her annual income had long since surpassed ten million yen, Eriko could say with confidence that the most valuable thing in this world was "time." With enough time, almost anything could be managed. Because everyone spent their days in such a rush, modern society placed a high premium on anything—be it a project, a product, or a piece of art—that had a lot of time invested in it. The reason why proper dining etiquette, letters spanning three or more pages that begin with gentle seasonal greetings, light makeup that looks like bare skin, and well-maintained leather goods are considered wonderful is out of respect for the time poured into them.

To make space for her breakfast, she first took out an alcohol disinfecting wipe and scrubbed her desk clean until it squeaked. She meticulously wiped the crevices of her computer keyboard and the mouthpiece of her phone.

People could mock her as neurotic, but if she didn't go to these lengths, she couldn't feel like this fraction of an overly vast space was truly her own territory. She tore into the mountain of circulating documents, scanning them rapidly, one by one. Her work progressed at several times the speed it would during regular hours, when the constant flow of people and incessantly ringing phones disrupted her. *Time could be created if you were resourceful.* That was the very first thing she had learned from her direct supervisor when she entered the workforce. A few minutes later, when the mountain of documents finally vanished, Eriko pulled the convenience store bag to the center of her desk, feeling the urge to rub her hands together.

She took out a paper carton of café au lait, and a packaged pastry—a new product called "Karinto Melon Pan." She had visited two neighborhood convenience stores this morning, but both were sold out, making this a precious item she had finally managed to find at a shop inside the subway station. Even knowing there was no way it contained anything good for her body, instinctively flipping it over to check the ingredients was probably an occupational hazard.

The major bakery manufacturer Mitsuzaki was a key client of the grain department. Artificial sweeteners, preservatives, food coloring. Even at her age, secretly eating foods her nature-oriented mother would abhor gave her a thrill of almost illicit pleasure. All for just ninety-eight yen. Back in middle and high school, some of her classmates used to buy sweet pastries like this at the school store to fill their stomachs. She was incredibly grateful for her mother's homemade bentos, of course, but Eriko had been inexplicably envious of those garishly colored, massive pastries. She had felt as though the bodies of her peers, so easily satisfied by frivolous junk food, housed carefree, uninhibited inner lives.

Expanding the dropdown list of her bookmarked sites, she hovered her cursor over the URL of her favorite blog and, with a click, plunged into a time that belonged only to her and its author. Following the text with her eyes, she bit into the melon pan, which was nearly the size of her face. The cookie crust shattered with a crunch, and the flavors of butter, brown sugar, and melon flooded her mouth. She was right. The balance of salt and sweetness wasn't bad, but it tasted incredibly cheap. Still, savored alongside the text flowing across the LCD screen, she felt its deliciousness seep into her.

*The idea of mashing up karinto and melon pan is just idiotic, isn't it? I found it at a convenience store and cracked up. But when you bite into that greasy, crunchy dough with its faint, sweet soy-sauce glaze flavor, the refreshing scent of melon opens up like you've just come out of a tunnel. I'm addicted right now. An idiotic flavor. An idiotic price. Knocking out lunch with "idiot food" like this makes things so much easier. It brings me a sense of peace, like I'm not taking life too seriously. Oh, I don't recommend it, though.*

*Not taking life too seriously.* What a great phrase, she thought. To Eriko, life had always been something to face with deadly earnestness, something to wrestle with. As she spaced out, filled with a faint sense of accomplishment and the heavy weight of old oil spreading in her chest, a voice dropped down from above her head.

"Eating like a high school girl, huh, Shimura? You'll get fat."

Regardless of the situation, Yasuyuki Sugishita, a colleague from the seafood team, had a knack for speaking in a way that instantly made you question if you were at fault. While you were reflexively second-guessing yourself, he would relentlessly push his own agenda. This attitude apparently deflected even criticism; despite his not being much of a perfectionist, she had never seen him reprimanded or warned by a superior. In a sales department full of athletic types, Sugishita stood out with his reedy frame, his handsome but slightly long face, narrow jaw, and thin, pallid lips. Possessing the unusual background of having studied architecture in graduate school, he was in the same entering class as her but two years older.

"Good morning. Oh, this pastry? It was featured on a blog. I finally managed to get my hands on one."

Disappointed that her solitary time had been cut short sooner than expected, Eriko reflexively pointed at her computer screen. Sugishita rested a hand on her desk and leaned right in. *Ah, he’s close*—she thought, but she didn't quite feel the need to pull back. The sharp scent of his favorite hair gel stung her nose.

"Huh, a housewife blog? What's so interesting about that to a single woman like you, Shimura?"

She had been reading *Ohyou’s Useless Housewife Diary* almost every day for the past two years. A top page with a blunt, unadorned design. Lengthy posts with few line breaks. The photos she occasionally attached were taken with a cell phone and could hardly be called good, even to be polite. And yet, it consistently ranked in the top thirty of a certain site’s popular housewife blog rankings. Its defining feature was perhaps its lack of the "anti" commenters that usually plagued housewife blogs.

"I know, but she's not overly 'housewife-y.' She's perfectly, effortlessly relaxed. Even though her diet and chores are pretty sloppy, there's a strange sense of cleanliness, or moderation, about her. And the fact that we're the same age makes me feel an affinity with her."

Ohyou lived in a Tokyo apartment with her husband, a supermarket manager. They apparently had no plans to have children for the time being. They didn't seem particularly wealthy, but she showed no signs of working a part-time job. Despite being a full-time housewife, she only cooked meals when she felt like it. Often, she would meet her husband at a family restaurant or a conveyor-belt sushi joint for dinner. Her husband didn't criticize her for this, and they seemed to get along exceptionally well. At the same time, she never went out of her way to flaunt their marital bliss. Eriko also loved her sense of humor in calling her husband "the Demon King" instead of "hubby" or by his first name.

*I want kids, but we never even get to the baby-making part! It’s not that the sex itself is bad once we start, but the lead-up to doing it is just such a hassle, you know? Hey, Demon King, are you reading this?*

She would drop risqué remarks like that with total nonchalance. 

It had been immensely gratifying when she wrote flatly that she refused to collect reward points at supermarkets or drugstores, and never used coupons; it was a matter of principle.

*Those things are stealing our precious time (angry face). All that petty accumulation for what, a five-hundred-yen discount in the end? When you think about the hassle of digging through your wallet for a point card every single time you're at the register, it's so much more refreshing to just ditch the whole thing. If you added up the time spent on that hassle over a lifetime, it'd probably be about ten hours. We aren't rich or anything, so we might be taking a huge loss, but it's just too much of a pain, so whatever~~. I took the plunge and threw them away, and wow, my wallet is incredibly light! So breezy~*

Since she was young, Eriko had hated how her mother stuffed her wallet to bursting with point cards and coupons. Her father had become the president of a Nakamaru Shoji subsidiary quite early on, and despite living a fairly affluent life, it saddened Eriko to see her mother bowing to the whims of retailers just to save a hundred or two hundred yen. At first glance, her mother seemed to move at her own pace, but she was easily pressured and struggled to assert her own will. She was the type who couldn't throw away things she was given, keeping them forever. Perhaps Eriko found it all the more unpleasant because she recognized those very traits in herself. Ohyou probably understood, on an instinctual level, that time was more valuable than money. That was why she could live her own time so freely, unconstrained by anything.

"What's so interesting about a diary like this? It has absolutely nothing to do with your life. It just feels so frumpy and domestic."

For some reason, Eriko felt slightly wounded by Sugishita's complete lack of emotional response, and she spoke faster.

"I like that she's so natural and relatable. I like that she never seems like she’s trying too hard. I just think she's kind of cute. I also love how time seems to flow so abundantly and slowly for her."

"So you enjoy making a fool out of a bored housewife? Women are awful. You're just always eager to find a woman beneath you to make yourselves feel secure."

While she was used to male employees speaking like this, it didn't mean it didn't bother her. When she first joined the company, she had been shocked by how casually misogyny passed as the norm in the office, but by now she had given up on it. The trick was to let it slide and not bite back at every little thing. They meant no malice and were shockingly thoughtless, so taking them seriously was a waste of energy. Getting worked up would only result in embarrassing herself. With her father’s backing, as long as Eriko—a permanent employee—never forgot to look more immaculate than anyone else and worked just as hard as the men, she would be treated as an equal. There was no point in getting irritated by trivial, everyday remarks. She made sure not to let her cheerful demeanor slip.

"Hmm. That's not it at all. Usually, housewife blogs are all about showing off how happy they are, how well they do their chores, or how good their cooking is. They have this overwhelming desire to be understood. But she..."

The phone on her desk rang, interrupting their conversation. She picked up the receiver; it was a tuna supplier from California. Calls from America poured in during this time slot.

"May I have your name, please? When shall I have him return your call?"

After informing the caller that the person in charge was away from his desk, Eriko turned back to Sugishita. 

It was frustrating not being able to explain it properly. She loved Ohyou's seemingly scatterbrained yet razor-sharp way of thinking. Her words flowed pleasantly through Eriko's heart. Though her phrasing was gentle, it conveyed intelligence, and she never, ever hurt anyone. Even though she lived an utterly lazy life, she wasn't ashamed of it, nor did she try to force meaning onto it. There was no trace of anxiety over not having children. Above all, her blog was full of memorable turns of phrase and effortless ideas that made you want to copy them.

For instance, on humid days, she would do chores in her swimsuit, pull a dress over her head, and head straight to the local public pool to jump in. At the library, she borrowed exclusively the works of Sen Ishida. Just to read the entirety of Kiriko Kubo's *Bucket de Gohan*, she frequented an unmotivated neighborhood cafe clearly run as a tax write-off, deliberately ordering the most labor-intensive items on the menu. She and her husband would go to karaoke and sing nothing but Western music. They would order pizza and watch Hollywood movies broadcast on TV. She would hop from convenience store to convenience store searching for Garigari-kun Rich Corn Potage flavor.

Eriko was drawn to how she spent her days doing absolutely nothing, seeking no meaning, making no plans, simply living as whimsically as a cat. When was the last time Eriko had spent a day like that? It had been so long she couldn't even remember. Just reading her blog slightly eased the nerves Eriko kept pulled taut by nightly business dinners and market research.

At all times, Eriko made it a point never to waste a second. Even away from work, she poured her energy into beauty, research, and studying, while naturally prioritizing rest and sleep. She was confident that every single action she took was tied to some form of profit. She had no doubt that the Eriko of today was superior to the Eriko of yesterday. Yet, perhaps since she turned thirty, there were moments when the back of her nose would suddenly sting. She would succumb to a feeling of oxygen deprivation, as if she were flailing her arms and touching nothing. She couldn't help but take a deep breath, tell herself over and over that she was fine, and count out her strengths, her growth, and her contributions to the company one by one. When she really couldn't calm down, she would act unnecessarily humble to draw praise from someone, and only then regain her composure. Yet no matter how much she struggled, with the end of every day and the coming of every morning, she acutely felt her possibilities wearing down a little more, making it harder and harder to correct her trajectory. Was this an anxiety born of age? Would she get used to it in a few more years? When she caught a sudden glimpse of a similar anxiety in another woman, she couldn't stop a twisted surge of relief and joy from seeping out.

But she sensed no such anxiety in Ohyou.

More than anything—she was drawn to the fact that Ohyou's blog featured almost no one but her husband, with a complete absence of female friends. In a sea of housewife blogs prone to boasting about their social networks, it stood out brilliantly. Eriko harbored a secret hope that Ohyou knew how to tame the exact same kind of complexes that she herself struggled with. 

Eriko was saved by the fact that even a woman like Ohyou had no friends. Could it be that even with a miraculously balanced character that offended no one, just a slight misalignment in timing could make it impossible to make friends? Was not having female friends really such a shameful defect? Perhaps it wasn't a defect at all, but just one variation of human relationships.

*I’m a thirty-year-old housewife living with my husband in a run-down apartment in Tokyo. My favorite food is the engawa at Nikkori Sushi, the ninety-eight-yen conveyor-belt sushi place in our neighborhood.*

Reading Ohyou's profile, Sugishita, who had seemed utterly uninterested, finally leaned forward.

"Huh. Her favorite is conveyor-belt sushi *engawa*. If she knows the *engawa* at those dirt-cheap sushi joints is actually *ohyou*—Pacific halibut—instead of flounder, and chose that handle, she's got a pretty good sense of irony."

Pacific halibut could weigh over two hundred kilograms and reach lengths of nearly three meters, so the *engawa*—the meat from the fin—they yielded was enormous. When pulled from the water, they thrashed so violently that in Alaska, fishermen apparently used shotguns to finish them off. Eriko felt as though she herself had been complimented and wanted to puff out her chest.

"Now that you mention it, Sugishita-kun, you were in charge of Russian halibut last year, right? Weren't we doing business with Center Village Holdings too?"

That major restaurant chain was supposed to be the parent company of Nikkori Sushi. Seeing Sugishita's smirk, Eriko instinctively clapped her hands.

"Wow... Amazing. It all connects. Ohyou-san's favorite food is your handiwork, Sugishita-kun."

Filled with emotion, Eriko surveyed the office, where it was still just the two of them. She loved these moments more than anything—when, while chasing the sprawl of numbers and products, the world suddenly felt small enough to grasp in her own hands. Before she knew it, the sun had risen higher, and the late-August rays were slanting into the room. The white carpet, its pile fuzzed from frequent cleaning, began to give off a sun-baked scent.

Working at a trading company, one could sometimes perceive an unexpected depth in everyday scenery. This café au lait, this bread, this newspaper, for example. In which countries were the raw materials born and raised? How were they distributed, processed, and brought here to exist in this moment? If she narrowed her eyes, it was as if each of their stories unfurled before her. Both Eriko and Sugishita possessed this sort of clairvoyance.

Japan's seafood consumption was approximately 6.52 million tons. Forty percent of that relied on imports. Among that total, there were countless nameless fish—species handled as substitutes or fraudulently labeled. Since being assigned to the seafood team, Eriko had become capable of instantly seeing through a processed fish's true identity, regardless of what the label said.

"It makes you feel a little guilty toward the consumers. '*Engawa*' is just the name of the cut, so we aren't exactly deceiving them, but still."

"But if you think about it logically, there's no way you could eat actual flounder or turbot *engawa* for under a hundred yen. The consumers have a vague idea of that too."

"It's less a food fish and more of a little alien at this point. It's scary."

"They eat nigiri made from those aliens and say, 'Yum, yum,' so everyone's happy. The 'fish' in a fish burger, the 'white fish' in a white fish meunière. If you tracked down exactly what kind of fish they are and where they're from, there'd be no end to it. There are truly a lot of things in this world you're better off not knowing."

Though Sugishita spoke in this detached manner, when it came to business negotiations, he displayed endless charm and a shockingly smooth tongue. He gave off a neurotic impression, but he was actually the son of a third-generation kimono merchant in Kawagoe.

"Speaking of which, Shimura, you're in charge of Nile perch starting this month, right? With how much this woman loves conveyor-belt sushi and family restaurants, the fish you deal might end up in her mouth someday."

Eriko flipped through the files she had taken over from her predecessor. The Nile perch is a freshwater fish in the Latidae family of the order Perciformes. At its largest, it reaches two meters and two hundred kilograms. Native to the African continent, it inhabits lakes and rivers. It is a carnivorous fish with a ferocity unimaginable from its mild, delicate taste. In fact, when released into Africa's Lake Victoria, it caused the extinction of over two hundred endemic species of small cichlids. Its light, inoffensive flavor made it easily accepted in Japan as well, and in the nineties it was distributed as 'sea bass' or 'white sea bass,' but recently ninety percent of it had been exported to Europe and the Americas, making it a rare sight here. Eriko had spent the past few months trying to reopen sales channels and set up local facilities. The more she learned about the Nile perch, the more she was hopelessly drawn to it.

"Nile perch just have incredible vitality, don't they?"

"Yikes, it's carnivorous, right? Who knows what it's eating. Scary, scary. Oh yeah, apparently you should watch that documentary, *Darwin's Nightmare*. They say it shows exactly how the Nile perch destroys ecosystems."

"I know I have to watch it eventually, but I just don't have the time. Plus, it's information I'd rather not put in my head until after the next negotiation is over... If humans hadn't released them into the lake, the Nile perch would have lived their whole lives never knowing they were so ferocious. It's kind of sad. Oh, I hear they're popular as ornamental fish too. They're silver, very beautiful fish. They have this, I don't know, lonely look on their faces..."

"You can see them at the new aquarium that opened in Shinjuku, right? Want to go together sometime?"

To his mumbled invitation, Eriko gave neither a yes nor a no, instead pretending to be engrossed in the blog again. She was well aware that Sugishita harbored a faint affection for her. He seemed to have imposed a rule on himself that putting it into explicit words meant losing, leaving him with a pained expression caught between desire and embarrassment. Picking fights with her like this was surely his way of expressing affection, too. Perhaps unwilling to admit he had been dodged, he hastily steered the conversation back.

"Well, maybe it's fine as long as you're just reading her blog. If you actually met her, it would never work out anyway."

"That's not true. I feel like Ohyou-san and I could be good friends."

"Yeah, right. You don't even have any female friends."

She felt her fingertips instantly turn cold. No matter how much effort she put in, no matter how she focused all her nerves on that single point, "she didn't look like she had female friends." The undeniable fact sent her heart pounding. In contrast to her shock, there was something cloying, almost sexual, in Sugishita's tone. When a man sees a woman unable to integrate into the female pack, he feels a faint stir of lust. He even exuded a kind of pride, as if he had uncovered a massive flaw in the enemy camp without getting his own hands dirty. Pretending not to notice, she made sure her voice sounded perfectly carefree.

"Well, true enough. Since I started working, I've lost touch with my group from college. And even when I get invited to girls' nights, I never have time to go."

Accepting the premise to maintain the advantage was a tactic she had learned in business. No such friends existed, nor had she ever been invited to a girls' night. Grown women brazenly calling themselves "girls"—their audacity and childishness sometimes felt blinding to Eriko.

"You don't go to lunch with the temp girls or our cohort from other departments, either."

He didn't state it outright, but apparently, Sugishita was secretly dating Maori Takasugi, one of the temporary employees. That was likely why he was so suspiciously well-versed in the female employees' social dynamics.

"Oh, that. My schedule never lines up with our cohort, and going out to eat with the temp girls requires so much tiptoeing around. A lot of them want to keep it under a thousand yen, so I can't just casually invite them out."

Unable to admit that she simply wasn't invited, Eriko maintained a bright tone.

"Actually, I've probably always been bad at socializing with other girls. I mean, I know I'm saying this about myself, but I'm the intense, overachieving type, right? I was always pushed into being class rep or the organizer, and before I knew it, I was alone. I guess I never really understood those invisible rules girls have, and I always end up alienated without even realizing it."

She could tell she was speaking surprisingly fast. She prayed like a mantra: *Please, don't ask anything more.* Everything past this point was uncharted territory. She had no confidence she wouldn't slip up.

"Well, I'm sure a beauty like you who can do everything without trying just gets envied. Women are deeply jealous and terrifying creatures."

Sugishita's eyes narrowed affectionately. Was he trying to compliment her? He was probably comparing her to Maori Takasugi. Incompetent, chatty, and carrying a soft layer of flab. She was likely being toyed with by Sugishita. But Maori was always surrounded by female employees, and Eriko envied her for that.

*Without trying.* Sugishita had probably never once considered how much time and money went into her flawless skin and glossy hair. Eriko suddenly began to wonder what kind of toner Ohyou used. Was it some cheap brand dumped at a discount at the drugstore? Or perhaps she was surprisingly an avid user of organic cosmetics? She wanted to try using the same one. As she scoured past diary entries, her lips moved.

"Still, it really seems like this Ohyou-san lives in my neighborhood."

"Wait. Seriously?"

"Look, the organic cafe she mentions here, Giselle. It's one station over, but my mom was involved in setting it up a long time ago, so I know it well. In the first place, all of Ohyou-san's favorite fast food and chain restaurants are clustered right around my closest station. Granted, they're the kinds of places you find everywhere, but still."

"It's kind of creepy how detailed you're getting with this. I mean, from her perspective, you sound like a stalker."

Startled by a word she had never once associated with herself, Eriko turned around and stared hard at Sugishita.

"What? What do you mean? I'm just reading her blog. I just happened to notice we might live close by."

"But you're copying this Ohyou's diet, monitoring her daily life, and you live in the same neighborhood. Even if you mean no harm, from her perspective, it's a bit creepy. Don't go overboard."

Cutting himself off, he spun on his heel and headed back to his own desk. Employees were beginning to trickle in. The reason he came to work so early was probably to steal time alone with her, away from Maori Takasugi's eyes. Was it conceited to think so? Over a year had passed since she broke up with her last boyfriend, but she didn't feel lonely yet. She wanted to get married eventually, but right now, commuting between home and work took everything she had.

Had Ohyou-san ever imagined an office lady reading her blog while nibbling on the exact same pastry in an early-morning office like this? Eriko had a feeling that surely Ohyou sensed someone like her was out there. At first glance, Ohyou-san played it cool, but perhaps she was embedding messages meant for many people. *You're fine the way you are. Relax your shoulders. It's okay to be alone.*

As if cherishing a second version of herself in the mirror, Eriko meticulously began to reread Ohyou's diary.

Shoko Maruo paused the fingers that had been updating her blog on her cell phone, and looked up.

*That laundry looks like it's going to fall,* she thought, staring at the denim flapping on the balcony of the apartment building opposite hers. It must belong to a young woman; at thirty, Shoko could never pull off skinny jeans like that anymore. Back in the day, she used to stand on the department store floor in pants so tight they cut off her circulation. Standing for over ten hours a day was the norm. There were months when she only had two days off. What would her past self think if she saw her life now? She might be surprisingly envious.

Either way, she had arrived a bit too early. The organic cafe "Giselle" was a favorite spot of hers, located a fifteen-minute walk from her apartment. Perhaps because it was Saturday, she noticed a few other women dining alone, much like herself. She casually popped the food left on her large plate into her mouth—konjac and chicken liver tossed in mustard mayonnaise, coleslaw with bitter melon, brown rice balls with sesame and red shiso, and rolled omelet with sea lettuce—polishing it off with a speed that surprised even herself. She hadn't eaten such a vegetable-rich, balanced meal in ages, nor had she cooked one for Kensuke. Once again, she felt like a failure of a housewife. Tonight, she would at least simmer some hijiki seaweed. No... just imagining it felt like a hassle, so maybe she'd have them pack something up for her here. She didn't want to wash dishes. Shoko hated washing dishes more than anything.

She had met Kensuke, the manager of a supermarket specializing in imported foods, through a part-time job, and they married three years ago. She generally tried to be frugal so they could live solely on Kensuke's salary, but she hated the idea of letting that constrict her mindset. For small indulgences, she could just dip into her savings from her single days; it was no big deal. Lately, she had even been earning a small income from affiliate ads on her blog. They hadn't held a wedding, hadn't bought rings, hadn't gone overseas. Naturally, they weren't going to buy a house, either. In exchange for foregoing grand luxuries, they wouldn't penny-pinch over the little things. Rather than fighting over who washed the dishes, they would choose to eat out, even if it was at a cheap place. That was the promise they made when they registered their marriage.

Truthfully, Doutor or a family restaurant would have been fine for today, too, but since the person she was meeting was from the media industry, she had named the most stylish cafe she knew. The wood-toned interior was always calming. Hashimoto, a part-timer who came over to clear her plate, suddenly leaned in.

"Shoko-saaan. Thanks for introducing our fall menu on your blog."

"Whoa, too close, too close. Don't scare me like that."

His breath tickled her ear. Hashimoto was twenty-five, the same age as her younger brother, which made him easy to talk to. When she lightly swatted his arm, she felt the firm biceps beneath his shirt, unbefitting his gangly appearance. The hardness that bounced her hand right back was a far cry from the flabby upper arms of her husband, who had recently started putting on weight again.

"On the days we're on your blog, we get just a little more foot traffic than usual."

For Shoko, who preferred family restaurants and fast food, Giselle was one of her few regular independent spots. Since taking a short-term part-time job here just for the New Year holidays this year, she had become acquainted with the staff, and even after quitting, she still visited as a customer once or twice a month. She had turned down their offer to work as a contract employee. She knew that if it became her workplace, the social dynamics would inevitably become a headache, and she would lose the ability to drop by casually like this.

Shoko dropped her gaze back to her cell phone. Time flew by just tracing the comments on her blog. There were almost no remarks condemning her slovenliness. Rather, the overwhelming majority were supportive comments saying things like, *It puts me at ease,* or *You encourage me that it's okay to be this way.* Many people also posted links recommending products or stores. She was happy to have finally found a place where she belonged, but being showered with exclusively kind words sometimes made her feel like she was deceiving her readers. Still, the pleasant warmth of positive feedback was addictive, and she would spend hours rereading the comments. It was perhaps very similar to the sensation of soaking in a low-temperature carbonated bath at a super sento. When the stress was so minimal, it became hard to know when to get out.

Whether online or off, she wanted to secure as many places where she could relax and people she could share a smile with as possible. Eight years after moving to the capital, she still couldn't get used to Tokyo. When she was working, she was too busy to have time to play, and after her health broke down, she had completely turned into a homebody. As the door chime rang, a woman in her forties wearing a suit appeared. She spotted Shoko immediately and strode purposefully toward her.

"Ohyou-san... Shoko Maruo-san? It's a pleasure to meet you. I'm Satoko Hanai from the Third Books Editorial Department at Shumeisha."

Shoko hurriedly stood up, bowed, and accepted her business card. Satoko Hanai had well-ordered features and a decent figure, but her skin was dry and the bags under her eyes were prominent, making it impossible to shake off an impression of exhaustion.

"Wow, I'm surprised. Ohyou-san, you're much cuter and more charming than I imagined."

In her refusal to use the word "beautiful," Shoko recognized the caution of an elite. *She's sizing me up.* She could feel her long-dormant sensors activating. A washed-out T-shirt and Kensuke's old chinos from when he was thinner, worn with zero makeup. She knew perfectly well this wasn't an outfit suited for a first meeting. Since quitting the apparel company, her passionate interest in indiscriminately buying clothes had completely faded. She didn't have eye-catching looks to begin with. Her body lacked curves, she had monolids, and freckles stood out on her pale skin. Her lips were shaped in a way that curled upward, so when she smiled, her gums showed—a complex of hers.

Yet, a certain type of woman, as if by prior agreement, completely despised Shoko.

*She panders to men.*

That was their claim. But she wasn't pandering. It was simply that the consideration and attentiveness women naturally extended to their own sex, she could effortlessly extend to men. There were many moments when she had no idea what to say in front of a woman, but with the opposite sex, her intuition told her exactly what to do. This might have been partly because her parents had divorced, leaving her to be raised by her father and surrounded by older and younger brothers. She wasn't manipulative by nature, nor did she have all that much romantic experience. To be judged like that simply because she felt at ease around men was deeply unwarranted. Because of that caution, she had made it a point to act unnecessarily brash in front of other women.

After ordering a coffee, Satoko Hanai laid out several housewife magazines and housewife blog books published by Shumeisha on the table and launched into a detailed explanation. Truth be told, Shoko wasn't particularly enthusiastic. She had always hated having expectations placed upon her. Reading the writings of housewife bloggers, practically radiating heat as they flaunted their happiness, was exhausting. She had a disposition that sought to avoid bothersome things at all costs. Oblivious to Shoko's reluctance, Satoko rattled on at a rapid clip.

"Currently, housewife bloggers are neatly compartmentalized into various niches, but your selling point, Ohyou-san, is your 'naturalness.' The fact that you have no 'musts' or 'shoulds' is fantastic. Amidst the acceptance of diverse values, there are many housewives who feel conflicted and adrift. And not just housewives—there must be so many people who resonate with your unwavering, at-your-own-pace lifestyle. You don't come off as pushy, and I believe you have a personality that working, single women won't find off-putting either."

"Um, right... But even if a diary like that became a book, who would read it...?"

"Yes, exactly! So, how about adapting your diary and polishing it into a collection of essays? We'd like you to write a lifestyle guide as a relatable woman living her life without trying too hard."
