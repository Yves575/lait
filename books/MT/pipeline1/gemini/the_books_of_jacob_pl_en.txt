PROLOGUE

The swallowed scrap of paper stops in the gullet somewhere near the heart. It becomes soaked with saliva. The black, specially prepared ink slowly dissolves, and the letters lose their shape. In the human body, the word splits in two, into substance and essence. As the former vanishes, the latter, remaining formless, lets itself be absorbed by the body's tissues, since essence perpetually seeks a material medium; even if this should become the cause of much misfortune.

Yente awakens, even though she was already almost dead. She feels it clearly now; it is like a pain, like the current of a river, a trembling, a pressing, a movement.

A delicate vibration returns to the area around her heart; the heart beats faintly but steadily, sure of itself. Warmth flows once more into Yente's withered, bony chest. Yente blinks and raises her eyelids with difficulty. She sees the worried face of Elisha Shorr leaning over her. She tries to smile at him, but she is not quite in control of her face. Elisha Shorr’s brow is furrowed; he looks at her reproachfully. His lips are moving, but no sound reaches Yente's ears. Hands appear from somewhere—old Shorr's large hands, which reach for her neck and slip under the blanket. Clumsily, Shorr tries to roll the limp body onto its side and peer beneath it, at the sheet. No, Yente does not feel his efforts; she feels only the warmth and the presence of the bearded, sweaty man.

Then suddenly, as if by some blow, Yente is looking down at everything from above—at herself and at the balding crown of Shorr’s head, for in the struggle with her body, his cap has fallen off.

And from then on, that is how it is: Yente sees everything.








I. THE BOOK OF MIST

1

1752, Rohatyn

It is late October, early in the morning. The Reverend Dean stands on the porch of the rectory, waiting for his carriage. He is accustomed to rising at dawn, but this time he feels half-conscious and does not really know how he ended up here—alone before a sea of fog. He remembers neither how he got up, nor how he dressed, nor whether he has eaten yet. He looks in surprise at his sturdy boots protruding from beneath his cassock, at the somewhat frayed skirts of his faded woolen cloak, and at the gloves he holds in his hand. He slips on the left one; inside it feels warm and fits his hand perfectly, as if the hand and the glove had known each other for years. He breathes a sigh of relief. He touches the bag slung over his shoulder, mechanically feeling its rectangular edges, hard, thickened like scars beneath the skin, and slowly he remembers what is inside—a heavy, familiar, pleasant shape. Something good, something that has brought him here, certain words, certain signs—all of this is deeply connected to his life. Oh yes, he knows now what is in there, and this awareness slowly begins to warm his body, and because of this, the fog becomes seemingly more transparent. Behind him is the dark opening of the doorway, one leaf pulled shut; the cold has probably set in by now; perhaps a frost has already nipped the plums in the orchard. Above the door is a faint inscription—he sees it without looking, for he knows what is written there, having commissioned it himself; two craftsmen from Podhajce spent a whole week carving the letters into the wood, since he had ordered them to be done ornately:








WHAT WAS TODAY, TOMORROW IS GONE.
WHAT HAS FLED, WE CANNOT OUTRUИ.

In the word "outrun," which irritates him greatly, the letter "N" is written backward, like a mirror image.

Irritated by this for the umpteenth time, the priest shakes his head vigorously—and this finally wakes him. That inverted letter is an "И"... What carelessness! One has to constantly watch their hands, supervise them at every step. And since these craftsmen were Jews, they naturally made the inscription somewhat Jewish; the letters are too curly, wobbly. And one of them even argued with him, insisting that such an "N" was also acceptable, that it was even prettier because the crossbar goes from bottom to top and from left to right, the Christian way, and that the reverse would, in fact, be the Jewish way. This minor irritation restores his senses, and now Father Benedykt Chmielowski, Dean of Rohatyn, sees why he had the feeling he was still asleep—he is standing in a fog that is the color of his bed linens: a grayish, off-white that has already been tainted by dirt, those vast reserves of gray that form the lining of the world. The fog is motionless, densely filling the entire yard; beyond it loom the familiar shapes of the great pear tree, the low wall, and further on—the wicker cart. It is an ordinary cloud from the sky that has fallen to the earth and pressed its belly against it. He read about this just yesterday in Comenius.








Now he hears a familiar creaking and rattling, which inevitably plunge him into a state of creative meditation during every journey. Following close behind the sound, Roszko emerges from the fog, leading the horse by the bridle, along with the Reverend Dean's calash. At the sight of it, the priest feels a surge of energy; he slaps his glove against his palm and clambers up onto the seat. Roszko, silent as usual, adjusts the harness and casts a long glance at the priest. The fog turns his face gray and makes him appear older to the priest than ever before, as if he had aged overnight, though he is just a young lad.








At last they set off, but it feels as though they are standing still; only the swaying of the vehicle and the soothing creak testify to their movement. They have traveled this road so many times, over so many years, that there is no longer any need to look at the scenery, and no landmarks will be necessary. The priest knows that they have now turned onto the road that skirts the forest, and they will proceed like this until the crossroads where the shrine stands—a shrine erected by the priest himself years ago, when he took over the parish in Firlejów. He had pondered for a long time over whom to place in this shrine, and Saint Benedict, his patron, had come to mind, or Onuphrius, the hermit miraculously fed with dates by a palm tree in the desert, to whom angels brought the Body of Christ from heaven every eighth day. For Firlejów was supposed to be just such a desert for the priest when he arrived here, after years of tutoring His Excellency Lord Jabłonowski’s son, Dymitr. Upon reflection, however, the priest decided that the shrine was not to be built for him and the satisfaction of his own vanity, but for the common folk, so that they might have a place to rest at the crossroads and somewhere from which to lift their thoughts to heaven. And so, on a whitewashed brick pedestal stood the Mother of God, Queen of the World, wearing a crown. Beneath her small pointed slipper, a serpent writhed.

But today she, too, vanishes in the fog, along with the shrine and the crossroads. Only the treetops are visible, a sign that the fog is beginning to settle.








"Look here, Benefactor, Kaśka doesn't want to move," Roszko says gloomily as the calash comes to a halt. Roszko climbs down from the box and crosses himself several times with sweeping gestures.

Then he leans over and peers into the fog, as if looking into water. His shirt tails hang out from beneath his festive red, though somewhat faded, jacket.

"I don't know where to drive," he says.

"What do you mean, you don't know? We're already on the Rohatyn high road," the priest says in surprise.

And yet! He steps out of the carriage and follows his servant; they helplessly circle the vehicle, straining their eyes into the whiteness. They think they see something, but their eyes, unable to anchor on anything, begin to play tricks on them. That such a thing should happen to them! It is like getting lost in one's own pocket.

"Quiet!" the priest suddenly says, raising a finger, listening intently. And indeed, from somewhere on the left, out of the billowing fog, comes the faint murmur of water.

"Let's follow the sound. That's the river flowing," the priest decides.

They will trudge along slowly now, beside the river known as the Gniła Lipa. The water will guide them.








Soon the priest relaxes in the calash, stretches out his legs, and lets his eyes wander over the sea of fog. Immediately, he slips into a traveling reverie, for a person thinks best while in motion. Slowly, sluggishly, the mechanism of his mind comes to life, the cogs and gears catching, setting the drive wheels in motion, just like the clock that stands in the hallway of his rectory, purchased in Lwów; he paid a hefty sum for it. In a moment, it will chime ding-dong. Did the world perhaps originate from a fog just like this? he begins to wonder. After all, the Jewish historian Josephus Flavius maintains that the world was created in the autumn, during the autumnal *aequinoctium*. One might assume so, since there was fruit in paradise; if an apple hung on the tree, it must have been autumn... There is some reason to this. But another thought immediately comes to him: What sort of argument is that? Could Almighty God not have created such measly fruits specially, in any season whatsoever?








When they reach the main road leading to Rohatyn, they merge into a stream of pedestrians, horsemen, and carts of every kind, emerging from the fog like little bread figurines molded for Christmas. It is Wednesday, market day in Rohatyn. Peasant wagons drive by, laden with sacks of grain, crates of poultry, and all sorts of agricultural produce. Among them march peddlers at a brisk pace, bearing every conceivable ware—their little stalls, cleverly folded up for now, can be carried on their backs like yokes, and in a moment they will become tables full of colorful fabrics, wooden toys, eggs bought up in the villages for a quarter of the price... Peasants also lead goats and cows for sale—the animals, terrified by the commotion, balk in the puddles. A speeding wagon passes them, covered with a tattered tarp, full of noisy Jews converging on the Rohatyn fair from all over the region; behind them pushes a wealthy carriage, which struggles to maintain its dignity in the fog and the congestion on the road—its brightly varnished doors are black with mud, and the expression of the coachman in a blue cape looks grim; evidently, he had not expected such chaos, and now his eyes search desperately for a chance to turn off this infernal road.

Roszko is stubborn and refuses to be forced into the field; he keeps to the right side and, driving with one wheel on the grass and the other on the road, makes steady progress forward. His sad, elongated face flushes, and a sort of damned grimace spreads across it. The priest casts an eye at him and is reminded of an engraving he had been looking at no later than yesterday—it depicted the denizens of hell, and they had the exact same grimaces on their faces as Roszko does now.








"Make way for His Reverence the Benefactor! Giddy-up! Step aside, folk!" shouts Roszko.

The first buildings spring up before them, suddenly, without warning. The fog clearly alters the sense of distance, for even Kaśka the horse seems taken by surprise. She suddenly shies, jerking the shaft, and were it not for Roszko’s firm reaction and his whip, she would have overturned the calash. Perhaps Kaśka was frightened by the sparks showering from the forge, or the restiveness of the horses waiting their turn to be shod...

Further on lies an inn, wretched and poor, resembling a peasant hut. Like a gallows, the well sweep juts out above it, piercing upward through the fog, its tip vanishing somewhere high above. The priest sees that the dusty carriage has stopped here; the tired coachman has dropped his head almost to his knees and does not jump down from the box, nor does anyone emerge from inside. Yet a tall, thin Jew is already standing before it, accompanied by little girls with tangled hair. That is all Father Dean sees, for the fog swallows every passing sight; it disappears somewhere, soaking in like a melted snowflake.








Here is Rohatyn.

It begins with mud huts, clay dwellings with thatched roofs that seem to press the buildings to the ground; the closer to the market square, however, the more slender the houses become, the thatch grows finer, and eventually gives way to the wooden shingles of townhouses built of unfired clay brick. There are also a parish church here, a Dominican monastery, the Church of Saint Barbara by the market square, and further on, two synagogues and five Orthodox churches. Around the square, little houses sprout like mushrooms, and inside each one, some business is being done. A tailor, a ropemaker, a furrier, all of them Jews, and beside them a baker named Bochenek, which always delights the Reverend Dean, as it reveals a certain hidden order that ought to be more visible and consistent, for then people would live more virtuously. Next door is the workshop of a swordsmith called Luba; the facade stands out for its prosperity, the walls freshly painted blue, and above the entrance hangs a large rusted sword—evidently this Luba is a fine craftsman, and his clients' pockets are full. Further down is a saddler who has set a wooden trestle outside his door, and upon it a beautiful saddle, its stirrups likely silver-plated, they gleam so brightly.








The cloying smell of malt is everywhere, permeating every good laid out for sale. One could fill up on it as if it were bread. In the suburbs of Rohatyn, in Babińce, there are several small breweries; it is from there that this filling aroma wafts over the entire vicinity. Many small stalls here sell beer, and the better shops stock both vodka and mead, mostly the *trójniak* variety. The storehouse of the Jewish merchant Wakszul offers wine: genuine Hungarian and genuine Rhenish, as well as a somewhat tart kind that they bring all the way from Wallachia.








The priest makes his way past stalls constructed from every conceivable material—planks, pieces of coarsely woven canvas, wicker baskets, even leaves. Some good woman with a white kerchief on her head is selling pumpkins from a cart, and their bright orange color attracts the children. Nearby, another touts rounds of cheese laid out on horseradish leaves. Further along stand many other market women who have taken up trade because they were widowed or have drunkards for husbands: sellers of oil, of salt, of linen. The priest usually buys the wares of that pastry woman, and now he sends the vendor a pleasant smile. Behind her are two stalls adorned with a green branch, which means that fresh brewed beer is sold there. And here is the rich stall of Armenian merchants—beautiful, lightweight fabrics, knives in ornate sheaths, and right next to them—*wizina*, or dried sturgeon, whose sickly smell permeates the woolen Turkish tapestries. Further on, from a box suspended from his scrawny shoulders, a man in a dusty gabardine sells eggs packed by the dozen in baskets woven from grass. A second man offers eggs by the threescore, in large baskets, at a competitive, almost wholesale price. Bagels hang all over a baker's stall—one has fallen into the mud, and a small dog is now eating it with relish.








They trade in whatever they can here. Floral fabrics, kerchiefs and shawls straight from the bazaars in Istanbul, little shoes for children, fruit, nuts. A man by the fence has a plow and nails of various sizes, from ones as small as a pin to massive ones for building a house. Next to him, a buxom woman in a starched bonnet has laid out clappers for night watchmen: small ones—whose sound resembles the nocturnal chirping of crickets rather than a call to sleep—and large ones, which, on the contrary, would wake the dead.

How many times had Jews been forbidden to trade in items related to the Church. Both priests and rabbis had thundered on this matter, but to no avail. So here are beautiful prayer books, with ribbons between the pages, the covers embossed with wondrously silvered letters that, when you run your fingertip across them, feel warm and alive. A clean, almost elegant man in a fur hat holds them like relics—wrapped in thin paper, cream-colored tissue, lest this dirty, foggy day stain their innocent, Christian pages, redolent of printer’s ink. He also has wax candles, and even holy cards of saints with halos.

The priest approaches one of the itinerant booksellers, hoping to find something in Latin. All of them, however, are Jewish, for beside them lie objects whose purpose the priest does not know.








The further you cast your gaze down the side streets, the more squalor rises to the surface, like a dirty toe poking out of a torn shoe; a coarse, quiet poverty, bent to the ground. These are no longer shops, no longer stalls, but hovels fit for dogs, cobbled together from thin planks scavenged from trash heaps. In one of them, a cobbler repairs shoes that have already been sewn, resoled, and patched many times over. In another, entirely draped with iron pots, sits a tinker. His face is gaunt and sunken, his cap covering a forehead full of some brown blotches. Father Dean would be afraid to have pots repaired by him, lest from the touch of this wretch’s fingers some terrible disease should pass to others. Nearby, an old man sharpens knives and all manner of sickles and scythes. His workshop consists of a stone wheel strapped around his neck. When he receives an item to sharpen, he sets a primitive wooden frame on the ground—a few leather straps turn it into a simple machine, its wheel, set in motion by hand, licking the metal blades. Sometimes a few very real sparks fly from this machine, landing in the mud, bringing special joy to the dirty, scabby children. At his trade he earns but pennies. He could use that wheel to drown himself in the river; that is the other benefit of his profession.

Women in rags gather wood shavings and dung from the street for fuel. It is hard to tell from these rags whether this is a Jewish, Orthodox, or Catholic poverty. Yes, poverty has neither faith nor nationality.








*Si est, ubi est?* the priest asks himself, thinking about paradise. Certainly not here in Rohatyn, nor—so it seems to him—anywhere on Podolian soil. And should anyone think that things are better in the big cities, they would be sorely mistaken. To be sure, the priest had never in his life made it as far as Warsaw or Kraków, but he knows a thing or two from the tales of the Bernardine monk Pikulski, a more well-traveled man than he, or from what he overhears around the manor houses.

Paradise, or the garden of earthly delights, was moved by God to a beautiful and unknown place. And as it is written in *Arca Noe*, paradise is somewhere in the country of the Armenians, high in the mountains, whereas Brunus claims it is *sub polo antarctico*, beneath the South Pole. The signs of paradise's proximity are the four rivers: Gebon, Philon, Euphrates, and Tygris. And there are authors who, unable to find a place for paradise on earth, locate it in the air, some fifteen cubits above the mountains. But this, to the priest, seems rather foolish. For how would that work? Would those living on earth beneath paradise see it from below? Would they be looking at the heels of the saints?

On the other hand, however, one cannot agree with those who attempt to spread false notions that the holy text about paradise is only meant to possess a mystical meaning—that is, to be taken in a spiritual or allegorical sense. The priest—not only because he is a priest, but out of his own deep conviction—believes that Holy Scripture must be taken literally.








He knows almost everything about paradise, for only last week he finished a chapter of his highly ambitious book, a chapter compiled from all the volumes he has in Firlejów, and he has a hundred and thirty of them. For some, he traveled to Lwów, and even as far as Lublin.

Here is the corner house, a modest one—this is where he is going. This was the advice of Father Pikulski. The low double doors are wide open; wafting from within is a spicy aroma, uncommon amidst this stench of horse dung and autumn dampness, along with another pungent scent, one already familiar to the Reverend Dean: *kaffa*. The priest does not drink *kaffa*, but he must eventually become better acquainted with it.

The priest glances behind him, searching for Roszko; he sees him flipping through sheepskin coats with gloomy concentration, and beyond him—the whole market entirely absorbed in itself. No one is looking at the priest; everyone is consumed by their haggling. Noise and hubbub.

Above the entrance to the building hangs a rather clumsily made signboard:

SHORR GOODS STOREHOUSE

Followed by Hebrew letters. Beside the door hangs a small metal plaque, and next to it some symbols, and the priest recalls that Athanasius Kircher mentions in his book that the Jews, when a wife is in childbed and they fear a witch, write upon the walls the words: "Adam, Hawa. Huc—Lilit," which is meant to signify: "Adam and Eve, come hither, and thou, Lilit, that is, thou witch, flee." This must be it. A child must surely have been born here recently.

The priest steps over the high threshold and immerses himself entirely in the warm, spicy scent. It takes a moment for his eyes to adjust to the gloom, for light is admitted here only by a small window, blocked, moreover, by flowerpots.








Behind the counter stands a youth whose mustache has only just begun to sprout; he has full lips that first tremble slightly at the sight of the priest, and then attempt to form a word. He is completely taken by surprise.

"What is your name, boy?" the priest asks boldly, to show how confident he feels in this dark, low-ceilinged little shop and to encourage the youth to speak, but the boy does not answer. "Quod tibi nomen est?" he repeats officially, but the Latin, which was supposed to facilitate understanding, suddenly sounds far too solemn, as if the priest had come here to perform an exorcism, much like Christ in the Gospel of Saint Luke, who addresses the possessed man with the exact same question. But the boy only widens his eyes further, repeats "bh, bh," and then suddenly darts behind the shelves, knocking against a braid of garlic hanging from a nail.

The priest has behaved foolishly; he should not have expected them to speak Latin here. He inspects himself critically—the black haircloth buttons of his cassock are showing beneath his cloak. That is probably what frightened the boy, the priest thinks—the cassock. He smiles to himself, reminded of the biblical Jeremiah, who also nearly lost his head and stammered: *Aaa, Domine Deus ecce nescio loqui!*—Ah, Lord God! Behold, I cannot speak.

From then on, the priest calls the boy Jeremiah in his mind. He does not know what to do now that the boy has vanished so abruptly. So he looks around the shop, buttoning up his cloak. It was Father Pikulski who persuaded him to come here; he had listened, but now it no longer seems like a good idea.








No one enters from the outside, for which the priest silently thanks God. It would not be an ordinary sight—a Catholic priest, the Dean of Rohatyn, in a Jew's shop, waiting to be served like a burgher's wife. Father Pikulski had advised him to go to Rabbi Dubs in Lwów; he visited him himself and had learned much from him. And the priest had gone there, but old Dubs had probably had his fill of Catholic priests questioning him about books. He was unpleasantly surprised by the request, and what Father Benedykt was most interested in, he either did not have, or pretended not to have. He adopted a polite expression and shook his head, clicking his tongue. And when the priest asked who might be able to help him, Dubs waved his hands and, turning his head backwards as if someone were standing behind him, signaled that he did not know, and even if he did, he would not say. Later, Father Pikulski explained to the Reverend Dean that it had to do with Jewish heresies, and although they boast that they have no heresies among them, it seems they make an exception for this particular one and genuinely hate it, without mincing words.








Finally, Pikulski advised him to go to Shorr. A large house with a shop by the market square. He had, however, looked at the priest somewhat askance, with irony, or so it had seemed to the priest. Perhaps he should have procured these Jewish books through Pikulski? Though Father Dean does not particularly like him. He would not have to stand here now, embarrassed and sweating. But there is a great deal of contrariness in the priest, and so he had come himself. And there was also something not entirely rational—a little wordplay had become entangled in the whole affair; who would believe that such things could influence the world? The priest had been working diligently on a certain passage in Kircher, where mention was made of the great ox Shorobor. And perhaps the similarity of the words had drawn him here—Shorr and Shorobor. Strange are the Lord's judgments.








But where are these famous books, where is this figure who commands fearful respect? The shop looks like an ordinary stall, and yet the owner is reputedly a descendant of the famous rabbi, the highly esteemed sage Zalman Naftali Shorr. But here there is garlic, herbs, crocks of spices, jars and little jars containing spices of every sort—crushed, ground, or still in their natural shapes, like these vanilla beans, these clove spikes, these nutmeg spheres. Bales of fabric have also been laid on hay upon the shelves—silk and satin, it seems, very bright, catching the eye. The priest wonders if perhaps he might not need something, but his attention is already drawn to a clumsy inscription on a sizable dark-green jar: "Herba the". He already knows what he will ask for when someone finally comes out to him—a bit of that herb which puts him in a better mood, which for the Reverend Dean means that he can work without tiring. Plus, it improves digestion. And he would buy some cloves to spice his evening mulled wine. The last few nights had been so cold that his freezing feet had prevented him from concentrating on his writing. He looks around for a bench, and then everything happens at once: from behind the shelves emerges a well-built, bearded man in a long woolen gown, from beneath which protrude Turkish shoes with pointed toes. A thin, dark-blue cloak is draped over his shoulders. He squints, as if he had just climbed out of a well. Peeking out curiously from behind his back is that Jeremiah who had taken fright earlier, along with two other faces, very similar to Jeremiah's—inquisitive and ruddy. And from the other direction, in the doorway leading to the market, a slightly built boy has just paused, panting—or perhaps a young man, for facial hair has sprouted profusely into a light goatee. He leans against the doorframe and pants; evidently, he ran here as fast as he could. He impudently bores his eyes into the Reverend Dean and smiles roguishly, revealing healthy, widely spaced teeth. The priest is not certain whether it isn't a mocking smile. He prefers the dignified figure in the cloak, and it is to him he addresses himself most politely:








"Pray forgive this intrusion, good sir..."

The man looks at him tensely, but a moment later his expression slowly changes. Something akin to a smile appears upon it. Father Dean suddenly realizes that the man does not understand him, so he begins anew, in Latin, joyfully certain that like has found like.

The Jew slowly shifts his gaze to the boy in the doorway, the breathless one, who boldly steps inside, tugging down his jacket of dark cloth.

"I will translate," he announces in an unexpectedly deep voice with a soft Ruthenian lilt, and jabbing a thick finger toward the Reverend Dean, he declares earnestly that this is a real, genuine Catholic priest.








It had not occurred to the priest that a translator would be needed; somehow he hadn't thought of it. He is flustered and does not know how to extricate himself, for the entire matter, intended to be delicate, has suddenly become public and in a moment will draw the entire market in here. He would most gladly walk out of here into the cold fog smelling of horse dung. He begins to feel cornered in this low-ceilinged room, in the air thick with the scent of spices, especially as someone from the street is already peering in curiously to see what is going on here.

"I would have a word with the esteemed Elisha Shorr, by your leave," he says. "In private."

The Jews are surprised. They exchange a few sentences among themselves, Jeremiah vanishes, and only after a long moment full of unbearable silence does he return. Evidently, the priest has been granted permission, and they now lead him behind the shelves. This is accompanied by whispers, the light patter of children's feet, muffled giggles—as if crowds of other people were behind the thin partitions, peering curiously through cracks in the wooden walls at the Dean of Rohatyn wandering the nooks and crannies of a Jewish house. And it turns out, too, that the little shop on the square is merely the outpost of a much more elaborate structure, a sort of beehive: rooms, short corridors, and little stairs. The whole house is larger and built around an inner courtyard, which the priest catches only out of the corner of his eye through a small window in a room where they pause for a moment.








"I am Hryćko," the boy with the goatee speaks up as they walk. The priest realizes that if he wanted to retreat, he would not even know how to get out of this beehive of a house. He begins to sweat at the thought, and just then one of the doors creaks open, and in it stands a slender man in the prime of life, with a pale, smooth, inscrutable face and a gray beard, wearing a gown reaching below the knee; on his feet are woolen socks and black slippers.

"This is Rabbi Elisha Shorr," Hryćko whispers earnestly.

The room is small, low, and very sparsely furnished. In the center sits a wide table; upon it lies an open book, and beside it several stacks of others—the priest’s gaze greedily sweeps across their spines, trying to read the titles. The priest knows little about Jews in general, and those of Rohatyn he knows only by sight.

The priest suddenly finds it agreeable that both of them are of small stature. He always feels somewhat flustered around tall people. They stand facing each other, and for a moment the priest has the impression that the other man is also pleased by this similarity. The Jew sits down softly, smiles, and motions with his hand toward a bench for the priest.

"By your leave, and under these unusual circumstances, I come here to your grace quite *incognito*, having heard much of your great wisdom and erudition..."

Hryćko stops mid-sentence and asks the priest:

"In-co-gnito?"

"Indeed, which is to say, I supplicate for your discretion."

"And what's that? Sup-pli-cate? Dis-cre-tion?"








The priest falls silent, unpleasantly surprised. Some translator he's ended up with; he sees the boy does not understand him. So how are they supposed to converse? In Chinese? He will try to speak simply:

"I ask that you keep this a secret, for I do not hide the fact that I am the Dean of Rohatyn, a Catholic priest. But above all, I am an author." He emphasizes the word "author" by raising a finger. "And I would prefer to converse here today not as a clergyman, but rather as an author, who is working tirelessly on a certain *opusculum*..."

"O-pu-scu-lum?" Hryćko's doubtful voice interrupts him.

"...a modest little work."

"Ah. Forgive me, Father, I'm not schooled in Polish, I only know the plain language people speak. I only know what I've picked up around horses."

"From horses?" the priest wonders immensely, annoyed at his poor translator.

"Well, 'cause I work with horses. Trading."

Hryćko speaks, gesturing with his hands. The man looks at him with dark, inscrutable eyes, and it occurs to the priest that perhaps he is dealing with a blind man.

"Having already read several hundred authors from cover to cover," the priest continues, "borrowing here and buying there, I feel that many tomes have eluded me, and I can gain no access to them whatsoever."

He pauses now and waits for the man to speak, but Shorr only nods with an obliging smile that conveys absolutely nothing.








"And since I have heard that your grace possesses a rather fine library here, not wishing for the world to incommode..." he reluctantly corrects himself at once "...to intrude or trouble you, I have mustered the courage, contrary to custom, but for the benefit of others, to come here and..."








He falls silent, for the door abruptly opens and into this low-ceilinged room, without any announcement, walks a woman. In her wake, whispering faces, half-visible in the gloom, peer inside. A small child whimpers for a moment, then falls suddenly silent, as if everything were to focus on the woman: bareheaded, framed by a halo of lush curls, she strides boldly, her gaze fixed straight ahead, not looking at the men at all; she carries a pitcher and dried fruit on a tray. She is wearing a wide, floral dress, covered by an embroidered apron. Her pointed shoes tap on the floor. She is petite but shapely; her figure draws the eye. Behind her trots a little girl carrying two glasses. She stares at the priest with such terror that she carelessly bumps into the woman walking ahead of her and falls over. The glasses roll across the floor—it’s a good thing they are of thick glass. The woman pays no attention to the child; instead, she casts a single glance at the priest, swift and insolent. Her dark, somber eyes flash, large and somehow fathomless, and her terribly white skin is instantly covered in a flush. Father Dean, who does not often associate with young women, feels caught off guard by this sudden intrusion; he swallows hard. The woman loudly sets the pitcher and plate on the table, along with the glasses retrieved from the floor, and, once again staring straight ahead, she exits. The door slams. Hryćko, the translator, also looks flustered. Meanwhile, Elisha Shorr leaps up, lifts the child, and sits her on his lap, but the little girl tears herself away and vanishes after her mother.








The priest would bet his life that this entire entrance of the woman and child was solely so they could get a good look at him. The nerve! A priest in a Jewish household! Exotic as a salamander. And what of it? Am I not treated by a Jewish doctor? And is it not a Jew who grinds my medicines? And the matter of books is also, in a way, a matter of hygiene.

"Books," the priest says, pointing his finger at the spines of the folios and Elzevirs lying on the table. On each of them, two characters are painted in golden paint, which the priest takes for the owner's initials, for he knows how to recognize Hebrew letters:

He reaches for his ticket on this journey among the people of Israel and carefully places the book he has brought before Shorr. He smiles triumphantly, for it is *Turris Babel* by Athanasius Kircher, a magnificent work in terms of both content and format, and the priest had risked a great deal lugging it here. What if it had fallen into that stinking Rohatyn mud? What if some cutpurse at the market had snatched it from him? Without it, the Reverend Dean would not be who he is, but would rather have become some narrow-minded parish priest, a Jesuit tutor at a lord's manor, a vain church official, be-ringed and averse to the world.

He pushes the book closer to Shorr, as if presenting him with his wife. He taps gently now on the wooden boards.

"I have more of them. But Kircher is the best." He opens it at random, and they look at a drawing of the Earth, depicted as a sphere, with the long, slender cone of the Tower of Babel atop it.








"Kircher proves that the Tower of Babel, the description of which we find in the Bible, could not have been as high as is depicted. A tower reaching all the way to the sphere of the Moon would disrupt the entire order of the cosmos. Its base, resting on the globe of the Earth, would have to be enormous. It would eclipse the sun, which would have catastrophic consequences for all creation. Humans would have had to use up the Earth's entire supply of wood and clay..."








The priest feels as though he is uttering a heresy, and he does not really know why he is telling this to a silent Jew. He wants the man to take him for a friend, not an enemy. But will that be possible? Perhaps it is possible to reach an understanding, even if they do not know each other’s languages or customs, nor each other, nor their belongings and objects, nor smiles, nor the hand gestures making signs, nothing; so perhaps they can reach an understanding by means of books? Is that not the only possible way? If people read the same books, they would live in the same world, but instead they live in different ones, like those Chinese of whom Kircher wrote. And there are those, a great multitude, who do not read at all; their minds are dormant, their thoughts simple, animalistic, like those peasants with empty eyes. If he, the priest, were king, he would mandate that one day of corvée labor be devoted to reading; he would drive the entire peasant estate to the books, and the Commonwealth would immediately look different. Perhaps it is even a matter of alphabet—that there is not just one, but many, and each structures thoughts differently. Alphabets are like bricks—from some, fired and smooth, cathedrals are built; from others, earthen and rough, ordinary houses. And although the Latin one is surely the most perfect, it seems that Shorr does not know Latin. So he points his finger at the engraving, and then another, and another, and he sees the man leaning over them with growing interest, until finally he pulls out from somewhere a pair of spectacles neatly framed in metal wire—Father Chmielowski would like to have a pair like that himself; he must ask him where to order them. The translator also proves to be interested, so the three of them lean over the engraving.








The priest peeks at them, pleased to have hooked them both, and he sees gold and chestnut hairs in the Jew's dark beard.

"We could exchange books," he suggests.

He says that in his library in Firlejów he has two more works by the great Kircher, *Arca Noe* and *Mundus subterraneus*, locked away with a key, too valuable to be handled every day. He also knows there are other titles, but he knows them only from mentions here and there. And he has gathered many other thinkers of the past world, as well as—he adds to flatter him—the Jewish historiographer, Josephus.

They pour him fruit compote from the pitcher and push the plate of dried figs and dates toward him. The priest places them in his mouth with reverence, he hasn't eaten them in a long time—their unearthly sweetness instantly improves his mood. He understands that he should state his business now, it is high time, so he swallows the sweets and gets straight to the point; but before he even finishes, he knows that he has rushed it and will accomplish little here.

Perhaps he recognizes this from the sudden change in Hryćko's behavior. He would swear, too, that the boy is adding something of his own to what he translates. He just doesn't know whether these are warnings or, quite the opposite, something meant to support the priest. Elisha Shorr retreats slightly in his chair, tilts his head back, and closes his eyes, as if withdrawing to consult his own inner darkness.








This lasts until the moment when—against his will—the priest exchanges a knowing look with the young translator.

"The rabbi is listening to the voices of his elders," the translator whispers, and the priest nods as if in understanding, although he does not understand. Perhaps this Jew really does have some magical contact with various little devils—as if there weren't plenty of them among the Jews, all those lamias and liliths. This hesitation of Shorr's and his closed eyes convince the priest that it would have been better had he not come here at all. The situation is delicate and unusual. Heaven forbid he expose himself to infamy.

Shorr stands up and turns to the wall, bows his head, and stands like that for a moment. The priest begins to grow impatient—is this a sign he is supposed to leave? Hryćko has also closed his eyes, and his long, boyish eyelashes now cast a shadow on his fuzz-covered cheeks. Have they fallen asleep? The priest clears his throat softly; their silence has robbed him of the last of his self-assurance. He already regrets coming here.

Suddenly, as if nothing had happened, Shorr moves toward the cabinets and opens one of them. With reverence, he pulls out a thick folio bearing the same symbols as all the other books, and places it on the table before the priest. He opens the book from the back and turns a page. The priest sees a beautifully crafted title page...

"*Sefer ha-Zohar*," he says devoutly, and puts the book back in the cabinet.

"Who'd read it for you, Father..." Hryćko says consolingly.








The priest leaves two volumes of his *New Athens* on Shorr's table as an incentive for a future exchange. He taps them with his index finger, then points to himself—aiming right at the center of his chest: I wrote this. They ought to read it, if they knew the language. They would learn a great deal about the world. He waits for Shorr's reaction, but he merely raises his eyebrows slightly.

Father Chmielowski and Hryćko step out together into the cold, unpleasant air. Hryćko is still prattling on about something, while the priest observes him closely—his young face with the beginnings of future stubble, his long, curled eyelashes that give him a somewhat childish appearance, and finally his peasant clothes.

"Are you a Jew?"

"Nah..." Hryćko replies with a shrug. "I'm from right here, in Rohatyn. That house there, see. Supposedly Orthodox."

"Then how do you know their tongue?"








Hryćko moves closer and walks almost shoulder to shoulder with the priest; clearly, he feels encouraged by this familiarity. He says his father and mother died of the plague in 1746. They did business with the Shorrs; his father was an artisan, a tanner of hides, and when he died, Shorr intervened on behalf of Hryćko, his grandmother, and his younger brother, Ołeś, paid off his father's debts, and took the three neighbors under his wing. And so they live as neighbors; he deals more with Jews now than with his own kind, and somehow he doesn't even know when he picked up their language, but he speaks it like his own, fluently, which often comes in handy for various matters and business dealings, since Jews, especially the older ones, are reluctant to use Polish or Ruthenian. The Jews are not as people say they are, and the Shorrs in particular. There are many of them, and their home is warm, hospitable; they will always give you something to eat and a glass of vodka when it's cold. Now Hryćko is learning his father's trade, so he can take over the tanning business; there will always be a demand for that.

"And have you no Christian family?"

"Well, I do, but far away, and they don't care much about us. Oh, and this is my brother, Ołeś." A boy of about eight, covered in freckles, runs up to them. "Don't trouble yourself over us needlessly, Benefactor," Hryćko says cheerfully. "God created man with eyes in the front of his head, not the back, which means a man should concern himself with what is to come, not what was."








The priest indeed considers this proof of God's ingenuity, though he cannot recall where in Scripture this was said.

"Learn the language with them, and you can translate these books."

"Not likely, Your Grace, I've no draw to books. Reading bores me. I'd rather take up trading, I like that. With horses, preferably. Or like the Shorrs: with vodka and beer."

"Oh, you've gone completely astray with them..." says the priest.

"And what of it, are they any worse than other goods? People need to drink, 'cause life is hard."
