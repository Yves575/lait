May 1980

At eight years old, I love feeling my upper body suspended in mid-air, the feel of my knees bent over the metal. I love the moment I squeeze my eyes shut, let go, and let the dizziness wash over me. When my hands are flat on the black asphalt, it means I’ve pushed past my fear. And then, the image of my favorite gymnast, Nadia Comăneci, appears. Her arms are wide open. Victory.

Hanging by my knees is how I spend my recess, how I wait for my sister, Ana. This morning when she left, she told me, We’ll meet here, and be on time, okay? Otherwise I’m walking home alone. “Here” is at the bottom of the stairs, near the metal bar that separates the parking lot from the schoolyard.



Ilaria! Get down from there! We’re going to Chez Léon. Come on!

I recognize Papa’s voice. Surprised, I lift the fabric of my dress that’s blocking my view. It’s definitely the tips of his shoes, definitely his impatient tone. I do a flip, land on my feet, smooth down my dress.

Ana will be here soon.

No, no. Plans have changed. Maman is picking her up from school and we’re all meeting at Chez Léon. Come on!

I take his hand, it’s clammy.



Since our parents separated and Papa moved to Turin, we see each other once a month at a restaurant. It was Maman who came up with the idea. She prefers a neutral place. She says that at home they argue too loudly. And it’s true that at Chez Léon, they hold back. Even if Papa clenches his jaw and Maman looks off into the distance with an indifferent air.







No, Papa still hasn’t found a job. When he says “no-no-work” his voice is sad, tired. Maman turns her head slightly to hide her smile and Papa gets angry. He repeats the word “humiliation.” Fortunately the waiter arrives and sets plates of perch fillets or dishes of meringue with whipped cream on the table. Thank you.

After dessert, Ana and I leave the table and, on the little beach, pick out pebbles. We practice skipping stones.

Did you see?

What?

Papa took Maman’s hand.



To get to Chez Léon, we pass through the village of Hermance, cross the Franco-Swiss border, and continue on the road to Yvoire. Papa has a navy blue BMW, a 320 coupe.

If you see a phone booth, tell me. He lights a cigarette. There! He stops, gets out of the car, and takes some coins out of his pants pocket. With his back pressed against the glass, the folds of his shirt form V’s and W’s. I wait, roll down the window, let in a little air. The leather seat no longer burns the backs of my thighs; it’s even soft when I stroke it with the palm of my hand.

Inside the booth, Papa speaks loudly, raises his voice, turns around. Our eyes meet. He smiles, lowers his eyes. From the way he moves his hands, I can tell he’s agitated. His body is stiff. It worries me. When he comes back, he says that Maman has changed her mind and doesn’t have time for lunch anymore. We’re going to spend the weekend together. And school? You’ll just miss a few days of school… It’s no big deal.







Papa’s voice is sharp. I count on my fingers: Thursday, Friday, Saturday, Sunday. Four days. And Ana? I’d like to protest, but when Papa is nervous, it’s best to keep quiet.

He pulls away abruptly and stubs out his cigarette with a swift motion. His forehead is covered in sweat.



Mont Blanc Tunnel, Franco-Italian border, gallery vaults, the hairpin bends of the Aosta Valley, carsickness. We stop under a sky covered by a gray blanket. The landscape is metallic. I throw up by the side of the road; Papa hands me a white cotton handkerchief. Let’s go get a drink, it’ll do you good. A few kilometers further down, at the gas station bar, Papa’s face is pale. It must be the neon lights. He pays the cashier for two slices of Margherita pizza, a whiskey, a coffee, and a lemonade. I hate lemonade but don’t say anything; my mouth is dry.

Do you have any phone tokens for sale?

How many?

About twenty.

The cashier scrupulously counts out the yellowish coins and hands them to Papa.

The booth is outside, on the left.

Her nails are very long and painted with very red polish. I follow Papa.

What are these tokens for?

I need them to make a call. In Italy, you can’t use coins in phone booths.



Between Geneva and Turin, Papa makes several calls. Five in total. As soon as he sees a gas station, he stops. Are you happy to spend the weekend with me? Cat got your tongue? What are you thinking about? Nothing.







Turin. We drive around for a long time to find a parking space, and when Papa pushes open the door to his building with his shoulder, he looks relieved. I dash up the stairs. What floor is it? The fourth. On the landing, he looks for his keys. You’re going to see your room. Are you happy? I say yes. Yes, I’m happy. Listen, Ilaria, he turns the key in the lock, I have to introduce you to someone I haven’t told you about yet, a certain Geneviève. Who is that? The lady of the house. Papa opens the door and points to an inanimate mannequin, dressed in a black dress with a white lace collar and a hat. He bursts out laughing. Geneviève’s face is made of white Styrofoam; she has no eyes. Come on! Go in. Your room is at the end of the hall, past the bathroom.

Impatient to see it, I take a quick look at the other rooms and turn on the light in my room. The space is crowded with two beds and two dressers. I step closer to the poster of Topolino. Mickey looks at me with his tunnel-shaped eyes. Papa stays in the doorway.

I haven’t found nice bedside lamps yet. We could buy some together tomorrow, right? If you want. Running my hand over the fabric, I ask him if these are the sheets from Florence.



You remember them?

I think so, yes. When was Florence?

Two years ago.

The orange sunflower patterns stand out on the sheets. They remind me of the blurry images I have from that period. Two years, and yet I feel like it’s been centuries since Maman, Ana, and I moved to Switzerland.







The next day we wake up early and go for a *giro*, a drive around Turin. Papa tells me about the FIAT kingdom, the workers' strikes, the arrival of immigrants from the South. He says the Turinese hate them.

Through the windshield, he points out the *palazzi*, the Po, the Basilica of Superga, the statues. You see the kids over there between the cars? They're *posteggiatori*. That’s how they earn money, by helping people park.

When we walk under the arcades of Corso Vittorio Emanuele, Papa holds my hand tightly; his is still clammy, and I don’t dare pull mine away until, standing in front of the window of the Al Sogno toy store, I let go.



The window display looks like a zoo of harmless animals. Even the snakes look friendly. Papa follows my gaze. Is that the teddy bear you’re looking at? Come on, let’s buy it. The store is full of wooden toys, mobiles, dolls in sparkly dresses. I am in awe. What about Ana? What should we get her? Yes, a doll… Ana loves them. The one I choose has very long eyelashes, and when she lies down, her eyelids close. While we wait for the saleswoman to wrap it in beautiful wrapping paper, Papa hands me the teddy bear. What should we name him? What do you say to Birillo? Yes. I look at Birillo and immediately take to his shiny milk-chocolate eyes, his cream-colored belly, his soft fur. Papa pinches my cheek between his index and middle fingers, with a very soft look.

That gesture on my cheek is like his signature. He'll repeat it for two years, and I'll eventually come to hate it. Are you happy?







At eight years old, I am a taciturn, docile, rather skinny child. I don’t complain when Papa leaves me alone in the apartment to go make his phone calls at Chez Carmelo, the bar downstairs. He doesn’t have a phone. I’ll be right back. He always promises he’ll just be “two minutes.” Alone, lying on my bed, I draw, I stick Sarah Kay stickers into my new album. Papa bought it for me at the newsstand. And when I’m tired of waiting for him, I know where to find him: at the counter. My landmark is the big coffee machine that gives off a smell of burnt caramel.

At Chez Carmelo, the local workers come to eat a sandwich at lunchtime or have a drink before going home. They all have a first name and their habits. *Una birra alla spina per Emilio e due tramezzini per Marco.* Gianna is the owner. She presses the large keys on the cash register very quickly. *Sei mila lire.* Perched on her little podium, she smiles, hands out the *scontrini*, the receipts, the packs of cigarettes, and when there is no one around, she sweeps the floor. Carmelo polishes the counter, serves coffees, dries the glasses with a cloth, evens out the sugar in the sugar bowls.







When I walk into the café, the atmosphere is lively. The workers sing in chorus to a song playing on the radio. *Vorrei essere libero, libero come un uomo.* To reach the counter, I weave my way through them and hoist myself onto the high stool. Listen, listen! It’s a song by Giorgio Gaber, *La libertà*. Carmelo laughs, and I see metallic spots in the back of his mouth. How many cavities? I don’t have time to count them. Are you looking for Fulvio? He points to the phone booth with a hand covered in soap bubbles.

And how was your day? Really good. Tomorrow we’re going back to Geneva. You’ll come back soon, eh? Carmelo’s smile is gentle. I’ll be very sad not to see you anymore. Promise? I promise.

Later, Papa appears. His legs are all stiff. With a funny face, he leans close to my ear. We’re leaving tonight. His breath is like a draft of air. Hurry up. We have to go upstairs and pack our bags.

Say goodbye and thank Carmelo and Gianna properly.



In the back seat of the car, I have trouble breathing. Where are we going? To the seaside. What about Ana? And Maman? When are we meeting them? Stop sniffling. We’ll go back to Geneva in a month. A month isn’t long. And the end-of-year festival? You told me we'd just spend the weekend together… You’ll go next year. Now stop with your questions.

The image of the pretty dress I chose with Maman for the parade appears. I think of Sarah, my best friend, of my teacher, of the little flag we made in class, of Ana. My head is heavy. I tuck my knees under my chin and squeeze Birillo tightly against me. My sleep is cold, restless.







I want to hear your voice. STOP. The little one is doing well. STOP. She would like to talk to you. STOP. I am waiting for you. STOP. Wait for me.

Your husband



Here! Put this in the glove compartment.

Now we stop not only at phone booths but also at the post office. Papa sends a bunch of telegrams, and keeps a copy of them. I don’t dare read them when he hands them to me, but curious, I control and slow down my movements to catch a few words.



*Zzaaak, zzzaaak.* A family, a lone man, a woman. Two white heads of hair are driving at 30 km/h in the right lane. Papa gets impatient, lets go of the wheel, lights a cigarette. Come on!!! Let me pass. He weaves between the vehicles. Are you scared? A little. He notices my hand gripping the door handle. The traffic at the entrance to Genova is heavy, the tunnels numerous. Shadow, light, shadow, light. The lanes are full of cars and long trucks. I can’t take this road anymore. Get out the map, Ilaria.



Genova, Milano, Brescia, Alessandria. Yes, Brescia, then we’ll go to Alessandria. I follow our route with the tip of my finger. But there’s no sea in Alessandria! It doesn’t matter. I’m showing you around your country. Papa’s tone is playful. He's this close to winking at me.

Papa downshifts and merges onto the exit lane for the gas station.

I have to make a call, go to the bar.

No, I'm staying here.

While I wait for him, I hop on one foot around the booth. Papa inserts the tokens into the phone's slot, lights a cigarette. *Pronto?* The conversation lasts a while.







Sitting in the car, I leave the door open, dangling my legs in the air. The sky is covered in white streaks, the clouds are like balls of yarn. It’s muggy, I’m thirsty and hot. Other cars arrive, leave, back up in the parking lot. Kids run around. Careful, look before you cross! their mothers yell. The families passing by seem perfect to me, washed, combed, ironed; they are going somewhere specific. Where are we going?



Roads, phone booths, post offices, little hotels, bars. The days pile up. We often stop at Autogrills to get gas, eat a sandwich, use the restrooms. I really like those places. Inside, everything is colorful: the stacks of candy, the baskets of food wrapped in crinkly paper, the bins of music cassettes, the games, the stuffed animals. And while I wander the aisles, Papa talks with customers at the bar counter.

He loves to chat.

He can't help it; he needs to talk to someone. Anyone.

And he's very good at striking up a conversation. It always starts with a remark about the traffic or the weather, or a joke. Papa knows how to put even the shyest people at ease. He looks right at home with his way of leaning against the counter, his way of talking to the bartender. When he plays the clown, he is very quickly surrounded by smiling faces. I prefer seeing him like that, happy with his laughing eyes, rather than being forced to listen to his theories about people.







Yes, yes, let's go. Always one last drink. He drinks very fast, and when he grabs his glass, I’m sure that even if he were blind, he would have the same confidence. His lips would tremble just the same. He makes me feel like he's afraid he won’t have time to finish his whiskey.

He told me one day that it’s medicine for him, and pinched my cheek.

Say thank you. I thank the bartender, say goodbye to everyone.

On the road again. We leave the counters just like that, abruptly.
