Prologue


Seeing Nothing Anymore


Everything went dark. It was like a mourning garment. And then, here and there, sparkles, like the spots the sun makes when the eyes stare at it in vain behind squeezed-shut eyelids, just as one clenches a fist to resist pain or emotion.

Of course, she hadn't described it like that at all. In the mouth of a fresh and anxious ten-year-old, distress is articulated dryly, without embellishment or lyricism.

"Mom, everything's black!"

Mona had cried out these words in a strangled voice. A complaint? Yes, but not only that. In spite of herself, she had slipped in an edge of shame, which her mother, whenever she identified it, took very seriously. For if there was one thing Mona never faked, it was shame. The moment it nestled into a word, an attitude, an intonation, the die was cast: an unpleasant truth had taken hold.

"Mom, everything's black!"

Mona was blind.





The effect seemed devoid of cause. Nothing in particular had happened; she was working quietly on her math, a pen in her right hand, a notebook wedged under her left palm, at the corner of the table where her mother was studding a fatty roast with garlic. Mona was delicately removing a pendant from her neck that was bothering her because it was swinging over her worksheet, and because she had developed the bad habit of slouching to write. She felt a heavy shadow fall over her eyes, as if they were being punished for being so blue, so large, so pure. The shadow did not come from the outside, the way it usually does when night falls or the lights dim in a theater; the shadow seized her sight from within her own body, from the inside. An opaque sheet had slipped inside her, cutting her off from the polygons drawn in her school notebook, the brown wooden table, the roast resting farther away, her mother in her white apron, the tiled kitchen, her father sitting in the next room, the Montreuil apartment, the gray autumn sky hanging over the streets, the whole world. By some evil spell, the child was plunged into darkness.

Frantic, Mona’s mother called the family doctor. She gave a confused description of her daughter’s veiled pupils and clarified, because the doctor asked, that she didn't seem to be suffering from any speech impairment or paralysis.

"Sounds like a TIA," he let slip, unwilling to commit himself further.





He demanded immediate, high doses of aspirin and, above all, the urgent transport of Mona to the Hôtel-Dieu hospital, where he would call a colleague for an immediate admission. He hadn't thought of this colleague by chance: he was a formidable pediatrician, known moreover as a very good ophthalmologist and, incidentally, a talented hypnotherapist. Normally, he concluded, the blindness shouldn't last more than ten minutes, and he hung up. A good fifteen minutes had already passed since the first cry of alarm.

In the car, the little girl was crying, beating her temples. Her mother held her elbows, but, deep down, she too would have liked to strike that frail, chubby little head, the way one smacks a broken machine in the stupid hope of getting it to restart. The father, at the wheel of his rattling old Volkswagen, wished he could catch the affliction his little girl was suffering from. He was angry, convinced something had happened in the kitchen and they were hiding it from him. He kept rehashing the inventory of all possible causes, from a burst of steam to a bad fall. But no, Mona declared a hundred times:

"It just came on its own!"

And the father didn't believe a word of it:

"You don't just go blind like that!"





Yet you do: you can also go blind "just like that"—here was the proof. And "you," today, was Mona, with her ten years and her tears of fear flowing freely—tears she perhaps hoped would wash away the soot that had stuck to her pupils, this October Sunday, as evening fell. Hardly had they reached the threshold of the hospital adjoining Notre-Dame, on the Île de la Cité, when she abruptly stopped sobbing and froze:

"Mom, Dad, it's coming back!"

Standing in the street, where a cold wind was blowing, she rocked her head back and forth to encourage the return of her perception. Like a blind being raised, the veil obstructing her eyes lifted. Lines reappeared to her, then the angles of faces, the contours of nearby objects, the texture of the walls, and all the shades of color, from the brightest to the darkest. Once again, the child saw her mother’s slender silhouette, her long swan neck and frail arms, and the heavier one of her father. Finally, she perceived the flight of a gray pigeon in the distance, which filled her with joy. Blindness had seized Mona, then let her go. It had passed through her, the way a bullet pierces the skin and exits the other side of the body, hurting, certainly, but leaving the organism to heal itself. "A miracle," thought her father, who scrupulously tallied the time the attack had lasted: sixty-three minutes.





In the ophthalmology department at the Hôtel-Dieu, there was no question of letting the little girl leave before running a battery of tests and providing a diagnosis and prescriptions. The anguish was postponed, certainly, but not entirely dispelled. A nurse pointed to a room on the first floor of the building. It was the office of the pediatrician notified by the family doctor. Dr. Van Orst was biracial and struck by premature baldness. His large, radiant white coat contrasted with the sickly greenness of the walls. His enormous smile, carving little jovial wrinkles into his face, made him likable; he was nonetheless a depository of many tragedies. He stepped forward:

"How old are you?" he asked in a voice raspy from tobacco.









Mona was ten years old. She was the only child of two parents who loved each other. Camille, the mother, was nearing forty. She was of modest height, sported short, tousled hair, and had in her voice a distant trace of working-class cheek. Her charm lay in her "slightly unhinged" side, her man would say, but backed by a formidable determination: in her, anarchy was constantly braided with authority. She worked in a temp agency, a good, committed, zealous employee. In the morning, at least. The afternoon was another story. She exhausted herself with volunteer work. Any cause was a good one, from lonely elderly people to abused animals. As for Paul, he was past fifty-seven. Camille was his second wife. His first had disappeared with his best friend. He wore a tie to make people forget his frayed shirt collars, and toiled as a small-scale antiques dealer, primarily passionate about 1950s American culture: jukeboxes, pinball machines, posters... And since it had all started in his adolescence with a collection of heart-shaped keychains, he possessed an impressive array of them, which he didn't want to sell and which, anyway, would have interested no one. With the rise of the Internet, his shop, lost in Montreuil, had very nearly closed its doors. So he had taken to it as well, showcasing his expertise with a website he constantly updated and translated into English. Despite having next to zero business sense, he could count on a clientele of attentive collectors who regularly saved him from ruin. The previous summer, he had repaired a 1955 edition of the Gottlieb "Wishing Well" pinball machine and had fetched the tidy sum of ten thousand euros for it. A salutary transaction, after months of lean times... And then, once again, nothing. The crisis, he was told. Every day at the shop, Paul would drain a bottle of red wine, then stick it, like a trophy, onto one of those hedgehog-shaped bottle racks that had secured Marcel Duchamp’s posterity. He raised his glass alone, unable to find a way to hold a grudge against anyone. In his head, he toasted Mona. To her health.









While a nurse led the child through the labyrinth of the hospital to undergo various tests, Dr. Van Orst, ensconced in an enormous armchair, delivered an initial diagnosis to Paul and Camille:

"TIA, or Transient Ischemic Attack."

This meant that blood had momentarily stopped supplying the organs and that they now had to find the cause of this malfunction. But Mona’s case, he continued, baffled him: on the one hand, the attack, exceedingly rare for a girl her age, seemed violent to him, since both eyes had been affected and it had lasted more than an hour; on the other hand, it had completely spared her ability to move and speak. The MRI would doubtless reveal more. They had to, he added looking uncomfortable, prepare for the worst.





Mona had to lie down on a sliding table in a horrible machine and surrender to it docilely, without moving. They asked her to take off her pendant. She refused. It was a fine chain made of fishing line weighted with a tiny seashell that had belonged to her grandmother and brought her luck. She had always had it, and her beloved "Dadé" wore the exact same one. The two talismans connected them to each other, she thought, and she didn't want to feel torn away from her grandfather. Since the pendant contained no metal, they let her keep it. Then, her head, her pretty head, haloed by shoulder-length brown hair with tawny highlights and endowed with an adorable round mouth, was encircled in an ogreish chamber where the noise of a factory resonated. During the fifteen minutes the torture lasted, Mona continuously sang songs to herself to resist, to inject a little good humor and life into this coffin. She sang a slightly sappy lullaby her mother used to hum to her when tucking her in; she sang a pop song, a melody that played on a loop in supermarkets and whose music video full of slick-haired boys she liked; she sang catchy advertising jingles; she also sang "Une souris verte," in memory of the day she had screamed the lyrics to exasperate her father, without success.





The MRI results came in. Dr. Van Orst called Camille and Paul in and hastened to reassure them. There was nothing. Absolutely nothing. On the cross-sectional images, the brain’s anatomy revealed only homogeneous zones. No tumor on that front. Other tests were run, endlessly. All through the night: from the depths of her pupils to her inner ear, via her blood, bones, muscles, arteries. Nothing there either. The calm after the storm. Had it ever even taken place?

The face of a clock lost in a hallway of the Hôtel-Dieu read five in the morning. A nursery rhyme image came to Camille's mind: it was as if, she confided exhaustedly to her husband, some evil creature had stolen Mona's two eyes before giving them back to her. As if it had gotten the wrong victim, added Paul. Or as if it had sent a sign, a warning, and was preparing to repeat its misdeed, they both thought in silence.





In the courtyard, the bell rang. The flood of children, channeled by Ms. Hadji, headed up to the second floor. The teacher informed her fifth-grade students that they wouldn't be seeing Mona, their classmate, again until after the All Saints' vacation. She herself had just been informed by Camille, who had explained on the phone almost everything about the infernal night, without hiding the gravity of the events. Naturally, the children asked questions. Was she allowed to go on vacation a week before everyone else?

"She is a little sick," the teacher merely offered, not entirely satisfied with the phrasing.

"A little sick: so lucky!" exclaimed Diego, in the third row, and his squeaky voice met with the agreement of the whole class.





For most children imagine illness as an open sesame to freedom...

At the back of the room, right next to the chalk-dusted curtains, Lili and Jade, Mona’s two best friends, who knew every corner of her bedroom, were salivating even more. Oh, how they would have loved to be with her! "A little sick"? Yeah, sure, Lili thought, but without a doubt she was going to spend her days in her dad’s antique shop. And Jade, her eyes turned toward the empty seat left by Mona, projected herself with delight in her company, inventing all sorts of games and stories in the modest shop filled with old objects smelling of America—a whole bric-a-brac of shimmering, amusing, mysterious objects that made children dream. But Lili protested:

"No, no, when she's sick, her grandpa Dadé comes to watch her, and he scares me."

Jade forced a mocking laugh to show that nothing frightened her, least of all Mona’s grandfather. Yet deep down, the little girl conceded: yes, faced with this old man, immense, skinny, and scarred, speaking in a deep, mineral voice, she didn't feel so brave either...





"Hello, Dad, it's me."

It was noon when Camille, her limbs numb, decided to call her father. Henry Vuillemin refused to use a cell phone; he invariably answered calls on his landline with a dry, burning little "yes" that left little room for enthusiasm. His daughter hated this ritual and missed the days when her mother, still alive, used to pick up the receiver. She enunciated each syllable.

"Dad, I have to tell you: something terrible happened last night."

She recounted everything, in order, trying to control her emotions.

"Well?" Henry asked with a hint of impatience.





But Camille had held back her tears for so long over the course of her story that a gigantic sob heaved her body and choked her: she was unable to answer.

"Darling, well?" her father pressed.

This unexpected "darling" gave her a breath of oxygen; she caught her breath and breathed:

"Nothing! For now, nothing. She's okay, I think."

Then, Henry let out a long sigh of relief, tilted his neck back, threw his head back, and observed the cheerful motifs of plump fruits, scrolls, and spring flowers on his ceiling moldings.

"Let me have a word with her."

But Mona, curled up in a living room armchair beneath a rust-colored throw, had dozed off.

The poet Ovid described the phase when consciousness falls asleep as entering a vast cave sheltering the languid and indolent god Sleep. He imagined a cavity inaccessible to Phoebus, master of the Sun. Mona had learned from her grandfather that on a human scale, there was no journey more regular than the one toward those mysterious and shifting lands... One therefore shouldn't neglect these realms where one constantly wanders, throughout all of life.









In the days that followed, Dr. Van Orst at the Hôtel-Dieu initiated new tests. They still revealed no particular anomaly. The explanation for those sixty-three minutes of blindness stubbornly remained elusive, to such an extent that the practitioner was now reluctant to use the term "transient ischemic attack," which implied a vascular deficiency he was no longer entirely sure of. In the absence of a clear diagnosis, he suggested to Mona—and to her parents—resorting to hypnosis. This idea provoked a stunned silence in Paul. As for the little girl, she wasn't quite sure she knew what it entailed. She associated the term with the "choking game" she had vaguely heard about at school, which terrified her. To correct this false perception, Van Orst explained that by placing Mona in a state of hypnosis, he could temporarily put her under his influence. Such an experience would allow him to go back in time and take her to that original moment when her sight had extinguished, have her relive it, and, potentially, identify the cause. Paul protested vehemently. It was out of the question; it was dangerous. Van Orst didn't insist: for a child to be hypnotized effectively, they must surrender themselves with complete trust. Now, between Mona’s prejudice and her father’s angry overreaction, the situation was a minefield. Camille, for her part, had said nothing.

Consequently, Van Orst prescribed a standard medical regimen for his young patient: weekly blood and arterial checks, visits to the ophthalmologist, a ten-day convalescence. He ordered Paul and Camille to monitor "any appearance of subjective signs of a symptomatic nature," which meant they had to be extremely attentive to how their daughter felt. To that end, he suggested consulting a child psychiatrist:

"It's everyday prophylaxis, rather than therapeutics strictly speaking," he assured them.





Paul and Camille registered his recommendations with some confusion, but deep down, there was only one question gnawing at them: "Is Mona at risk of eventually losing her sight?" Curiously, Dr. Van Orst never once brought up the threat of a permanent relapse, and despite the parents’ dread of it, they preferred to avoid the subject. They even told themselves that after all, if the doctor sidestepped it, it was because there was no reason to bring it up.

Henry Vuillemin tackled it head-on with his daughter. He was not the type to flee from questions, even when they were abysses. Usually quite stingy with phone calls, unless it was to hear Mona, he called constantly that week. In a warm, impassioned voice, he badgered Camille: yes or no, was his darling granddaughter, the treasure of his life, going to go blind? Henry also insistently demanded to see Mona, and Camille could not decently refuse him. She suggested he come on All Saints' Sunday, exactly one week after the bout of blindness. Paul, guessing the nature of the conversation, secretly resigned himself and downed a glass of harsh Burgundy almost in one gulp. In front of his father-in-law, he felt miserably stupid. Mona, on the other hand, stamped her feet with impatience when she heard the news.





She loved this grandfather, full of years and strength. She also adored watching how everyone who crossed his path let themselves be charmed by his towering silhouette and his heavy, almost square-rimmed glasses. In his company, she felt protected. And transported. Henry had always made a point of speaking to her as he would have spoken to an adult. She was the one who demanded this asymmetry and savored it, and amused by it. She was never afraid of not understanding and laughed at mistakes and misunderstandings. Because she monitored her own language in return, and it was obviously more a game than a serious matter.

Henry didn't want to turn her into a little trained monkey. He didn't want to be one of those parody grandfathers who hunt down the mistakes of youth to correct them in a pedantic voice. It wasn't in his nature. He had never made her do her homework, didn't meddle with report cards. Besides, he adored the way Mona expressed herself. Even more, he was fascinated by her turns of phrase. Why? There, he didn't have the key. He failed to understand. He had always been obsessed, haunted by something in her childish language. Was it an added element or a certain *je ne sais quoi* missing? A quality or a flaw? This impression was all the more troubling as it wasn't recent: Mona's "little music" had always secreted an enigma that Henry was firmly resolved to discover one day, through sheer listening.





Camille sometimes conceded her astonishment at a relationship she judged "too good to be true," but she acknowledged that it worked wonderfully and that her daughter was happy because of it. And then Henry, who gladly quoted Victor Hugo’s *The Art of Being a Grandfather*, reminded anyone willing to listen of one of the cardinal principles of transmission: it matters little whether one immediately understands everything someone says, as if each new word had to already be a blossoming tree in the vast orchard of the brain. The blossoms would come on the appointed day, provided one had plowed the furrows and planted the seeds.

These furrows and seeds, in Henry Vuillemin, were a flow of rich, confident words that hooked you from the first intonation and never let you go; it was a very simple way of speaking but of such scope that it was euphoric; it was a storyteller's rhythm that often accelerated, before slowing down, then becoming tinged with gentle emotion. It was a steamroller of worldly experience and quiet erudition.

Thus, with this "Dadé," the relationship was of a unique nature. From grandparents to grandchildren, from grandchildren to grandparents, a miraculous bond is sometimes created, stemming from the fact that, by a sort of existential curve, the elders return, from the heights of their old age, to the feelings of their early youth and grasp, better than anyone, the spring of life.





Henry Vuillemin occupied a beautiful apartment on Avenue Ledru-Rollin, right above the Bistrot du Peintre, a narrow, wood-paneled establishment imitating the Art Nouveau style. He went down every morning and had his habits: a coffee and a croissant, reading the national press, a random chat here and there, with the everyday customers and the waiters on break. He felt he belonged to an old world and, ritually, walked at a very slow pace to the Place de la Bastille, liked to examine the furniture in the shop windows on the Rue du Faubourg-Saint-Antoine, headed back up toward the Place de la République via the central median of the Boulevard Richard-Lenoir, and cut across to the Boulevard Voltaire. In the late afternoon, at home, he would leaf through the art books that piled up to the ceiling. Henry, who was one centimeter taller than General de Gaulle, grabbed the most inaccessible ones without a step stool or ladder, and by a curious coincidence, those were regularly the ones that attracted him most. His memory was prodigious, though one had to distinguish his propensity to talk about what he knew from his personal memories, which he protected beneath layers of modesty. Mona knew the rule. The only taboo with her grandfather was bringing up Colette Vuillemin, who had left him a widower seven years earlier. In tune with her father, Camille never said anything about her either. No matter how much the child tried to open a breach from time to time, she met with a leaden silence. Colette was not talked about. Never. The only exception to this taboo: Henry kept the charm honoring his late wife around his neck. It was a pretty little cerith shell mounted on a fishing line that he had picked up with her on the French Riviera during the summer of 1963—he no longer knew exactly which day, but remembered the sweltering heat and having sworn many things to Colette. Mona, as mentioned, wore the same pendant, inherited from her grandmother.





We all have our ways of swearing. Henry Vuillemin swore on "all that is beautiful on earth." The expression surprised Mona, and when she heard it, she systematically shrugged her shoulders with a little confused laugh: all that is beautiful on earth was at once a bit of everything and a bit of nothing. And then, she wondered if he, her revered grandfather, was part of it. Henry had evidently been an attractive young man and remained imposing, charming, spectacular. His octogenarian face, emaciated and sharp, exuded a tremendously seductive vigor and intelligence. But he was scarred. A scar tore across the right side of his face, starting beneath the cheekbone and rising to the eyebrow. The wound must have been very painful. It had not only torn away a furrow of skin, but a piece of the cornea. It was a war memory. A horrific memory: on September 17, 1982, during a photo report in Lebanon for Agence France-Presse, a Phalangist had struck him with a knife to stop him from advancing. He was approaching the Shatila camp. Rumor had it that massacres were being perpetrated there, that Palestinian refugees were being arbitrarily executed without trial, in retaliation for the assassination of President Bachir Gemayel. He had wanted to verify, to bear witness. His path was blocked with inhuman violence. Henry had lost a lot of blood and the use of his eye. This infirmity, combined with his height and, over the years, an increasingly pronounced thinness, had given his appearance a supernatural air. The handsome reporter, who resembled Eddie Constantine, had become a figure of legend.









On All Saints' Day, Mona was in good form. Her parents had worked to make the heavy November atmosphere joyous. Jade and Lili, her two friends, had come over to watch an installment of *Toy Story*, the animated film where toys come to life. They had goofed around gently. Jade, especially. She was a mischievous, pretty little girl, with fine Eurasian eyes, olive skin, and perfectly combed hair. Yet she was driven by a surprising passion: pulling faces. She knew how to morph her harmonious face into a shifting, unhinged theater, where bizarre and burlesque expressions passed like frenzied actors. Mona always demanded more, delighted.

At seven p.m., the intercom rang. Paul pursed his lips and raised his eyebrows. Camille pressed the button:

"Dad?"

It was indeed him, arriving right on time. Paul, after greeting him, left to walk Jade and Lili back to their respective parents, and for the duration of the trip, Mona, her mother, and her grandfather found themselves alone in the apartment. After an irrepressible outburst of joy, the little girl, who had kept from telling her two friends about her misadventure, launched into a detailed account of her sixty-three minutes of ordeal and the trials she had endured at the hospital. Camille did not interrupt her.





Henry, meanwhile, as he listened to Mona talk and talk, examined the place where the child lived with clinical detachment. Even her bedroom, despite the enchanting tackiness of the decor, seemed extremely sad to him. That wallpaper with the garlanded flower motif, those knickknacks studded with sequins in the shape of hearts or animals, those pink or brown stuffed animals, those grotesque posters of stars barely out of adolescence, that plastic jewelry, that furniture imitating cartoon princesses... The garish, candy-colored hues of all this junk caught him by the throat. In this whole ensemble dripping with bad taste, there were only two openings onto a form of beauty. A sturdy 1950s American industrial lamp with an articulated arm, which Paul had hunted down, given to Mona, and fixed to her little writing desk. And then, above the bed, framed, an exhibition poster reproducing a painting. Extraordinarily subtle colors of a cold tonality crackled within it. They depicted a nude woman, in profile and leaning forward, sitting on a stool draped with white fabric, her left ankle resting on her right knee. In one corner, one could read: "Musée d’Orsay Paris – Georges Seurat (1859-1891)."





Despite these exceptions, Henry made the distressing observation that the time of childhood is, out of convenience, mostly steeped in futile and ugly objects. And Mona was no exception to the rule. Beauty, true artistic beauty, only entered her daily life clandestinely. It was absolutely normal, Henry noted: the refinement of taste, the construction of sensibility would come later. Except for this—and the thought choked him—that Mona had come very close to losing her sight and that, if her eyes went dark permanently in the days, weeks, or months to come, she would take with her, into the confines of her memory, only the recollection of flashy and vain things. An entire life in the dark, mentally making do with the worst the world has to offer, with no escape for her memories? It was impossible. It was terrifying.

To his daughter’s great annoyance, Henry was taciturn and distant throughout dinner. When Mona finally went to bed, Camille, looking resolute, turned up the volume of Coltrane's saxophone playing on a vintage chrome jukebox, to mask their voices and ensure the little girl would hear nothing.

"Dad. For now, Mona seems to be digesting... (she hesitated over the words)... what just happened. But the doctor recommends follow-up with a child psychiatrist. It might be weird for her, and I was wondering if you could take her to the appointment, just so she feels reassured..."

"A shrink? Is that really what's going to stop her from going blind?"

"That's not the point, Dad!"

"I think it is, and it will be as long as you don't dare ask the doctor! Doctor who, again?"

"His name is Van Orst and he's very good," Paul offered clumsily to take part in the conversation.





"Dad, wait," Camille resumed. "Listen to me. Paul and I are going to do everything we can to make sure nothing happens to Mona, do you hear me? But she's ten years old and we can't pretend she hasn't been through anything. The doctor says her psychological balance is a priority. So I'm just asking to see if you want to handle this, because I know Mona will trust you. Do you hear me, Dad?"

Henry heard perfectly. But at that precise moment, in a fraction of a second, an Apollonian idea had lit up in his head, which he jealously kept to himself. He wouldn't take his granddaughter to see a child psychiatrist, no... Instead, he would have her follow a cure of a completely different nature, a cure capable of compensating for the ugliness her youth was steeped in.

Mona, who had complete trust in him, who gave him credence she granted no other adult, was going to have to accompany him to the places that hold the most beautiful and human things the world has produced: she was going to have to accompany him to the museums. If, by some misfortune, Mona were to one day go permanently blind, she would at least enjoy a sort of reservoir, in the depths of her brain, from which to draw visual splendors. The grandfather thus had his project... Once a week, following an immutable ritual, he would take Mona by the hand and take her to contemplate a work—just one—first in long silence, so that the infinite delight of the colors and lines could penetrate his granddaughter’s mind, then in words, so that she would move past the stage of visual rapture to understand how artists speak to us about life, and how much they illuminate it.





For his little Mona, he therefore imagined something better than medicine. They would go to the Louvre Palace first, the Musée d'Orsay second, and finally to Beaubourg. There, yes, there, in these places devoted to the preservation of the boldest and most beautiful things humanity had provided, he would find a tonic for his granddaughter. Henry was not one of those art lovers who are content in themselves, outside the world, with the polish of flesh painted by Raphael or the rhythm of a line traced by Degas's charcoals. He loved the almost incendiary qualities of the works. Sometimes, he said: "Art is pyrotechnics, or it’s hot air." And he loved that, in their entirety or through a detail, a painting, a sculpture, a photograph could stoke the meaning of existence.

At the moment Camille asked for his help, Henry was thus assailed by hundreds of images: the rocky volumes behind the *Mona Lisa*; the carved ape on the back of Michelangelo’s *Dying Slave*; the alarmed expression of the child with blond curls to the right of the *Oath of the Horatii*; the strange gelatinous kidneys in Goya’s *Lamb*; and then the clods of earth in Rosa Bonheur’s *Ploughing in the Nivernais*; the butterfly-shaped signature Whistler uses in his mother’s portrait; the wavering apse of Van Gogh’s church... Or Kandinsky’s colors, Picasso’s fractures, or Soulages’s *outrenoir*. All of this burst forth like so many signs calling to him, demanding to be seen, heard, understood, loved. Like a counterfire to the ashes threatening Mona's eyes.





Henry sported a broad smile:

"Understood. I will take Mona with me every Wednesday afternoon. From now on, it's me, and only me, who will manage this psychological follow-up. It will be our business, between the two of us. Agreed?"

"You're going to find someone good, Dad? You'll ask your old friends for advice?"

"Is it agreed in principle? I will handle it with no questions or intervention from anyone."

"But you're not going to pick a child psychiatrist at random, do you hear me? You'll be very careful."

"Do you trust me, darling?"

"Yes," Paul stated with authority to counter any hesitation from Camille. "Mona admires and respects you, and she loves you like she loves no one else, so yes, we trust you."

Camille, at her husband's firm words, nodded tenderly without adding anything. Henry felt a slightly moist gleam cross his good eye. Coltrane's sax made the walls ripple. Mona, in her bedroom, slept, watched over by Georges Seurat.





Part I


LOUVRE

Sandro Botticelli


Learn to Receive




The great glass pyramid amused Mona. Hoisted insolently amidst the stone pavilions of the Louvre Palace, its ethereal shape, its transparency, its way of catching the cold November sun enchanted her. Her grandfather wasn't talking much. Yet she could tell he was in an excellent mood because he squeezed her little hand in his with the self-assured tenderness of happy people, and swung their arms smoothly. His joviality, though silent, radiated in a childlike way.

"How beautiful the pyramid is, Dadé! It looks like a big Chinese hat," Mona judged, while weaving through the clusters of tourists on the plaza.





Henry looked at her and mixed a sort of doubtful pout into his smile. It gave him an odd look that made the little girl giggle. They entered the glass structure, passed through security, glided down an escalator, found themselves in the immense concourse so little different from those of train stations or airports, and made their way to the Denon Wing. The commotion around them was stifling. Stifling, yes, because most visitors who make up the crowd of a great museum don't know what they want to do; they produce a generalized drifting, infusing the stagnant, hesitant, and even slightly murky atmosphere typical of these places when they are victims of their own success.

Amidst the hubbub, Henry bent his long, skinny legs to speak to his granddaughter eye to eye. He did this every time he had something truly important to tell her. His mineral voice, pure and deep, cut through the surrounding din. It seemed to silence the idle chatter and exhausting outbursts of the entire universe.

"Mona, every week, the two of us will go see one artwork—just one artwork, no more—at the museum. These people around us would like to swallow everything at once, and they get lost, not knowing how to pace their desires. We will be much wiser, much more reasonable. We will look at a single work, first without saying a word, for many long minutes, and then, afterward, we will talk about it."

"Oh really? I thought we were going to the doctor's." (She would have liked to say "the child psychiatrist," but wasn't entirely sure of the word.)

"Tell me, Mona, do you want to go to the psychiatrist's, afterward? Is that important to you?"

"Like I'd want to! Anything but that!"





"Then listen to me carefully, darling. There will be no need if you look attentively at what we are going to see."

"Really? And is it bad to skip the... (she stumbled over the word again and opted for her simpler phrasing) doctor?"

"No, it's not bad. I swear to you on all that is beautiful on earth."





After navigating a maze of staircases, Henry and Mona found themselves in a modestly sized room that saw heavy foot traffic but where almost no one bothered to fix their eyes on the artwork that held pride of place there. Henry let go of his granddaughter's hand and told her with infinite gentleness:

"Now, look, Mona. Take all the minutes you need to look, to really look."

And Mona planted herself, intimidated, in front of a very damaged painting, severely cracked in several places, with missing pieces, a painting that, at first glance, exuded the feeling of an altered and distant past. Henry contemplated it too, but mostly he examined his granddaughter, felt her wavering, her perplexity. There she was, furrowing her brows, then stifling a slightly embarrassed laugh. He knew that, even when faced with a Renaissance masterpiece, a ten-year-old girl, however bright, curious, sensitive, and clever she might be, could not immediately fall into ecstasy. He knew that, contrary to popular belief, it took time to penetrate the depths of art, that it was a demanding exercise and not an easy rapture. He also knew that Mona, because it was him asking her, would play the game and that, despite her embarrassment, she would scrutinize the forms, the colors, the texture with the promised attention.





The image was laid out simply. Far to the left, one could make out a fountain, in front of which, like a frieze, four young women with long, curly hair, surprisingly similar, stood spread out. They grasped one another by the arm, intertwining as if composing a human garland punctuated by the diversity of their outfits: green and mauve for the first, white for the second, pink for the third, yellow-orange for the fourth. This multicolored procession gave the impression of forward movement, and facing it, on the right side of the work, isolated against a neutral background, stood a fifth woman, young, extremely beautiful, adorned with a magnificent pendant and a crimson dress. She too seemed animated by a forward movement, as if she were going to meet the procession. Indeed, she held out a sort of cloth in their direction, into which one of the creatures—the one in pink—delicately placed something. What was it? Impossible to say. The object was erased. There was also, in the foreground, in a corner, a little boy, blond-haired, in profile, slightly smiling. The setting was entirely stark: the only echo to the fountain on the left was a truncated and highly faded column enclosing the scene on the right.



Mona played the game. But after six minutes, it was already too much. Six minutes in front of a faded image was an unusual and arduous ordeal. So she turned to her grandfather and struck up a conversation with an insolence only she could get away with:

"Dadé, your painting is pretty beat up! Compared to it, your face looks brand new..."





Henry looked at the work and all the degradations scarring it. He bent his knees.

"You'd do better to listen to me instead of talking nonsense... A 'painting,' you say! Wrong! First of all, Mona, it's not a 'painting.' It's what we call a 'fresco.' Do you know what a fresco is?"

"Yes, I think so... but I forgot!"

"A fresco is a painting done on a wall, and it's very fragile, because if the wall gets damaged—and a wall crumbles a lot over time—well, the painting gets damaged too..."

"Why did the artist paint on this wall? Because it's the Louvre?"

"Not at all. It's true that an artist might want to make a fresco at the Louvre, because the Louvre is the greatest museum on the planet and it would be normal for a painter to want to paint his work directly on it, so that it becomes like the skin of the palace. But you see, Mona, the Louvre wasn't always a museum. It's only been one for about two hundred years. Before, it was a castle, where kings and the court lived. When the artist executed this fresco, it was around 1485. And so he didn't create it for the walls of the Louvre, but for those of a villa in Florence."

"Florence?" (She absently fiddled with the pendant around her neck.) "That makes me think of the name of an old fiancée of yours, before Grandma, huh?"





"Doesn't ring a bell, but it's not impossible! Now listen closely. Florence is an Italian city. In Tuscany, precisely. And it's the cradle of what we call the Renaissance. In the fifteenth century—the Quattrocento, as the Italians say—Florence experienced extraordinary effervescence. It had about a hundred thousand inhabitants, and the city was prosperous, thanks to commerce and banking. And you see, religious orders, high political dignitaries, and even ordinary citizens, among the highest on the social ladder, wanted to leverage their wealth and showcase their prestige by supporting the creations of their contemporaries. We call them great patrons. So, painters, sculptors, and architects took advantage of their trust and the means granted to them to craft incredibly beautiful paintings, statues, and buildings."

"They were made of gold, I bet..."

"Not quite. There were indeed, in the Middle Ages, very beautiful paintings copiously covered in gold leaf. It gave the object value, and it symbolized divine light, on top of that! But, during the Renaissance, painting progressively did away with the flashy effect of gilding to seek to better render reality as we see it, with its landscapes, the singularity of faces, animals, the movement of beings, things, the sky, and the sea."

"They like nature, is that it?"

"That's exactly it: people start to love nature. But you know that when we talk about nature, we aren't just talking about what grows on the earth."

"Oh, and what else do we talk about?"

"We also talk, more abstractly, about human nature. Human nature is actually what we are deep down, with our zones of shadow and light, our flaws and our qualities, our fears and our hopes. And you see, it is precisely this human nature that the artist seeks to improve."





"And how's that?"

"If you cultivate your garden, you do good to nature. You allow it to flourish. This fresco seeks to do good to human nature by telling it something very simple, but essential, and which you must remember forever, Mona."

But Mona, to provoke the old man, plugged her ears and closed her eyes, as if she didn't want to hear or see anything he could tell her. After a few seconds, she discreetly cracked an eyelid to observe his reaction. He was smiling nonchalantly. So, she stopped her little game and mobilized all her attention. Because she sensed that her grandfather, after these long minutes of silence, contemplation, and discussion, after this little journey through the damaged image laid out before her eyes, was finally going to reveal to her one of those secrets you keep engraved in the depths of your heart.

Henry motioned for her to direct her gaze to the slightly erased area where there seemed to be an object the young woman on the right was taking in her hands. The little girl complied.

"The procession of these four women on the left is composed of Venus and the Three Graces. They are generous divinities. And they are offering a gift—we don't know what it is because a little bit of the paint is missing—to a young girl. The Three Graces are what we call allegories, Mona: they don't exist in real life and you'll never run into them, but they represent important values. It is said they represent the three stages that make us sociable and hospitable beings, that is, truly human humans. And this fresco says just how crucial these three stages are; it seeks to anchor them within each of us."





"Three stages? What stages?"

"The first consists of knowing how to give, the third of knowing how to give back. And between the two, there is one without which nothing is possible, which is like a sort of keystone, a keystone that supports all of human nature."

"Which one, Dadé?"

"Look: what is the young girl on the right doing?"

"You told me: she's lucky because she's receiving a gift..."

"Exactly, Mona. She's receiving a gift. And that is what is absolutely fundamental. Knowing how to receive. What this fresco says is that we must learn to receive, that human nature, to be capable of great and beautiful things, must be ready to welcome: to welcome the benevolence of others, their desire to please, to welcome what it doesn't yet have and what it isn't yet. There will always be time for the one who receives to give back, but to give back, that is to say, to give again, it is essential to have been capable of receiving. Do you understand, Mona?"

"Your story is complicated, but yes, I think I understand..."

"I am certain you understand! And you see, if these ladies are so beautiful, with this drawing so supple and graceful, this uninterrupted line which suffers no jarring, no doubt, it is to express the importance of this continuity, of this chain that must link humans to one another and improve their nature: give, receive, and give back; give, receive, and give back; give, receive, and give back..."





Mona didn't know what to say anymore. Above all, she didn't want to disappoint her grandfather. She had already used humor during their conversation and stayed quiet to avoid adding anything too naive, since she knew very well he was speaking to her and bringing her to this immense museum so she would become a little more grown-up. For the moment, she merely felt a twinge, because this call to grow up, this exploratory intoxication of a new world, had an extraordinary magnetic power, all the more so since the call came from Henry, whom she revered. Yet, in the very depths of her soul, there already existed this terrible premonition that what you give back, you will never see again. And this regret, sharp albeit very distant, of a childhood vanished forever twisted her heart.

"Shall we go, Dadé? On our way, troops?"

"Alright, Mona! Let's go!"

Henry took her hand in his again and they left the Louvre at a slow pace, without a word. Outside, night was beginning to fall. Henry was not unaware of the distress that had just made his granddaughter waver. But he categorically refused to coddle people under the pretext of ensuring only good, full, and pleasant moments in their company. No: he knew very well that existence is only worthwhile on the condition of accepting its hardships, and that these reveal, once sifted by time, a precious and fertile material, a beautiful and useful substance, which allows life to truly be life.





Besides, by that miracle of childhood, Mona's distress hardly lasted: as she walked briskly along, she started humming to herself. Henry never interrupted her in those moments, which he found incredibly touching. And then suddenly, as they neared her home, Mona stopped, remembering the white lie they had agreed upon to avoid the sessions with the child psychiatrist. She opened her large blue eyes and turned her cheeky little head, laughing at the trick they were playing on her parents.

"Dadé, what do I say if Mom and Dad ask for the name of the doctor I went to see?"

"Tell them his name is Dr. Botticelli."

The All Saints' vacation flew by, and Mona returned to school. Camille arrived early, around eight o'clock, under the dreary covered playground that offered shelter from a disagreeable autumn rain. She entrusted her daughter to Ms. Hadji, quickly explaining how her convalescence and the medical follow-up were going, punctuated among other things by a visit to a child psychiatrist every Wednesday. She insisted: the teacher certainly needed to be attentive to Mona, but without showing her any special treatment, nothing that would single her out from her classmates.
