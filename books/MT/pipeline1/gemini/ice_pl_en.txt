Ice

We do not freeze.

I
WARSAW

"If a soldier fires a rifle, at that very moment there begins to exist the wound that the bullet will bore into the enemy some time later; if someone takes a dose of an incurable poison, at that very moment the ultimate fatal process begins to exist, even if its final hour does not arrive for a few days. Rightly did the ancients say: we are already dead the moment we are born. Over the further path of the bullet and the poison (and partly over the living out of our lives) we have no power."

CHAPTER ONE

On the Thousand-Ruble Son

On July 14, 1924, when the *chinovniki* of the Ministry of Winter came for me, on the evening of that day, the eve of the Siberiad, only then did I begin to suspect that I did not exist.

Under the featherbed, under three blankets and an old gabardine coat, in fustian long johns and a woolen sweater, wearing socks pulled over socks—only my feet stuck out from under the featherbed and blankets—after a dozen or so hours of sleep, finally thawed out, curled almost into a ball, with my head pressed under a thickly cased pillow, so that even sounds reached me soft, warmed, encased in wax, like ants bogged down in resin; that was how they pushed their way deep down, slowly and with great toil, through the sleep and through the pillow, millimeter by millimeter, word by word:
"Gospodin Venedikt Yeroslavsky."
"That’s him."
"Spit?"
"Spit, Ivan Ivanovich."
A voice and a voice, the first low and hoarse, the second low and singsong; before I lifted blanket and eyelid, I already saw them bending over me, the hoarse one by my head, the singsong one by my feet, my tsarist angels.





"We have woken young master Venedikt," Ivan stated when I heaved up my other eyelid. He nodded at Biernatowa; the landlady meekly left the room.
Ivan pulled up a stool and sat; he kept his knees together, and on his knees rested a black bowler hat with a narrow brim. A tall *Vatermörder* collar, white as snow in the southern sun, stung my eyes, a white *Vatermörder* and white office cuffs, blinding against the uniform blackness of their attire. I blinked.
"If you'll allow us, Venedikt Filipovich."
They allowed themselves. The other sat at the foot of the bed, his weight pulling the featherbed down until I had to let it go; grabbing the blankets instead, I propped myself up on the pallet, and thus uncovered my back as well; the cold air pushed its way under my sweater and long johns; I shivered, wide awake.
I threw the coat over my shoulders, pulled my knees up under my chin.
They looked at me with amusement.
"How is your health?"
I cleared my throat. Night phlegm had gathered in my throat, a corrosive acid atop all the stomach contents—the garlic sausage, gherkins, whatever else we had consumed yesterday, the warm cornel-cherry liqueur and cigarettes, a multitude of cigarettes. I leaned toward the wall and hawked into the spittoon. It doubled me over. Bent in half, I coughed heavily for a long moment. I wiped my mouth with the torn sleeve of my coat.
"Strong as a horse's."
"Ah, that is good, that is good, we were afraid you wouldn't get out of bed."
I stood up. My wallet lay on the windowsill, wedged behind a pot with a dead pelargonium. I took out my *bumaga*, shoved it under Ivan's nose. He didn't even look.





"Why, gospodin Yeroslavsky! Are we some kind of beat cops!" He straightened up on that stool even more—I thought it was impossible, but he straightened up even more, and now the walls seemed crooked, the wardrobe hunchbacked, the doorframe scoliotic; offended, the *chinovnik* lifted his chin and puffed out his chest. "We are very politely inviting you to join us on Miodowa Street, for tea and sweets, the commissar always orders sorbets, tartlets, cream horns, straight from Semadeni, a true debauchery of the palate, if I may say so, eh, Kirill?"
"You may, Ivan Ivanovich, by all means," Kirill sang out.
Ivan Ivanovich had a walrus mustache, heavily pomaded and curled upward; Kirill, on the other hand, was entirely, smoothly shaven. Ivan took an onion watch from his vest pocket on a tangled fob chain and announced that it was five to five; Commissar Preiss highly values punctuality, and what time does he leave for dinner? They have an appointment with the major general at the Francuska.





Kirill offered Ivan some snuff, Ivan offered Kirill a cigarette, and they watched me as I got dressed. I splashed ice-cold water into the washbasin. The stove tiles were cold. I turned up the wick in the lamp. The room's only window looked out onto a cramped courtyard, and the panes were so overgrown with dirt and frost that even at noon little sunlight leaked through them. When I used to shave—when I still shaved—I had had to place the lamp turned up to full flame before the mirror. Zyga had parted ways with his razor as soon as he arrived in Warsaw; he had grown a beard worthy of an Orthodox priest. I glanced at his bedding on the other side of the stove. He has lectures on Mondays, he must have gotten up at dawn. On Zygmunt's bed lay the *chinovniki's* black fur coats, their gloves, walking stick, and scarf. For the table was cluttered to the edges with dirty dishes, empty bottles, books, magazines, notebooks; Zyga was drying his socks and underwear by hanging them from the edge of the tabletop, pressed down by anatomy atlases and Latin dictionaries. And in the middle of the table, on a well-read, grease-stained copy of Riemann's *Über die Hypothesen, welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen* and on a pile of yellowed *Kuryer Warszawski* newspapers, kept for kindling, for caulking cracks burst open by the frost and drying out boots, as well as for wrapping sandwiches—there rose a double row of candles and stubs, the ruins of a stearin Parthenon. Against the wall opposite the stove, however, towered neat stacks of hardcover volumes, arranged by format and thickness, and by frequency of reading. The gorget with Our Lady of the Gate of Dawn hanging above them on the soot-stained wall—the sole remnant of the previous tenants, whom Biernatowa had thrown out onto the street for "unseemly conduct"—had blackened completely and now looked more like a piece of medieval armor for lilliputians. Ivan scrutinized it for a long time, with great concentration, seated stiffly on the stool, his left hand holding a cigarette out to the side at a forty-five-degree angle to his body, his right resting on his thigh next to the bowler, wrinkling his brows and nose, bristling his mustache—then I understood that he was almost blind, that he was a clerical myope, he had marks from a *pince-nez* on his nose and under his eye sockets; without the *pince-nez* he was left to rely on Kirill. They had come straight in from the frost and Ivan must have taken his glasses off. My own eyes sometimes water here. The air inside the tenement is thick, heavy, rubbed with all the odors of human and animal organisms; no one opens the windows, the doors are immediately slammed shut and the cracks above the thresholds stuffed with rags so that the heat does not escape the building—after all, fuel must be paid for, and whoever had enough money for coal would not nest in such dark outbuildings in the first place, where the air is thick, heavy, you breathe it as if drinking water spat out by a neighbor and his dog, every breath of yours has passed a million times before through the tubercular lungs of peasants, Jews, carters, butchers, and whores, coughed out of black larynxes it returns to you again and again, filtered through their saliva and mucus, passed through fungal, lice-ridden, and festering bodies, they have coughed it, blown it out their noses, puked it straight into your mouth, you must swallow, you must breathe, breathe!





"Ex-excuse me."
The outhouse at the end of the corridor was fortunately unoccupied. I vomited into a hole from which an icy stench wafted into my face. Cockroaches crawled out from under the shit-stained board. I crushed them with my thumb when they crept up toward my chin.
Stepping back out into the corridor, I saw Kirill standing in the doorway of the room—he was keeping an eye on me, standing guard in case I fled from them into the freezing cold in my long johns and sweater. I smiled knowingly. He handed me a handkerchief and pointed to my left cheek. I wiped it. When I tried to hand it back, he took a step back. I smiled for the second time. I have a wide mouth, it smiles very easily.
I donned my only going-out outfit, that is, the black suit in which I had taken my final exams; were it not for the layers of underclothes beneath, it would now hang on me as on a skeleton. The officials watched as I laced my shoes, as I buttoned my vest, as I fought with the stiff celluloid collar attached to my last cotton shirt. I took my documents and the rest of my cash, three rubles and forty-two kopecks—a bribe from this amount would be barely symbolic, but with empty pockets in a government office, a man feels naked. About my old sheepskin coat, however, there was nothing I could do: patches, stains, crooked seams, I had no other. They watched in silence as I squeezed my shoulders into the asymmetrical sleeves, the left one longer. I smiled apologetically. Kirill licked his pencil and meticulously noted something down on his cuff.





We left. Biernatowa had evidently been peeking through the cracked door—she immediately appeared beside the *chinovniki*, flushed and chattering away, to lead them back down the stairs from the second floor and through both well-like courtyards to the main gate, where the watchman Walenty, having adjusted his cap with its brass badge and hidden his pipe in his pocket, hurriedly swept the snow from the pavement and helped the *chinovniki* into the sleigh, taking the gentlemen by the elbows so they wouldn't slip on the iced-over trottoir; while Biernatowa, once they were seated and wrapping their legs in plaids, showered them with torrents of complaints about malicious tenants, about gangs of Powiśle thieves who break into houses even by day, and about the cruel frosts that cause the moisture-swollen windows to warp inside, and the pipes to burst in the walls, and no plumbing or sewage system can last long in the ground; finally she fervently assured them that she had long suspected me of various misdeeds and infamies, and would undoubtedly have informed the proper *vlasti*, had it not been for a thousand and one other woes piled upon her head—until the driver from his box behind Kirill's back cracked his whip and the horses jerked the sleigh to the left, forcing the woman to step back, and thus we set off on our way to the Warsaw branch of the Ministry of Winter, to the former Palace of the Kraków Bishops, 5 Miodowa Street, corner of Senatorska.





Before we turned from Koszykowa onto Marszałkowska, it started to snow lightly; I pulled my *shapka* over my ears. The officials in their voluminous furs and bowler hats resembling nutshells, seated on the low benches of the sleigh, Ivan next to me, Kirill with his back to the *izvozchik*, resembled the beetles I had seen in Zyga's *uchebnik*: thick, oval torsos, short little legs, a small head, all glossy black, enclosed in the geometric symmetry of ellipses and circles. A shape so close to the ideal of a sphere carves itself out from the world. They stared ahead with dispassionate gazes, lips tightly pursed and chins held high by their stiff collars, limply yielding to the motion of the sleigh. I thought I would learn something from them on the way. I thought they would start demanding handouts in exchange for favor, for a lack of haste and urgency. They remained silent. Should I ask them—how? what about? They would pretend not to hear. Flakes of sticky snow swirled between us. I hid my cold hands in the sleeves of my sheepskin.





In the Francuska Café, lights were burning, the electric glow beating through the large windows knitted halos of wool around the silhouettes of passersby. The summer sun should have still been high in the sky, but as usual, heavy clouds hung over the city; even the streetlamps had been lit—very tall ones, with spirally coiled tops. We turned north. From Ostrowski's pastry shop, near the intersection with Piękna, girls ran out in little red coats and white capes with hoods, their laughter piercing momentarily above the street din. I remembered my unfinished letter to Miss Julia and her final shout-question. Next to Ostrowski's, at Wedel's, we used to arrange card evenings with Fredek and Kiwajs. Just over here, past the Sokół cinema, at Kalka's, the Sweet Prince used to rent a room for night sessions. If I were to lift my head and look left, above Ivan's bowler, I would glimpse a window on the second floor of the tenement at number 71, the window from which Fredek fell.





At the intersection with Nowogrodzka hung a fat cow, frozen to a streetlamp; a sinew of dark ice connected it to the top of the facade of a four-story building. The cow must have come from the last cattle drive to the slaughterhouse in Ochota; the winter-men hadn't hacked it away yet. In the perspective of the street, above the roof of the Sfinks tenement, a blue-black nest of ice loomed, a great clot of permafrost hard as diamond, connected by a web of icy threads, icicles, spans, and columns to the tenements on both sides of Marszałkowska and Złota—to the tenements, the lamps, the stumps of frozen trees, the balcony balustrades, the bay windows, the spires of domes and turrets, the attics and chimneys. The Sfinks cinema had, of course, been closed for a long time; no lights burned on the top floors.

The sleigh slowed down as we passed Nowogrodzka. The driver pointed at something with his whip. The cart in front of us was pulling off onto the trottoir. Kirill looked behind him. I leaned out to the right. At the intersection with Aleje Jerozolimskie stood two policemen, using whistles and shouts to herd traffic away from the middle of the roadway—a *luty* was just freezing its way across.





For a few minutes we were stuck in the traffic jam it caused. Usually, the *lute* move above the roofs, less often coming down to the ground in cities. Even from such a distance, I thought I could feel the waves of cold flowing from it. I shivered and instinctively pressed my chin into the collar of my sheepskin. The officials of the Ministry of Winter exchanged glances. Ivan peeked at his watch. On the other side of the street, behind an advertising column plastered with posters for a wrestling match at the Okólnik circus, an English-dressed man was setting up an archaic camera to take a picture of the *luty*; the photo almost certainly wouldn't appear in the paper anyway, confiscated by the men from Miodowa. Ivan and Kirill didn't even pay attention to him.





The *luty* was exceptionally brisk; before dusk it should manage to cross to the other side of Marszałkowska, overnight it would climb above the roofs, by Friday it would manage to reach the nest above the movie theater. When the *mroznik* crossed from Praga to the Castle via the Alexander Bridge last year, the bridge was closed for nearly two months. Meanwhile, this *glacius* here—wait a quarter of an hour and I could probably discern its movement, how it freezes from place to place, shifts in the ice, through the ice, from ice to ice, how one crystalline thread snaps behind it, then another, and the blue-white crumble slowly showers down, a minute, *kshrk*, two minutes, *kshrk*, the wind carrying away the lighter particles along with the snow, but most of it freezing into the black sheet that was congealing the street mud behind the *luty*, ice of ice; and this path of rough permafrost, like the trail of snail slime, stretched for several dozen meters east of Jerozolimskie, both on the trottoir and on the hotel's facade. The rest had already been chipped away by the winter-men or had thawed on its own; yesterday afternoon the thermometer at Szniccer's showed five degrees above zero.





The *luty* did not move in a straight line, nor did it maintain a constant height above the cobblestones (they also freeze beneath the surface of the earth). Three or four hours ago, judging by the crumbled architecture of the ice, the *luty* had begun to change its trajectory: until then it had been moving barely a meter above the center of the street, but then, three hours ago, it had set off on a sharp parabola upward, above the tops of the lamps and the crowns of the frozen trees. I saw the row of slender stalagmites it had left behind; they gleamed with the reflected glow of the streetlamps, the reflections of colored neon signs, the lights beating through windows and displays. The row broke off above the tram tracks—the *luty* hung with its entire weight on a star-shaped web of frost-strands, stretched horizontally and reaching upward, toward the facades of the corner buildings. One could have walked under it, had anyone been insane enough.





Ivan nodded to Kirill, and he scrambled out of the sleigh with a reluctant grimace on his face, reddened from the biting frost. Maybe I'll get lucky, I thought, maybe we'll be late, Commissar Preiss will have already left for his scheduled dinner with the major general, and they'll send me away from Miodowa empty-handed. Thank You, God, for this icicle-monstrosity. I shifted on the bench, leaning my shoulder against the side of the sleigh. A newsboy ran up—"Hirohito Beaten!", "Special Express, Merezhov Triumphant!"—I shook my head. At traffic jams in the center, crowds immediately form, street hawkers appear, sellers of cigarettes and holy water, holy fire. The policemen were chasing pedestrians away from the *luty*; they couldn't possibly keep an eye on everyone, after all. A gang of urchins sneaked up from the direction of Briesemeister's restaurant. The bravest one, his face tied with a scarf and wearing thick, shapeless gloves, ran up to within a dozen paces of the *luty* and threw a cat at it. The tomcat flew in a high arc, spreading its paws, screaming bloody murder... soon the terrifying screech broke off. It fell upon the *luty* probably already dead, only to slowly roll off it into the snow, frozen solid: an ice sculpture of a cat with splayed limbs and a tail straightened like a wire. The boys ran off, howling with glee. A Jew with sidelocks shook his fist after them from the threshold of Epstein's jewelry shop, cursing colorfully in Yiddish.





Kirill, meanwhile, had cornered the older policeman and, grabbing him by the elbow so he wouldn't wander off in pursuit of the street urchins, began to persuade him of something in a hushed voice, but with the prominent help of sweeping gestures from his other hand. The beat cop turned his head, shrugged his shoulders, scratched his crown. The younger of the police pair shouted at his companion, move it, help out! On the Aleje, two sleighs locked runners, causing an even greater commotion, carts drove onto the sidewalks, pedestrians, cursing in Polish, Russian, German, and Yiddish, fled from under wheels and hooves; in front of the wine shop, a matron the size of a Gdańsk wardrobe slipped on the frozen mud, three gentlemen tried to lift her, a potbellied officer rushed to help, and so they hoisted her with the strength of four, heave-ho—she fell—heave-ho—she fell—heave-ho—by now half the street was bursting with laughter, and the little woman, red as a cherry, squeaked piercingly, dangling her fat little legs in their tiny boots... No wonder it was only at the sound of tearing sheet metal and the crack of breaking wood that we looked back toward the intersection. An automobile had collided with a coalman's cart; one horse had fallen, one wheel had popped off. The policeman shoved Kirill away, broke into a run toward the crash. Trapped inside the closed machine, the automobilist began honking his alarm horn; on top of that, something backfired under the motorcar's hood, as if he had fired a double-barreled shotgun. That was too much for the dapple-gray harnessed to the sleigh next to it. Spooked, it jerked forward, straight toward the *luty*. The driver grabbed the reins, but the horse itself must have also felt what kind of wall of frost it had run into—it bucked even more vigorously to the side, spinning the sleigh in place. Did the runner catch on the curb? Did the dapple-gray slip on the black sheet of ice? I was already standing in the ministerial sleigh, watching the accident along with Ivan over the row of vehicles ahead of us, but it all went too fast, too unexpectedly, too much movement, shouting, lights, and shadows. The dapple-gray fell over, the sleigh it was pulling overturned, its cargo tumbled out, over a dozen bulbous demijohns in wicker baskets with sawdust; the baskets and demijohns rolled into the middle of the intersection, some of them must have smashed, for a pale-green glistening liquid spilled across the ice—kerosene, I thought—and instantly fire flared up, from what, from an electric spark from the automobile, a dropped cigarette, the strike of a shod hoof against the cobblestones, I do not know. The blue flame danced across the entire width of the puddle, high, higher and higher, a meter, a meter and a half up—almost reaching the *luty* frozen into the aerial web.





The photographer, hunched over his camera, slowly, methodically flashed photo after photo. And what will he see in them later, what will be preserved on the glass and printed on the paper: snow—snow—pale halos of streetlamps—dark mud, dark cobblestones, dark sky—gray facades of tenements in the perspective of the broad canyon of the city—in the foreground a chaos of angular shapes of vehicles blocked in the jam—from between them and from between the human silhouettes bursts the glare of pure fire, so bright that the card seems completely unexposed in this spot—and above it, above the flame whiter than white, in the heart of the hanging arabesque of ice spreads the *luty*, the *luty*, a massive thunderbolt of frost, a starfish of rime, a living bonfire of cold, the *luty*, the *luty*, the *luty* above the fur toques of the women, the *luty* above the caps and bowlers of the men, the *luty* above the horse heads and the canopies of the carriages, the *luty* above the neon signs of cafes and salons, shops and hotels, pastry shops and greengrocers, the *luty* over Marszałkowska and Aleje Jerozolimskie, the *luty* over Warsaw, the *luty* over the Russian Empire.

Later, as we drove toward the Saxon Garden, and down Królewska Street, past the Garden dead beneath years of permafrost and the colonnade hung with icicles, past the tower and the Orthodox cathedral on Saxon Square covered in snowy overhangs, toward Krakowskie Przedmieście, that image—the afterimage of the picture and the imagination—haunted me time and again, an intrusive memory of unclear meaning, a sight seen yet misunderstood.





The officials exchanged gruff remarks in undertones, the driver shouted at inattentive pedestrians; the blizzard had eased, but it was getting colder and colder, my breath froze on my lips, hanging in a white cloud before my face, the sweating horses moved in clouds of sticky dampness—the Royal Castle was getting closer. Before turning into Miodowa, I saw it above Sigismund's Column: the Castle encased in a block of shadowy ice—and the great nest of *lute* above it. The violet-black frost-clot reached halfway down the roofs of the Old Town. On clear days, one can see waves of frost standing in the air all around the Great Tower. Thermometers lack the scale to measure this cold. Gendarmes keep watch by bonfires at the edge of Castle Square. When a *luty* freezes its way out of the nest, they close the streets. The Governor-General had established a cordon here of dragoons from the Fourteenth Little Russian Regiment, but the regiment had meanwhile been dispatched to the Japanese front.

The roof of the Palace of the Kraków Bishops, however, remained free of icy buildup. In the outbuilding on the Senatorska Street side, elegant shops were still located—electric spotlights illuminated the advertisements for Nikolai Shelekhov's Exclusive Delicatessen and the Moscow Trading House of Sergei Bazylovich Perlov's teas—but the main wing on Miodowa, beneath its rococo cresting and in the pilasters with Corinthian capitals, belonged to the Ministry of Winter. Above both gate passages hung black, two-headed eagles under the crowns of the Romanovs, inlaid with onyx tungetite.





We drove into the inner courtyard, the runners of the sleigh grating on the cobblestones. The officials got out first, Ivan immediately disappearing through the doors, perching his *pince-nez* on his nose; Kirill stopped on the steps, before the threshold, and looked back at me. I opened my mouth. He raised an eyebrow. I lowered my gaze. We went in.
The usher took my sheepskin and *shapka*, and the doorman pushed forward a large ledger in which I had to sign my name in two places, the pen slipping from my numb fingers, *perhaps I could sign for you, sir*, no, I, I will do it myself. Unlettered plebs also visit the corridors of the Winter brass.
Everything here shone with cleanliness: marble, parquets, glass and crystals, and rainbow winter-iron. Kirill led me up the main staircase, through two secretariats. On the walls, beneath the portraits of Nicholas II Alexandrovich and Pyotr Rappacki, hung sunlit landscapes of steppe and forest, of spring Saint Petersburg and summer Moscow, from the times when spring and summer still had entry to them. The staff did not lift their heads from their desks, but I saw councilors, referendaries, clerks, and scribes stealthily following me with their eyes, and then exchanging crooked glances with one another. When do office hours end? The Ministry of Winter never sleeps.





Extraordinary Commissar V. V. Preiss occupied a spacious office with an antique stove and a disused fireplace; the tall windows overlooked Miodowa and Castle Square. As I entered, passing Ivan in the doorway, who had evidently already announced me, the commissar was bustling about the samovar, his back turned. He himself possessed the figure of a samovar, a bulbous, pear-shaped torso, and a small, bald head. He moved with frenetic energy, his hands fluttering over the table, his feet never ceasing their dance, a little step to the left, a little step to the right—I was certain he was humming under his breath, smiling under his breath, that merry little eyes looked out at the world from his ruddy face, that the smooth forehead of the commissar of Winter did not wrinkle. Meanwhile, because he did not turn around, I stood by the door with my hands clasped behind my back, and allowed the warm air to fill my lungs, to wash over my skin, to melt the blood congealed in my veins. It was almost hot in the office, the large, colorfully painted majolica stove did not cool for a moment; the windowpanes eventually fogged up so much that through them I saw mostly blurred rainbows of streetlights, strangely spilling and melding on the glass. It is a matter of great political importance that it never be cold in the Ministry of Winter.

"Well, and why aren't you sitting, Venedikt Filipovich? Sit, sit."
Ruddy face, merry little eyes.
I sat down.





Sighing loudly, he dropped down on his side of the desk, cradling a cup of steaming tea in his hands. (He did not offer me any). He hadn't been in office here very long, the desk wasn't his, he looked behind it like a child playing at being a minister; he would surely have the piece of furniture replaced. They must have only just sent him, sent him, the tsar's extraordinary commissar—from where? From Petersburg, from Moscow, from Yekaterinburg, from Siberia?
I took a deeper breath.
"If Your Excellency permits... Am I under arrest?"
"Arrested? Arrested? How could such a thought ever enter your head?"
"Your officials—"
"My officials!"
"If I had received a summons, I certainly would have come myself—"
"Did they not invite you politely, Mr. Gierosławski?" He finally pronounced my surname correctly.
"I thought—"
"My God! Arrested!"
He started panting.
I clasped my hands on my knee. It is worse than I thought. They won't throw me in the *tyurma*. A high tsarist official wants to talk to me.
He started taking papers out of the desk. He took out a thick wad of rubles. He took out seals. Sweat ran down me under my underwear.
"Yees." Preiss slurped his tea loudly. "Please accept my condolences."
"Excuse me?"
"Your mother passed away last year, didn't she?"
"Yes, in April."
"You were left alone. That is sad. A man without a family is like... well, he is alone. It's bad, oh, it's bad." He turned a page, slurped, turned the next one.
"I have a brother," I muttered.
"Yes, yes, a brother on the other side of the world. Where was it he left for, Brazil?"
"Peru."
"Peru! What is he doing there?"
"Building churches."
"Churches! I'm sure he writes often."
"Well, more often than I do."





"That's nice. He misses you."
"Yeah."
"You don't miss him?"
"Miss him?"
"Miss your family. When was the last time you heard from your father?" Page, page, slurp.
Father. I knew it. What else could it have been about?
"We don't correspond, if that's what you're asking."
"That's awful, awful. Aren't you interested in whether he's even alive?"
"Is he?"
"Ah! Is Filip Filipovich Gierosławski alive! Is he alive!" He fairly leaped up from behind his operatic desk. Against the wall stood a large globe on a very light frame of wet winter-iron, on the wall hung a map of Asia and Europe; he spun the globe, smacked the map with the back of his hand.
When he looked back at me, not the slightest trace of the recent merriment remained on his chubby face; his dark *glaza* watched me with clinical attention.
"Is he alive," he whispered.
He picked up yellowed papers from the desk.
"Filip Gierosławski, son of Filip, born in the year eighteen seventy-eight in Wilkówka, in the Kingdom of Prussia, in East Prussia, Lidzbark district, a Russian subject since nineteen oh five, husband of Eulalia, father of Bolesław, Benedykt, and Emilia, sentenced in nineteen oh seven to death for partaking in a plot against the life of *Yevo Imperatorskavo Velichestva*, His Imperial Majesty, and in an armed rebellion; nu, by way of clemency the sentence was commuted to fifteen years of *katorga* with the deprivation of civil rights and the sequestration of his estate. In the year nineteen seventeen, he was pardoned the remainder of his sentence, with an order imposing a *prikaz* of *zhitelstvo* within the borders of the Amur and Irkutsk Governor-Generalships. He hasn't written? Never?"
"To mother. Maybe. In the beginning."
"But now? Recently? Since seventeen. At all?"
I shrugged.





"I'm sure you know perfectly well when and to whom he writes."
"Don't be insolent, young man!"
I smiled weakly.
"I apologize."
He studied me for a long moment. On the finger of his left hand he wore a ring with some dark stone in a setting of precious tungetite, engraved with the emblem of Winter; he tapped the ring against the desktop, *truk, trukk*, the even beats were stronger.
"You graduated from the Imperial University. What is your occupation now?"
"I was preparing for my *rigorosum* exams—"
"How do you support yourself?"
"I tutor mathematics."
"And do you earn much from this tutoring?"
Since I was already smiling, all that remained for me was to lower my eyes to my clenched hands.
"A lot, a little."
"You are a frequent guest of usurers, all the Jews on Nalewki Street know you. You owe Abiezer Blumstein himself over three hundred rubles. Three hundred rubles! Is this true?"
"Once Your Excellency tells me in what matter I am being interrogated, it will be easier for me to confess."
*Truk, trukk, truk, trukk.*
"Or maybe you really have committed some crime, that you're sweating here so much from fear, eh?"
"Your Excellency might deign to crack the windows."
He stood over me; he didn't even have to lean in particularly much to speak directly into my ear, first a whisper, then a growling soldierly tone, finally almost a shout.





"You are a gambler, my dear Benedykt, a compulsive cardplayer. What you win, you lose—what you earn, you lose—what you borrow, you lose—what you beg from friends, you lose—you have no friends anymore—you have nothing anymore, and even that you lose, you lose everything. Vingt-et-un, baccarat, *zimucha*, poker, any way is good. Once you won half a sawmill—you lost it the same night. You have to lose, you cannot walk away from the table until you lose yourself so thoroughly that no one wants to play with you anymore. No one wants to play with you anymore, Venedikt Filipovich. No one wants to lend to you anymore. You have pawned yourself two years in advance. Bolesław doesn't write to you, you write to him, begging for money, but he sends no more. You don't write to your father, because your father has no money. You wanted to marry, but your prospective father-in-law set the dogs on you after you pawned and lost your fiancée's dowry. If you would at least put a bullet in your head, as befits a nobleman, but you won't do it, *tfoo*, some nobleman you are, you scum!"
I smiled apologetically.
Commissar Preiss caught his breath, then patted me amicably on the shoulder.





"Well, don't be afraid, we are well-acquainted with scum too. Never mind that you care as much about your own flesh-and-blood father as you do about the Emperor of Japan's gout—what you do care about is a thousand rubles! Right? You care about a thousand rubles like... well, you care very much. We will give you a thousand, and then maybe a second, if you do well. You will go visit your father."
Here he fell silent, evidently awaiting my response. Since it was not forthcoming, he returned behind his desk, to his steaming tea (it had cooled somewhat, so now he slurped it longer and louder), to his papers and seals. With a massive pen he affixed his signature to a document, stamped it with the *pechat*, added a second one; satisfied, he rubbed his little hands together and sprawled in the leather-upholstered armchair.
"Here you have your passport and a *napravleniye* to our branch in Irkutsk, they will take care of you on site. They bought you a ticket right away, you leave for Moscow tomorrow, otherwise you won't make it to the Siberian Express—today is the first of July, your ticket is for the fifth, departure at ten in the evening from the Yaroslavsky Railway Station, you'll be in Irkutsk on the eleventh, there they'll put you on the Winter line to Kezhma. Here is the thousand, sign for it. Buy yourself some clothes so you look like a human being! And if it should enter your head to take the money and gamble it all away—oh, go ahead and gamble it away, as long as you make it to Lake Baikal. Now, sign!"





A thousand rubles. What do they want me to do there? Wring names out of my father that he didn't give up at the trial? Only why is the Ministry of Winter turning to me with this, and not Internal Affairs?
"I'll go," I said, "I'll visit my father. And what then? Is that it?"
"You will talk to him."
"I will talk to him."
"If you talk to him, well, that will be good enough."
"I do not understand what Your Excellency—"
"He sent no letters, and naturally this did not surprise you." Commissar Preiss opened a folder bound in broadcloth and tortoiseshell. "Irkutsk writes us... He was a geologist, wasn't he?"
"Pardon?"
"Filip Filipovich studied geology. He didn't lose his interest in it in Siberia either. They report to me here... From the beginning he was very close, they took his company into the second or third expedition that went there in the spring of nineteen ten. Most of them died of frostbite. Or froze solid on the spot. He survived. Then he went back. To them. I don't believe it, but so they write me here. They gave the order, they gave the money, so I am sending a man. Your father speaks with the *lute*."


On That Which Cannot Be Uttered

My life is governed by the principle of shame.
One gets to know the world, one gets to know the language of describing the world, but one does not get to know oneself. Most people—almost everyone, I suppose—will not, until their dying day, learn a language in which they could describe themselves.





When I say of someone that he is a coward, it means I consider him to be acting cowardly—it means nothing more, because of course I cannot peer deep into his soul and find out if he is a coward. Yet I cannot use this language to describe myself: within the scope of my experience, I remain the only person for whom my words, deeds, and omissions constitute a merely pale and fundamentally accidental reflection of what lies beneath them, what constitutes their cause and source. The existence of this source I experience directly, whereas of some of my behaviors I am not conscious at all, and all of them I perceive in an incomplete, distorted way. We ourselves are always the last to find out what idiots we made of ourselves in company. We know the intentions of our actions better than those actions themselves. We know better what we wanted to say than what we actually said. We know who we want to be—we do not know who we are.
A language to describe our behaviors exists, because this reality is experienced by many people, and they can discuss amongst themselves someone's ostentatious politeness or someone's faux pas. A language to describe myself does not exist, because no one other than me experiences this reality.





It would be a language for single-person use, an unutterable, unrecordable language. Everyone must create it for themselves. Most people—almost everyone, I am convinced—never bring themselves to do so until their dying day. At best, they repeat in their minds other people's descriptions of their own person, expressed in that derivative language—in a language of the second order—or they imagine what they would say about themselves in it if they were given the chance to look at themselves from the outside.
To say anything at all about themselves, they must make themselves strangers to themselves.
My life is governed by the principle of shame. I have no better words to tell this truth.
A beggar holds out his hand in a pleading gesture, I have money, I can give it to him, even five, even two, even one kopeck, no one else is looking, it is just the two of us, me and the beggar—I give nothing, I turn away and walk off quickly, burying my head in my shoulders.
Who broke the jar of preserves? Mother raises her voice. Who broke it? Not me, and I have no idea whether Bolek is to blame or Emilka, but mother asks again, and again, and then I point decisively at Bolek. Him.
Laughter in company, we are all mocking a mutual acquaintance who, naturally, is not here with us now; one by one, we recall memories of his embarrassments and weaknesses. I stand up, I do not laugh, furrowed brows and pursed lips, I stand up and—have you no shame!





A *krasavitsa* clings to me, spills over my shoulders, slides down onto my chest, onto my lap, as the droshky slows and stops before the gate of the tenement where I rent a room with Kiwajs; Kiwajs is with distant relatives in the provinces, the room is vacant, the drunken girl giggles, biting the button of my frock coat—the droshky driver turns around, winks at me, *shall I help, sir?* I look on in terror. I shove a crumpled ruble into his fist. Take the *blyad* wherever she wants to go! I throw the *devushka* off me, leap out of the droshky, and flee deep into the dark gateway.
In the forest, past my grandfather's village, in a hollow by a stream lies the carcass of a roe deer. Half-eaten by predators and scavengers, beneath a black canopy of flies. I tell no one, I come every morning and every evening, I poke it with a stick, roll it over, I watch as the insects swarm it anew, as the meat changes color, and dark liquids flow from the body, slowly soaking into the earth. Where do I spend so much time, grandfather asks. I lie. He sees that I am lying. Where do I go, he asks. I stay silent. He thrashes me with a belt. I cry. I do not tell the truth, I do not tell anything. All summer long I walk through the forest, searching for dead animals. I carry a thick stick with me, like a club. I beat the carrion with this stick in a dull frenzy, until the rot comes off the bone. Take that! Take that! Take that!





Eighteen and seventy-four! Eighteen and seventy-four! The fairground barker tears his throat shouting, announcing the winning numbers. We all bought tickets as we entered the fair. Laughing, red from the frost and the plum brandy, we now search our pockets for the little cardboards. I find it, pull it out. Eighteen and seventy-four. Feigning great disappointment and fury, they fling their tickets into the mud, curse their bad luck, and mock the fool who doesn't come forward for the winnings. I tear up the winning ticket and do the same as they do.
At night, when no one can see, I practice terrible faces, bulging eyes and bared teeth, too wild to mean anything in the language of human physiognomy; terrible faces, and also absolute deadness of visage, the stillness of the tiniest cranial muscles, which I can never achieve in the light of day and among people; I have no power over the grimaces of my face then. At night, when I sleep, I see beneath my eyelids a diagram, like from Zyga's yellowed engravings, an anatomical diagram of this betrayal: dozens of strands of hard twine threaded beneath the skin of my cheeks, my brows, my chin, my lips; and the other ends of these threads are held by the people surrounding me, in their hands, between their teeth, tied around their watch fobs and cufflinks, on their wedding rings, on the heads of their walking sticks and the stems of their pipes, some even have them sewn into their own facial muscles, or into their hearts, straight through the breast and breastbone. Then the engraving comes to life—it is day—there is movement—people—I—
I smile—I smile—I smile.
…





I recall behaviors, I use the second language...
I must become a stranger to myself.
Benedykt Gierosławski was a good child. Who wouldn't want to have such a child? He was well-behaved—he always obeyed his parents, obeyed his elders, did not speak unless spoken to, made no mischief, did not fight with other children, said his prayers before bed, had the best grades in school. Other boys wandered the town, spent their time in the streets—he read books. Other boys peeked at girls and teased their sisters—he taught his little sister her letters. He washed his ears. He bowed to neighbors and acquaintances. He did not pick his nose, did not point his finger. He didn't get sick. He caused his parents no trouble. Who wouldn't want to have such a child?
When he turned seventeen and entered the Imperial University, he met others who had been good children.
They recognized one another by their smiles.
Was it already too late? Could I have turned back—turned back to what, to whom, to what kind of me? There is no single moment in the past that, miraculously altered, would have reversed the course of my life, no catastrophe that befell me to make me different from what I ought to be; I can point to nothing of the sort.





It is, in fact, exactly the opposite: however far back I reach into my memory, I will always find, beneath the thin epidermis of my words and deeds, the same principle, the muscle of anxiety, hope, and revulsion tensed in the same direction, whether I was three, thirteen, or twenty-three. Not a feeling, something else, not a thought—it escapes, it eludes, it slips away, immaterial, indescribable—stop! look me in the eyes!—you, you—I shall call you Shame, though you are not it, I shall call you Shame, because there are no better words in human language.


On the Laws of Logic and the Laws of Politics

Alfred Tajtelbaum was waiting for me in Herszfield's restaurant. He sat alone by a large window looking out onto the Simons Passage, reading a crookedly folded newspaper. He had ordered *peisakhovka* with goose cracklings and was probably already on his second serving—from Miodowa to Długa is but three steps, but after I telephoned him from the vestibule of the Palace, Ivan and Kirill detained me again: there were forms to fill out, a certificate of *blagonadyozhnost* to extract, minor travel documents to write up... When I entered Herszfield's, the clocks showed seven.
