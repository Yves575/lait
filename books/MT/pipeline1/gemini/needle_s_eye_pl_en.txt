Chapter 1

It was just as I say. He tripped over his walking stick. I don't know why he looked back. No one was coming down behind him. Of course I recognized him. Even though he had changed beyond recognition—it’s hard not to change after so many years. But I didn't let on that I recognized him. Maybe that's why he thought I didn't. I pretended he was just another one of those people constantly going down or up those stairs. I even wondered whether to warn him not to go down into that old wild, green valley, because it is no longer there. Old people, however, like to visit places that no longer exist and may never have existed at all. I don't understand what longing draws them so. Unfortunately, the longings of old age can be dangerous. If only that valley could be reached across flat ground, maybe I would have understood him. Except no valley can be reached on the flat; such is the nature of valleys. To go down those stairs at his age and with a walking stick, when your legs refuse to obey and your eyes fail to focus not just on the world, but on every step you take, is the same as imagining in your youth that you have wings instead of arms and are fluttering down into that valley.

I was expecting someone else, yet unexpectedly, here he was. A tremor washed over me; I shrank into myself, wishing in that moment that I were neither here nor anywhere else. Having felt out the first step with his stick, he tentatively placed one foot on it, and then dared to place the other. With similar effort, he made his way down to the next, and the next. Feeble but stubborn, I thought. Why are old people so stubborn? Can they not forgive those who will live on after them?

He paused for a shorter or longer moment on every step, looking down at his feet, as if unsure whether these were the stairs he had always gone down and up, or if his memory was failing him. At his age, memory sometimes excuses itself from remembering, since remembering burdens a person with obligations of which they are already tired enough, having lived so many years. He tapped his stick against each step below, making sure he could safely descend. First he placed his left foot on the step, then his right. And with that same caution, lowering his right foot to his left, he stepped down to the next. The walking stick he held in his right hand seemed unsteady to me; it trembled as he touched it to the step.

I was afraid he might get dizzy if he kept looking down at his feet on every step. I wondered whether to step out of his way, or rather not let him pass once he reached the Needle's Eye.

He wasn't surprised when he saw me; maybe he even expected me to beat him here before he made it from the park through town. He stopped on the step above me and, without looking me in the eye, said:

"I went to the park one more time, but it's the very last time. I don't know if you remember that she would sometimes walk home from school through the park. I sat on a bench and decided to wait. A Gypsy woman walked by with a baby at her breast. Do you think she stopped like she did back then? Not at all. She didn't even ask if I wanted my fortune told, even though the baby whimpered as she passed me. Evidently she decided my future wasn't ahead of me, but behind me, and she couldn't add any years to my life, for where would she get them. I had been sitting there a long time and was about to get up and walk through town, because maybe she'd be walking back through town today too, if she wanted to buy something, but a little old woman sat down next to me, asking if she could.

"‘Unless you're waiting for someone?’

"‘No, go ahead,’ I say. ‘I used to wait, but that was a long time ago.’

"‘Long ago or not, if you loved someone, you keep waiting.’

"‘And did you love someone?’

"‘What a question. Is there anyone who has never loved? You've been alive a good many years, I see, just like me, and you don't know that? I still love him, though probably in vain by now. It aches that I never told him.’

"‘And why didn't you tell him?’

"‘I was sick, and the doctors didn't give me long to live, so I didn't want to burden him with my love. Especially since he left after passing his finals. I didn't even know where, and now maybe he's not even alive. Would you have told someone if you weren't sure you were going to live?’

"‘I don't know, especially after so many years. Maybe I, too, wouldn't have wanted to burden her with the guilt of forsaking my love, if it were her time to die.’

"‘Does that mean you loved someone?’

"‘I still love her, though I'm also not sure if she's alive. But if she were, I probably wouldn't tell her even now if we met, because she wouldn't recognize me, nor I her. We are no longer the same people we were in our youth.’

"‘If you love, you are always the same. If a person knew that in their youth, they'd live differently. Otherwise, you waste so much. Most often love. Well, I'll be going now. I have to feed the cats.’

"‘Do you have many?’

"‘It varies. However many show up.’

"‘I'll walk you.’

"‘No, thank you. I don't have far to go. And where are you headed? Because maybe I should walk you? You have a walking stick, and I, thank God, am still on my own two feet.’

"‘I'll manage, I have much closer to go than you.’"

He checked the next step with his stick, but as if he didn't trust it, he dragged its tip left and right across the step before placing his foot. I wanted to ask him why, in that case, he was going down to that old wild, green valley, but he beat me to it:

"I pity you, that you have to go on living. But maybe you will have better luck. I wish it for you. Although, in my opinion, nothing has ever come of wishes for anyone." He stepped down to the next step, checking it the same way with his stick. "Truth be told, every life is a repetition of a life after someone else. The past precedes us, you must know this, we just drag along behind it. For who could ever keep up with their own life?" He paused on the next step. "Oh, how many legs must have passed this way. Have you noticed how worn these stairs are? It takes centuries. Who knows if they aren't still walking this way. Maybe I'll tag along with them. What, does that surprise you? It's always merrier in a crowd." And feeling the step below with his stick, he swayed, but it was as if someone held him up. "When I went to high school... But why should I tell you about it? You'll experience it yourself, because you have to experience it. Without that, you couldn't ask yourself if it was worth it. All the questions we ask ourselves in life boil down to this single one: whether it was worth it. Well, not at your age. Only after years, years later. Right now, you wouldn't know what to answer yourself. Just remember that youth can string you along for a long time. Don't let it deceive you. Suddenly it might turn out that you were young many years ago. Unfortunately. I apologize for these bitter words, but they are all I have left. In return, I give you my life. It will come in handy when emptiness overwhelms you. Emptiness is the worst thing that can happen to us. And the only way to remedy it is by living in someone else's life. Besides, no one starts their own life from themselves. You don't understand that yet. But in time, when you begin to visit it... I would like to know what your impressions will be. Mine were better and worse, as it is with life. Once, I ran away from my childhood, but you may never want to leave yours. It happens. I will tell you, however, that it won't do you any good. Life is wandering behind yourself without hope that you will ever find yourself. Well, enough, or they'll pass me by. And there are still so many steps here."

So I stepped out of his way. And then the stick slipped from his hand and rolled downward, and he swayed and plunged after it. He fell all the way to the road at the bottom of the stairs, striking his head on the cobblestones. Nobody happened to be going down or coming up, so nobody saw it. I rushed down after him. I tried to lift him in some mindless hope that maybe he was alive. He was heavy, though. Tall in life, and in death utterly limp, making him all the heavier. Suddenly I heard a voice above me:

"Leave him. The ambulance is on its way."

So I laid his bloodied head back on the cobblestones, and only then did I see a few people standing nearby. Where they had suddenly come from, I don't know. Maybe it's like this with every accident, that people seem to spring up from the ground or flutter down from the sky, but when he fell down those stairs, I could have sworn there wasn't a living soul around. So there is no point listening to people, because nobody saw him trip over his stick. They might say all sorts of things, but everyone will say something different anyway, as always happens when people don't want to be witnesses. And as it turned out later, nobody did. The one who told me to leave him alone was just recounting a similar incident, pointing to a nearby house:

"Oh, in that house over there. Two friends, unfamiliar with firearms, found a revolver from the war. And since it was rusted, and the trigger wouldn't even pull, one of them started banging the barrel against the table. Well, and it went off into the other guy's stomach. He collapsed, and the first guy started to lift him, probably even straightened him up. The doctor said later that if he hadn't lifted him, he would have lived."

At that moment, his words were interrupted by an ambulance arriving with its sirens on. Two paramedics jumped out with a stretcher, along with a doctor, who stretched open the man's eyelids, put a stethoscope to his chest just in case, and pronounced him dead. Right after the ambulance, the militia also arrived on the scene, sirens blaring. Two uniformed men got out of the car: one an officer, with stars on his epaulets, the other younger, with bars. After them, from the back seat, scrambled out a third man, so fat he looked almost comical in his uniform.

The officer began questioning the people standing around the body about who saw what and how it happened, and the younger one, with the bars, wrote it down, though he had nothing to write, because even though they all talked over one another, no one had seen anything. One of them had only seen me running down the stairs when the body was already lying on the cobblestones. And the one who forbade me to lift him had arrived exactly as I was trying to lift him, and said:

"Leave him." And he was even starting to say that, oh, in that house over there..., but the officer interrupted him:

"Did anyone know him?"

Someone began musing that maybe if he had seen him alive, to which the officer waved his hand dismissively.

"Search his pockets," he tossed out.

The fat one tried to bend over the body but couldn't manage it, so he knelt down instead and pulled a wallet from the inside pocket of the suit jacket, handed it to the younger one, who, without looking inside, handed it to the officer. The wallet had many compartments, but apart from money and a photograph of some young woman, there was nothing in it, not even a receipt, for example from the dry cleaner's.

"No ID card?" the officer marveled. "Search again, thoroughly, maybe it's somewhere. No?" he was even more amazed.

"A citizen without an ID card is as if he didn't exist," said the fat one.

"The body is here, so the ID should be too," said the younger one with the bars. "Without an ID, it's as if this body didn't exist either. And how do you conduct an investigation?"

"Maybe it fell out and is under the body. Lift the body," ordered the officer.

The paramedics lifted the body, but underneath was nothing but a spreading pool of blood.

"Put him back. Just as he was lying."

"What about the girl in the picture?" The lower-ranking officer peered into the open wallet in his superior's hands. "Pretty girl."

The officer nodded.

"Maybe his daughter, someone would be more likely to recognize her. Show them?" He took the photograph from the officer's hands and shoved it under everyone's nose.

They shrugged, indicating they didn't recognize her. One person merely said:

"Must be old. Scalloped edges. They don't make them like that nowadays. I have similar ones from my grandparents."

"Well, and you," he turned to me, "do you recognize her?"

Yes, it was her, I recognized her. But I didn't admit it. And so that not a shadow of suspicion would fall on me, I drew the officer's attention to the walking stick:

"Maybe someone would recognize him by his stick. Walking sticks become attached to people. And almost everyone carries a different one. One of the professors at the high school..."

The officer didn't let me finish; he looked at me suspiciously, practically boring his eyes into me.

"We already know how we're supposed to identify someone. Please leave that to us." And he ordered: "Look for that stick just in case, and put the money in evidence. And chalk the body. Who knows if he isn't some tourist; he could have left his ID with the guide, in case he got lost."

They outlined the body in chalk, the ambulance drove off with the body, and the younger officer and the fat one started looking for the stick. That white chalk outline of his body on the cobblestones with the pool of blood where he had struck his head seemed to me as if it were still breathing his breath, as if it were even whispering his words, and this was more poignant than when the body had lain in that spot before the paramedics took it away.

"We'll have to drive around town and wherever a coach is parked, ask around," the officer issued an order when, despite searching both the stairs and the surroundings, they didn't find the stick. "It's Saturday, so plenty of them have descended on us today. They park at the foot of the castle, they park by the granary, they park in the new town, and wherever else they park, ask around. Fortunately, there's one hotel, ask there too. And ask at the private lodgings, get the list from the magistrate, they have to register them, after all." He took off his cap and scratched his head, as if his thoughts were making it itch. "It would be useful to put up some notices that a man, aged... except we don't know how old he was, we didn't find an ID. Damn it, we didn't even bring a camera, we could have taken his picture."

"And how were we supposed to know it was a fatality?" the fat one cut in. "They reported an accident, and an accident could mean a broken arm or leg. Haven't enough arms and legs been broken on these blasted stairs?"

"We could hang up a picture," the one with the bars interjected. "Just not here, better in town, otherwise they'll be afraid to walk this way later, thinking it's haunted. Though a picture of a corpse... who's going to admit they knew him? It would be better to use the daughter's. Someone would be more willing to recognize the daughter."

"And how do you know it's a daughter, not a granddaughter?" the officer reprimanded him. And the other man blinked, surprised he hadn't thought of that himself.

"From the looks of it, you're our only witness," the officer turned to me. And he seemed to hesitate, adding: "And perhaps the only starting point from which the investigation should be launched, since there's no ID of the deceased, no photograph, and no other witnesses. You'll come with us, we'll take down a formal statement."

At the station, he seated me at a desk across from him, asked if I'd like some tea, and picked up the telephone receiver:

"Two teas," he ordered. "Do you take sugar? Then I'll have mine sweetened today too. And sugar. Right, and tell Bronka to come in and type."

A woman thin as a rail, who turned out to be the cleaning lady, brought two glasses of tea, sugar in some tin hard-candy box, and one teaspoon. He sweetened his own, stirred it, then handed the teaspoon to me.

"I suppose I'll have to bring a teaspoon from home. These aluminum ones keep breaking, and there aren't many left for the whole station," he said, as if apologizing for the single spoon.

Bronka arrived as well, cast a contemptuous look at me, sat down at the typewriter, and spread out some colorful magazine in front of her.

"Before you finish your tea, I'll browse the autumn fashions."

"Type. What does tea have to do with the interrogation?"

"I wouldn't know."

He waited until I had taken at least the first sip; he had already taken maybe his third, and then he pushed his chair back from the desk, leaning his back against the rest, intertwined his fingers, clearly relaxing, and probably stretched his legs out under the desk too, because he touched me.

"Alright then, speak."

"What am I supposed to say?"

"What you know."

"I don't know anything more than what I've already said."

"You know, you know, we already know that you know. Every suspect being interrogated says that: gives their first name, last name, date of birth, and claims they know nothing more. Experience teaches us, however, that a person doesn't know what they know themselves unless they're helped. There are various forms of such help. But I use a simple conversation, an illuminating one, so to speak. I work on the assumption that everyone has to convince themselves of what they know. So?"

Evidently I hadn't understood anything yet, because I decided to remind him of what he had told me when taking me to the station:

"Excuse me, but there's been some misunderstanding. I was only supposed to be a witness."

"That's true, I don't deny it, you are still a witness. Only you are the sole witness. We have no other witnesses with whom we could confront you to confirm your credibility. And when nothing is certain, the question arises whether 'witness' is your only role in this incident, or whether another could be assigned to you. I don't know what kind yet, but we must try to clarify that. Both of us. So I would like you to understand that I must interrogate you in various roles. Besides, the boundaries between roles are fluid. Circumstances determine them. To find a comparison, a liquid state can turn into a gaseous state or a solid state, depending on the temperature. Water, steam, ice. The same thing, but not the same. We cannot afford to be helpless. That would disqualify us. We would be unnecessary. So we must inquire, sometimes to the point of pain. Truth, as you know, carries the highest price and isn't subject to a discount, even after years. So I wouldn't advise banking on that. The truth is not subject to a statute of limitations. Regardless of how long a case might drag on. If only we had at least a second witness. But in your case, that could only be the deceased. Unfortunately, we cannot interrogate him. Though it's interesting to wonder what he would have had to say. We can only guess. And it's not entirely unfounded. Guesswork is usually the starting point for finding a lead, since very few people get caught red-handed. Take, for example, all those cheating husbands and wives. Seldom does one catch the other, as they say, in flagrante. Most infidelities begin with suspicion. And only suspicion prompts the search for evidence. It's entirely possible, then, that the deceased would testify against you, just as you testify that it's his own fault because he tripped over his walking stick. Only where is that stick? We searched, you saw. If it was there, we would have found it. A walking stick doesn't have wings to fly away." He laughed, but with the sort of laugh that chilled me. "Fine, we'll look again. In the meantime, tell me, which hand was he holding it in, left or right, when, as you say, he tripped over it? You don't remember. Pity. You're surprised such a detail could matter? Then let me tell you that a detail can tell us more than we might suspect. Any minor detail can overturn all testimony, contradict all witnesses. The detail is the cosmos. The world is supposedly vast, but it's the details that decide things: that someone met someone here and not there; that an apple fell from a tree, thanks to which Newton discovered the law of gravity; that the bacteria cultivated by Fleming were unexpectedly killed by some mold, and in this way penicillin was discovered. And so on, and so forth. Did you type that down, Bronka? Then throw that page away."

He dragged out this interrogation and I must say he sometimes surprised me with his perceptiveness:

"You said no one was coming down behind him, right? And yet he looked back. Maybe the sun blinded him, because it's glaring today, and seeing nothing, he turned his head, and then you, let's say, wanted to help him down to the lower step?"

Sometimes he'd break off the interrogation mid-sentence and step out, as if giving me time to think. Or he would fall deep in thought, and then tell Bronka to cross something out, even though it seemed important to me. Evidently his thoughts were following a different lead. And sometimes it was as if he wanted to soften the interrogation and give it a tone closer to confidences, as he'd interject something about himself, that he had two children, a son and a daughter, from his first wife who had died, and he had to raise them alone.

"And you know how it is. Duty comes first."

He had married a second time and now they were expecting a child again. Sometimes he'd ask me questions that seemingly had nothing to do with the case:

"Mother, father alive? Even though you're young, things happen. I barely remember my own mother, she died of tuberculosis. And my father passed away two years ago."

And suddenly he surprised me again:

"When did you pass your high school finals? And why didn't you go to university?"

"They didn't accept me."

"What, you didn't pass the exam?"

"I passed."

"How's that? You passed and they didn't accept you? We should look into that, too. Interesting. And what do you do now?"

"I work."

"Where?"

"At the fruit and vegetable processing plant. Jams, marmalades, juices."

"We know that plant. We conducted an investigation there once; five railcars of cherries sat unloaded in barrels on the siding until they rotted. And where do you live?"

"With my parents."

"And why did you come here? Were you meeting him?"

"Meeting who?"

"The deceased, of course."

"I didn't know him."

"You didn't know him, but you recognized him. That's what you said, that you recognized him. Or maybe it was just him who thought you didn't recognize him. Well, we can't ask him. But I wonder, since it was a chance encounter, why did you happen to meet at the Needle's Eye, and not on the street, in the park, in a café, a restaurant, or wherever else chance encounters usually happen? Think about it. I'll be right back." And he left.

Oh, he won't be easy, I thought. But I couldn't exactly tell him that I had been waiting for her, and he had shown up unexpectedly. I had even thought he was dead by now, since a year had passed since the time the Gypsy woman had foretold he would die. He'd immediately ask me: and who is she, first name, last name, where does she live? And what, did she come? What do you mean, you knew she wouldn't come, yet you waited for her? You wait for someone who might be late or even not show up that day because something unforeseen held them up, but they'll come another time.

He returned shortly and from the doorway threw an impatient question at me:

"And do you have anything to tell me? But let's assume you didn't know him. We'll pursue another lead. You saw him going down, yes? Where was he going down?"

"Downwards."

"Obviously, one goes downwards, not upwards." He sat behind the desk, staring into my eyes with a strange intensity. "Downwards, you say? And how would he get back up, if he could barely get down, didn't you consider that? At his age and with a walking stick."

"I don't know. Maybe he had no intention of coming back up."

"He had no intention, you say. And how do you know he had no intention?"

"I'm guessing."

"Then I must declare to you that your suppositions can also be evidence. And we can use them against you, regardless of the fact that you're supposing in favor of your own innocence. Everything has two sides, every thought and every word. Nothing is unambiguous, as it surely seems to you. Unambiguity is an illusion. The fact that you merely suppose betrays you, shows that something is hiding behind it. And that is the most important thing: what hides behind something. True, you safeguarded yourself with that 'maybe.' But sometimes people toss out a 'maybe,' though they don't do it without reason, either. Words govern us, you should know that. You don't need a high school diploma for that. Often you wouldn't want to say a certain word, and it says itself. So, what's it going to be, because something isn't adding up here." He fell silent, as if awaiting my reply.

It unsettled me that he remained silent for so long, staring at me and keeping silent. In his eyes, fixed upon me, I felt I could see a question he wanted to ask, but wasn't sure if he wouldn't entangle himself in something by asking it. I feared this question and waited in suspense for him to ask it. He didn't. He only cleared his throat:

"Hmm..." And that was the only sound that escaped his lips for quite some time. After a moment, he cleared his throat again: "Hmm..."

That "hmm" made me curl up inside. In such an "hmm", there's no telling what might be hiding—even an accusation for which there is admittedly no evidence, but in which rings the conviction that evidence will be found; if not sooner, then later. Later it might even be easier to find. Investigative techniques will improve, research methods will be perfected, new equipment will emerge that will read things on its own, without asking questions. And without Bronka, because there will be no need to type anything, the suspect will just be displayed on a screen. If he has a soul, even his soul will be plain to see. Patience: what recedes will also approach. And in the meantime, it doesn't hurt to dream. Over that time, the suspect will age too, experience many things, understand many things; maybe one day he will even come and confess of his own free will, he won't need to be detained, there'll be no need to go anywhere or trail anyone. They'll just sit behind desks and receive the guilty the way one receives applications for benefits or housing. If only they could get at people's consciences, because so far, consciences rarely open up on their own, and if they do, it's only in old age. Maybe instead of the militia, there will only be agencies, bureaus, and departments, and salaries will increase. The militia is having a tough time of it, especially with the young. The young are the hardest material to interrogate—they spin tales, muddy the waters, deny everything, codes and paragraphs mean nothing to them; they think their youth permits them anything. They have no respect for the militia. Well, they'll see, once we become the guiding light of the world.

At that moment, an officer with one more star came in and placed a note on his desk.

"Got him in your grip yet?" he asked, glancing at me.

"Almost."

"What, is he that tough? Maybe toss him to Edek."

"No, I can handle it."

"Suit yourself."

After he left, the interrogating officer glanced at the note the other man had brought, set it aside with suppressed fury, and turned that fury on Bronka:

"Put that magazine down, Bronka! Type!"

"I am typing the whole time," Bronka bristled. "Only I have nothing to type."

He bored his furious eyes into me, as if expecting that at least my hands would start trembling.

"Let's return to that walking stick."

"‘Let's return to that walking stick,’ I've got that down," repeated Bronka.

"What did the stick look like?"

"‘What did the stick look like?’" Bronka repeated again.

"Stop reading back what you're typing, goddammit!" Oh, he was visibly furious. "Well, so what did the stick look like?"

"Ordinary, like a walking stick."

"Exactly. What color was it?"

"I think it was black."

"You think, or was it black?"

"It could have been black."

"‘It could have been’ is not an answer. It seems you people don't understand that you are being interrogated in connection with what might even be a murder. You could just as easily have tripped him up, as he tripped over his stick. Because so far, we haven't found a stick."

"Black," I said, mildly petrified. I felt as if he were slowly starting to get at my thoughts.

"Right, so we have the color. And then?"

"The rest was black too."

"You're mocking me, are you? And here I am wanting to give you a chance."

No, I wasn't mocking him, I wouldn't dare. I might have said something stupid, but that was from the sudden confusion in my head that he had started addressing me in the plural, and what that entailed.

"And the handle?"

"What about the handle?"

"Well, what shape was it? They come in all sorts. We had one guy here, a young guy like you lot, walked with a stick, had a handle shaped like a snake's head."

"No, semi-circular, smooth."

"And what else did it have?"

"It also had a non-slip rubber tip at the end."

"Which means he couldn't have slipped, because it gripped the ground. And he fell?"

"Maybe because it gripped the ground, and he wanted to step down lower. And he tripped."

He looked at me, as if thinking: you're clever, brother, but you won't pull the wool over my eyes.

"Alright then. Let's leave the stick be until we find it."

He started drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desk, as if wondering whether he shouldn't toss me to that Edek after all, as suggested by the officer one star higher who had brought him the note. I didn't know who Edek was, a name like any other, but the mere idea of tossing me to some Edek seemed threatening in itself. Until now he had been mild, sometimes friendly; I even had the impression it pained him to have to interrogate me. And fear washed over me. He supposedly hadn't agreed to this Edek, but maybe there was an Edek sitting inside him too, since he had said he could handle it.

"You do know that confessing to the crime is a mitigating circumstance," he said, with a hint of weariness in his voice. And he might have even yawned.

"But what am I supposed to confess to?"

"That's not my business, I'm not the one who has to confess. And truth be told, everyone has something they could confess to. Even if we just pulled random people off the street for interrogations. And maybe one day it will be like that. For now, our scope of operations is limited."

He stopped drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desktop, switched to his left hand, and, as if with a surge of energy, drummed louder and faster, and my fear rose right up to my throat.

"Oh, I forgot to mention, on that stick, just below the handle, there was a gold plaque. And engraved on it was..."

"Gold, you say," he interrupted me. He picked up the telephone receiver. "Get the fat guy in here. Well, you see. You have to keep digging until it hurts." He stopped drumming his fingers, and even seemed to smile at me. "That would solve our case."

The fat officer appeared in the doorway.

"What is it, chief?"

"Go find that stick. There's a gold plaque on it."

"If it's gold, there's no point looking, we won't find it." And he began to air his doubts: "Gold is pricey right now. Trade is forbidden, so it's booming. How many gold rubles, gold dollars have we confiscated, and they're still selling. How many people have we locked up, and for what? They even steal from the churches and melt it down into rubles and dollars."

"Don't give me that bullshit. Go. That's an order."

"Yes, sir." The fat officer clicked his heels, swaying slightly. Evidently his body was too heavy for his thin legs.

"I don't smoke, otherwise I'd gladly light up," he said, suddenly seeming a bit deflated. He took out a small comb and ran it through his hair. He had thick, black hair, though already graying at the temples. "I used to smoke. But my first wife: quit, the kids are little; my second: quit, I'm pregnant, do you want the baby to be born a cripple? Well, we'll wait until they bring in that stick. You can't begrudge time when it comes to getting at the truth."

And time dragged on endlessly, not only for me but for him too, because he stepped out a couple of times, leaving me alone with Bronka, who took the opportunity each time he left to browse her women's magazine, without uttering so much as half a word. I asked her if she had any idea how long this might take.

"Who knows, who knows," she said, not taking her eyes off the magazine. "If they don't finish by nightfall, they'll start again in the morning. I've only typed one page. With Edek I'd have about five by now. Oh, Edek doesn't mess around. He'll smack you, he'll choke you if someone isn't telling the truth. And no one tells the truth. You're spinning tales yourselves, listening to you. And everything you say is somehow off. Edek would squeeze it out of you. Edek can even bend horseshoes. He lifts me up to the ceiling like a feather. You don't get bored with Edek when he's interrogating. Compared to Edek, almost everyone else here is a midget. Everyone spills their guts to Edek. You get to know life. You won't read in a book what you'll find out when you're with Edek. Nobody says as much in confession as they do to Edek. If Edek put you through the wringer, you'd learn a thing or two about yourselves too. Edek wouldn't let you sit the whole time, like this one does. Though he's a good man and not stupid, he often doesn't know what he wants himself. He asks and asks, and there's nothing to type. And he offered you tea. Edek wouldn't give you a drop of water. And he was calling you 'sir', until you annoyed him. With Edek, the interrogation would be over by now, I could go home. But with this one, you never know. He told them to go look for the stick, but what's a stick going to tell him. It's starting to get dark. If you'd just confess, maybe he'd get promoted, because he's been waiting a long time to get another star added. He'd get a higher salary. He's got two kids from that other wife to raise, and a third on the way with this one. And it would be easier on you, too, if you got it off your chest."

Only what was he supposed to confess to, to make things easier on himself? Things were never easier for him again, ever since he had let the Gypsy woman tell his fortune, and she predicted a long life, but that didn't matter to him, whereas when he heard that she wouldn't be returning home from school through the park today, that she would go through town because she had to buy something—hooks and eyes or snaps—he nearly leaped off the bench.

The park was usually empty at this time, and besides it was small, as such downtown parks tend to be; moreover, it had been heavily thinned out by an almost apocalyptic storm that had struck the town a few years prior, an event people still recalled with horror. So it wasn't as popular as it used to be. You couldn't hide from people's eyes in it, so hardly anyone arranged to meet there anymore. The young preferred going to the thicket by the river, and the old were deterred from strolling and sitting on the benches by their sorrow for the park as it used to be. Once, the trees at this time of year had resounded with birdsong; now, the silence was rarely disturbed, so he was certain he would hear her footsteps as she approached.

He closed his eyes against the glaring sun, because in this silence, no one could slip past him. He could hear all footsteps from afar and didn't have to open his eyes every time to see whether it was her or not. Besides, he knew her footsteps by heart; they seemed to touch the ground ever so lightly, you might think she was walking in sandals, although she always wore raised heels, unable to stand flat shoes. She stepped from the toe rather than the heel; he would have recognized those footsteps even in starless darkness.

So he surrendered to his imagination beneath those closed eyelids, especially since he wasn't expecting her until the last class ended. He had a one-class head start on her, having skipped Latin to wait for her in the park when she headed back, and she usually headed back through the park. It wasn't the first time he'd skipped class, and not only Latin, which he disliked, but also physics and math, subjects in which he was among the best students. When he left, as she did, after the final bell, she would sometimes get lost among the several classes pouring out all at once. There were several parallel classes, each quite large, so that when lessons ended, a crowd spilled outside. And by the time he found her, she was almost always walking with someone already. Oh, there were many, including boys from the older grades, lying in wait just to walk that short stretch with her or to walk her all the way home.

What he might have been dreaming about is probably not worth mentioning, since dreams at that age are quite commonplace and universally known, so anyone can recall their own. Suddenly he heard approaching footsteps, or rather a rustle, like that of bare feet. No, they weren't her footsteps, he would have known them at once. These were unhurried, stealthy rather, so he wouldn't have abandoned his dreams if a child hadn't suddenly whimpered almost within arm's reach. He forced his eyelids open violently, until the glare of the sun blinded him, and in that brightness he saw before him a Gypsy woman with a baby at her breast.

She was right in front of the bench he was sitting on, and evidently would have kept going, as her footsteps betrayed no intention of stopping by him. Experience had evidently told her there was no point telling the fortune of someone like that; she wouldn't squeeze so much as a zloty out of him. If he had any money, he was saving it for the movies or the circus. Only what girl would let him invite her to the movies or the circus? The prettier ones look around for boys who have a future. And what future could he have? Even if he lived a long time, does a long life guarantee a future? Pale, emaciated, as if caved in on himself, maybe even sick, only he didn't know it yet. She could only predict that he would live a long life, because there's no way to verify that other than by actually living a long time.

So maybe it wasn't a coincidence that when she drew level with him and was about to take the next step, her infant whimpered because the nipple slipped from its mouth; she pressed the nipple back in and stifled its cry. She had to stop for a moment, however, and then her eyes and his met. Briefly, because his gaze chilled her, and she lowered her eyes to the head of the suckling baby, even stroking it tenderly. She had practice in taming human distrust or even contempt toward herself, but his eyes seemed too old for his young years. A sad gleam emanated from them. She had never yet encountered so much suffering in young eyes; one often doesn't see that much even in old ones. The best proof was that he only opened them when he heard her infant cry. Could he have inherited this suffering from someone? Everyone is, after all, an endless inheritance. Still, since she had stopped by him, she couldn't just walk away without a word. Her baby's cry was already that first word to him. So, as if prolonging that cry, she said:

"Let me tell your fortune, and I'll tell you if she'll be walking back this way."

And without waiting for his consent, she sat on the bench. With a practiced motion, she drew her right breast from under her blouse and shifted the baby to it. Torn from its nursing, it whimpered again, but didn't have time to break into a cry, for it already had the other nipple in its mouth.

"When something aches, it's best to have your fortune told. There hasn't yet been a wise man who didn't believe things would get better. Fortunes can console, and they don't take hope away from anyone. They can also change many things. And you have your whole life ahead of you. If you'd give me something for a rattle, I'd foretell your whole life."

And without waiting for his consent or refusal, she freed her left arm from under the shawl which, tied at the neck and waist, supported the infant carried at her breast.

"Give me your hand," she said. "Not that one, the other one."

And she pulled that hand onto her lap, grasped it in both her hands, turned it palm up, and began tracing over it with her index finger.

"You have a long life line. Oh, long. From here to here." But he didn't even look to see how long. "You'll live and live." She named the number of years, which coincidentally fell just last year. "Well, just look, from here to here. If you had a bigger hand, you'd live even longer. But it's long anyway. You'll get your fill of living." Evidently she noticed the distrust in his eyes, because she curled his hand slightly and whispered almost with fear: "There is a second line here, too, of the heart. The Gypsy woman makes no mistakes, the Gypsy woman always tells the truth."

But that second line didn't interest him either. A longer or shorter life meant nothing to him. He was immersed in this single moment for which he was waiting, sitting in the park by the main alley. For this moment he would have given up the longest life. There are such moments that, like the sun in a magnifying glass, focus an entire life into a single point, burning a mark into us forever. Who knows if we don't live solely for such moments, and not for however many years.

He barely listened to what she was foretelling, for in his mind he was just walking out of school with her after the last lesson, out the doors of the building, then through the gate from the school courtyard; he was crossing to the other side of the street, walking a bit down the sidewalk, then turning toward the church; he was already at the church, in a moment he would enter the park, he was already in the park, so that her footsteps heading toward him should reach him any second. He already imagined he heard them, still very quiet, she must be somewhere at the start of the side alley, but they were her footsteps, how could he not recognize them, when suddenly, like a blow to the head with a blunt axe, the Gypsy woman's words struck him:

"She won't be coming back this way today. She was going to, but she remembered she has to buy something, hooks and eyes, snaps, and she'll go through town."

She hadn't even finished when he snatched his hand from hers, sprang up, and bolted toward the town.

"And won't you give me something for a rattle?!" she called after him, so loudly that the nipple popped out of the baby's mouth, and it burst into loud crying.

He turned back, dug a coin out of his pocket, and tossed it into her lap.

"You can't buy a rattle with this!" she shouted in disappointment. "May he be cursed!"

If the Gypsy woman had told the truth, that she would be walking back through town, then the way to her house led down the stairs through the Needle's Eye into that old wild, green valley separating the hill on which the town lay from the opposite, much lower one, where her house stood.
