Hearing Transcript

LAMAR: Will you be needing a Bible?

HUNT: No, thank you.

LAMAR: Please raise your right hand. Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?

HUNT: I do.

LAMAR: I understand you possess information relevant to this committee?

HUNT: That’s correct.

LAMAR: Please give your testimony.

HUNT: Where should I begin? From the day of the accident?

LAMAR: No. From the beginning.

June 2021

Nate Hunt was waiting for his wife. Lucy was late.

"Dad, I'm bored," whined their daughter, Eliza. Nate was holding her on his lap. The child's hair, full of static from rubbing against his sweater, tickled his nose.

"Yes, I know," he sighed. "You already told me."

Nate was bored, too. All these ceremonies, tours, and press conferences seemed to have no end. At the same time, he was also terrified. It might seem like these two emotional states were mutually exclusive, but that wasn't the case at all. He had gone through this the last time, eight years ago. Only back then, he had been here alone. He hadn't had to rehearse in his head what he would tell his child if something went wrong. He still couldn't find the right words. It was entirely possible they simply didn't exist.

"When is Mommy coming?" the little girl asked.

"Soon," he replied.

"But how soon exactly?"

"I don't know, sweetie."

"Ty khochesh konfetu, rebyonka?" The woman in the next chair rustled a bag of hard candies and smiled encouragingly. But Eliza wasn't in the mood to talk to strangers. She turned away from the woman and buried her face in her father's chest.

"Prostite, ona sonnaya." Nate offered an apologetic smile. He had learned Russian somewhat by accident. He had quizzed Lucy so many times before her exams that he had picked up a thing or two. His favorite word, *pochemuchka*—meaning someone who asks too many questions—fit Eliza to a T.

The woman nodded in understanding; she had probably gotten up before dawn today, too. *I wonder how many times she's done this,* Nate thought. *Her third time, her fourth?* She didn't look nervous; quite the opposite, a broad smile never left her face. Maybe because, aside from candies, she also had a pack of tranquilizers in her bag.

"Let's at least play a game," Eliza pleaded.

"Sure," Nate agreed. "How about 'I Spy'?"

"Okay. You go first."

Nate looked around the room. It resembled a classroom or a dentist's waiting room: harsh fluorescent light, beige walls decorated with faded posters, rows of plastic chairs, spindly plants on the windowsill. Except that a thick pane of Plexiglas ran right through the middle of the room. On the other side stood a long table holding three microphones, each with a nameplate: Cyrillic on top, Latin alphabet underneath. Anton Kovalyov. Lev Zaytsev. Lucy Poplaski.

"I spy something beginning with 'm'," Nate whispered.

"Map?" Eliza asked.

"No."

"Marker?"

He shook his head.

"Microphone?"

"Yes. Your turn."

The little girl started to fidget, paying absolutely no mind to the fact that she was jabbing her father in the stomach with alternating elbows and knees. The people gathered in the room—members of other families, officials, journalists—waved and smiled at her. But Eliza ignored them completely, entirely focused on the game. She took any form of competition extremely seriously. When she took second place in a sack race at some school event, she cried for the rest of the day. "Eliza is a very ambitious little girl," the teacher had told them in parting; her tone indicating that perhaps she was *too* ambitious. "She gets that from her mother," Nate had replied without a moment's hesitation.

"I spy something beginning with 'r'," she said.

"Radiator?"

"No."

"Hmm..." Nate rubbed his chin. "Remote?"

"No."

"Railing?"

"Nope." The little girl shook her head. With obvious satisfaction.

"I give up, then."

"Rocket!" Eliza triumphed.

Nate looked out the window. It rose toward the sky amidst the boundless expanse of sun-scorched grass—surrounded by a halo of milky-white vapor, peeking through the latticework of the scaffolding. It didn't belong in this landscape: among the faded Kazakh steppes, the crumbling, peeling buildings, the howling of stray dogs. It looked like the remnant of some extinct alien civilization. In a way, that's exactly what it was: the Baikonur Cosmodrome had, after all, been built by the Soviet Union. Two factors had dictated its location: first, the latitude made it easier to climb into the desired orbit. Second, if something went wrong—for example, if the rocket exploded right after launch—the risk of burning chunks of metal falling on someone's head was relatively low. There was nothing but grass as far as the eye could see.

Nate had seen an explosion like that. He was six years old at the time, visiting family in Florida, and it just so happened that the frequently postponed flight of the space shuttle *Challenger* was approaching. His parents took the boy to the Kennedy Space Center to see the launch with his own eyes, especially since the crew included Christa McAuliffe, an ordinary teacher who was supposed to conduct a lesson from space. He remembered it with a child's memory, as if he had brought a camera with him and taken random snapshots: poorly framed, out of focus, overexposed with emotion. He had been given a paper NASA baseball cap that kept falling over his eyes, which made him very sad. They took their seats in the bleachers right behind a man in a cowboy hat. The man blocked his entire view, so little Nate was already angry at the whole world. His father put him on his shoulders so he could see better, ignoring the protests of the people in the row behind them. Then came the blinding flash and the roar, the hot air in his hair, the vibrations spreading through his whole body until his teeth rattled and his skin tingled. The rocket climbed upward, drowning out the cheers, spewing fire, faster and faster, until it turned into a tiny dot in the sky. And then that dot flashed and turned into several smaller dots. Some of them began to fall, while others drew fanciful squiggles in the air. For a fraction of a second, Nate thought this was exactly how it was supposed to be, that these were cosmic fireworks, like on the Fourth of July, and he was thrilled. The joy didn't last long, and what followed—the sobs, the screams, the chaos—haunted his dreams for years afterward. Despite this, he too had tied his life to space. The vastness fascinated, terrified, and drew him in.

A murmur rippled through the room, accompanied by the rapid-fire clicking of camera shutters. Finally, Nate breathed a sigh of relief. On the other side of the glass, a door opened, and the Soyuz crew—Lev, Anton, and Lucy—entered the room. The lenses were all pointed toward his wife. First of all, because she was undoubtedly more photogenic than the Russians. Dark curly hair tied in a ponytail, a broad smile, a turned-up freckled nose—she looked like the friendly girl next door, someone you could become friends with in five minutes. Secondly, she was one of the last American women who would fly into space from Kazakhstan. After a hiatus of over a decade, the US finally had its own means of transport to orbit again: the Dragon capsules, built in the best American tradition by an eccentric billionaire. The first flights had been successful, and NASA was already negotiating a new, larger contract that would make the Americans completely independent of Russian assistance. The clock was ticking.

"Uvazhayemyye kollegi, dorogiye gosti..." began the director of Roscosmos, Yuri Rybkin, a gray-haired, solidly built man in a cream-colored jacket.

"What is that man saying?" asked Eliza.

"Nothing interesting."

"But what?"

"That a beautiful chapter in the history of friendship between our nations is coming to an end, but a new one is beginning."

"What does that mean?"

*That soon the Russians won't be able to pocket ninety million dollars per seat on the Soyuz,* Nate thought. *That without these regular injections of cash, Roscosmos will teeter on the brink of bankruptcy. That behind the declarations of readiness for continued cooperation lie envy, resentment, and bitterness.*

"That we'll be flying into space together, but separately," he said instead.

The Russian official finished his speech, and the microphone was taken over by the NASA representative, Deputy Director for Human Spaceflight Steve Ayers—a stocky, balding man in thick-rimmed glasses. More lofty platitudes, wishes for a successful mission, agency newspeak. Finally, the time came for goodbyes with the family: forced intimacy in front of the cameras, among strangers, through Plexiglas. Nate sat down with his daughter across from Lucy. He wanted to tell her a multitude of things. That he was damn proud. That he would miss her. That he was happy for her. That he was scared for her. But he couldn't bring himself to do it, not in this crowd.

"Hey." She was the first to speak. "How's your morning going?"

Banalities, then; empty words. But empty words can be filled with something, after all; they can resonate with something entirely unrelated to their actual meaning. The timbre of a voice, a pause, a look—this was how they told each other everything that couldn't be said, everything they wouldn't even be able to name, defying semantics.

"It's fine," Nate replied. "Right, Eliza? It's fine?"

"When are you coming back?" the little girl asked. The question was simultaneously an accusation.

"You know. In six months."

"You won't be here for my birthday."

"I will be, honey," Lucy replied with a sad smile. "More than once, even."

"What?"

"Remember? The station orbits the Earth once every ninety minutes. If your party lasts for three hours, I'll fly over our house twice during that time. And I'll be thinking about you then. Okay?"

"It's not the same."

Lucy pressed her hand against the glass. A substitute for real contact, a quarantine requirement.

"I know, sweetie."

"Then why are you going?"

Lucy didn't get a chance to answer. The time for goodbyes had come to an end. The next time they would see each other would be in six months. Assuming everything went well.

The Orthodox priest’s gold-threaded robes sparkled in the flashes of the cameras. The aspergillum traced a wide arc in the air, and holy water streamed down Lucy’s cheeks like tears. Another tradition, another superstition. For the Russians, every stage of flight preparation—and there were many—was accompanied by some ritual, and after every mission, another one was added; there was more folklore here than at a country wedding. Every flight participant had to lay flowers at the Yuri Gagarin monument. Hoist their country's flag up a mast. Plant a tree in the Cosmonauts' Alley. Sign the door of their hotel room in Baikonur. On the eve of the flight, watch the movie *White Sun of the Desert*, a 1970s Soviet box-office hit. On top of that, of course, came the mandatory blessing by a clergyman, regardless of personal preference or religion, and not just once, but twice. It was as if the Russians believed the success of a flight didn't depend at all on the laws of nature or mathematical equations, but on the will of the capricious heavens, which had to be appeased by ceremonies lasting for weeks.

Lucy boarded the bus that was to take the crew to the launchpad. She was already wearing her Sokol spacesuit: custom-tailored, equipped with dozens of laces, buttons, and snaps, a veritable cosmic corset. Putting it on took nearly two hours and required the assistance of several technicians, and since it was fully airtight, each crew member was additionally equipped with a portable ventilator—a small blue suitcase—so they wouldn't boil alive.

When the bus started moving, when finally, if only for a moment, Lucy found herself out of range of the cameras and microphones, she rested her forehead against the window and closed her eyes. She had gotten up at four in the morning, it was approaching six in the evening, and she still had a three-hour flight to the International Space Station ahead of her in a capsule the size of an elevator—so she had to take advantage of every opportunity to rest; the Russians, for that matter, were already asleep.

She, however, couldn't fall asleep. Her thoughts kept returning to Eliza's question. Why? What for? Of course, she had answered it thousands of times before: first during endless job interviews, then in media interviews, public appearances, and private settings. But back then she said what was expected of her, what she ought to say, what other astronauts had been repeating for decades: that this way she would serve science, that space exploration was humanity's most important mission, that she wanted to be an inspiration to little girls all over the world, and so on and so forth. Only, those were lies. If someone had given her a truth serum—if such a thing existed—her answer would have been less lofty. Why? Because nothing gives you such a kick, such a thrill, as flying at thirteen thousand kilometers an hour. Because it's an incredible adventure. Because others couldn't, and I did. Because it's confirmation that I'm the best.

All this made the resentment in her daughter's voice, her reproach, hurt even more. It wasn't really about the birthday. Lucy was hardly ever home. She had spent the last four years living out of a suitcase, flying between Washington, Houston, Toulouse, Moscow, and Cologne—endless training sessions, courses, preparations, and exams. Every hour, no, every minute of the mission had to be planned and rehearsed multiple times at least a year in advance. Lucy had to know every safety procedure by heart, cram all the instructions and technical diagrams into her head, and know the name of every screw, switch, and cable, in both English and Russian. If she were truly doing all this for the good of humanity, maybe she could have justified it to herself, silenced her pangs of conscience. But she wasn't. She wondered if her male colleagues harbored such thoughts too, if they also mourned missed first steps and words, or if this was solely the lot of women: to feel guilt over one's own ambition.

Something else was gnawing at her, too. The previous day, she had had a meeting with Deputy Director Ayers. It hadn't been planned, it wasn't on any schedule, and the words that had been spoken would be sought in vain in any press releases. Mounting tensions. Conflict at the highest levels. Fear of escalation. It was hard to believe something like this could happen in the twenty-first century, and in blood-soaked Europe, no less. But Director Ayers had been completely serious. Clouds were gathering on the horizon.

The International Space Station was a child of the optimism of the nineties. The Russians and Americans, liberated from the logic of the Cold War, no longer had to race each other among the stars, so they joined forces for the good of humanity. Through a joint effort, with the participation of Europe and Japan, they constructed a laboratory orbiting the Earth—for a trifling one hundred and twenty billion dollars. A symbol of a new era, of shared dreams, of the end of history. Of course, pragmatic calculations lurked behind the lofty slogans. The Americans wanted access to Soviet technology, while the Russians needed money to keep their space program alive amidst the chaos of perestroika, and no one but the Americans was capable of putting up the necessary sums at the time. Everyone got what they wanted, and everyone was happy.

But today, over twenty years later, the math no longer added up. First of all, from a technical standpoint, Roscosmos had nothing left to offer the Americans: it still relied on Soviet solutions from the nineteen-sixties. Secondly, a new superpower had emerged, China, which was ready to pump money into the Russian agency without asking pesky questions about human rights, democracy, or freedom of the press. In other words, the marriage of convenience was beginning to crumble, but the cosmic house still had to be shared.

The bus stopped in the middle of nowhere, a few kilometers from the rocket; the chirping of crickets carried across the steppe. Ah yes, time for the final tradition. She stood up, and Anton let her pass into the aisle, making an exaggeratedly gallant gesture in the process. Broad-shouldered, with a square jaw and short blond hair, he was actually quite handsome. The effect was ruined only by his cigarette-yellowed teeth. They had served on the station together when she flew there for the first time, eight years ago. They knew each other well. That was exactly why she avoided him.

"Ladies to the left, gentlemen to the right," Anton ordered when they stood on the hot asphalt. A step behind him walked Lev: a head shorter, introverted, with a mustache as bushy as his eyebrows. Lucy had gotten to know him during their joint training sessions—and liked him. He was a man of the old school, sincere and devoted to the cause. He deeply believed that the chasm between East and West could be bridged, or at least that from the perspective of orbit, that division ceased to make sense. He was the embodiment of the Soviet ideal: hardworking, rational, convinced that the future could be better than the past. This was his last mission, his farewell to space. He looked as if that were already filling him with sadness.

A moment later, released snaps clicked and zippers hissed. The Sokol suits, which barely an hour ago had been meticulously fastened by a swarm of technicians in latex gloves, cotton caps, and plastic shoe covers, right in front of the families and journalists, were now being depressurized. Of course, everyone, including the aforementioned technicians, had known this was exactly what would happen. Because on April 12, 1961, in this very spot, Yuri Gagarin had asked the driver to stop the bus. The man who was about to become the first to cast off the shackles of gravity first had to deal with the most mundane of needs: he had to take a leak. And since he subsequently flew and returned in one piece, from then on, every successive cosmonaut made the exact same pit stop—just in case. As is usually the case, the men had it easier. The women, in order to satisfy the demands of tradition, brought along previously voided urine in a small plastic bottle. Lucy poured its contents onto the rear tire and flicked off the last drops with a brisk flick of the wrist, a move she had gleaned from the priest who had blessed them earlier.

The official cosmonaut anthem, *Trava u doma*, roared from the loudspeakers, heralding that the crew was already in the rocket and they were less than two hours from launch. Steve Ayers wondered how many times he had already heard this sentimental track, how many times he had stood on the observation deck of the Baikonur Cosmodrome. The twentieth? The twenty-fifth? He had no earthly idea. He had seen off so many missions here that they were blurring together; his passport was filled with stamps from Kazakh customs. But this moment, this final stretch, still got his adrenaline pumping. In a moment, they would send humans into space. It was impossible to get used to that; it never ceased to amaze. A few more photos, speeches, a teleconference with mission control in Moscow, and the officials stepped out onto the observation deck. It was approaching eight in the evening.

"Something ends, something begins." Director Rybkin leaned against the railing. A daily program, folded in four, stuck out of his back pants pocket. Rybkin came from a military background, and it was obvious he hadn't served behind a desk. His eyes betrayed him: alert, focused, constantly in motion, trained to spot enemies, to seek out traps. This habit might still come in handy for him. His predecessor had been dismissed less than a year earlier amidst a scandal, surrounded by corruption charges that threatened him with a long stint in a penal colony. No one wondered whether he was guilty or not; one only had to look at the jewelry worn by his much younger wife to form an opinion on the matter. What was more puzzling was why the prosecutor's office had turned its attention to him *now*. In other words: how had he disappointed his masters? What expectations had he failed to meet? What had changed behind the thick walls of the Kremlin in the meantime?

"It doesn't have to end at all," Ayers replied. They were speaking in English; it was more convenient that way.

"Ah, so you do want to buy a seat on the next flight after all?"

"If you drop your price below the competition's..."

"Right... The free market." Rybkin grimaced. "Unfortunately, I don't think that will be possible."

"There are other projects."

"For example?"

Ayers cast a pointed look toward the sky. The sun had already set, and the Moon was peeking through the clouds. The last time a human had set foot on its surface was over fifty years ago. There had been many plans to return, but they lacked enthusiasm, money—and a good reason. During his long career at NASA, Ayers had witnessed plans for a return to the Moon sent to the shredder multiple times. Just as he was slowly losing hope that anything would come of it, that in his lifetime people would once again fly beyond Earth's orbit, the situation changed completely. SpaceX—a private company, a startup that industry veterans had laughed at just a dozen or so years ago—had shown that you could fly cheaper, more frequently, and farther than anyone had dared to dream. Furthermore, advances in the development of nuclear fusion meant that long-held dreams of using the boundless reserves of lunar helium-3 as a revolutionary new fuel had a chance to become reality. America was looking at the night sky with growing interest. And not just America.

"We will find another partner." Rybkin stepped back from the railing and clasped his hands behind his back.

"China?"

"Do you have an objection?"

"China won't be a partner to you."

"And were you?"

Ayers didn't answer immediately. He felt that from this point on, he should weigh every word.

"I'd like to think so," he replied.

The Russian turned his gaze back toward the rocket. Bathed in the glow of spotlights, amidst the nocturnal blackness of the steppes, it looked like a monumental statue erected in honor of—what? Human curiosity? Power? Arrogance?

"Do you know how much we pay Kazakhstan to use Baikonur?" Rybkin asked after a moment.

"The figures are publicly available."

"Say it out loud. Let's remind ourselves."

"One hundred and fifteen million a year."

"But one hundred and fifteen million what? Rubles, perhaps?"

"No. Dollars."

"Dollars, exactly." The Russian nodded. "Is that a lot of money or a little?"

"To me? A lot."

"Right? And how much money did Kazakhstan put into building this cosmodrome, which they are now so graciously letting us use? Are those figures also publicly available, Mr. Ayers?"

"You're trying to drag me into a conversation about politics. And I..."

"Please, just tell me: how much?"

Ayers looked Rybkin in the eye. He tried to understand where this was going, what he wanted to achieve. Whether this was just about old grievances, or something more.

"Kazakhstan was part of the Soviet Union at the time," the American replied diplomatically.

"Ah, exactly. And what exactly happened that caused the Soviet Union to collapse?"

"I don't believe that's relevant to our conversation."

"It's relevant to every conversation."

"Let's just say there are differing opinions."

"Yes, very differing," Rybkin agreed. "Mine is that our partner helped us with that. The very same one who just took away half my budget."

"Private companies are simply cheaper. That's not our fault."

"Ah, it's like I'm back in the nineties," the Russian snorted. "Perhaps you'd like to tell me about the invisible hand of the market next?"

*Uh-oh*, Ayers thought, *time to change the subject.*

"I get the impression our conversation is not heading in a constructive direction," he said, plastering on the kind of warm smile that only an American can muster.

"Really?" Rybkin wouldn't let it go. "Because in my opinion, some very specific conclusions are emerging from it."

*"Zazhiganie... Predvaritelnaya..."* crackled over the loudspeakers. The scaffolding pulled away from the rocket, the umbilical cables were thrown off; it looked like a flower opening its petals. *"Desyat! Devyat! Vosem!"*

"What kind of conclusions, if I may ask?" the American wanted to know.

*"Sem! Shest! Pyat!"*

"That all of this," Rybkin pointed first to himself, then to Ayers, "was a mistake."

Their faces were bathed in the light of the flames.

Right above Lucy's head dangled a plush dog. It had a black nose, pink fur, and huge eyes. It weighed exactly twenty-three grams and was a crucial piece of equipment on the Soyuz. It reminded her of a souvenir Nate had once brought back from a conference in Edinburgh. It was a framed stone bearing the inscription "Scottish Weather Station." If the stone is warm—the sun is shining. If it's wet—it's raining. If you can't see it—fog. For the dog, the instruction manual would look like this: If the dog isn't moving—you haven't launched yet. If it starts to vibrate—the engines have ignited. If it bounces—you've lifted off the ground. If it floats in the air—you've reached orbit. If it's on fire—something has gone very wrong.

The plush toy began to shake. As did her hands, legs, stomach, tongue, eyes, and brain—the vibrations pulsed through the entire capsule. Lucy was sitting, or rather lying, on the left side, curled up with her knees pressed right under her chin, wedged into a recess molded from a plaster cast of her body; to withstand several hours in this position, everyone preemptively swallowed a horse pill of painkillers. In the cramped interior of the Soyuz cabin, every cubic centimeter had to be utilized, which was why the cargo bays, stuffed to the brim, bulged right above her head, which was sheltered in a Plexiglas bubble. The capsule commander, Anton, was responsible for packing them. If the weight was distributed unevenly, if there was even half a kilogram more on one side than the other, the spacecraft would veer off course. And that, when you're traveling through the boundless expanse of space, was never good news.

"Booster readings nominal," a voice echoed in her headphones.

Lucy felt an immense force pressing her into the seat, squeezing the air from her lungs. Beneath her, forty thousand kilograms of rocket fuel was burning. She glanced at the screen: the G-force was at two Gs, two and a half, three, three and a half... She wanted to laugh, to cry, to scream with joy—finally, after eight years of waiting, of work, of sacrifices, her own and others', she was tearing herself away from the Earth again. But she stifled it, just as efficiently and dispassionately as if she were throwing a lever on a control panel or pressing a button. This skill was just as vital for an astronaut as knowledge of orbital mechanics or first-aid techniques. Lucy was constantly being observed, assessed, and evaluated; even now, at this very moment, she had a camera in front of her face transmitting the feed to mission control centers in Houston, Moscow, Toulouse, and Tsukuba. She knew that even though she was essentially playing the role of a passenger on this flight (Anton and Lev were in control, and even they didn't have much to do right now, as the onboard computer was handling most of the tasks), she had to look serious and focused—otherwise she'd end up on the radar of the psychologists overseeing the crew. So she monitored the readings on the screens and checked off the next items on the checklist.

"First stage separation," Anton said.

She felt as if someone had suddenly taken their foot off the gas pedal. The rocket shed its tail, along with the empty tanks and four engines, each the size of a truck. They would soon burn up in the atmosphere, like sacrifices offered to gravity.

"Launch escape system separation."

A loud bang. The spire concealing the engines—which, in the event of an accident, would instantly tear the capsule away from the rest of the rocket—jettisoned. The most dangerous phase of the flight was behind them. They could breathe.

"Fairing jettison."

Light flooded the capsule. Until now, the windows had been covered by metal panels. Lucy looked outside. Half the window was filled with boundless blackness, amidst which shone stars, supposedly the same ones she looked at in the night sky, yet different: without the obscuring shroud of the atmosphere, their light was much sharper, more distinct. She felt as though she had been trying out lenses at the optometrist and had finally found the right ones, the ones that showed the world as it truly was. Below, the Earth slid by. It was night over Asia; the city lights burned. She had missed this view. Every day. Every moment.

"Still impressive, huh?" Anton remarked.

"It is."

"Second stage separation," Lev read the prompt provided by the computer. Of the towering spire that had launched from Baikonur, only the capsule remained. "Third stage ignition... Initiated..."

"Just like the view from the Cupola that time," Anton continued. There was a mocking tone in his voice. A challenge.

"Speed thirteen thousand seven hundred and two miles per hour," Lucy said, ignoring the bait.

"We were flying over Turkmenistan then, too."

"Solar array deployment..." The astronaut flipped the corresponding switch. "Initiated..."

"Those lights down there are Ashgabat... And there's Bishkek..."

"Attention," sounded in their headphones. Mission Control in Moscow. "Loss of signal in thirty seconds. Reacquisition of signal in approximately twenty minutes. Begin preparations for the first delta-v maneuver."

"Copy."

Silence fell over the cabin. For exactly half a minute. The bitter words that were spoken afterward were not recorded in the official transcripts.

The Baikonur Cosmodrome began to empty out. The loudspeakers went silent; the spotlights illuminating the launchpad clicked off. The officials got into the limousines waiting for them; the journalists packed up their tripods and put their cameras away in their cases. Nate wondered what the demand for their footage would be, who would want to air it, and—more importantly—who would want to watch it. While the prospect of an expedition to the Moon or Mars still fired the imagination, while people were excited by the feuds of billionaires zipping around in orbit, flights to the International Space Station had become commonplace. News about successive rocket launches was, of course, followed by a loyal group of enthusiasts, but it no longer made the front pages—nor even the second or third.

And it's hardly surprising: the ISS was, after all, a laboratory—a flying one, but a laboratory nonetheless—and how could one get excited about pipetting, even in zero gravity? NASA's press office understood this perfectly well. So they did what they could to drum up enthusiasm for the station: astronauts recorded videos where they explained how the space toilet worked (look, you strap your feet in with these bands, and you pee into this funnel...), spun like tops, squeezed juice out of a pouch and caught the fleeing drops, absolute acrobats of microgravity. Eventually, however, even that lost its novelty. And so something that just half a century ago was merely a dream, a pipe dream really, had already managed to blend into everyday life, melting silently into the background. Such is the fate of every breakthrough, every technology: we are capable of getting used to anything. Perhaps, moreover, this is the key to our success, the reason why an animal built for life on the grassy savanna, a bipedal ape, hasn't yet gone mad in a world of glass, cables, and screens. Or rather, perhaps: hasn't gone completely mad.

"Dad, are we going?" asked Eliza. The little girl was tired. A taxi was waiting downstairs.

"Just a minute."

Nate raised his eyes to the sky, to the spot where just a moment ago the fire-breathing engines had been flickering. There was no trace of the Soyuz left; it had dissolved among the stars, a dot amidst thousands of dots. The man took a deep breath—he could smell the exhaust fumes and the heated asphalt—and then slowly let the air out of his lungs. The fear that had been gnawing at him for weeks, that had latched onto every thought and seeped into every dream, had finally eased slightly. But it hadn't disappeared, because it never disappeared: it was ready to return at the first opportunity, at the slightest impulse, like a spider sensing the faintest tremor on the web strung inside his head. That was exactly how he imagined it: thousands of silver threads strung inside his skull, and among them gleamed black, unblinking eyes.

That was why Nate was glad to be going home, back to everyday life, to the familiar routes between home and school, the store and the gas station, the park and the library. He needed routine to tame reality, to cope with it. This was perhaps what set them apart the most, him and Lucy. She constantly craved new experiences; he avoided them, even when it came to something seemingly pleasant and simple. A weekend out of town, say. Sounds great, but what if the weather turns bad, they run out of gas, get a flat tire, Eliza gets sick, or they get there and find out something is wrong with the reservation? Of course, they eventually went—because he *wanted* to go, after all, or at least he knew he *should* want to—but the questions never vanished. So Nate would devise mental contingency plans that were increasingly complex, branching out like the Amazon delta, and take extensive preventative measures. He'd gas up the car the day before, check the tire pressure and oil level, double-check that he'd packed the raincoats and that the hotel room was booked for the right dates. Nate didn't go on vacation; he went on a sort of three-person military maneuver, during which, as the chief staff officer, he constantly had to check the maps, receive reports, and identify potential threats. Naturally, it was hard to relax under those conditions. He only ever admired the views after returning home, in the photos.

Lucy was his polar opposite. Always composed, always in control of the situation. For her, a new problem was an interesting challenge, a puzzle to be solved. Hurrying to the airport and hit traffic? Great, let's quickly chart an alternative route. The deadline to submit a paper is in a few hours and the computer crashed? An excellent opportunity to expand one's knowledge of IT. She was impossible to stump, impossible to throw off balance.

They had met fourteen years ago at the International Planetary Science Conference. He, a geophysicist, was a member of the team working on a probe destined for Titan (it didn't launch; the funding dried up). She, an astrophysicist at the time, was presenting the results of a spectroscopic analysis of the planets in the Gliese 581 system in the constellation Libra. She caught his eye because her poster, instead of being on a folding stand, was draped over an improvised contraption made of a broomstick, a straightened wire hanger, and thread from a hotel sewing kit. It turned out the airline had lost her luggage, so she'd had to construct a new stand using whatever she had on hand, and in under an hour. They chatted for a bit, he made a joke, she laughed. He asked if she had any plans for the evening. She didn't. Sometimes—rarely, but still—he regretted not asking what her plans were for the next ten years, too. Who knows, maybe they wouldn't have gone out for drinks then, wouldn't have kissed in the hotel elevator, wouldn't have spent the night in his room; in short, there wouldn't have been this whole chain of cause and effect that had brought Nate here, to a cosmodrome in Kazakhstan. The partners of astronauts had it rough. Partners with his psychological makeup had it especially rough.

"Nate!" Someone placed a hand on his shoulder. Deputy Director Ayers. Even though this was the third time they'd seen each other in their lives, he sounded as though he were addressing a best friend. You could tell he had spent his life in politics. He had a reputation as a player who was as slick as he was ruthless. "Will you be at the farewell dinner?"

"Sorry, not this time. The little one needs to get to bed."

"I understand. If you need anything..."

"Thank you, we're all set."

"Excellent... In that case, we'll all see each other back here in six months?"

*If everything goes as it should*, said a voice in the back of Nate's head. If Lucy's capsule doesn't re-enter the atmosphere at too steep an angle and burn up. If it doesn't enter at too shallow an angle and skip off the planet's envelope of gas like a stone skipped across a lake. If the heat shield doesn't fail. If the cabin doesn't depressurize. If the parachute opens. If they don't fall into a spin that causes lethal G-forces. If they don't smash into the Kazakh steppe.

"Exactly," he replied, forcing himself to smile with great difficulty.

The latches clicked open, and the hatch began to swing wide. The Soyuz was filled with the deafening roar of fans and the distinct odors she remembered from her previous stay: sweat, heated metal, stale plastic. In the opened airlock, above them, appeared the faces of the crew. Anton went out to them first—a light push-off and he was floating toward the exit, as if swimming through the air. Lucy gathered the hair waving in front of her face and tied it back tightly with an elastic band. She was ready; she couldn't wait. Now Lev—one flick of his hand and he was up. It was her turn. She stood on the seat, bent her knees slightly, and flew after the others.

Even though she had been here before, even though she had completed hundreds of parabolic flights during which she had acclimated her body to weightlessness, her head still spun, her stomach clenched. Up, down—suddenly those words lost their meaning, they were useless. She felt as if she had stepped inside an optical illusion where nothing was where it was supposed to be, one that scrambled the senses the longer you looked at it. Lucy felt sour saliva in her esophagus. *Don't puke,* she thought, *just don't puke.* Someone was already hugging her, patting her on the back. She nearly banged her head into a camera lens. They were in the Russian section, in the Rassvet module, so it was tight, too tight for two people, let alone nine. They swarmed like fish in a net.

Lucy recognized the three astronauts they were relieving, whose Soyuz was returning to Earth in a few days: Igor Volkov, Kate Hortchak, Francesco Russo. A moment later, the Dragon pilot, Ezra Wendley, was greeting her. Red-haired, muscular, and as white as a sheet, he was a former military man, and he looked exactly the part. His capsule had docked three months earlier and was scheduled to stay for another three. To her left flashed Lafayette Summers, an eternally smiling botanist, an African American from Louisiana. Last in line—though it was hard to call it a line, since everyone was in motion, and it would be easier for her to plan a checkmating move in 3D chess than to say who was currently where, whose arm was whose, and whose leg was whose—was Devaki Anand, an American of Indian descent. Lucy knew her the least. She was the only one in the group who wasn't a professional astronaut, but a space tourist. Naturally, this didn't mean she got to sip drinks with little umbrellas; NASA had put this crew member to work too. How much she had paid for her stay on the station was a secret, but it was undoubtedly a seven-figure sum. For Devaki, who had made billions in Silicon Valley, such an expense wasn't much of an issue.

Lucy dreamed of finally heading to her quarters (a grandiose word—in reality, it was a space smaller than a broom closet), taking off her clothes (though no one said it aloud, the astronauts flew in diapers, officially and euphemistically called "high-absorbency garments"), and washing up (which meant wiping her entire body down with disposable wet wipes, because there were no showers; free-floating water plus microgravity equaled a guaranteed short circuit). But there was one more item on the agenda: the flight-crowning press conference. They moved into Nauka, one of the station's oldest and simultaneously newest modules. Oldest, because its construction began back in the nineties. Newest, because the Russians had only managed to finish it and send it into space at the beginning of this year. What they hadn't managed was a safe docking: when Nauka had attached, its previously malfunctioning main thruster had unexpectedly fired, pushing the entire station out of orbit, which had very nearly ended in disaster. From the inside, the module looked like any other: cables, LEDs, and electronics protruded everywhere, from every surface.

"Okay, let's line up..." The station's rotational crew commander, Volkov, took the initiative. "Newcomers up front... The rest in the back..."

It took them a moment to get into their assigned positions. To avoid confusing the viewers on Earth, they all adopted the same orientation, as if they were standing on the floor, even though half of them could just as well have been hanging upside down. Everyone grabbed onto something—those who had just arrived at the station instinctively used their hands; the rest, already accustomed to microgravity, hooked their feet into footholds; it was more comfortable that way. Three, two, one, the light by the camera's glass eye glowed red.

"ISS, this is Moscow," the controller called them. "Not too cramped in there?"

"We're managing," Volkov replied with a smile. His hair, like the rest of the Russians', was cropped short, and his neck was red from razor burn; shaving in space was exceptionally unpleasant. Above his head gleamed a gold Orthodox cross stuck to the wall.

"Excellent. Soyuz, how was the flight?"

"Without a hitch," Anton replied.
