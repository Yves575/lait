*Koukun* Volume 1: The Girl from the West



Prologue: The Blue Flower


The wind howled in her ears and whipped at her hair.
The passing shower from early afternoon had left the earth damp, and the rock to which she clung with arms and legs was as cold as ice.

“…Sister!”
A high-pitched voice called out, and fine flecks of mud rained down.
Aisha instinctively let go of the rock with one hand, reached for the heel of her younger brother’s shoe—vaguely white in the gathering dusk—and pressed her palm against it to support him.
The moment she did, her body tilted, and her other hand nearly slipped from the rock. She frantically regripped the rock, barely steadying herself, and the moment she exhaled, her knees began to shake.

A fall meant certain death.
Aisha gritted her teeth and supported her brother with all her might until he planted his foot firmly in a hollow naturally worn into the rock and steadied himself.
Even after her brother had found his balance, Aisha remained frozen in that position for a while.
Panting heavily, she waited for her dizziness to pass.

Even if they managed to climb this rocky cliff path, there was probably no future for them. She had realized this a little while ago. From the top of the crag, a stiflingly strong smell of leather, metal, and sweat was wafting down.
They had been told that few knew of this cliff path. The fact that warriors were lying in wait meant that their old retainer, Uchai, who had acted as a decoy to draw the enemy’s attention and let them escape, had been captured almost immediately.
She could not believe that *Jiiya* had confessed, but the warriors had clearly deduced where Aisha and her brother were fleeing and had intercepted them.

(Should I let go?)
Vaguely visible in front of her were her brother’s mud-stained shoes. Remembering his beaming smile when those shoes were bought for him, Aisha’s face contorted in anguish.


Suddenly, the surroundings brightened.
The wind had blown the clouds apart. The setting sun, just moments from vanishing behind the mountain, cast myriad beams of light across the rocky peak.
In that unexpected bright light, she saw a small blue flower blooming from a weed rooted in a crevice just above the rock she was gripping. Battered by the wind, the flower swayed as if it might be torn away, yet it held on.
She heard the voice of the flower’s scent. Faint but resolute, the fragrance drifted on the wind like a thread. Soon, insects hearing the voice of this scent would weave their way through the wind to reach it.

“…Sister.”
The voice fell from above again. Carried on the cold wind came the scent of fear, like a cowering puppy.

“Stay strong, Mircha!”
Aisha raised her voice.
“Grip the rock tight and climb! If you slip, I’ll catch you!”

Just a little further, and they would emerge at the top of the crag.
Sensing from the presence above her that her brother had started climbing, Aisha slowly began to climb as well.





Chapter One: Encounter


1. Ritaran


The colors of the bonfires, which had been invisible until a moment ago, now stood out starkly against the blue-black darkness.
The wind that had been making the tents flap loudly had eased somewhat.
From the countless tents pitched in the encampment, columns of white smoke rose wavering into the air, melting into the evening sky. The faint aroma of the evening meal cooking drifted over.

“The foot soldiers get to eat sooner than we do,” grumbled a bearded man in a low voice, sitting heavily on a folding chair alongside men with fierce, rugged faces.
Mashu looked down at his bearded colleague, Urairi, and chuckled.
“You don’t have to keep me company. I can handle the observation on my own.”
Urairi started to make a flippant remark, then stopped, turned his gaze toward the grasslands, and muttered,
“…If I could, I'd do just that.”


Against the majestic backdrop of the Tenro Mountains sinking into the evening gloom, across the vast grasslands spreading at their foot, the flickering torchlight of a dozen or so horsemen could be seen advancing toward them.

“Oroki.”
Urairi twisted his neck to look back at the man standing in waiting behind them. The hunting dog at the man’s feet looked up as if it were the one being called. Oroki placed a hand on the dog’s head and said,
“Yes.”
“You said the heir is about eight, and the older sister fifteen or sixteen?”
“The heir is nine; the older sister is fifteen.”
Urairi nodded and let out a sigh.
“They should have just left them alone instead of going out of their way to capture them.”
Urairi looked up at Mashu and continued.
“Don’t you think? If some faction were trying to prop them up, that would be one thing. But what power could the descendants of a king who was despised by his people and driven from his throne possibly have?
In fact, that must be what they thought, which is why they were left alone until now. Why on earth did Jukuchi decide to do this now, and right in the middle of a war at that?”

Mashu didn’t answer the question. Keeping his eyes fixed on the horsemen, he murmured,
“Just as I thought. They're coming from that direction, after all.”
Urairi frowned.
“Who are?”
“The horsemen. The fact that the soldiers who captured them are returning from that direction means the descendants were trying to flee into the Toura Ira Valley in the Tenro Mountains.”
“…”
Urairi stood up and stepped up beside Mashu.
“You said ‘just as I thought.’ Does that mean you saw this coming?”
Mashu glanced at Urairi.
“If they were going to run, that’s the only place they could go.”
“Surely not? Weren’t the descendants living in hiding in the woodlands? It would be easier to flee east through the forest than to climb dangerous mountain paths.”


“Even if it meant taking a risk, they must have thought they'd have a better chance of surviving by fleeing to Tenro.”
“Why?”
“Earlier, you called Keruan a king despised by his people, but up in Tenro, there are those who don't despise that king.”
Urairi’s expression hardened.
“…I never heard anything about that. Mountain folk in Tenro? Which ones?”
Mashu stared intently at the mounted warriors, whose figures were now clearly visible.
“You aren’t the only one who didn’t know. In fact, few people do.”
Urairi narrowed his eyes.
“When did you find out?”
Mashu looked at Urairi and answered quietly,
“Me? I’ve known since I was a child.”

From the grand tent Lord Jukuchi used for his administration, one of the lord’s close aides emerged. Scanning the area through the gathering dusk, he spotted Mashu and Urairi and approached briskly.
“Inspectors.”
Crossing his arms neatly before his chest and bowing deeply, the aide said,
“I bring words from Lord Jukuchi. ‘The prisoners will arrive shortly; please enter the tent.’ That is all.”
Mashu nodded and replied curtly,
“Understood.”
The aide bowed deeply once more, then led the two toward the grand tent.


The inspectors of the vassal kingdoms served as the eyes and ears of the Emperor of the Umar Empire, which held dominion over the four vassal kingdoms.
The word "vassal" in "vassal kingdom" derives from the Umar word *rachi*, meaning "that which demarcates a boundary, encloses, and protects."
The vassal kingdoms were once independent nations, and even now, under the rule of the Umar Empire, they were granted a degree of autonomy. In exchange for the duty of defending the borders (the *rachi*) of the Empire’s domain, a vassal lord was granted governing authority over the land he had once ruled—but he was no longer the king of an independent nation. Jukuchi, merely the Lord of the West Kantar vassal kingdom, was permitted to hide nothing from Mashu and his colleague.

Leaving their martial escorts behind, Mashu and Urairi walked slowly through the blue darkness toward the entrance of the grand tent, which shone so brightly it seemed cleanly cut from the night.


＊


Inside, the grand tent was more spacious than its exterior suggested.
The skylight for ventilation was thrown wide open, and the central hearth glowed with bright red flames. Lamps were placed here and there, providing enough light to make out the expressions of the senior vassals and clan chiefs seated along the walls.
At the far back, directly facing the entrance, stood an altar beautifully painted in vermilion, blue, and gold. With his back to the altar, Lord Jukuchi sat in a large chair, speaking in low tones to an aide beside him. When Mashu and the others entered, he rose from his chair, offered a slight bow, and gestured for them to take the nearby seats.

Though the wind outside was cold, inside the grand tent it was hot and humid. Small tables set before each person held fruit juice to quench their thirst, along with nuts and dried fruits to stave off hunger.


Shortly after Mashu and Urairi took their seats, the sound of a bell ringing announced the arrival of the prisoners. The murmuring ceased, and a hush fell over the grand tent.
Jukuchi sat down again, took a cloth from his aide, and wiped the sweat from his forehead and the nape of his neck.
He was a large man, but there was no softness about him; his skin was tanned dark from the sun. Everything about his build was massive, from his hands to his feet, but his most striking features were his eyebrows and eyes. Whenever he fixed someone with those huge, bulging eyes, they couldn’t help but feel uneasy.
He hailed from a cadet branch so distant it was only rumored to be connected to the royal bloodline, but he was a man gifted with a unique instinct for warfare. Granted troops by the Emperor on the condition that he act as a bulwark for the Empire in times of crisis, he overthrew the previous king—who had taken the throne after King Keruan was ousted but had failed to quell the skirmishing among the clans—and became the Lord of this West Kantar vassal kingdom.
One clan still refused to recognize Jukuchi, so it couldn’t be said that he had complete control over all of West Kantar, but Mashu believed that if things continued as they were, he would subjugate them before long.
The Emperor desired Jukuchi to pacify West Kantar swiftly, and Mashu, too, hoped for the same outcome.


The moment the heavy cloth of the doorway was hauled wide open from both sides, a cold night wind blew in.
Carrying the night breeze with them, two burly warriors entered, one hauling a young boy and the other a slender girl.
Neither prisoner was bound with ropes, but their dominant arms were firmly gripped by the warriors. One of the warriors pressed an arm to his chest and announced in a resonant voice:
“In accordance with your orders, we have brought the descendants of Keruan.”


Jukuchi nodded, offered a word of thanks to the warriors, and made a gesture indicating they could withdraw.
The warriors, however, hesitated.
“What is it? You may withdraw,” Jukuchi called out, and one of the warriors spoke up.
“Begging your pardon, my Lord, but the older sister has shown no intent to submit. If we let go of her, there’s no telling what she might do.”
Hearing this, Jukuchi raised his thick eyebrows.
“Is that so? Well, we shall keep an eye on her. Let her go.”
The warriors bowed, released their grip, and took a step back, their eyes locked vigilantly on the girl so they could restrain her at a moment’s notice.

The girl, however, did not move. Not even an eyebrow twitched. With dark, immovable eyes like stones, she simply stared straight at Jukuchi.
Jukuchi met her gaze and said, “You are Keruan’s grandchildren, Mircha and Aisha, aren’t you?”
The girl opened her mouth.
“I have no name to give to a usurper.”
Her voice was hoarse, but steady.
Jukuchi let out a sigh.
“I see. So you truly show no intent to submit.”
Rising slowly, he looked down at the siblings. He snatched his longsword from the nearby stand and violently struck the floorboards with the end of its scabbard.
The senior vassals flinched, and the siblings jumped in fright. The younger brother’s face crumpled, and he began to sob.
“Your brother is crying,” Jukuchi said, his eyes boring into the girl. “Mind your situation and your place when you speak. Depending on your attitude, your brother’s head could go flying right before your eyes.”
The girl stared up at Jukuchi with a pale face, but eventually, in a frail voice, she spoke.
“…That is a lie.”
Jukuchi’s thick eyebrows shot up.
“A lie?”
The girl nodded.
“Does the fate of our lives truly depend on something like my attitude?”


Jukuchi’s eyes widened slightly.
He stared at the older sister in silence for a while, then the corner of his lips curled up slightly.
“If not your attitude, then what determines your fate?”
He expected her to think for a moment, but she answered immediately.
“Your own gain and loss.”


A faint stir passed through the senior vassals.
Mashu shifted his gaze from the girl to Jukuchi.
(Now, how will he answer?)


Jukuchi stared at the girl in long silence, then let out a sigh.
“My gain and loss. …Well, you're not wrong.”
With that, he sat heavily back down in his chair.
Lifting a gold-trimmed horn cup from the small table, he guzzled the milk wine, wiped his mouth with the cloth his aide offered, and wiped the sweat from his face and neck for good measure.
Then, he turned back to the older sister.
“You're not wrong, but you're not entirely right, either. Your existence is a detriment not so much to me, but to the Empire as a whole.”
The younger brother looked up at his sister questioningly. The sister didn't look at him; she just kept her eyes fixed on Jukuchi.
“You were trying to flee into the Tenro Mountains, weren't you? To be precise, into the Toura Ira Valley.”
Noticing the girl’s expression, Jukuchi added,
“Don’t get the wrong idea. That old dotard didn’t confess. He is a remarkably loyal fool. He put up a resistance you wouldn’t expect from a man his age, and because I suspected he might be acting as a decoy, I split my troops into two groups from the start.”
The girl’s face stiffened. Watching her, Jukuchi said,
“The one who informed us of the place you might flee to was the Inspector of the Umar Empire.”


Urairi shifted in his seat. He didn’t do anything so obvious as look at Mashu, but his surprise emanated from his entire body.
Mashu’s expression did not change; he simply watched Jukuchi and the young girl.


Jukuchi spoke in an indifferent tone.
“So, you see, your existence does no good for West Kantar, nor for the Empire. Do you know why?”
The girl’s face had gone pale. She had likely realized that there were no avenues of escape left to her.
Only Jukuchi’s voice echoed through the grand tent.
“Your grandfather Keruan was a foolish man. Because of him, many people starved to death. The majority of people in this country despise Keruan from the bottom of their hearts. I am one of them.”
The girl’s eyes wavered. Her pallid face grew even whiter.
Jukuchi had his horn cup refilled with milk wine and gulped it down again.
“However, I hear there are still folks on the frontier who revere you.
The mountain folk known as the *Makishi*. Since they are a marginal group with insignificant numbers and hold no ambition for hegemony in this country, I’ve left them alone until now. But right now, their existence is a nuisance. —The location they control is problematic.”


Mashu heard Urairi murmur beside him, *So that’s what this is about.*
The clans they planned to subjugate on this expedition controlled the foothills of the western Tenro Mountains, but their dominion did not extend into the mountains themselves.
They lacked the strength to hold out for long against the expeditionary force led by Jukuchi, but if they could secure the aid of the Kingdom of Shinketsu across the Tenro Mountains, there remained a possibility they could reverse the tide of the war.


The Tenro Mountains, which enclosed West Kantar from the north to the west, were rugged and steep. There were limited places where a large army from Shinketsu could invade, and at those locations, forts had been built and were jointly garrisoned by the Imperial Army and the West Kantar forces.
In the Toura Ira Valley, in the western region of the Tenro Mountains, there was also a mountain path through which a small unit of warriors accustomed to mountain crossings could traverse from Shinketsu, but there was no fort there.
Around the Toura Ira Valley, the ground hid unexpected subterranean hollows, and stepping into one could cost you your life. One could not navigate the mountain trails without a guide.
Unless the mountain folk—the *Makishi*—who controlled the Toura Ira Valley and its surroundings sided with them, Shinketsu warriors could not descend into West Kantar. Moreover, without deploying a massive army, it would be impossible to bring the *Makishi*, who knew the valley like the back of their hands, under control. Therefore, until now, neither the Lord of West Kantar nor the Empire had placed much importance on this region.
However, if the *Makishi* still considered Keruan’s descendants to be the legitimate offspring of the true king, the situation could change drastically if the hostile clans were to rally around these two.
Among the clan chiefs seated along the walls of the grand tent, some wore expressions indicating they finally understood the meaning of what was currently happening.
The clan chiefs who had followed this expedition from the southern and eastern parts of the vassal kingdom were largely ignorant of the affairs surrounding the western Tenro Mountains. Having only recently submitted, Jukuchi had deliberately kept the details from them to prevent them from entertaining unnecessary thoughts.


“You are, so to speak, ant holes bored into a levee. Normally, there’s no harm in leaving them be, but when a storm comes, the levee crumbles from those holes, and disaster spreads across the country.
I am not a heartless man; I do feel some reluctance to kill you merely because you are that man’s grandchildren. But it cannot be helped.
There is the option of keeping you captive under my supervision, but that leaves a slight margin of danger.”
Having said this, Jukuchi suddenly looked weary.
“So, that is how it is. —If you wish to bear a grudge, curse your bloodline and your fate.”
At a jerk of Jukuchi's chin, the warriors from earlier stepped forward and tried to grab the siblings’ arms.
The brother, terrified, reached out for his sister as if clinging for his life. Grasping her brother’s hand firmly, the older sister looked up at Jukuchi.
A hesitant color flickered across her pale face. Her lips parted, then closed again, as if she had thought better of it.
Jukuchi frowned suspiciously.
“Do you have something to say?”
The older sister thought for a moment, then, as if making up her mind, said:
“You are being poisoned.”
Jukuchi furrowed his brows sharply and stared at her, once again wiping the sweat from his face and neck with the cloth. However, the sweat could not be contained by a mere wipe, drenching his skin at a rate that was unnatural even to an onlooker.
“What did you say?”
The sister stared at Jukuchi with unreadable eyes.
“You smell of *chitcho* weed. —Someone has poisoned you.”
The senior vassals erupted into a commotion, some even half-rising from their seats.
Jukuchi wiped the sweat from his eyes and offered a fearless grin.
“A final, venomous lie? It takes admirable guts, but if you’re going to lie, make it a more believable one.”
“I am not lying.”


“Of course you’re lying. A poison brewed from the roots of the *chitcho* weed has no taste and no smell.”
The girl looked as if a realization had dawned on her.
“…Then, I suppose it is fine if you continue to believe that.”
She let out a sigh, put an arm around her brother’s shoulders, and turned her back on Jukuchi. Flustered, the warriors grabbed their arms and began hauling them toward the tent’s exit.


Mashu stood up abruptly and called out to them.
“Wait.”
The siblings stopped and looked at him. Jukuchi, the senior vassals—everyone was staring at him, wondering what was going on.
Mashu asked the girl.
“Lady Aisha Keruan. —Do you smell anything on me?”
The older sister stared intently at him. Her dark eyes were like obsidian.
Just as he thought she might not answer, she frowned slightly and spoke.
“…Are you *Ritaran*?”
Mashu’s eyes widened. A cold sensation spread from between his brows through his entire head, and his heart began to pound painfully fast.
His lips felt numb. Parting them, Mashu asked in a hoarse voice,
“Why do you think so?”
Still frowning, the girl replied.
“Because you smell of blue incense grass.”
Mashu stood dumbfounded, staring at the girl.
“…Hey, what’s wrong?”
Without answering Urairi’s question, Mashu took long strides toward Jukuchi.
Seen up close, it was glaringly obvious that Jukuchi’s condition was abnormal. His eyes could not focus.
Mashu turned to the aide.
“Tell the healer to bring *kaful*! Make no mistake, tell him *kaful*! And bring plenty of water! Hurry!”
He then called out to the warriors holding the siblings.
“Take those two and lock them up securely!”


A dull thud sounded, like a sack of flour dropping to the floor. Mashu turned to see that Jukuchi had slipped from his chair and collapsed on the floorboards.
Amidst the chaos of the senior vassals standing up and shouting over one another, Mashu rushed over to Urairi, lowered his voice so the others couldn’t hear, and spoke rapidly.
“Stay with those siblings. Take the guards with you. Let no one lay a finger on them. Ensure they aren’t poisoned; be vigilant about their food and even the smoke from the insect repellent. Then, send Oroki to me later.”
Urairi gave a silent nod and headed toward the siblings. Together with the warriors, he accompanied them out of the grand tent.


2. A Scentless Poison


It was around dawn the next day when Jukuchi's condition passed the critical stage.
Throughout that long night, senior vassals visited the tent one after another, but Mashu only let them look inside from the entrance and refused to allow them in.
Some showed indignation, but as long as the poisoner remained unknown, Mashu’s measures were perfectly reasonable, so no one tried to force their way inside.
The only person permitted entry was his subordinate, Oroki. Mashu gave him detailed instructions and sent him out into the encampment along with his dog, which followed him like a shadow.
Having stayed awake beside the Lord’s pillow with the healer all night, Mashu waited until he was certain Jukuchi’s condition had stabilized before spreading out a bedroll in the corner of the same tent and drifting off to sleep.


When he awoke, it was shortly before noon, and the Lord was breathing deeply and quietly in his sleep, just as he had before.
Standing up and stepping out of the tent, Mashu took a deep breath.
He smelled rain-wet grass. Come to think of it, he felt like he had heard the sound of a sudden downpour beating against the tent in his dreams. Showers like that were common in this area around this time of year.
The sky was covered in leaden clouds, but the wind was strong, and whenever the heavy clouds were blown away and broke apart, sunlight streamed through, illuminating the Tenro Mountains with an unexpected brightness.
Preparations for the midday meal must have begun, for the smell of smoke was mingled with the savory aroma of baking *tapa*, a thin flatbread made from rice flour.
Hearing footsteps in the grass, Mashu turned and saw Oroki approaching with his dog.
“How did it go?” he asked, and Oroki replied in a low voice,
“Exactly as you surmised.”
“You found it?”
“Yes.”
Hearing the detailed report, Mashu smiled.
“Good work. Get some rest.”
Oroki bowed, clicked his heels, and turned his back on Mashu. Mashu called out to his retreating figure,
“Make sure to feed your partner a good meal, too.”
Oroki glanced back, his cheeks relaxing faintly, and nodded. Knowing he was the one being talked about, the dog wagged his tail loosely.
Taking a deep breath, Mashu turned away from them.
(…Now then.)
Mashu murmured to himself.
(Now comes the real work.)
Entering the tent from the bright outdoors blinded him for a moment.
As his eyes adjusted to the dimness, he could make out Jukuchi lying in the bedding laid out near the hearth, and the healer tending to him by his side. The scent of medicinal broth mingled with the smoke.


As Mashu approached the pillow, Jukuchi’s eyes fluttered open, perhaps roused by the noise. He gestured for water.
The healer supported Jukuchi’s head, raising it, and brought a bowl of water to his lips. Jukuchi slurped it down noisily.
Seeing him drink without choking, the healer’s face relaxed in relief.
Coughing two or three times to clear the phlegm in his throat, Jukuchi raised his head and looked at Mashu. Strength had returned to his eyes.
“…What I was given,” Jukuchi said in a raspy voice. “Was it really *chitcho* root?”
Mashu looked at the healer, and the middle-aged man spoke up.
“We have no definitive proof, but it appears highly likely. The symptoms you exhibited closely matched those of *chitcho* poisoning. Moreover, the medicinal broth prescribed as an antidote for *chitcho* proved remarkably effective.”
Jukuchi looked sullen.
“But *chitcho* is supposed to have neither smell nor taste, isn’t it?”
The healer nodded.
“It does not.”
Jukuchi returned his gaze to Mashu.
“Then why did that girl say my sweat smelled of *chitcho*?”
How to answer when asked this question—Mashu had been pondering it all through his night watch, but every answer that came to mind left him with a sense of hesitation.
Yet, now that he was actually being asked, he felt no hesitation in speaking the answer in his head.
Mashu looked toward the healer.
“Lord Jukuchi’s condition seems stable. Will it be alright if you step away for a while?”
The healer got to his knees, checked Jukuchi’s pulse, and nodded.
“For a short while, I believe he will be fine without me.”
“Then please, take a rest. I will notify you immediately if there are any changes in Lord Jukuchi’s condition.”
The healer bowed and exited the tent.
Waiting until his footsteps faded away, Mashu turned back to Jukuchi.


“As to your question from earlier.”
“…”
“Perhaps the girl really could smell the *chitcho*. She likely has a sharper nose than most.”
A harsh light sparked in Jukuchi’s large eyes.
“You cleared the room, yet you spout such nonsense? What I wanted to know is why that girl said something so unnecessary. She knew I had been poisoned. In other words, she is connected to the one who poisoned me…”
Mashu slowly shook his head side to side. Jukuchi scowled in frustration.
“You say I am wrong? How am I wrong?”
“That girl is not connected to the poisoner.”
“How can you be so certain?”
“Because, as you yourself questioned, she informed you that you were being poisoned.”
“…”
“It truly was an unnecessary remark. If she were on the poisoner’s side, believing that she and her brother would be saved if you died, it is a remark she absolutely never would have uttered.”
Mashu let out a small sigh.
“She put up a brave front, but she is still only fifteen. When she realized a man would die if she kept her realization to herself, it simply slipped out.”
“A man who was trying to kill her, no less.”
“Even so, there are those who simply cannot bear to stand by and watch someone die.”
“But the *chitcho* root…”
Cutting off Jukuchi before he could finish, Mashu said,
“You are fixated on the fact that *chitcho* is odorless and tasteless, but the signs of being poisoned manifest in many ways. Even I noticed your excessive sweating. The girl, facing you directly, would have seen it clearly.”
“…”


“A king is constantly exposed to the danger of assassination, so naturally he becomes well-versed in its methods. That girl is King Keruan’s granddaughter. She must have been thoroughly educated on the ins and outs of assassination growing up.
Therefore, she discerned from your unnatural sweating and unsteady gaze that you had been dosed with *chitcho*.”
Jukuchi seemed half-convinced, but something still bothered him, and he started to open his mouth.
Mashu continued before he could speak.
“Besides, whoever attempted to poison you could not have known the value of Keruan’s descendants.”
Jukuchi’s eyes flew wide open.
“Have you discovered whose doing this was?”
Mashu nodded.
“The chief of the Rima clan.”
The muscles around Jukuchi’s eyes tensed.
“Rariha? —Where is the proof?”
“His servant was holding a cup-polishing cloth. I am told that servant was in charge of polishing the cups set out in the grand tent last night.”
Jukuchi sighed and shook his head.
“If that is the basis of your suspicion, you are mistaken. I wash all my own cups, polish them myself, and keep them where I can see them. There’s no way he could have smeared poison on my cup.”
Mashu smiled.
“He didn’t smear poison on your cup.”
“…?”
“He smeared *kaful* sap on everyone else’s cups.”
Mashu clinically explained the poisoning method.
“Fearing poisoning, you only drink milk wine poured from the same vessel as everyone else. If one were to mix poison into the milk wine, Rariha would have to drink it as well.
One could kill everyone in the grand tent and be the sole survivor, but even if they succeeded in eliminating you, they would incur the wrath of the other clans.”
A look of astonishment spread across Jukuchi’s face.
“I see! He exploited the fact that I polish only my own cup separately!”


Mashu nodded.
“Exactly. The *chitcho* poison is weak against *kaful* sap. Coming into contact with that sap neutralizes its toxicity. The antidote the healer prescribed for you was also made from *kaful* sap.”
“…He smeared it on his own cup and the cups of the others beforehand.”
“Yes. It took my subordinate all night, but he found the man carrying the polishing cloth that smelled of *kaful* sap.”
Mashu gave a fleeting smile.
“Using a dog’s nose, rather than his own, that is.”
Jukuchi groaned and looked down for a moment, lost in thought.
Stroking his chin, Mashu said,
“Given the current situation, punishing Rariha will be quite difficult. We must carefully formulate a plan to handle the aftermath before acting.”
“…There is that, yes,” Jukuchi muttered, then looked up at Mashu.
“But what to do about that girl.”
“Because she saved your life?”
Jukuchi nodded.
“At least just the girl…”
Mashu shook his head.
“That will not do. If you kill the brother, that girl will direct a fierce hatred toward you for the rest of her life. It will only leave a root of future calamity.”
To a clouded Jukuchi, Mashu said,
“If you wish to show mercy, then rather than beheading, have them executed by poison.”
Jukuchi drew his eyebrows tightly together.
“That would be more agonizing.”
“No, you only need to choose the right poison. There are poisons that bring a painless death, like falling asleep.”
Jukuchi stared at Mashu with a complicated expression. Mashu simply met his gaze in silence.




3. Frost-Weed


Aisha and her brother Mircha had been confined to a tent pitched on the outskirts of the encampment.
Because it was pitched in the grasslands isolated from the other tents, anyone approaching would stand out.
Guards who had accompanied Mashu kept strict watch both inside and outside the tent. They would only hand over meals, changes of clothes, and the like to the siblings after thoroughly inspecting them.
Shortly past noon on the day Jukuchi recovered, Oroki came to the tent once, called Urairi outside, whispered something to him, and then took about three of the guards and went somewhere. Other than that, there was almost no movement of people.
In addition to the skylight, the tent had two small windows for ventilation. Whenever shouts from drills or other noises echoed from the encampment, the younger brother would go to the windows and peek outside. But the older sister never went near the windows; she simply sat inside the tent, passing the time embroidering a cloth she had been given to stave off boredom.
When the sun had fallen and the soldiers’ evening meal was over, a late dinner was brought to the siblings.
Behind the servants carrying the dinner stood Mashu and two of the Lord’s aides.
When the guards lifted the doorway flap and whispered inside, Urairi emerged. Noticing Mashu and the others standing behind the dinner attendants, Urairi’s face stiffened.
Mashu brought a finger to his lips, and Urairi signaled his understanding with his eyes.


The servant carrying the large tray of food bowed his head slightly and entered the tent, while Mashu and the Lord’s aides each approached a window.
The sun had completely set, and the lights of the encampment were far away, so from inside the brightly lit tent, one could hardly see the faces of those standing outside the windows, provided they stood a little ways back. Even if a shadow was visible, the guards had stood outside the windows in the same manner countless times before, so the siblings shouldn’t have realized anything unusual was happening.
The dinner was far more lavish than what had been served to the soldiers; in addition to roast mutton, there was a vegetable stew and even fruit. The thin *tapa* flatbread was spread with butter and sprinkled with precious sugar. Naturally, milk wine was served with it.
The younger brother must have been hungry; his face lit up happily when the dinner was set before him. But the older sister had kept her head bowed since before the servant even entered.
Seeing her posture, Mashu narrowed his eyes.
Her shoulders were tense. She must be nervous.
(Did she notice?)
The boy reached for the flatbread first, stuffed his cheeks with the bread soaked in melted sugar and butter, and ate with obvious delight.
The sister also began her dinner quietly. Like her brother, she started with the flatbread, and ate the mutton and the soup slowly, savoring every bite. She ate all the fruit as well.
After tasting a bit of everything, the brother reached for the milk wine. At that moment, the sister stopped eating and looked at him.


(…Will she stop him?)
Mashu involuntarily leaned forward, staring at the sister’s profile.
Her hands were trembling faintly. Her slender neck was tense, its tendons standing out.
Her brother grasped the handle with both hands and brought the vessel of milk wine to his lips.
The sister made no move to stop him. She merely stared fixedly at him.
The boy drank the milk wine deliciously, his throat gulping loudly, set the cup on the floor, and reached for the remaining fruit.
And then, just like that, he violently shook his head and nearly slumped forward.
The sister quickly reached out, caught him, laid his body down, and placed his head on her lap.
Something sparkled on her cheek.
She leaned forward as if hugging her limp brother’s head, but eventually straightened her back and reached for her brother’s empty pitcher of milk wine.
No sooner had she grasped the handle of the pitcher than she hurled it with all her might.
The pitcher smashed with a dull thud right beneath the window where Mashu stood; he instinctively pulled his face back.
Urairi and the others inside stood up in surprise, but the sister, without so much as a glance in their direction, simply snatched up her own pitcher of milk wine, locked eyes with the window where Mashu stood, put the pitcher to her lips, and drained it with a gulp.
Her dark eyes held a fierce, terrifying light as she glared right at him.
Mashu tightened his gut and stared right back into the girl’s eyes.
(That’s right. I’m the one who poisoned you.)
Something hot surged from the depths of his chest, and his whole body began to tremble.
(You caught the scent of my fingers that pinched the poison in—and you knew the owner of that scent was right here.)
Without realizing it, Mashu was smiling. His entire body felt as if it were burning hot.
He watched steadily as the strength faded from the girl’s eyes and she collapsed forward.




＊


Escorted by his aides, Jukuchi stepped out of his tent.
A large woolen rug was spread on the grassy ground in front of the tent. The light from the torches held by the soldiers dimly illuminated the bodies of the girl and the boy laid out on the rug.
Their faces looked white even in the night, and they seemed a size smaller than when they were alive.
Jukuchi looked down at the bodies for a while, then crouched, placed a hand by the boy’s mouth, and confirmed he was not breathing.
He did the same for the sister, then gently touched her cheek, quickly pulling his hand back in surprise.
“She’s quite cold. Hasn’t it only been a short while since they died?”
Standing on the other side of the bodies, Mashu said in a quiet voice,
“I used *hirin*, the frost-weed.”
Jukuchi nodded, stood up, and let out a sigh. Then he turned to the soldiers.
“Wrap them in cloth and bury them.”
Saying only that, he turned his back on the corpses and went into the tent.


In West Kantar, the bodies of children were buried beneath a Yugi tree.
According to legend, the Yugi tree grew from the body of a mother who, mad with grief over the loss of her child, threw herself into a river and washed ashore. It was said that if a child was buried at its roots, the spirit of the Yugi tree would gently guide the souls of those who died young to the heavens.


The soldiers, who had been ordered by Jukuchi to search for a Yugi tree since evening, had already found a good burial site and returned. The two bodies were quickly loaded onto the bed of a wagon, carried across the grasslands and into a forest along a narrow river, and buried in a hole dug at the roots of a Yugi tree.
The soldiers were human too, and parents themselves, so they did not just toss the bodies in; they wrapped them in woolen blankets so they wouldn’t be cold, laid them at the bottom of the hole, and covered them with earth to prevent beasts from ravaging them. Then they bowed deeply and returned to the encampment.


The moment the sound of their horses’ hooves faded away, three figures leapt from the dark grove of trees and began frantically digging up the earth of the burial site.
Lifting the two bodies from the hole and laying them on the grass beside it, they hastily pulled away the cloths completely covering their heads.
Then, wrapping the bodies carefully in different cloths, two of the men each took a sibling in their arms and vanished into the depths of the forest.
The remaining man placed the original cloths that had wrapped the bodies back into the bottom of the hole, shaped the earth a bit, and then piled the dirt back over it, returning the grave to its original appearance.
The light of the sinking moon made the tips of the forest branches faintly glimmer.




4. The Voice of Scents


She smelled something savory and toasty.
(…*Jiiya* is baking *tapa* flatbread.)
*It's time to get up. I need to wake Mircha too,* she thought. Suddenly, a terrifying memory flashed like lightning, and a sharp pain pierced her chest. The pain spread like bitter water from her chest to her throat, and into her head.
Aisha opened her mouth and drew in a sharp breath.
Her throat whistled like a flute, and the smell of baking bread, the smell of smoke, and the smells of two men and a dog all rushed into her nose at once.
Amidst that flood of scents was Mircha’s smell. Their arms seemed to be touching, for she could feel his warmth.
(Mircha… is alive!)
She tried to open her eyes to look at her brother, but her surroundings were spinning violently, so she squeezed her eyes shut again and waited for the dizziness to subside.
Hearing a man’s voice say, "Looks like you’re awake," Aisha desperately tried to piece together what was happening with her confused mind.
(I drank the milk wine laced with *hirin* frost-weed…)
She was sure she had watched Mircha down the deadly frost-weed laced in the milk wine, and then drank it herself.
(So why?)
She felt a man stand up and approach her, her eyes still closed. Even with her eyes shut, she knew who was approaching.
“You are awake, aren’t you? Lady Aisha Keruan.”
Aisha slowly opened her eyes and looked at the man. She was still a little dizzy, but she could clearly see his face.
A sun-tanned, rugged face and dark eyes were staring fixedly down at her.
“…Why?”
As she murmured, perhaps noticing the hoarseness of her voice, the man turned his head over his shoulder and said, "Bring the water pitcher."
When the water pitcher was brought over, the man gently slipped his hand under Aisha’s head, supporting her as he slowly helped her sit up. It was a thick, large, warm hand.


Taking a sip from the wooden bowl, the water smelled of old cedar. It had probably been transported in a barrel. Even so, it was delicious to her parched throat.
Draining the water, she returned the bowl to the man, who accepted it in silence.
Aisha stared at the man.
Every time he moved, the scent of blue incense grass grew stronger. He must have been keeping it in his breast pocket. It had mingled with the man’s own scent and altered, but there was no mistaking that it was the scent of blue incense grass.
“Why…”
She tried to ask again, but the man shook his head.
“I don’t have time to explain right now. I will explain tonight, so until then, please rest here.”
As Aisha tried to look toward her brother, the man stood up and said,
“Your younger brother is also safe. He woke up a moment ago and fell back asleep. I’ve wrapped him in a blanket so he shouldn’t catch a cold. You ought to warm yourself as well. I’m leaving my subordinate behind as a guard, so if anything happens, follow his instructions.”
Saying this, he took long strides back to the campfire, handed the wooden bowl back to the man sitting beside the fire, picked a piece of flatbread from a plate with his fingers, popped it into his mouth, and walked away into the trees.
Beside the man cooking at the campfire was a dog. The dog was lying down, watching her.
“If you can get up, would you like to warm yourself by the fire and have some flatbread?”
The man called out to her. He had a slender face, but with a certain sharpness to him, like a hunter.


Aisha put a hand on the grass and slowly stood up. She felt slightly dizzy again, but it quickly passed.
Sitting by the campfire, the heat of the fire seeped into her skin, and she realized just how thoroughly chilled her body had been.
(*Hirin* frost-weed freezes the body.)
Anyone who mistakenly consumed this herb would rapidly grow cold and slip into a painless, sleep-like death… or so it was supposed to be.
(Why am I alive?)
The man deftly peeled a thin piece of *tapa* flatbread—which had been stuck to the small pan, releasing a savory aroma—with his fingers and placed it on a wooden plate. He then slathered it generously with butter, drizzled honey over it, and handed it to her.
The moment she felt the warmth of the wooden plate in the palms of her hands, something hard that had been encasing her heart shattered, and the sensation of being alive spread through her entire body.


She had thought there were no more paths left. She had thought her life was over.
Having envisioned being beheaded, when she learned the execution would be by poison, she thought it would be an easier death. While waiting for the execution, she had been terrified to the bone, but when the moment arrived and she watched her brother drink the poisoned milk wine, her fear vanished, and her entire world went black. She, her brother, everything was sealed in a pitch-black darkness.
Yet, the instant she caught the scent of that man’s fingers in the milk wine, a fierce rage pierced right through her brow.
Because of their bloodline, their lives had to end—the unreasonableness of it all, and the frustration of having to leave this world unable to right that wrong, erupted into an indescribable rage against something she couldn’t even name, burning her from within.


Lifting the warm flatbread from the wooden plate, folding it to a manageable size, and putting it in her mouth, the rich saltiness of the butter and the sweetness of the honey mingled and spread through her mouth.
It was a familiar taste.
The flatbread they had been served at every meal since being captured smelled of ingredients she had never eaten before, but this flatbread was the familiar one she was used to eating, and as its aroma spread through the back of her nose, tears welled up.
So the man wouldn’t see her tears, Aisha looked down and stuffed her cheeks with the warm, savory flatbread.
The dog sluggishly stood up and came to her side. He pressed his cold nose against Aisha’s elbow.
