NOVEMBER 6

Because of the rain, I missed the village sign. It blurred the valleys, erased the ruts; I ended up driving blind and pulling over on the shoulder. All this water battering the hood. The storm started yesterday. I haven't passed a soul since leaving the highway. Even though the radio advised staying off the roads, I had no choice. It’s five o’clock, the sky anthracite. I haven't been able to adjust the seat's incline. I wait, sitting bolt upright, dazed by the din. At least the van seems sturdy. It looks like a municipal utility vehicle, plasma-colored. I insisted on practicality at the rental agency.





An hour passes. Finally, the downpour eases. I start the engine again. The GPS takes me deep into the forest. Soon, neither rain nor light pierces the forest canopy. I turn on the high beams. The steering wheel is sticky. I crawl along for miles, guessing the path between the tracks under the brambles, until I emerge at the foot of a steep slope. A little further up, the gate is open. For the first time, I replicate my father's movements. I shift into first, accelerate, the wheels spin in the gravel but hold fast, I cut the engine in front of the house. The motion-sensor light flicks on. A rabbit bolts.

The building looks tired, the roof sagging over the bricks like a giant asphyxiated by ivy. A car is parked under the hazelnut tree. Ferns split the porch steps apart. Through the window, I can make out a light. I press my face against the peephole, recoil immediately. I wasn't expecting my sister's face—massive forehead, wide-set eyebrows, fish eyes—my sister swollen by this magnifying glass that my father claimed to have deliberately installed backward. According to him, we had nothing to fear and nothing to hide; our wealth was internal, and the whole world should know that the most beautiful people lived here.





"Hi."

My voice sounded louder than intended. Véra replies with a smile too large for her mouth. She takes the suitcase from my hands, sets it at the bottom of the stairs in the kitchen. I rediscover the stone floor, the wooden furniture, the bathroom door in the shadow of the fireplace. I hadn't known it like this, the hearth blocked with books. Above the table, a birdcage replaces the light fixture. Cheeses are piled up behind the bars.

Véra points to the stairs, then gestures to herself at the counter; I’m to settle in while she finishes preparing dinner. I knew her to be messy. I compliment her. She types on her phone, shows me the screen:

"It’s to give you a proper welcome."

I reply a bit sharply that we are sisters and this is my home too, let's spare ourselves this kind of politeness. She lights the gas with a hasty gesture. I can't help adding:

"Especially since we aren't keeping anything."

The stairs swish beneath my socks. I have to be careful not to slip. Our parents' bedroom door is ajar. I linger on the threshold, in the draft of the poorly insulated French window. Black hardwood floor. In the center of the room, the large bed, the naked mattress, no sheets or blankets. I still wonder how my parents could sleep without a wall behind their heads. I pull the door shut, vaguely relieved. I don't know what I dreaded more: sleeping in that bed, or sharing our room with my sister now that we're adults.





Her sweet perfume catches in my throat. She kept our bunk beds. Tonight, the sight of them saddens me. The wrought iron seems too frail to support us. The dresser and the desk are in their places, as plump as before, painted salmon pink. I check to make sure I have internet. I'm going to spend nine days here; I need to be able to communicate with my colleagues. The network only shows a single bar. Sometimes it disappears.

Leaning out the window, I spot the van. Its orange color makes me laugh. It looks like a giant bumblebee. It looks as out of place as my sister and I do, reunited for the first time since my father's death five years ago.

Véra has poured wine into the crystal glasses. Tense from this ritual, I say that I don't drink. She raises her eyebrows, pours the wine back into the bottle, it spills, I wipe it up with my sweater, then take it off; I'm hot. The bamboo dishware is alien to me. Proudly, Véra shows me the cheese wrapper adorned with chestnuts, then points to the fireplace: it's a smoked cheese. I push away the thought that it's made from raw milk. She has made an endive salad with figs and walnuts. I ask her if she has thought about how we should proceed over the next few days—I haven't, I've been too busy. She taps away:

"Congratulations on your award."





I murmur that it's kind of her.

I don't know what she knows about the films I've written. The latest one just won a prize at an Italian festival; I couldn't attend, and anyway, I hadn't invited her.

"Have you heard from Octave?" I ask in a voice I'd like to be neutral.

She nods, of course, she points to the figs, the walnuts, they’re from him... I cut her off. I say that as far as I'm concerned, there's nothing I want to keep. She can do her sorting, and we'll take the rest to the dump. Her fingers tense on her phone. With her chin, she points to the armoire, the kitchen, the bathroom. I look up; surely we aren't going to rummage through all that? Her face lights up in the backlight of her screen:

"Whatever you want."

I soften. It's just that I have work to do. I've fallen behind. I'm going to have to isolate myself to write. With a simplicity that disconcerts me, she shows me the screen again:

"Whatever you want."





Then she asks me about my current projects. I mention my latest commission, adapting Georges Perec’s novel, *W, or the Memory of Childhood*. Véra punctuates my sentences with smiles. I feign nonchalance when mentioning the prestige of the production, the fame of the prospective actors, that of my co-writers. We have to create six episodes. Filming is scheduled in two years. Véra applauds. I qualify this. Adapting this text is no easy task. And I am only the dialogue writer. I start to introduce Perec; she nods vigorously, she knows him, she has read *A Void*.

"You read?"

She gestures that it's obvious.

"I don't know, my father..."

Silence.

"I mean Dad. He didn't tell me." Without dropping her smile, Véra serves me the last of the figs. I was fifteen, Véra twelve, when I left for the United States. The stay was supposed to last just through high school, with a host family. Véra hadn't spoken in a long time. She was learning to read and write, but I hadn't thought her capable to this extent.





"And how are you?" I ask, realizing I haven't asked her a single question since I arrived.

Our last exchanges date back to last year, for her move to Périgueux. Until then, she had lived here with my father, even after his death. I helped her from a distance. The studio apartment was already furnished; she didn't want to take anything from our childhood home without my consent. Now that the sale is signed, Véra was counting on my coming to empty it out.

I read over her shoulder. She makes an annoyed gesture; I have to give her time. I apologize, serve myself more salad.

Following a parcel rezoning, our house is no longer considered a tenant farm of the neighboring château, Le Pigeon Froid, owned by Octave's family. Safety standards have changed. To comply with them, we'd have to overhaul the roof, the heating, the electrical system; we can't afford it, so we accepted an offer from a campsite that’s going to raze the place. Octave wants to salvage our stones to restore the dovecote.

Véra shows me the screen. Shop work bored her. She is training in floral preservation.

"Preservation?"

"I make flowers that don't fade, thanks to chemistry."

I look suitably intrigued.

"And does it work?"





"Depends on the flowers."

I clarify that I meant the business. She shrugs:

"People don't want the hassle anymore."

We clear the table.

"It's true that flowers don't grow in November," I finally say flatly.

Véra soon goes to bed. I stay in the living room, penetrated by the night. The floor lamps cast warm pools of half-light, but I am not at ease. The windows have no curtains. I see my reflection on the sofa ringed in black, amid the hum of the refrigerator, the smell of the cheese that Véra put back in the cage. I feel stifled by the blocked fireplace, the dozens of Van Gogh sunflowers pinned to the walls. My father used to collect theater and art exhibition posters. He didn't go see them and paid no mind to the artists' names, but chose the most colorful images to plaster all over the house.

I feel weary at the thought of the massive clear-out. If we set fire to the books, only the stones would remain; that would be the simplest way, given that they are all we’ve been asked to preserve.





I put off sorting through my emails until tomorrow and spend the evening scrolling through my instant messages, worried by Irvin's silence. It's almost evening in New York. He would have had time to write to me. I am tempted to wait for him to reach out first, find myself childish, wish him a good night, tell him I arrived safely. I hesitate. Add that I miss him.

The bathroom is true to my memory. It looks like a cave, the floor and walls of rough stone. Véra has laid out a towel for me, neatly folded on the cabinet. It has always disgusted me with its woodworm holes, even though they've been treated. She has taken off her jewelry. Ethnic style, feathers, shells. I open a drawer. It is full of amber, necklaces, brooches. I kick aside the wet bathmat, step into the shower stall, and wash some of Véra's hair down the drain. I hope I don't clog it.





I stay under the scalding water for a long time. My hair holds the crease of the hair tie; it has dried out with the drop in hormones. I don't know when my period will return. I turn toward the stone. I still have the reflex to look for support when I'm naked, to not look down at this lower belly that Irvin claims he never sees swollen. According to him, it's all in my head. He wants to reassure me. It isn't his fault. He didn't see it empty out down my legs, redden the water, then disappear down the drain. He knows nothing about my body.

The less noise I try to make, the more the stairs creak. The bedroom is bathed in the greenish glow of our charging phones. Only Véra's head peeks out from the covers, her hands steepled on her chest. Her clothes balled up on the dresser. The ladder squeaks, the sheets rustle. Lulled by the smell of laundry detergent, I fall asleep instantly.





NOVEMBER 7

I proceed by category. Items to destroy, to donate. I laugh at myself for applying the advice of tidying influencers. The window lets in a cold light. The spiders flee. They don't spin webs but cling to the corners, around the oven. I dispose of the dead ones at the bottom of the pots. I started with the kitchen, which seemed like the most neutral room. Into a large trash bag, I toss the expired products. Mustard, tomato paste. A jar of white mass—duck fat. An entire shelf is dedicated to cheese. The fridge shrinks under my assaults. The freezer compartment needs defrosting. The plastic is cracking. In the crisper drawer, potatoes have sprouted. Véra has only come back once a month since she moved. The smell of the cheese turns my stomach. My movements are swift. Moldy jams, leftover butter, withered herbs.

Véra is tackling our bedroom. I'm uneasy knowing she's nearby. I listen to the noises she makes. Earlier, she came down to make coffee while I was sniffing some applesauce; she asked if I wanted some too, and not knowing whether she meant the applesauce or the coffee, I tossed the jar into the trash like a rabbit caught in the headlights throwing itself under the wheels.

Véra is making headway, too. At this rate, I’m afraid we’ll have too much time for ourselves. What will I do with her for another six days? I try to slow down.





The kitchen's clutter resembles Véra. I prefer it to the neatness of the living room when I arrived. Oil has spilled. I scrub the greasy bottoms, the sticky clumps. As I wipe a cloth across the back of a cupboard, I feel the panel move. I press harder. It comes loose, sliding out easily. It concealed a space the size of an apple crate, carved into the coolness of the stone. It is full of bags of sugar and hard liquor. I call Véra. She too was unaware of this alcove. Does she think our father... She shakes her head, probably not. It's true that I never saw him drink.

"And Mom?"

Véra looks pensive. I grab a bottle.

Quince liqueur. I want to throw it in the trash; she holds me back, mimes us drinking straight from the bottle. I shake my head no, but set it back down and close the hiding place. In the bottom of the sink, chunks of jam mingle with the grayish water. I have to plunge my whole arm in to scoop them out.

Véra seems impressed by my sorting. She watches me, tugs on her sweater, and points to the stairs: I need to take a look at my clothes.

"I'm donating them."

She insists. I can feel her watching me go up. This gaze weighs on me; it lends importance to a gesture that has none in my eyes.





My side of the dresser has stayed just as it was before the States, minus a few sweaters. I try on a pair of jeans anyway. Just for fun. My father had mended a hole by ironing on a flower-patterned patch with a pot of boiling water. I am surprised to find I have slimmed down. Even though I’ve gained weight in recent months—something Irvin likes, which reassures and distresses me at the same time. I put the jeans in a plastic bag, do the same with the flannel pants, a material I have never worn since, cotton pajamas, t-shirts, and wool sweaters. I begin to rummage, with the growing sensation that I am looking for something.
