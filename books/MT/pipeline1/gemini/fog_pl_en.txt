**Prologue**

He arranged to meet Zofia at eight. She had called in the morning and asked to meet. He thought, "No." He said, "All right." He regretted it later, but it was too late. She seemed nervous. She stammered into the receiver, gasping loudly for air.

"I have to see you," she said. "I wouldn't ask if it weren't really important."

As always, he felt helpless against her quiet, low voice, against the *ł* she emphasized with such deliberate care, and the *ą* she pronounced with too much nasality, as if a round little animal with a twitching tail had settled deep in her throat and was tickling her larynx. He involuntarily gave in to emotion before he had time to think, to remind himself that all these beautifully articulated syllables no longer belonged to him. Delicate, exquisite Zofia with her charming speech impediment had stopped noticing him. And yet she had called... Why? What for? Had something happened? And once again, anxiety washed over him. Zofia was always shrouded in an aura of mystery. That was what attracted him most. From the very beginning of their acquaintance.





On Thursdays, she was always the last to arrive. During the group meetings, she would sit on the sidelines. She spoke little, then left quietly without saying goodbye to anyone. At first, she provoked unease, but eventually, everyone grew accustomed to her silent presence. From time to time, the facilitator would ask her a question, which she answered briefly and matter-of-factly. The others soon lost interest in her. Everyone except him. He waited for Zofia every Thursday. He stealthily observed her white hands, her liquid dark-brown eyes, her chestnut hair hiding her face. After a month, without asking permission, he changed his place in the circle and sat by the wall. That day, he asked her out for coffee. Two weeks later, the facilitator called them in for a serious talk. He forbade private contact and ordered them to sit apart. He spoke sharply and firmly, stating he had no intention of tolerating insubordination in the group. He addressed this mainly to Zofia.

"I've been watching you for a while now," he said. "I don't understand why you even come here. You should seriously think about it. You're a bad influence on the others."

She listened to him calmly, then picked up her coat and purse.

"All right." She turned at the door. "I won't be coming back."

He ran after her. He knew he would have to give up the meetings, but it didn't bother him in the least. That was how the most beautiful three months of his life began.





He lowered his head and flinched, as if the sight of his own hands had surprised and frightened him. Old feelings returned in a trembling, surging wave. Three months, he grimaced. Three months during which he believed he was truly important to someone. It didn't last long... The affair ended just as abruptly as it had begun. They hadn't even had time to get to know each other properly. He knew so little about her... He hadn't asked any questions, though. He preferred for their love to blossom slowly, to gather strength and momentum. He hadn't wanted to insist or rush anything. He dreamed of holidays together, waited for the day she would finally introduce him to her parents; then he would move in with her, they'd renovate, put in a double bed... Eventually, they'd buy a bigger apartment for the kids... For three months, he wove increasingly expansive plans for the future; he felt calm and secure. And then suddenly, it was all over. Zofia wasn't answering her phone. He went to check on her. He knocked on the door. She didn't open it, but he had the inescapable feeling that someone was inside the apartment. Sitting on the stairs, he heard the muffled sound of a conversation, shuffling, and the creaking of the floorboards. That was when, for the first time in three months, he thought about the Thursday meetings, and fear gripped him. He went back home. The next day, he went to her place again. She opened the door but didn't let him in, blocking the entrance. It immediately occurred to him that someone was hiding behind her. *A lover?* he thought, and he was overcome by such despair and weakness that he had to lean against the wall. Zofia looked at him seriously with her large, moist eyes.

"I'm sorry," she said. "I shouldn't be handling it like this, but..."

He heard his own loud, ragged breathing. He felt himself growing pale. His head was spinning.





"Something has happened," she continued firmly. "We can't see each other anymore."

That was it. Like the drop of a guillotine. No excuses, no explanations. They met one more time, and she repeated exactly the same thing: certain changes had occurred in her life; they wouldn't be seeing each other anymore. Her eyes had lost their former softness. They had become cold and resolute. "Certain changes" could only mean someone else, he had no doubt about that.

The following Thursday, he tried to return to the circle. They didn't let him back in, but the facilitator scheduled individual sessions for him. He made no secret of his deep concern. He claimed that the shock caused by the relationship with Zofia could have serious consequences, not just for him, but for the entire group. He lectured at length on responsibility, obligations, and accepted rules of conduct. Then he questioned him in detail about his relationship with Zofia: about what they had done for those three months, where they had met, and what they had talked about. He felt the facilitator was being overly insistent and nosy, but he tried to answer anyway. Not much came of it. He suffered from a strange kind of amnesia. Nothing remained in his memory but despair, which was slowly turning into rage and uncontrollable jealousy. If Zofia didn't want to be with him, she couldn't, she had no right to be with anyone else!





But today she had called, he thought. Had she decided to apologize after all? His heart beat faster. To come back, to reconcile, after she had used him and pushed him away? Just who did she think she was? He felt humiliated at the thought of the past few weeks, of all those pleading attempts to make contact, the tearful phone calls, the letters, and the sleepless nights. He had constantly tried to approach Zofia, to force her to talk, to find that former closeness which surely couldn't have just vanished, evaporated without a trace... He kept thinking he would find the right words, convince her, break the spell, and the old, delicate Zofia, *his* Zofia, would stand before him once again! Meanwhile, she remained unfailingly indifferent, distant, and alien... He sprang from his chair and began to pace the room, bringing his hands to his face and tugging at the hair falling over his forehead. He walked to the window. He looked out at the wet, ugly city: squares covered in mud and melting snow, gray sidewalks, the colorless facades of the apartment blocks opposite. Occasionally, an indistinct figure flitted behind one of the panes of glass. Outside the neighborhood store, a few people stamped their feet and huddled under umbrellas. She had used him as if he were a thing, a dead object to be consumed and then thrown away, not a living, feeling being. A human being. A man, he corrected himself in his thoughts. He paced the room again, turning sharply at the door and the opposite wall, only to freeze a moment later by the window. Dozens of apartment blocks stretched out in every direction: heavy, gloomy, smelling of stale air. Strangers bustled about in small concrete apartments. Identical concrete modules, vertically and horizontally. Inside, the same tables, beds, and armchairs. Almost identical men and women cooked dinners, watched TV together, went to the bathroom to take a warm bath or take a piss. Did she know, did she have any idea how much she had hurt him?! He looked at his watch. It was almost one o'clock.





A sense of extreme irritation hadn't left him all day. He tried browsing the net, watched two episodes of *Brigada*, a Russian series about the mafia. In the early afternoon, he visited the neighborhood gym. He ran for an hour, then lifted weights and worked on his abs. He tried to switch off, to think only of Sasha, Cosmos, Pchela, and Phil—the brutal, risk-ready heroes of the series. Finally, he went to the sauna and took a cold shower. After returning home, he took some milk from the fridge and sat at the table. He drank straight from the carton in long, slow gulps. His irritation morphed into a hard, brutal rage. He replayed the course of the morning's conversation once more and concluded that Zofia hadn't called with the intention of reconciling. Her voice had sounded uncertain, but there was no remorse in it. Short, matter-of-fact sentences, no: "I missed you," or "I'm glad I'll see you." A perfunctory "Goodbye" at the end. *Bitch*, he thought, tracking an ascending airplane. A jagged streak tore through the thick gray clouds. But what could Zofia possibly want from him—he asked himself the same question for the hundredth time that day. It didn't matter. This would be his moment. She had invited him, so she would politely listen to what he had to say to her. He imagined himself entering the apartment, telling her to sit down, and explaining things briefly, concisely, in a firm tone, and she finally understanding... And if she didn't want to listen? He could hit her, or even tie her up. Tape her mouth shut and watch her cry soundlessly, terrified, defenseless, at his mercy. The plane climbed higher and disappeared above the clouds. In the apartment upstairs, someone put on loud dance music. He heard muffled laughter and the sound of small, quick footsteps.





It was almost half-past six. He put the milk back in the fridge. He put on a short bomber jacket, gloves, and sneakers. He pulled a cotton hood over his head. Outside, a strong, piercing wind was blowing; wet snow clung to his shoes, which soaked through immediately. Despite this, he didn't turn toward the subway. He walked stubbornly along the sidewalk down KEN Avenue, not stopping and not getting out of anyone's way. Zofia's face appeared before his eyes, her wide mouth, her straight nose, the small mole at the corner of her eye, and her gaze darting sideways as she said: "You have to finally understand that I want nothing to do with you." He recalled his own agitation, fear, and the burning feeling of rejection. How much he had humiliated himself and made a fool of himself... He quickened his pace until he finally began to run, driven by sorrow and impotent anger. "*Yob tvoyu mat'!*" "*Srat' tebye v rot!*" he repeated, unconsciously imitating the voice of one of the characters from *Brigada*. He didn't stop until he reached Służewiec, went down to the platform, and took the subway to Racławicka, after which he set off down the snowy street again, passing the illuminated windows of cafes, artisan workshops, and small neighborhood stores. Just past the school playing field, he turned into an archway and almost collided with an elderly gentleman dressed in a thick wool coat and a fur hat.

"*Idi na khuy!*" he snarled, then shoved his way brutally past, pinning the man to the wall.

He gasped for breath. Persistent thoughts went round in circles, making him sickeningly dizzy. Drops of sweat ran down his face.





Zofia returned home before five. She put her shopping bag on the kitchen table, took off her soaked shoes, and leaving wet footprints on the floor, went into the bathroom. She lay in the bathtub, thinking about how complicated her life had become over the last three months. *And whose fault is it?* she wondered, wringing out a sponge. "Whose fault?" she repeated aloud. *I attract bad luck like a magnet*, she continued to think, standing up and taking the soap in her hand. *The more I like someone, the bigger a freak they turn out to be. But is it that, for some incomprehensible reason, I only appeal to lunatics, or do I seek out their company myself?* She rinsed off the lather. She got out of the tub and wrapped her head in a towel. From a wicker basket, she took out gray sweatpants and warm woolen socks. For a moment, she observed her face in the mirror. *What should I do now?* she asked her own reflection. *What am I supposed to do?*

Despite her worries, the person in the mirror definitely had no intention of looking tired or unkempt. Zofia placed two fingers on her cheeks and pulled the skin slightly toward her temples. She raised her eyebrows, then reached for powder and mascara. Refreshed and carefully made up, she left the bathroom. In the kitchen, she brewed tea. She set two cups and a small plate of French pastries on the counter. Suddenly, she stopped mid-step and stood for a moment staring at the set table, as if trying to resolve an important question in her mind. After a moment, she took out her cell phone and dialed the same number twice, to no avail. Finally, she left a voicemail.

"I wanted to cancel our meeting," she said, lowering her voice to give the words a firm tone. "Something important has come up. I'm very sorry. I'll call you tomorrow."





She looked hesitantly at the phone. She had no guarantee the message would be heard. For a while she stood there, nervously drumming her fingers on the windowsill. Finally, she shrugged and sat in the window alcove. It was almost seven. She looked out at the small, fog-shrouded square and an abandoned construction site fenced off from the street by a wooden fence. A lone rook perched on the dark branch of a maple tree. She needed to make concrete decisions and weigh their consequences. Nothing sensible came to mind, however. No theoretical solution seemed good. She was in a trap with no way out. The rook shook its head, ruffled its feathers, and hobbled sideways toward the trunk. Zofia pulled her knees up to her chin. Down the dark street, the wind chased an empty plastic cup. She closed her eyes.

At a quarter past seven, the intercom buzzed. She sighed and silently pressed the button by the door. And then, driven by a sudden impulse, she grabbed her purse lying under the coat rack. With a decisive movement, she pulled out a medical certificate. Walking toward the bathroom, she tore it into tiny pieces, then threw them into the toilet.

"I'm in the bathroom," she called out, hearing the door shut. "I'll be right out," she added, flushing the toilet.

At that moment, she felt a powerful blow to her temple. One, then another. It didn't hurt. She wasn't afraid. A tremendous weariness washed over her. *Who is it?* she wanted to ask before she fell asleep, but blood flowed from her open mouth. She tried to turn around and look at her attacker, but she only groaned and plunged into deep darkness.





At eight o'clock, a piercing wind blew off the icy river. At half past eight, due to a malfunction, the riverside streetlamps went out. It grew dark. Only in the middle of the Vistula, where the current was strongest, did the lighter outline of a drifting ice floe loom. At nine, a beam of car headlights hurriedly swept across the surface of the water and vanished into the fog. In the thicket, an awakened bird let out a sharp cry. On the escarpment rose the city, surrounded by a halo of light. Above it spilled a second river. Wide and dark. A slick, polished moon slowly sailed onto it and glided smoothly among the clouds.

Ada knew, however, that polar bears, even the smallest ones, were among the most dangerous animals in the world. She rolled gently onto her left side and curled up amidst the excrement that covered the sheet. *He must have been doing his business in the bed all night*, she thought. *This needs to be resolved somehow in the future, buy a litter box or something like that...* The bear heaved itself up, slid to the floor, and began to pace the room, sniffing and stepping around snowdrifts.





"Get up, time for breakfast." A pale, disheveled woman sat on the other side of the bed. She pulled an infant away from her breast and laid it on the pillows. "Do you think I can leave her here?" she asked.

"Sure." Ada was glad that Kasia hadn't noticed the soiled bedding. Otherwise, she surely wouldn't have laid the baby right in the middle of the mess.

At that moment, she was convinced she was telling the truth.

They put on slippers, closed the door behind them, and set off through a dimly lit hall. The floor of unplaned boards creaked under their feet. The kitchen was narrow, dark, and lined with tomato-soup-colored tiles. A small, barred window looked out onto a fenced-in yard. It smelled of burning and rotting rags. Kasia made tea in glasses and prepared sandwiches. Only as they were returning to the room did Ada remember that the day before, the bear had eaten two puppies and the neighbor's cat. There was nothing to be done now. She opened the door. The white beast was lying on the bed, licking its muzzle. All that remained of the baby was a bloody stain on the pillow.

A desperate cry woke her. Filip-Oleńka was standing at the foot of the bed, meowing. *Kasia doesn't have children*—that was the first thought that came to her mind. She felt relief. She sat up, combed her fingers through her short, tousled hair, and petted Filip-Oleńka. Then she stood up and, stumbling over the cat who kept getting in her way, headed for the kitchen. The first cold patches of dawn were falling on the windowsill. A garbage truck pulled up outside the house; somewhere high beneath the graying sky, terns were crying. She turned on the light. The kitchen bore no resemblance to the one from her dream. A bright, clean floor of white-painted floorboards, wooden cabinets, a large square window—the whole thing designed exactly according to an IKEA catalog. Five minutes later, clean and wrapped in a towel, she was standing before her open wardrobe. She was nearing forty, but had maintained a slim figure and a light, girlish way of moving. She had grown used to the idea that she still looked like a college student. This had been true ten years earlier, but time marched inexorably forward, leaving a merciless mark on her pale, delicate face—a mark she stubbornly chose to ignore. The skin on her cheeks had slackened and sagged slightly around her chin. Her eyes, dark and watchful, had lost their former luster, giving way to an expression of cold, matter-of-fact certainty, and her wide mouth, with its strange, almost inverted shape, was bracketed by the timid parentheses of her first wrinkles. "Not now!" she raised her voice, trying to give it a firm tone, and yet she couldn't suppress a smile as she watched the animal shift from paw to paw, arch its back, and adopt increasingly ingratiating poses.





"You can't eat so much, or you'll get fat, and I don't think we want that," she said gently, and went out into the hallway. Filip-Oleńka blocked her path with a soft, stubborn trot. She pushed him aside. She put on her coat, gloves, and tall brown leather boots, grabbed her purse, and closed the door behind her.

Detective Inspector Marcin Sawicki forced his old, wheezing Opel into a sharp turn.

"Police, you dick!" he yelled at the honking driver of a Nissan he had just cut off, and slammed on the brakes on the sidewalk, right in front of a no-parking sign.

"A woman," he grumbled, struggling out of his seatbelt. "A woman," he repeated, walking down the street in a direction he had absolutely no business going. He passed a coffee bar, a hosiery shop, and a newsstand, and pulled open the door to a Starbucks. He shuddered, and then, against all common sense, ordered a double espresso and a chicken bagel. He paid eighteen zlotys and immediately felt an unpleasant pang of remorse. *Almost twenty down the drain*, he thought with horror, waiting for his coffee. *Money that can never be recovered.* He tried to calculate how much he spent a month on eating out, but stopped immediately when it occurred to him that he also had to factor in the cigarettes, beer, and chocolate bars he was addicted to. He was almost one meter ninety tall, but right now, leaning against the counter, wedging his elbow between bowls of white and brown sugar and nervously picking at a paper napkin that read *Have a nice day*, he seemed to shrink and collapse in on himself. He hunched his shoulders, stuck out his belly, and his broad, likeable face took on the miserable expression of a wronged little boy. He felt his cheeks quiver.

"Espresso-bagel, order up!" called out a pretty, dark-haired girl tied into a company apron.





He flinched, cast the barista the look of a beaten dog, and collected his order. With a tired step, he walked to a table. He hadn't slept a wink last night. He had spent long hours in front of his computer, watching episode after episode of an American TV series, masturbating during the uncensored sex scenes, hoping that the darkness outside the window would never be pierced by the pink streak of morning. But dawn had broken anyway. Not pale pink, but corpse-blue, and Sawicki had to get up and face reality.

*This is not good*, he thought, swallowing the last bites of his bagel, from which he had meticulously removed the arugula. He had nothing against women working in the police force, as secretaries, for example. Even in patrol. But a female homicide cop... A female detective... And in the terror division, to boot... *Maybe a detectivette*, he chuckled bitterly, finishing his coffee.

Detective or detectivette, Sawicki was going to have to babysit her for the next few months, until whoever up top had lost their damn mind sobered up and transferred her. He had no doubt this was a temporary situation. Nobody in their right mind actually thought a woman could be a field op... an operative. He had heard of such cases, admittedly: female homicide cops, butch women with no families, no friends, no personal lives, possessed by a mania for chasing criminals. Supposedly, they were good at it. But at Mostowski Palace, no female investigator had worked in the field for a long time. And everyone was happy with that.





It was past nine. He set down his cup. He wiped his mouth on the words *Wonderful coffee, wonderful life*, heaved himself up, and left the cafe. It was the last day of February. All of Warsaw was anxiously waiting for spring, but it was in no hurry to arrive. The pale winter sun had risen above the rooftops, reflecting off the snow in a thousand sparkling glints like a thousand painful needles. Ruffled pigeons settled on the plastic roofs of tram stops, cooing and mincing in place, following every passing car with a dull gaze. Rickety, leafless saplings stood in disciplined rows along the sidewalks. A crowd bundled up in down jackets and wool hats—which were worn in a particularly idiotic fashion this season, pushed halfway back off the forehead—trudged across Bank Square. A few people paced nervously in front of the reception desk: a young man in a short military jacket with a terrifyingly skinny girl with dyed-pink hair, an elderly gentleman, and two men in work overalls. "Good morning. It's so cold out, isn't it?" Ms. Dagmara shook her teased blonde mane from behind her desk and the green fern resting on it. "Un-bear-a-ble."

The secretary shot him a warm smile, sniffled, and went back to staring into space. He looked at the blue elephant-shaped wall clock hanging just above her head. He was nearly twenty minutes late; that woman must already be waiting. *Or maybe she didn't show up at all?* the thought crossed his mind. *Maybe she quit, or got transferred somewhere else, like Krakow, or Olsztyn, or Białystok? "No," she replied, turning her head with a cool, contented slowness.

She stood up, grabbed her purse, and vanished before he could reply.

A stunned Sawicki stared blankly at the wall. Was that it? All over? The die was cast? The whole conversation hadn't even lasted five minutes. He had been defeated, pinned to the mat with one gentle gesture by this tiny woman who had the appearance of an aging teenager. He ran a hand over his face.





Ada Rochniewicz had worked for eight years in the Wrocław police. He had received this information a month earlier, along with the announcement that from the beginning of March he would have a female partner, a female cop, a female detective, neither one thing nor another, and absolutely no means of appealing his superiors' decision. He knew little else about her.

In early February, after a talk with the Chief, Rogowski had returned to their shared office and sat down at his desk, coughing and sniffling.

"A chick? Are you sure?" Sawicki had asked, not taking his eyes off his computer.

"A chick, yeah. The Old Man is working on softening our image. Our political correctness will shoot up when we take her in."

He swiveled in his chair. Sawicki knew Róg was glancing at him nervously, but he stubbornly kept tapping at his keyboard. He had a lot of work; he didn't feel like talking. Rogowski snorted and loudly blew his nose into his plaid handkerchief. Sawicki stuck out his lower lip.

"What?" he finally asked, hitting "Delete" instead of "Tab" and erasing a painstakingly filled-out field in a report.

"You're going to take care of her," Rogowski explained, immediately succumbing to the dry, hacking cough of a chain smoker.

Sawicki was dumbstruck. For a moment he stared at his monitor, then slammed his open palm onto the desktop.

"Are you fucking kidding me?! Don't I have enough work without having to babysit some useless chick?" he snapped. "This was your idea, wasn't it?" He raised his voice more and more as Rogowski's coughing fit grew. "Get it out of your bald head."

He was seriously furious. Rogowski was the deputy chief, but everyone knew he informally acted as the head of the section. He made most of the decisions. Sawicki jumped up and stormed out of the room.

The next day, when his anger had subsided slightly, they managed to have a calm conversation.





"It's not that bad," Rogowski explained in a comforting tone. "I saw her file. She finished officer training. And before that, nursing school... Supposedly she's a good investigator. She solved a few major cases; you'll be happy."

Sawicki shrugged.

"Eight years in the Wrocław police," Róg continued, undeterred. "Glowing letters of recommendation. A commendation for taking part in dismantling the BAF organized crime group," he recited like a poem at a May Third Constitution Day assembly.

Sawicki recalled hearing something about that once. Seven people were arrested and forty kilos of cocaine secured before it could be smuggled across the German border.

"But..." the deputy chief added less confidently, "in twenty-ten she was transferred to the analysis unit... desk work."

Sawicki raised his eyebrows in surprise. A desk bitch? Why was she being seconded back to homicide right now? After two years of warming a seat and pushing paper, you don't just go back out on the streets...

"That doesn't add up," he said.

"I know. Unless..."

"Unless some higher-up really wanted to transfer Adela Rochniewicz, the nurse-detective, to Warsaw and pulled the right strings," he finished angrily. "She have a husband? Kids?"

"The 'marital status' box was blank."

"Why does that not surprise me?" he muttered.

They didn't talk about her anymore until yesterday, when Rogowski told him to show up in the briefing room at nine the next morning and take charge of the new detective.

Sawicki sighed, stretched his arms out in front of him, interlaced his fingers, and cracked his knuckles, then slowly stood up and went down to reception to buy a coffee from the vending machine. The men in overalls were dozing on wooden benches against the wall, the guy in the military jacket was swearing loudly, and the undernourished girl was trying to calm him down. A large blue sign above the counter read: "Reception." The duty officer was still nowhere in sight. Heading back upstairs, he picked up some forms to fill out from the secretariat and decided he would spend today exclusively on paperwork.





**3**

On Friday morning, Sawicki woke up almost rested. He had slept for seven hours. He got up a few minutes after six. He got dressed quietly and limited his morning routine to brushing his teeth. He wanted to drink his coffee alone, in peace. He put the kettle on. The thermometer outside the kitchen window read zero. Clouds obscured the sky. Heavy, wet snow began to fall. At seven, he woke his wife and daughters. Just before eight, he arrived at work. Complete calm reigned in the reception area, not a trace of the general public. The duty officer was dozing, his head resting on his hand. Upstairs, the door to the secretariat was closed. Walking down the corridor, he wondered if Adela Rochniewicz was already waiting for him. He had no doubt that punctuality was one of her traits. *Punctuality, order, common sense*, he thought, taking off his jacket and looking around the room uncertainly. Stacks of paper towered on both desks. A pile of clothes of unknown origin and sweaty old shoes languished in the wardrobe. A potted plant was dying on the windowsill. Algae had taken up residence in the water cooler. He wiped the dust off the sill with his sleeve, cleared the papers off the desk that had belonged to Rogowski until now, and carefully peeked into the drawer. He found a rotting sandwich in a plastic bag and an empty vodka bottle.

Ada arrived at three past eight. She gave his hand a short, masculine shake. She took off her pea coat and a fancy hat with a colorful pattern. *Where does she get the cash for clothes like that?* he wondered. *For little sweaters, wool shorts, and elegant boots like that? She's wearing about two police salaries.* Observing the new detective out of the corner of his eye, he was uncomfortably reminded of his own wife's cheap, brightly colored clothes.

"Let's go, I'll introduce you to the boys," he offered a strained smile.





He wanted to get it over with as quickly as possible. He felt uneasy leading her down the corridor. He opened door after door. He repeated: "This is Detective Adela Rochniewicz, she's working with us starting today." Ada offered brief, polite greetings. Her face wore a cool, official expression. Not once did she show any interest. She ignored the low whistles and snickers behind her back. When they entered the largest room on the floor, several cops looked up in surprise. They seemed taken aback. Ada fit in at the precinct like the Queen of England in a baseball team's locker room.

"Well, he sure found himself a shrinking violet..." muttered Cabbage, a blond-haired cop with a pockmarked, unhealthy complexion and a red forehead. He said it quietly, as if speaking only to himself, but everyone heard him perfectly. Muffled giggles broke out across the room. Sawicki clenched his jaw. A shrinking violet? Where did that moron even learn words like that? Ada's face remained impassive.

In the secretariat, Ms. Dagmara pulled doughnuts from the fridge and threw herself into boiling the electric kettle.

"I knew you were coming today, and I'm prepared," she babbled. "You have to sit for a minute and have a coffee with me. You just have to come in here often! Sawicki is such a grump," she added, completely ignoring Marcin. "It's so hard working with men anyway. But you know that well enough..."

Dark mascara had smudged around her left eye. Heavy breasts swayed beneath her pink blouse. She hadn't even managed to get the glasses out when Rogowski's round, bald head appeared in the doorway.





"We've got a homicide reported in Upper Mokotów," he said. "Seems some young chick got her head bashed in. You two will head over there. Cabbage and Chlorine are interrogating the car smuggling guys. Wiktor and I are taking the Old Man to Praga."

"But she just got here..." Sawicki muttered reluctantly.

"All the better, let her dive right in. I don't know how it is in Wrocław, but around here we don't start the day by eating pastries."

"A girl with a bashed-in head?" he tried to object. "Can't the patrol guys handle that? We have enough of our own work."

"They're already there." Rogowski let out an ugly cough. "The patrol from the second precinct and the paramedics are there. And Modzelewski," he added pointedly, before reaching out a plump hand, snatching a doughnut, and vanishing out the door.

Ada raised her eyebrows.

"Modzelewski is the prosecutor," Dagmara explained hastily. "He always calls in the metro division. For every murder. He must be on duty today."

"And we're just out of luck," Sawicki finished.

It was almost nine by the time they left the station. The snow had stopped falling; a pale, distant sun shone through the clouds. A layer of ice covered the inner courtyard, with trickles of brown water running across it. Slipping and sliding, they made it to the Opel in silence and set off toward Mokotów. The morning rush hour had just ended. They didn't turn on the sirens. Within fifteen minutes, they were at the scene. They parked outside a pre-war, four-story tenement and walked through a narrow gate into the courtyard. A pair of ruffled crows watched them from a leafless tree. Sawicki pushed open the stairwell door, which was propped open with a brick. Ada felt the skin prickle on the back of her neck. A cold shiver ran down her spine. It suddenly hit her that she was starting a homicide investigation. She was back in the field. Two years had passed since the accident that had upended her stable life in Wrocław. Two years spent sidelined, walled off by resentment and a lack of trust. Now she was coming back to work. She could once again do what she was best suited for and what she was truly good at. The long-forgotten feeling of excitement returned, a moment of focus, and finally a sudden, invigorating surge of energy, similar to what a wrestler experiences right before stepping onto the mat. Ada took a deep breath, slipped past Sawicki, and headed up the stairs first.





Waiting on the landing between the second and third floors were two identical-looking men dressed in matching leather jackets and identical wool trousers, creased down the middle. Both were smoking, raising their cigarettes to their lips with the same quick, nervous motion. Both took short drags and loudly exhaled the smoke through half-open mouths. Ada felt as if someone had placed a mirror on the landing.

"These are the famous Mokotów Twins." Sawicki laughed, seeing her bewilderment. "Detective Rochniewicz," he introduced Ada.

"Hey," they answered simultaneously.

She nodded.

"So you're taking over the case." The twin on the left rubbed his hands together with satisfaction.

"Modzelewski already took off. You're supposed to drop the paperwork by his office tonight," said the one standing on the right. "Paramedics were here too. Pronounced her dead. The guys from the morgue will show up in forty minutes. At least, that's what they say."

"We didn't call the meat wagon," they added in unison, and began zipping up their jackets.

At that moment, the sound of sobbing reached them. Sitting in front of an open door on the third floor was a pale, disheveled girl in an unbuttoned coat. Right next to her, leaning back against the wall, stood a uniformed police officer, flipping through a morning tabloid. The crying clearly made absolutely no impression on him.

"Ah... that's Monika Lewicka. The deceased's friend," explained the right twin.

"She's the one who called it in," added the left.

Sawicki nodded.

"Well, we're out of here. Have fun."

They turned as if on command and jogged down the stairs. The uniformed officer started after them.

"Hold on a second." Ada stopped him mid-step. "Please buy this lady some tea," she gestured with her chin toward the sobbing girl. "In the cafe downstairs."





The cop stopped in surprise.

"Is there something wrong with you?" Ada raised her voice.

"Wrong with me?" He bugged out his eyes and twitched his mustache nervously.

"Yes, something wrong," she repeated sharply. "You've been standing here for an hour reading the paper. And the witness is about to faint on me. Have you lost all human decency?!"

The cop violently reddened.

"Tea?" he stammered. "With my own money?"

Ada opened her wallet.

"Here." She took out ten zlotys.

He recoiled indignantly and opened his mouth to protest, but snapped it shut immediately upon seeing her furrowed brows. He sighed, shuffled his feet like a scolded dog, and reluctantly took the money.

"And one more thing!" she called after him.

"Yeah?"

"Bring it back in twenty minutes, no sooner. And make sure it's hot. With sugar," she added emphatically.

He nodded obediently, checked his watch, and went down the stairs.

Sawicki was so amused by the sight of the mustache-twitching cop fleeing in panic that he bit his lip to keep from bursting into laughter.

"Monika Lewicka?" he addressed the crying girl. "You're the one who called the police?"

"Yes..." she replied, unsuccessfully stifling a sob. "She's inside, in the bathroom..." she added with effort, raising her head and brushing the hair from her swollen face.

He pushed the ajar door open. Right past the threshold, he stepped over a puddle of vomit.

"I'm sorry," she mumbled. "That was me..."





Ada took a large step, crossed the threshold, and went in first. Sawicki followed her. She stopped in the bathroom doorway. He looked over her shoulder, flinched, and lightly touched her back. She turned her head. Their eyes met for a moment. Lying on her stomach on the tiled floor was a dead woman. Her arms and legs were splayed out limply, as if she had fallen from a great height. Blood had crusted in her long hair. Ada squatted down and leaned over the body. He didn't notice the slightest hesitation in her movements. She pulled on a pair of silicone gloves. *Does she just carry those around in her coat pocket?* the thought crossed his mind. She gently parted the tangled hair and felt the back of the skull. There was something alien and surreal in the sight of these two women: the dead one, and the living one—who was just as detached as the corpse lying at her feet. This wasn't Sawicki's first time examining a crime scene. He was used to seeing mangled bodies. And yet, he had expected something more from his new partner than a stone face and a pair of white gloves. What exactly? Tears? Wringing her hands? He knew she was an experienced officer, after all... She stood up slowly.

"We should probably call the CSIs," she said calmly.





Ada immediately sensed Sawicki's tension. For a moment, something akin to sympathy blossomed in her for this big, strong man who was clearly apprehensive about her reaction. She didn't want to make him uncomfortable. She took the gloves from her pocket and crouched over the body. This was a young girl, in her twenties. Slim, athletic. Ada noted a muscular calf. There wasn't much blood. It had flowed from a wound on the temple. The skull in that spot was deformed and nastily caved in. Whoever delivered the blow had hit very hard. She gently pushed aside the tangled hair. The wide-open eyes had clouded over and filmed with a yellow membrane. The lips and the wings of the nose had taken on a livid, brownish hue. She lifted the edge of the sweatshirt to assess the livor mortis. She stood up.

"She's been dead for at least four hours," she stated, and was about to neatly step around Sawicki, but he stopped her and pointed at the floor. Right next to the toilet lay an abandoned object.

"The murder weapon..."

"Yeah," she nodded. "The killer left it."

For a while they stared blankly at the bloody hammer. A clear stamp was visible on the wooden handle: "AM-NAT Hardware Store."

"That's very strange," she finally spoke.

Sawicki flinched and pulled his cell phone from his pocket.





While he called the forensics team, Ada inspected the pleasant, large bedroom: clean floor, neatly made bed, an armchair with a folded tartan blanket on it. She smiled involuntarily. She liked order. She instinctively straightened a black-and-white print that hung slightly askew on the wall. Light drapes hung in the window. There were no sheer curtains. She moved to the kitchen: smooth white tiles, a wide teak table, a sink with a pull-out faucet, a dishwasher, an electric grill... Everything breathed a comfortable, middle-class atmosphere. She had read recently about wealthy Varsovians buying up apartments in rundown pre-war tenements. Praga and Old Mokotów had become trendy. On the table sat a porcelain teapot painted with forget-me-nots, two cups, and a plate of cookies.

She returned to the hallway. Through the open door, she saw Sawicki leaning against the metal railing on the landing, questioning Monika Lewicka.

"Police," he said toward the gathering crowd of curious neighbors. "Please return to your homes and do not interfere with the investigation. If we need your help, we'll come to you," he added in a tone so sharp and commanding that the stairs emptied immediately.

She headed toward the next room.

"Marcin!" she called out suddenly. "Come here. I think you'll want to see this."

The entire furnishing of the small room consisted of a desk, a laptop hooked up to a large monitor on it, a desk chair, and an old dresser. All the drawers were pulled out. Papers, folders, and cardboard boxes lay gutted on the floor. Sawicki's eyes went wide.

"This couldn't have been a simple robbery," he said, surprised. "No signs of forced entry. The computer and tablet were left on the desk. Together they're easily worth a good few grand."

"The other rooms are in perfect order," Ada agreed.





"Looks like someone was looking for something specific and knew where to find it... They probably had their own key and caught our victim by surprise in the bathroom."

"I don't think so. I think she let him in herself. Anyway, she was expecting someone. There are two empty cups and some cookies in the kitchen."

The CSIs arrived a moment later. Two serious men in protective suits, silicone gloves, and blue plastic shoe covers unrolled police tape and marched into the apartment. They placed number tents next to the body, the abandoned hammer, and the blood smears on the bathtub, and got to work securing evidence. The camera flash went off continuously. Ada stood motionless in the bedroom doorway.

"You need to step out now," said the chubby-faced tech. "We'll be done in an hour."

Downstairs, a door slammed. Approaching footsteps could be heard. Clambering up the stairs was a small man in a white lab coat poking out from under a short down jacket. He gave off a strange, unsettling vibe. His head and hands were disproportionately large compared to the rest of his body; he stopped right on the borderline of dwarfism, reaching barely five feet tall. Ada caught the distinct smell of formaldehyde and male sweat. She shrank back in involuntary disgust. The little man peered into the apartment and, without a word to anyone, marched briskly straight into the bathroom.

"That's the Creep from the morgue," Sawicki whispered. "Better not engage with him, he's not a pleasant guy..."

Five minutes later, the little man appeared on the landing.





"No two ways about it, someone bashed her in the head," he said, casting quick, nervous glances from beneath bristling eyebrows. "No way she did it to herself." He let out a high, unpleasant laugh. "This was no accident," he added, bending the fingers of his left hand back so far that the joints popped. "She's been dead for at least four hours. That's all I can say for now. I don't have time to mess around with you guys right now," he continued, struggling to catch his breath. "I got three stiffs waiting in line for a dance down on Oczki Street. Call the meat wagon and check in with me on Monday."

He left without saying goodbye to anyone. An awkward silence fell. Sawicki searched for his phone. He finally found it in the inside pocket of his jacket and dialed the number for the mortuary transport.

Monika Lewicka was still sitting on the stairs. She had stopped sobbing, but she was very pale and shaking as if gripped by a fever. Sawicki touched her shoulder.

"I'm sorry, but we have to ask you a few more questions," he said, and Ada detected a gentler tone in his voice. "The first few hours after discovering a murder are the most critical..."

"I understand," she nodded, and began to cry again.
