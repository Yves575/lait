To Loss.

And also – to the birds.




The Wild Cherry


Dunaj is howling. And they are already here, just as I remember them – hungry, restless. They gleam as if their wings were doused in petrol; violet, blue, red, and yellow shimmer on them.

The flock circles above the village.

June has let in a lot of sun; even in its early days, the fruit of the wild cherry trees is sweet.

Grandfather, who avoids grandmother’s illness, to whom the ceremonies surrounding the illness are alien—he, who wants to know nothing of the illness, touch nothing belonging to the illness—watches from the veranda as the starlings lower their flight over the orchard.

He strikes an aluminum milk churn with a metal rod. It sends a boom resounding through the valley.

I watch him. It must be booming inside him too, his body trembles, his skin is like the skin of the men inhabiting the Carpathian Arc, I think. Bronze. Ebony.

The starlings take flight, a swarm of them. Fluttering over the house, they cast a shadow on the floor of the west room, the one heated by a cast-iron stove in the cold months.

After a moment, they return over the orchard.

They are stubborn.

Further strikes on the metal send them into nervous twitches. The booming keeps them in flight.

Grandfather takes a cigarette break. They take advantage of the silence and land in the spreading tree. They stuff their small bodies with fruit.

They have already learned to recognize silence, I say to Ann.

Yes, she agrees.







Ann is brightness here. She is interested in light and is light herself. She arrived here this morning, straight into a warm May, from a distant country. The journey is still in her. There is still a scent from elsewhere in her—freesia, patchouli. She stands with me on the veranda, letting her eyes adjust to the early summer.

Her eyes follow the light.

Ann knows light.

She studies light.

She hunts light.

She divines from light.

I touch her neckline with my fingers, there, where constellations of moles grow.

Ann says: They must be counted.

Alright, I answer.

In my childhood, I used to count grandmother’s birthmarks. She hid little from me. She let me count the tiny dots that covered her back, as well as those around her breasts. She would let my gaze into crevices like the whorls of her ears or the crooks of her elbows.

You are of my body too, she would say.

We move from the veranda to the west room. Grandfather’s melody can be heard. Grandmother watches the shadow of the flock of birds. She raises a gaunt hand, as if volunteering to answer a question.

There, she says, and points to the wardrobe.

She asks me to take out her summer dresses. This surprises me. Every day she wears the pajamas I buy for her at the market in the nearby town, Stary Sad. She likes the ones in powder mint.

I lay the dresses out on the sofa bed.

This one, whispers grandmother.

She has chosen the blood-colored one. I take it in my hands. It’s velvet.

Take it to him, she asks.

I do so without a word of protest.







On the veranda, grandfather finishes smoking another Klubowe—out of sentiment for the past, this is what he calls all cigarettes, a habit I picked up from him. He takes the dress in his hands. His rough skin catches on the fabric. He smells the cloth.

Róża, he says.

I look at him. The last few years have dried out his body. More and more often he averts his eyes, hides his gaze, uses it to evade. He cannot hold or return a look. I would like to see him stronger, I think.

He turns away and says: Come with me.

I follow.

He heads toward the workshop.

Once there, he nails hazel branches together into something resembling a cross and dresses this cross in grandmother’s blood-red dress. He inspects his handiwork. He adjusts the little sponge shoulder pads, which give the object an expression of surprise, as if something alive were raising its arms.

Will it do? he asks.

It’ll do, I answer.

I know he needs my agreement. Here, in this illness, there is no room for opposition.

Grandfather nods at my nodding. He takes the effigy in his hands and walks into the orchard with the dress-clad skeleton. A procession follows him: Dunaj the dog and Klakier the tomcat.

Grandmother watches us from the window of the west room. With her nails, she claws open the skin on her cheek. I know this reflex. It is born in her whenever she observes something closely. She can scratch herself all over and know nothing of the scratching.

Grandfather leans a ladder against the trunk of the wild cherry. I assist. He carefully places his feet on the rungs. He reaches the top. He fastens the scarecrow to a branch with wire.

A scare-hunger, I mutter under my breath.







Grandmother’s red dress begins to move in the wind. A gust enters it and fills the space where a warm body used to be. I get the impression that the dress has come alive.

The spirit of the orchard, I say to Ann, who joins us.

Nothing works like a red dress, she hums.

The starlings circle above the tree.

Back in the west room, I ask grandmother if she remembers wearing that dress last spring. There are photographs of it.

She nods.

The red highlighted her gray hair.

You know, she says, the illness must have been inside me even then. Can you imagine?

I nod.

With my fingertips, I examine the wound she scratched into her cheek.

For the rest of the day, we take turns going to the window of the west room and looking out into the orchard. The blood red of the dress blends with the wild cherries ripening in full sun. We will gather the fruit, salvaged from the starlings’ beaks, for grandmother. Grandfather will make sweet jams from them.





The West Room


We are freshening up the room located in the western part of the house for the illness. In my childhood, it served us for years as a spacious cold room. We stored the meat of pigs slaughtered for church holidays in there. My grandparents kept furniture here, old paintings, jars filled with preserves, jugs of honeydew honey, cardboard boxes full of Christmas decorations. After the tragedy, the one from many years ago that befell my parents and my aunt, grandmother made her den here.







Soon we’ll put in radiator ribs here, grandfather says, standing on the threshold of the room.

Yes, I nod.

Grandmother mustn’t get cold in her illness, he says.

Yes, yes, I nod.

I can accept the cold, but get this pink out of here, grandmother says.

Yes, yes, I nod.

Pink reminds me of meat, she says.

Yes, I nod.

Make it white in here, she asks.

I nod.

I am obedient; that is why I returned here from afar, that is why I left my life in the eternally bright city—to be obedient.

Obedience, I say to Ann.

Obedience, she repeats, and runs her hand through my hair.





The Deal


We start freshening up the room. As if the illness demanded special measures and actions from us.

We are cooperating with it, dealing with it, I think.

It already has us too, I say to Ann, handing her the dungarees I used to wear here in my teens.

The two of us work together. The illness binds us in physical labor. We need to exhaust ourselves.

Grandmother’s eyes are watchful.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us and watch, watch.

Let’s work ourselves to death, I say.

Grandmother peeks into the room every so often, nods her head, and watches, watches. She likes that against the white walls, the plants and holy pictures will be clearly visible.

Make it white in here, she asks again.

The instructions are simple – white.

We strip the old layers of paint with scrapers. We hand each other the tool in moments when pain touches the arms and forearms of the one working. I feel dust in my mouth; it grinds between my teeth.







I hold the ladder on which Ann stands. She wrestles with the wall. Her arms are bare; the fine dust settles on them. You can clearly see her muscles working. She is beautiful when she makes those movements with the scraper. She removes the layers of paint that have been applied here over the last thirty years. You can read a lot from them. Recorded in them are the grand occasions that demanded the room be freshened up: baptism, communion, confirmation, and now illness. I recognize each of the colors being stripped away.

Perhaps if one were to examine them closely, one could read more, just as environmental changes are read from a soil profile.

The history of this house falls to the floor.

We are making room for the new.

Ann is beautiful when she tenses her body, sets her thoughts in motion, talks while doing so—when everything that she is goes into motion.

We need what is bright, I say to Ann, mixing the paint.

Yes, she agrees.

We wallow in the white.

At night, while the room dries, I go inside to look at the interior.

Hoo-hoo, hoo-hoo, I hoot.

Behind me, I hear Ann.

Hoo-hoo, hoo-hoo, she repeats.

The sounds vibrate within these dozen or so square meters.

We wheeze from the dust. We have it in our throats. We spit white.

In the morning, we show grandmother the room.

White, she says.

She presses her cool hands to our faces and strokes us, just as she strokes Dunaj when he comes home after days of running wild.

Grandmother starts bringing plants into the west room.

Life, life, she says.



After two pots of calathea, she grows weak. She sits on the floor, leans against the wall. Wordlessly, we take over her work; we become her hands. We carry the plants—which had been put in the vestibule for the duration of the renovation—into the west room: aloe, asparagus fern, fern, geranium, Siberian ivy, Christmas cacti, philodendron, croton, dracaena, begonia, caladium.

Grandmother’s eyes are watchful.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us.

Grandmother’s eyes watch, watch.









Hunchbacked


When the smell of paint is gone, we spend the night in the west room, the three of us. We don’t yet know that it will stay this way, that the following nights will be shared. But that is not yet. Right now, the moon is waxing.

There it is, waxing, hunchbacked, I think.

I am getting used to the closeness.

We share the bed and the sofa bed.

We strip off our daytime layers of clothes.

We mix our scents.

My women, I think as I fall asleep, are beautiful, and they smell sweet, they smell sweet.





Throat


In the room, you can hear the breathing of grandmother and Ann. The night has stilled our pupils, which had been darting around during the day. Ever since the illness moved in with us, I am the last to fall asleep.

Only my watch is strong, I think. No one else can keep guard this long.

In the corner of the room, the cast-iron stove releases heat. The last days of June have brought a chill here, to the valley.

It’s about to begin.

Light.







Light slices our faces. I look at Ann, whose face is illuminated by the headlights of a truck carrying cattle to the slaughterhouse. She opens her eyes. And hers are deep, bottomless. They examine the waking world, and when they notice my looking, they pause.

Ann keeps watch with me for a moment.

However, she cannot keep guard like I do.

She slips into sleep.

And here, oh, it begins. A few dozen meters from the west room, large trucks take the corner, their headlights filling the interior with light.

Light and dark.

Light and dark.

Light.

Dark.

Light.

This will last a million years.

I have time, I think.

The trucks are bringing animals to the slaughterhouse. I imagine the bodies of the cows – warm, damp.

In my childhood, I didn’t always manage to stay awake until this moment of the night. Sometimes I would fall asleep and be woken by the spectacle already underway. It was accompanied by sounds like today’s: guttural, drawn-out sounds which, instead of dissipating in space, can hang over the village for a long time.

The bellowing of cows.

The whining of dogs.

The hooting of the night.

And also this—the smell: of dung, of urine, of sweat.

The bodies of my women are clearly visible when illuminated by the slow sweeps of the Jelcz trucks' headlights. Their features are—depending on the falling light and shadows—at times dead, at times alive.

Grandmother is lying on her side, her hand tucked under her left cheek. She looks like a child. A child, the kind they had wanted me to bear.







But now is no time for life. Death—that is what occupies my mind. That is what I serve. Grandmother is my child. I am my grandmother’s mother. This is good, I think.

Ann’s body is nearby. The porcelain of her skin, the whiteness of her hair, eyelashes, and eyebrows never cease to amaze me. There is something twin-like about us, and there is also in us—and we know this—something that binds us: much that is prodigal.

The bodies of the women dear to me react to the light; their eyeballs dart beneath their eyelids.

Just a moment more.

They open their eyes.

One after the other.

Grandmother looks and whispers into the light: Water or fire will take care of this.

Ann, awakened once more, places her bare feet on the floorboards, her toenails painted a dark burgundy. Just like me. We paint them for ourselves, no longer for our people, who are far away now. They must be far away; I do not allow them to get close to the illness. I belong to the illness, not to them. There is only room for Ann here. She is capable of plunging nails painted like this into dung or compost, of wallowing in it.

Sisters in decay, I think of us.

Ann pulls a pack of Klubowe from the pocket of grandfather’s coat, which she has thrown over herself.

Come, she says to me.

I throw a blanket over myself and go. Grandmother watches the lights racing across the ceiling.

It’s possible, I think, that she has fallen asleep again, but left her gaze in the waking world. She is most terrifying when she looks in such a way that you can’t tell who her eyes belong to.

We step out onto the veranda. I feel the chill.

A concert, I say to Ann.







She hands me a lit cigarette.

It’s starting, I say.

Yes, she agrees.

Oh, here it is.

We listen.

The hooves know no rhythm, I say.

After a moment, the animals begin to sing.

They are singing, I say.

Ann places a finger to my lips, and with this gesture, traps the cigarette smoke inside me.

The animals are singing. Their song comes from the inside, from their warm bellies, I think.

After a moment, the dogs sound off. First, Rybowicz’s wolfspitz beneath the escarpment on the other side of the village; then the throats of the slaughterhouse dogs get going; a moment later Dunaj joins in, followed by the village headwoman’s Samoyeds and the dogs tied to kennels at the rectory, descendants of those restless mutts that have crossbred for generations through the ravines of the village. They bear the names of the apostles: Mark, John, Luke. There is also one black dog there, which the village baptized Blackie.

How strange, I think, I can distinguish these howls, the melody of each dog. A little while longer here and I will be guessing what these howls are for, what purpose they serve.

Ann takes her finger away from my lips.







I say: You know, I always wanted to be the bitch of this village, to have the whole of May all to myself. To know no borders. I was tempted to be far from what is human. To have a dog’s body and act in accordance with it, to succumb to hunger, to deny myself nothing. To fawn only over the chosen few, to nip and bark at everything else. To be a picky bitch. To love one human, to have duties toward him, to obey him. And also: to disappear for many days. To have the right to do so, to indulge.

Indulge, Ann repeats.

The barking carries and wakes up more sounds.

The whole village is howling.

Tonight, this place is the deep throat of a hungry animal, I think.





Faith


At night, the slaughterhouse wakes the birds. This carries consequences for the valley. Time gets jumbled. Something in time comes loose. People open their eyes at the wrong time, walk around dazed during the day. At noon, they look at the sun, and its position in the sky doesn't seem right to them.

Things get the most muddled for grandfather. Grandfather gets up with the birds. The waking world takes him by surprise.

I wake up and it’s empty, he tells me about such times.

Empty? I ask.

Empty, yes, there is nothing to grab onto, nothing to recognize oneself in, he says.







He gets up in the night and walks around the house. He clambers up the ladder to the attic and paces there. Taking steps calms him. He is also calmed by stroking the antlers he has been collecting for decades. When grandfather confuses night and day, he can fall asleep up there.

In the mornings, when I go to take my place at the desk to work on code—clean, intuitive, beautiful code, the kind I sometimes dream about—I often find him among the stag crowns. Grandfather has a story for every set of antlers. He invents lives for these bone tissues. For him to tell such a story, you have to coax him, you have to praise the collection in the attic. The stories about the deer are full of animal panic, of the forest; they take place in rain and in fog. Only out of such weather do the animals emerge in grandfather’s stories.





Rivers


Waking the birds is a sin, grandmother says.

In the orchard belonging to the slaughterhouse, nothing has borne fruit for years. The earth there is trampled by herds of cows. Most of the animals are unloaded at the top of the hill and led straight into the concrete walls. The ones for which there is no room in the cold pens are let into the orchard. They take advantage of this, emptying their deep bellies, pissing as if they held endless rivers inside them. Urine in such quantities scorches the earth; trees find it hard to grow here.

The orchard is bare, stunted, sick.

It’s from the dampness out of the insides of the cows, I say.

Fire or water will take care of it, grandmother says.

I believe her.







The greengage trees in that orchard haven’t borne fruit for years, she says.

I nod.





Sweet


Grandmother cannot resist the living. Anything like that, she would take into the west room, invite it in. She would wish for the living to walk before her eyes, to move, to scrabble, to flutter.

Let it parade, she says.

Let it fly, she says.

Let it be, she says.

Grandmother has adored the living forever, that is, for as long as I can remember. One could, I think, trace the generations of what has multiplied here, in the western part of the house. And if one were to ask these latest generations, the living ones, where they are from, they would probably say, if they could talk, that they are from the cold room of a human, a little human, of Róża.

She wishes, I think, for the living, especially now, to occupy her mind, to breed obligations, to distract her from the illness.

Grandmother studies the living with curiosity. This passion—as I call it—this weakness—as grandfather calls it—has rendered her useless so many times.

It was scary taking Róża out to the field, grandfather begins, reminding me, all at once, of those childhood days.
