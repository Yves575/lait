To Berenice





There are thirty-two of them. Thirty-two still living in the abbey on this autumn day in 1986, at the end of a road enough to make anyone who travels it pale. In a thousand years, nothing has changed. Neither the steepness of the road nor its dizzying heights. Thirty-two strong hearts—as you must be when living perched on the edge of the void—thirty-two bodies that were strong, too, in their youth. In a few hours, they will be one fewer.

The brothers form a circle around the one who is leaving. There have been many circles, many farewells, since the Sacra raised its walls above them. There have been many moments of grace, of doubt, of bodies braced against the coming shadow. There have been and there will be other departures; so they wait patiently.

This dying man is not like the rest. He is the only one in this place who has never taken vows. Yet, he has been allowed to stay for forty years. Whenever there was a debate, whenever questions were raised, a man in purple robes would arrive—never the same one—to settle the matter. He is staying. He belongs to this place, as surely as the cloister, its columns, and its Romanesque capitals, whose state of preservation owes much to his talent. So let us not complain; he pays for his stay in kind.





Only his fists stick out from the brown wool blanket, on either side of his head—an eighty-two-year-old child in the grip of a nightmare. His skin is yellow, at the breaking point, vellum stretched over overly sharp angles. His forehead is shiny, waxed by a greasy fever. His strength had to fail him one day. A pity he never answered their questions. A man is entitled to his secrets.

Besides, they have the impression they know. Not everything, but the essentials. Sometimes, opinions differ. To relieve the boredom, they discover a passion for gossip. He is a criminal, a defrocked priest, a political refugee. Some say he is held against his will—the theory doesn’t hold up, he’s been seen leaving and coming back—others claim he is here for his own safety. And then there is the most popular version, and the most secret, for romanticism is only smuggled in here: he is here to watch over her. She who waits, in her marble night, a few hundred meters from this little cell. She who has been waiting patiently for forty years. All the monks of the Sacra have seen her once. All would like to see her again. They would only need to ask Padre Vincenzo, the superior, for permission, but few dare to do so. Out of fear, perhaps, of the impious thoughts that come, it is said, to those who approach her too closely. And the monks have quite enough impious thoughts as it is, when pursued, in the heart of darkness, by angel-faced dreams.





The dying man struggles, opens his eyes, closes them again. One of the brothers swears he saw joy in them—he is wrong. A cool cloth is placed gently on his forehead, on his lips.

The sick man stirs again and for once, everyone agrees.

He is trying to say something.





Of course I’m trying to say something. I’ve seen man fly, faster and faster, farther and farther. I’ve seen two wars, nations collapse, I’ve picked oranges on Sunset Boulevard—don’t you think I have something to tell? Forgive me, I am ungrateful. You clothed me, you fed me when you had nothing, or so little, when I decided to hide among you. But I have kept quiet for too long. Close the shutters, the light hurts my eyes.

He’s stirring. Close the shutters, brother, it seems the light is bothering him.

The shadows watching over me, backlit against a Piedmont sun, the voices becoming muffled as sleep approaches. It all happened so fast. Barely a week ago they could still see me in the vegetable garden, or up a ladder; there was always something to repair. Slowed by age, true, but given that no one would have bet on me at birth, it was enough to command admiration. And then one morning, I couldn’t get up. I read in their eyes that it was my turn, that the death knell would soon be rung and I would be carried to the little garden facing the mountain, where poppies grow over centuries of abbots, illuminators, cantors, and sacristans.

He is fading fast.





The shutters creak. Forty years I’ve been here, they’ve always creaked. Darkness, at last. Darkness like in the cinema—which I saw born. An empty horizon, at first nothing. A blinding plain that, by dint of staring at it, my memory populates with shadows, with silhouettes that become cities, forests, men, and beasts. They step forward, take the front of the stage, my actors. I recognize a few of them; they haven’t changed. Sublime and ridiculous, cast in the same crucible, inseparable. The currency of tragedy is a rare alloy of gold and tinsel.

It’s only a matter of hours now.

A matter of hours? Don’t make me laugh. I’ve been dead for a long time.

Another cool compress. He seems to be calming down.

But since when can the dead not tell their story?





*Il Francese*. I always hated that nickname, even though I’ve been given far worse. All my joys, all my tragedies are from Italy. I come from a land where beauty is always cornered. Let it fall asleep for five minutes, and ugliness will mercilessly slit its throat. Geniuses sprout here like weeds. We sing the way we kill, we draw the way we deceive, we let dogs piss on church walls. It isn’t for nothing that an Italian, Mercalli, gave his name to a scale of destruction, that of earthquake intensity. One hand demolishes what the other has built, and the emotion is the same.

Italy, kingdom of marble and filth. My country.





But it is a fact: I was born in France in 1904. My parents had left Liguria in search of fortune fifteen years earlier, barely married. By way of fortune, they had been called *Ritals*, spat upon, mocked for the way they rolled their r’s—yet, as far as I know, the word *roll* does indeed begin with an r. My father had narrowly escaped the racist riots of Aigues-Mortes in 1893; two of his friends had perished there: brave Luciano and old Salvatore. They were never again spoken of without those adjectives.

Some families forbade their children from speaking the language of the old country, so as not to "sound *Rital*." They scrubbed them raw with Marseille soap in the hope of whitening them up a bit. Not the Vitalianis. We spoke Italian, ate Italian. We thought Italian, which meant with bursts of superlatives where Death was often invoked, tears abundant, hands rarely at rest. We cursed the way others passed the salt. Our family was a circus, and we were proud of it.





In 1914, the French state, which had shown so little zeal in protecting Luciano, Salvatore, and the others, declared that my father was without a shadow of a doubt a good Frenchman, worthy of conscription, especially since a civil servant had, by mistake or as a joke, shaved ten years off his age when copying his birth certificate. He left with a long face, without a flower in his rifle. His own father had lost his life in the Expedition of the Thousand in 1860. Nonno Carlo had conquered Sicily with Garibaldi. It wasn’t a Bourbon bullet that killed him, but a prostitute in the port of Marsala with questionable hygiene, a detail the family preferred to pass over in silence. But he was indeed dead, and the message was clear: war killed.





It killed my father. A gendarme showed up one day at the workshop above which we lived in the Maurienne valley. My mother opened up every day on the off chance of an order her husband could fulfill upon his return; he would have to go back to carving stone one day or another, restoring gargoyles, carving fountains. The gendarme put on the appropriate expression, looked even more apologetic when he saw me, coughed, explained that there had been a shell, and, well, that was that. When my mother, very dignified, asked him when the body would be repatriated, he stammered, explained that there had been horses on the battlefield, other soldiers, that a shell does a lot of damage, and that, as a result, you didn’t always know who was who, or even what was man and what was horse. My mother thought he was going to cry, had to offer him a glass of Amaro Braulio—I never saw a Frenchman drink it without grimacing—and didn’t cry herself until many hours later.

Of course, I don’t remember all of this, or remember it poorly. I know the facts, I restore them with a bit of color, these colors that are now slipping through my fingers in the cell I have occupied for forty years on Mount Pirchiriano. Even today—at least, a few days ago, when I was still capable of it—I speak French poorly. I haven’t been called *Francese* since 1946.





A few days after the gendarme’s visit, my mother explained that in France, she wouldn’t be able to offer me the education I needed. Already her belly was swelling with a brother or sister—who was never born, or at least not alive—and she smothered me in kisses, explaining that she was sending me away for my own good, sending me back to the old country because she believed in me, because she saw my love for stone despite my young age, because she knew I was destined for great things, and that she had given me a name for just that reason.





Of the two burdens in my life, my first name was undoubtedly the lighter one to bear. Yet I hated it with a passion.

My mother often went down to the workshop to watch her husband work. She realized she was pregnant when she felt me start at the strike of a chisel. She had not spared her efforts up to that moment, had helped my father move enormous blocks, which perhaps explains what followed.

"He will be a sculptor," she announced.

My father grumbled, replied that it was a filthy trade where hands, backs, and eyes wore out much faster than stone, and that unless you were Michelangelo, you might as well spare yourself the trouble.

My mother nodded, and decided to give me a head start.

My name is Michelangelo Vitaliani.









I discovered my country in October 1916, in the company of a drunkard and a butterfly. The drunkard had known my father and had avoided conscription thanks to the state of his liver, but the turn of events suggested his cirrhosis wouldn’t protect him for much longer. They were drafting kids, old men, cripples. The newspapers claimed we were winning the game, that the Boche would soon be ancient history. In our community, the news of Italy joining the Allies the previous year had been welcomed like a promise of victory. Those who returned from the front sang a different tune, for those who still felt like singing. *L’ingeniere* Carmone, who, like the other *Ritals*, had scraped salt at Aigues-Mortes, then opened a grocery store in Savoy, where he consumed a good portion of his own wine stock, had therefore decided to go back. If he was going to die, he might as well do it in the old country, lips stained red with Montepulciano to wash down the fear.

His own country was the Abruzzo region. He was a kind man, and agreed to drop me off at Zio Alberto’s on the way. He did it because he took a little pity on me, and also, I believe, because of my mother’s eyes. Mothers’ eyes are often quite something, but mine had irises of a strange blue, almost violet. They had started more than one fistfight, until my father put everything in order. A stonemason has dangerous hands, and I won't be the one to tell you otherwise. The competition quickly bowed out.





My mother shed big violet tears on the station platform in Modane. My uncle Alberto, a sculptor too, was going to take care of me. She swore she would join me soon, as soon as she’d sold the workshop and earned a little money. A matter of a few weeks, a few months at most—it took her twenty years. The train huffed, spitting out black smoke the taste of which I can still sense, and carried away the tipsy *ingeniere* and her only son.

Whatever anyone says, at twelve years old, sadness doesn’t last very long. I didn’t know what this train was swaying toward, but I knew I had never taken a train before—or at least I didn’t remember it. Excitement quickly gave way to discomfort. Everything was moving too fast. As soon as I focused on a detail—a fir tree, a house—it immediately disappeared. A landscape is not meant to move. I felt unwell, wanted to confide in the *ingeniere*, but he was snoring with his mouth open.





Fortunately, there was the butterfly. It flew in at Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne, and settled on the window, between me and the passing mountains. After a brief struggle against the glass, it gave up and stayed still. It wasn’t a beautiful butterfly, one of those glories of color and gold I would see later in the spring. Just a mediocre, gray butterfly, a little bluish if you squinted hard, a moth dazed by the daylight. I thought for a moment of torturing it, like all boys my age, then realized that by staring at it, the only tranquil element in a furious world, my nausea was fading. The butterfly stayed there for hours, sent by some friendly power to reassure me, and this was perhaps my very first inkling that nothing is ever truly what it seems to be, that a butterfly is not just a butterfly but a story, something enormous lurking in a very small space, which the first atomic bomb would confirm a few decades later and, perhaps even more so, what I am leaving behind as I die in the foundations of the country’s most beautiful abbey.





When *l’ingeniere* Carmone woke up, he detailed his project to me, because he had one. He was a communist. "Do you know what that is?" I had heard the insult several times in the community, back in France; people were always wondering if so-and-so was one. I replied: "Pfft, of course, it’s a man who loves men."

The *ingeniere* burst out laughing. In a way, yes, a communist was a man who loved men. "Besides, there is no wrong way to love men, do you understand that?" I had never seen him so serious.





The Carmone family owned a plot of land in the province of L’Aquila, to which geography had dealt two injustices. One, it was the only province in Abruzzo without access to the sea. Two, earthquakes ravaged it at regular intervals, just like the Liguria of my ancestors, except that that bitch Liguria did have access to the sea.

His land offered a pleasant view of Lake Scanno. The *ingeniere* planned to build a tower mounted on giant ball bearings and house the local proletarians in it, all for a modest rent that would allow him to make a decent living—especially since, like a good communist, he was reserving the top floor for himself. Thanks to two teams of horses working in twelve-hour shifts, the building would revolve on its axis throughout the day. Its inhabitants would thus enjoy, without exception, without profiteers or exploited classes, a view of the lake once a day. Perhaps electricity would one day replace the horses, even though Carmone confessed it would probably never reach that far. But he liked to dream.

The ball bearings would also have the advantage, if an earthquake occurred, of decoupling the structure from the ground. In the event of a degree XII earthquake on the Mercalli scale—he was the one who taught me the name—his building had a thirty percent greater chance of surviving than a normal building. Thirty percent doesn’t seem like much just like that, but since degree XII wasn’t exactly a laughing matter, he explained, widening his eyes, it was enormous.

I surrendered to a half-sleep, eyes glued to my butterfly, and we crossed into Italy while the *ingeniere* spoke to me tenderly of devastation.









Italy and I embraced like old friends at our first meeting. In my haste to get off the train at the Turin station, I tripped on the step and landed with my arms outstretched on the platform. I lay there a moment, not even thinking of crying, with the beatitude of a priest at his ordination. Italy smelled of flint. Italy smelled of war.

The *ingeniere* decided to take a carriage. It was more expensive than walking, but my mother had entrusted him with some money in an envelope and, just as wine was meant to be drunk, he declared, money was meant to be spent; besides, let’s go buy ourselves a quarter-liter of red from the Po valley before we hit the road, if you don’t mind.

I minded quite well, marveling at what I was discovering: soldiers on leave, soldiers shipping out, porters, train conductors, and a whole crowd of shady characters whose functions or ambitions seemed mysterious to the kid I was. I had never seen shady characters in my life. I had the impression they were kindly returning my insistent stares, as if to say *you’re one of us*. Maybe they were just staring at the blue bump growing smack in the middle of my forehead. I walked forward through a forest of legs, blissfully entranced by other smells: creosote and leather, metal and cannons, perfumes of twilight and battlefields. And there was the noise, the din of a forge. It creaked, squealed, clanged—*musique concrète* played by illiterates, a far cry from the concert halls where blasé dignitaries would one day flock to pretend to appreciate it.





I was arriving, without knowing it, right in the middle of Futurism. The world was nothing but speed—the speed of footsteps, of trains, of bullets, of shifting fortunes or alliances. Yet all these men, all this mass seemed to be braking with all their might. Their bodies exulted, rushing toward the train cars, the trenches, a barbed-wire horizon. But something, between two movements, two bursts of momentum, was screaming *I want to live a little longer*.

Later, when my career took off, a collector proudly showed me his latest acquisition: the Futurist painting *The Revolt* by Luigi Russolo. It was in Rome, at the turn of the thirties, I think. The man considered himself an enlightened connoisseur, passionate about abstract art. He was an idiot. Unless you were present that day at the Porta Nuova station, you cannot understand this work. No one can understand that there is nothing abstract about it. It is a figurative painting. Russolo painted what was exploding right in our faces.

No twelve-year-old formulates it in these terms, obviously. At the time, I just looked around, wide-eyed, while the *ingeniere* quenched his thirst in a cheap dive at the end of the platform. But I saw all of that. A sign, if any more was needed, that I wasn’t quite like everyone else.









We left the station in light snow. We had barely stepped outside when a *carabiniere* intercepted us and asked to see my papers. Not my companion’s, just mine. His fingers numb from the cold and the little red wine from the Po, *l’ingeniere* Carmone handed him my pass. The officer looked at me suspiciously, a look he must have put on every morning to go to work and hung up at night, unless he was born with it.

"Are you a little *Francese*?"

I didn’t like being called a "Frenchman." I liked being called "little" even less.

"Little *Francese* yourself, *cazzino*."

The *carabiniere* nearly choked; *cazzino* was the favorite insult in the courtyards where I grew up, and *carabinieri* didn’t choose a profession where you get to wear such handsome uniforms just to have the size of their manhood insulted.

Like the good engineer he was, the *ingeniere* pulled my mother’s envelope from his pocket and greased the gears that had seized up. We were soon able to set off again. I refused to get in a carriage, pointed to a tram. Carmone grumbled, consulted a map, asked a few questions, and determined that the tram wouldn’t drop us off too far from where we needed to go.

My backside on a wooden bench, I traversed the first big city of my life. I was happy. I had lost my father, I didn’t know when I would see my mother again, but yes, I was happy and intoxicated by everything still ahead of me, this mass of future to scale, to carve to my own measure.

"Say, Signor Carmone?"

"Yes?"

"What is electricity?"





He stared at me in bewilderment, then seemed to remember that I had spent the first decade of my life in a Savoyard village I’d never left.

"That's it, my boy."

He pointed to a streetlamp topped with a beautiful golden globe.

"So it’s like a candle?"

"But one that never goes out. It’s electrons flowing between two pieces of carbon."

"What’s an electron? Some kind of fairy?"

"No, it’s science."

"What’s science?"

The snowflakes swirled, as light as a girl’s dress. The *ingeniere* answered my questions without impatience or condescension. We soon passed a massive building under construction: the Lingotto, where a few years later, Fiat cars would drive up a helical ramp to the roof, where they would take their first spin after assembly—a mechanical Sacra di San Michele. The suburbs thinned out, roads gave way to dirt tracks, and the tram stopped in what looked like a field. We had to walk the last three kilometers. I am grateful to that guy, Carmone, for walking me so far, despite the cold, despite the times. We walked in the mud, and I imagined that my mother’s eyes were already beginning to fade in his memory, seeming less violet to him. But he guided me unerringly all the way to Zio Alberto’s door.





We had to abuse the bell and pound on the door several times before Alberto deigned to open it, wearing a dirty undershirt. The same cloudy eyes as the *ingeniere*, cracked with little red veins: the two men shared an immoderate love of the grape. My mother had written to announce my arrival, so there wasn’t much to explain.

"Here is your new apprentice, Michelangelo, son of Antonella Vitaliani. Your nephew."

"I don’t like being called Michelangelo."

Zio Alberto looked down at me. I thought he was going to ask what I preferred to be called, to which I would have answered "Mimo." The nickname my parents had always given me, the nickname I would still be called for another seventy years.

"I don’t want him," Alberto said.

Once again, I had forgotten a detail. Because yes, it is a detail.

"I don’t understand. I thought Antonell... that Mrs. Vitaliani had written to you, and it was agreed upon."

"She wrote me. But I don’t want an apprentice like that."

"And why on earth not?"

"Because nobody told me he was a midget."









*C’è un piccolo problema*, old Rosa had commented, the neighbor who delivered my mother on a stormy night. The stove rattled, stoked by a headwind; a hellish draft made the walls glow red. A few neighborhood matrons who had come to witness the event, curious to catch a glimpse of the firm flesh that their husbands fantasized about, had long since fled, crossing themselves and muttering *il diavolo*. Old Rosa, undaunted, kept humming, sponging, encouraging. Cholera, the cold, or simply bad luck—a knife that wouldn’t have been drawn had there been less drinking—had taken her children, friends, and husbands. She was old, she was ugly, she had nothing to lose. So the devil left her alone; he knew how to recognize a source of trouble. He had easier prey.

*C’è un piccolo problema*, then, she said, pulling me from Antonella Vitaliani’s womb. It all came down to that word, *piccolo*; it was obvious to anyone looking at me that I would remain more or less *piccolo* my whole life. Rosa laid me on my exhausted mother. My father took the stairs four at a time; Rosa later recounted that he had frowned upon seeing me, looked around as if searching for something else, his real son rather than this rough draft, then nodded, *I see, that’s how it is*, just like when he struck a hidden fissure in the heart of a block of stone that shattered weeks of work. You can’t blame the stone.





To the stone, precisely, they attributed my difference. My mother hadn’t known how to rest, carrying enormous blocks around the workshop that would have put the local strongmen to shame. Poor Mimo, if the neighbors were to be believed, had paid the price. Achondroplasia, they would say later. They would refer to me as a person of short stature, which, frankly, was no better than Zio Alberto’s "midget." People would explain to me that my height didn’t define me. If that were true, why mention my height? I’ve never heard anyone referred to as a "person of average stature."

I never resented my parents. If the stone made me what I am, if some black magic was at work, it also compensated me for what it took. Stone always spoke to me—all stones: limestone, metamorphic, even tombstones, the ones I would soon lie upon to listen to the stories of recumbent effigies.

"That's not what was planned," the *ingeniere* murmured, tapping his lips with a gloved finger. "This is unfortunate."

It was snowing hard now. Zio Alberto shrugged and tried to slam the door in our faces. The *ingeniere* blocked it with his foot. He pulled my mother’s envelope from the inside pocket of his old fur coat and held it out to my uncle. Inside was nearly all the Vitalianis’ savings. Years of exile, of toil, of skin burned by sun and salt, of starting over; years of marble under the fingernails, with occasionally an ounce of that tenderness which had seen me born. That was why those dirty, crumpled bills were so precious. That was why Zio Alberto reopened the door a little.





"This sum was for the little guy. I mean, Mimo," he corrected, blushing. "If Mimo agrees to give it to you, he would no longer be an apprentice, but a partner."

Zio Alberto nodded slowly.

"Hmm, a partner."

He was still hesitating. Carmone waited as long as possible, then sighed and pulled a leather pouch from his pack. Everything about the *ingeniere* celebrated wear and tear, patchwork, an aesthetic of passing time. But the leather of the pouch, new and supple, still seemed to quiver with the hot temper of the beast it had once clothed. Carmone ran a cracked glove over it, opened it, and reluctantly pulled out a pipe.

"This is a pipe I acquired at great expense. Carved from a briar burl upon which the Hero of Two Worlds, the great Garibaldi himself, is said to have sat during his noble and fruitless attempt to rally Rome to our beautiful kingdom."





I had seen dozens of these pipes, sold in Aigues-Mortes to those foolish Frenchmen. I didn’t know how this one had ended up in Carmone’s hands, how he had let himself be taken in. I felt a little ashamed for him and for Italy in general. He was a naive, generous man. The gesture cost him, and I know he did it sincerely, to help me, not because he was in a hurry to get home or dreaded being saddled with a twelve-year-old boy of unusual proportions. Alberto accepted, and they sealed the deal with a shot of booze whose sourness stung the air inside the hovel. Then Carmone stood up—one last little drink for the road—and soon his unsteady silhouette faded away into the snow.

He turned back one last time, hand raised in the yellow phosphorescence of a dying world, and smiled at me. The Abruzzo region was far away, he was no longer young, the times were harsh. I never went to Lake Scanno later, for fear of discovering that there wasn’t, and never had been, a tower mounted on ball bearings there.





I owe a lot to so-called "fallen" women, and my uncle Alberto was the son of one of them. A brave girl who lay beneath men at the port of Genoa, without anger or shame. She was the only person my uncle spoke of with respect, a fervor bordering on veneration. But the saint of the alleys was far away. And since Alberto could neither read nor write, his mother grew, with each passing day, more and more mythological. For my part, I wrote rather well, which my uncle, when he realized it, was thrilled about.





My uncle Alberto was not my uncle. We didn’t share a single drop of blood. I never managed to completely clear up the matter, but his grandfather apparently owed a debt to mine, an unpaid loan whose moral burden was passed down from generation to generation. In his own perverse way, Alberto was honest. Solicited by my mother, he had agreed to take me in. He owned a small workshop in the outskirts of Turin. Since he was a bachelor and cared little for luxury, a few commissions here and there were enough to meet his needs, or had been enough until the moment I arrived. Because the war—an enterprise of progress touted by many zealots at the time, who, incidentally, didn’t like the term "zealot" and preferred "poet" or "philosopher"—the war, then, had popularized materials cheaper than stone: lighter, easier to produce and to work with. Steel was Alberto’s worst enemy; he cursed it even in his sleep. He hated it even more than the Austro-Hungarians or the Germans. For a *Crucco*, as Krauts were called down there, one could still find extenuating circumstances. Their food, their ridiculous spiked helmets—they had reason to be angry. Whereas one had no business building with steel, and he who laughed last would laugh best when it all collapsed. Alberto hadn’t understood that everything had already collapsed. To his credit, steel had a hand in it; it had made fine cannons.





Alberto looked old, but he wasn’t. At thirty-five, he lived alone in a room adjoining his workshop. His bachelorhood was surprising, especially since after a shower, washed clean of marble dust and dressed in his only suit, he didn’t look half bad. He always patronized the same brothel in Turin, where he treated the girls with legendary respect. The expression "going about it like Alberto" was popular in the early twenties in the southern neighborhoods of the city, between Lingotto and San Salvario, before fading away when Alberto moved, taking his marbles and his slave—meaning me—with him. Partner, it still makes me laugh.

I’ve been asked a lot what role he played in what came next. If by "what came next" people mean my career, none. If, however, they mean my final work, a few fragments of him are undoubtedly included in it. No, not sparks of him, fragments—I wouldn’t want anyone suspecting he had ever shined. Zio Alberto was a bastard. Not a monster, just a pathetic loser, which amounts to the same thing. I think back on him without hatred, but without sorrow.





For nearly a year, I lived in this man’s shadow. I cooked, I cleaned. I hauled, I delivered. A hundred times I was almost run over by a tram, trampled by a horse, or beaten up by a guy who had mocked my height, to whom I had replied that at least my height wasn’t a problem *al piano di sotto*, down below, preferably in front of his girlfriend. *L’ingeniere* Carmone would have been delighted to find the atmosphere so electric in our neighborhood. Every interaction was a potential lightning strike, a displacement of electrons whose outcome was never certain. We were at war against the Germans, the Austro-Hungarians, our governments, our neighbors—which is to say we were at war with ourselves. One wanted war, the other wanted peace, voices would be raised, and the one who wanted peace ended up throwing the first punch.

Zio Alberto forbade me from touching his tools. He caught me once correcting a small holy water stoup commissioned by the neighboring parish of Beata Vergine delle Grazie. Alberto got monumentally plastered once or twice a week, and the latest bender had left its mark. The stoup was crude, insulting; a twelve-year-old could have done better, and a twelve-year-old did, while the other man was sleeping off his wine. Alberto woke up and caught me red-handed, chisel in hand. He examined my work in bewilderment, then beat me black and blue while hurling insults at me in a language I didn’t understand, a Genoese dialect. Then he went back to sleep. When he opened his eyes again and found me crippled with pain and covered in bruises, he pretended not to know what had happened. He went straight to his stoup, remarked that he wasn’t unhappy with his work, and magnanimously offered to deliver it himself.





Alberto regularly dictated a letter to his mother, and allowed me to write one to mine at the same time—he generously paid for the stamp. Antonella didn’t always reply, constantly on the road, hunting for the job that would let her hold out for another week, then another. I missed her violet eyes. My father, the man who had guided my first clumsy strokes, the man who had taught me the difference between a tooth chisel, a riffler, and a drove chisel, was fading away.

Work grew scarcer over the course of 1917, Alberto’s moods grew darker, his drinking binges more violent. Columns of soldiers sometimes marched past against a backdrop of twilight; the newspapers spoke only of it, the war, the war, but we sensed only a vague unease from it, a feeling of dissociation from our surroundings, a sense of never being in the right place. Out there, a foul beast was battering the horizon. But we led an almost normal existence, a shirker’s life that lent a faint taste of guilt to everything we ate. At least until August 22, when bread ran out, and there was nothing left to eat at all. Turin exploded. The name of Lenin appeared on the city walls, barricades went up, a revolutionary even stopped me in the street on the morning of the 24th to warn me to be careful, that their barricades were electrified, which proved to me more surely than anything else that the world was changing. The guy called me "comrade" and patted me on the back. I saw women confronting sheepish soldiers on the barricades, climbing on armored cars, baring furious, conquering breasts at which the soldiers dared not shoot. Not right away, at any rate.





The revolt lasted three days. No one could agree on anything, except that we had had enough of the war. The government finally got everyone to agree with machine guns; fifty dead cooled people’s zeal. I holed up in the workshop. One evening, as calm had barely returned, along with a little bread, Zio Alberto came home in a more cheerful mood than usual. He pretended to throw a slap at me, chuckled when he saw me dive under the table, then ordered me to take up a pen and dictated a letter to his mother. He smelled of the cheap vinasse served at the corner tavern.

Mammina,

I received the money order you sent me. Thanks to it, I’ll be able to buy the little workshop that I talked to you about at Christmas. It’s in Liguria, so closer to you. There ain’t no work left in Turin. But over there, they got a castle that always needs fixing, and a church that the authorities care a lot about, so that means work. I sold this place, for cheap but whatever, I just signed with that rat Lorenzo, and I’m leaving soon with that little shit Mimo. I’ll write you from Pietra d’Alba, your loving son.

"And give me a nice signature, *pezzo di merda*," Zio Alberto concluded. "One that shows I’ve been successful."





When I think back on that time, it’s strange: I wasn’t unhappy. I was alone, I had nothing and no one, they were churning up forests in northern Europe, sowing them with metal-laced flesh, plus a few shells that would explode years later in the faces of innocent strollers; they were inventing a desolation that would have made Mercalli turn pale, he who had given his poor scale a mere twelve degrees. But I wasn’t unhappy; I realized this every evening, when I prayed to a personal pantheon of idols that changed throughout my life and even included, later on, opera singers and soccer players. Perhaps because I was young, my days were beautiful. Only today do I measure how much the beauty of the day owes to the foreknowledge of the night.





The abbot leaves his office and begins the descent via the Stairway of the Dead, aptly named. In a few moments, he will go to the bedside of the man dying in the annex. The brothers have sent word to him that the hour is near. He will place the bread of life upon his lips.





Padre Vincenzo crosses the church without paying attention to its frescoes, passes through the Doorway of the Zodiac, and emerges onto the terraces at the summit of Mount Pirchiriano, from where the abbey looks down over Piedmont. Before him lie the ruins of a tower. Legend has it that a young peasant girl, beautiful Alda, once took flight from it to escape enemy soldiers, aided by Saint Michael. *Vanitas vanitatis*, she wanted to repeat the feat in front of the villagers, just to impress them, and crashed to her death below. As would, in the fourteenth century, a part of the tower bearing her name, brought down by one of the numerous earthquakes that regularly shake the region.

Further on, a few steps sink into the ground, blocked by a chain and a "No Entry" sign. The abbot steps over it with an agility commendable for his age. This is not the way to the annex where the dying man awaits him. Before joining him, the priest wants to see *her*. She who sometimes gives him an uneasy sleep, for he dreads an intrusion, or worse. You never know what might happen, like that time fifteen years ago when Fra Bartolomeo had caught someone right in front of the final grate protecting her. The man, an American, had tried to pass himself off as a lost visitor. The abbot had immediately sniffed out the lie; he knew its scent by heart, it was the smell of the confessionals. No tourist could wander so deeply into the bedrock of the Sacra di San Michele by accident. No, the man was there because he had heard the rumor.





The abbot had been right. Five years later, the same man had returned with formal authorization signed by a Vatican bigwig. They had therefore opened the door for him, and the list of those who had laid eyes upon her had grown a little longer. Leonard B. Williams was the name of this professor from Stanford University in California. Williams had devoted his life to the captive of the Sacra, trying to pierce her mystery. He had published a monograph on her, a few articles, then silence. His work, though brilliant, lay dormant on forgotten shelves. The Vatican had played its hand well, opening that door as if they had nothing to hide. For years, calm had returned. But in recent months, the monks had been reporting tourists who were nothing of the sort—snoopers. You could spot them a mile away. The pressure was mounting again.

For long minutes, the abbot descends, navigating the maze of corridors unerringly. He could find his way in the dark, he has walked it so often. A jingling of little bells accompanies him—the sound of the ring of keys in his hand. These blasted keys. There is one for every door in the abbey, sometimes two, as if behind every last door a mystery were palpitating. You’d think the mystery that brings them all there, the Eucharist, wasn’t enough.





He is nearing his goal. Smells the earth, the damp, the scent of billions of granite atoms crushed by their own weight, and even a little of the greenness from the surrounding slopes. The grate, at last. The old one has been replaced; it is now fitted with a five-point lock. The remote control doesn’t work on the first try; Padre Vincenzo jabs relentlessly at its rubber buttons. It’s the same every time—talk about progress, it’s 1986 and they can’t even produce a remote control that works? He catches himself: *Lord, forgive my impatience.*

The red light finally goes out; the alarm is deactivated. The final corridor is monitored by two state-of-the-art cameras, no bigger than shoeboxes. It is impossible to enter without alerting someone. And even if an intruder managed it, what would be the point? He wouldn’t walk out with her. It had taken ten men to carry her down.

Padre Vincenzo shudders. It’s not theft they need to fear. He hasn’t forgotten that lunatic Laszlo Toth. He chides himself again; "lunatic" isn’t charitable—let’s say "unbalanced." They had narrowly avoided tragedy. But he doesn’t want to think about Laszlo right now, about the Hungarian’s sinister face and wild-eyed stare. Tragedy had been averted.





They lock her up to protect her. The irony does not escape the abbot. *She’s in there, don’t worry, she’s doing wonderfully, except no one is allowed to see her.* No one but him, the Padre, the monks who request it, the rare cardinals who locked her down there forty years ago and are still alive, and probably a few bureaucrats too. Thirty people in the whole world, at most. And of course her creator, who had his own key. He came at will to tend to her and wash her regularly. Because, yes, she needs to be washed.

The abbot opens the last two locks. He always starts with the top one, a tic that betrays, perhaps, a form of nervousness. He would like to break the habit and promises himself—just as he did on his last visit—to start with the bottom one next time. The door opens in silence—the locksmith who boasted about the quality of the hinges did not lie.





He doesn’t turn on the light. The original neon tubes were replaced at the same time as the grate by a softer lighting, and just as well; the neons were too harsh on her. But he prefers to see her in the dark. The abbot steps forward, touches her with his fingertips, out of habit. She is slightly taller than he is. In the center of a round room, a primitive sanctuary with Romanesque vaults, she stands slightly curved on her pedestal, lost in a dream of stone. The only light comes from the corridor, outlining two faces, the bend of a wrist. The abbot knows every detail of the statue sleeping in the shadows, having scrutinized her until his eyes ached.

They lock her up to protect her.

The abbot suspects that those who put her there were trying to protect themselves.





The city of Savona had given Italy two popes, Sixtus IV and Julius II. Pietra d’Alba, barely thirty kilometers to the north, almost gave her a third. I believe I am somewhat responsible for that failure.

I would have laughed out loud if someone had told me, on that morning of December 10, 1917, that the history of the papacy would be altered by the kid dragging his feet behind Zio Alberto. We had been traveling for three days, almost without stopping. The entire country was hanging on news from the front, following the thrashing the Austro-Hungarians had handed us at Caporetto. Word was that the positions had stabilized not far from Venice. Word was also the opposite: that the enemy was going to turn up and slit our throats in our sleep or, worse, force us to eat cabbage.

Pietra d’Alba appeared, carved out in the morning light, upon its rocky peak. Its position, as I realized an hour later, was an illusion. Pietra wasn’t perched on a spur but sitting on the edge of a plateau. Right on the edge, meaning that between the village wall and the edge of the cliff, there was a passage barely wide enough for two men abreast. Then fifty meters of empty space, or rather of pure air, heavy with the scents of resin and thyme.

You had to cross the entire village to discover what had made its reputation: a vast plateau undulating toward Piedmont, a piece of Tuscany moved there by the whims of geology. To the west as to the east, Liguria kept watch, reminding it not to get too comfortable. These were the mountains, slopes covered by a forest of a green almost as black as the beasts that prowled within it. Pietra d’Alba was beautiful with its slightly pink stone—thousands of dawns had embedded themselves within it.
