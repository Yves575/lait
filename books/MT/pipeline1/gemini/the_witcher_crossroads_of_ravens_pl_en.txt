Kaedwen is a land wedged between the Dragon Mountains to the north, the Blue Mountains to the east, and impassable forests to the west. Hereditary kings of the House of Topp reign there. Their original capital was in ancient Ban Ard, but in the year 1130 *post Resurrectionem*, King Dagread gifted that stronghold to the sorcerers for a school, and moved the capital to Ard Carraigh, situated in the center of the realm.

Other major strongholds of Kaedwen are Ban Fearg, Daevon, Ban Fillim, and Ban Gleann.

The coat of arms of the Topps and the entire land was, since time immemorial, a unicorn—*d'or, licorne effrayée de sable*.

Encircling the royal lands of Kaedwen lie borderlands belonging to the realm, known as marks or marches. These are entrusted to the administration of margraves—that is, counts, either hereditary or appointed by the king. The name derives from the fact that these lands serve as a sort of advance guard and, by pushing ever further, they are to wage conquest, wrest new lands for Kaedwen from the elves, push back the borders, and plant their markers—*id est*, marks—ever further afield. These marches are: the Western, Upper, Lakeland, and Lower Marches.

Chapter One


Despite his sincerest efforts—and for rather important reasons—Geralt simply could not focus on the voyt’s prattle. All his attention was consumed by a large stuffed crow on the voyt’s table. The crow, eyeing the witcher with a glass eye, stood on a base of green-painted clay, both of the crow's little legs embedded in said clay. Thus, despite its absolutely lifelike appearance, the crow could not possibly be alive; that was beyond question. Why then, Geralt marveled, had the crow already blinked at him with its glass eye several times? Could it be magic? Probably not, because his witcher medallion hadn’t twitched or vibrated, not once and not even a little bit. A hallucination, then? A delusion? Brought on, perhaps, by the fact that he had been whacked in the head a few times?

"I shall repeat the question," Voyt Bulava repeated the question. "I shall repeat it, though I am not in the habit of repeating myself."

Voyt Bulava had already assured Geralt several times that he was not in the habit of repeating himself. Despite this, he repeated himself constantly. Evidently, he liked to, even if he wasn’t in the habit.

"I will repeat my question: what was this really about? What did you have against that deserter that you hacked him up so terribly? Some old grudge? Because I simply won’t believe, you see, that it was about that peasant and his little daughter’s maidenly honor. That you supposedly rushed to the rescue. Like some shitty knight errant."

The crow blinked. Geralt shifted his hands, bound behind his back, trying to restore circulation. The rope cut painfully into his wrists. Behind his back, he could hear the heavy breathing of a village goon. The goon stood right behind him, and Geralt was certain he was only waiting for an excuse to wallop him in the ear with his fist again.

Voyt Bulava wheezed, slumped back in his chair, and pushed out his belly and his velvet doublet. Geralt stared at the front of the doublet and identified what the voyt had eaten today, yesterday, and the day before. And that at least one of those dishes had involved tomato sauce.

"I thought," the voyt said at last, "that I’d never have to lay eyes on one of you witchers. Not a single witcher has been seen in years. Folks said that after the year one hundred and ninety-four, hardly anyone made it out alive, up there in the mountains. Then word carried that the rest had died out too, from starvation or plague. And yet here we are, one of your sort shows up right in my village. And the first thing he does is commit murder. Then, caught red-handed, he has the audacity to cite some fucking edicts."

"By virtue of the named edict of the year one thousand one hundred and fifty," croaked Geralt, clearing his throat, "issued by Dagread, King of Kaedwen and the Border Marches, *primo*: witchers are permitted the free practice of their profession in the territories of the Kingdom and the Marches, and are exempt from the jurisdiction of local authorities..."

"Firstly, *primo*," Bulava interrupted sharply, "it’ll soon be half a century since Dagread turned to dust, and his personal autocratic edicts along with him. Secondly, *primo*, no king is exempting anyone from anything on my watch, because the king is in Ard Carraigh, far from here, and local authority rules here. Meaning me. And thirdly, *primo*, you weren’t arrested for practicing your profession, my friend, but for murder. Catching werewolves and slaying leshens, that’s your witcher business. But no king gave you the privilege to carve up people."

"I acted in self-defense..."

"Daryl!"

The goon obediently walloped Geralt with his fist, this time in the back of the neck.

"Your repetition," the voyt looked at the ceiling, "is irritating. Do you know what irritation can lead to? Even in a peaceful man like me?"

The crow stared with its glass eye. Geralt remained silent.

"You," Bulava finally said, "are no witcher. You are a defect. You require fixing. You ought to be returned to that mountain Settlement of yours that folks talk about. I don't know how things work up there with you lot. It's possible that a botched job like you gets dismantled into component parts, to be used in the production of new, better witchers. Because that's how it's done up there, ain't it? From various human pieces, witchers are assembled, sewn together or glued, or whatever. People say all sorts of things. So then, not to wag my tongue in vain... I am going to dispatch you, you botched witcher, back to the mountains, beyond the Gwenllech. In a week."

Geralt remained silent.

"Won't even ask why in a week?" The voyt bared his yellow teeth. "You like citing edicts and laws. Well, I'm all for the law too. And the law here in the commune is that newcomers aren't allowed to carry weapons. And you came in here with a weapon."

Geralt wanted to object that he hadn't come in, but had been dragged in. But he didn't get the chance.

"The penalty," declared Bulava, "is twenty lashes of the whip. The penalty will be administered by Daryl here, and he has a heavy hand. You won't be back on your feet for at least a week. Come on, take him. Out to the yard with him, tie him to the post..."

"Hold your horses," a man entering the room stopped the goons. He wore a dun cloak, heavily soiled at the hem. "Why in such a rush to the post and the whip, Bulava? Want to maim the witcher for me? Aye, nothing doing. I need the witcher whole and healthy at the construction site."

"And why are you," the voyt put his hands on his hips, "interfering in my executions, Blaufall? It's bad enough I have to suffer you constantly taking my village folk for corvée labor. But don't meddle in my jurisdiction; my jurisdiction is none of your business. A crime must be punished..."

"Pfft, crime," Blaufall interrupted. "There's no offense here at all; it's self-defense and rescuing folk. Don't pull faces, don't pull faces, because I have a witness here. Step forward, goodman. Come on, don't be afraid. Speak up, tell us how it was."

Geralt recognized the peasant. The very same one he had saved from robbery yesterday, who had fled into the woods instead of thanking him. The father of the little missy whom he remembered stripped to her shift.

"I testify..." the peasant groaned, pointing a finger at Geralt. "I give my word that this youth rushed to my rescue against the bandits... He saved my property... Rescued my daughter from defilement... Delivered an innocent from the bandits' clutches..."

"And that deserter," prompted Blaufall, "lunged at him with an axe, the youth was merely defending himself. Self-defense! Confirm it, goodman, that it was so."

"It was so... Exactly so! Master Voyt, this youth is innocent!" The peasant was pale and unnaturally loud. "Master Voyt! Release him, I beg you. And here... Please, take this... By way of, hmm... Perhaps there were some costs or losses... Glad to compensate..."

Bowing obsequiously, the peasant handed the voyt a small purse. Bulava quickly hid it in the pocket of his baggy trousers, so deftly that the purse didn't even clink.

"Self-defense!" he snorted. "Carved a man to ribbons with a sword. Innocent youth... I'd have him..."

They went out into the yard. The goons shoved Geralt outside, without untying his hands.

"And you, Blaufall," asked the voyt, "why in such a rush that you even dragged a witness in here? Need this witcher that badly?"

"What, like you don't know? We're laying a road, the Great Road; it's supposed to run from Ard Carraigh through the woods all the way to Hengfors. And not just any road, not some forest track, but a highway, dry and level, paved with corduroy and fascines, so wagons and teams can pass. It's an important thing, the Great Road; heavy trade will flow that way, between our lands, that is, and the North. The king himself supposedly ordered us to hurry. But there are monsters in the woods and the swamps, every now and then some worker goes missing, killed or snatched by a beast..."

"And since when do you care about workers? You always said a worker doesn't matter; if one's gone, there'll be another..."

"To hell with the workers, since most of them are corvée anyway. But sometimes a monster kills a foreman, and that disrupts the work, the whole work front goes to hell. Ah, what's the use of talking. I'm telling you, I need a witcher. I'll blow the deadline, and not only will my bonus go to the devil, but they'll send an inspection on my head. And an inspection..."

"Always finds something," Bulava nodded knowingly. "Be it materials sold under the table, inflated estimates, or..."

"Don't digress," Blaufall grimaced. "And release the witcher at once, without delay, I'm taking him with me to the site right now... Oh, bloody hell... What's going on?"

"Veterans from the watchtower." The voyt shielded his eyes with his hand. "Master Carleton's troops."

A dozen or so horsemen galloped into the yard, kicking up dust and scattering chickens. Armed. Clad colorfully, garishly, and rather raggedly. Only the two at the front were more elegant. The commander, a mustachioed man in an elk-leather jerkin, with a gilded baldric and a hat sporting a tuft of ostrich feathers. And a long-haired elf with a headband, in the green uniform of a scout.

"Captain Reisz Carleton," Bulava greeted him, stepping forward. "Welcome, welcome. To what do we owe the honor?"

Captain Reisz Carleton leaned in his saddle and spat copiously. Then he gave a sign to the scout. The elf rode up to a post with a crossbeam and deftly threw a piece of rope ending in a noose over the timber.

"Oho." Bulava put his hands on his hips and checked if his goons were standing behind him. "The captain has evidently come down to my village for a hanging. Ah, I even see whose fate it is to hang today. I see, I see, those two in bonds... Hah, so the captain has caught the deserters from his watchtower! The ones who've been assaulting the peasants and lasses in the woods around here?"

"I have no intention," Captain Reisz Carleton twirled his mustache, "of hanging those men. They'll both run the gauntlet, through a lane of switches. To teach them a lesson. And that's all. I have too few men to hang them for just anything. And to let some stray vagrant murder them with impunity."

The captain straightened up in his saddle, raising his voice, addressing not only the voyt but also the village goons, Blaufall, his lackeys, and the small crowd of peasants that was already gathering.

"What am I to punish my soldiers for? For what? For unauthorized absence? For wanting to fuck a lass? We're out here at the watchtower like it's the end of the world, like exile, like a punishment. You get no beer here, no women... Is it any wonder the lads sneak out for a bit of action now and then, grab some..."

"Because why the hell," Reisz Carleton raised his voice, "are wenches wandering around the woods? And this goodman here, what, did he have to take his daughter right through there, couldn't he have left her at home? So why be surprised the lads wanted to... I do not condone it! I do not condone it, but I understand it! Master Aelvarr? Is it ready there?"

"Ready, Captain."

"Then hand over that witcher, Bulava. He killed my soldier, he'll go to the noose. A fearsome example must be made. And don't cut him down, voyt, let him hang, as a warning to others."

Blaufall stepped forward, looking as if he wanted to speak, then thought better of it. The goons grabbed Geralt but stood uncertainly. Rightly so, as it turned out.

It suddenly grew strangely quiet. And as if something cold had blown through.

Into the yard, from behind the barns, very slowly strode a pitch-black horse. It bore a rider. White-haired, in a black leather jerkin studded with silver rivets on the shoulders. Above the rider's right shoulder jutted the hilts of two swords.

Very slowly, even gracefully, the black horse passed the peasants and the voyt. Only to stop before Captain Carleton's cavalry.

For a moment, silence reigned. Then the black horse tossed its head. The rings of its curb bit jingled.

"Voyt Bulava," spoke the white-haired rider in the silence, "will immediately release the young witcher. He will return his horse, his weapons, and his belongings. Immediately."

"Yes..." the voyt coughed. "Right away, Master Holt. Anon..."

"Captain Carleton." The rider bowed slightly. "Greetings."

"Master Witcher Preston Holt." Reisz Carleton touched the brim of his hat. "Greetings."

"The captain," the rider raised his voice, "will kindly take this elf, his rope, and the rest of his subordinates out of here. You are no longer needed here. Today's lynch mob is canceled."

"Indeed?" The captain stiffened in his saddle, resting his hand on the guard of his sword. "Are you that sure of yourself, master witcher?"

"Yes, I am that sure. Farewell. Master Voyt, is the boy free? His belongings returned?"

"Ah, you son of a bitch!" yelled one of Carleton's horsemen, ripping his sword from its scabbard and spurring his mount. "I'll..."

He didn't finish. The rider named Preston Holt raised his hand and made a short gesture. The air howled and shrieked; the peasants covered their ears. The horseman screamed, flew from his saddle as if from a catapult, crashing heavily and limply right beneath the hooves of his comrades' mounts; their horses spooked, snorted, stamped, tossed their heads, and one reared. The fallen man's horse bolted between the cottages, bucking and kicking its hind legs.

It grew very quiet.

"Anyone else?" Preston Holt raised his gloved hand. "Anyone eager to stand up to me? To play the hero? No? I thought as much. I bid you soldiers farewell. Is the young witcher already in the saddle?"

"I am," replied Geralt.

"On our way, then. Follow me."



The Upper March has set its border marker on the river Gwenllech. It is known that the ambition of the margraves there is to make further incursions into the valleys of the Dragon Mountains; therefore, they customarily refer to their domain as cismontane, as though it were soon to be enlarged by a transmontane portion, once the elves are chased deeper into the mountains. However, the years run on, and nothing of the sort occurs.

Chapter Two


The Kingdom of Kaedwen was known throughout the inhabited world for its cold and capricious weather. Shut off from the north by the barrier of the Dragon Mountains and from the east by the mighty massif of the Blue Mountains, the land suffered from unpredictable and frequent movements of air masses, resulting in long, freezing winters, cold springs, and short, rainy summers. As for autumn, it varied—sometimes sunny, warm, and pleasant, and sometimes quite the opposite.

Now, in the month of March, called Birke by the elves, snow still lingered in patches in the ravines and hollows, lying in white patches in the depressions on the clearings. Yellowing sheets of ice still covered some puddles and ditches. The little sun seemed to warm things up, but when the wind blew from the mountains, it bit with a frost no less sharp than in January.

Geralt had set out from Kaer Morhen the day before the Equinox. That was the witchers' custom. For it had been borne out by practice that monsters were at their most ravenous after the winter, and so aggressive that people in villages and settlements were willing to shell out to hire a witcher, even though they had eaten through their provisions during the lean period and were practically without means of livelihood. But Geralt hadn't had the chance to be hired. Because things turned out the way they did: barely two days' ride from the mountains, bam, a peasant and his daughter, marauders, a bald goon with rotten teeth, wham, bam, and there you are. He had found himself on trial before Voyt Bulava of the locality of Neuhold, and from said trial, and then the threat of a lynch mob at the hands of soldiers from the nearby watchtower, he had been delivered by this strange white-haired individual with two swords on his back, mounted on a black horse, in whose tracks Geralt was now following.

"I propose," the strange individual turned in his saddle, "that we ride together for a while. Captain Carleton might still want to hang you; it was obvious he wanted to very badly. He isn't stupid enough to pursue me, but you, alone, could be an easy target. Therefore, if you do not mind my company..."

"Of course not," Geralt hastily assured him, urging his dun mare forward. "Gladly... I... I am..."

"I know who you are. Did your hair turn white after the mutations? After the Changes? Loss of pigment, just like me?"

"Aye... But how do you..."

"How do I know who you are? Because I keep track of what goes on at your Keep. And rumors reached me that a certain *Wunderkind* was trained there, that they call him Geralt, and that he's due to set out on the Trail soon."

"But Vesemir..."

"He never mentioned me? The name Preston Holt never slipped from his lips? I'll explain: Vesemir and I have been moving in different orbits, so to speak, for some time now. If you catch my meaning."

Geralt didn't quite know what an orbit was, but he nodded with a sagacious expression.

They rode in silence for a while. Side by side.

"So you set out from Kaer Morhen on the Trail," Preston Holt finally spoke. "Your beginning might not have been the best, but that's how it is with beginnings. And I have no intention of reprimanding you; quite the contrary, I examined the body of that marauder, and your cuts must be acknowledged as flawless. Perhaps unnecessary, perhaps ill-considered, perhaps inelegant—but ultimately flawless."

They fell silent again, observing a herd of cattle driven out into a pasture, and a little cowherd running from cow to cow to warm his freezing feet in fresh, warm cowpats. The cowpats probably warmed him only moderately, but the running surely did.

"They're driving the cows out," noted Holt, "even though the grass is barely poking out of the ground. That's a sign the season has begun, and you'll find work without difficulty, Geralt. Villages will soon be eager to pay for the protection of their herdsmen and livestock. Let's spur the horses, over there, into the birch grove, along the bank of the leat."

"Along the bank of what?"

"The leat. This ditch, here, is a leat, a channel used in the past to drain water from the mine. We are, as you undoubtedly know, in the part of the kingdom of Kaedwen called the Upper March. The wealth of the Upper March is its minerals: primarily salt, but also silver, nickel, zinc, lead, lapis lazuli, and the like. At least, that's how it used to be; today, most of the minerals seem to be running out. Nothing lasts forever."

Geralt made no comment.

"Do you see that hill ahead of us? It's called Podkurek; that's how it appears on official maps. And the name came about because, maybe a hundred years ago, a peasant named Podkurek accidentally dug up a lump of silver there the size of a large cabbage head. A mine was established here instantly, cutting into the hillside. Large amounts of silver and galena, lead ore, were extracted here. But the deeper the miners delved, the more trouble they had with water. There are more leats like this one here; you'll see them. Eventually, the costs of drainage made the entire excavation unprofitable. The miners moved on to other areas. They left behind a labyrinth of corridors and workings, partially flooded. And now for the best part: the abandoned and flooded mine was taken over and claimed by roaming *zatrawce*. You know what *zatrawce* are, I trust?"

"*Zatrawce*," Geralt recited, taking a breath, "are small creatures resembling dog-headed monkeys. They are pack creatures, living underground, in darkness. In a pack, they are dangerous..."

"They are dangerous as hell," Preston Holt cut off the recitation. "And they quite frequently make themselves a nuisance to amateur miners, who come to dig in the slopes of Podkurek in search of the silver that can still be unearthed here. Oh, and here is an illustration of my point: what is gleaming over there are the canvas covers of wagons and tents. We are heading straight into the camp of some brave diggers. The first of this spring."

The brave diggers first greeted them with a delegation armed with shovels and cudgels. The menacing expressions of the welcomers sent a simple message: clear off, strangers, we were here first. Soon enough, however, their expressions softened—the strangers were not, as the inspection revealed, undesirable competitors. And lo, joy was painted on the faces that had been so reluctant only moments before.

"Lord above, praise the gods!" cried the chief digger, hiding behind his back the mattock he had been brandishing as a warning just a moment before. "Praise be to the gods! Lord above, it's the honorable master witcher! Word reached us that you were in the area, and we were already aiming to send for you! And in the meantime, here you are, falling right from the sky for us, right from the sky!"

"It is my custom," Preston Holt straightened up in his saddle. "To fall from the sky for those in need. For I am a witcher."

The diggers were meanwhile joined by a few lamenting women. It was getting louder and increasingly incoherent. Preston Holt, with voice and gesture, began to demand order. It took a while before it could finally be pieced together what the matter was and what the diggers and their women really wanted.

"We need a witcher, a witcher!" the head digger cried out, waving his mattock. "A lad of ours was snatched by those subterranean beasts that dwell under the mountain! They attacked, snatched him, dragged him into the abyss! Who else will deliver him now, if not you?"

"It hasn't even been two weeks," Holt said, "since I warned you to stay away from the shaft and the adit. Didn't I? To dig on the other side of the mountain? The lad, you say, was snatched by *zatrawce*? How old? Five? How long ago? Hah, the day before yesterday? You weren't exactly in a hurry to send for me. Dismount, Geralt."

Preston Holt dismounted. Geralt couldn't help but notice that dismounting wasn't easy for him. And that he limped heavily on his left leg. With a gesture, Holt shooed away the women who had besieged him with their wailing. He spoke for a moment with the senior digger, then tugged at Geralt's sleeve.

"Well, young witcher," he said. "We'll have to help people in need, harmed by monsters. For we have just been hired."

"If the *zatrawce* snatched the boy the day before yesterday," Geralt grumbled, "then there's a slim chance he's still..."

"Alive? Yes, that's doubtful. But perhaps we'll at least find... Hmm... We'll give the mother the chance to bury what's left... Whence this hesitation, young Geralt? A moment ago you threw yourself without a second thought to defend a girl's honor and carve a man to death, and now, hesitation?"

"Did I say anything?" Geralt shrugged. "Or hesitate? I'm not hesitating."



On the mountain slope survived the remains of wooden structures, likely drainage horse whims. A shaft gaped there, partially caved in, revealing the abyss.

"What's left of the mine is a shaft," Holt explained, "and an adit, over there, further along. Both lead to an upper working. The working is winding, as it follows the vein, which has been completely mined out. From there, a decline slopes down, leading to a lower working, even more winding, with numerous branches. Below that is another adit, but completely flooded. The *zatrawce* are holed up in that lower gallery. There might also be some holes there, connections with natural caves.

"The plan, therefore, is this: with my leg, I'm not going down, so I will head over to the adit, make some noise there, and draw the *zatrawce* to me. Then all hope lies with you. You will descend the shaft to the gallery, and from there down the decline. There you have a chance to find... What needs to be found. With a bit of luck. Which I wish you, my boy. See you up top."

"Maybe first," Geralt ventured, "we should scope out..."

"Do what?" Holt winced. "Ah, I understand. However, there is no point in... scoping anything out. Down your elixir, medallion in hand, and climb down the shaft."

"With all due respect," mumbled the head digger standing nearby, "but this lad is awful young... Will he manage? We figured it'd be you going down, Master Holt. In person..."

Preston Holt turned and looked at him. The digger shrank, mumbled. And did not finish.



The gallery was high; Geralt could easily walk upright and still there were a couple of spans to the ceiling. Everywhere, water dripped from the walls; besides the sound of falling drops, there was nothing else to be heard. He quickened his pace; he wanted to reach the decline as fast as possible, before Holt, true to his word, raised a ruckus at the adit.

In the walls of the working there were deep caverns, traces of excavated clusters of ore. In one such cavern, he noticed a strange brick structure, or rather the remnants of one. He couldn't have known that these were the ruins of an ancient shrine.

Miners, something else Geralt did not know, clung to belief in the gods longer than any other social group. Working in conditions of constant peril, they needed to believe that some providence watched over them and that prayers offered to it would ensure their safety. As one might easily guess, practice soon proved that prayers were of no use, that cave-ins and methane explosions happened just as frequently to the pious as to the nonbelievers. But the miners believed anyway, built shrines, lit candles, and prayed. For a long time. But not forever. Common sense, as usual, finally won out.

The corridor suddenly began to slope downward; he was already on the decline. He strained his ears, but still heard nothing save the dripping water.

He was already, it seemed, right at the lower gallery, when it began.

A large stone whistled from the darkness, grazing his hair. Others followed, some hitting their mark. One struck Geralt in the head, making him stagger, and the *zatrawce* threw themselves at him from all sides, wildly howling and barking, scratching and biting. It was impossible to count how many there were; they swarmed around him too fast. Geralt jerked his sword from its scabbard, only to lose it immediately—two creatures immobilized him, a third knocked his weapon away with a blow from a large rock. The beast raised the captured sword high and roared in triumph, opening its dog-like maw to its full width. The triumph was premature—Geralt threw off his two attackers, grabbed a stone, and hurled it, hitting the *zatrawiec* squarely in the teeth. The *zatrawiec* dropped the sword; Geralt lunged, caught the weapon before it fell, and hacked down the creature struggling with the stone wedged in its maw. With two swift cuts, he cleaved another two. And he broke into a run toward the shaft.

Behind him rose wild barking, and stones flew—again, some finding their mark. One hit him in the back of the head—a flash; a myriad of stars illuminated the working. A second stone struck him in the small of his back and almost knocked him over. Twice the *zatrawce* caught up with him, sinking their teeth into his calves; leather gaiters saved him from serious injury. One leaped and bit him painfully above the knee, where the gaiter offered no protection. Geralt did not slow down to kill it.

Under a hail of stones, he reached the shaft, scrambled up the remnants of the ladder, surfaced, collapsed there, and lay still. For some time.

"Well, well," he heard. "You made it out. And even without any major injuries. Blood, I see, is trickling from a few spots. But it's not gushing from anywhere, which is something. I am full of admiration."

Preston Holt stood over him, gnawing on a roast chicken leg.

"Oh, come on," Geralt groaned, still lying on the ground. "Come on! You were supposed to be at the adit... Make some noise... To draw them off..."

"Really?" Holt tossed the bone away. "Ah, yes. Forgive me, I completely forgot."

Geralt swore. With his mouth still to the ground.

"I'll tell you something else." Holt licked his fingers. "They won't pay us a thing. Because the boy was found. He showed up. He had simply been wandering off somewhere, and the diggers, as usual, blamed the *zatrawce*. Get up, young Geralt. Witcher Geralt. Allow me, I'll help you. Can you walk? Let's go then. As I said, there will be no payout. But they will feed us and put us up for the night. The girls will dress your scrapes. If you ask nicely, maybe one of them will be sweet to you."

They walked toward the encampment and the smoking cauldrons. Witcher Geralt took his steps with difficulty.

Chubby girls bandaged him up in a few places. And fed him. And the diggers let them spend the night. Holt in a tent, Geralt on a wagon.

One of the girls came to Geralt in the night and was sweet to him. But only sweet, just a little bit and nothing more. And right after that, she left.



At dawn, Geralt clambered out of the wagon and began to saddle his mare, still hissing in pain. Holt caught him in the act.

"Why the rush?" He rubbed an eye. "Wait. They will treat us to breakfast, then we'll move on."

"Oh, come on," Geralt hissed through his teeth. "What if I'm not entirely sure I want to head anywhere with you? Maybe I prefer to set off on my own?"

Holt leaned against the trunk of a birch, observed the sky. Clear, without a single cloud.

"I fully understand," he said, "your attitude. But I had to—placing the emphasis on *had to*—give you a preliminary examination, see what you're like in action."

"I might not have made it out of there."

"But you did."

"No thanks to your help. That is why now..."

"I ask you," Holt interrupted, "to accompany me on horseback at least until noon. Which translates, roughly speaking and calculating the time of day, to five or six leagues. That time and that distance will be enough, I think, for you to vent your anger at me and look at the world more soberly. And I will then have a proposition for you."

"What sort of," Geralt narrowed his eyes, "proposition?"

"Six leagues. At noon."



It was indeed near noon when the sky suddenly went black with wings, and resounded with the flapping of feathers and one massive cawing. Dozens, if not hundreds, of black birds took flight from the ground and the branches of the surrounding trees and flew off.

"Ravens," Geralt sighed. "So many ravens! It can't be! Ravens don't fly in swarms! Never!"

"Undoubtedly," Holt admitted. "So many ravens at once is an unprecedented thing; I am surprised myself. We are undoubtedly dealing with an unusual phenomenon. And we also find ourselves in a rather unusual place. If you've noticed."

"A parting of the ways." Geralt looked around. "Meeting roads."

"A parting of the ways. A symbolic place. Four roads, to the four points of the compass. A place of choice and decision. Which you will now have to make, Geralt. Witcher Geralt."

The ravens settled on the upper branches of the trees. They cawed, watching the riders.

"Three of the roads, including the one we're on, are your paths as a lone witcher; this is the fate you chose when you set out on the Trail from Kaer Morhen. If you take any of these three roads, if you make that decision, we will part ways. If, however, you choose the fourth road, you will hear my proposition."

The ravens cawed.

"I am, as you've surely noticed, up in years. I will add that I am well up in years. You would be surprised how well. I am also—and this is also apparent and cannot be hidden—a cripple. My days of witcher glory are a song of the past. I shall not take to the Trail with my swords again; the radiance of my blades, if I may put it that way, will no longer dispel the darkness. But the darkness exists; monsters still go bump in the night. You can face them and defeat them. People threatened by monsters look for your help.

"True, I'm waxing poetic here, and you're waiting for the proposition. Here it is: I propose we work together. I was known here in Kaedwen and still am; I never complained of a lack of clientele, and even now people ask me for help that I can no longer provide. But you can. I have observed you, and I say: be my successor, Geralt. Instead of wandering the highways hungry, come live with me. Benefit from my reputation, and you will not lack for work. And after work, you will have a place to return to. And a place to winter. As for me... It will make me happy to see someone continue my work. And also, I won't hide it, that someone will assist in sustaining me in my old age.

"You don't have to decide right away. For now, it's enough if you don't ride on alone, but with me. Keep me company a while longer. Agreed?"

"Well, I don't know... Agreed."

"On our way, then. Let's leave this crossroads to the ravens."

The ravens saw them off with cawing.

"Take no offense," Holt turned in his saddle, "but I insist that, in my presence, you make an effort to speak with some degree of correctness. In particular, refrain from saying 'scope out' and 'oh, come on'."

Chapter Three


As usual, cats and children were the first to take notice of them. The cats, which were swarming the outskirts of the town, reluctantly yielded the road, turning their heads away and hissing as they retreated. The children fled into the houses crying and wailing, abandoning what they had been playing with, mostly lumps of dried mud.

Aside from the cats and the children, none of the inhabitants of the town of Spynham paid the slightest attention to the entering witchers. You would have thought that riders in black cloaks with swords on their backs rode into Spynham so regularly that they had ceased to be of any interest whatsoever.

Preston Holt knew the town; he knew at which stable to leave their mounts. From there they proceeded on foot, down a muddy lane, scaring off more cats and brats.

"Great lord," a beggar woman squatting against a wall with an infant in her lap accosted Geralt. "Great lord, spare a copper... For milk for the child..."

Before Holt could react, Geralt fished out a mark from his pouch and tossed it to the crone, who thanked him effusively. Holt remained silent for some fifty paces, then finally stopped.

"You are no great lord," he hissed through his teeth, looking Geralt in the eyes. "You are, on the other hand, a great fool. For a mark, you can buy the milk of twelve cows. As you will soon find out, they will want to pay you little more than that for killing a monster. And killing a monster takes a little more effort than whining for a handout and baring rotten teeth. And the child was likely borrowed from a crony who is sitting in the tavern waiting for a client, to suck him off in the outhouse for half a mark. I warn you, do not walk down this lane again; people will have a good laugh if you show up here again."

For a moment, Geralt had half a mind to stand up to Holt and snap back at him, to tell him to lay off and mind his own business. He didn't. Somehow, instinctively, he recognized Holt as his senior in rank, almost a master. Perhaps because Holt so strongly resembled Vesemir. In his behavior and speech. Because physically, he was very different.

Before them gleamed the whitewashed colonnades of a temple, beneath which squatted a whole army of beggar women and beggar men. The temple had been inactive and abandoned for years, but the beggars squatted. And stretched out their hands for alms. Out of habit.

Holt prudently pulled Geralt over to the opposite side of the street.

"Do you know why they call us witchers?" he asked. "Because we are the children of witches."

"Oh, come on..." Geralt stammered. "I mean, what a fabrication. Witchers from witches. Give me a break."

"You might find it amusing, but it actually happens to be true. The first witchers were the children of women with uncontrolled magical abilities, called witches. They were feeble-minded and often served lusty young men as sexual playthings. The children, the results of such play, were abandoned. Or left on doorsteps. And from orphanages and asylums, it happened, they found their way to witcher schools."

"Yeah, right. You made that up. It wasn't like that."

"It was. All of us witchers are descended from feeble-minded wenches. Doesn't that amuse you?"

"Not at all. Because it wasn't like that at all."

"It was, it was. But a long time ago! Now you won't find any witches outside temples. The sorcerers exterminated them all. Hah! Nothing lasts forever."

The closer they got to the market square, the more crowded it became. For Geralt, this was a novelty. He was not used to crowds; he felt uneasy in them. The noise irritated him. He was starting to run out of breath, and the stench was becoming increasingly bothersome. It smelled of smoke, burning fat, rotting fruit, manure, and the devil knows what else.

In the market square, they had to wade into a gauntlet of stalls and a throng of buyers. Here, too, only novelties and surprises awaited Geralt. And ultimately, awe. Never in his life had he imagined there were so many trades in the world and such an abundance of wares offered for sale. And that there were so many people eager to buy. Leather thongs and goods, glazed and unglazed clay pots, fur caps, short sheepskin coats, moccasins, embroidered shawls, copper skillets, rakes, pitchforks, mattock handles—and pretzels, pretzels, pretzels.

Holt paid not the slightest attention to the marvels on the stalls. At a certain point, however, he ceased pushing his way through and grabbed Geralt's sleeve.

"Eyes down," he hissed. "Eyes down, don't stare."

"Why?"

"Sorceresses."

Geralt obediently averted his gaze. Reluctantly. Two women by a stall selling amber jewelry drew the eye quite magnetically. With their rich attire. And picture-perfect beauty.

"They," Holt explained once they had moved away, "consider ordinary people cattle, and they hate witchers. They are prone to picking quarrels, and might take staring as an act of provocation. It is also better they do not sense our medallions."

Holt stopped in front of an imposing house, above the door of which a signboard was nailed. The painting on the signboard, quite well-executed, depicted a mermaid with an ample bust. The inscription below the mermaid read: "Lorelei."

Holt grabbed the knocker and knocked energetically. Very energetically.

The massive door opened, and in it stood a broad-shouldered goon with a jaw like a loaf of bread. The goon sized the witchers up for a long moment. Then he stepped back, yielding the way invitingly.

Holt, evidently worldly, pulled both swords from his back without being asked and handed them to the goon. Geralt hastened to follow his example.

Disarmed, they entered an antechamber lit by several lamps. Geralt struggled to catch his breath; the antechamber was heavily saturated with the scent of perfume and incense. On the wall hung a second mermaid, painted on a canvas. Similarly busty. There was no inscription.

"Master Preston Holt," said a woman, entering.

"Lady Pampinea Monteforte." Holt bowed. Geralt also bowed. And closed his mouth, which he had opened a moment before. In awe.

Unversed in the art of dressmaking, Geralt could not have known that the fabrics of Pampinea Monteforte's black gown were sheer chiffon, muslin, and crepe. Nor did he know that it is a great art to tailor a gown so that it covers while uncovering. And vice versa.

Geralt also did not know what the quintessence of femininity was.

But what did it matter that he did not know, when he saw it.
