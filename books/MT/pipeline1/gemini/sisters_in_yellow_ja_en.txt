Chapter One: Reunion





1





I had always thought that no matter where I ended up living, no matter how old I got or what became of me, I would never forget her.

But just a moment ago, until I saw her name in a short online article I had stumbled upon by chance, I hadn't realized that I had completely forgotten everything—not just that I had ever felt that way, but her name, her existence, the time we spent together, and everything we did then.

Kimiko Yoshikawa.

The thought that it might just be a namesake crossed my mind for a second, but I knew instinctively that the Kimiko in the article was *that* Kimiko.





〈The first trial of unemployed Shinjuku Ward resident Kimiko Yoshikawa (60)—charged with assault resulting in injury, criminal intimidation, and unlawful confinement—was held on December 23 at the Tokyo District Court. The defendant is accused of confining a woman in her twenties from Ichikawa, Chiba Prefecture, in a Shinjuku apartment for a year and three months starting in May of last year, during which time she allegedly assaulted the victim, causing severe injuries. During the arraignment, Defendant Yoshikawa stated, "I will remain silent," and her defense counsel entered a plea of not guilty.

According to the indictment, from around February 2018, Defendant Yoshikawa confined her female roommate in a room of a Shinjuku apartment for a year and three months, inflicting severe injuries requiring a month to heal.

In their opening statement, prosecutors explained that the victim, who had no fixed address, began living at Defendant Yoshikawa's home in 2017. Initially, they lived together without issue, but the defendant eventually began managing the victim's belongings and social interactions, and monitoring her movements. Subsequently, the defendant repeatedly threatened the victim with statements such as, "Even if you go outside, you won't be able to survive on your own anyway," instilling fear and stripping the woman of her will to escape. The prosecution also pointed out that the defendant illegally confined the victim, subjected her to multiple assaults, placed her under her control, and forced her into submission. The incident came to light when the woman managed to escape on her own and alerted the authorities.〉










After reading the article three times over, I let out a breath like a solid lump from deep within my chest. My heart was pounding so hard I could feel a faint trembling in my fingertips. It was her. There was no mistake. *That* Kimiko had been arrested.

I searched the name "Kimiko Yoshikawa" and only brought up one article with similar content and another brief report just a few lines long. Both treated the story as minor news. Everything else was just pages about fortune-telling by name, stroke-count divination, or recommendations for baby girl names. Aside from the incident I had just read about, it seemed there was no information about Kimiko herself anywhere on the internet.

I tried to sort out what I should think about and where to begin. I went back to the first article's page and checked the date.

The article was published on January 10, 2020. About three months ago. And it said the incident happened in May of last year, 2019.

But no matter how carefully I read, I couldn't quite grasp what those dates actually meant. I understood that three months had passed since the first trial, but I had no idea what state the victim and others involved were in right now, or how the case or the trial would unfold from here. How was Kimiko doing now? What was going to happen to her? Where on earth was I supposed to find out these things?

I couldn't even imagine the procedures and rules for interrogations or detention centers. All I could picture were the useless, clichéd images I'd seen in TV dramas and news programs—stark gray rooms, handcuffs, expressionless judges, sketches by courtroom artists.

And then there was Kimiko's face.

The Kimiko I had lived with for a few years back when I was still young, some twenty years ago. The article said she was sixty. *That* Kimiko was sixty? I couldn't believe it. Logically, that much time really had passed, and since I was already forty myself, it only made sense, but still, the age printed beneath Kimiko's name just didn't feel like a real number.






I closed my eyes and told myself I was fine. It went without saying, but I had nothing to do with this incident. There was nothing to worry about. For twenty years, I hadn't known what Kimiko was up to, I hadn't been in touch with her, and we were no longer connected in any sense of the word. A long time had passed since then; everything had faded away and ended. So far, aside from this incident—the confinement case Kimiko supposedly caused last year—nothing else had become a public issue. At least, nothing had surfaced on the internet. *I'm fine*—I told myself over and over.

When I looked up from my phone screen, the room was filled with the dark blue of twilight, which hadn't even been there moments before, and the shadows of various objects had deepened. On the low table sat a plate of instant meat sauce spaghetti I had meant to eat. But in the steadily encroaching presence of the night, for some reason, it no longer looked like food.





I woke up multiple times during the night, and morning came with me having barely slept.

The curtains, catching the spring morning light, reminded me of a large sheet of pure white drawing paper. When I closed my eyes against the glare, various colors bled into view and then faded away. Dark blue, deep red, yellow—and then Kimiko's face floated into my mind.

Kimiko would gather her wavy, jet-black hair that reached down to her mid-back in her hands and laugh cheerfully, saying, *I have so much hair, you wouldn't even notice if a whole black cat was hiding in here, right?* I laughed, and everyone else laughed too. The old house. Each room was cramped, cluttered, and overflowing with things, but the entryway was always spotless. We were limited to two pairs of shoes per person, and there was a rule that since the entryway was where good luck came in and the toilet was where bad things went out, it always had to be kept absolutely clean.






I closed my eyes, rolled over, and tried to shake off the things surfacing in my mind. But all sorts of things I hadn't thought about in forever came rushing back to me, hand in hand, one after another. The creaking of the hallway that sagged in places turned into our laughter; the wood grain on the ceiling I used to stare at before falling asleep turned into someone's cigarette smoke, as if whispering to me.

Cosmetics left scattered in front of the mirror, clothes and underwear stuffed to bursting into the cheap particleboard shelves in the closet, and cup noodles piled high in a basket in the cramped kitchen came to mind. They evoked the very scent of those days we had lived.

After agonizing for about thirty minutes in bed, wrapped in my futon, I sent a message to my part-time job's LINE group.

〈Good morning, this is Ito. I've had a bit of a cough since yesterday. I don't have a fever, but just to be safe, I'd appreciate it if I could take the day off today. I'm sorry, and thank you.〉

The person in charge of scheduling at work replied immediately.

〈Got it. Our future policy will be decided early next week, so I'll let you know then. Given the times, I'll go ahead and inform headquarters just in case. Take care!〉

〈Thank you! I think it's just normal cold symptoms, but if I do develop a fever or anything, I'll let you know right away. Thank you again!〉






Until the middle of last month, there had still been a general atmosphere of skepticism toward the infection. People were making a fuss, but many argued it was ultimately just like the flu, so there was no need to be so afraid, and that masks were pointless. There was a sense of floating, a mix of anxiety and strange excitement, but even so, the prevailing mood was that it was all still within the normal margin of error of daily life.

But the news from overseas grew increasingly terrifying, and from late last month into the beginning of this month, rumors began to circulate about whether Japan was finally going to go into lockdown too.

Then, five days ago, the state of emergency was declared, and the tension that had been steadily mounting suddenly burst. It wasn't just on the news; actual panic-buying was happening at the neighborhood supermarket. Masks, hand sanitizer, and toilet paper completely vanished from drugstores, people disappeared from the streets, and my workplace was left scrambling to respond. I worked as a sales clerk at a delicatessen in a shopping arcade within walking distance of my apartment, a shop run by a major supermarket chain with branches all over Tokyo.






It was a small shop. Around thirty plates of side dishes and salads lined the counters and refrigerated cases; customers chose what they wanted, and we packed the food into plastic containers. Everything was delivered pre-made from a central kitchen in the morning and we just sold it, so there was no kitchen, and the space was full with just four staff members. For the three years since I started working there, the menu had been on a constant repeat, and with so many regulars coming in every day, I often wondered if they didn't get bored. But perhaps that very consistency offered peace of mind; sales were always good, and it was a popular spot that always had lines at noon and in the evening. Since the end of last month, however, the number of customers had understandably plummeted, and things quickly fell out of sync. When customers finally did show up, they would get into arguments with each other over wearing masks or not, and we started getting phone complaints claiming our infection control measures were insufficient.

Lying down, I thought that maybe it hadn't been a good idea to message them on LINE that I had a cough. Why had I lied about having a cough? What was I thinking, going out of my way to say I had a cough at a time like this? Even I didn't know.






After a while, I picked up my phone, went back to the site with Kimiko's article, and took my time slowly reading it again from the beginning. My mood darkened anew with every read, and my limbs felt heavy. And, regardless of the reason I had given, I was glad I had taken the day off after all. Even if the job just involved standing there, I was in no condition to work like this.

I got out of bed, took some barley tea from the fridge, and drank it. Then I pulled a box from the closet shelf.

It was a slightly oversized shoebox with crushed corners and a small tear in the lid. Inside, I kept things like old letters, pocketbooks, and notebooks.

Worn out and its original navy blue completely faded, the box had once held a pair of high heels my mother bought somewhere long ago. I remember how happily she paraded around the room in those pure white heels, and how, perhaps out of sheer joy, she had sat right on the tatami mats still wearing them to eat instant ramen. I had asked my mother for the empty box and decided to use it to keep stickers, little magazine supplements, and small notes I passed back and forth with friends at school. After that, it became a habit to put in things I wanted to keep at the time. Through moving from place to place, leaving things behind and losing things along the way, I realized that this shoebox was the only thing that had remained. But normally, I never picked up the box, let alone looked at what was inside. Looking at it now, I felt as if I were looking at someone's keepsakes after they had died, even though these were my own possessions that I had somehow managed to hold onto through thick and thin without throwing them away.






When I opened the lid, I saw a small navy-blue flip phone and a charger sitting in the corner. Even though I was the one looking for it, my heart skipped a beat the moment I found it. Not knowing if it still worked, I plugged the charger into the phone and the wall outlet, waited about thirty minutes, and pressed and held the power button. The small screen brightened, as if slowly drawing breath, and the startup chime played.

I had changed my phone number and cut all ties so that no one could reach me, yet I had kept the numbers of my past acquaintances without deleting them. Had I subconsciously sensed that I would need them like this someday?

There were only seventeen contacts in the address book.

Under the 'K' row, I saw the characters for Kimiko. I selected the name right above hers, Ran Kato, and displayed the number. Next, I went to the 'M' row. Momoko. Momoko Tamamori. I recorded both of their numbers in the memo app on my current phone.

I had no idea where Ran Kato and Momoko Tamamori were now, or what they were doing.

The last time I saw them was when they moved out of that house. We were twenty, or somewhere around there. I hadn't been in touch with either Ran or Momoko since. If I hadn't stumbled upon the article about Kimiko yesterday, I might never have remembered them again.






The time we all spent together in that house came rushing back like a disjointed video stitched together without any logic.

Various voices and expressions played back, the resolution rapidly sharpening and blurring on repeat. I didn't think these numbers would still connect, and it wasn't as if I wanted to contact them now either. But Ran and Momoko were the only ones I could talk to about Kimiko. They were the only ones who could share the anxiety I was feeling right now.

I was terrified that Kimiko might have talked about her past with us.

Perhaps, while she was being interrogated, they found all sorts of evidence in Kimiko's room from the days we lived together, and an investigation had actually begun behind closed doors. The thought made me so restless I couldn't sit still. No one had come to me yet, but there was a chance that Ran or Momoko had already been contacted, called in, and questioned.

Thinking about it rationally, the statute of limitations had probably run out on what we did. Maybe we wouldn't even be charged with a major crime. Ran, Momoko, and I were still young back then, and we were only doing what Kimiko told us to do. But then, what about Kotomi? Ultimately, whose fault was it that Kotomi died, and why did it happen? Could we really say it had nothing to do with us?

The more I thought about it, the more an anxiety indistinguishable from fear weighed down on me. I felt a terror as if my chest were being silently crushed by a massive iron plate or something, and tears welled in my eyes. What should I do? Should I pretend I never saw the article, not contact Ran or Momoko, and just keep my mouth shut? Or would it be better to go to the police and tell them what I knew?






My imagination kept swelling in the worst possible directions, obscuring my vision. How were those two doing? Where were they, and what were they up to now? Of course the calls wouldn't connect. But if they weren't going to connect anyway, shouldn't I at least try? I set the phone aside and pulled the futon over my head to drown everything out. The daylight was blocked out, and I lay in the warm spring darkness, blinking repeatedly for a while. And then, just like that, I slept a little.

I had an elusive dream, knowing only that it was a nightmare. Why is it that even when no specific events or characters appear, when there is nothing there but time, you can always tell a nightmare is a nightmare? Like a dark, relentless wave, the nightmare bore down on me endlessly. When I woke up, my chest and back were drenched in sweat. And then I dialed Ran Kato's number.





After six rings, I heard a bright, "Ye-es?" I realized my lower jaw was trembling from nerves.

"Excuse me, um... is this Ran Kato's... number?"

"Yes."

Ran replied, her voice dropping slightly. It was Ran. My heart gave a heavy thump.

"Um, it's Hana."

"Hana?"

"Yes, um, my name is Hana Ito, and, back in the day, we were together..."

"Hana?" After a brief silence, Ran spoke. "...Hana-chan?"

"Yeah, it's Hana. Sorry for calling so suddenly." I switched the phone to my other hand and pressed it tightly against my ear. "I never expected it to actually connect. Sorry, out of nowhere."

"...What's wrong? How did you know my number?"

"I'd left it saved on my old phone."

"Ah."






I heard Ran let out a small sigh.

"Really, I'm sorry for surprising you out of nowhere."

"No, it's fine, but... I'm just a bit shocked. I mean, it's from so long ago."

"I know, I'm sorry. The reason I called is, actually, about Kimiko."

Behind Ran, I could hear the voices of children making noise and having fun. There were women's voices mixed in, too. The sounds grew a bit distant, and I could tell Ran had moved to another spot.

"...Kimiko, as in *that* Kimiko?"

"Yeah."

"What about her?"

"Yesterday, I found an article about Kimiko's case."

"What's that?"

"I found it online."

"What do you mean, case?"

"Kimiko got arrested. I was shocked, too. The trial has already started, and, well, this is just a 'maybe,' but I thought our stuff might end up being related, too. That's why I wanted to talk to you."

"Hold on a second," Ran cut me off. "What do you mean? I don't get this at all. Why was Kimiko arrested? What do you mean it might involve us too? Did Kimiko say something?"

"No, it's not that. Kimiko... she locked a girl up in her apartment, injured her, and got arrested. I think... she was doing the same kind of thing as back then, and got caught. Maybe the past will become an issue too, and all sorts of things might, you know, come to light. I have no idea, but I'm just scared."

I took the plunge and asked.

"...Has anyone contacted you? The police, or anywhere like that?"

"Of course not." Ran laughed, as if the idea were ridiculous, but I could sense a faint trace of anxiety in her voice.






"I've been anxious since yesterday, and I thought, you know, maybe I should go to the police and talk."

"Huh?" Ran asked back, surprised. "Talk? About what?"

"About the past, and what I know about Kimiko."

"Hey, stop joking around," Ran said, dropping her voice but speaking with a sharp intensity. Just then someone called out to Ran, and she cheerfully replied, "Okay!"

"Listen, I have people over right now."

"Yeah, right. Sorry."

"Hana, where are you living now? Tokyo?"

"Yeah."

"We should probably meet up and talk. With COVID and all, it's a bit tricky, but... it's probably better not to do this over the phone if possible."

"Yeah, I think we should meet and talk too... Oh, right, Momoko. There's Momoko too. I don't know if her number still connects, but we should probably contact her too, right? If she can come."

"Momoko is a no-go," Ran said shortly.

"Why?"

"I'll tell you when we meet. Hana, anyway, don't go to the police or do anything like that, absolutely not. Got it?"

"Got it."

We decided on a date, time, and place to meet, and hung up.





2





Meeting Ran Kato for the first time in twenty years, she seemed to have changed her aura quite a bit.

She was still generally petite, just as I remembered, but her body seemed a size larger, giving her slightly mismatched proportions. If she hadn't spotted me and waved, I don't think I would have recognized her right away, even in this sparsely populated cafe.






Ran was wearing a fluffy ecru cotton tunic and a large mask that completely swallowed the lower half of her face. Her hair, dyed a light brown, was tied back, and white hairs at her hairline glimmered faintly under the lights, as if brushed on with a stroke of paint. Traces of the old Ran remained around her eyes, which were heavily coated in brown eyeshadow and thick mascara.

Her narrow forehead hadn't changed. I remembered a midnight, ages ago, when the three of us—Ran, Momoko, and I—had measured and compared the widths of our foreheads and eyes. Come to think of it, Ran had loved doing makeup since back then. Laughing, she would ask how much I thought my face—clueless as I was about such things—could change with her techniques. She would try out all sorts of makeup on me, and we would all burst out laughing when we saw the final result. As I looked at Ran in front of me while recalling such things, the realization that a truly long time had passed welled up inside me, bringing a slight ache deep in my chest.

But we didn't exchange the cheerful greetings you'd expect when reuniting with an old friend—no "Long time no see" or "How have you been?"—and just ordered our respective drinks. Because her mouth was covered, I couldn't tell what expression Ran was making, but we sat in silence for a while in an atmosphere where it felt as if nothing could move a muscle.






After two iced coffees were placed on the table, I ventured, "I'm glad you recognized me so easily."

"You were looking all over the place at the entrance," Ran replied shortly. She stopped fiddling with her phone and took off her mask, which had a heavy smear of brick-red lipstick on it.

"Kimiko," Ran said. "What happened? What kind of incident did she cause? I tried searching for it, but I couldn't really figure it out."

Ignoring my tension, Ran dove right in, as if continuing a conversation we had just been having.

"Was it on the news? Is it, like, really bad?"

"Not the news, exactly." I pulled up the bookmarked article and handed the phone to Ran. Ran stared at the screen with a serious expression, scrolling slightly with her fingertip, and then became absorbed in the screen again.

"How did you find this?" she asked, after sliding the phone back across the table to me.

"I was just casually reading articles about other major crimes on Yahoo, and similar cases kept popping up at the bottom. Connected by content. Links to cases from years ago, or small blurbs from local papers I'd never heard of, a bunch of those kept coming up. I kept clicking through them, and eventually I hit this article."

"Shinjuku, huh... So it's not that place in Sancha," Ran said, furrowing her brow. "It says Kimiko is sixty... which means back then she was forty or something. About the age we are now, right."

"Yeah."






"Looking back, it's completely insane, isn't it."

Ran spat the words out.

"I can't believe it. I mean, forty? That would be like us now gathering girls who were around twenty and living like that. She's completely unhinged."

Saying that, she gave a small shake of her head.

"But with this Kimiko thing, why are you so spooked? It has nothing to do with us, does it? Plus, the incident itself was last year, and the trial has already started, right?"

I nodded.

"When we lived together—sure, I think we did some sketchy stuff. But we were around twenty back then, and it was ages ago. I don't see how something like that could possibly become a problem now."

"But if, for example, she still had... you know, that bundle of cards from back then left in her apartment, I thought the police would want to know what it was. And if Kimiko talked, even if we aren't charged with a crime, they might at least call us in for questioning. I'm sure there's a statute of limitations, but with the things we were doing, how do I say it... I just don't know what might be an issue or to what extent."

"So you were going to go out of your way to go to the police and say, I'm someone from her past?" Ran widened her eyes, looking exasperated. "You're overthinking it."

"Am I?"

"Yes. Your imagination is running wild in the worst way. To begin with, the stuff we did wasn't even that big of a deal. At least, I don't think it was. It's definitely not something to be agonizing over twenty years later. Young kids today do way more messed up stuff, don't they?"

"Do they?"

"They do." Ran spoke as if thinking it over slightly. "Besides, we were... forced to do it, right?"

"But Kotomi died, and we still don't really know what actually happened, and we dragged her into it... anyway, things ended up like that."






"Who was Kotomi again? Oh," Ran gave a small nod. "Right. The hostess from that club in Ginza you went to on those expeditions."

"Yeah."

"No, no, no, I really don't think that's related. That was completely her own situation... that's what we concluded back then, wasn't it? Am I wrong? Sure, it was a shock, but... wait, don't tell me you still think it was your fault?"

"Not exactly," I said, shaking my head.

"That's ridiculous. Those people were all crazy anyway." Ran gave a slight shrug. "So come on, just calm down. Lighten up and change your mood. Don't make that face. There's no point agonizing over something that happened a million years ago that no one even remembers. I mean, sure, I'd be shocked too if Kimiko's name suddenly popped up like this. Locking a girl up in a similar situation and all. I get how you feel. But you're fine. If things were really going to hit the fan, the police would have come knocking a long time ago. Really, that's just a dark past. A youthful indiscretion, and a dark past."

"A youthful, dark past."

"Exactly. Honestly, COVID is way more serious right now. How are you holding up, Hana? My kids' school is closed, and it's absolute hell. COVID is crazy, right? How are things for you?"

"I'm," I exhaled softly through my nose and said, "doing normal. I live alone."






Ran stirred her iced coffee round and round with her straw and gave a nod of mild interest. We were in a cafe in front of Jiyugaoka Station. Ran apparently lived somewhere along the Toyoko Line, and had designated this place because it was a straight shot for her and she could run errands at the same time. A chain coffee shop with floor-to-ceiling glass windows. Ran hadn't asked me where I lived, what kind of work I did, or where and how I had been living since back then.

"There's hardly anyone around. It's usually packed. This self-restraint stuff is crazy."

Looking out at the street, it was true that pedestrians were sparse, and partly because of the heavy overcast sky since morning, the whole town had a gloomy, sunken atmosphere.

"But man, time really goes around, doesn't it? We're already forty or something. I never thought I'd see you again like this. The way we parted ways, or rather, we were just all scattered."

"You hadn't changed your phone number, Ran, but did Kimiko ever contact you after that?"

"Nope," Ran said. "I can't really remember the details anymore, and I don't want to... but back then we kind of left in the confusion, right? That was it. But you must have had it the hardest, Hana. You were the favorite, after all... Honestly, it makes total sense that Kimiko ended up getting arrested. She's terrifying. Way too unhinged. We were young, basically just kids, so we had no idea, but she was completely out of her mind."






"Hey, you mentioned this briefly on the phone," I said. "About Momoko... you said she was a no-go. Did something happen?"

"Oh, Momoko." Ran shook her head slightly. "Remember her? She had that crazy sister. Drop-dead gorgeous, but with absolutely terrible teeth. She came to the house once too, remember? From what I heard, she got into a huge fight over money with her sister and her sister's guy, and eventually ended up disappearing. I heard a rumor like that. I was in touch with Momoko for a little while after we left that place, but then she just suddenly vanished."

I sighed.

"Man, everyone was just crazy back then. We were just completely clueless."

Ran's voice had suddenly grown loud, ringing out theatrically as if she were practicing lines for a play. The spacious cafe was sparsely populated; the scattered customers were all sitting alone, wearing masks, and bossa nova-style music played softly from speakers hidden somewhere in the ceiling. The clattering of dishes from the sink behind the counter blended with the voices of the staff. Aside from us, no other customers were talking. An elderly man sitting at a table a little ways off rattled his newspaper loudly as he opened it, and our eyes met when he glanced in our direction. I took a sip of water and checked the mask hooked under my chin with my fingertips.






"Well, whatever happened, it's over now. I'll say it a million times, but it's in the past. Just the past. There's nothing for you to worry about... Hey, Hana."

Ran stared straight at me, unblinking.

"Seriously, absolutely no police or anything like that. It's literally pointless. It'll 100 percent just make things worse. Really, Hana, I'm counting on you for this. You're okay, right? I came today and listened to what you had to say. Just forget about it now. Seriously."

I nodded.

"Really, you're okay, right?"

"Yeah."

After that, we fell silent for a long time. Ran sighed, hunched her back, and stared at a fixed point on the table. Traces of exhaustion showed on her face. I felt as though there was something else I needed to say to the reunited Ran—something important. And perhaps Ran felt the same way too. But I had no idea what it was, or how it could come out of my mouth as the right words.

"Well, I should probably—oh, right, Hana."

As if pulling herself together, Ran straightened her back and put her mask on as she spoke.

"About this, could you delete my phone number? I'll make sure to delete your incoming call from mine, too."

When I remained silent, unable to say anything, Ran picked up the bill, checked it, placed the money for her share on the table, slung her bag over her shoulder, and stood up.

"Alright, Hana, I'm taking off. Be careful with COVID."






Even after Ran left the shop with those parting words, I couldn't bring myself to stand up from my seat right away. I knew I had other things I needed to think about, but my mind wouldn't work properly. I had no appetite, but since I hadn't eaten since morning, I must be hungry, and I vaguely wondered if I should order some food.

As I stared at the small menu with photos on the table, I heard a strange noise. Outside, somewhere in the distance, a sound—whether heavy or light, I couldn't tell—echoed as if something massive were slowly rolling. After a moment, I realized it was thunder. The second I looked out through the glass window, the rain came pouring down all at once with a bursting sound. The drops were so large it felt as if you could visually track every single one, and I watched a few pedestrians running away, shielding their heads with their bags or hands. The rain picked up intensity, violently lashing against the awnings of the shops diagonally across the street and the asphalt below, splashing up in a faint white smoke.





Chapter Two: Financial Luck





1





The first time I met Kimiko was the summer I was fifteen.

One morning just after my final summer vacation of junior high school had begun, instead of my mother, who was supposed to be sleeping next to me, a strange woman was sleeping there.

I couldn't see her face, and though she was wearing my mother's pajamas, I knew immediately that the woman snoring heavily with her back turned to me was not my mother.






Propped up on my elbow, I recoiled slightly, but quickly decided it was nothing and went back to sleep. My mother worked at a neighborhood snack bar, and there had been several times before when she had brought girls from the bar or friends home to sleep over like this.

When I woke up next, the woman was nowhere to be seen, and the pajamas were folded neatly and placed on top of a futon that had been folded in three. They were worn-out pajamas my mother had been wearing for years, but seeing them perfectly squared away as if they were being sold in a store was so refreshing that I stared at them for a while.

The norm was to pluck clothes and underwear directly from the hangers hooked onto the curtain rail by the window once they dried. The room was packed with things and always a complete mess, so that one spot stood out cleanly, like a perfectly blank, round space on a faintly smudged notebook erased carefully with an eraser.

My mother and I lived in a small town on the outskirts of Higashimurayama City, in an old, cramped wooden apartment building hidden from the main street.






Between the detached houses facing the road, there was an unpaved, three-meter-wide alleyway. If you walked to the end of it and turned left, you'd find the communal entrance. The characters reading *Seifuso* were so weathered and blackened you could barely make them out. The entrance looked like an ominous cave, and the hallway, lit only by a few low-wattage bulbs dangling from the ceiling, was dark even on the sunniest of days.

It was a two-story wooden building with four identical rooms on each floor, but apart from the middle-aged landlady who lived in the back room on the second floor, there was no one else living there but us. Pulling open the flimsy wooden sliding door—one a child could have kicked in—revealed a tiny entryway, which opened up to a kitchen no larger than three tatami mats. Beyond that were two four-and-a-half-mat rooms, arranged one behind the other. At the back of the first floor was a communal toilet that we used entirely for ourselves. Because the building was surrounded by other structures on all sides, opening the window only offered a view of the neighboring concrete wall, and hardly any light came in.

We rented two rooms on the first floor. They were the rooms immediately to the left and right as you entered Seifuso, and my mother and I primarily used the room on the right.






The room on the left was nominally my father's space, but aside from a TV, a futon, and a few pieces of clothing hanging in the closet, there was no sense of anyone living there, and to begin with, my father was someone who rarely stayed home.

At the time, I didn't really know what he did for a living. He had a sturdy build and his skin was always deeply tanned, so I imagine he worked day labor, like as a construction worker or a truck driver, sometimes taking jobs that required him to travel for long stretches. (Once, on my way home from elementary school, my father called out my name from a massive truck.) There was a period when he would bring home coworkers in similar work clothes to have hot pot, grill meat, and drink, but that didn't last long.

My father, whom I saw only sporadically as if he had just remembered me, was always in a good mood. He would give me stuffed animals that looked like they came from badminton sets or UFO catchers, and he'd suddenly come home in the middle of the night and go out of his way to wake me up just to feed me sushi, insisting it was delicious.

I didn't dislike my father, but we spent so little time together and I never knew what to talk to him about, so whenever he came home, I would get strangely tense and overly accommodating, constantly on edge and secretly wishing he would leave sooner. And then the thought that he might sense this would darken my mood even further. I knew my father wasn't what you'd call a "normal" dad, but I also vaguely felt that it might be pitiful for him to have his own child feel this way about him, which brought on a whole different kind of tension and self-hatred.






By the time I was in the upper grades of elementary school, my father had truly stopped coming home, and I never saw him again. I have no idea how he is doing or what has become of him now. I realized much later, though it was only a vague understanding, that my father had another home—or rather, other people he lived with.

Living alone with my mother was easygoing in its own way, but it was basically the same as having nothing at all. My mother loved fun things and drinking, even though she couldn't hold her liquor well; she had a lot of friends, and an easily influenced personality. Her pride and joy was that, after graduating from a local commercial high school and working full-time at a stocking factory, she had once served as a leg model in front of everyone when the bigwigs from headquarters came for a tour. But she only lasted a few years there, after which she bounced around local snack bars with her friends, and somewhere along the way, I was born.

Compared to the mothers of my school friends, my mother had a very youthful face. She was flashy, and even to her own daughter, she didn't feel like your typical "normal mom." She was petite with somewhat childlike features, optimistic, and had a cheerful personality and laughed a lot, but whenever she drank, she would cry without fail. It wasn't as if there was a specific trigger or reason for it; she was just a crying drunk. To my mother, drinking, having a good time, making noise, and crying all seemed to be virtually the same thing.






She didn't seem to have any attachment to my father, either. I never heard her complain or speak ill of him, and she showed no particular interest in him even after he stopped coming home. Sometimes she would jokingly talk about how awful her estranged mother's personality was, but even that was limited to when she was drunk, hyped up, and feeling sentimental.

Fundamentally, when my mother was with me, she preferred not to be just the two of us, but to have someone else join us. She often spent time aimlessly hanging out with hostesses from the bar she worked at, someone introduced by a customer, or old friends from town. Whenever I was alone with my mother, I always felt just the slightest bit tense. Her schedule was basically the exact opposite of mine: she slept while I was at school, woke up past noon, did her makeup in the evening, went to work, and came home late at night.






There was only one other kid in the neighborhood in a similar situation, and we became close. From the time we were in elementary school, neither of us had a curfew, so the parents of the kids who had places they weren't allowed to go or strict dinner times didn't seem to like their children hanging around us more than necessary. At the very least, they were banned from coming to play at our houses. I once asked another friend I rather liked—someone who would interact normally with me at school but wander off with a different group of kids after class—for the reason. She looked a bit uncomfortable and told me, "They said I can't go to your place because weird adults are always coming and going, and it's not a proper home." It wasn't a malicious rumor or a lie; it was the absolute truth, so there was nothing I could do about it.

When I entered junior high, everything—the faces of my friends, their attitudes, and various other things—came into sharper focus. I felt like I could tell at a glance which kids were from normal homes and which weren't, as if they were wearing different-colored hats. In a normal home, you don't wake up in the morning to find a strange woman sleeping next to you. You probably don't feel a surge of brightness just because a futon and pajamas are neatly folded, and you certainly don't feel the urge to stare at them.






I folded my own futon in three just like the other one and lined it up next to the one already folded. I was alone in the room, and there was no one in the kitchen either. Slipping on my sandals, I stepped across the hallway and opened the door to the room across from ours. I heard the noisy chatter of the TV, and when I went inside I saw the back of the woman lying down, watching a daytime talk show. A fan we'd been using forever slowly swept its head left to right, occasionally letting out a loud creak.

Noticing me, the woman didn't change her posture but simply turned her face toward me and smiled warmly. Her smile was so natural I almost thought she was someone I'd met before. But she was a strange woman—she might have slept on the adjacent futon in the same room last night, but this was definitely the first time we were coming face-to-face. The woman turned her face back to the screen and laughed happily, her shoulders shaking slightly at the cheers of the people on TV. I stood at the threshold between the kitchen and the room, watching the TV, the fan, and the woman.

"Let's eat something, yeah?"

When the program ended and cut to a commercial, the woman stretched and spoke.

"You're hungry, right?"

We stood in the cramped kitchen and made bag ramen.

The woman was about a size larger than me, and her arms and legs seemed exceptionally long. Her long, wavy black hair was gathered in a single tie, sagging at the back of her neck, the ends scattered across the white background of her oversized T-shirt printed with English words. I stood a short distance away from her, quietly watching the water for two portions come to a boil in an aluminum pot.






The woman deftly tore open the packages, loosened the noodles in the boiling water, quickly stirred in the powder soup, and dished out the ramen into the bowls I had set out. She tried to pick off a piece of egg that had stuck to the side of the pot with the tips of her chopsticks, but when it wouldn't peel off, she said brightly, "Oh well, I'll bring them over," and gestured toward the room. I pulled out the bare skeleton of a kotatsu table that had been leaning against the wall, and we sat facing each other, slurping our ramen.

"It's summer vacation, right. Are you going anywhere?"

I let out a vague noise that was half a reply and half not. It hadn't been that hot just a moment ago, but I suddenly felt sweat prickling my skin. I reached out, turned on the AC, and pressed the "High" button.

"Are you going anywhere with Ai?"

Ai was my mother's name.

"We don't really do that."

"Huh. What's your name? I'm Kimiko."

"I'm Hana."

"Hana-chan, huh. Who named you? Ai?"

"I don't really know," I answered in a small voice.

"I see."

"Um, are you my mom's friend?"

"Yep."

The conversation stopped there, and we silently went back to eating our ramen. A quiz show played on the TV, and the old AC unit's motor roared loudly. After a while, the faint sound of an ambulance drifted in from outside; it seemed to draw a little closer before quickly fading away again.

"Does your Hana have kanji?"

"Just the normal one for flower."

"My Kimiko is the character for yellow, then beautiful, and child. Kimiko."






I gave another vague nod and drank my soup. When I glanced up, still holding my bowl, I met Kimiko's eyes. She had finished eating first and was resting her elbows on the kotatsu, and I instinctively looked away. After that, I kept stealing glances at Kimiko's face over the edge of my bowl. Kimiko had a face different from any woman I had seen up until then.

Everyone's face is different, so it was only natural, but even so, Kimiko's face possessed a certain presence—something I couldn't quite explain with the words available to me at the time. She had just woken up and wasn't wearing any makeup, yet her eyebrows were thick and dense, and the lashes on her deep double eyelids were dark and long, and the space between her eyebrows and her eyes was narrow. The bridge of her nose was pronounced right from the root, and the hair trailing from her temples to her sideburns mixed with sweat to form tiny, tight curls.

Rather than being pretty or beautiful, Kimiko's face felt strong.

It reminded me of the young, dignified pharaoh protagonist of a shojo manga set in ancient and modern Egypt that was hugely popular in elementary school, which I had also been obsessed with. Watching Kimiko crack her neck and shift her posture while watching TV, I almost felt as if I could see that protagonist's lines floating in the air diagonally above her head along with the borders of the manga panels, and it made me feel a little bit happy.

"I suddenly want some cider. Wanna go buy some?"

Having carried her bowl to the sink, Kimiko came back to the room and invited me. Dressed exactly as we had woken up, we stepped outside and walked the path to the convenience store.

"You and Ai have two rooms, but you don't sleep separately, huh."






"The room we just ate ramen in, we call the TV room, and the other one is the futon room, and we just kind of stuck with it."

"Like a living room, then."

"It's nothing that nice," I said, looking down.

"Still beats sleeping alone, right?"

The summer sun was sharp enough to make me squint, and with every breath I took, I could feel the heat seeping into my skin.

The apartment I lived in was right behind the junior high school I attended.

Walking about two hundred meters along the long gray wall surrounding the campus and turning left at the corner, you would find the main gate, and the convenience store was right across the street from it. We were forbidden from shopping at the convenience store while in our school uniforms, but hardly any students actually followed that rule. Over the narrow sidewalk, the vivid green of the nameless trees planted on the school grounds jutted out, casting deep blue shadows on the asphalt.

As the main gate drew closer, I saw a few kids hanging around and making a commotion.

Because there were club activities, students were coming and going even during summer vacation, but the kids gathered there weren't those kinds of students. They were the delinquents with bad behavior, the so-called "Yankees."
