**In the Place Leading to the Shells**

Standing in the shadow of the deserted station building, I waited for a visitor whose face had half-faded from my mind. Even if I scoured my memory and somehow managed to piece together an image of his face, it would immediately collapse like water. Even so, I gathered the fragments and crammed them inside an outline to create a patchwork portrait. This repetition was filled with a hesitant sensation, much like probing an aching tooth with one's tongue.





The early July sunlight had grown whiter, radiating heat unsparingly. While making colors more vivid, those rays simultaneously reduced everything to black and white. There were no shadows in the white, luminous plaza in front of the station. On the benches there, people sat spaced apart, like statues that would never melt even in this heat. In stark contrast to these still figures pinned down by the sunlight was a black dog darting about. The dog was playing around the transparent water rising up from the plaza. Multiple fountain spouts had been bored into the cobblestones, from which water shot up and stopped at irregular intervals. The narrow pillars of water, stretching up as high as a child, would bloom and quietly vanish like rapidly cycling glass plants, leaving behind wet, black circular traces. Delighted, the dog twisted his body to wrestle with these white, transparently gleaming water snakes. Every time the water disappeared, he would pounce toward another spout. He alone remained oblivious, enjoying even this oppressive sunlight.





Next to the white, sun-scorched cobblestone plaza in front of the Göttingen station, bicycles formed a tangled forest. Intricately crammed together to thread through the gaps in the parking area, the bicycles' metallic frames gave off a gleam that would not melt even in the sunlight. Even now, three years after arriving here, I still do not understand how such complex three-dimensionality maintains its equilibrium. Even as I watched, a man slammed on his brakes, wedged the bicycle he was riding into a seam in that metal forest, and ran frantically toward the station building. Smoothly slipping on a mask as he ran, he was sucked into the depths beyond the doors. The forest, its complexity increased by a single bicycle, had turned into such a mass of objects that it had already lost the meaning of "bicycle." In the light tracing its surface, a separate image of this mass of objects stripped of meaning surfaced, and began to throb in imitation of a toothache.





Checking the time, I peered inside through the main entrance of the station. A train must have arrived, for the almost empty hall was suddenly overflowing with people, filling the white space with a rich array of colors. But as they quickly passed through the doors and walked away into the white sunlight, the newly arrived travelers inevitably lost their color once more. When I called the dog's name, he bounded over to my feet immediately, though reluctant to leave his watery play. Attaching the leash to his collar, I stepped inside the station building. Within the white hall, the black of the dog's wet fur took on more weight; its color was so vivid it was like a stake driven halfway into the white, and I stood there relying on that hue.





The stream of people broke off, the voices ceased, the announcements of arriving and departing trains fell silent, and for a brief moment, the hall was filled with a void. I stepped forward out of the soundless white and proceeded a little further down the concourse. Because the station's front and rear entrances are connected by a straight concourse, you can see clear through to the doors at the far end—albeit small in the distance—save for the few glass elevators placed in between. Standing at the edge of the quiet concourse and casting my gaze toward the back, I saw vibrant green framed by the doorway, looking like a painting. Yet, that vividness did nothing to close the distance between it and me. Like looking through a telescope from the wrong end, it merely flickered in my field of vision as a fragment of a distant world. When I focused on it, a human figure slowly coalesced. In that concourse with its distorted sense of perspective, Nomiya stood, his back to the greenery. Perhaps noticing me, he approached, the monotonous sound of his suitcase wheels drawing closer. As the distance closed, I could feel the time that had already slipped away, and the memories that had remained frozen, beginning to move in imitation of the squeaking wheels. His tall, thin figure skillfully deflected the oppressive heat. The proportions of his broad forehead and the large eyes beneath it seemed modeled after those of a medieval sculpture. Perhaps because he was silhouetted against the greenery swaying in the sunlight, he suddenly reminded me of a saint depicted in the stained glass of a high church window.





But there, my memory ceased to function properly. When Nomiya stopped at a suitable distance for speaking, his mouth was covered by a white mask, blurring the impression of everything below his nose into the color of that cloth. In the shadow of the white that rendered his contours ambiguous, neither the movement of his mouth nor the hint of any words was clearly visible. The same was true for me, and right now, I was profoundly grateful for this hot, breath-obstructing fabric. Lurking behind it, hiding my own mouth, I, too, was holding my words in reserve. However, once we moved away from the station's main entrance, we would have to take them off, and then we would need to reveal our words in order to connect the time. Nomiya stepped out of the impression of a saint trapped in glass, and finally, once outside the station, removed his mask. His mouth tried to form what looked like the words *It's been a long time*, but realizing the sheer length of the severed time, it broke down into a small, ambiguous smile without ever becoming a voice. Even so, seeing his slight but polite bow, I felt a painful catch in my throat; my memories unraveled, and the words I had prepared were lost. *He was the kind of person who greeted you like this.* My patched-together, broken memory cried out. More than his face or figure, those nine years sought to connect through that gesture. At the same time, I was relieved by his figure, which held a presence solid enough not to let the sunlight pass through. Even for the dead, shadows pool at their feet. I, too, returned a mirrored bow in place of words. We both smiled wryly at that clumsy mirroring, but once our lips had moved, the starting point of our conversation was naturally set. When I said, *It was a long journey, wasn't it*, Nomiya nodded, taking in the multiple meanings of the phrase, and formed a natural smile as he replied, *I'm a little tired.* Nomiya went on to speak in a calm tone about how his train had been delayed and he had missed his connection, how he had asked a station attendant about other trains but struggled to catch the words because the man spoke fast and his voice was muffled by a mask, and his impressions of an old fortress he had seen from the train window. Judging by the way he spoke and looked, I could only think of him as an ordinary traveler. All the things I had imagined based on the stories of his friend Sawada melted into white in the face of this reality, leaving me capable of nothing more than doll-like reactions, simply nodding along. Over this unbalanced conversation, silence fell with a thud, like the snapping of a thread holding up a stage curtain.





*There is so much greenery, but no sound of cicadas.* Nomiya murmured this, unconcerned by the broken conversation, and narrowed his eyes against the sunlight, trying to search for the voices that scorch the summer. His body, in which time had stopped, absorbed the heavy white light, but his gaze was somehow untethered from reality. Seeking the cicadas' cries to fill the dense void, his eyes were directed toward a distant time.

The dog was observing Nomiya from a slight distance. His body language was easy to read: his tail, white only at the tip, was suspended midway in the air, tracing a sign of reservation. Without straying from his own orbit, the dog waited for the other to approach him. He probably had not realized that Nomiya was a ghost. His attitude toward unknown strangers was consistent; to him, there seemed to be no distinction between the living and the dead. Feeling a sense of relief at this, I urged Nomiya on and began walking toward the street leading to the old town. From a distance, the dog, Nomiya, and I probably all looked as if we were losing our colors, melting away into the whiteness. That white blurred the perspective of memory. Yet, it could not bridge the severance of time and distance.





Göttingen is a city where the seams of time are unnoticeable. One can glide effortlessly from one time to another through layered memories. Like many German cities, it is centered around a core old town thickly stored with history, around which the city spills over and expands in all directions, as if to surround and cover it. The circular old town is neatly enclosed by the remains of the city wall. The walls have long been removed; instead, a thin, ring-shaped forest surrounds it, threaded with paths packed down by the feet of woodland-walking enthusiasts. It takes about half an hour to walk from one end of the old town to the other—a size easily grasped by walking around. Tracing the streets woven through it, you realize that time is layered here in a polyhedral structure.

I had also heard where Nomiya would be staying through Sawada. It was located to the north, away from the old town, close to the natural sciences campus of the University of Göttingen. "So, it's better to take the bus," I said. Nomiya nodded, and we made our way toward the main road that cuts through the forest of the old city walls. There is a bus stop in front of the station as well, but no direct bus runs from there. So, we decided to head for a bus stop inside the old town.

It was about two weeks ago that I learned of Nomiya's arrival. Until the day of his arrival, we never sent messages directly to one another, relying instead on Sawada's polite and meticulous mediation. Because of this, I couldn't shake the feeling that, on some level, I was here as a physical proxy for Sawada.





Dragging our shadows behind us, as soon as we reached the old town, we came upon a golden sphere gleaming dully in the sunlight. On the sidewalk facing a light-colored, stone-walled hotel, it stood exposed like an overripe bud atop the stem of a dark bronze plaque. Nomiya's gaze slowly traced its outline. *This is the sun.* My own voice conveying this sounded unexpectedly distant, and I felt as though I was about to lose my bearings.

Built into Göttingen is a scale model of the solar system called the *Planetenweg* (Planet Walk). In this land layered with old memories, the history of this model is still shallow, yet it has already blended into the setting to become one of the town's several symbols. The sun model, placed on Goetheallee—which extends in a straight line east from the station—serves as the starting point, with the planetary models from Mercury to Neptune lined up sequentially along the street. Because the arrangement is scaled to one two-billionth, the distances between the planetary models vary greatly, and if you aren't paying attention, you'll walk right past them. Mercury through Saturn are located within the old town, but Uranus and Neptune stand quietly in the residential areas beyond it. Neptune, sitting at the edge of the solar system, is located right at the border with the forest. The locations skillfully layer a metaphorical sense of the distances between the planets of the solar system.





I had heard that there are roughly fifty Planet Walks installed in Germany alone, but I sometimes wonder why these miniatures of the solar system are placed on the ground. Is it because the space travel that once sparked near-future dreams is growing ever more distant? Or perhaps we simply need to concentrate on matters of the Earth so much that we must keep our feet firmly on the ground, leaving us no choice but to settle for substitutes. Whatever the background of their installation, they serve as perfect landmarks for distance traveled or turnaround points for walking and jogging enthusiasts. Casting sidelong glances at the planets, they draw closer to the sun, and then move away from it.





At first glance, the bronze sun and eight planets look like tourist guide maps. In addition to a simplified map of the Planet Walk threading through the city, they are inscribed with general information such as each planet's size, orbital period, number of moons, and distance from the sun. Furthermore, scaled planetary models are displayed there. The sun at the starting point is represented by a large metal sphere with a dull golden shine, its precarious balance supported by a bronze plaque. Looking at that sphere, it seemed as though it might gently float up into the sky, like a balloon swollen by the heat of the sunlight. In contrast, the presence of the planetary models was quite modest. A viewing window is set into the upper part of each bronze plaque, displaying a to-scale planetary object. That said, from Mercury to Mars, this amounts to little more than a pinhole-sized sliver of metal embedded in a thick plate of glass. Peering through the glass covered in fine scratches, the excessively small planets get lost in the distorted scenery. Only the planets orbiting beyond Mars finally acquire visual representation as objects.





The intervals between the sun and Mars are short. Nomiya and I, along with the dog, proceeded from the sun to Mars, halfway down Goetheallee. Every time we encountered a bronze plaque, Nomiya stopped, causing the rhythm of our walk and our conversation to stall. Since leaving the station building, we hadn't exchanged many words. I knew the temporal distance of nine years was the cause, but I also began to feel as if the Planet Walk was impeding our conversation. Yet, on some level, I welcomed this. These bronze signposts filled the gaps in our near-silent conversation.

Once we crossed the river flowing through the town, the street changed its name to Prinzenstraße. Though it transforms from Goethe to a Prince, there is no noticeable change in the street's atmosphere. From there, we didn't see another bronze plaque for a while, and, finally becoming flustered by the verbal void, I found myself giving him a disjointed tour of the town. We proceeded with an antiquarian bookstore, a pizzeria, an instrument shop, and a stationery store on our left, while I pointed out the silent facade of the library on our right. Eventually, near a flower shop and a secondhand bookstore facing the main avenue running north-south through the old town, we finally encountered the bronze Jupiter.





The dog I had brought along to greet Nomiya belonged to Agatha. She is my housemate; we have been renting an apartment together for a year now. She studied economics at a university in Hamburg, worked in an office for a while, and then came to Göttingen to study informatics.

Her dog was about the size of a beagle, with somewhat long black and white fur that formed a distinct pattern without the colors mixing. Most of his face and back were black, but his belly, paws, chest, nape, and part of his tail were white. He had a long snout and somewhat ambiguous features, as if another animal had been projected onto him.

When talking about this dog, everyone other than his owner, Agatha, usually referred to him not by his name, but as "the Truffle Dog." That moniker was already more familiar to our ears and tongues than his actual name, Hector. A "Truffle Dog" is literally a dog with a nose for finding truffles. Having memorized the scent of the special mushrooms and been trained to sniff out the aroma trapped beneath the soil, he had acquired the skill of unearthing truffles. Agatha's dog gained his nose for mushrooms from his original owner, her mother. She lived with the dog in a small town far south of Göttingen, freely enjoying her hobby of walking in the woods. However, I was told she passed away from breast cancer a year and a half ago.





Most of the anecdotes surrounding the Truffle Dog were also handed down from Agatha's mother. The small town where her mother lived was also practically contiguous with the forest, and supposedly, scent-filled mushrooms lay quietly hidden there. In the late summer three years ago, during their daily walk in the forest, Hector sniffed out a black truffle. Twitching his long snout, he suddenly dived into the bushes, pressed his nose against the ground, and began pawing at the dirt. As her mother watched him, he unearthed a black, round lump from the ground. The richly aromatic black object was small, but dense and heavy. Feigning innocence, she supposedly slipped it into her jacket pocket and took it home. Normally, the harvesting of wild truffles is prohibited in Germany for environmental reasons. *They'll overlook just one.* Her mother apparently laughed about it. However, she didn't leave that discovery as a mere coincidence.





Seeing the dog rub his nose against the pocket where the mushroom's scent lingered, the mother brought the truffle close to his nose again and again, weaving the memory of the scent into him. The forest plays a myriad of scents. The smell of water held within the greenery. Rotting fallen leaves and mold on the undersides of leaves. Earth thawing in the warmth of the sun. The cold, bluish-green scent of cypress. The dry fragrance of deciduous trees deeply layered with autumn colors. Into this, the new nuance of the truffle's scent was incorporated. Within the dog's olfactory field of vision, the forest appeared crystal clear. However, just as one cannot decipher in detail every color woven into an Impressionist painting, it remained a secret kept only between the forest and the dog. Though Hector became a Truffle Dog from then on, he never found more than three black mushrooms. *He has good manners toward the forest, doesn't he?* Agatha's mother would often smile as she buried her hands in the dog's soft, smoky black fur.





After the mother's death, the Truffle Dog was taken in by Agatha in Göttingen, and he adapted to the new environment. This city, too, is enveloped by forest. The remains of the city wall surrounding the old town make for a perfect walking course, and Agatha would take the Truffle Dog and loop around the ring-shaped forest. Every time I saw the owner walking with her dog bounding around her feet, I pictured a planet pulling its satellite along in orbit. On weekends, they would often venture further out, stepping into the forest that spread beyond the green space where Neptune's bronze plaque was located. Agatha didn't seem to have high expectations regarding mushrooms, but she often joked, *If we find any, we'll share the secret.* Once, she showed me a photo of a truffle her mother had found the year she died. Wrapped in a small, black, hard outer crust, it looked very much like a star that had fallen from the heavens and burned out.

Because the story of the Truffle Dog was repeated so often, the impression of the black, spherical mushroom's taste and aroma had already become woven into the story itself. Through this anecdote, my nose and mouth would involuntarily try to conjure up the impression of this unknown meteorite-like object. The name "truffle" taking on a life of its own. It was like a ghost haunting my senses of taste and smell.





From the streets of the town to the dog. Unable to get on track, I was recounting this little tale about truffles like a game of word association. Taking advantage of Nomiya's quiet, attentive ear, words mimicking Agatha's storytelling spilled from my mouth. What my accelerating tongue spun was nothing more than a fabrication borrowing the guise of storytelling. It was a bad habit of mine. To avoid core topics, I would escape into talking about animals as a way to string the conversation along. I had brought the Truffle Dog along as a first-aid measure for when the conversation frayed. Eventually, when the words ran out, Nomiya and I both fell silent, each pretending to be distracted by the Truffle Dog's behavior. The dog walked at a rhythm that didn't interfere with our pace, but he offered no hooks for conversation. Keeping the span of nine years in my field of vision, I was still merely circling around it endlessly. Was Nomiya doing the same? Even straining my ears against the silence, I could not fathom the depth of his quietude.

Upon reaching Jupiter's bronze plaque, we turned left at the corner and passed through the town toward the north. This Weender Straße, which divides the old town east to west, sees no cars save for mail trucks, garbage trucks, and police vehicles; only the feet of pedestrians tread its cobblestones.





Leaving Jupiter behind, we departed from the Planet Walk. Seeing a scale model whose presence finally registered in the viewing window only upon reaching Jupiter, Nomiya lightly traced its outline with his fingertip. *He always did this unconsciously when describing an artwork*, my memory faintly creaked. He would express the contents of a painting or sculpture in words, moving his fingers over the surface of a desk or in the air to depict the outlines and arrangement. As if trying to carve the memory into his fingers as well.

"We're heading toward the bus stop, so unfortunately, Jupiter is as far as we go."

"Is Saturn far from here?"

"Way down there. It's at the border of the old town, so you should take a look at it another time."

Nomiya readily lifted his finger from Jupiter and, without hesitation, proceeded into the bustle of the city and its unfamiliar-sounding words.





The crowds on Weender Straße were returning, but people walked with an awareness of distance from one another, and an increasing number were walking alone. *It's a main street, but it's not very crowded, is it,* Nomiya let his voice slip quietly, running his eyes over the street. When I had contacted Sawada, I had explained the situation in the city, but I didn't know how much of that had been conveyed to Nomiya. Since March, the shadow cast by the unfamiliar word *corona* had continued to mutate into something monstrous whenever you took your eyes off it.

"There was a cluster infection last month, so the situation regressed again. But soon enough, more people will be out and about," I said, and received a nod in return.

Just as it seemed the lockdown was easing, a sudden spike in numbers had immediately impacted the atmosphere of the city. The tension was once again starting to loosen, but I couldn't shake the stupefying sensation of having a door slammed shut right in my face. The sudden shift from relaxation back to tension had somehow put a massive crack in my sense of reality, and a lingering feeling like jet lag still made this place opaque. I should have been well aware of the fragility of reality, yet I must have unconditionally believed that its foundation was stable. Perhaps having received some explanation from Sawada, Nomiya voiced no questions; he simply moved his feet from the sunlight into the shade, gazing at the city. Rather than further explaining the surreal reality that had lasted for months, I limited myself to briefly conveying the rules under these circumstances. For him, what was necessary might not be words, but rather closing the distance to reality through his senses. How would he accustom himself to the nine-year time difference? How did this place look in Nomiya's eyes? This quiet life, distorted like the world beyond.





Along the way, the Truffle Dog nudged my leg with his nose, complaining of thirst. From the bag on my back, I took out a small bowl and a plastic bottle of water that had grown lukewarm, pouring it for the impatient dog. At the unexpectedly loud sound of the water, I flinched and checked on Nomiya. He was distracted, looking up at the buildings lining the street. Numerous faces were plastered to their facades, looking down toward Nomiya and meeting his gaze. A man's face bearing a bizarre smile, an indignant demon's face—these took on a kind of mask-like effect, plastered onto the city and casting their gazes downward. Buildings bearing faces and houses haunted by faces are another thing that still escapes my understanding. Will they, as I grow used to seeing them, become a part of my own time as much as the faces of my family, or, like house-bound ghosts, are they things from the other side of the boundary, treated as inconvenient lodgers?
