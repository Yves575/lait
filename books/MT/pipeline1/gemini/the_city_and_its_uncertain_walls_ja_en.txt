You were the one who told me about the city.

That summer evening, breathing in the sweet scent of grass, we followed the river upstream. We climbed over several small weirs, pausing now and then to watch the slender silver fish swimming in the pools. We had both taken off our shoes a while before. The clear water washed coolly over our ankles, and the fine sand of the riverbed enveloped our feet—like soft clouds in a dream. I was seventeen, and you were a year younger.

You had casually stuffed your low-heeled red sandals into a yellow vinyl shoulder bag and kept walking just ahead of me, stepping from sandbar to sandbar. Blades of wet grass clung to your wet calves, forming lovely green punctuation marks. I carried my battered white sneakers in my hands.

As if tired of walking, you casually sit down in the summer grass and look up at the sky in silence. Two small birds dart across the sky side by side, giving sharp cries. In the silence, the blue harbinger of evening begins to envelop us. When I sit down beside you, a strange feeling comes over me. It is as if thousands of invisible threads are delicately binding your body and my heart together. Even the momentary flutter of your eyelids, or the faintest tremble of your lips, stirs my heart.





At such times, neither you nor I have a name. Seventeen and sixteen, a summer twilight, vivid emotions on the grass by the river—that is all there is. Soon, the stars will begin to twinkle one by one above our heads, but the stars have no names either. In a world without names, we sit side by side on the grass by the river.

"The city is surrounded by a high wall," you begin. You draw the words from the depths of the silence. Like a pearl diver plunging into the deep sea with nothing but their own body. "It's not a very large city. But it's not so small that you can take it all in at a glance, either."

That is the second time you have mentioned the city. And just like that, the city acquires a high wall around it.

As you continue to speak, the city acquires a beautiful river and three stone bridges (East Bridge, Old Bridge, and West Bridge), a library and watchtowers, an abandoned foundry and modest communal housing. In the pale light of a late summer evening, you and I gaze at the city, our shoulders pressed together. Sometimes narrowing our eyes from a faraway hilltop, other times with our eyes wide open from close enough to touch it.

"The real me lives inside that city enclosed by the high wall," you say.

"Then the you in front of me right now isn't the real you?" I ask, naturally enough.

"Right. The me here right now is not the real me. She's nothing more than a stand-in. Just a fleeting shadow."

I think about that. A fleeting shadow? But for now, I decide to reserve my opinion.

"So, what does the real you do in that city?"





"I work at the library," you answer in a quiet voice. "My hours are from around five in the evening to around ten at night."

"Around?"

"Time there is always approximate. There is a tall clock tower in the central square, but it doesn't have hands."

I picture a clock tower without hands. "So, can anyone go into that library?"

"No. Not just anyone can go in freely. You need a special qualification to enter. But you can go in. You have that qualification."

"A special qualification—what kind of qualification is it?"

You smile softly. But you do not answer the question.

"But if I just go there, I'll be able to meet the real you, right?"

"If you can find the city. And if..."

You fall silent there and blush faintly. But I can hear the words you do not voice.

*And if you truly want the real me...* That is the phrase you dare not speak out loud. I gently slip my arm around your shoulders. You are wearing a pale green sleeveless dress. Your cheek rests against my shoulder. Yet, in that summer twilight, the one whose shoulders I embrace is not the real you. As you say, it is nothing more than your stand-in, a shadow.

The real you is inside the city enclosed by a high wall. There lies a beautiful sandbank overgrown with river willows, several low hills, and quiet, single-horned beasts wandering everywhere. The people live in old communal housing, leading simple but comfortable lives. The beasts like to eat the leaves and nuts of the trees growing in the city, but during the long, snowy winter, many of them will die of cold and starvation.





How fiercely I wished to enter that city. To meet the real you there.

"The city is surrounded by a high wall, and getting inside is very difficult," you say. "Getting out is even more difficult."

"How can I get in?"

"You just have to wish for it. But wishing for something from the bottom of your heart isn't that simple. It might take time. Along the way, you might have to discard a lot of things. Things that are important to you. But don't give up. No matter how long it takes, the city will not vanish."

I imagine meeting the real you inside that city. I picture the vast, lush apple orchards outside the town, the three stone bridges crossing the river, and the voice of the unseen nightbird. And the small, old library where the real you works.

"A place is always prepared for you there," you say.

"A place for me?"

"Yes. There is exactly one vacant position in the city. You will fill it."

What kind of position could that be?

"You will be a Dream Reader," you say in a hushed voice. As if confiding an important secret.

Hearing this, I can't help but laugh. "Listen, I can barely even remember my own dreams. It'd be pretty hard for someone like me to become a Dream Reader."

"No, a Dream Reader doesn't need to have their own dreams. All you have to do is read the many Old Dreams gathered in the library stacks. But it's not something just anyone can do."

"But I can do it?"

You nod. "Yes, you can do it. You have the qualification. And the me over there will help you with your work. By your side every night."





"I'm a Dream Reader, and I read lots of Old Dreams every night in the stacks of the city library. And you're always by my side. The real you," I repeat the presented facts aloud.

In my arms, your bare shoulders in the green dress tremble slightly. Then they suddenly stiffen. "That's right. But I want you to remember one thing. Even if I meet you in that city, the me there won't remember a single thing about you."

"Why?"

"You don't know why?"

I do know. Yes, the one whose shoulders I am gently holding right now is nothing more than your stand-in. The real you lives in that city. In a mysterious, faraway city surrounded by a high wall.

Even though the shoulders in my hands feel so smooth and warm, and I can't think of them as anything but the shoulders of the real you.

In this actual world, you and I live a short distance apart. It isn't awfully far, but neither is it close enough to just pop over on a whim. It takes about an hour and a half and two train transfers to reach the town where you live. And neither of the towns we live in is surrounded by a high wall. So, of course, we are free to come and go.





I live in a quiet suburban residential area near the sea, and you live in the heart of a much larger, bustling city. That summer, I am in my third year of high school, and you are in your second. I attend a local public high school, and you go to a private girls' school in your city. Due to various circumstances, we only actually see each other once or twice a month, something like that. We more or less take turns: I visit your city, or you come to mine. When I visit your city, we go to a small park near your house, or to the public botanical garden. The botanical garden charges an admission fee, but there is a cafe next to the greenhouse that is never too crowded, and it has become our favorite spot. There we can order coffee and apple tarts (a small luxury), and lose ourselves in quiet conversation just between the two of us.

When you visit my town, we almost always go for a walk by the river or along the beach. Since there are no rivers flowing near your home in the middle of the city, and certainly no sea, the first thing you want to see when you come to my town is the river or the sea. The sheer volume of natural water there—you are drawn to it.

"Looking at water calms me down for some reason," you say. "I like listening to the sound it makes."





We met by chance last autumn, and it has been about eight months since we became close. When we meet, we hold each other in places as far from public eyes as possible and gently share a kiss. But our relationship doesn't progress beyond that. A lack of time is one reason. There's also the practical matter of not being able to find a suitable place to form a deeper, more intimate connection. But more than that, the main reason is likely that we are completely engrossed in our conversations, devoting every spare moment to talking. Neither of us had ever met someone with whom we could so freely and naturally express our true feelings and thoughts. Meeting someone like that seems truly close to a miracle. That is why, whenever we see each other once or twice a month, we just talk, forgetting the passage of time. No matter how long we talk, we never run out of things to say, and when the time comes to part and we say our goodbyes at the station ticket gate, we always feel like we have forgotten to discuss many important things.





Of course, it isn't that I don't have physical desires. There is no way a healthy seventeen-year-old boy wouldn't be driven by sexual desire when faced with a sixteen-year-old girl with lovely, swelling breasts, much less when wrapping his arms around her supple body. But I instinctively feel that such things can wait. What I need for now is to see you once or twice a month, take long walks together, and talk frankly about all sorts of things. To intimately exchange information about each other and get to know one another more deeply. And to hold each other in the shade of a tree somewhere and share a kiss—I do not want to hastily introduce any other elements into such wonderful moments. If I did, something important might be damaged, and we might never be able to return to the way things were. Let's save the physical things for later. That is what I think. Or rather, my intuition tells me so.

But what on earth did we talk about, with our heads pressed together like that? I can no longer remember. We talked about so much that I probably became unable to pinpoint any single topic. But once you started talking about that special city surrounded by a high wall, it came to dominate our conversations.





Mainly, you would describe the origins of the city, I would ask practical questions about it, and you would provide the answers; through this process, the specific details of the city were decided and recorded. The city was originally something you created. Or perhaps it had existed inside you all along. But I believe I lent no small hand in building it up into something visible, something depicted in words. You spoke, and I wrote it down. Just as ancient philosophers and religious figures were accompanied by their own faithful, meticulous scribes, or people called apostles. As a capable scribe, or a loyal apostle, I even made a small, dedicated notebook to record it all. That summer, the two of us were completely absorbed in this collaborative work.

In autumn, the beasts' bodies are covered in radiant golden fur in preparation for the coming cold season. The single horn growing from their foreheads is sharp and white. They wash their hooves in the chilly river water, gently stretch their necks to devour red berries, and chew on the leaves of the broom.

It was a beautiful season.





Standing in the watchtower built along the wall, I wait for the twilight horn. Shortly before the sun sets, the horn is blown: one long blast, three short ones. That is the rule. The soft sound of the horn slips through the darkening cobblestone streets. The echo of the horn has likely been repeated without change for hundreds of years (or perhaps even longer). Its tone has seeped deeply into the crevices of the stone walls of the houses, and into the stone statues lined up along the hedges in the square.

When the sound of the horn echoes through the city, the beasts raise their heads toward ancient memories. Some stop chewing leaves, some stop tapping their hooves on the pavement, some wake from an afternoon nap in the last patch of sunlight, all lifting their necks at the exact same angle.

For a moment, everything is frozen like a statue. The only moving thing is their soft golden fur swaying in the wind—nothing else. But what on earth are they looking at? With their necks turned in one direction, staring into space, the beasts do not twitch a muscle. And so they listen intently to the echo of the horn.

When the last echo of the horn is absorbed into the air and fades away, they stand up, aligning their front legs, or stretch to adjust their posture, and begin to walk almost simultaneously. The momentary spell is broken, and for a while after that, the city streets are dominated by the sound of the beasts' tramping hooves.

The line of beasts makes its way down the winding cobblestone streets. No one takes the lead, and no one guides the procession. The beasts simply lower their eyes and sway their shoulders slightly from side to side as they descend a river of silence. Even so, it appears as though an undeniable, intricate bond connects every single one of them.





After watching them several times, I realize that the beasts' route and pace seem to be strictly determined. Gathering companions into their herd here and there, they cross the gentle arch of the Old Bridge and walk to the square with the sharp spire (the clock on the clock tower there, just as you said, is missing both its hands). There, they pick up a small group that had gone down to the sandbank in the river to graze on the green grass. They proceed upstream along the riverside road, pass through the factory district along a dried-up canal stretching north, and sweep up a group that had been foraging for nuts in the forest. Then they turn west, pass under the covered corridor of the foundry, and climb the long stairs up the northern hill.

The wall surrounding the city has only one gate. Opening and closing it is the duty of the Gatekeeper. It is a heavy, sturdy-looking gate, crisscrossed with thick iron plates. Yet the Gatekeeper pushes it open and closed with ease. No one other than him is permitted to lay a hand on the gate.





The Gatekeeper is a truly robust giant of a man, yet extremely devoted to his work. His pointed head is cleanly shaved, and his face is entirely smooth. Every morning he boils water in a large pot and meticulously shaves his head and face with a large, sharp razor. It is impossible to guess his age. Blowing the horn morning and evening to gather the beasts is also one of his duties. He climbs a watchtower about two meters high in front of the gatehouse and blows the horn toward the sky. Where in this rugged, almost coarse-looking man does such a soft, lustrous sound originate? I wonder this every time I hear the horn.

At twilight, once all the beasts without exception have been let out beyond the wall, he pushes the heavy gate shut once more and finally locks the large padlock. With a dry, chilling clack.

Outside the Northern Gate is a place for the beasts. There, the beasts sleep, mate, and give birth to their young. There are woods and thickets, and a small river flows through it. And that place, too, is enclosed by a wall. It is a low wall, just over a meter high, but for some reason, the beasts cannot cross it. Or perhaps they simply do not try.

On the walls on either side of the gate, six watchtowers are set up. Anyone can go up there via the old wooden spiral staircases. From the watchtowers, one has a sweeping view of the beasts' dwelling place. But normally, no one climbs up there. The residents of the city seem to have absolutely no interest in the lives of the beasts.





However, for just one week at the beginning of spring, people willingly climb the watchtowers on the wall to see the beasts fighting fiercely. During that time, the beasts become unimaginably wild compared to their usual selves; the males fight over the females, forgetting even to eat, battling with all their might. Growling, they try to plunge their sharp single horns into their rivals' throats and bellies.

For that one week of mating season, the beasts do not enter the city. The Gatekeeper keeps the gate closed so that no danger befalls the townspeople (consequently, the morning and evening horns are not blown during this period). A significant number of beasts are deeply wounded in the conflicts, and some even lose their lives. And from the red blood spilled upon the ground, a new order and new life are born. Just as the green branches of the willow trees all bud at once in early spring.

The beasts live within their own unique cycle and order, one we cannot possibly fathom. Everything is regularly repeated, and order is paid for with their own blood. When that violent week passes and the soft April rain washes away the spilled blood, the beasts return once again to their original tranquil and gentle existence.

But I have not actually witnessed such a sight with my own eyes. I only heard the story from you.

The autumn beasts remain crouched in their respective places, their golden coats shining in the evening sun, silently waiting for the echo of the horn to be absorbed into the air. Their numbers are likely no less than a thousand.

Thus ends a day in the city. Days pass, and seasons change. But days and seasons are, in the end, merely fleeting illusions. The city's true time exists elsewhere.





Neither you nor I visit each other's homes. We never meet each other's families, nor do we introduce each other to our friends. In short, we do not want to be interrupted by anyone—by anyone at all in this entire world. You and I are fully satisfied just spending time together, and we have no desire to add anything else. Moreover, from a purely physical standpoint, there is simply no room to add anything. As I mentioned before, we have a mountain of things to talk about, and the time we can spend together is limited.

You almost never talk about your family. All I know about them are a few fragmented facts. Your father was a local civil servant, but when you were eleven, some blunder forced him to resign, and now he works as a clerk at a cram school. I don't know what kind of "blunder" it was. But it seemed to be the sort of incident you didn't want to talk about. Your biological mother died of internal cancer when you were three. So you have almost no memory of her. You can't even remember her face. When you were five, your father remarried, and the following year your younger sister was born. So your current mother is your stepmother, but you once said something to the effect that you "might still feel a bit more affinity" for her than for your father. Like a casual footnote printed in tiny type in the corner of a page. As for your sister, who is six years younger, the only information I got was that "my sister is allergic to cat hair, so we can't keep a cat at our house."





When you were a child, the only person you could naturally and wholeheartedly feel close to was your maternal grandmother. Whenever you had the chance, you would take the train alone to visit her house in the neighboring ward. During school holidays, you would sometimes stay over for a few days. Your grandmother loved you unconditionally. From her meager income, she would buy you various little things. But every time you went to see your grandmother, you saw a look of displeasure on your stepmother's face, and though nothing was explicitly said, you gradually stopped visiting your grandmother's house. That grandmother, too, passed away suddenly from heart disease a few years ago.

You would tell me these circumstances bit by bit, in fragments. Like slowly scooping out some tattered objects from the pocket of an old coat.

Another thing I still remember well: whenever you talked to me about your family, for some reason you would always stare intently at your palms. As if, in order to follow the thread of the story, it was absolutely necessary to carefully decipher the palm lines (or whatever) there.

As for me, I could find almost nothing to tell you about my family. My parents are completely ordinary, run-of-the-mill parents. My father works for a pharmaceutical company, and my mother is a stay-at-home housewife. They act like ordinary parents and talk like ordinary parents. We keep one old black cat. I have nothing in particular to say about my school life, either. My grades aren't that bad, but they aren't outstandingly good, either. My favorite, most relaxing place at school is the library. I like to kill time there, reading books alone and losing myself in daydreams. I've already read most of the books I want to read in the school library.





I remember well the first time I met you. It was at the awards ceremony for the "High School Student Essay Contest." The top five prize winners were invited there. You and I were in third and fourth place, and our seats were right next to each other. It was autumn; I was in my second year of high school, and you were still in your first. The ceremony was a tedious affair, so during the breaks we would talk to each other briefly in low voices. You were wearing your school uniform: a navy blazer with a matching navy pleated skirt. A white blouse with a ribbon, white socks, and black slip-on shoes. The socks were stark white, and the shoes were polished flawlessly, without a single smudge. As if seven helpful dwarves had spent the hours before dawn polishing them with care.

It wasn't that I was particularly good at writing. I've loved reading since I was little and would pick up a book whenever I had free time, but I didn't think I had any talent for writing myself. But everyone in my class was forced to write an essay during Japanese class for the contest, and out of all of them, mine was chosen, sent to the selection committee, made it to the finals, and, unexpectedly, won a top prize. To be honest, I couldn't understand what was so excellent about my writing. Even when I read it over, it just seemed like a mediocre composition with no redeeming features. But well, if several judges read it and decided it deserved an award, there must have been something worthwhile in it. My homeroom teacher, a woman, was very happy that I won. In all my life, a teacher had never been so favorably disposed toward anything I had done. So I decided not to say anything unnecessary and gratefully accepted the award.





The essay contest was held jointly across the district every autumn, with a different theme assigned each year. The theme that time was "My Friend." Unfortunately, I couldn't think of a single "friend" I wanted to write about for five pages of manuscript paper, so I wrote about my pet cat. About how this old female cat and I got along, lived together, and communicated our feelings to each other—though with obvious limitations, of course. I had a lot to say about that cat. She was a very smart cat with a lot of personality. There were probably a few cat lovers among the judges. Because, for the most part, cat lovers naturally feel fondness and sympathy for other cat lovers.

You wrote about your maternal grandmother. About the emotional connection between a lonely elderly woman and a lonely young girl. About the modest, genuine values they forged together. It was a charming, touching essay. Several times better than what I had written. I couldn't understand why mine was in third place and yours was in fourth. I told you so honestly. You smiled brightly and said that, on the contrary, you thought my writing was several times better than yours. "It's true, I'm not lying," you added.

"Your cat sounds like a really wonderful cat."

"Yeah, she's a really smart cat," I say.

You smile.

"Do you have a cat?" I ask.

You shake your head. "My sister is allergic to cat hair."

That was the first tiny piece of personal information I got about you. Her younger sister was allergic to cat hair.





You are a very beautiful girl. At least, that is how you appear to my eyes. You are petite, with a rather round face, and your fingers are slender and pretty. Your hair is short, with neatly trimmed black bangs resting on your forehead. Like carefully curated shadows. Your nose is straight and small, and your eyes are very large. By the standard criteria of facial features, one might say the balance between your nose and eyes is off, but for some reason, I am drawn to that asymmetry. Your pale pink lips are small and thin, always dutifully closed. As if holding some important secrets hidden deep inside.

The five of us prize winners go up on stage in turn and are respectfully awarded our certificates and commemorative medals. The tall girl who won first place gives a short acceptance speech. The supplementary prize was a fountain pen (a fountain pen manufacturer was the sponsor of the contest; I would end up using that pen fondly for many years to come). Just before the long, boring awards ceremony concludes, I write my name and address in ballpoint pen on a memo page in my notebook, tear out the page, and secretly slip it to you.

"If you'd like, would you write me a letter sometime?" I say to you in a dry voice.

I don't usually do such bold things. I'm naturally shy (and of course, timid as well). But the thought of parting ways with you there and perhaps never seeing you again feels vastly wrong, completely unfair. So I gather my courage and take a leap.

With a slightly surprised expression, you take the slip of paper, fold it neatly into quarters, and tuck it into the breast pocket of your blazer. Right over the gentle, mysterious curve of your breast. Then you touch your bangs and blush just a little.





"I want to read more of your writing," I say. Like someone making a lame excuse after opening the door to the wrong room.

"I'd love to read your letters, too." And you give a few small nods. As if to encourage me.

Your letter arrives a week later. It is a wonderful letter. I read it over at least twenty times. Then I sit at my desk and write a long reply using the new fountain pen I'd just received as my prize. And so we begin our correspondence, and our relationship—just the two of us.

Were we lovers? Can we simply be called that? I don't know. But at least during that time, for almost a year, you and I had purely, flawlessly bound our hearts together as one. And soon, we built and shared a special, secret world known only to us—a mysterious city surrounded by a high wall.

I pushed open the door to the building on the evening of my third day in the city.

It is an old stone building with no particular distinguishing features. It sits just past the central square facing the Old Bridge, after a short walk east along the riverside road. There is no sign at the entrance; anyone who didn't know would never guess it was a library. A tarnished brass plate engraved with the number "16" is brusquely nailed to it, nothing more. The plate is discolored, the numbers hard to read.





The heavy wooden door groans deeply as it opens inward, revealing a dim, square room inside. There is no one around. The ceiling is high, the light from the wall lamps is feeble, and the air smells like someone's dried sweat. The gloom is such that everything seems faintly hazy, ready to dissolve into molecules and be sucked away somewhere. The worn cedar floorboards creak sharply here and there when walked upon. There are two tall, narrow windows, and not a single piece of furniture.

At the very back of the room, straight ahead, is a door. A simple wooden door with a small pane of frosted glass at about face height, also bearing the number "16" in an old-fashioned, decorative font. A pale light is visible through the frosted glass. I knock lightly twice and wait, but there is no reply. No footsteps either. After a moment's pause, I steady my breathing, turn the tarnished brass knob, and push the door open gently. The door squeaks, as if issuing a warning to the surroundings: "Someone is here."





Beyond the door is another perfectly square room, about five meters on each side. The ceiling is not as high as in the previous room. And here, too, there is no one around. There are no windows; it is enclosed by plaster walls. There are no paintings, photographs, posters, or calendars, and of course no clock—just smooth, bare walls. There is a crude wooden bench, two small chairs, a table, and a wooden coat rack. No coats are hanging on the rack. In the center of the room sits a rusted, old-fashioned woodstove with a fire burning brightly inside, and a large black kettle steaming on top. The far end of the room serves as a sort of checkout counter, upon which a ledger is left wide open. As if someone had been interrupted mid-task by urgent business. Most likely, that someone (presumably the librarian) will return to this room before long.

Behind the counter is a dark-colored door that seems to lead to the stacks. If so, then this must indeed be the "library." Though not a single book is in sight, an unmistakably library-like atmosphere remains. That special atmosphere shared by libraries all over the world, whether large or small, old or new.

I take off my heavy coat, hang it on the rack, sit down on the hard wooden bench, and warm my hands by the stove's heat while waiting for someone to appear. It is completely soundless all around. A silence like being at the bottom of deep water. I try clearing my throat once as a test, but it does not sound like clearing my throat.





It is about fifteen minutes later (or so I guess, for without a clock I cannot be sure of the exact time) when you open the door to the stacks and appear from within. Seeing me sitting on the bench, you stiffen in surprise for a moment, your eyes widening. Then you take a slow breath and say, "I'm sorry to keep you waiting. I didn't realize someone was here."

Unable to find the right words to say, I simply nod several times in silence. Your voice doesn't sound like your voice. It is different from the voice I remember. Or perhaps, in this room, all sounds and voices echo differently than usual.

Just then, the lid of the kettle suddenly chatters, trembling slightly like an animal waking from sleep.

"By the way, how can I help you?" you ask.

What I seek are Old Dreams.

"Old Dreams, is it," you say, pressing your small, thin lips into a straight line as you look at me. Of course, you do not remember me.

"But as you know," you say, "touching Old Dreams is restricted to the Dream Reader."

Silently, I take off my dark green glasses, lift my eyelids, and show them to you. There is no mistaking them: the eyes of a Dream Reader. Eyes that cannot go out into the glaring light of day.

"I understand. You have the qualification," you say, lowering your eyes slightly. Perhaps the condition of my eyes has unsettled you. But it can't be helped. In order to enter this city, I had to let my eyes be altered like this.

"Will you begin your work today?" you ask.

I nod. "I don't know if I'll be able to read them well yet, but I have to get used to it little by little."





The room is still completely devoid of sound. Even the kettle is keeping silent once again. Excusing yourself, you quickly finish the ledger work you had left half-done. I watch you from the bench. Visually, you haven't changed a bit. You look exactly as you did on that summer evening. I remember the bright red sandals you were wearing. And the grasshopper that suddenly leaped from the nearby grass.

"Haven't I met you somewhere before?" I find myself asking. Even knowing it's a useless question.

You look up from the ledger and, holding your pencil in your left hand (yes, you are left-handed, in this city just as in the other one), stare at my face for a moment before shaking your head.

"No, I don't believe we've ever met," you reply. You answer in a polite tone probably because, while you remain sixteen, I am no longer seventeen. To you, I am now a much older man. Even though it can't be helped, the passage of time pierces my heart.

Finishing the recording work you had started, you close the ledger, put it away on the shelf behind you, and make herbal tea for me. Taking the kettle from the stove, you carefully mix the hot water with crushed herbs to brew a dark green tea. Then you pour it into a large ceramic cup and place it in front of me. It is a special beverage provided for the Dream Reader, and preparing it is one of your duties.





I take my time drinking the herbal tea. The tea has a thick, distinct bitterness and is by no means easy to drink. But its nutrients heal my still-damaged eyes and calm my mind. It is a special drink meant for exactly that. You watch me from across the table. You are probably worried about whether I like the herbal tea you made. I give you a small nod. As if to say, *It's fine.* At that, a smile of relief comes to your lips. A nostalgic smile. I haven't seen it for a long time.

The room is warm and quiet. Even without a clock, time passes silently. Like a slender cat walking atop a wall, masking its footsteps.

We didn't exchange letters all that frequently. About once every two weeks or so. But each one was quite a long letter. And generally speaking, I think the letters you wrote were somewhat longer than mine. Not that the length of the letters held any particularly great significance in our correspondence, of course.





I've kept every single letter you wrote to me, but I never made copies of my own letters, so I can't really remember exactly what I wrote in them. But it couldn't have been anything that big a deal. I mainly wrote about my daily life and the small events happening around me. I wrote about the books I read, the music I listened to, the movies I watched. I wrote about things that happened at school. I was in the swimming club (though I only joined due to unavoidable circumstances and was far from a dedicated swimmer), so I think I wrote about our practices, too. With you as my audience, I could write naturally about anything. I could express my thoughts and feelings surprisingly well, exactly as I wanted to. It was the first time in my life I'd been able to write so fluently. As I said before, I had always thought I was bad at writing. You must have skillfully drawn that ability out from deep within me. You always appreciated the little bits of humor included in my writing. "That's probably what's most lacking in my life," you said.

"Like Vitamin something-or-other?" I said.

"Yes. Like Vitamin something-or-other," you said with a firm nod.

I was infatuated with you, and I think I was thinking about you almost constantly when I was awake. And probably in my dreams, too. But in my letters, I held myself back as much as possible so as not to confess those feelings outright. And I made up my mind to write only about practical, concrete things as much as possible. At the time, I suppose I wanted to cling to the world I could actually touch with my own hands—infusing it with a bit of humor, if possible. Because I felt that if I started writing directly about internal emotional movements, so to speak—like love and romance—I would end up driving myself deeper and deeper into a dead end.





In contrast to my letters, yours contained more about internal thoughts than concrete things around you. Or about dreams you had, or short little pieces of fiction. A few of the dreams in particular left a deep impression on me. You frequently had long dreams and could vividly recall their details. Just like remembering something that actually happened. To me, that was hard to believe. I myself rarely dream, and even when I do, I almost never remember the contents. The moment I wake up in the morning, those dreams all unravel and are sucked away somewhere. Even if I wake up with a start in the middle of the night after having a vivid dream (which rarely happens), I immediately fall back asleep, and when I wake up the next morning, I remember nothing at all.

When I told you this, you said:

"In my case, I keep a notebook and pencil by my pillow, and as soon as I wake up, I record the dreams I had that night. Even if I'm busy and pressed for time. Especially when I have a vivid dream and wake up in the middle of the night, no matter how sleepy I am, I write down the contents in as much detail as possible right then and there. Those are often important dreams, and they teach me many valuable things."

"Many valuable things?" I ask.

"Things about a me that I don't know," you answer.

For you, dreams were on almost the same level as events that actually happened in the real world; they weren't things to be easily forgotten or to vanish into thin air. Dreams were like a precious spring for your mind, communicating many things to you.

"That sort of thing is the result of training. If you make the effort, you'll surely be able to remember your dreams down to the smallest details, too. So give it a try. Because I really want to know what kind of dreams you're having."

Sure, I'll give it a try, I said.





But despite making a reasonable effort (even if I didn't go so far as keeping a notebook and pencil by my pillow), I just couldn't drum up any interest in my own dreams. The dreams I had were too scattered, lacked consistency, and were generally incomprehensible. The words spoken in them were blurred, and the scenes I saw rarely had any semblance of a plot. And sometimes, they contained disturbing content I could never share with anyone. Rather than dealing with that, I preferred to listen closely to the stories of your long, colorful dreams.

Occasionally, I would appear in your dreams. Hearing that made me very happy. Because, in whatever form, it meant I could participate in the imaginary world inside you. And you also seemed pleased that I showed up in your dreams. Though in most cases, my role in your dreams wasn't all that significant, more like a supporting character in a drama.

Did you never have explicit dreams that would be hard to mention in front of me—the kind of dreams I often had (dreams that sometimes, against my will, left my underwear soiled)? Were you honestly telling me every dream you had? That was what I always found myself wondering as I listened to your dream stories.

You seemed to speak frankly, hiding nothing. But no one knows the whole truth. It is my belief that there is no one in this world who does not harbor a secret in their heart. It's necessary in order for people to survive in this world.

Isn't that so?

"If there is anything perfect in this world, it is this wall. No one can cross this wall. No one can break this wall," the Gatekeeper declared.





At first glance, the wall looked like nothing more than an old brick fence. It looked as if it would easily crumble in the next strong storm or earthquake. How could he call such a thing perfect? When I said so, the Gatekeeper made a face like someone whose family had been insulted without cause. Then he grabbed my elbow and led me right up to the wall.

"Take a good look from up close. There are no joints between the bricks, right? And the shape of each individual brick is slightly different from the rest. Yet every single one fits together so perfectly there isn't room for even a single strand of hair."

He was right.

"Try scratching the bricks with this knife." The Gatekeeper took a utility knife from his jacket pocket, snapped the blade open, and handed it to me. It looked like an old, worn knife, but the blade was meticulously sharpened. "It won't leave a single scratch."

He was right. The tip of the blade only made a dry scratching sound; it didn't leave a single white mark on the brick.

"You see? Storms, earthquakes, cannons—nothing can bring this wall down. Nothing can even scratch it. It's never happened before, and it never will."

Leaving his palm against the wall as if striking a pose for a commemorative photo, he tucked his chin back and looked at me triumphantly.

No, there is no such thing as perfection in this world, I mutter in my heart. Anything that takes a physical form must surely have a weakness or a blind spot somewhere. But I do not say it aloud.

"Who built this wall?" I asked.

"Nobody built it," was the Gatekeeper's unwavering view. "It's been here from the very beginning."





By the end of my first week, I picked up several of the Old Dreams you had chosen for me and tried to read them. But those Old Dreams told me absolutely nothing of meaning. All I heard were faint, uncertain murmurs, and all I saw were several fragmented, unfocused images. It was like being shown an audio tape or film of randomly spliced fragments played in reverse.

In the library's stacks, countless Old Dreams line the shelves in place of books. It seems no one has touched them for many long years, and they are all covered in a fine layer of white dust. The Old Dreams are shaped like eggs, each varying in size and color. Like eggs laid by various kinds of animals. But they aren't exactly egg-shaped. When you hold one up close and look at it, you can see that the lower half is more swollen than the upper half. The balance of its weight is also lopsided. Yet because of this lopsidedness, it sits securely, and will not roll off the shelf even without support.

The surface is hard and smooth like marble. But it lacks the weight of marble. I have no idea what kind of material it is made of, or how durable it is. Would it break if I dropped it on the floor? In any case, they must be handled with great care. Just like handling the eggs of a rare creature.

There is not a single book in the library—not even one. Once, shelves of books must have lined the walls, and the townspeople likely visited in search of knowledge and pleasure. Like a normal town library. Such an atmosphere still lingered faintly in the air, like a lingering scent. But at some point, it seems all the books were removed from the shelves, and the Old Dreams were lined up in their place.





It seems there is no Dream Reader other than me. At least for now, I appear to be the only Dream Reader in this city. Was there another Dream Reader before me? There might have been. Judging by how detailed the rules and procedures regarding the Dream Reader are constructed and maintained, there probably was.

Your duty in the library is to protect and properly manage the Old Dreams lined up there. You select the dreams to be read and keep a record in the ledger that they have been read. Before evening, you unlock the library door, light the lamps, and, in cold seasons, start a fire in the stove. You ensure that the rapeseed oil and firewood needed for this do not run out. And you prepare the dark green herbal tea for the Dream Reader—that is, for me. It heals my eyes and calms my mind.





With a large white cloth, you carefully wipe the white dust accumulated on the Old Dream and place it on the desk in front of me. I take off my green glasses and place both hands on the surface of the Old Dream. I envelop it in my palms. After about five minutes, the Old Dream gradually awakens from its deep sleep, and its surface begins to glow faintly. A pleasant, natural warmth is transmitted to the palms of my hands. And then they begin to spin their dream. Like a silkworm spinning its thread—timidly at first, and eventually with considerable fervor. They have something to tell. They must have been waiting patiently on the shelf for the time to come out of their shells.

However, their speaking voices are so frail that I cannot fully catch their words. The images they project lack distinct outlines; they just fade, crumble, and are sucked into the air. Or perhaps it isn't their fault, but that my new eyes are not functioning well yet. It may be that my comprehension as a Dream Reader is not yet fully developed.

And so the time to close the library arrives. There is no clock anywhere, but when that time approaches, you naturally know.

"How is it? Is your work progressing well?"

"Little by little," I reply. "But just reading one wears me out quite a bit. Maybe I'm doing something wrong."





"No need to worry," you say, turning the knob to close the stove's air vent. You blow out the lamps one by one, sit across the table, and look me straight in the face (when you look straight at me like that, I feel flustered). "There is no need to hurry. We have all the time in the world here."

Following the prescribed procedures precisely one by one, you close up the library. With a serious look in your eyes, unhurried, with a steady calm. From what I can tell, the order of these tasks never fluctuates. Do you really need to lock up this library so strictly, I wonder as I watch you work. In this quiet, peaceful city, who on earth would break into the library in the middle of the night to steal or destroy Old Dreams?

"Do you mind if I walk you home?" On the third night, as we step outside the building, I take the plunge and ask.

You turn back and look at my face, your eyes wide. A single star from the sky is reflected in white in your dark pupils. It seems you don't quite understand the meaning of my offer. Why should you have to be walked home by me?
