The Clamor of the Past

Unless you can get ahead of the course of events and, conjuring the future, write a good ending. You can't, I know what I'm talking about. You're bogged down, your legs tangled in dried roots. The drought does you no favors; you could really use months of torrential rain—the roots would turn slick, and you'd pull yourself free that much faster. In the meantime, I encourage you to practice bidding farewell to memories. You've been running late for years; you must confront linear time. Memory loops are the domain of the elderly, for whom the passing years cut off retrospection. It’s time to set time free. Do it slowly, the body can play tricks, it might react to sudden change with shock. Turn the circle into a time axis, not a line segment—it sounds like a strange geometry problem.

I can't promise that once time finally moves forward, your life will become vibrant. Everything has its flaws and few virtues. Pull your head out of the clamor of the past. That is where you're from, but there's no one left there except you. Look around and throw away the things your children won't want to inherit. Do the same in your head, though that is much harder. Whatever you can—recount it in laments. Sometimes that works, allowing you to push the subject to a safe distance. Safe, meaning one that opens you up to thinking about your own life. Have you ever thought about it? Are you living well? Whose life are you living? It's no longer about whether they would be proud of you; that's over. Think about tomorrow starting today.







The English

They were really close now; all that was left was to leave the square and walk two hundred meters.

"If he starts yelling for the whole street to hear again, you're going by yourselves this time," she said.

Ever since the boys were born, Sunday dinners at the in-laws' had become mandatory. Fortunately not every week, but at least once a month. The scenario repeated itself down to the letter.

They would emerge from around the corner and look toward the exact same spot—the second-floor balcony. He would be standing there waiting, even though it was only a quarter to two. You could see him from afar, an older, red-haired gentleman gesticulating energetically. The closer they got, the clearer it became that he was not limiting himself to frantic hand movements.

"How old are you, for God's sake?! Can't you keep an appointment?! I've been standing here for fifteen minutes dying of fright that something had happened to you! How dare you put me through such stress?!"

"I said I'd do it, and I will. I can't take this anymore."

"Mom, come on, don't let it get to you. Grandpa is just like that."

She looked at her husband. He spread his hands helplessly and smiled a pleasant smile, raising his bushy eyebrows. She didn't want to come across as hysterical, but she'd had enough of these balcony scenes a long time ago.

"He's acting like a lunatic, maybe this time you'll actually say something to him?" she asked, though she knew the answer perfectly well.

"What can I say to him? He's telling the truth, he is dying of fright, and there's nothing we can do about it. Let's agree that when he dies, we'll stop coming over." He looked at her with a warm smile.

"Do you think Grandpa will die today?" asked one of the sons.

"I hope not just yet."

"What about you, Mom? Would you like Grandpa to die today?"

"I'd rather he didn't. We'll have plenty of excitement as it is."







Ignoring the increasingly loud shouts coming from the balcony, they reached the building entrance, passed the mailboxes, and headed up the stairs. They never took the elevator; it was so small there was no way all four of them could fit inside.

Grandpa would be waiting in the open doorway. First he would bend down to the older grandson, squeezing him too tightly and a little too long.

"My firstborn, my firstborn. Let me look at you—my, how smart you look. Who would have thought you'd even be born!"

Then came the younger one's turn. He hugged him too tightly, too.

"You've got a black mop, maybe it'll lighten up. Good thing you're not a ginger."

Without taking his eyes off the boys, he would hug his son, and finally greet his daughter-in-law.

"You've borne me beautiful grandsons. Come in, come in."

"Dad, could you spare us the yelling from the balcony next time? We were supposed to be here at two, we arrived fifteen minutes early, and you're screaming that we're late?"

"Everything will be fine, don't worry. I was afraid a car had hit you or some other misfortune... The most important thing is that we're all alive and together. By the way, I wonder if I'll live to see great-grandchildren. Well, boys, can I count on you?"

"Who knows if you'll live that long," the younger grandson said.

"Oh, please, how witty!" Grandpa remarked with a smile.







After the greetings, they would go into the dining room, where a large round table stood. A white tablecloth, plates with a gold trim, and silver cutlery—everything was already set. An old woman sat at the table, tiny and shrunken. She had suffered from Alzheimer's for years and did not react to the noise that filled the room.

"Boys, say hello to Grandma," the father would say.

They didn't like getting close to her; they didn't understand what was happening to her. She rarely spoke, and never in Polish. They would walk up, quickly kiss her on the cheek, and sit in their seats. Sometimes a barely noticeable smile flitted across her wrinkled face; it was hard to say whether she was aware of it.

Dinner was cooked by Grandpa's housekeeper. She would place the pots and pans on the windowsill and leave, despite sincere invitations to sit down at the table with everyone. Chicken soup with homemade noodles, minced meat patties, mashed potatoes, and lettuce with sweet cream, which no one but Grandpa ate. The youngest grandson would quickly devour his entire dinner to get a second helping of soup before it vanished back into the kitchen. Afterward, he would politely ask to be excused from the table and lie down on the floor, where he played with his toy cars.

The rhythm of these dinners was invariable. The whole thing lasted about an hour and a half, then Grandpa would take a nap, and they would leave, stopped by no one.

"A bodybuilder from Dachau picked up a brick and got winded," the younger grandson blurted out one Sunday, lying on the floor with his belly full of soup.

"Oh God!" Grandma moaned in Polish.







Silence fell, and the parents' eyes turned to the older son.

"I didn't teach him that!"

"My friends at school taught me," the youngest family member replied, completely unfazed.

"Why did you decide to share this with us right now?" the father asked.

"I just remembered it."

"Look at him, he just remembered! And do you know that some people never managed to forget?" an agitated Grandpa asked.

"Yeah, I know."

"And how do you know, if I may ask?"

"From you guys, you're always talking about it. I don't understand why you always talk about Jews."

"Because we are Jews," Grandpa answered.

"All of us?"

Grandpa looked around and smiled.

"Yes, all of us."

"Me too?"

"You too."

The boy returned to his toy cars for a moment.

"I'd rather be English," he said after a long silence.

"Want me to tell you something?" Grandpa asked.

"Yeah, tell me."

"I'd rather be English, too."

Grandpa reached for his glass of tea, but it had already grown so cold it no longer interested him. He got up from the table and went to take a nap.





I Tell Everyone



This guy was the prankster type. He loved medicine and women, in that exact order. And half the city loved him back. The half he treated. The other half was divided into those he drove crazy and those who were indifferent. He was ginger and freckled, which meant hot-tempered. He could punch someone in the face without warning, down a half-liter, and carry on as if nothing had happened.







He had managed to live to see the second half of the twentieth century, and he decided to seize the opportunity and live without denying himself any pleasures. He considered his quota of drama exhausted. His wife handled raising the child; he only got involved when the situation required a doctor. He believed a father's lot in life was a thankless one, like in that joke about the picky-eater son. The whole family was worried because the kid was skinny and wouldn't eat a thing. Finally, the father couldn't take it anymore and asked what his little boy would like most of all. The little boy thought about it and answered: shit. What do you mean, shit? Just shit. Shit on a plate. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, after which he got up and went to the bathroom. He returned with shit on a plate. He set it in front of his son. The boy looked at it and said he wanted fried shit. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, after which he got up and went to the kitchen. He came back, set it in front of his son. The son looked at it and said he wanted Daddy to try it. Mother at father, father at mother. He tried it, said it was exquisite, and encouraged his son to finally start eating. I won't eat it, because Daddy ate the best part.







The household ran smoothly, the son grew like a weed, and the ginger doctor kept meeting new women and spending delightful moments with them. When asked by friends how he was doing, he would tell the joke about the Jew who goes to a priest to confess. The priest asks if the penitent is Catholic. He isn't. Then why is he coming to confession? Because it's very important to him. The priest enters the confessional, and the Jew starts telling him about all the women he's slept with lately. How this one was blonde, that one had curly hair, and yet another had such small breasts... Why did you choose me, of all people, to tell this to? the priest asks. I tell everyone.







His wife pretended not to hear. After what they had been through, she knew that life could bring far more serious problems. Sometimes she complained to her friend, who, upon returning home, would ask her husband to have a word with the doctor. They would arrange to meet, go out for vodka, and the family friend would delicately reveal the mission he'd been sent on. The redhead would look at him and say that, at this very moment, he was reminded of the joke about the tax collector who goes to the Jewish community. What do you do with the wax from melted candles? We collect it and make new candles out of it. Aha. And what do you do with the crumbs from the matzo? Oh, from those we make delicious matzo balls. Aha. And when you circumcise little boys, what do you do with those, you know, things? Ah, with those. We collect them, send them to the tax office, and from there they send us pricks like you.

He snuffed out friendly impulses in the blink of an eye. If his interlocutor was persistent, he would call the waitress over and ask her to open a window. Nothing to be afraid of, he would say, my friend here is no eagle, he won't fly away.

The son married a tiny brunette, a fine arts academy graduate. The doctor admittedly had other plans for him, but he accepted the news and stopped searching for the perfect match.







The ginger doctor's wife began to suffer from rapidly progressing Alzheimer's. He took excellent care of her. He brought a young girl from the provinces into the home. She cooked, looked after his sick wife, and things went quite well between her and the doctor, too.

For his grandsons, he became a wonderful doctor, but a strange grandfather who had his moments. Once a year, he would take them shopping. To everyone's surprise, he might buy a pool table or a bicycle. Sometimes he took them to a restaurant, and after placing the order, he would quickly disappear. Only years later did the growing grandsons realize that the main reason for these excursions was by no means a desire to spend time with them, but rather a waitress in a short skirt. He didn't know how to be a grandfather, yet he was proud that the family was growing.

A year after his wife's death, he invited his older grandson to dinner. After they chose their dishes, he didn't leave the table as usual. While they waited for the food, Grandpa remembered a joke about Rosenzweig, who paces around the house all night. Around five in the morning, his furious wife gets up and asks him why he's pacing so much. I'm pacing because I can't sleep, Rosenzweig answers. Why can't you sleep? Because I owe Hozenduft money. A lot? A lot. Then call him and tell him you won't pay him back. The husband thinks for a moment and calls. It's Rosenzweig. Yes, I know what time it is. I just wanted to tell you that I won't pay the money back, goodnight. And he hangs up the receiver. And now what? he asks his wife. Go to sleep, now he's the one who can't sleep.







The grandson, despite being trained in the art of the joke since childhood, doesn't understand why Grandpa is telling him this right now. A shapely waitress brings the food. Grandpa smiles at her, but stays at the table.

"Listen, there is a Jewish law that says a healthy man should not be alone. This law also says that when a wife dies, the husband can marry her sister. Your grandmother's sister perished back then, when everyone else did."

What's going to happen now? the grandson thinks. Grandpa takes a deeper breath and announces that he's getting married. He immediately adds that his son won't be pleased with his choice of wife.

"How do you know?" the grandson asks.

"Something tells me. People rarely like their stepmothers. My half-sister couldn't stand my mother."

"Did she have her reasons?" asks the grandson.

"Oh, she had every reason!"

The grandson worries he's been delegated to break this strange news to his father. He senses it will be neither easy nor pleasant. Deliverance comes unexpectedly.

"But for now, let's not say anything to your father..." Grandpa decrees, then pauses. "You choose a second wife, one for your old age, without thinking of others. You look for someone who understands you. Even if you know she speaks before she thinks, that she is difficult, that she hurts everyone around her. You go for it because you want to drown out the loneliness. You figure you'll deal with the mismatch later."

The grandson is a teenager and feels that later might be too late, but these are adult matters, after all. He doesn't yet know that in his family, the stepmother type will turn out to be hereditary.











An Underwater Odyssey




A lie doesn't have short legs. His father always said it did, but he didn't realize how wrong he was. It floats to the surface when you pay too little attention to hiding it.

When he was a few years old, he didn't lie—they said he made up beautiful stories. When he became a teenager, they started calling it lying. He didn't dwell on it much; he managed his life on his own terms. 

He forged his value system to fit the need of the moment.

The most important rule: calm parents—calm life. There were some loopholes in this system, but over time it became an integral part of the cycle: ensure the parents' peace of mind between parent-teacher conferences, and survive their return from the conference with dignity and hope for a better tomorrow. Find solace in the post-argument household silence and don't take the grievances or anger personally. After a few days, give them something to lift their spirits; it didn't have to be true, all that mattered was that it worked temporarily. Remember that the "calm parents—calm life" rule is the priority, and that the best moments flow between parent-teacher conferences. Don't push your luck, but don't surrender by forfeit, either.

He was a cheerful boy, the first post-war child in his family. He sensed they tolerated unreliability poorly. He naturally developed a modus operandi. You could say he drew his patterns from nature. He flowed like a stream—where there was less resistance, not where he was expected to. A principle as simple as it was frowned upon.
