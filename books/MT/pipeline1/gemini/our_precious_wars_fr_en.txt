GALLIMARD


Cool rain on a blue lawn. Damp summer grass, the rich scent of black earth. Always these August showers on the short, gold-burnt stems. The heavy drops stream down the windowpane, winding, meandering, and intertwining into long ribbons of liquid light. How many afternoons spent behind the diaphanous veil of the curtain, tracing their nervous, languid paths with a finger. Wisps of hair curl around the cheeks, and you marvel that they are so white when you are so young, haloed in ether beneath the window. And suddenly your gaze drops from the window to the hand parting the curtain, and the hand is old, so old.

There are places that harpoon you. That wind their netting around your dreams, that sink their claws in just enough to let you grow, but leaving the bruise of their grip in your flesh.
There are doors whose sound, when you push them, is like a cry of time, shattering oblivion once again.
There are staircases whose steps you would so love to climb again, just once, letting the cold polish of the banister glide through your palm.





That is the House.

There are, on the other hand, places that slide the clammy unease of the stranger down the back of your neck. Places you do not feel resonating within you. Places forced upon you, that you are made to endure; it’s temporary, it’s transient, you have no choice. When the world tires of you, they offer you the TV by the bed, the nightstand for your glasses case, the yellow wallpaper. Yellow is a cheerful color, and besides, you have everything you need here, you'll be well taken care of. They make us trade the House for a room in a medically sanctioned hospice. This yellow wallpaper—a sledgehammer blow to the back of my old neck.

Anyway, the armchair is the only thing I love anymore. It’s the only piece of furniture I was able to bring with me. It has always been an old person’s chair; great-grandfather’s, whom I never knew, then great-aunt Babel’s, whenever she came to visit. I understand why you grow to love it as you age; you recognize yourself in its dust, in its sagging softness. With the tip of my fingernail, I tease the scuffs in its dusty-rose velvet.
The comfort of wedging your arms into the intimacy of the armrests. The fabric slips right up against you like a second skin. It’s like returning to the embrace of a sweet-talking lover, one who has remained far more constant than any man I could ever have known.





Thinking it over, I was the only one who truly loved the House. Even great-great-grandfather, who desired it, imagined it, built it, did not love it as much as I did. I loved it enough to stay there my entire life. To abandon my studies in the City, because I suffered too much, far from the woods of my childhood. To say no to Oktav when he held out that little ring on the Black Bridge one Wednesday afternoon, for he, for one, would never have wanted us to live in the House. He wanted to move into a large apartment in the old center, not far from the high school where he would have taught and where our future children would have learned to surpass us. I loved the House enough to wish for nothing else, in my whole existence, but to remain there, nestled in the hollow of familiar things, letting time weather me to a patina exactly like the banister of the spiral staircase.

I always close my eyes when I want to remember it. I do it every day, perhaps every waking minute. I force myself with impatience, with violence even, to recreate every room, every nook. I cling to the details, to the shape of the light switches, to the sound of doorknobs turning, to the thin layer of dust on the yellow lightbulbs. I want to see everything, to feel everything again. I want to hollow out the space of the present and, with frantic bursts of memory, bring back the places I loved so much, that I knew by heart, that I roamed my whole life, and which, now that I am no longer there, are fading, crumbling away.





The House as I left it, the one from four months ago, maybe five—I remember it. I remember it a little too well, in fact. The slow decay of deserted rooms, of dull floorboards, of cold corridors where the paint is peeling—that, yes. The broken pane of the stained-glass window through which the dead leaves blow in, swirling weakly on the hall floor like an exhausted couple waltzing at the end of the night—I still see it, and very clearly. And also the cast-iron radiators, a faded white, freezing cold in the rooms where no one lives anymore, the walled-up fireplaces, the sheets draped over the furniture in the bedrooms where no one sleeps anymore. That was how it was, at the end.

I want to banish this image of the House. Revive the colors, throw wide the bright windows to let the intoxicating morning air sweep in, wax the staircase, clean the immense dining room table. Set the table for some fifteen people, like in the golden days when everyone, without exception, came.





I want to see again the tall House of white wooden planks that suddenly loomed when we returned from the garden, nestled between the great blue firs and the maples, with the grass breaking into emerald spray against the flight of front steps. To see again the facade with its narrow windows, with the veranda jutting out on the left side, the tall, pointed roof bristling with chimney stacks and immaculate white gables, with the bullseye window of the attic, perched like the round, golden eye of a reassuring giant, all hemmed in carved wood. I want to see again the strange, sharp roofs that capped the bow windows, which, in winter, were fringed with icicles. I want to see again the windows that sparkled in the crisp air, brushed by the branches of trees too close to the House whenever a light breeze blew. I want to see again the great House, with the carved wooden columns framing the front door, glazed in a warm varnish of solid caramel, and the stained-glass window of intertwined flowers that let shards of color filter into the hall when the sun shone through. Painted with immense trellises of tropical foliage, the hall glowed with a soft blue. There the spiral staircase surged upwards, in a whirlwind of coppery wood.





Everything was open; the scents of the kitchen circulated freely and invaded the entire House. Voices rumbled from the living room to the attic like the murmur of a comforting torrent rolling through the walls. Deep in my ear, I hear it still. The floorboards creak and give off the scent of pine, and even our lightest child-footsteps make the thick planks groan. We run up the stairs, because it’s more fun to be dizzy when we reach the top. The first-floor hallway leads to my uncle and aunt’s old bedrooms, the ones from when they were little, which they still occupy when they come back to see us with the cousins. They sleep right across from our room—Harriett’s and mine. It’s more practical for playing; they only have to cross the hall and whisper the password at the door so we know for sure they aren’t meddlesome adults coming to scatter everything with their long, clumsy legs. At the very end of the hall is the bathroom, the green one, with its window that sticks a little, but through which you can catch a glimpse of the orchard. Opposite is great-aunt Babel’s room, for when she comes to set down her trunks oozing with camphor. When everyone leaves, we have the floor almost entirely to ourselves. We can hardly play on the second floor; it’s the parents’ floor, the serious floor, the one with the two most spacious bathrooms. Well, Louisa, the lucky thing, has a private one, with a small ceramic bathtub, behind her wardrobe. Because she is the eldest of the girls, she has the biggest room, and she has it all to herself. Yet I wouldn’t trade my room for hers for anything in the world, because if I did, Harriett and I wouldn't be able to make as much noise, given the proximity to the parents' room. That is also where the storage room is, which we don’t even use for hide-and-seek anymore: we know full well it’s the first place anyone thinks of when they need to slip away from the world. The best place to hide is, of course, the attic. It is smaller than when Little Father was a child because, since then, they split it in two to build Klaus's room. I quite like Klaus’s room. It smells like a boy, because he doesn’t open the window much. Since the adults never go up there, we are left in peace. You can hear all the creaks of the roof on stormy nights, and there is a reassuring je-ne-sais-quoi about this warm garret, like a wooden cocoon suspended above the forest.





But the images roll by in faded colors, drained of life. The faces are blurred, and that is the most painful thing—realizing that the image of my family, young, alive, is lost forever. I have lost myself as well. Who was I, at eight years old? Now that old age breaks my back and cripples my fingers, I sense how annoyed I would have been, as a child, by my presence today, burdened by what is no more. I would hate the puppet theater I erect in my mind to endlessly replay these dead images. Who were we, in the woods and in the bedroom, in the kitchen where the soup steamed? I dangle puppets whose faces are fading. No matter; there comes a time when certain people are so familiar to us that we no longer even need their physical presence for them to be there. We can only truly say we know the people we grew up with. Our own specific evolution has been lightly tinted by that of the others, like water into which a drop of syrup falls—just one, enough to color the entire glass pale mint. I know exactly what Little Father or Louisa would have answered to such-and-such a remark, I can still see Little Mother’s smile when she watched us dance on spring evenings. And so, it seems I relive for a time, in this body of today, this painful, creaking body, the days that ended and the days that followed them, deep within the House.





I want to remember the intertwining voices of glorious children, the cold, reserved tenderness of Little Father, the sudden bursts of inspiration of Little Mother when she would leap up from the table, grab the brushes from the jar on the sideboard, and rush to her studio in a corner of the veranda. I want to remember when Klaus, Louisa, and Harriett were still at the House, Klaus already on the cusp of adolescence. He was handsome, the prodigy older brother—brilliant, funny, insolently talented. I would like to grumble again when he ran through his scales at six in the morning and the sweet, raspy wail of his trumpet woke the whole family. I want to find Louisa again, the prettiest of the girls, as if she had absorbed all of our mother’s radiance, leaving none for me or Harriett. I remember that when I walked by, dragging my feet on the second-floor floorboards, past Louisa’s half-open door, she was neither on her bed nor in her armchair, but always in front of the mirror of her vanity, melting her gaze into her own reflection, watching for the gold flecks deep in her irises.





What I wouldn't give to be back in the room with the two little beds side by side, Harriett’s and mine, with the lamp in the middle—the cause of so many arguments between us, since I always wanted to read later than she did! Her grumpy baby face that she hid under the duvet, screaming that I was preventing her from getting her eight hours of sleep... "You'll just have to get up later," I would gloat, slowly turning the pages of my novel to infuriate her. "You know perfectly well that Klaus is going to wake us up again with his cursed trumpet, so turn it off!" she would protest, indignant, tears in her eyes, punching her pillow. "I’ll turn it off when I finish my chapter." "Let me see where you are." I would show her a fake page. "See, almost done," I would assure her. She would start hissing like a snake: I was lying, I was the worst sister, if she told Louisa... She would leave the threat hanging and shoot me a furious glare from under the sheets. Harriett constantly deferred to our older sister to settle our disputes, because Louisa was absolutely biased and unjust. She always sided with our youngest sister, who had adorable dark curls that Louisa loved to style, while she claimed my mop of hair was "perplexifying." But since Harriett was far from docile, more often than not Louisa would storm out of the room, slamming the door, and lock herself in hers to dream of grand chandeliers and dinner parties with friends.





Even the House of my adolescence, I would like to see again. The one that haunted me every night in my student room, making me hate the College and curse my studies. They had torn me away from my home, from my life, from my walks in the woods and my afternoons spent reading, curled up on the too-hard windowsill. But the triumph of Friday evenings! When I rushed onto the train, bounded into my seat, stamping with impatience, my lips stretched into an irrepressible smile, and the inexorable machine carried me far from the towers of the City! How I loved the arrival at the station, which nevertheless seemed to take forever, and the moment I saw Little Father by the open trunk waiting for my suitcase, with Harriett already in the car, for she finished earlier on Fridays.

"Mrs. Aberfletch? Can you hear me? I’m putting your tray on the desk; you can eat whenever you like."
And here is that kind nurse, coming to dispel everything in her rose perfume. To hell with her, with her white coat and her insolent youth, her clear voice of a living girl, who gets to go home at night. She must wait for summer all year long—that season of the liquid body, of blonde evenings.


Summer


I loved nothing so much as summers at the House. Everything radiated then, in the humid languor of the holidays, which seemed infinite, stretched out by long days of delicious boredom. From the first heats of June, everything began to sparkle, to overflow with life. The maples and firs seemed gorged with an incandescent sap; the grass, an insolent green, was crossed by broad swathes of sunlight.





Everyone would return from the City, flowing back toward the familiar countryside and the forests haloed in lustral shadow. You only had to stand at the edge of the woods to feel the resin-scented wind exhale its murmur. When we were children, my father’s brother and sister, Uncle Bertie and Aunt Hilde, came every summer with their families. The joy when it was announced: "The cousins are arriving tonight!" The day would then become steeped in an impatient agitation that had us skipping in circles in the living room, until, scolded by Little Father, we would head back up the stairs, stifling our nervous giggles. The afternoon was spent in bustling preparations; I loved assisting my parents, paying meticulous attention. Like a little despot, I made sure the attic was aired out, the toy chests open, the sheets neatly folded on the beds. I would fly down the spiral staircase that had been waxed for the occasion, I would twirl in the hall crossed by the fractured light of the stained glass. I would pop into the kitchen twenty times, lean over the stove, and breathe in the aromas, lifting the lids of the soup tureens with a slightly trembling hand. I knew that the almost-ready food was the signal: the cousins would soon be here, and shortly, we would all be seated at the table together in an unbearable hubbub of booming laughter, releasing the frenzy of a long day of waiting.





The evening of the cousins' arrival, at the start of every summer, was always the same. They were usually exhausted from the drive they had endured all day, the cramped quarters of the cars, the hot breath of the asphalt, the sun glaring off gleaming chassis. Nevertheless, we sat down to eat with gusto. When everything seemed to waltz with lights and laughter and scents, when we were constantly interrupted in our stories by a passing dish or another story erupting from the other end of the table, and when I leaned back in my chair to stealthily study those we hadn’t seen since Christmas, I felt immersed in a bath of liquid joy, golden and sparkling, perfumed by the honey-scent of Uncle Bertie’s cigars or the thyme melting into the chicken juices. My gaze would sweep around the table and flow mechanically over the familiar faces, catching on those of the cousins, always prettier year after year. I was fascinated most of all by their noses, small and round, which came from Bertie’s in-laws. He had not escaped the family nose, hooked and long, down which the light slid as if along a blade. Amelia and Magda took entirely after their mother—a sort of blonde, fleshy grace, with skin perfectly pink beneath the neck, a skin like fresh flowers pearled with water in crystalline vases. Always sitting side by side, carefully passing the pitcher when it came to them, they would laugh openly at all of Klaus’s or Harriett’s antics. Every summer, the two cousins, as soon as they stepped out of the car and set foot on the front steps, would size each other up with Louisa. In those first moments of their reunion, one never knew if they and Louisa were going to tear each other to pieces or trade pretty-girl barbs; but no, they would fall into each other's arms and head into the House laughing, trailing a scent of precious oils in their wake. Harriett and I spent little time with the cousins, who shared Louisa’s temperament and were the same age as her. We were the little nuisances—noisy, and a bit unnerving, I think. Klaus showed no particular interest in the cousins and sat at the table next to Aleksander, my Aunt Hilde’s darling son. Aleksander paired the traditional Aberfletch nose with a steely gaze that contrasted sharply with the murky pond-like depths of our own eyes. He had hair slightly more ashen and pale than ours, and his very upright head caught the reflections of the dining room lamp, which cast its warm halo over the entire table. Aleksander and Klaus didn't get along especially well, but being the eldest boys, they had learned to stick together in a sort of defiant brotherhood, halfway between competition and solidarity. They competed in dexterity when pouring themselves water: it was a contest to see who could lift the carafe the highest without the thin stream deviating from its course. We would clap our hands, staring at the churning water rapidly filling the glass, and shriek when it was about to overflow.





The adults were on edge; they would send us to bed early, telling us not to "raise a ruckus until all hours like last year." The cousins and we would then exchange a joyful glance, our complicity suddenly rekindled by this reminiscence of a shared past. For a brief moment, memories drowned in summer light paraded in our eyes, trembling with laughter and scraped knees. We would dance the cousins to their room, thrilled to have them back at our house, and Klaus would make his good, breaking voice rumble, playing the "grown-up" by saying, "I'm heading up to my room; be good, children." Aleksander would grumble a bit, because he was the same age as Klaus but had to sleep with the two cousins, whom he blithely ignored most of the time. Then Louisa and Klaus would disappear back into the gloom of the staircase, and we—Harriett, the cousins, and I—remained in the dark hallway, the neutral territory between our two rooms. Through the half-open door, we were pleased to see that their suitcases had been miraculously brought up by a devoted parent, and rested on their respective beds in the lamp’s yellow halo. This room remained empty all year, the bedframes bare and cold, except when the cousins came. Then we would unfold large, crisp sheets for them, still scented with the sprigs of lavender scattered in the wardrobes. The cousins always had tired eyes that first night, but they seemed genuinely happy to be back. Harriett and I would linger in their doorway, watching them rush to the window as if to check whether the view had changed since last summer. Then, satisfied, they would rub their eyes, yawning, and wish us a good night. I would grab Harriett by the shoulder to force her back into our room, because she was far too worked up to sleep and wanted to play, right away, a game of hide-and-seek in the dark, or spy on the adults, or sneak up on Klaus in his attic, climbing the stairs ever so softly to keep them from creaking. I would tell her that we had every night of the entire summer to play all that, and she would follow me with a resignation tinged with pleasure.





In my bed, lying with my eyes wide open, turned entirely toward the ceiling drowned in blue shadow, I felt the weight of the two floors above me, and I could picture the bustling of the adults below, clearing the table, with the warm rumble of their gruff voices. I listened to the sounds of the night settling in—Louisa upstairs, walking with hurried steps from her washroom to her bed; the cousins across the hall putting their things away in the cupboards, unpacking, settling in, reclaiming the room they returned to every summer. I felt surrounded by the people I loved, as if I were part of a whole, and the House, finally full of this large family of the same blood as mine, was truly alive. I knew it wasn't too tragic if we went to bed a little early today, because there would be others—other nights. Harriett’s little raspy voice would always eventually rise in the silence, venturing a: "It's nice that they're here, the cousins." On other nights, I would have told her to shut up, because I had no desire to talk. But here, I was snuggled in my sheets, and outside the summer wind was blowing softly against the shutters. So I simply answered: "Yes, it really is." We would talk about what we were going to do tomorrow. In the near-total pitch of the room, all I could make out of Harriett was a small dark mass on her pillow, all puffed up with pale shadow, enormous compared to her head. And this was my favorite moment of the day, full of promises, plans, ideas made even wilder because they were already entangled in a dream-filled semi-consciousness. We slipped into the silence like a spark on the surface of the pond, and the waves of sleep closed over our eyes.





We let the days slip away like a trickle of liquid light. It was the precious time of elastic hours, of evanescent mornings, of infinite afternoons.

Morning was always a time of every man for himself. You hardly noticed the House was populated by anyone else, so silent was everything, with the closed doors of the late sleepers, the living room empty but still smelling of Bertie's honey cigar.





I always woke up before Harriett, because my bed was closest to the window, and a ray of white sun always filtered through the crack in the shutters, cutting a warm shaft across my sheets. I would stay in the warm sheets for a few minutes, but I could hear the birds outside, the muffled buzzing of wasps bumping against the window frame, and I would suddenly be struck by a mad urge to look at the garden, to see the sparkling morning lawn unfold before my eyes, and to feel the glorious breeze billow my dress. I didn't open the shutters, so as not to wake Harriett, and my eyes, accustomed to the pale shadow, easily made out the obstacles between my bed and the door. I slipped slowly out of the room, careful not to let the floorboards creak too much. My toes first met the firmness of the wood floor, then the slightly scratchy rug that tickled the soles of my feet. I carefully avoided the sharp corners of our two beds, the toy chest, the wardrobe, casting an anxious glance at the little ball curled up under her flowered duvet to make sure she hadn't moved. Then I placed one hand on the handle, the other on the latch, and I pulled with a sharp tug, so the door wouldn't squeak—for a poorly oiled door always makes more noise when opened slowly. The morning light flooding the hallway made me unsteady on my feet for a moment. The row of closed doors was intimidating, and made it impossible to know if I was the first one up or not. It was still a little groggily that I went down the stairs. There too, you had to avoid the groaning of the wood, control your footing, favor certain steps over others, and thus take giant strides, clinging to the banister so as not to lose your balance. This was the first adventure of the day, repeated every morning.





When I crossed the deserted hall, the painted foliage unfolded frighteningly there, for the morning light cast a cold reflection upon it, and the painted flowers seemed faded, like ancient stars in the process of disappearing. The floor tiles were always freezing, and I hurried quickly into the kitchen. There, the clock ticked, and this was the only time I noticed it, for the rest of the day its mechanical pulse was drowned out by the noises of the House—the laughter, the voices, the discordant clatter of pots and pans being banged together as they were put away. The kitchen was cool; the windows and shutters on the garden level had been left open to let the night air circulate. In the morning, we would chase out the bark-colored moths that had slipped in to huddle against the warmth of the walls. They fluttered clumsily, their quivering wings beating against the windowpanes without finding the exit.
I would close the windows, biting my lip when they banged a little too loudly. The dishes on the sink had dried, and a puddle of cold water trickled drop by drop from the edge down to the drain. I was very fond of the kitchen windows, with their small panes framed in white, looking out over the waking orchard. I would hoist myself onto the counter cluttered with pots and spatulas, and press my nose to the glass. I observed, through the fog of my breath, the vague silhouettes of trees heavy with fruit. The timid emerald leaves quivered in the sun, like little green-veined fish.





I would move into the living room, into the library, all deserted, and I would already sigh with boredom, telling myself that the adults were decidedly too lazy, sleeping like that while the day unfurled without waiting for us. Suddenly, I hear a movement upstairs. The sound of light slippers makes the floorboards creak; the game is then to guess who it is. If it's coming down the stairs fast, it's Harriett or Klaus. The cousins dash down fast too, but less so than us, especially the first few days: they have to get used to the treacherous steps in their shining wood. A heavy, slow step is Uncle Bertie. Bertie smells extraordinarily good in the mornings, because he always washes up before coming down, and I saw him, once, slapping handfuls of cologne onto his freshly shaved neck with the flat of his hands. His skin is then red and gleaming beneath the chin, his wake blooming with tall grasses swaying in the wind.
