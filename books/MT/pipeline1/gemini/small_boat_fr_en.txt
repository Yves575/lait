I

I didn’t ask you to leave, I had said.

You’re the one who wanted this, and if you didn’t want to get wet, mate, you shouldn’t have set sail. I didn’t push you into the water and I’m not the one who came looking for you in your village or your field, in your ruined suburb, to tear you away from there and put you in your damn leaky boat, and now you’re floundering and I can well believe you’re scared, and you’re calling me for help as if it were my fault, you’re asking me to save you and you’re losing patience. You’re counting on me. But I didn’t ask anything of you. So let me do my job and bear it patiently.

And I must have been thinking all of this so loudly that I said it out loud, the first sentence anyway, at least if the recordings were to be believed, and there was no reason not to believe them, I’m willing to admit.

To which I should have added, while I was at it, sentences like I’m not the Good Lord either, because I must have been thinking that too, and I could have said it. I could have said There are guys like you everywhere tonight, forty Titanics supposedly sinking in the Channel at the same time and I can’t take care of everyone at once. So you’re going to have to be patient, mate, or else tell the others, who are sinking too, to calm down and hang up their phones so I can deal with just you, why don’t you call all the others since you have a cell phone, all the others who get into rotten dinghies every night, without a compass, without navigation lights, thirty of them on a floorboard that can barely hold the weight of five, without instruments, without bearings, without knowing the sea, with women and children.





But in the end I’d said to Julien, next to me: These people are unbelievable, they throw themselves into the water and then they practically yell at you because you’re not tossing the lifebuoy fast enough, they’ve really got some nerve. He had smiled, before diving back into his book. Deflated, more like, under the circumstances, he had pointed out. And at three in the morning, that was the kind of joke that made you laugh.

That particular joke wasn’t recorded, I don’t think it would have gone down well and Julien can sleep soundly: he’s not the one who’s going to look like a monster for making a joke about deflating rubber dinghies. Besides, on the station’s recordings, you only hear me, or almost, just my luck.

After that I had gone back to my monitors, my PC, my microphone, probably telling myself that, after all, those guys should be happy: since they wanted to go to England, here they are, in British waters, and now on a British vessel, with British survival blankets, wrapped up like candies in gold foil, and they’ll be able to carry on their conversation in English for as long as they want.

But ultimately the currents had washed their bodies back into French waters.

And now they were floating on the investigator’s desk, at the Maritime Gendarmerie headquarters. There were twenty-seven of them if you counted right, a little girl among them, scattered between the pens, the notepads, the files, floating around the Gendarmerie captain’s computer, and among them too the body of the one who had called me fourteen times that night and who, of course, couldn’t be heard anymore. The sea was calm on the desk, no wind and no swell, and next to the bodies only neatly arranged papers on the surface.





While she was making me listen to the recordings, the gendarme would sometimes stare at me, sometimes look at God knows what out the window where you couldn’t see the sea and a good thing too, because I saw the sea all the time from my signal station and, all things considered, I preferred seeing what I saw today, a stretch of street with a construction site across the way, workers, African mostly but at least alive and not wet and chilled to the bone, and no women and no children, so I gladly watched that while on the recording you could hear Please, please and I could hear myself saying Calm down, help is on the way.

The gendarme had her hair pulled back and tied in a severe ponytail, exactly like me, I’d told myself, and a straight back like me, military in a way, coat-hanger shoulders as Éric used to say, with the exact same face as me for all I knew, but ten years older, her in her blue gendarmerie sweater and me obviously in civilian clothes, I had thrown on a ridiculous sweatshirt and a pair of sneakers as if returning from my morning jog, so that I looked like a kid despite my strict ponytail, looking like a sullen little girl, my face locked up tight, summoned to the principal’s office, and it was undoubtedly this ridiculous and vaguely humiliating resemblance, by virtue of which I had a caricature of myself sitting across from me or the mercilessly dismal portrait of what I must look like in reality, it was undoubtedly that, this resemblance, that had instantly made her unlikable to me, unpleasant even, even if there was no question of feeling sympathy under these circumstances anyway.





As she showed me into her office, she had said Thank you for coming in of your own accord, your director had refused to give us your contact details until now. She had added Your colleague, it seems, the one who was on watch with you that night, didn’t have this, this, this, but she had hesitated on the choice of word that should come next, it could have been Scruple, or Courage, and why not Moral conscience or Sense of duty, but she couldn’t find the right word, so she corrected herself, saying Hasn’t taken this step, for the time being.





And fortunately she hadn’t asked me why I had taken this step, as she ultimately put it, and why I had come like this of my own initiative, before the judge and the gendarmes might well have ended up ringing my doorbell directly anyway to haul me off in front of my little girl and stick me in the dock on charges of failing to assist persons in danger or whatever else, anyway fortunately she didn’t ask me why, that morning, after dropping Léa off at my parents’, I had gotten in the car to drive to the Maritime Gendarmerie in Cherbourg, a four-hour drive from Boulogne, on the highway a bland coffee on the surface of which I saw little dinghies bobbing but they were biscuit crumbs and around me people who behind my back were pointing at me in silence, a phone call to my parents to see if everything was okay with the little one who was worried about not going to school, a phone call too to the gendarmerie to say I’m coming I’m on the road and me entirely surprised not to hear English spoken at the other end of the line, not to hear Please, please and screaming in the background, but no, simply a We’re expecting you, as reasonable shipwrecked people should have said, bearing it patiently since there wasn’t much else to do besides pray and look to see if there wasn’t a boat in the vicinity, We’re expecting you they would have said calmly, instead of endlessly repeating useless supplications, instead of not understanding the difference between being in French waters and being in British waters, instead of calling fourteen times in two hours to say they were sinking, instead of annoying me by repeating that as if I were the one not understanding, but I’ve fully understood, me, that your feet are in the water but it’s English waters, not French waters, even if it’s just as cold I readily believe it, so look over that way instead if you still know which way to look, where north is, where south is,





so fortunately she hadn’t asked me why I had finally made up my mind, because I wouldn’t have known what to answer her, at any rate I don’t think I would have used words like Scruple or Sense of duty, and certainly not Courage, maybe rather words like Exhaustion or Nausea, but more likely like Anger,

which is to say like Shame.





It was also that I wanted to hear myself. That is, to hear myself rather than read about myself, rather than read my words in the papers, hear my voice rather than the voice of journalists repeating what I’d said on television, alongside phrases like Migrant Drama, Migrant Tragedy, as if every evening at eight o’clock we were at the theater and the same play was being put on again,

and it is the same play, as a matter of fact, and me in the front row, you could say I don’t miss a single performance, every night I come to attend the Migrant Drama, I get in for free and I even get paid for it, there are those in the boxes, those in the balcony, but for me they set up a signal station in the front row so I can see clearly, so I’m simultaneously in the front boxes and the front balcony overlooking the sea, and even if it sometimes bears a different title, sometimes Migrant Drama, sometimes Migrant Tragedy, Drama in the Channel after Drama in the Mediterranean, it’s always the same play of course, and always the same character too, and at one point the character picks up a phone to address the audience, it’s interactive, he calls out, it starts with Please please, but it’s not really the audience he’s addressing, which meanwhile lets out dismayed ohs and ahs and sometimes sighs of indignation, it’s me, me alone he’s addressing, and then it’s up to me to answer and to answer well otherwise the audience isn’t happy and then the sighs of indignation are for me, and the next day the theater critics come down hard on me, because the turn the show takes depends on my answers and if I do a good job the Migrant Drama is a little less of a Migrant Drama, not to mention that while all this is going on I also have to deal with what’s happening on other stages where they’re putting on the Adrift Boater Drama or the Capsized Trawler Tragedy.





So, hearing myself, hearing my voice, in that Maritime Gendarmerie office, to be absolutely sure of what I had said, rather than hearing a dolled-up journalist ventriloquizing me, saying that I had said this and that, and among the things I had said, horrible, scandalous, or even appalling things, before saying that I had done or hadn’t done things that I should have done or shouldn’t have done.

But mostly it was him you heard on the recordings, I don’t know what to call him: the Migrant, the Phone Guy, the Sinking Man, but his voice I knew by heart, and what he said too, because, in reality, it’s always the same guy who calls me every night, the same voice and the same pleas, because this idiot, no matter how many times you pull him out of the drink he goes right back in, once, ten times, a hundred times: one night you pull him out of there in a desperate rush, he’s safe and sound, he can say thank you, and the next night it’s him calling again, because he’s back in it as if he hadn’t learned his lesson and he says Please please, every day we put him back on dry land, every night he starts all over again, and the feeling then that it’s going to be like this until Judgment Day: the same guy, always, going back into the water and starting to sink in the middle of the Channel and waiting for someone to come get him so he can do it all over again the following night, and eventually it gets tiresome.





But my own voice too, all the same, although not quite my voice either, not because the recording inevitably distorts the timbre, but because it was my professional voice, the one that curtly manages the disaster every night with phrases like Calm down and Give me your GPS location via WhatsApp or Help is on the way. So you mostly heard phrases like that, at 2:05 a.m., at 2:36 a.m., at 3:12 a.m., at 4:22 a.m. and right up until 4:32 a.m. where, from then on, you didn’t hear anything anymore, and among these phrases there were others that it seems I shouldn’t have uttered, like the one that said, then, I didn’t ask you to leave and the investigator asked me how to interpret that particular phrase.

So I told the investigator that that particular phrase, since it was obviously a problem without me even knowing whether I was going to be thrown in the dungeon for stating an obvious truth, namely that it certainly wasn’t me who had asked them to leave, if that was part of the faults I was being accused of, though what fault exactly, that phrase, at any rate, shouldn’t be given undue importance, in my opinion, because, after all, I could have said it to any irresponsible boater, to the moron who heads out to sea without checking the weather conditions or who is incapable of properly maneuvering his stupid little sailboat, and who ends up stranded and dumbfounded and panics and then you have to mobilize the entire Navy to keep him from swallowing seawater. To that guy too, if he comes and tells me I’m not moving fast enough as if I were responsible for what’s happening to him, I’d tell him in the exact same way that I didn’t ask him to set sail, after all.





But she looked at me, as if she didn’t believe me and I had my doubts too. But why?

So she seized the opportunity, all too happy to disingenuously ask me You don’t see the difference between an irresponsible boater and a migrant dinghy? The question probably wasn’t very well-intentioned, but ultimately it was easy to answer, because my job actually required me not to make a distinction, which is what I said then, and that I should be congratulated for not making that distinction: we come to the rescue of everyone, we don’t differentiate. Am I supposed to be more eager for one than the other?

But that wasn’t what she meant, of course, because I sounded like I thought it was the same thing, some hick who takes to the sea in his pleasure boat and people fleeing poverty and war, as if they had all set off in the same boat for a fishing trip. The difference to be made wasn’t between one drowning man and another drowning man, but between the reasons, the causes that put him in such a situation. The difference to be made is why someone leaves and that explains why you set off with thirty people in a cockleshell and not all alone on your First 24. So the problem was supposedly that I wasn’t making the distinction, that I didn’t seem to understand that the situations are different, that people don’t leave in the same way and especially not for the same reasons and therefore not in the same conditions either.





Yet the insidious question implied that, on the contrary, I was making a distinction, but not in the right way, namely that in the end I’d have been more eager to save some upstart wanting to show off on his yacht on a blustery night than to mobilize rescue services for a handful of Iraqi Kurds who stubbornly insisted on splashing around in the Channel every night. In other words, I wasn’t making a distinction when I should have been, and when I shouldn’t have been I was, and all that could only be explained by one reason, always the same one, and since she was eventually going to ask me if I had something against migrants, or if I considered a migrant’s life to be worth less than that of a boater or a fishing skipper, then, to forestall a question of that kind, I said I didn’t have any opinions on migrants, no more opinions on migrants than on immigration policy or the right of asylum, on the relationship between the North and the South, on the problems, the solutions, on the misery of the world, on injustice, that having opinions on migrants wasn’t what was asked of me.





No, I don’t have opinions on migrants, I actually have fewer than other people, I think, and besides I’m asked not to have any at all and that’s just fine. If anyone has opinions on migrants, it’d be more like my ex, for example, Léa’s father, he’s got them, and what’s more he’s got pretty much the same ones as the baker at the end of the street and the cashiers at the local supermarket whom I chat with from time to time when I’m off duty for a week, pretty much the same ones, in fact, as half of Calais, Boulogne, or Dunkirk. And Éric’s opinions on migrants, I heard them all the more because I was doing this job, at the CROSS, because not only did he not see why we went to so much trouble, at the CROSS, to help these parasites, and why we didn’t just chuck them straight into the drink as soon as we saw one, with a good smack of an oar if possible to make damn sure they didn’t come back up to the surface.





It had started with the whole business with Calais and the Jungle, because he was from there, Éric, from Calais, and in the end he simply couldn’t stand the idea of me doing this job anymore, to help trawlers or cargo ships, sure, but migrants, no, and every time I came home, I’d get an earful, and how many did you drag out of the drink again this time, and how much is this bullshit costing us, and why don’t you push them into English waters and let them fend for themselves since they want to go over there so badly, and why, once you’ve fished them out, don’t you ship them straight back to Africa, and don’t you think we have better things to do than tend to their boo-boos, let them sort out their own mess or stay home, etc. I’d heard it a thousand times, until, for reasons that actually had nothing to do with it, I asked him to leave, once and for all, but I wasn’t there to tell my life story with Léa’s father and how we had eventually separated.
