"A blue tiger came in this morning," Kaede's grandfather said.

"How did it turn the knob, I wonder. Clever thing."

More than the tiger's coming into his study—and more even than the fact that its body was covered in blue fur—her grandfather seemed surprised that it had opened the front door and come in.

"At least it didn't bite you."

Kaede deliberately made light of it.

Inside, though, she felt a little disappointed. He was awake for once, and it was this again.

She came only about once a week, but her grandfather was almost always asleep.

Even when he did happen to be awake, all he talked about were his visions.

And he would spend the whole time on such stories until Kaede left, so that no ordinary conversation was possible.

Even so, Kaede listened to the "blue tiger story," and kept giving little responses.

Because the time she spent in her grandfather's house—her home as well—felt irreplaceable.

"And then the tiger..." her grandfather said, imitating the way it walked, its forelegs crossing.

"As it was leaving, it showed me such a happy smile."

"The tiger smiled?"

Oh... this again, she thought with a wry smile.

It was a story about a vision—something impossible in the real world—and yet here she was, listening in earnest again.

Yes—she always started out only pretending to listen with interest, but perhaps because his way of telling it was so skillful, before she knew it she was drawn into that world every time.

And today, of all days, she could have sworn a real blue tiger was about to spring from an illustration in one of the books on the shelves.


Having said all he had to say, he was probably satisfied.

Her grandfather's eyelids slowly drifted shut.


All day long, her grandfather sat in the motorized reclining chair in this room.

They had chosen a larger size to suit his tall, gaunt frame, but it had proved more comfortable than expected, and the fact that he now scarcely left the chair was a serious miscalculation.

Leaning against the side table beside him was the wooden cane he could not do without to get around.

But the care manager who had recommended the cane had sighed to Kaede, "He does use it when he goes to the toilet, but when he's choosing a book from the shelves he finds it too much bother and simply won't use it. I worry about him falling."

(He still loves books. But their contents—)

A sad thought crossed her mind: probably almost none of it was getting through anymore.

In the study, crammed with books, the scent of ink gone sour drifted through the room.

It reminded Kaede of the used-book district of Jimbocho, which she loved.

When she noticed, the dappled light streaming through the window had formed a camouflage pattern across her grandfather's sleeping face.

The high bridge of his nose and the wrinkles etched at the corners of his eyes shaped intricate shadows across a face that, for some reason, had almost no age spots at all despite his being seventy-one.

Compared with years ago, the flesh had fallen away from his jaw and cheeks, but that only made the cut of his features stand out more.

His long, abundant hair, parted at the center of his broad forehead, was about seventy percent white, with the remaining black shading into it, and this too gave him the sculptural presence of an emperor engraved on an ancient Roman coin.

Even allowing for a granddaughter's partiality, he was an imposing-looking man.

(I bet he was popular.)

Kaede gently pulled the blanket that had slipped down back up to her grandfather's thin neck.



Once she had finished cleaning and misted the study with a soap-scented antibacterial spray, careful not to let any of it touch the books on the shelves, it was already time for the physical therapist to come for his rehab session.

The antibacterial spray was not there merely to keep the room clean.

Her grandfather frequently had hallucinations of tiny insects, the mosquito-like sort.

When that happened, it could double as makeshift 'insect spray.'

(See you, Grandpa.)

(──I'll come again.)

Beside the study door stood a vanity that had been passed down from her late grandmother.

Perhaps it was not wear and tear at all, but wear and bloom.

Accumulated time had been painted into the vanity's wood grain as makeup in intricate hues, giving it a richness all its own.

Kaede took a hairbrush from the vanity drawer, ran it quickly through her hair, and fixed her expression in the mirror.

(Smile.)

The study door, once made of heavy oak, had already been remodeled into a sliding door in preparation for the day her grandfather would have no choice but to rely on a wheelchair.

Kaede slid the door as quietly as she could and left her grandfather's house in Himonya.

On the way home, swaying on the Toyoko Line, she looked at the train window and saw an expressionless face reflected there.

The smile she had taken the trouble to make was already gone, without even a trace left.

The sky was already dimming, as though it had put on a light shade of lipstick.

With autumn just beginning, the thunderheads had vanished, and clouds of all shapes were scattered across the sky.

Suddenly, a memory of her grandfather stirred in Kaede's chest.





Twenty-three years earlier. Kaede, four years old.

On the veranda, perched in her grandfather's lap as he sits cross-legged, she gazes at the sky turning crimson.

Her grandfather lowers his gaze to his lap, his clear eyes alive with intelligence.

"Kaede. What does each of those clouds over there look like to you? Use all of them and make a single story."

Looking back now, it was san-dai-banashi, the rakugo form of three-topic storytelling.

Perhaps he had meant to set her imagination fluttering──

That, presumably, was his own way of cultivating her sensibilities.

Kaede answers without hesitation.

"That cloud's a little grandpa. And that cloud over there's a flat grandpa. And then, um... the biggest cloud is a grandpa even fatter than you, Grandpa."

"You can't make a story out of that," her grandfather said, smiling all the same.

Then, astonishingly, as if it couldn't be helped, he improvised a children's tale in Kaede's place, titled "The Three Grandpas."

She didn't remember the details of the plot very well.

But one thing had stayed with her: the ending, or something like it, in which the greedy "fat grandpa" mistook all the cold medicine in the world for sugar and swallowed it, was mocked to no end, and yet in the end lived longer than anyone else in the world...

It was probably meant as a kind of lesson for Kaede, who couldn't stand bitter powdered medicine.

But his way of telling it was so wonderfully funny that Kaede clapped her hands in delight.

"Look, Kaede. See?"

When she looked up, only the "big cloud"—the "fat grandpa"—was left, while the "little grandpa" and the "flat grandpa" had, quite literally, scattered like clouds and vanished like mist.

Just like the ending of the story.



Stunned, Kaede kept looking back and forth between her grandfather and the "fat grandpa" in the sky, again and again.

Thinking back, her grandfather must have been quietly checking the clouds as he spun the tale.

And if the "little grandpa" or the "flat grandpa" had lasted to the end, the story would surely have turned out very differently.

"Hey, Grandpa. Tell Kaede some more stories. Or else—"

Little Kaede looked up and tugged at the hair growing from the mole on her grandfather's Adam's apple.

It came out surprisingly easily, and she remembers finding that so irresistibly funny she burst out laughing.

Perhaps at that moment, Kaede thought.

(I might have uncorked Grandpa's intelligence.)



It was only six months ago that her grandfather first began to seem visibly not himself.

When she went walking with him, his stride had plainly shortened.

"Grandpa, maybe you're fatter than you look. Your legs can't keep up with you."

He tilted his head and gave a self-mocking smile, saying it must be age.

At first Kaede too thought it was weight gain, or simply growing old—or rather, she tried to think so.

But from there it progressed quickly.

When he drank the coffee he loved so much, the hand holding the cup would tremble.

Whenever she went to see him, he would be in the chair in his study, nodding off, his head bobbing.

His posture grew more and more stooped, and whatever he did, his movements were slow.

No—more than any of that.

More than anything, Kaede would never forget the shock of that day.

In the middle of the night, her smartphone rang.

When she answered the phone, rubbing her sleepy eyes, the caller, who sounded like a young man, introduced himself as though, for some reason, he found it hard to say the words. "Um... I'm one of the ambulance crew."

Then, looking deeply apologetic and faltering again and again, he continued.



"May I confirm that this is Ms. Kaede herself?—ah, yes, I see. The reason for my call is that your name was written on the emergency contact memo posted on the wall. The thing is, your grandfather, who is here, made a 119 emergency call, and then, well—um..."

"What has happened?"

"He's saying, 'Kaede is lying here, covered in blood.'"



At his regular clinic, they said it appeared to be Parkinson's disease, but since they could not say for certain, they recommended that he go to a larger hospital.

They go to the university hospital. He undergoes detailed tests, including a CT scan.

The result—with her grandfather deeply asleep in the chair, the young doctor announced it as if it were nothing.

"It's Lewy body dementia."



That her grandfather, once so brilliant, had developed dementia at an age barely past seventy—for Kaede, there was simply no way she could accept that fact straightaway.

But when she looked into it in her own way, on the internet and in materials she had ordered, every one of his symptoms matched this illness exactly.

She learned for the first time that there were apparently more than six million people with dementia in Japan alone—and that even when people simply say "dementia," there are actually many different kinds.

What people generally call dementia can be divided, roughly, into three broad categories.

The most common, accounting for roughly seventy percent of cases, is "Alzheimer's-type dementia," which apparently develops when a kind of protein called amyloid beta builds up in the brain.

This is probably the type most people picture first when they hear the word dementia.

Next most common is "vascular dementia," caused by the aftereffects of cerebral infarction or stroke, which accounts for about twenty percent of all dementia cases.



Both of those types often bring symptoms such as memory problems that make a person repeat the same story over and over, disorientation that blurs the sense of time and place, or wandering about outside.

And then—"Lewy body dementia," the diagnosis her grandfather had received—also called DLB, from the initials of "Dementia with Lewy Bodies"—accounts for roughly ten percent of the total.

Since the disease was given that name only in 1995, it is probably, by the standards of humanity's long history with illness, one of the more recently identified disorders.

In recent years it has been drawing attention as "the third dementia," and it seems that efforts to clarify its pathology are moving ahead rapidly, not only in the medical field but in clinical trials as well.

In the brains and brainstems of DLB patients, there are always small deep-crimson structures like little fried eggs—Lewy bodies.

And it is precisely these "little fried eggs" that bring on Parkinsonian symptoms such as trembling in the limbs and difficulty walking, the shouting in one's sleep known as REM sleep behaviour disorder, the daytime drowsiness that leaves patients sleeping much of the day, and visuospatial impairment that makes it hard to judge distance.

But—

The hallmark of DLB, the symptom unlike any other, is above all its visual hallucinations.

They vary from patient to patient, appearing in monochrome for some and in colour for others, but what they all share is this fact: the hallucinations are vivid, stark, unmistakably clear.

For example, a person may wake in the morning, open their eyes, and find as many as ten people standing silently in the room with expressionless faces, staring at them as though to bore holes through them.

Or a giant snake may be coiled ponderously on the dining table.

Sometimes a girl with pigtails follows them everywhere they go, all day long.



Outright fantastical hallucinations are not rare, either.

Briskly crossing before their eyes—a pig walking on two legs.

Gracefully springing about on a plate—a fairy.

And the blue tiger her grandfather had seen as well—

Strangely, in many cases these are not accompanied by auditory hallucinations.

The "things that stir" within the hallucinations are, after all, no more than visual phantoms; they do not speak to the patient.

Yet of the information human beings receive from the outside world through the five senses, vision is said to account for a full ninety per cent.

Which means that for most DLB patients, "the things that stir" are clearly real.

The proverb they would most like to quote might be "seeing is believing."

After all, they can see it there before their eyes, plain as day.

No matter how much the people around them try to deny it, would it not be nearly impossible to make them accept that nothing is there, that no one is there?

And yet if those around them heedlessly say, "There's nothing like that here," "There's no way it could be there," "Pull yourself together," the patient will sometimes grow angry.

That is why people say caring for someone with DLB is difficult.

When Kaede tried reading a handbook for carers, this was the sort of advice it contained.



"If a patient says, 'I can see a big bug' or 'I'm scared,' do not deny it by saying, 'It's just your imagination,' or brush them off with, 'You're sick, so don't give me trouble.' Instead, clap your hands and say gently, 'Look, it's gone now, isn't it? You're all right now.' It can also be effective to change the subject."—



That was probably just how things were, she thought.

And besides, more than anything, she wanted to avoid ending up in a quarrel with her grandfather, who had never once been angry with her.

That was why Kaede had avoided talking in depth about his illness, and why, when it came to the visions her grandfather spoke of, she had consistently refrained from denying their reality in front of him.

In the first place, getting a patient to recognize their own dementia was probably next to impossible, and even if it could be done, it seemed too cruel.

And yet.

Kaede felt a strange dissonance in her own thinking and behavior, as if a division that ought to have come out clean had somehow left a remainder.

It seemed tinged a little differently from the escapist thought—or rather, the wish—that her grandfather could not possibly have dementia, could not possibly go on losing his intellect like this.

(Yes. Something's off—)

But what that dissonance was.

For now, Kaede could not explain it in any concrete way.

Fifteen minutes of swaying on the bus from Gumyoji Station.

When she got back to her studio apartment, a book had arrived.

It was a collection of critical essays by Setogawa Takeshi, the literary critic who had loved mystery fiction above all else.

The colophon read: "First edition, first printing, April 1, 1998."

If Kaede remembered correctly, he had died not long afterward, at the young age of fifty.

In other words, this book was Mr Setogawa's final work.

Having grown up under her grandfather's tutelage, Kaede had become a full-fledged mystery fanatic. Before long, novels alone no longer satisfied her, and she had gone on to read even the Setogawa essay collections on her grandfather's shelves.

And then—she was less surprised than stunned.





He would lay all manner of works on the dissecting table and speak of their appeal from his own distinctive angle, with effortless freedom—and yet those columns were sometimes—no, almost without exception—more interesting than the works themselves.

In one serial column, Masterwork Pilgrimages, he singled out major works by the big three of classical detective fiction—Ellery Queen, Agatha Christie, Dickson Carr—and thoroughly tore into them with lines like "Are they really such masterpieces?" The pieces were more logical and more thrilling than the novels themselves, and the arguments were impossible to fault—and yet, whether Mr Setogawa knew it or not, his overflowing affection for those writers seeped through the lines, and every time Kaede read him, she felt a quiet warmth.

The "god" who had made Kaede a lover of classic foreign mysteries—

(Setogawa Takeshi)

Just murmuring the name inwardly was enough to make her heart leap.

Around 1970—in his youth, Mr Setogawa was said to have been the central figure in the legendary university club, the Waseda Mystery Club, which produced any number of mystery writers and critics.

Once, in Nishi-Waseda, there had been a coffee shop called Mon Chéri. There, day after day, students from the Waseda Mystery Club carried on heated discussions of mysteries, and at the center of the circle there was always the smiling Mr Setogawa, with his thick brows and clear-cut features.

Her grandfather too, it seemed, had been one of the club's leading members.


Coffee goes well with classical detective fiction.



Its tall, narrow sign, with the words "Coffee Specialty Mon Chéri" cut out in psychedelic white on a red background, was said to give the impression that anyone but a mystery specialist was being shut out.

Rich, bitter coffee, with foam swirling like a mystery whose bottom you could never see.

A yellow-tiled exterior that called Leroux's The Mystery of the Yellow Room to mind.

The sound of the troupe's footsteps ringing out from the little theater upstairs—was it Chesterton's The Queer Feet, or perhaps Rampo's The Stalker in the Attic?

Now that Mon Chéri was gone, all she could do was let her imagination spread its wings.

Yet the Mon Chéri as recounted by Mr Setogawa and her grandfather—wouldn't it surely have blazed with the heat and radiance of Tokiwa-so in the world of manga, or Liangshan Marsh in Water Margin?

(I wish I could have heard the two of them talking about classical detective fiction...)

Precisely because the café no longer existed, Kaede's daydreams swelled all the more.

Sadly, Mon Chéri was gone.

But books—those still existed.

When you love a book, you want to keep it close at hand.

And the books in her grandfather's house were so carefully kept in translucent glassine covers, with not a single crease on any page, that somehow she couldn't bring herself to borrow them.

So Kaede decided to buy the whole run of Mr Setogawa's critical collections in a single splurge.

(Good. It's practically brand-new. It even still has the band.)



Kaede was glad to see the book was in such good condition.

If she was honest, she would have preferred to buy them all new.

But this posthumous volume was already out of print, so she had no choice but to buy it from an online used-book shop.

Now she had the full set.

(A twenty-seven-year-old woman completing a set like this... what's that about?)

Aware of herself smiling, she stood there flipping through the pages.

Then—

From between the pages, four small slips of paper fluttered down onto the carpet like ginkgo leaves.

(Hm. What are these?)

Kaede carefully picked up the four slips and laid them out on the table.

Then she stared at those rectangular pieces of paper, all different sizes, and fell into thought.

(Too many to be bookmarks.)

(But)

(Too 'heavy' to be sticky notes—)

The four slips were clippings from magazines and newspapers.

And every one of them was an obituary for Mr Setogawa.

Taking advantage of the holiday, she went back to Himonya in Meguro for the first time in three days.

In a residential neighborhood near Himonya Hachiman Shrine, the local tutelary shrine—

at one corner stood her grandfather's house, a small two-story wooden home, quietly tucked away and beginning to go to seed.

From what little garden there was, a cherry tree and a fatsia stretched their branches and leaves over the wall.



On the wooden nameplate set into the gatepost, her grandfather's surname stood out in bold, vivid brushstrokes.

It was the hand she had known since childhood.

People say a nameplate is the face of a house.

Perhaps whatever dignity the house still possessed, whatever composed presence still clung to its exterior, came from the elegance of those characters.

Yet once she stepped through the gate, that impression was abruptly deflated.

Once, round stones had been laid here and there like markers leading to the front door, but since her grandfather developed dementia, the path had become a bland little strip of concrete.

When she turned the knob on the front door, whose renovation had been put off, the medicinal, almost soapy scent of antibacterial cleaner hit her at once.

Kaede almost called out to the helper—and then stopped herself.

There were no shoes by the entry that looked like they might belong to the helper.

The helper had probably just finished the cleaning and laundry and left.

Several handrails, still new-looking, had been installed here and there along the hallway walls.

For her grandfather, whose steps were unsteady, more than one handrail was essential if he was to move about the house.

When it came to buying assistive devices like these, applying for subsidies often meant a tangle of paperwork and an enormous wait, though the details varied from one municipality to another.

And so, in practice, people like her grandfather ended up having to cover most of the cost out of pocket.

She stepped into the living room on the left off the hall.



Glancing up, she saw that the great central pillar, still barely holding on to its sheen, was marked with a number of pencil lines drawn across it.

They were the marks used to measure her mother's height when she was little, and Kaede's too—the family's only grandchild.

The figures and dates written beside them had almost faded away, but even so, her grandfather's beautiful hand was still visible there.

But seeing the metal support of a handrail set into the pillar there, as though biting into those characters, made her chest ache.

Looking toward the window, she noticed several white T-shirts hanging there to dry indoors.

(Oh no. The helper slipped up.)

If there's a DLB patient in the house, it's best not to dry clothes indoors if you can help it.

They can mistake hanging clothes for people.

White T-shirts in particular can all too easily become a "white canvas" for the intense hallucinations DLB patients project onto them.

For the same reason, she'd heard it was better not to let patients see portraits or family photographs either, and there had even been times when she hurriedly tucked the photo frames lined up on the tabletop into the back of a dresser, somewhere out of sight.

She was hurrying to take the T-shirts off their hangers when—

her grandfather's relatively steady voice came from behind her.

"Sorry. Kanae hung those up before she left. I suppose the stains hadn't quite come out."

Her grandfather came into the living room, coffee cup in hand, and slowly sat down on the bed.

The upstairs bedroom had long since become a storage room, so these days he moved almost entirely between this living room, where the bed had been set up, and the study at the very back of the house.



But from the way he walked today, he seemed in much better shape than the last time she had come.

Wide day-to-day swings in condition are one of DLB's defining features.

"No, I was just smoothing out the wrinkles," Kaede said. She gave up on taking down the T-shirt.

"So it was Mom who came, not the helper."

"She seemed to have work left to do and hurried back. Unfortunately, you just missed her."

Kaede felt a rush of relief.

Better this way—

At least today, when she meant to put that question to him.

Lately, it was rare to see her grandfather looking this well.

Today really was her chance.

"Coffee Kanae makes is good even cold."

Still smiling, her grandfather shifted his weight slowly and settled himself again.

Then, with a hand that trembled only slightly, he sipped his coffee and spoke.

"It doesn't look as though I'm likely to spill my coffee today. Not that it's for me to say, but I must be feeling quite well. So I'd like to make sure of something. I can only call it intuition, really."

After another sip of coffee, he looked straight at Kaede.

"You have something important to say to me, don't you? I can tell from your face."



Kaede felt close to tears.

That "I" of his, unmistakably his.

That clear, dark, gentle gaze.

It was as though the grandfather from those days had come back.

Perhaps because he wasn't drowsy, even his speech was clear.

Come to think of it, in the last six months she had been so careful of his condition that they had scarcely had a serious conversation at all.

If she was going to find out, it had to be now.

Summoning her courage, Kaede began. "Yes. Actually..."

"There's something I want to ask you, Grandpa."

"What is it?"

"Grandpa..."

She fought hard not to cry.

"Grandpa... don't you know yourself that you're ill? Aren't you aware that what you keep seeing are visions, not reality?"

No good.

Her voice was shaking.

"But you don't want me to worry,"

Tears came.

She'd told herself she wouldn't cry.

"because you don't want me to worry, aren't you pretending not to know?"

Still wearing that gentle smile, her grandfather took another sip of coffee.

Then, with deliberate care, he set the cup on the dining table beside the bed.

"Yes. Just as you say, Kaede. I am unmistakably a patient with Lewy body dementia."



Her intuition had been right after all.

Her grandfather's dark eyes, the irises within them as delicate as glasswork, were filled with a fathomless depth that seemed ready to draw her in.

Yes—there in them was the light of intelligence, unchanged from long ago.

And that was the true source of that odd sense of something being off, though Kaede herself had not even noticed it.




Over the past two days, as Kaede looked more deeply into DLB, she learned a great many things.

Even among people with the same diagnosis, the places where Lewy bodies appear can make an enormous difference to the decline in memory and spatial awareness.




Some patients live in constant fear of their hallucinations, while others seem to grow used to them quite readily.

Their symptoms vary in intensity from one patient to the next; no two cases are alike.

There also seem to be many cases where, when the balance of various medications, including dopaminergic medication, works out just right, the hallucinations disappear as if "the fog had lifted."

In fact, depending on the person's physical condition, there are times when no decline in intelligence is perceptible at all.

What astonished Kaede most of all was the fact that many patients are clearly aware that what they are seeing is not reality but a product of their illness.

There are even remarkably positive people who look forward to the hallucinations that appear every morning when they wake, and make a hobby of sketching them afterward.

Scientific knowledge of DLB is still incomplete, and for that very reason misunderstandings arise easily.

Even in medicine, there is no shortage of doctors who look only at the surface of a patient's intense hallucinatory experiences and rush to conclude that "the dementia is progressing."

The onset of DLB does not necessarily mean a decline in intelligence.

When Kaede learned this, she felt that odd sense of something being off vanish, exactly as if "the fog had lifted."








Setting those Parkinsonian symptoms aside, her grandfather said, looking down at his faintly trembling hand.

"I had realized quite some time ago that my mental state was far removed from that of a healthy person. Let me think—for instance, when I happen to glance at the panel of that bookcase over there, I see it covered all over with delicate carvings, as fine as the work a master shrine carpenter might carve on a portable shrine. But when I touch it, there is no sense of carving at all. The panel is perfectly smooth. So which ought one to trust, sight or touch? For someone to carve the whole panel so elaborately in a single night without my noticing would be utterly impossible, would it not? Besides, nobody in the world has any motive to sneak into an old man's room just to carve that bookcase. Which means—regrettably—that touch is what I ought to trust. The flip side is that my sight is not to be trusted at all."

Kaede could only listen to her grandfather's confession, unable to find any words in reply.

"Then the question became what was causing it. My computer is broken and unusable. I thought I might look it up on my smartphone, but as you can see, my hands are far too unsteady. Or rather, ever since I saw 'Kaede's corpse' and called 119, Kanae has confiscated my smartphone, so there was no way to search in the first place."

Her grandfather pursed his well-shaped lips in a mischievous pout.

"So I begged my helper to call a care taxi for me, and went to the library to look into it. Just following the print makes my eyes blur and puts me to sleep at once, so it took all day... but I did come away certain what sort of illness I had. You know the expression 'the little grey cells,' don't you?"

Her grandfather quoted the Belgian master detective Hercule Poirot's catchphrase and smiled with a touch of self-mockery.

"In my case, then, deep orange Lewy bodies are spread across the surface of my brain, so I am, one might say, a man with 'scarlet cells.'"

"Then..." Kaede asked.

She could hear the slight rasp in her own voice.

"Why did you keep going out of your way to tell me about your visions, again and again?"

"Well, you see."

Her grandfather faltered, just for a moment.

"Especially when I speak of my visions, your expression changes from one moment to the next. You look surprised, or you smile. Above all, I hear your little responses. When that happens, I can feel with certainty that Kaede is really there."

"What... what do you mean? I'm always right here with you, Grandpa."

"Then perhaps if I say it this way, you will understand. Once before, I spoke to you with complete frankness about the future before me, which honestly I did not expect to be very long, and about how I meant to see it out—or, what shall I call it, since I do not wish to use the term 'end-of-life preparations,' familiar though it may be, because I dislike how insensitive it sounds. At the time I was vain enough to think my health was nearly perfect. I even thought that if I was going to tell you, it had to be then. And so yes... I talked for a good hour or so. But then Kaede, who for some reason listened to me in silence, with no expression on her face the whole while—"

Her grandfather broke off and lowered his gaze.

"All at once, she simply vanished before my eyes. That Kaede had been a vision."

Or perhaps the coffee had simply been roasted too dark.

For an instant, a bitter shade passed over her grandfather's face.

"Nothing could be more miserable or sad than that, could it? From then on, I resolved never again to speak of the illness unless Kaede herself broached the subject. Even if that meant being taken for an old man with dementia, one with whom no proper conversation could be had—it could not be helped, I thought."

(Grandpa.)

Once more, she tries the word in her mind.

(Grandpa.)

Her grandfather had been unable to talk to his only granddaughter about how he meant to order his own last days—no, had deliberately stopped himself from doing so.

Why had she not realized sooner what torment he was in?

He certainly sees visions.

And often.

He also suffers from memory impairment and, at times, various disturbances of consciousness.

The Parkinsonian symptoms make his movements extremely slow.

But—his basic intelligence had not declined in the slightest.

It must have been time for the nearby mission-school kindergarten to let out.

Children's voices drifted past the house, singing what sounded like nursery rhymes.

Their off-key notes only made them more endearing.

Her grandfather's face softened too.

Autumn daylight drops like a well bucket—

Even so, there still seemed to be time before dark.

"Actually, Grandpa, there's something I want you to look at."

Kaede took a collection of critical essays by Setogawa from her black bag.

If her grandfather had been asleep in his chair as usual, she had meant to drape the freshly laundered blanket she had brought over him, sit beside him and read for a while, then go home.

But now, the way her grandfather was—

Her grandfather set the rimless reading glasses he took from the pocket of his gown on his high nose and, still holding the book a little away from him, said, clearly moved.

"Why, it's Setogawa's posthumous work. You needn't have gone and bought it when I could have given you mine."

(I couldn't. Not a book so cherished, so happy.)

Kaede gave a small laugh to herself.

"I was sure it had gone out of print. You did well to find a copy."

"There are online shops now that specialize in used books, and even fairly rare ones can be surprisingly easy to buy. And then—there was something tucked inside it."

Kaede opened the book and once again drew out the four obituary clippings and laid them on the table.

"Prominent in Mystery and Film Criticism, Setogawa Dies"

"Setogawa Dies: A Much-Missed Talent"

"An Era of Multilayered Criticism: What Setogawa Left Behind"

"Setogawa Speaks of the Happy Meeting of Mystery and Film"

"Yes. I read every one of them at the time."

After only a brief glance at the headlines, her grandfather murmured, a little sadly,

"I think two other papers ran articles as well. Of course, I've clipped every one of them."

"I see."

Once again, Kaede marveled at her grandfather's memory.

Because of his illness, memories of the very recent past could simply fall away altogether, but when it came to long ago, perhaps the drawers of memory still opened at will.

"And, Grandpa—this is where the puzzle starts. I think it's one of those things that seems like it should happen all the time, but hardly ever does—what they call a 'mystery in everyday life.'"

"I see," her grandfather said with a nod.

"In other words, the theme of the mystery is this: who on earth tucked these four obituary clippings between the pages of the book, and for what purpose?"

"Exactly. For one thing, there are too many of them to be bookmarks, right? But as markers, don't obituaries make the whole atmosphere somehow too heavy?"

"Rather like Harry Kemelman."

Removing his glasses, her grandfather named an old mystery writer.

Kemelman's best-known work, The Nine Mile Walk, is a classic mystery devoted entirely to logic: from a single line dropped in a conversation between patrons at the next table in a pub, "A nine-mile walk is no easy matter, especially in the rain," it lays bare at a stroke the whole truth of a murder committed the day before.

Then, abruptly, her grandfather pleaded:

"Kaede. Let me have a cigarette, would you?"



Perhaps because of its neat cadence, the phrase sounded somehow like a spell.

Kaede fetched a blue cigarette pack from a drawer in the dressing table in the study.

A French brand: Gauloises.

They were not especially expensive, but neither were they something you could buy just anywhere.

Whenever Kaede made the rounds of the used-book shops in Jimbocho, it was her habit to pick them up at a tiny sundries shop known only to those in the know.

"I'd be grateful if you'd light it for me. Yes, that's fine. My hands shake, you see. When I'm by myself, I try not to partake of one."

Her grandfather never said he "smoked" a cigarette. He said he "partook of" one.

Perhaps it was a remnant of the days before today's anti-smoking culture, when tobacco, like alcohol, was accepted as an ordinary indulgence.

Even in his youth, he indulged in only a few cigarettes a week, and lately it was very rare for him to partake of one at all.

For that very reason, Kaede thought she ought to leave him at least this small pleasure.

Once he had partaken of the cigarette, a look of bliss came over her grandfather's face.

Kaede did not dislike the scent of Gauloises, but she was afraid it would cling to the T-shirt hanging out to dry, so she opened the window a crack.

Letting violet smoke curl slowly upward, her grandfather said in a clearer voice than before, "Now then—"

It was as though the cigarette had switched his mind into a higher gear.

"What kind of story do you spin from these materials, Kaede?"



Kaede's heart began to hammer.

Her grandfather had always called a hypothesis a "story."

Now he really was back.

That grandfather.

"Here's the story I've come up with."

Striving to sound calm, Kaede laid out the hypothesis she had thought through again and again.

"Story one. 'The person who tucked the obituaries into the book was its former owner. He, or she, wanted other fans of Mr Setogawa to share the emptiness left by his death, and so deliberately placed the obituaries inside the book.'"

When she glanced at her grandfather, he was nodding as though she had hit on exactly the right idea.

As ever, telling a story in front of him made her tense.

And yet—

(I'm glad.)

"Um, story two."

Coming back to herself, Kaede went on.

"'It was someone at the secondhand bookshop who slipped the obituary clipping into the book. He or she was a fan of Mr Setogawa. Then, for the first time in decades, an order came in for one of Mr Setogawa's out-of-print books. Delighted, he or she slipped the clipping into the book for an unknown fellow enthusiast—that is, me—thinking of it as a kind of present.'"

Maybe it's the excitement of thinking it through, but her throat is dry.

"What do you think? Those are the two stories I've come up with."

Her grandfather answered.

"Hm, not bad. Each one has its own logic, and neither could be called forced. But both contain a major contradiction."

"I see."

Kaede bit her lip.

"Listen. The contradiction in Story One is this. Would someone devoted enough to Setogawa—an old senior of mine—to keep his obituary clipping really sell a cherished book? This book, moreover, was Mr Setogawa's final work. Anyone with ordinary sensibilities would keep it, together with the clipping, and treasure it as part of their own collection."

Kaede could only nod.

"Yes. From a book lover's point of view, that may be hard to accept."

"Story Two feels better than Story One. But the contradiction is still there. If someone at the bookshop slipped the obituary clipping into the book out of kindness... why not add even a brief note? If they took the trouble to place the clipping inside so carefully, they could at least have written something like, 'As a fellow enthusiast, I was delighted. I have taken the liberty of enclosing an article reporting Mr Setogawa's passing, in tribute to his memory.' Why would he or she begrudge even that slight effort? In short—"





Her grandfather said it bluntly.

"Both Story One and Story Two are fundamentally broken as stories. There is another Story X besides these."




"Then..."

Kaede asked in a hoarse voice.

"Grandpa, are you saying you can spin that Story X?"

Her grandfather said nothing. As if reluctant to part with it, he barely managed to pinch the Gauloises, now little more than a stub, between thumb and forefinger and blew out one last puff of smoke.

His eyes slowly—but surely—narrowed.

Kaede worries he is about to fall asleep.

But that fear was groundless.

Her grandfather opened his eyes wide and said, "I've just seen the 'picture.'"

"Unfortunately, the book's former owner, a man, has already passed away."

"What?"

"Why, look. There's a man right there with a peaceful expression on his face."

A vision.

But this vision was grounded in clear logic—Kaede felt that instinctively.

Her grandfather went on.

"Story X goes like this. While he was alive, grieving the death of Mr Setogawa Takeshi, whom he so admired, he had tucked the obituary into one of the books he treasured. But then—after he died, his wife, never knowing what a treasure it was to him, sold it off with a bundle of other books to a secondhand bookshop while sorting through his belongings."

(The truth—)

She could not help thinking so.

Wasn’t this something without the slightest contradiction, something that settled cleanly in the gut—a perfect “story”?




But Kaede pressed on.

“How do you know the book had belonged to a man? It could have been a woman too.”

“No,” her grandfather said offhandedly.

“When a spouse dies, it’s women who can hold their grief in check and still act calmly. Men are no good at all. Take me, for instance—”

Her grandfather lowered his gaze.

“When my wife died before me, I couldn’t do a thing.”

For an instant, her late grandmother’s round, soft-featured face crossed Kaede’s mind.

For a while, silence continued.
