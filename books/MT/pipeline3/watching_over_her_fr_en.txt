For Berenice




There are thirty-two of them. Thirty-two still living in the abbey on this autumn day in 1986, at the end of a road steep enough to blanch anyone who travels it. In a thousand years, nothing has changed. Neither the steepness of the way nor the vertigo. Thirty-two strong hearts—you must be, to live perched at the edge of the void—thirty-two bodies that were once strong too, in their youth. In a few hours, they will be one fewer.

The brothers form a circle around the one who is leaving. There have been many circles, many farewells, since the Sacra raised its walls above them. There have been many moments of grace, of doubt, of bodies braced against the coming shadow. There have been other departures, and there will be more; they wait patiently.

This dying man is not like the others. He is the only one here who has not taken vows. Yet he has been allowed to stay for forty years. Whenever debate arose, whenever questions were raised, a man in a purple robe arrived—never the same one—to settle the matter. He stays. He belongs to the place as surely as the cloister, its columns, its Romanesque capitals, whose state of preservation owes much to his talent. So let us not complain; he pays his way in kind.




Only his fists jut out from the brown wool blanket, one on either side of his head, an eighty-two-year-old child in the grip of a nightmare. His skin is yellow, at breaking point, vellum stretched over angles too sharp. His forehead gleams, waxed by a greasy fever. One day his strength was bound to fail him. A pity he never answered their questions. A man is entitled to his secrets.

Besides, they feel they know. Not everything, but the essential thing. Sometimes opinions differ. To stave off boredom, they discover in themselves a gossip's ardor. He is a criminal, a defrocked priest, a political refugee. Some say he is kept here against his will—the theory does not hold, he has been seen leaving, and coming back—others insist he is here for his own safety. And then the most popular version, and the most secret, because romanticism can only be smuggled in here: he is here to watch over her. Her, waiting in her marble night, a few hundred meters from the little cell. Her, patient these forty years. Every monk of the Sacra has seen her once. All would like to see her again. One need only ask Padre Vincenzo, the superior, for permission, but few dare. For fear, perhaps, of the impious thoughts that come, they say, to those who draw too near. And the monks have more than enough impious thoughts as it is when, in the heart of the darkness, they are pursued by dreams with an angel's face.




The dying man struggles, opens his eyes, closes them again. One of the brothers swears he read joy there—he is mistaken. A cool cloth is laid on his forehead, on his lips, gently.

The sick man stirs again and for once they all agree.

He is trying to say something.




Of course I am trying to say something. I have seen man fly, faster and faster, farther and farther. I have seen two wars, nations sink, I have picked oranges on Sunset Boulevard—you don't think I have something to tell? Forgive me, I am ungrateful. You clothed me, you fed me when you had nothing, or next to nothing, when I decided to hide among you. But I have kept silent too long. Close the shutters, the light hurts me.

He stirs. Close the shutters, brother, it seems the light troubles him.

The shadows watching over me against the light, under a Piedmont sun, the voices growing muffled as sleep draws near. Everything happened so fast. Barely a week ago they still saw me in the vegetable garden, or on a ladder, there was always something to repair. Slowed by age, true, but since nobody would have bet on me at my birth, that alone compelled some admiration. And then one morning I could not get up. I read it in their faces: it was my turn, they would soon toll the passing bell and carry me to the little garden facing the mountain, where poppies grow over centuries of abbots, illuminators, cantors, and sacristans.

He is very ill.




The shutters creak. Forty years I've been here, they have always creaked. Darkness, at last. Darkness as at the cinema — whose birth I witnessed. An empty horizon, first nothing. A blinding plain I stare at until my memory peoples it with shadows, silhouettes that become towns, forests, men and beasts. They move forward, camp downstage, my actors. I recognize a few of them; they haven't changed. Sublime and ridiculous, fused in the same crucible, inseparable. The currency of tragedy is a rare alloy of gold and cheap trinkets.

It is only a matter of hours now.

A matter of hours? Don't make me laugh. I have been dead a long time.

Another fresh compress. He seems calmer.

But since when can't the dead tell their story?




Il Francese. I always hated that nickname, even if I was given far worse ones. All my joys, all my dramas belong to Italy. I come from a land where beauty is always at bay. Let it fall asleep for five minutes and ugliness will slit its throat without mercy. Geniuses grow here like weeds. People sing as they kill, draw as they deceive, let dogs piss on church walls. It is not for nothing that an Italian, Mercalli, gave his name to a scale of destruction, the one that measures the intensity of earthquakes. One hand demolishes what the other built, and the emotion is the same.

Italy, kingdom of marble and filth. My country.




But it is a fact, I was born in France in 1904. My parents had left Liguria in search of fortune fifteen years earlier, barely married. By way of fortune, they were called Ritals, spat on, mocked for the way they rolled their r's — though as far as I know, the word roll itself does begin with an r. My father had narrowly escaped the racist riots of Aigues-Mortes in 1893; two of his friends died there: brave Luciano and old Salvatore. They were never spoken of again without those adjectives.

Some families forbade their children to speak the language of the old country, so as not to "play the Rital." They scrubbed them with Marseille soap in the hope of whitening them a little. Not among the Vitalianis. We spoke Italian, ate Italian. We thought Italian, that is to say in superlatives, with Death often invoked, tears in abundance, hands rarely still. We cursed as we passed the salt. Our family was a circus, and we were proud of it.

In 1914, the French state, which had shown so little zeal in protecting Luciano, Salvatore, and the others, declared that my father was, without the shadow of a doubt, a good Frenchman, worthy of conscription, all the more so because some civil servant had, by mistake or for sport, made him ten years younger when copying out his birth certificate. He left with a long face, with no flowers in his rifle. His own father had lost his life in the Expedition of the Thousand in 1860. Nonno Carlo had conquered Sicily with Garibaldi. It was not a Bourbon bullet that killed him, but a prostitute from the port of Marsala with dubious hygiene, a detail the family preferred to pass over in silence. He was dead all the same, and the message was clear: war killed.



It killed my father. One day a gendarme turned up at the workshop above which we lived, in the Maurienne valley. My mother opened every day on the chance of an order her husband might fill when he came back; one day or another they would have to start cutting stone again, restoring gargoyles, carving fountains. The gendarme assumed a suitably solemn expression, looked even more distressed when he saw me, cleared his throat, explained that there had been a shell, and that was that. When my mother, very dignified, asked him when the body would be brought home, he stammered, explained that there were horses on the battlefield, other soldiers, that a shell did damage and that, as a result, you could not always tell who was who, or even what was man and what was horse. My mother thought he was about to cry, had to offer him a glass of Amaro Braulio - I never saw a Frenchman drink it without pulling a face - and only wept herself many hours later.

Of course, I do not remember all this, or only badly. I know the facts; I restore them with a little color, those colors now slipping through my fingers in the cell I have occupied for forty years on Mount Pirchiriano. Even today - or rather a few days ago, when I was still capable of it - I speak French badly. No one has called me Francese since 1946.



A few days after the gendarme's visit, my mother explained to me that in France she would not be able to give me the education I needed. Her belly was already beginning to swell with a brother or sister - who was never born, at least not alive - and she drowned me in kisses as she explained that she was sending me away for my own good, sending me back home because she believed in me, because she could see my love of stone despite my young age, because she knew I was destined for great things, and that she had given me a first name for that.



Of the two burdens of my life, my given name was no doubt the lighter one to bear. I hated it passionately all the same.

My mother often went down to the workshop to watch her husband work. She realized she was pregnant when she felt me start at the strike of a chisel. Until that moment she had spared herself no effort, had helped my father move enormous blocks, which perhaps explains what came next.

- He will be a sculptor, she announced.

My father grumbled and replied that it was a rotten trade, one in which your hands, back, and eyes wore out much faster than the stone, and that if you were not Michelangelo, you might as well spare yourself all that.

My mother agreed, and decided to give me a head start.

My name is Michelangelo Vitaliani.

I discovered my country in October 1916, in the company of a drunkard and a butterfly. The drunkard had known my father, dodged conscription thanks to the state of his liver, but the way things were going suggested his cirrhosis would not protect him much longer. They were drafting kids, old men, cripples. The newspapers insisted that we were winning, that the Boche would soon be ancient history. In our community, the news that Italy had joined the Allies the previous year had been greeted as a promise of victory. Those who came back from the front were singing a different tune, for those who still felt like singing. The ingeniere Carmone, who, like the other Ritals, had worked the salt pans at Aigues-Mortes, then opened a grocery in Savoy, where he drank a good part of his stock of wine, had therefore decided to go home. If he had to die, he might as well do it back home, lips reddened by montepulciano to help wash down the fear.

His country was the Abruzzi. He was kind, and agreed to drop me off with Zio Alberto on the way. He did it because he felt a little sorry for me and also, I think, for my mother's eyes. A mother's eyes are often something, but mine had irises of a strange blue, almost violet. They had started more than one brawl, until my father put an end to all that. A stonecutter has dangerous hands, I will not be the one to say otherwise. The competition quickly gave way.

My mother shed big violet tears on the station platform in Modane. My uncle Alberto, a sculptor too, was going to look after me. She swore she would join me soon, as soon as she had sold the workshop and made a little money. A matter of weeks, a few months at most; it took her twenty years. The train blew, spat black smoke whose taste I can still feel, and carried off the tipsy ingeniere and her only son.

Whatever people say, at twelve sadness does not last very long. I did not know what this train was lurching toward, but I knew I had never taken a train before, or did not remember it. Excitement quickly gave way to unease. Everything was going too fast. As soon as I fixed on a detail, a fir tree, a house, it vanished at once. A landscape is not made to move. I felt sick, wanted to tell the ingeniere about it, but he was snoring with his mouth open.

Fortunately, there was the butterfly. It came in at Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne and settled on the window, between the mountains streaming past and me. After a brief struggle against the glass, it gave up and did not move again. It was not a beautiful butterfly, one of those glories of color and gold I would see later in spring. Just a mediocre butterfly, gray, a little bluish if you squinted hard, a moth stupefied by daylight. For a moment I thought of torturing it, like all boys my age, then I realized that by staring at it, the only still thing in a world gone mad, my nausea was lifting. The butterfly stayed there for hours, sent by some friendly power to reassure me, and that was perhaps my very first intuition that nothing is really what it seems to be, that a butterfly is not just a butterfly but a story, something enormous lurking in a very tiny space, which the first atomic bomb would confirm a few decades later and, perhaps even more, what I am leaving behind as I die in the foundations of the most beautiful abbey in the country.

When the ingeniere Carmone woke up, he laid out his project for me, because he had one. He was a communist. Do you know what that is? I had heard the insult several times in the community, back there in France, where people were always wondering whether so-and-so was one. I answered, "Pfff, of course, it's a man who loves men."

The ingeniere burst out laughing. In a way, yes, a communist was a man who loved men. "Besides, there's no bad way to love men, do you understand that?" I had never seen him so serious.

The Carmone family owned a piece of land in the province of L'Aquila, to which geography had done two injustices. One, it was the only province in the Abruzzi with no access to the sea. Two, earthquakes ravaged it at regular intervals, like the Liguria of my ancestors, except that the bitch Liguria at least had access to the sea.

His land had a pleasant view over Lake Scanno. The ingeniere intended to build a tower there, mounted on a gigantic ball bearing, and house the local proletarians in it, all for a modest rent that would allow him to live decently himself, especially since, being a good communist, he was reserving the top floor for himself. Thanks to two teams of horses relieving one another every twelve hours, the building would revolve over the course of the day. Its inhabitants would thus enjoy, without exception, with no profiteers and no exploited classes, a view of the lake once a day. Perhaps electricity would replace the horses one day, although Carmone admitted it would probably never reach so far. But he liked to dream.

The ball bearings would also have the advantage, if an earthquake struck, of decoupling the structure from the ground. In the event of a degree XII earthquake on the Mercalli scale — it was he who taught me that name — his building had a thirty percent better chance of holding up than an ordinary building. Thirty percent doesn't sound like much, just like that, but degree XII was not exactly a laughing matter, he explained, opening his eyes wide, so it was enormous.

I drifted into a half-sleep, my eyes fixed on my butterfly, and we entered Italy while the ingeniere spoke to me tenderly of devastation.

Italy and I embraced like old friends the first time we met. In my rush to get off the train at Turin station, I tripped on the step and landed on the platform with my arms flung out in a cross. I lay there for a moment, without thinking of crying, with the beatitude of a priest at his ordination. Italy smelled of gunflint. Italy smelled of war.

The ingeniere decided to take a cab. It cost more than walking, but my mother had entrusted him with some money in an envelope and, just as wine was meant to be drunk, he declared, money was meant to be spent; besides, let's go buy ourselves a little jug of rough red from the Po before we set off, if that suits you.

It suited me fine, dazzled as I was by what I was discovering: soldiers on leave, soldiers on their way out, porters, train drivers, and a whole crowd of shifty characters whose role or ambitions seemed mysterious to the child I was. I had never seen shifty characters in my life. I had the impression they returned my insistent looks with kindness, as though to tell me, you're one of us. Perhaps they were simply staring at the blue lump growing in the middle of my forehead. I moved forward through a forest of legs, beatific, spellbound by other smells: creosote and leather, metal and cannon, scents of half-light and battlefields. And there was the noise, a blacksmith's din. It scraped, squealed, crashed — musique concrète played by illiterates, a far cry from the halls where jaded worthies would one day crowd in to pretend to enjoy it.

Without knowing it, I had arrived in the heart of Futurism. The world was nothing but speed - the speed of footsteps, trains, bullets, changes in fortune or alliance. And yet all those men, that whole mass, seemed to be digging in their heels. Bodies exulted, pressed toward the carriages, the trenches, a barbed-wire horizon. But something, between two movements, two surges, was screaming I want to live a little longer.

Later, when my career took off, a collector proudly showed me his latest acquisition, Luigi Russolo's Futurist painting The Revolt. It was in Rome, at the turn of the 1930s, I think. The man fancied himself an enlightened connoisseur, passionate about abstract art. He was an idiot. Unless you had been there that day, at Porta Nuova station, no one could understand that work. No one could understand that there is nothing abstract about it. It is a figurative painting. Russolo painted what was exploding in our faces.

No twelve-year-old boy puts it that way, obviously. At the time, I merely looked around me, eyes wide, while the ingeniere quenched his thirst in a dive at the end of the platform. But I saw all of it. One more sign, if any were needed, that I was not quite like everyone else.







We left the station under a light fall of snow. We had barely stepped outside when a carabiniere stepped in front of us and asked to see my papers. Not my companion's, only mine. His fingers numbed by the cold and the cheap red from the Po, the ingeniere Carmone handed him my laissez-passer. The other man looked at me with a suspicious air, an air he must have put on every morning to go to work and put away again at night, unless he had been born with it.

"You're a little Francese?"

I did not like being called "Francese." I liked being called "little" even less.

"Little Francese yourself, cazzino."

The carabiniere nearly choked - cazzino was the favorite insult of the back courtyards where I had grown up, and carabinieri do not choose a profession with such handsome uniforms so people can insult the size of their manhood.

Like the good engineer he was, the ingeniere pulled my mother's envelope from his pocket and put some grease back into the gears that had seized up. Before long we were on our way again. I refused to get into a cab and pointed to a tram. Carmone grumbled, consulted a map, asked a few questions, and determined that the tram would leave us not too far from where we needed to go.

My backside on a wooden bench, I crossed the first big city of my life. I was happy. I had lost my father, I did not know when I would see my mother again, but yes, I was happy and drunk on everything still ahead of me, that great mass of future to climb, to carve to my measure.

"Tell me, Signor Carmone?"

"Yes?"

"What's electricity?"




He stared at me in astonishment, then seemed to remember that I had spent the first decade of my life in a village in Savoy I had never left.

"That's it, my boy."

He pointed to a streetlamp topped by a handsome golden globe.

"So it's like a candle, then?"

"Only it never stops. It's electrons moving between two pieces of charcoal."

"What's an electron? Some kind of fairy?"

"No, that's science."

"What's science?"

The snowflakes spun, light as a girl's dress. The ingeniere answered my questions without impatience or condescension. Before long we passed an immense building site: the Lingotto, where Fiat cars would, a few years later, climb a helical ramp to the roof and make their first circuits after assembly - a mechanical Sacra di San Michele. The suburbs thinned out, the roads gave way to tracks, the tram stopped in what looked like a field. We had to walk the last three kilometers. I am grateful to that man, Carmone, for accompanying me so far, despite the cold, despite the times. We walked through the mud and I imagined that already my mother's eyes were beginning to pale in his memory, to seem less violet to him. But he led me without faltering to Zio Alberto's door.




It took leaning on the bell and hammering the knocker several times before Alberto deigned to open, wearing a filthy undershirt. The same clouded eyes as the ingeniere, crazed with red veins: the two men shared an immoderate taste for grappa. My mother had written to announce my arrival, so there was not much to explain.

- Here is your new apprentice, Michelangelo, Antonella Vitaliani's son. Your nephew.

- I don't like being called Michelangelo.

Zio Alberto lowered his eyes to me. I thought he was going to ask what I preferred to be called, to which I would have answered "Mimo." The nickname my parents had always given me, the nickname I would still be given for another seventy years.

- I don't want him, Alberto said.

Once again, I had forgotten one detail. Because yes, that is what it is: a detail.

- I don't understand. I thought Antonell... that Mrs. Vitaliani had written to you, and that it was agreed.

- Yeah, she wrote. But I don't want an apprentice like that.

- And why not?

- Because nobody told me he was a runt.







C'è un piccolo problema, old Rosa had said, the neighbor who was delivering my mother on a stormy night. The stove clattered, stoked by a contrary wind, a hellish draft reddened the walls. A few matrons from the neighborhood, who had come to attend the event, curious to catch a glimpse of that firm flesh that set their husbands dreaming, had long since fled, crossing themselves and murmuring il diavolo. Old Rosa, unflappable, went on humming, sponging, encouraging. Cholera, the cold, bad luck plain and simple, a knife that would not have been drawn if there had been less drinking, had taken children, friends, husbands from her. She was old, she was ugly, she had nothing to lose. So the devil left her alone; he knew how to recognize a source of trouble. He had easier prey.

C'è un piccolo problema, then, she said, wrenching me from Antonella Vitaliani's entrails. Everything lay in that word, piccolo; it was obvious to anyone who saw me that I would remain more or less piccolo all my life. Rosa laid me on my exhausted mother. My father came pounding up the stairs, and Rosa said later that he had frowned when he saw me, looked around him as if searching for something else, his real son rather than this sketch, then nodded, I see, that's how it is - the way he did when he struck a hidden crack in the heart of a block of stone and the work of several weeks blew to dust. You cannot blame the stone.

It was stone, precisely, that they attributed my difference to. My mother had not known how to rest, hauling enormous blocks to the workshop, enough to shame the neighborhood strongmen. Poor Mimo, if the neighbors were to be believed, had suffered for it. Achondroplasia, they would say later. I would be described as a person of short stature, which frankly was no better than Zio Alberto's "runt." They would explain to me that my height did not define me. If that were true, why speak of my height? I have never heard anyone speak of a "person of average height."

I never held it against my parents. If stone made me what I am, if some black magic was at work, it also lavished on me what it took away. Stone always spoke to me, every kind of stone, limestone, metamorphic stones, even tombstones, the ones I would soon lie down on to listen to stories of gisants.

"This wasn't what was planned," murmured the ingeniere, tapping his lips with a gloved finger. "This is troublesome."

It was snowing hard now. Zio Alberto shrugged and tried to slam the door in our faces. The ingeniere blocked it with his foot. He drew my mother's envelope from the inside pocket of his old fur coat and held it out to my uncle. It contained almost all the Vitalianis' savings. Years of exile, labor, skin burned by sun and salt, of starting over, years of marble under the nails, with now and then an ounce of the tenderness that had seen me come into the world. That was why those dirty, crumpled banknotes were precious. Why Zio Alberto opened the door a little wider.

"This sum was for the little one. I mean, Mimo," he corrected, blushing. "If Mimo agrees to give it to you, he would no longer be an apprentice, but a partner."

Zio Alberto nodded slowly.

"Hmm, a partner."

He was still hesitating. Carmone waited as long as he could, then sighed and took a leather pouch from his pack. Everything about the ingeniere celebrated wear, patching, an aesthetic of time passing. But the leather of the pouch, new and supple, still seemed to quiver with the rages of the beast it had clothed. Carmone passed a cracked glove over it, opened it, and reluctantly took out a pipe.

"It's a pipe I acquired at great expense. Carved from the root of a briar on which the Hero of Two Worlds, the great Garibaldi himself, is said to have sat during his noble and fruitless attempt to rally Rome to our beautiful kingdom."

I had seen dozens of those pipes, sold in Aigues-Mortes to those fool Frenchmen. I did not know how this one had ended up in Carmone's hands, how he had let himself be taken in. I felt a little ashamed for him, and for Italy in general. He was a naive, generous man. This cost him, and I know he did it sincerely, to help me, not because he was in a hurry to get home or dreaded burdening himself with a twelve-year-old boy with unusual proportions. Alberto accepted, and they sealed the bargain with a shot of rotgut whose sourness prickled the air inside the hovel. Then Carmone got to his feet, had one last for the road, and soon his wavering silhouette was moving away through the snow.

He turned back one last time, hand raised in the yellow phosphorescence of a dying world, and smiled at me. The Abruzzi were far away, he was no longer all that young, the times were hard. Later, I never went to Lake Scanno, for fear of discovering that, over there, there was no tower mounted on ball bearings and never had been.

I owe a great deal to the women they called "lost," and my uncle Alberto was the son of one of them. A brave girl who lay beneath men in the port of Genoa, without anger or shame. She was the only person my uncle ever spoke of with respect, with a fervor bordering on veneration. But the saint of the alleyways was far away. And since Alberto could neither read nor write, his mother became, with each passing day, more and more a creature of myth. As for me, I wrote rather well, which delighted my uncle when he noticed it.



My uncle Alberto was not my uncle. We did not share a single atom of blood. I never managed to clear the matter up entirely, but his grandfather apparently owed mine a debt, an unpaid loan whose moral burden was passed down from generation to generation. In his own perverse way, Alberto was honest. At my mother's request, he had agreed to take me in. He owned a small workshop on the outskirts of Turin. Since he was a bachelor and not much drawn to luxury, a few jobs here and there were enough to meet his needs, or had been until I arrived. Because the war, that enterprise of progress so many enthusiasts were praising at the time, though they did not care for the word and preferred "poet" or "philosopher," the war, then, had popularized materials cheaper than stone, lighter, easy to produce and easy to work. Steel was Alberto's worst enemy, and he insulted it even in his sleep. He hated it more even than the Austro-Hungarians or the Germans. A Crucco, as they called the Boches there, could still be granted extenuating circumstances. Their cooking, their ridiculous spiked helmets, they had enough to be angry about. But building in steel was unthinkable, and he would laugh best who laughed last when everything came crashing down. Alberto had not understood that everything had already collapsed. To his credit, steel was not foreign to that; it had made some fine cannons.



Alberto looked old, but he wasn't. At thirty-five, he lived alone in a room next to his workshop. His bachelorhood was surprising, especially since, after a shower, washed clean of marble dust and dressed in his only suit, he was not bad-looking. He kept going to the same brothel in Turin, where he treated the girls with legendary respect. The expression "to go about it like Alberto" was popular in the early twenties in the southern districts of the city, between Lingotto and San Salvario, before falling out of use when Alberto moved away, taking his marbles and his slave with him, meaning me. Partner, that one still makes me laugh.

People have often asked me what part he played in what came next. If by "what came next" they mean my career, none. If, on the other hand, they mean my final work, a few flashes of him are no doubt in it. No, not flashes, fragments; I would not want anyone to suspect he ever shone. Zio Alberto was an asshole. Not a monster, just a sorry man, which comes to the same thing. I think of him without hatred, but without sadness.

For nearly a year, I lived in that man’s shadow. I cooked, I cleaned. I carried, I delivered. A hundred times I nearly got crushed by a tram, knocked down by a horse, beaten bloody by some fellow who had mocked my size and whom I had told that at least my size was no problem on the piano di sotto, on the floor below, preferably in front of his girlfriend. The ingeniere Carmone would have been delighted to find the atmosphere in our neighborhood so electric. Every interaction was a potential lightning strike, a displacement of electrons whose effects no one could ever foresee. We were at war with the Germans, the Austro-Hungarians, our governments, our neighbors, which is another way of saying we were at war with ourselves. One man wanted war, another peace, voices rose, and the one who wanted peace ended by throwing the first punch.

Zio Alberto forbade me to touch his tools. One day he caught me correcting a small holy-water stoup commissioned by the neighboring parish of Beata Vergine delle Grazie. Alberto got monumentally drunk once or twice a week, and the last binge had left its mark. The stoup was crude, insulting, a twelve-year-old could have done better, and one did while the other slept off his wine. Alberto woke up and caught me red-handed, chisel in hand. He examined my work in astonishment, then thrashed me while insulting me in a language I did not understand, a Genoese patois. Then he went back to sleep. When he opened his eyes again and found me stiff with pain and covered in bruises, he pretended not to know what had happened. He went straight to his stoup, remarked that he was not displeased with his work, and magnanimously offered to deliver it himself.

Alberto regularly dictated a letter to his mother, and allowed me to write one to mine at the same time — he generously paid for the stamp. Antonella did not always reply, forever on the roads, chasing the job that would keep her going for one more week, then another. I missed her violet eyes. My father, the man who had guided my first clumsy blows, the man who had taught me the difference between a claw chisel, a riffler, and a cutting chisel, was fading.

Work grew scarcer over the course of 1917, Alberto’s moods darker, his drunken bouts more violent. Columns of soldiers sometimes marched against a backdrop of dusk, the newspapers spoke of nothing but it, the war, the war, yet all we felt of it was a vague unease, the impression of being out of joint with our surroundings, of never being in the right place. Over there, some filthy beast was mauling the horizon. But we led an almost normal existence, a shirker’s life that gave a faint taste of guilt to everything we ate. At least until August 22, when bread began to run short and there was nothing left to eat at all. Turin exploded. Lenin’s name appeared on the city walls, barricades went up, and a revolutionary even stopped me in the street on the morning of the twenty-fourth to tell me to be careful, that their barricades were electrified, which told me more surely than anything else that the world was changing. The fellow called me comrade and clapped me on the back. I saw women stand up to sheepish soldiers on the barricades, climb onto armored vehicles, bare triumphant, furious breasts at which they did not dare shoot. Not right away, in any case.

The uprising lasted three days. Nobody could agree on anything, except that everyone had had enough of the war. In the end the government got everyone to agree with machine-gun fire; fifty dead cooled their ardor. I holed up in the workshop. One evening, calm had barely returned, and a little bread too, when Zio Alberto came home in a cheerier mood than usual. He made as if to slap me, cackled when he saw me dive under the table, then ordered me to take up a pen and dictated a letter to his mother. He stank of the rotgut they served on the corner.

Mammina,

I got the money order you sent me all right. Thanks to it, I’m going to be able to buy the little workshop I told you about at Christmas. It’s in Liguria, so closer to you. There’s no more work in Turin. But over there they got a castle that always needs repairs, and a church the authorities care a whole lot about, so that’s work. I sold up here, not for much but all right, I just signed with that rat Lorenzo, and I’m leaving soon with that little shit Mimo. I’ll write to you from Pietra d’Alba, your son who loves you.

“And give me a nice signature, pezzo di merda,” Zio Alberto concluded. “One that shows I’ve made good.”

When I think back to that time, it is strange: I was not unhappy. I was alone, I had nothing and no one, in the north of Europe they were turning forests inside out, sowing them with flesh larded with metal, plus a few shells that would explode years later in the faces of innocent walkers, they were inventing a desolation to make Mercalli himself go pale, and he had given his poor scale only twelve degrees. But I was not unhappy, I observed as much every evening, when I prayed to a personal pantheon of idols that changed throughout my life and even came to include, later, opera singers and football players. Perhaps because I was young, my days were beautiful. Only now do I grasp what the beauty of day owes to the foreknowledge of night.

The abbot leaves his office and starts down the Stairway of the Dead, aptly named. In a few moments he will go to the bedside of the man in agony in the annex. The brothers sent word to him that the hour is close. He will place the bread of life upon his lips.

Padre Vincenzo crosses the church without paying attention to its frescoes, passes through the Zodiac Portal, and comes out onto the terraces at the summit of Mount Pirchiriano, from which the abbey stares down at Piedmont. Before him stand the ruins of a tower. Legend has it that a young peasant girl, fair Alda, once flew from there to escape enemy soldiers, helped by Saint Michael. Vanitas vanitatis: she tried to repeat the feat in front of the villagers, just to impress them, and crashed below. As would part of the tower that bears her name in the fourteenth century, brought down by one of the many earthquakes that regularly shake the region.

Farther on, a few steps lead down into the ground, blocked by a chain and a sign reading NO ENTRY. The abbot steps over it with a suppleness commendable for his age. This is not the path to the annex where the dying man is waiting for him. Before going to him, the priest wants to see her. She who sometimes gives him uneasy sleep, because he dreads an intrusion, or worse. You never know what may happen, like that time fifteen years ago when fra Bartolomeo caught someone right in front of the last grille protecting her. The man, an American, had tried to pass himself off as a lost visitor. The abbot had smelled the lie at once; he knew that smell by heart, it was the smell of confessionals. No tourist could descend so deep into the base of the Sacra di San Michele by accident. No, the man was there because he had heard the rumor.

The abbot had been right. Five years later, the same man came back with due authorization signed by some Vatican grandee. So they opened the door to him, and the list of those who had contemplated her grew a little longer. Leonard B. Williams, that was the name of this professor from Stanford University in California. Williams had devoted his life to the Sacra's captive, trying to unravel her mystery. He had published a monograph on her, a few articles, then silence. His work, brilliant though it was, slept on forgotten shelves. The Vatican had played its hand well, opening that door as if they had nothing to hide. For years calm had returned. But for the past few months the monks have been reporting tourists who were no tourists at all, people nosing around. You could recognize them among a thousand. The pressure is rising again.

For long minutes, the abbot goes down, never once losing his bearings in the maze of corridors. He would find his way in the dark, he has walked it so often. A jingling of little bells accompanies him - the sound of the bunch of keys in his hand. Those blasted keys. There is one for every door in the abbey, sometimes two, as if a mystery were throbbing behind the slightest door. As if the mystery that gathers them all there, the Eucharist, were not enough.

He is almost there. Smells earth, damp, the scent of billions of granite atoms crushed by their own weight, and even a little of the greenery of the surrounding slopes. The grille, at last. The old one has been replaced; it is now fitted with a five-point locking system. The remote-control unit does not work on the first try, padre Vincenzo stubbornly presses its rubber buttons, every time it's the same, some progress this is, it's 1986 and they still can't produce a remote control that works? He catches himself. Lord, forgive my impatience.

At last the red light goes out, the alarm is disabled. The final corridor is watched by two state-of-the-art cameras, no bigger than shoeboxes. It is impossible to get in without alerting someone. And even if an intruder managed it, what would be the point? He would not leave with her. It had taken ten men to bring her down.

Padre Vincenzo shudders. It's not theft he needs to fear. He has not forgotten that lunatic Laszlo Toth. He reproaches himself again, *lunatic* is not charitable, let us say *disturbed*. They had come close to disaster. But he does not want to think of Laszlo at this moment, of the Hungarian's sinister face and wild, lit-up gaze. Disaster had been avoided.

They lock her away to protect her. The irony is not lost on the abbot. She is there, do not worry, she is perfectly well, except that no one is allowed to see her. No one apart from him, the padre, the monks who request it, the few cardinals who locked her in there forty years ago and are still alive, probably a few bureaucrats too. Thirty people in the world, at most. And of course her creator, who had a key of his own. He came as he pleased to look after her and wash her regularly. Because yes, she has to be washed.

The abbot opens the last two locks. He always starts with the top one, a habit that perhaps betrays a kind of nervousness. He would like to break himself of it and promises himself, as he did on his previous visit, that next time he will start with the bottom one. The door opens without a sound - the locksmith who boasted of the quality of the hinges had not lied.




He does not switch on the light. The original fluorescent tubes were replaced at the same time as the grille with softer lighting, and so much the better, those tubes brutalized her. But he prefers to see her in the dark. The abbot steps forward, touches her with his fingertips, out of habit. She is a little taller than he is. In the center of a round room, a primitive sanctuary with Romanesque vaulting, she stands slightly bent over on her plinth, sunk in a dream of stone. The only light comes from the corridor, picking out two faces, the break of a wrist. The abbot knows every detail of the statue sleeping in the shadows, having scrutinized it until his eyes were worn out.

They lock her away to protect her.

The abbot suspects that those who put her there were trying to protect themselves.




Savona had given Italy two popes, Sixtus IV and Julius II. Pietra d'Alba, barely thirty kilometers to the north, very nearly gave her a third. I believe I was a little responsible for that failure.

I would have laughed if anyone had told me, on that morning of December 10, 1917, that the history of the papacy would be bent by the boy dragging his feet behind Zio Alberto. We had been traveling for three days, almost without stopping. The whole country was hanging on news from the front, after the drubbing the Austro-Hungarians had handed us at Caporetto. They said the lines had stabilized not far from Venice. They also said the opposite, that the enemy was about to show up and slit our throats in our sleep or, worse, force us to eat cabbage.




Pietra d'Alba appeared, cut from the light of the rising sun, on its rocky peak. Its position, I understood an hour later, was an illusion. Pietra was not perched on a spur but set on the edge of a plateau. Truly on the edge, which is to say that between the village wall and the edge of the cliff there was a passage barely wide enough for two men abreast. Then fifty meters of emptiness, or rather of pure air, laden with the scents of resin and thyme.

You had to cross the whole village to discover what had made its reputation: an immense plateau undulating toward Piedmont, a piece of Tuscany shifted there by the whims of geology. To the west as to the east, Liguria kept watch and reminded it not to get too comfortable. Beyond stretched the mountains, slopes covered with a forest whose green was almost as black as the beasts roaming through it. Pietra d'Alba was beautiful with its faintly pink stone - thousands of dawns had embedded themselves in it.
