Faith

My name is Cléo Louvent Johnson. My mother is French, my father American; I navigate easily between the two languages. My mother is a statistician, my father an Egyptologist; I take shape between numbers and hieroglyphs. I grow up in a book-filled apartment in a quiet corner of the 14th arrondissement of Paris. My father, who affectionately calls me Cleopatra, puts my drawings of pyramids and sphinxes up in the kitchen with magnets. On Saturdays, my mother takes me to the neighborhood library; on the way home, we stop to say hello to the gray herons in Parc Montsouris.





I am four years old. My father tells me a story in my room. My favorite books are the ones he illuminates with his commentary. An Egypt specialist, passionate about the ancient world in all its forms, he narrates the misfortunes of Isis and Osiris, their struggle against Set, but also the wanderings of Odysseus, the childish antics of the gods of Olympus; I drink in his words when he details the exploits of Ramesses II, Pericles, Alexander the Great. Each story becomes a pretext for detours through history and its epics, teeming with figures on extraordinary quests and marvelous animals. Soon it is time to sleep; my father tucks me in before turning out the light, when suddenly he senses that I am preoccupied. When he gently insists, I finally confide in him: “Daddy, I’d like to be as famous as Céline Dion.”

I have no memory of it, but for years my parents delighted in reminding me of this anecdote, which made them laugh a great deal. I imagine a swell of tenderness mixed with surprise. Where did she get that from?

My parents live in the microcosm of their academic research, their publications, their conferences. They have no idea who the celebrities of the moment are; they would be incapable of recognizing the idols of their century if they passed them in the street. Red carpets, celebrity couples, Hollywood, private jets, celebrity magazines: it is an exotic country they have no wish to vacation in.

At the school gates, my parents look older than the others. My mother explains that her pregnancy lasted not nine months but nine years. I will be their only child. Their difficulty conceiving is no secret; my Wikipedia page says I was born through IVF, and for some obscure reason that detail adds to the magic surrounding my birth: did science have to step in to create someone as exceptional as me?

I am a short-haired little girl, well-behaved, solitary, slipping plane-tree leaves into the pockets of my coat to make salad for my dolls. My parents hear me talking to myself in my room, notice that I always shut the door to play.

They have no idea their darling child spends Wednesday afternoons filling notebooks in search of a star's signature. At seven, I practice signing autograph after autograph as fast as I can. The following year, I take part in my first interviews. The questions come naturally; I work out my answers methodically, in French, in English. At my feet, my stuffed hedgehog, whale, ladybug, and squirrel listen attentively. My super menagerie forms a captive audience, the best I could wish for.

I sing along with my favorite stars too, putting their music on the CD player on the floor beside my desk. I know every lyric by heart; I read them in the booklet, sitting cross-legged on the pale blue carpet in my room. As a teenager, the site "paroles.net," with its thousands of cataloged songs, will have taken over. I will spend hours there, captivated.





So many times I have explained my trajectory to journalists. So many times I have recounted my past in interviews. So many times I have taken part in this dangerous game without understanding its gravity.

I fictionalized my life, embellished my answers to make myself seem more amazing, more impressive. From my story, and little by little from my memory, I cut the episodes that did not fit my legend. I fabricated false memories. In search of the piquant detail or the moving anecdote, I picked through other people's pasts, through my reading, through films, until I forgot what truly belonged to me.

Memories are fragile; all it takes to realize that is to recount the same event several times. By the third or fourth telling, the past begins to rearrange itself. Gently, you change the lighting, remove certain details, heighten the drama to hold your audience. You turn the past into a story, and with each retelling, it is the memory you damage. There are anecdotes I have repeated so many times that I no longer know whether they are true or false.

It is tempting to rewrite your life; I have done it often. The signs that foretold my destiny are legion: the early interviews with my stuffed animals, my extraordinary mezzo-soprano voice, my fascination with song lyrics, my ear for music, my ease at the piano, the encouragement of my guitar teacher, my solid grounding in music theory, my passion for choral singing. It is so much easier to trace the path backward.





I begin music lessons at seven, but make no mistake. Where I come from, the conservatory is the after-school activity of good students. My parents have no ambition to make me a celebrity by encouraging me to learn to play an instrument; we are not in Los Angeles. In my world, music is a bourgeois reflex, not an encouragement to become a global pop star.

My desire for fame comes not from my family but from my flaws. All my life, I will seek the admiration of millions of strangers, force respect from everyone who crosses my path. The baker, the pharmacist, my kindergarten teacher, the neighbor on the third floor. I have only one aim: for everyone to acknowledge my superiority.

People talk a lot about celebrities' mental illnesses. How many become alcoholics, suffer from addictions, sink into depression, commit suicide? Fame drives you mad, and you have to be mad to want to be famous. Public life is a pot of honey that attracts narcissists, perverts, sociopaths. Well-adjusted people have no need to vampirize love on such a scale.

I think back to that four-year-old girl confiding in her father how sad she was not to be famous. In that thirst to be admired, praised, applauded, there was a need that had always exceeded all normal bounds.

I leave the party without saying goodbye to anyone, it isn't even midnight, I haven't blown out my candles, they'll go on celebrating my coming into the world without me. Certainty. Certainty. Feeling. Joy. Peace. This night is my Night of Fire, the night of revelation — and what a dazzling flash. Intoxication multiplies my exaltation tenfold, the icy wind burns my face, I ride up Boulevard Saint-Michel on my bike, pedaling through the night, out of the saddle to climb the hill. I skirt the Luxembourg Gardens, straight on to Avenue de l'Observatoire, my heart throbbing with happiness, my birthday ought to be a public holiday. In my headphones, pure jubilation, the iconic World Cup victory song, “Samba de Janeiro”; I don't need to speak Portuguese to understand the euphoria, I let the percussion resound through me. I run one red light, a second, weave between the cars around Place Denfert-Rochereau, close my eyes, invincible, let go of the handlebars with one hand, the other, lift my arms, fly like a bird. I ignore the drivers honking, you don't kill off the main character at the beginning of the film. In the frozen night, I shout louder and louder the four prophetic letters that spell out my destiny: C-L-É-O.

When I get home, I spit onto the sidewalk the last traces of semen still on my gums. My phone goes off, Nathan asks where I disappeared to. I'm not surprised, he's already in love. I don't answer his message. What's the point? We can't be together; it'll be too complicated when I'm famous.





The apartment is silent, my parents are already in bed, and yet I'm home before curfew. I drink a glass of water in the kitchen, gather a few supplies. The night promises to be long, the longest of them all.

At fifteen, I have just received irrefutable proof of my future fame, it is time to find out how my life's mission will take concrete shape. I open a graph-paper notebook, my handwriting hurried, punctuated by arrows, underlined words, sentences in capital letters, numbered ideas. Half drunk, I conduct my first skills assessment.

1. My discipline, my perfectionism, my need to be the best, my refusal of laziness and banality: it all makes sense. I'll be a major celebrity, not some two-bit starlet. I don't aspire to be just a singer, but an illustrious figure. Like Cleopatra, I'm on this earth to leave my mark on my era. Tutankhamun, Homer, Plato, Aristotle, Alexander the Great, Caesar, Charlemagne, Shakespeare, Napoleon. The list is short on women. I volunteer.

2. I have a dazzling voice. Hard to ignore, I get compliments as soon as I open my mouth to sing. Besides, I'm not just another soprano with a smooth, high-perched tone like a nightingale's. My voice is low, husky, gravelly; it is broken, complex, distinctive – my vocal signature will be easy for the public to identify, recognizable among all. Everybody will adore me.

3. I'm passionate about poetry, excellent at literary subjects, capable of writing songs in French and in English, which will give me international reach. Pretty voices – you find them on every street corner: I'm worth more than that. I won't be a mere performer; I'll use my pen. My destiny is to become famous singing my own songs.

4. Puberty is rarely a gift. For me, it's a blessing. The features of my heart-shaped face are softening, my breasts are filling out, exercise is sculpting my body. I'm more willowy, more slender – the awkward, bundled-up little girl is disappearing, making way for a star's figure. There's still work to do: get a new haircut, find the right lipstick, buy new clothes. But with all that, we'll have enough for a beautiful poster.

My proof before my eyes, all that remains is not to procrastinate. My plan of action is to compose, right now, the song that will make me famous. I search for a melody under my breath so I don't wake my parents, asleep in the next room. When I tackle the chorus, I think of Céline Dion: her mother and brother wrote her first song when she was thirteen. Unfortunately, I can't turn to my family for help. They may not be potential lyricists, but if only they had at least been able to open the doors of show business for me. I would have dreamed of having a director for a mother, an actor for a father. How can they settle for such tiny lives? No one knows them, no one will remember their names – and you don't become a millionaire bent over your research. My parents are simple people, fogeys, losers, boors. On Saturday night, they aren't invited to premieres: they watch a documentary about abstract impressionism or Turkish ambitions in the Mediterranean, read on the living-room sofa, and go to bed at eleven. I love my parents, but they're no use to me. And yet I refuse to think like a victim. There are always more reasons to fail than to succeed. I plunge back into my song. If Thérèse and Jacques Dion managed it, I can do it too.

I spend the next few hours carving and polishing my song. Inspired, determined, illuminated, I embroider an impossible love story. Full of that I-know-what-I-want look, I prepare my coup d'état. I'm composing a masterpiece in my bedroom, and everyone will be stunned the first time they hear this miraculous anthem. "You wrote that all by yourself?"; "We had no idea we had a musical genius among us!"; "This song is incredible, I'm sending it to my aunt immediately, she works in television, I'm sure she can find you a producer!"

The first rays of sunlight signal the end of my Night of Fire. Footsteps in the hall, the sound of keys, the apartment door banging shut with a metallic clang. As he does every Sunday morning, my father goes down to the bakery to buy croissants. Meanwhile, my mother gets ready in the bathroom listening to the radio. She hums in the shower, and I have finished my song.

I said so often that I'd started writing songs at the age of eight that I ended up believing my own lies. The reality is more mundane: I composed my very first song at fifteen. And though I may have been struck by the fire of inspiration, what I produced is lousy.



When you've got an overall average of 19.75 out of 20 in the baccalauréat, when you're top of your class at Sciences Po, you dream of a prestigious career in the civil service, not of going on television and signing autographs. At twenty, I have everything going for me, I'm pretty as an angel, the future lies wide open before me; yet I feel stifled, embittered, I feel like I'm living a life that isn't mine and I resent the whole world. Everyone says I'm successful, but that isn't how it feels to me. I passed the semester with one certainty: I'm pouring far too much heart into this degree.

Obviously, I have no intention of interrupting my brilliant university studies to play opening sets in shitty bars. I deserve better than playing in dingy venues, going to auditions, becoming a backup singer. But my model-student cover takes up an absurd amount of time; it draws me in without inspiring me. Composed in the evening after a day spent slogging away, my songs have no poetry, no substance, no nuance. They are everything I refuse to be: mediocre.



So I stay in the shadows, preparing my grand entrance. I am not standing still, I am paralyzed. I wait for the right idea, I wait to write better songs, I wait until I can play the piano better, I wait until I can afford a new guitar, I wait to finish my degree, I wait until I find an internship, I wait for my voice teacher's advice, I wait to be thinner, I wait to buy myself a corduroy jacket, I wait for the twelfth of never, the Greek Calends, the thirtieth of February. For the moment, I don't need anyone to slow my progress toward glory. I do that perfectly well all by myself.

"Could you keep it down? We're in a library."

First warning. I cut in sharply before plunging back into my studying, and I unlock my computer with the password I've used for years: Cléo_célèbre!

Despite my warning, the group of four clowns sitting at my table is still just as rowdy. I come here to surround myself with focused faces, not losers playing on their phones instead of studying. Second warning. I throw them several pointed looks to make them shut up — if only my eyes could spit fire. Unfortunately, my fine friends keep talking.
