The goddess was there. In an octagonal room, deftly curving her ample form.

"Um, hello."

"Ah, it's you. Welcome."

Her voice was surprisingly husky. Somewhere between mezzo-soprano and alto, leaning toward alto — if she ever had occasion to sing in a choir.

When I moved to hand over the letter of introduction, the curator swiftly took it and spread it open before the goddess. I freeze. How had I meant to hand it to her? But she showed no sign of minding, and after a moment she spoke.

"Rika Horauchi, yes? Would you mind if I called you Hora?"

I nodded. The sudden pronouncement of a name threw me, but it wasn't as though I had a preference of my own. The goddess narrowed her eyes. Eyes without pupils.

"I would like you to call me Venus — not Aphrodite. The name has a Greek-mythology ring, but I was born in Rome, you see. Though 'Venus,' too, is the English reading."

I nod at this too. Venus narrows her eyes further. Her carved smile, brimming.

"Very well. Tell me, do I speak too quickly? Does it match the style you learned at university? If anything seems off, please don't hesitate to say so."

"No… it's very easy to follow."

Like a textbook, I add after a moment's hesitation, and rub my palms against my raincoat. The sweat was terrible.

"I'm glad. Well, a specimen of sorts, perhaps. After all, it was a time before Latin had split into Italian and French and all the rest — when no one could have imagined the Roman Empire dividing."



Venus laughed aloud. Her bare white shoulders, the smooth belly wrapped in its thin layer of fat — neither so much as stirred. Apparently the rule was that a marble body did not move below the neck.



The part-time job was by no means a bad deal. It was just a kind of work I hadn't heard much about.

The one who had put me forward was a professor whose class I had taken only once. For the first time in years I got off at that station, passed the rotary — the commotion like a wildfire — through the campus gates, and up the hill past girls in matching outfits like pajamas to the office in the north building, where a worn carpet the color of young grass caught my eye. First-years, maybe. As I stared at the carpet's nap, impassively soaking up the cheers that seeped in from the courtyard, a letter of introduction was held out to me. An envelope printed with the university name.

When I looked up, someone I recognized was standing there.

But though my hands accepted it, they remained suspended in midair.

"I'm sorry, I just don't have the confidence to…"

"In terms of conversation, there is no one better suited than you. At least, no living person."

The professor seemed to be addressing someone standing behind me.

"Think of it as something like tutoring. Simpler than that, even. All you have to do is talk."

"But they're practically a native speaker, and besides—"

And besides, the talking — But the rest of my words fell soundlessly away before the yellow vinyl. Like spray from a park fountain at closing time. While I drew breath and pursed my lips, the professor — had invisible water gotten in? — bounced in low little hops, the way you shake water from your ear, then turned to the mirror and began straightening his jacket. The conversation, it seemed, was over. I leave the university, resigned. The girls in pajamas were eating matching sandwiches on the bench by the campus gate.

The museum was some distance by bus. Walking from the stop, nothing caught my eye but magnolia petals — houses and shops alike buried in that bone-like whiteness — and it was only by consulting the map the professor had given me that I finally found the building. Its nearly windowless concrete walls and bronze doors were visibly aged — less a place where cultural treasures and records from all times and places had been assembled than a student dormitory everyone had left. I walked past the “Closed Today” sign, made my way to the back entrance, took out my phone, and called the number I’d been given. On the second ring the door opened, and I step inside as prompted. Like a members-only party.





But this place did not seem to welcome visitors. Least of all the living. Perhaps to preserve the exhibits, the interior was cold as a morgue, the smell of chemicals soaked into the floors, the walls, every surface. The chandelier enthroned on the soaring ceiling and the delicate milk-glass lamps lining the hall had softly, darkly lowered their eyes for the day. Past the glass cases of fossils and earthenware and such, I noticed a rack by the main entrance lined with flyers for children’s events and a neighborhood flea market — but would any of it really happen? That the dates and times on these drowsy flyers would someday arrive somewhere in the world, that children would gather for science experiments, that old sweaters and audio equipment would be sold along a shrine approach.

The only moving thing in that faded-calendar view was the curator, walking ahead of me.

“Watch your step.”

He turned back as if he had foreseen me tripping on my hem on the stairs. The curator, Hashibami, had dreadfully fine features. I couldn’t tell his age, but in his white shirt and slim trousers he had the air of a young man, with a beauty closer to something crafted — a vase, a piece of lacquerware — than to a person. I clutch the letter of introduction in my pocket. I was no good with handsome men. Especially handsome men who were kind, or who seemed to be.

But if some scholar were to insist that light exerts its own gravity, my left hand would be the first to volunteer as proof. When the door at the top of the stairs opened, I found myself drawn toward an arched window. You couldn't tell from outside, but the center of the building had been hollowed out to hold trees and a small pond, and the corridor that overlooked and encircled this modest courtyard was filled with a transparent brightness. Pointing to the bouquet reliefs on the columns, Hashibami explains:

"This building was donated to us — it was originally the home of a local man of means. The doors are closed today, but the second floor houses his collection."

From somewhere among the galleries, orchestral strains spill out. The smooth ascending leap of an oboe; a timpani roll like unearthing old memories with a wooden mallet. Past a door carved with seashell motifs, a tepid briny scent wafts out, and the sound of waves carries through. There were rooms, too, where a hem of ivory lace peeked from the gap beneath the door. A profile of the museum I'd never known was savoring its closed day.

And the room was at the very end. Hashibami selected a key from a heavy-looking bunch and slid it in; with a click, the door opened. Ignoring the yellow raincoat faintly reflected in the corridor's glass windows, I peer past the doorway.

"This way, please."

So this, apparently, was the workplace. One shift a week, no experience required, conversational Latin a must. Job description: conversation partner for an ancient Roman statue of Venus.



I looked over the gallery once more. Easing my pupils along the rims of my eyes, little by little, so Venus wouldn't notice.

It was a room out of myth. Under the ceaseless light pouring through a domed skylight, they stood on their pedestals like constellations afloat. Hera, glaring in my direction. Artemis with her hunting hound. Minerva in her armor. Eight goddesses of Greek and Roman myth. In what might have been Ancient Greek — or perhaps some other tongue — bloodless white lips, yielding to boredom, weave lace patterns. Beyond the shadows cast by those garrulous, overlapping patterns, at the far end of the octagon, there she was.

Oh, pardon me. Venus opens her mouth. In contrast to her shoulders, as if frozen solid, that jaw moved quite smoothly.

"Hashibami, bring Hora a chair. It won't do to have her standing."

The curator brings a chair from the corner of the gallery, one that looks meant for a guard. As he makes to return to the goddess's side, that alto voice cut in without missing a beat: "Thank you. You may go."

"But—" There was an earnest ring to his Latin.

"I'll be fine. I'll call if anything comes up," the goddess went on. "Do close the door. Without fail."

Hashibami glanced my way, then headed for the doorway, bowed once, and closed the door. When the hard footsteps had faded, she spoke. "Sit." I sit in the chair and raise my face, then immediately drop it. It was just the right height for my eyes to meet those of the slightly downcast goddess.


She was every inch the goddess of love and beauty. Atop her pedestal she curved her voluptuous body into a magnificent S and smiled with a face from which, beautiful as it was, everything that lends a human face its individuality had been carefully pared away. Her symmetrical face, unmarked by laugh lines or crow's feet, bore a spoonful of resignation carved into its happiness from the very start, making her all the more elegant, all the more mysterious. Her garments, whose folds flowed magnificently yet never stirred, seemed to lack any resolve to cover and protect the body as clothing should, concealing neither her full breasts nor her rounded hips, and that unsettled me. My own uncurved body and all. When I shifted, the goddess asked:

"Is the chair not right for you?"

"It's fine."

I answer as if by reflex and try to put on the most pleasant smile I can. But the answer was evidently not enough. Venus asked again, as if checking the pockets of a child caught stealing.

"Hora, is that chair comfortable?"

"Yes, it's fine."

"You know, did you attend some sort of school for ending conversations in the fewest words possible? Tell me—is that chair truly comfortable? For you."

For the first time I became conscious of the depth of the chair. Then the position of my spine, and how far my heels hovered. As if tracing an X-ray with my fingertip.

"The seat might be a little too high." Thinking the answer might still be too short, I add: "My legs are short."

"Thank you for telling me about yourself. I wouldn't know, you see—I have never sat in a chair. Now ring that bell over there and call Hashibami."


I approached the curtain as told and found a small golden bell, which I rang tentatively. Lips that have never known dryness or cracks ripple.

"I don't yet know about the length of your legs, but in any case, you needn't speak of yourself that way."

The goddess smiles. My body goes rigid. Like a sculpture.

When footsteps drew near and the door opened, Venus instructed the curator to bring a new chair. Thanking him for coming so quickly and specifying the size with precision, she was like an excellent supervisor. If she ever worked outside the museum.

Venus waited one beat after the sound of the door closing, then spoke again. "Now then."

"Thank you for coming today. You really are fluent in Latin. When Hashibami inquired with your professor, you were the very first to be recommended. Very talented, apparently."

"That's not… no." I exhale thinly, as if to escape those white eyes.

"I say it because it is true." The goddess spoke without hesitation. "I heard you have already graduated from university—are you in graduate school, perhaps?"

"Graduate school… no, right now, well… sort of a bit of work…"

I tried to catch the words. The ones I needed to string together. But the more desperately I chase them the more lightly they vanish, or fall pitifully, and I fall silent inside my raincoat. Faintly hoping to be fired from this job. Venus, though, murmured, “‘Task’—I like that,” and began to explain the position.



The duties and terms were no different from what the professor had described. The job was to talk with her. Shifts fell on Mondays, the museum’s closed day. If a Monday was a public holiday and the museum open, then the following day instead. Commuting expenses covered. A three-month trial period.

“If you need a day off, just let me know two days ahead and it’s no trouble at all. And of course, if it’s your health or the weather—even the same day.”

I nod. During university I had spent just six months tutoring, and that family had been relentlessly strict about punctuality. The day the snow stopped the trains, I scrambled from bus to bus in a panic to reach the boy’s house. Losing all feeling in my toes from the cold of my wet socks. But when I rang the doorbell at the appointed time, his mother told me right then and there that she was rescheduling—“It’s such lovely snow, I want to let him play”—and I stood for a while watching my student and the golden retriever, both in handsome matching Mont-Bell down jackets, race around a silver garden big enough for two tennis courts. Compared with that, this job seems far more favorable. At least the goddess seemed unlikely to change her plans on account of the weather. That those marble legs would suddenly step down from the pedestal.

But I was still searching for a reason to quit. I felt out of place. The goddess was beautiful and far too bare, while I was overdressed. Always.



I wipe the sweat beading on my forehead. At some point the yellow hood has slid down to my eyes.

Suddenly Venus gave a small yawn. “Listen, Hora.” Her alto comes out muffled.

“Do you sleep deeply?”

“Normal, I think,” I answer, looking down. Trying not to stare at the way her perfectly proportioned mouth goes crooked.

“How nice. My sleep is always light. If I had a healthy stomach, the very first thing I would take is a sleeping pill. The kind where never waking up would be perfectly fine.”

It was then that I first looked at her label. Aphrodite of Pariano — Roman copy of a Greek sculpture, 1st–2nd century. Two thousand years had turned to bottomless dust and the faint press of human breath, racing soundlessly past her—a staggering number of days and nights sweeping dizzily by. All of it adorned the naked goddess more elegantly than any ornament, and wrapped her, at the same time, in a solitude no one could approach.

Footsteps were drawing near from beyond the gallery door. “Listen,” Venus drops her voice. As though hiding a candle’s light from something large.

“No painting, no sculpture can match the beauty of a single night’s dream. There you can become someone else, go somewhere else, after all. I can escape from my own time that never ends.

"Won't you come too?" Those half-joking words stroked my bare skin without warning. Under the raincoat. Their meaning with nowhere to land.



Before I knew it, I had signed and sealed the employment contract Hashibami had brought along with the chair. Pay would be deposited by bank transfer on the tenth of the following month.



Oh — someone crossed.

I stop in my tracks. From the rust-covered stairs of the apartment I could see a bridge. A bridge almost no one crossed — like a sardine's fine bones. As I stood watching, the frosted-glass window behind me opened a crack. I breathe in through the gap in my hood. The warm scent of cooking, and lurking within it, a savory omen.

When I found this apartment, I applied to move in right away. Old, for one thing, and the two-story building standing as if lost in a district of small factories hardly got any sun, but the rent was cheap, and the ivy covering the white walls had a certain charm, depending on how you looked at it. Separate bath and toilet, a two-burner stove, a bidet seat — and for some reason, the place even came with a landline.

One thing I hadn't expected: it also came with a landlady. Attached to the building — or rather, to my daily life.

I ran down the stairs and opened the door. It wasn't locked. I stepped over the cardboard boxes piled in the entryway and pushed through the beaded curtain, and there, amid that year-round, tepid smell like stewing onions, was Seriko, peering into a pot. Under the incandescent bulb, an oven mitt — savorily browned — is about to become a small flame. Right beside her.
