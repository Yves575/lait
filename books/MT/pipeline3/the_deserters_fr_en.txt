I

He sets down his rifle and struggles to pull off his hobnailed boots, whose smell (excrement, mildewed sweat) only adds to his exhaustion. His fingers on the frayed laces are dry twigs, lightly singed here and there; his nails are the color of the boots, he will have to scrape them with the tip of his knife to get the filth out, mud, dried blood, but later, he has not the strength; two toes, flesh and dirt, poke out of the sock, big smeared worms crawling out of a dark trunk, knotted at the ankle.

Suddenly, as he does every morning, every evening, he wonders why these clodhoppers stink of shit, it is inexplicable,

you can rinse them in every puddle you come across, scrub them against the rasping tussocks, it does no good,

and yet there are not that many dogs or wild beasts, not that many, on these stony heights dusted with holm oaks, pines, and thornbushes where the rain leaves a fine pale mud and a smell of flint, not shit, and it would be easy for him to believe that the whole country reeks, from the sea and the hills of orange groves, then olive groves, to the deepest reaches of the mountains, these very mountains, maybe even himself, his own smell, not the shoes', but he cannot bring himself to it and flings the army boots against the edge of the ravine that hides him from the trail, a little higher up the slope.

He lies flat on his back on the gravel, sighs, the sky is violet, the sunset glow lights the fast-moving clouds from below, a canvas, a screen for fireworks. Spring is almost here and with it the often torrential rains that turn the mountains into bullet-riddled cans, disgorging from the slightest hollow a powerful spring, when the air smells of thyme and the blossoms on the fruit trees, white flakes strewn between the low stone walls by the violence of the downpour. It would take the devil himself for it to start raining now. At the same time it would wash the boots. The hobnailed boots, the fatigues, the socks, both pairs he owns just as stiff, cardboard-hard, tattered. Betrayal begins with the body,

how long since you washed?

For four days you have been walking near the ridgelines to avoid the villages,

the last water you splashed over yourself smelled of petrol and left your skin greasy,

you are a long way from purity, alone under the sky peering at comets.

Hunger forces him to sit up and swallow, without pleasure, three military biscuits, the last ones, brown, hard slabs, no doubt a mixture of sawdust and glue made from an old mare; for a moment he curses war and soldiers,

you are still one of them, you still carry weapons, ammunition, and memories of war,

you could hide the rifle and the cartridges in a corner and become a beggar, leave the knife too, beggars do not carry daggers,

the army boots that smell of shit and go barefoot,

the jacket the color of misery and go bare-chested,

when his meal is done he drinks what is left in his canteen and makes a game of pissing as far as he can toward the valley.

He lies down again, this time pressed right up against the rock face, the bottom of his pack under his head; he is invisible in the shadow, never mind the critters (red spiders, tiny scorpions, centipedes with teeth sharp as remorse) that will scamper over his chest, slide over his all-but-shaven skull, wander over his beard as rough as a bramble thicket. The rifle against him, the butt under his shoulder, the barrel toward his feet. Wrapped in the piece of tarred canvas that serves him as blanket and roof.

The mountain rustles; a little wind rounds the peaks, drops into the combe and trembles among the shrubs; the cries of the stars are chilling. There are no clouds left, it will not rain tonight.

Angel of God, my guardian dear, protector of my soul and body, forgive me all the sins committed this day and deliver me from the works of the enemy, for all the warmth of prayer the night remains a beast fed on anguish, a beast with blood on its breath, cities in ruins roamed by mothers brandishing the mutilated corpses of their children before disheveled hyenas who will torture them, then leave them naked, befouled, their nipples torn off with teeth before the eyes of their brothers, themselves violated in turn with batons, terror spread over the country, the plague, hatred and the night, that night that always wraps itself around you to drive you toward cowardice and betrayal. To flight and desertion. How much longer is there to walk? The border is a few days from here, beyond the mountains that will soon turn into hills of red earth planted with olive trees. It will be hard to hide. So many villages, towns, peasants, soldiers,





you know the region,

you are at home here,

no one will help a deserter,

you will reach the house in the mountains tomorrow,

the cabin, the hovel, you will take refuge there for a while,

the cabin will protect you with its childhood,

you will be caressed there by memories,

sometimes sleep comes by surprise like a sniper's bullet.





II





More than twenty years ago, on September 11, 2001, near Potsdam on the Havel, aboard that cruise ship, a small river liner grandly christened *Beethoven*, summer seemed to waver.

The willows were still green, the days still warm, but an icy mist rose off the river before dawn and immense clouds slid over us from the distant Baltic Sea.

Our floating hotel had left Köpenick east of Berlin very early on the morning of Monday the 10th. Maja was still alert, sprightly. She would go up to the upper deck to walk, a stroll between the showers, the deck chairs and the deck games. The green domes and golden spire of Berlin Cathedral captivated her from afar as we passed. She imagined, she said, all those little gilded angels leaving their stone prison to take flight in a cloud of acanthus leaves blown by the sun.

The water of the Spree was at times a dark, dull blue, at times a smoldering green. In the previous weeks all Germany had been shaken by storms whose spasms had swollen even the Havel and the Spree, though at this end of summer they were usually rather low.

We sailed amid the eddies.





I remember the confluence of the Spree, the wooded islets, the salt light dusting the tall black poplars and the muddy flow of the canal that the ship's wake mixed with the river's polished waters.

Maja and I were each in a canvas chair, in the sun on deck, at the back, at the stern as one is supposed to say, and we watched everything flee: the landscape widened as though the ship's bow were opening wide the green matter of the foliage.

We were celebrating, a few months late, the tenth anniversary of Paul's refounding of the Institute, while paying tribute to the founder himself. Or, more precisely, we were marking ten years since the Institute's "unification," in the spring of 1991, and forty years since its founding in 1961. But above all it was a celebration of Paul's work. I don't think anyone was missing - among the old guard, the ones from the East, everyone was there; the newer members, the colleagues from Berlin and elsewhere, had almost all shown up. A few, including Linden Pawley, Robert Kant, and several French researchers, had even come from abroad. This floating conference bore the title Paul Heudeber Days; there were to be two sessions a day, one in number theory, one in algebraic topology, and a session on the history of mathematics in which I was to take part.

The only absentee was Paul himself.

Maja had just celebrated her eighty-third birthday.

Maja drank liters of tea.

Maja was cheerful and sad and silent and talkative.

We all knew she had no business being there, on board the Beethoven for a mathematics conference; we all knew she was indispensable.

Most train travelers prefer to sit facing the direction of travel.

A historian is a traveler who chooses not to sit facing the direction of travel.

The historian of science is a historian who sits facing backward, turned toward what lies behind and, unlike most historians, does not look out the window.

The woman historian of mathematics is a woman historian of science who, seated facing backward, eyes closed, tries to prove that the Arabs invented trains.

No one laughed.

It has to be said that I was the only historian at the conference. Everyone else was a mathematician, a physicist or, worse still, a logician. All of them seated facing the direction of travel. Looking toward innovation, invention, discovery. I was the only one who cared less about the glorious proofs and inventions of tomorrow than about the honeyed meanders of the past. Meanders of the past that cast their light into the furthest depths of the future, and during that session of the Paul Heudeber Days on the Havel I could feel that this learned audience would listen to my paper on Nasir al-Din al-Tusi and irrational numbers with no more than the courtesy due the occasion, out of consideration for me and for my mother, who despite her great age would not miss a word of the papers between two walks on deck.

Maja had been behind the idea of holding the conference on the water; if memory serves, Jürgen Thiele, the secretary-general, had proposed "an afternoon excursion on the Spree or the Havel" to round off the Days, which were initially to be held at the Institute in Berlin; she had made a face, the Spree or the Havel, at best that was still Berlin, at worst Brandenburg, why not the Danube, and Jürgen Thiele had opened his eyes wide, the Danube, but that's a long way away, and I imagine Maja had started laughing, all right then, the Havel, but at least let the whole conference be on a boat, and Jürgen Thiele was very embarrassed (he explained it to me later), because he wanted to refuse my mother nothing for those days in Paul's honor but his budget was limited; this business of a conference on the water still seemed absurd to him, a whim of old age.

Yet a few weeks before the call for papers for the Days went out, Thiele was surprised to receive two letters on the same day: one informing him that the mathematics faculty at the University of Potsdam was offering to co-organize the Paul Heudeber Days with our Institute, the other that the Georg Cantor Foundation was awarding, though Thiele had asked for nothing, an enormous grant for the conference, making it possible to hold it on the water, though the idea remained just as absurd in his eyes, a thought he kept to himself.

Paul's tragic death a few years earlier had stirred intense emotion in the scientific community; everyone was ready to take part, and even if most of the organizers, Jürgen Thiele first among them, I thought, had no idea what lay behind Maja's wish, no one wanted to disappoint her. The two letters arrived at exactly the right moment, and Jürgen could only suspect, no doubt quite rightly, that Maja had taken up her pen or picked up the phone: though she had in theory withdrawn from politics after the federal elections of 1998, she still had the power to draw "benevolent attention" to projects still in the cradle. The money from the Georg Cantor Foundation was welcome; Jürgen Thiele got in touch, as co-organizer, with the University of Potsdam, which was celebrating its tenth anniversary and which Paul had helped found: many of the mathematics faculty had been his students.

The Paul Heudeber Days, then, would be held on the Havel, aboard a cruise ship with a conference room large enough for the fifty-odd delegates, most participants from outside Berlin being lodged in a hotel facing Peacock Island and therefore technically in Wannsee - a hotel with the name of a medieval or Alpine inn, La Chouette Blanche, an inn which Maja assured me (I wondered where she could possibly have got such certainty) had existed since at least the sixteenth century, though its current building - Doric columns supporting a monumental balcony, windows with green shutters, climbing roses, as in a fairy tale, softening the facade with their countless blooms of a very dark red, shading toward black - had been rebuilt by Karl Schinkel in the first third of the nineteenth century. La Chouette Blanche lay lost in the middle of the forest, on the shore of the immense lake through which the Havel ran. Only the keynote speakers and other VIPs of the conference were lodged on the Beethoven, because there were few cabins; the daytime 'sailings', by contrast, were open to everyone: Potsdam to the Elbe on Wednesday, the day of tribute proper, devoted to Paul's work, then Peacock Island to Köpenick by way of Spandau on Thursday to bring the festivities to a close. Only a few distinguished guests had arrived on Sunday to take advantage of the boat's 'repositioning' from Köpenick to Wannsee, and thus of an extra day of cruising through Berlin on Monday.

Jürgen Thiele was all empathy, disorder, and goodwill. Though Jürgen Thiele was still secretary general of the Institute, he now carried out that task only out of loyalty to Paul, whose student he had been thirty years before; he readily admitted that he was tired of organizing, setting things up, putting them in order - even putting together a Christmas lunch sends me into a panic, he would confess. So a conference with fifty people - imagine! The University of Potsdam had assigned him a co-organizer, a young doctoral student in number theory named Alma Sejdić who was trying, in her dissertation, to prove a corollary to Paul's first conjecture. The addition turned out to be as disastrous as it was hilarious: instead of adding up, the two forces seemed either to work together to no effect or else to cancel each other out. Oversights were overlooked twice, blunders repeated twice over. It was like a drawing traced by two ballpoint pens fastened together with a rubber band, parallels never meeting, for all their efforts, constrained by Euclid himself.

Jürgen Thiele had had to deploy all his diplomacy so as not to offend the University of Potsdam, which could not understand why, only a few kilometers from campus, it was supposed to finance the rental of a luxurious cruise ship - but Jürgen Thiele had pulled the Georg Cantor Foundation grant from his sleeve, and everyone had found the idea of a floating conference exhilarating.

And so, after a few months of this chaotic ballet, Maja and I boarded, as planned, on Monday, September 10, at Köpenick, in the company of Linden Pawley, whose flight from New York had landed at Tegel that very morning, the inevitable Robert Kant of Cambridge, and Jürgen Thiele - there were indeed five luxurious cabins prepared for us.

III

Every morning since he left, the cold wakes him shortly before dawn. He shivers. No sudden movement, so the dew, black beads on the canvas, does not run off. Patiently, folding his tent into a trough, he manages to collect a few centiliters in his canteen and drink that icy sweat of dawn, which will be his only meal that morning.

He sets out, once his stubborn feet are wrapped in that knitted sponge of a miserable green, still damp, in the direction of destiny, northward, for rout and oblivion must be named for what they are. He hesitates once more over abandoning the rifle, it weighs on him and its strap is awkward, too short since he cut it to make himself a belt, with that knife still so sharp, itself another mark of a dangerous solitude, drunk on blood, he no longer thinks, he is already walking while the first rays of the sun probe the shadows among the rocks. Those goads of light stir the sparrows, the warblers, the titmice, their wingbeats following in the wake of the morning song.

If he thinks so much about birds, if their presence and their song keep him so taut, it is because they sharpen his hunger, it would be so easy to settle in and lie in wait, nose to the wind, with the rifle, to wait for one of those little birds to give itself away, to bring it down and then eat it, but the power of a weapon of war would leave nothing but feathers, the shot would ring far through the heights and even if a fat pheasant or a partridge were to stray into his sights, he would have to cook it, and he has no intention of interrupting his walk for long, of giving himself away by fire or smoke.

He has resolved to reach the house.

You would find it even on a moonless night,

the cabin,

the path runs on through the day between holm oaks, scattered by the drought; a few mastic trees huddle between the rocks, releasing as the walker passes their apothecary scent, the scent of a forgotten pharmacy; he searches out the fresh wild savory that spring multiplies across the mountain so he can chew on a handful of it for a long time, bitter, tart, peppery, arbutus berries still survive the winter like forgotten Christmas ornaments, red and rough, they taste of strawberries past their best, of the flatness of oblivion.

These fruits are tiny stars, planets within reach,

little moons reddened by desire and the Evil One,

He walks like the last man, in the obsessive rustling of the mountain.

He envies the black specks of airplanes or distant birds of prey.

Numbing himself with memory, ass on a stone, one of those blue-gray outcrops that warm fast in the sun and smell of metal and flint, as smooth as they are hard: was there a first shiver, a hoarse wind, some first hint of that brutal logic, a bellow before the sovereign rut of war, he thinks not,

it was surprise that made you sit down there,

soon the black snakes will come out of their holes and the males will go looking for females,

His hands are rough; his pale palm is starred with darker calluses, stiffened by gripping wooden handles too hard. His tobacco-stained fingers end in yellowed nails furrowed with dark grime, the tracery of veins shows at the thumb and along the wrist; his cheeks are harsh with a bristling beard, his hair greasy and pasted together in clumps, matted into duller locks by dried blood,

you will reach the house before nightfall,

The house, the cabin, the hovel: it lies buried deep in his memories and his hopes. A cairn from the land of childhood. High enough up the mountain that no one ventures there. Hidden enough from the mountain world that he can shut himself away there. For a time. The roof may partly have caved in, the round cypress beams, still gleaming, will stand alone, bare of tiles, between the uneven stones. The door so low. The front porch, its wooden braces recalling Father's arms, its two rough-cut stone posts the columns of a brutal God's temple. The unplastered rubble-stone facade. The roof of old yellow clay tiles,

you will be able to carve faces into the beams with your knife, as once you did,

you are so hungry it's frightening,

you are hungry down to the roots of your hair,

to picture the little hearth beneath the porch of the cabin, with a fowl crackling there on the embers, wrings you with furious pain,

you are thirsty,

He passes his thoughts on to his boots, which scatter them among the stones. Then inner silence, until the return of the great fixed star of hunger.

The treachery of illusion, the scent of spring coming on.

you are as feverish as this place in your memory,

He finds the strength to get moving again, knees aching, thighs hard as stone, feet skinned raw; the farther the war recedes, the more his body breaks down, an old mechanism that habit used to keep running. He is almost incapable of climbing the few kilometers that still separate him from the cabin, the house, the violet mists and the hollows of cloud. It is his rifle that carries him and guides him, the oversized needle of a magic compass, the rod of a death dowser,

you walk badly, you stagger, you make too much noise,

you are too near the villages, you must not draw the attention of a shepherd passing that way,
