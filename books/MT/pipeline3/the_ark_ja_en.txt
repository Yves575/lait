1 The Ark



I



The seven of us left the walking trail near the highway and pushed into a copse of mixed woods. We followed the mountain path, stepping over rotten branches and dead leaves, then passed through a field thick with dry grass somewhere I didn't know.

By the time we reached an old-looking wooden bridge spanning a ravine about ten meters deep, the sun had already disappeared behind the mountain ridge.

Ryūhei grabbed the railing, made of lashed logs, with his thick arms and shook it.

Hearing the bridge creak, he twisted his deeply carved, wrestler-like face and said to Yūya beside him,

"Hey, we're seriously crossing this? You never said anything about that. This thing isn't gonna collapse, is it?"

"Nah, it'll be fine. I crossed it before, and it wasn't that dangerous. We can make it. See?"

Yūya stepped out onto the bridge and spread both arms, rocking his body to show us.

There was no other way, anyway. The six of us had no choice but to follow Yūya on.

Once we were across, I pulled my phone from the pocket of my windbreaker and checked the time: 4:48 p.m.

When Hana saw me holding my phone, she quickened her pace and came up beside me in her flashy fluorescent hiking clothes. Holding up her own phone in her right hand, she asked,

"Shūichi, you getting any signal?"

"No. I've been out of range for about an hour now."

"Oh. Same here. So we're not making it back to the vacation house today, right?"

No one answered. We all knew that wasn't possible.



Beyond the bridge was a rough field hemmed in by steep mountains. A few hundred steps farther on, Yūya shouted.

"There it is! There it is! Just a little more. It's right there."

Relief seeped into Yūya's voice; all the while he had been bearing the dissatisfied, distrustful stares at his back. But there was still nothing in sight that looked like the entrance to a building.



It was that morning. After we'd all gone out on the lake and had fun riding boats, Yūya said this:

"So there's this really interesting place you can walk to from here. You guys feel like checking it out? Deep in the mountains there's this insanely huge underground structure. Sounds like they used it for some pretty sketchy stuff a long time ago, but I don't think anybody even knows it's there anymore."

Since the day before, we'd been gathered at a vacation house in Nagano Prefecture owned by Yūya's father.

Yūya was a friend from college, and he was the one who'd suggested we all get together. It was a small reunion for the six of us who used to hang out a lot in our student days.

It had been two years since I'd last seen everyone. I still wasn't used to seeing Yūya with the dyed blond hair gone and only the black piercings left.

I had brought my cousin along, so there were seven of us staying at the vacation house.

An underground structure deep in the mountains. Even hearing it described that way, none of us could quite picture it.

Something as troublesome as an underground structure, and apparently an enormous one at that. Who had built such a thing, deep in the mountains, and for what? Hard to believe, but Yūya said he had been there about six months earlier.

It piqued our interest, and if it wasn't far, we decided we might as well go have a look.

But contrary to what we'd been told, we walked and walked and still didn't come to the underground structure. Yūya, who had made it sound like a casual twenty- or thirty-minute walk, kept staring uneasily at the map on his phone the whole time.

Naturally, the underground structure wasn't on the map. Yūya said he had marked the place in his map app when he visited before, but apparently the location was quite a ways off. By the time we finally figured out where it was after wandering around, the sun was already starting to go down.



"So, Yūya? This underground structure? You mean to stay there, right? There's no time to head back now. Is that okay? Didn't you say it was a pretty dangerous place or something?"

"No, I'm saying there's talk it might've been used for something dangerous a long time ago. A long time ago. It's fine if we just use it a little. There's no way anybody's there. It's basically like exploring ruins."

Ryūhei and Yūya went on ahead, leaving the rest of us about ten meters behind.

It had been like that the whole way here. In a tone that seemed to speak for everyone, Ryūhei kept complaining to Yūya, our guide.



Seeing how Ryūhei was acting, Mai, walking on my right, turned a troubled smile on me, or perhaps one meant to smooth things over. In the early darkness, the long-lashed eyes in her pale face stood out all the more clearly.

I tried to answer, but if I spoke, Ryūhei would surely hear, and I couldn't bring myself to say anything. Mai didn't seem to be asking for a reply either, and as if wanting to look away before Ryūhei noticed, she turned aside.

When I looked back, Sayaka, who had fallen a little behind, came hurrying up to us. Her warm-brown hair was tied up in a bun, and there was sweat on her forehead.

Sayaka spoke up about something she had apparently been worrying over all along.

"Um, what about toilets in this underground structure? And for sleeping, are we going to be using our backpacks as pillows and lying down on the floor? Is everybody okay with that?"

Yūya hadn't told us anything like that. We hadn't come here intending to spend the night.

My cousin Shōtarō, walking a step ahead of me, answered Sayaka.

"You shouldn't expect too much. It's probably a building with quite a history. But surely it's better than sleeping out in the open. Being underground, it probably won't get all that cold."

"I see. Right, that's true. It gets pretty cold at night, doesn't it?"

Sayaka politely showed her agreement with Shōtarō.

I had only introduced my university friends to my cousin for the first time yesterday. Shōtarō had fit in with them even more than I expected.

Five years ago, Shōtarō inherited a substantial estate from an aunt, and ever since then he has drifted through life without holding a regular job, traveling here and there, getting absorbed in geological research for a while, and so on. You might think he was content to live comfortably off the inheritance, but that didn’t quite seem to be the case either; sometimes he would take something like a million yen abroad and come back having multiplied it several times over.

He was my cousin, so I had known him longer than any of my friends. Even though he was the one I let my guard down with more than anyone, he was still the person I couldn’t grasp in full.

The reason I had brought Shōtarō along was that I had sensed a hint of trouble around this get-together. He said he had been interested in the geography of this area to begin with, which made him easy enough to bring.

So far, none of the trouble I had worried about had materialized. Instead, we had unexpectedly wound up heading for a mysterious underground structure. It was reassuring to have him with us, someone who looked like he could sort things out if anything happened.

2

In the middle of a wasteland ringed by steep mountains, Yūya suddenly stopped. Then he pointed at the ground and shouted.

"There! See? This is it. The entrance."

Crouching down, Yūya thrust a hand into the dead grass and lifted up a hatch, about eighty centimeters across, like a manhole cover.

When I peered inside, the shaft dropped straight down into the earth. Its sides were concrete, with iron bars set into the wall to form a ladder.

"So we go in through here, but—"

"What? No way. Scary. It’s really narrow."

Hana shone her phone’s light down into the hole. It wasn’t bright enough to reach the bottom.

I felt the same way. Of course, out in mountains this deep, it was only natural that the place should be something mine-like, but from the way Yūya had talked about it, I had been picturing something a little more civilized.

"I mean, yeah, sure, the entrance looks like this, but once you’re inside, it’s fine. It’s seriously huge down there. Goes all the way to the third basement level. We can manage one night, easy."

Hana and the others were plainly hanging back, and I wasn’t exactly eager myself.

Ryūhei was the first to move.

"Whatever. I’ll try it. Can I get through like this?"

Worried about his backpack scraping, Ryūhei climbed down the ladder into the earth. After glancing at the three girls to gauge their reactions, Yūya followed after him, not wanting to let Ryūhei get too far ahead on his own.

After whispering, "What do we do?" and "Who goes first?" among themselves, Hana, Sayaka, and Mai climbed down in turn. Shōtarō and I brought up the rear.

After descending seven or eight meters down the ladder, my feet touched ground.

Beyond that, the passage stretched on as a horizontal tunnel, cave-like in shape. It was fairly wide; you could walk through without even stooping.

It sloped gently downward. We went on with our phone lights held out before us.

After a short while, we came to a gigantic boulder lying in the middle of the passage.

It was far too large for anyone to move by hand. For some reason, it had been wound round and round with thick chains.

"What’s this? Did they try to dig it out and then give up?"

"Who knows."

Shōtarō said meaningfully.

When we slipped past the giant boulder, an iron door came into view.

Just before the door, the natural rock underfoot gave way to a dirty plank floor. Beyond this point, the place was plainly a man-made structure.

Yūya opened the door and shone his light inside.

"Whoa? You’re right. This is kind of amazing."

Ryūhei let out a sound that might have been admiration, or might have been flinching.

Beyond the door stretched a wide corridor. The ceiling was low. A little farther on the hallway curved, so we couldn’t see what lay ahead, but from the way the sound of the iron door opening echoed, I could tell this underground structure was fairly vast.

"Wow. -- It smells moldy."

Mai murmured.

A sour smell hung in the air. The dampness felt like the depths of a forest untouched by sunlight, with a faint chemical odor mixed in.

"What’s with the lighting in here? The power’s not on, right?"

"It’s not, but there was this huge generator. Kinda looked like it might still run. If it doesn’t, we might just have to make do with our phone lights, but. I’ve got a mobile battery on me, anyway."

Ryūhei and Yūya ducked through the iron door and stepped out into the pitch-black corridor.

Each of us had a light in hand, and we followed after them uneasily, like a line of firefly larvae.

The floor was covered in old, cheap-looking vinyl flooring. There were doors all along the walls to either side, almost like a hotel.




Before the corridor bent left, Yūya pointed to the door of the room on the right. A plate on it read 107.

"The generator’s in here. It doesn’t look broken or anything, but..."

Yūya twisted the knob and shone his light into the room.

It appeared to be some sort of mechanical room, with black cables crawling over the walls. The cables converged at the back of the room, where they connected to a generator.

It was a self-contained generator about the size of a bathtub, like the ones I’d seen at the hospital where I used to work part-time. An exhaust pipe ran from the wall up into the ceiling. The machine itself looked as though it had been built before I was born, but the several LP gas cylinders set up beside it looked new.

The gauges on the cylinders showed there was still gas left. Yūya and Ryūhei ran their hands over the generator, trying to figure out how to start it.

Seeing that neither of them knew how to operate it, Shōtarō spoke up, a little tentatively.

"First, check that the cylinder and hose are properly connected. Then switch on the engine. I think it’s designed so you start it by pulling the starter grip."

When Yūya did as he was told, an engine noise like a motorcycle’s rang out.

In the next instant, the fluorescent lights overhead flickered. Then bluish-white light flooded the underground structure, from the mechanical room all the way out into the corridor.

"What a relief. It really would've been hard without any lights, wouldn't it?"

Sayaka said, looking around at everyone's faces.

With a general air of relief, we left the cramped mechanical room.

This underground structure held countless mysteries. Part of me wanted to explore the interior now that it was lit up, but for now fatigue outweighed curiosity.

Leading everyone with him, Yūya went a little way back down the corridor toward the entrance, then opened door 106, on the side opposite the mechanical room.

"So this is, like, the dining hall, but d'you wanna take a little break?"

It was a large room, dozens of tatami mats in size. Long tables stood in the lengthwise room, with chairs packed tightly along them. It was a dining hall that could hold several dozen people.

Hana pulled out a nearby chair.

"Ugh, gross. This okay?"

It was the kind of cheap chair you'd find at a school. Worse, after being left underground so long, the backrest had sprouted black mold and was starting to rot.

After dusting off the seat, Hana gingerly sat down. It didn't break.

Looking closely, the long tabletops too were made of the same cheap veneered plywood. They were damaged as well, and looked dangerous to stand on.

There was a sink at the back of the dining hall. When I turned the faucet, it gurgled, then reddish-black water burst out. After I let it run for a while, the water finally turned clear.

"Huh. The water works."

I murmured without thinking.

There was a dish cabinet over the sink. Inside were large numbers of old-looking, thick plates and cups.

Sayaka was kneeling by the wall, fiddling with something.

"Ah, wow. The outlets work too. Look."

She had plugged her phone charger into an outlet with exposed wiring.

For a while we stayed in the dining hall, stretching and yawning.

There was hardly any conversation. It was like when you finally reached a mountain hut on an overnight ridge-traverse climb, and all you did was let the fatigue drain out of you. It was the moment that made me nostalgic for my student days more than any other since we'd all gathered yesterday.

But this rest area harbored too unsettling an air to laze around and stretch out in. After a while, Shōtarō tapped me on the shoulder.

"Shūichi, shall we have a look around inside the building? This is quite an interesting structure."

I had just started thinking maybe I should eat something, but this underground structure interested me too.

Then Yūya, seeming kind of awkward, cut in.

"Ah, Shō-san, if that's what we're doing, I can kinda show you around. Last time I came, I looked around at a whole bunch of stuff."

He ended up coming along too, and the three of us left the dining hall. Together, we began exploring the underground structure.

Taken together, with its low ceiling, the dim light of the fluorescent lamps, the dirty floor, the walls made of cheap, drab building materials, and the wiring stretched all over the place, the building had the feel of an old cargo ship.

It wasn’t just the atmosphere. In size and structure too, it was close to a cargo ship. The building had been made by welding steel plates onto a framework of steel beams running lengthwise and crosswise. It was long and narrow and built on three levels, with rooms that looked like storerooms and others furnished with simple bunk beds made of iron pipe lined up on either side of the corridor.

Each door had a room-number plate stuck to it, like in an apartment building. Going back to the entrance and standing with the iron door at your back, the room on the right was 101. The one on the left was 102. The farther you went down the corridor, the more the numbers advanced: 103, 104, and so on. Storerooms and living quarters alike, every room was numbered regardless of its purpose. Every door had an awkward gap where it met the wall. The whole place had a rough, no-nonsense build.

Room 104, next to the dining hall, was the toilet. It was the same sort you’d find in a public facility, with four stalls. I had no desire to use it, but it did at least have a shower booth attached.

The odor had built up inside, but not enough to be truly unpleasant. Apparently it hadn’t been used in some time, and the waste had had time to decompose.

“There’s no sewer here, right? How did they empty it?”

“They probably let it collect in a holding tank first, then pumped it up to the surface. The wastewater was likely handled the same way.”

Shōtarō answered my question while peering down into one of the squat toilets.

That was enough of the toilet. We went back out into the corridor.

Once we passed the mechanical room, 107, the corridor bent left. At the corner stood an iron staircase leading down to the second basement level.

We ignored the stairs for the moment and kept going. After another five meters or so, the corridor quickly turned right. Doors lined both sides there as well. Starting with 108, the continuation from the mechanical room, the room at the very end was 120.

On the wall facing the outside, there were places where blackish rock showed through. It had the same feel as the cave we’d come down through from the entrance. Water seemed to be seeping out here and there, leaving it damp.

Apparently this underground structure had been made by shaping a natural cavity in the earth, cutting it into levels, and setting partition walls in place until it became a building. The corridor bent at unnatural angles because that was apparently the result of respecting the original terrain.

When we reached the dead end, Shōtarō said, with the air of someone who had just finished taking in a museum exhibit:

“Twenty rooms, is it. It must have cost a fair amount too. Still, they really built the thing. It is an ultra-illegal structure, though.”

“And that’s not all, either. There’s still a second basement level.”

Yūya took the lead and headed back toward the stairs.

The second basement level was laid out more or less the same as the floor above. Doors ran on and on along both sides of a lightning-bolt-shaped corridor. There was no large room like the dining hall down here, but below the first basement toilet was the holding tank, occupying one full room. Yūya explained that this level also had twenty rooms, numbered from 201 to 220.

Fluorescent lights were on in the corridor on the second basement level too. But the stretch to the left at the bottom of the stairs, on the side with the lower-numbered rooms, was dark. The light fixtures were installed, so maybe a wire had been cut somewhere. Since there was a light on at the very end, that part might have been on a different circuit.

When we came down the stairs, before we started searching the rooms, Shōtarō asked,

"Yūya-kun. How was it you found a place like this again?"

"Oh, uh, I just kinda found it about half a year ago. I was gonna do some solo camping then, and I came way out into the mountains 'cause I figured if I wanted to go somewhere absolutely nobody'd come, it'd be out there, and then I found that hatch. So I went in, and man, it was wild."

Standing in the middle of the corridor, Yūya spread both arms.

"What the hell is this place, anyway? Who built something like this, and what for? I mean, seriously, doesn't it feel like it was used for some seriously sketchy shit?"

After thinking for a moment, Shōtarō answered.

"Probably a radical hideout from fifty years ago."

"No shit. Radicals? Like, seventies stuff?"

"At a glance, I'd say it was built around then. There was a pretty big rock partway along the entrance, right? The one with the chain wrapped around it. That was obviously set there on purpose, to use as a barricade if anything happened. They'd use that rock to block the iron door.

But after those radicals, it looks like some other criminal group used the place too. Some of the wiring seems to have been put in fairly recently, no more than twenty years ago. Radicals wouldn't have still been hanging on in a place like this that late."

I had dimly suspected as much myself.

Why build a structure this deep in the mountains, and underground at that, if not to keep it out of sight? But once someone says it outright, the eeriness only grows.

Shōtarō said brightly,

"Well, let's have a closer look and see what sort of place it is."

Right in front of us, we tried Room 208. It looked like a room where junk had been dumped.

We rummaged through the contents. Used work gloves, a rusty grass-cutting sickle, an old speaker, copper pipe, scraps of lumber, and all sorts of other junk kept turning up. Some of it looked quite old, some of it not. It was all the kind of stuff you'd see piled up at some street-corner trash pile.

"Oh? So even a criminal hideout has a straw hat."

Keeping a straight face, Shōtarō showed me a battered broad-brimmed straw hat.

"Yeah. I dunno, I kind of figured maybe we'd find pistols or white powder or something, but I guess it's not that kind of place."

"They would've taken that stuff with them when they cleared out. If the people using this place were into that sort of thing, anyway. Well, if we search every last inch, we might still turn up something."

Shōtarō carelessly tossed the straw hat onto a battered wooden box.

Then he opened the door to Room 209, diagonally across from us.

At a glance, this room too was another place where junk had been dumped. There was less of it than in the last one, but what looked like scrap had been swept into piles in the corners.

But when we turned on the lights, what was there was nothing like the ordinary, harmless things in Room 208. When I heard a criminal organization had used this place, I pictured weapons, drugs, things like that. What we actually found was something even more grim.



The first thing I noticed was a set of restraints with a long chain and manacles and shackles attached. At the very back was a jet-black iron chair, and its seat was strangely pointed.

There was also a club made from thick wood wrapped in leather, and a device I couldn't figure out how to use, with a vise-like fitting attached to a metal frame big enough for a person's head. There were rusty nails too, and concrete blocks.

Yūya, Shōtarō, and I looked at one another with the awkwardness of people who had just glimpsed someone else's secret.

Yūya walked over to the corner, crouched down, and let out a groan without touching the implements.

"No way. This is bad. These are torture devices, right?"

"That's certainly what they look like."

Shōtarō answered. It seemed Yūya hadn't seen this room the last time he was here.

"Do you think they were really used?"

"I dunno, but doesn't it look that way? I've only seen stuff like this in museums. It's like, do things like this actually exist?"

The torture instruments were old and rusted. There were no traces of dried blood left on them, but they were too badly worn to be nothing more than grotesque decorations. I looked around at the floor. Split-like gouges were left in the vinyl flooring. They looked as though someone had writhed there in agony, clawing at the floor.

I'd heard stories about radical groups in the seventies, cases where internal discord had escalated into people killing each other. If the history of this underground structure was what I imagined, then finding torture instruments here was not all that strange.



A bloody eeriness suddenly began to drift out of the sterile underground structure.

"But there's no proof these were actually used, right?"

"No. And even if they were, it was a long time ago by now. They're historical artifacts."

That seemed to reassure Yūya a little.

I decided to take Shōtarō's words as something to lean on too, and not think too much about what had been done here in the past. Needless to say, torture instruments had never had anything to do with my life up to now, and whatever happened, they absolutely never would from here on.



After that, we peeked into a few more nearby rooms, but nothing more unsettling than the torture instruments turned up.

"So, Yūya-kun, you said this place goes down to the third basement level, didn't you? Where do you go down to B3?"

Shōtarō asked. There was no staircase in the corridor leading to the third basement level.

"Oh, uh, the way down is at the very end, but, well, various things happened, so you can't go down there now. Still, let's at least go take a look. You'll understand as soon as you see it."

Yūya went on ahead and led the way.





We went down the corridor toward the lower-numbered rooms. The corridor lights were off along the way. It wasn't so dark that we couldn't walk, but we turned on our phone lights just in case.

At the end, we saw an iron door. It was like the one at the entrance to the first basement level, but this one was smaller and narrower.

Yūya pointed at it and said,

"See? This is probably right under the entrance we came in through."

Tracing the path we had taken to get here, he was probably right.





Yūya slowly opened the iron door.

The space beyond was unlike the other rooms. Right after you entered, it narrowed like the mouth of a bottle, and farther in, bare black rock showed on every side. Only the part of the ceiling near the entrance was boarded over, and conspicuously low. Everywhere else it too was bare rock. This room alone had the look of a natural cave.

And against the wall at the back of the room was a device like a winch, the sort that made you think it might have been used on a sunken ship.

"What is this? It's really rusted."

A thick chain was wound around the winch. Tracing the end of the chain, I saw that it branched, passed through pulleys, and went through the boarded ceiling near the door to the first basement level.

"Ah! Could this be the chain that was wrapped around that huge rock?"

"That's right. I told you that rock was a barricade."

If you turned this winch, the huge rock would be pulled over to block the iron door on the first basement level.

"Right, now that you mention it, this is obviously a barricade. I really wasn't thinking that hard about it. So that's why the ceiling here is boarded over only near the entrance. To pass the chain through."

"Well, yes, that's right. Though there may be another reason as well."

Shōtarō said meaningfully.

"Right, and the stairs to the third basement are in this room. But you can't go down there now. Look."

Yūya pointed to the back right corner of the room.

There was a square opening cut into the floor. The stairs were there.

I went closer and looked down, and I understood at once what Yūya had meant.





The third basement was flooded. The water came up to the fourth step down, almost level with the ceiling of the third basement. Crouching down and stretching my arm as far as I could, I touched the black, glossy surface with my fingertips.

"Cold. Seriously? It's flooded?"

"It's underground, after all. And it was amateur construction, so a little flooding isn't surprising. Surrounded by natural rock like this, it was inevitable, really. The drainage system's probably broken too. This may be why the underground structure was abandoned."

Sure enough, there had been water running down the outer wall of the first basement level too.

"Seriously, this isn't the time to be talking about a mansion with a pool. More than that, this is a little scary. I mean, doesn't this mean the whole building is definitely going to end up underwater someday? If this keeps up."

Yūya answered.

"Well, in theory, yeah, but it's gotta take a long time, right? It doesn't feel like the water level's changed that much since I came here half a year ago. Maybe it's gone up a little, but still. If it does happen, wouldn't that be, like, five years from now?"

That too made sense.

When I shone my phone's light into the water, the third basement level seemed to have rebar and steel framing encased in concrete lying around in a jumble.

There was nothing else to see, so we stepped back out into the corridor and retraced our path.

When we got close to the stairs, I saw Sayaka from behind on the far side of the corridor, holding up her phone camera. She seemed to be taking pictures out of curiosity.

"Yūya-kun, is that hole we all came through the only way in and out of this underground structure? It doesn't seem likely there's just one."

"No, there's one more, technically, but you can't use it. It's down there on the third basement level. There's this narrow thing like a chute that goes all the way up to the surface, but it's flooded, so you can't get through it at all."

"I see."

"Oh, right, there's something like a floor plan in the mechanical room. That'll be quicker."

The three of us went back to the mechanical room we'd been in earlier.

Yūya opened the desk drawer. It was crammed haphazardly with old adhesive bandages, nail clippers, and stationery like pencils and ballpoint pens.

He scooped them out onto the desktop. Mixed in among them, he eventually found a large A2 sheet folded into quarters: a blueprint.

"Ah, this is it, this is it. Man, I really shoved it way in the back when I looked at it before."

It was not exactly a very helpful floor plan, but it was a drawing that showed the structure of the building as a whole. It appeared to date from the time the place was built, and the paper had badly yellowed.

At the top, as if added later, in ballpoint pen, was the word The Ark. Apparently that was the name of this underground structure.

Just as we'd seen for ourselves, the Ark had three levels and a long, narrow shape, bent in the middle like an inverted Z. According to the plan, unlike the first and second basement levels, the third seemed to have only a few large rooms rather than being divided up into smaller ones. The entrance we'd come in through was on the west side, and on the east side of the third basement level there seemed to be another exit leading up to the surface.

"This one's probably, like, some kind of emergency-exit thing. This one too connects to the surface with a hatch like that other entrance. Actually, we passed the hatch for the emergency-exit side on the way here. Probably nobody noticed it, though."

Apparently there had been another hatch just past the bridge. I certainly hadn't paid it any attention, and neither had Shōtarō. By the time we'd gone through there, dusk had already started to fall, so it was only natural we hadn't noticed it."

"Yūya, did you look inside that emergency exit? Are you sure this floor plan is accurate?"

"Ah, yeah. I tried going down from the emergency exit, just to check. I climbed down something like a ladder and made it to just under the ceiling of the third basement level, but the third basement level was completely full of water. So I just turned back."

"Speaking of the emergency exit..."

There was something that had been bothering me for a while.

"What are these?"

I pointed at the desk.

Sitting there were two LCD monitors. Old fifteen-inch ones, like the kind you used to see in an elementary school library. Scrawled across the bezel of each one in black marker were the words we had just been talking about: ENTRANCE and EMERGENCY EXIT.

"Oh, those. I was wondering about them too the last time I came. They look like security camera monitors, right? They say Entrance and Emergency Exit, don’t they? When I looked in the daytime, there was something that looked like a camera near each hatch. They’re mounted up in the trees. There’s one by the western entrance and one by the eastern emergency exit.

So these probably show the footage from those cameras. But last time there wasn’t any power, so I couldn’t check."

Rattling that off, Yūya switched on the two monitors one after the other.

"Oh, looks like they still work? Ah, damn, they’re actually showing something."

With a faint mechanical buzz, the old monitors brought up the security camera images.

Night had already fallen completely, so the images were indistinct, like woodblock prints. The cameras, like the monitors, seemed to be old, and the picture quality was poor.

Even so, I could tell they were showing the withered field aboveground. By the light of the moon, something that looked like a hatch floated faintly near the middle of both screens. If anyone came near either the entrance or the emergency exit, you would know at once.

Tracing the hatches on the two screens with his finger, Yūya said,

"Right, right. This one’s the entrance we came in through. And this one’s the emergency exit by the bridge."

"Then the two of them must be about a hundred meters apart," said Shōtarō.

"Ah, yeah, that sounds about right. East and west. Maybe the idea was that if outsiders found one of them, you could still escape from the other?"

That was Yūya’s answer to Shōtarō.

Installing the cameras could not have been a simple job either. In some exasperation, I said,

"Pretty tight security. Seriously, what kind of people used this place?"

"It could actually have been some new religious movement doing some kind of unusual ascetic practice. The way the cameras are set up is strange if they were meant to watch for intruders from outside. They look more like something meant to guard against escape."

That was Shōtarō’s reply.

It was a surprisingly persuasive theory. I looked once more, carefully, at the words written across the top of the yellowed floor plan: The Ark.

"Do you think it really was named after Noah’s Ark in the Old Testament?"

"That’s the only origin I can think of."

I remembered flipping idly through a Bible in a cultural anthropology lecture back in college. The all-too-famous story of Noah's Ark was right near the beginning of that thick Old Testament.

When the world is in disorder and the earth is filled with violence, Noah, a good man, receives a revelation. God has decided to destroy mankind. Noah is told to build an ark and prepare for the flood. When the ark is complete, and Noah and his family, along with the males and females of every living thing, enter it, the flood strikes the earth. That is the story. The original account reads rather plainly as narrative, but in sermons and in novels and films based on it, people are often shown mocking Noah and his family for building an ark on a mountain even though no one believes a flood is coming.

A ship-like structure built deep in the mountains. The name may have been attached later, but calling the place the Ark made a certain kind of sense. For the people who used it, whether radicals or some new religious movement, this may have been a place to await salvation.



To me, it was nothing but a bad joke. There was no sign of salvation anywhere in this ominous underground structure. The only thing we had found was torture instruments.

Among the people who had come with me, there was no one devout, and no one with strong political convictions either. Apparently, our group was not Noah's family. We were the ones mocking the people who had built this place.



"Huh? What are you doing?"

Hearing the commotion we were making in the mechanical room, Sayaka poked her head in through the door we'd left open.

Then, right after that, Hana came by, apparently looking for Sayaka.

"Oh, Sayaka, so you were in here. Were you taking pictures or something?"

"Oh, yes. I probably won't come to a place like this again, so I thought I'd take some to remember it by."

It seemed she had been peeking into various rooms and taking pictures.

"I mean, it's not like I mind, but you should probably not put them online, just in case. If the people who used this place found out, it could get annoying."

"Ah, true. I won't. I'll stick to just taking them."

While Sayaka and Hana were talking, Mai and Ryūhei came in together as well.

The four others, too, had apparently been curious about the inside of the structure and had gone exploring on their own. That made all seven of us gathered in the mechanical room.

Everyone looked surprised that the monitors were powered on.

Yūya repeated to them the same explanation he had given Shōtarō and me before, about the flooding on the third basement level, the emergency exit, and the security cameras.



"...Yeah. Okay, I get it, but."

Hana cut Yūya off.

"Actually, I kinda wanna go outside for a bit. My boyfriend messaged me earlier, and if I don't reply by today, he might come over to my place while I'm out."

As she said that, Hana stroked the smartphone in her hand.

Yūya scratched his head.

"I mean... the signal's probably bad around here."

"Yeah, so if I try and it doesn't work, I'll give up, but... can't someone come with me?"

It was pitch-black deep in the mountains. Anyone would be scared to walk around alone.

"Then want me to come with you?"

But Hana seemed reluctant to go outside alone with Yūya.

Sayaka picked up on it at once and threw her a lifeline.

"Then maybe I’ll go too, just for the trip. I’ve probably got work messages anyway. Is that okay?"

"Oh, Sayaka, you’re coming too? Thanks. Yeah, that’d be better."

That settled it. Hana, Sayaka, and Yūya were going outside for a while.





Once the three of them had left, Ryūhei glared at the dark image on the monitor over my shoulder.

"Is that thing really showing anything?"

From a distance, it looked as if the connection was bad and nothing was showing at all.

But before I could answer, something moved on the screen.

"Oh. It's Hana and the others."

On the security camera facing the entrance, the three of them appeared, pushing up the hatch and climbing out in search of a signal.

The figure in front waved at the camera. That was Yūya, and the one after him in the flashy jacket was Hana. Behind her was Sayaka. Their faces were too dark to make out, of course.





A little later, the camera by the emergency exit picked them up too, walking along by the light of their phones. They crossed the wooden bridge and headed for the rise, apparently to see whether they could get a signal there.

"Huh. So it is showing them."

Ryūhei muttered, sounding satisfied.

After that, he and Mai idly handled the things Yūya had dumped on the desk. Eventually they got bored, and Ryūhei grabbed Mai's hand. The two of them left the mechanical room.





Shōtarō and I stayed in the mechanical room, absentmindedly watching the monitor.

At a loose end, I asked my cousin something pointless.

"Think everyone's eaten anything?"

"Who knows."

They had seemed so absorbed in exploring the underground structure that maybe no one had eaten yet.

A little over thirty minutes later, Yūya and the others came back. Thanks to the security cameras, we could tell at once that they were returning.

But something was strange about the group.

"Huh? Aren't there more of them? No... that can't be right."

The silhouettes on the entrance monitor had gone from three to six.

Something straight out of a horror movie was happening. Shōtarō and I left the mechanical room and went to meet the group, who seemed to have run into something, at the iron door.





Yūya was the first to come in when we opened the iron door. Hana followed. Her jacket was smeared with mud, so apparently she had fallen.

Then Sayaka.

And behind her, a frightened-looking family of three.





The father, close-cropped hair shot with gray, thick black-rimmed glasses, in his fifties. The mother, a little on the heavy side, with short hair. Their son, with somewhat thick lips, looked about middle-school age.

The moment Sayaka saw our faces, she was the first to explain.

"Um, these people said they'd gotten lost, and... so I brought them here with me. Since, well, there was at least somewhere with a roof. You were out mushroom-picking, was it?"

The father answered.

"Yes, something like that. Sorry."

Mushroom-picking was in season, but even so, they had come quite deep into the mountains.

It's not as though we lived here, but in any case, we ended up inviting them into the dining hall.
