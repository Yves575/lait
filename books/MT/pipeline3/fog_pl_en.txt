Prologue


He had arranged to meet Zofia at eight. She'd called that morning and asked to see him. He thought: “No.” He said: “All right.” Then he regretted it, but it was too late. She had sounded nervous. She stammered into the phone, gulping air.

“I need to see you,” she said. “I wouldn’t ask if it weren’t really important.”

As always, he felt helpless before that quiet, low voice — before the ł she stressed with particular care, and the ą she pronounced too nasally, as though a round little creature with a restless tail had settled deep in her throat and was tickling her larynx. He was moved despite himself, before he could think, remind himself that all those well-turned syllables were no longer his. Delicate, refined Zofia with her charming speech impediment had stopped noticing him. And yet she had called… Why? What for? Had something happened? And once again unease seized him. An aura of mystery always enveloped Zofia. That was what drew him most. From the start.



On Thursdays she arrived last. During the meetings she would sit to one side. She said little, then slipped out quietly without saying goodbye to anyone. At first she unsettled people, but eventually everyone grew accustomed to her silent presence. From time to time the facilitator asked her a question, which she answered briefly and to the point. The others quickly lost interest. All except him. He waited for Zofia every Thursday. He watched in secret: the white hands, the deep-brown, liquid eyes, the chestnut hair falling across her face. After a month, without asking permission, he changed his place in the circle and sat against the wall. That day he invited her for coffee. Two weeks later the facilitator summoned them for a serious talk. He forbade private contact and ordered them to sit apart. He spoke sharply and decisively: he had no intention of tolerating insubordination in the group. He was speaking mainly to Zofia.

“I’ve been watching you for quite some time,” he said. “I don’t understand why you come here at all. You ought to think seriously about that. You’re a bad influence on the others.”

She heard him out calmly, then took her coat and bag.

“Fine.” She turned in the doorway. “I won’t come back.”

He ran after her. He knew he would have to give up the meetings, but it didn’t worry him in the least. So began the most beautiful three months of his life.




He lowered his head and flinched, as though the sight of his own hands had startled and frightened him. Old feelings returned — a trembling, rolling wave. Three months, he grimaced. Three months when he had believed he truly mattered to someone. It hadn’t lasted long… The romance had ended as violently as it had begun. They hadn’t even had time to get to know each other properly. He knew so little about her… But he hadn’t asked. He preferred to let love blossom slowly, gather strength and momentum. He didn’t want to push or rush anything. He dreamed of holidays together, waited for the day she would finally introduce him to her parents; then he’d move in with her, they’d renovate the flat, put in a double bed… In time they’d buy a bigger place for the children… For three months he had spun ever more expansive plans for the future, feeling calm and certain. And then, suddenly, it was all over. Zofia wasn’t answering her phone. He went over to check on her. He knocked on the door. She didn’t open it, but he couldn’t shake the feeling that someone was inside the flat. Sitting on the stairs, he could hear muffled conversation, shuffling, the creak of floorboards. Then, for the first time in three months, he thought of the Thursday meetings, and fear seized him. He went home. The next day he went back. She opened the door but wouldn’t let him in, blocking the doorway. It struck him at once that someone was hiding behind her. A lover? he thought, and such despair and weakness swept over him that he had to lean against the wall. Zofia regarded him gravely with large, wet eyes.

“I’m sorry,” she said. “I shouldn’t be handling it this way, but…”

He heard his own loud, ragged breathing. He felt himself going pale. His head was spinning.



“Something has happened,” she went on firmly. “We can’t see each other anymore.”

That was all. Like a guillotine cut. No justifications, no explanations. They met once more and she repeated exactly the same thing: certain changes had taken place in her life; they would not be seeing each other again. Her eyes had lost their former softness. They had become cold and resolute. “Certain changes” could only mean a third person — he had no doubt about that.

The following Thursday he tried to go back to the circle. They wouldn’t take him, but the group leader assigned him individual sessions. He didn’t hide how worried he was. He maintained that the shock of the relationship with Zofia could have serious consequences, not only for him but for the entire group. He went on at length about responsibility, obligations, the rules of conduct. Then he questioned him closely about the relationship with Zofia: what they had done during those three months, where they met, what they talked about. He felt the group leader was too insistent, too prying, but he tried to answer. Little came of it. He suffered from a strange kind of amnesia. Nothing remained in his memory but despair, which was slowly turning into rage and uncontrollable jealousy. If Zofia didn’t want to be with him, she couldn’t, she had no right to tie herself to anyone else!

But she had called today, after all, he thought. Could she really have decided to apologise? His heart beat harder. Come back, make up — after she’d used him and pushed him away? And just what does she imagine? He felt humiliated at the thought of the past weeks, all those pleading attempts to make contact, the tearful phone calls, the letters, the sleepless nights. Again and again he had tried to approach Zofia, to force her to talk, to recover the old closeness that surely couldn’t have simply vanished, dissipated without a trace… It kept seeming to him that he would find the right words, convince her, break the spell, and the old, gentle Zofia would stand before him again — his Zofia! Yet she remained, as ever, indifferent, distant, and alien… He sprang from the chair and began circling the room, raising his hands to his face, tearing at the hair falling across his forehead. He went to the window. He looked out at the wet, ugly city: squares covered in mud and melting snow, grey pavements, the colourless facades of the apartment blocks opposite. Now and then a blurred figure flickered behind a windowpane. In front of the neighbourhood shop a few people stamped their feet and huddled under umbrellas. She’d used him as if he were a thing, a dead object you use up and then throw away, not a living, feeling creature. A human. A man, he corrected himself in thought. Again he paced the room, turning sharply at the door and the far wall, only to freeze by the window a moment later. In every direction stretched dozens of apartment blocks: heavy, grim, reeking of must. In cramped concrete flats strangers bustled about. Vertically and horizontally, identical concrete modules. Inside, the same tables, beds, and armchairs. Nearly identical men and women cooked lunch, watched television together, went to the bathroom to take a warm bath or have a piss. Does she know, does she suspect how badly she has hurt him?! He glanced at his watch. It was nearly one.

A fierce irritation dogged him all day. He tried poking around online, watched two episodes of Brigada, the Russian mafia series. In the early afternoon he went to the neighbourhood gym. He ran for an hour, then lifted the barbell and worked his abs. He tried to switch off, to think only of Sasha, Kosmos, Pszczoła, and Phil — the brutal heroes of the series, who’d stop at nothing. He finished with the sauna and a cold shower. Back home, he took the milk from the fridge and sat down at the table. He drank straight from the carton in long, slow swallows. The irritation hardened into a brutal rage. Once more he replayed the morning’s conversation and concluded that Zofia had not called to make peace. Her voice had sounded uncertain, but there was no remorse in it. Short, matter-of-fact sentences, no ‘I’ve missed you’ or ‘I’m glad I’ll see you.’ An offhand ‘Goodbye’ at the end. Bitch, he thought, tracking a plane as it climbed. A ragged streak rent the thick grey clouds. But what could Zofia want from him — for the hundredth time that day he asked himself the same question. Doesn’t matter. This will be his moment. She invited him, so she’ll listen politely to what he has to say. He imagined walking into the flat, telling her to sit down, and explaining briefly, firmly — and her finally understanding… And if she won’t listen? He could hit her, even tie her up. Tape her mouth shut and watch her cry soundlessly, terrified, defenceless, at his mercy. The plane climbed higher and vanished above the clouds. In the flat upstairs someone put on loud dance music. He heard muffled laughter and the patter of quick, light footsteps.

It was nearly half past six. He put the milk back in the fridge. He pulled on a short jacket with ribbed cuffs, gloves, and trainers. He tugged a cotton hood over his head. Outside, a strong, piercing wind blew; wet snow plastered his shoes, soaking them through at once. Even so, he didn’t turn towards the metro. He walked doggedly along KEN Avenue, not stopping, not stepping aside for anyone. Zofia’s face rose before him — her wide mouth, straight nose, the small mole at the corner of her eye, and her gaze slipping sideways as she said: “You have to finally understand that I want nothing to do with you.” He remembered his own agitation, the fear, and the burning sense of rejection. How badly he had humiliated himself, made a fool of himself… He quickened his pace until at last he broke into a run, driven by grief and impotent rage. “Job tvoju mat’!” “Srat’ tebe v rot!” he repeated, unconsciously mimicking the voice of one of the Brigada characters. He didn’t stop until Służewiec, where he went down to the platform and took the metro to Racławicka, then set off again along the snow-covered street, passing the glowing windows of cafés, craft workshops, and small neighbourhood shops. Just past the school playing field he turned into a gateway and nearly collided with an older man in a thick wool overcoat and a fur hat.

“Idi na khuy!” he snarled, then shoved brutally past, pressing the man against the wall.

He could barely catch his breath. His obsessive thoughts went round and round, bringing on a sick dizziness. Drops of sweat ran down his face.



Zofia got home before five. She set the bag of shopping on the kitchen table, took off her soaking shoes, and leaving wet footprints on the floor, went to the bathroom. She lay in the bath, thinking how complicated her life had become over the past three months. And whose fault was that?, she wondered, wringing out the sponge. “Whose fault?” she repeated aloud. I attract bad luck like a magnet, she thought, getting up and reaching for the soap. The more I like someone, the bigger a nutcase they turn out to be. But do only crazies find me attractive, for some inexplicable reason, or do I go looking for their company myself? She rinsed off the lather. She stepped out of the bath and wrapped a towel around her head. She took grey sweatpants and warm wool socks from the wicker basket. For a moment she regarded her face in the mirror. What do I do now?, she asked her own reflection. What am I to do?

Worries or not, the person in the mirror was decidedly not going to look tired or unkempt. Zofia pressed two fingers to her cheeks and drew the skin gently toward her temples. She raised her eyebrows, then reached for the powder and mascara. Refreshed and carefully made up, she left the bathroom. In the kitchen she brewed tea. She set two cups and a small plate of French biscuits on the table. Suddenly she stopped mid-step and stood for a moment staring at the set table, as if trying to settle an important question in her mind. After a moment she took out her mobile and called the same number twice, without success. Finally she left a voicemail.

“I wanted to cancel the meeting,” she said, lowering her voice to sound firm. “Something important has come up. I’m very sorry. I’ll call tomorrow.”



She looked at the phone, uncertain. She had no guarantee the message would be heard. For a moment she stood drumming her fingers on the windowsill. Finally she shrugged and sat down in the window alcove. It was nearly seven. She gazed out at the small square veiled in fog and the abandoned construction site closed off from the street by a wooden fence. A solitary rook crouched on the dark branch of a maple. She ought to make some firm decisions and weigh the consequences. But nothing sensible came to mind. Not even in theory did any solution seem good. She was trapped, with no way out. The rook shook its head, ruffled its feathers, and hobbled sideways toward the trunk. Zofia pulled her knees up to her chin. Down the dark street the wind chased an empty plastic cup. She closed her eyes.

At a quarter past seven the intercom buzzed. She sighed and pressed the door button without a word. Then, driven by a sudden impulse, she snatched the handbag from beneath the coat rack. With a decisive motion she pulled out the medical certificate. Walking toward the bathroom, she tore it into small pieces and tossed them into the toilet.

“I’m in the bathroom,” she called out, hearing the door slam. “I’ll be right there,” she added, flushing.

At that moment she felt a hard blow to her temple. One, then another. It didn’t hurt. She wasn’t afraid. An immense weariness engulfed her. “Who is it?” she wanted to ask before she fell asleep, but blood flowed from her open mouth. She still tried to turn and look at her attacker, but only groaned and sank into deep darkness.





At eight a piercing wind blew off the icy river. At half past eight a fault knocked out the lamps along the embankment. It went dark. Only in the middle of the Vistula, where the current ran strongest, did the paler outline of a drifting ice floe loom. At nine a shaft of headlights raced across the water and vanished into fog. In the undergrowth a bird, woken, cried out sharply. On the escarpment the city rose, wrapped in its glow. Above it spread a second river. Wide and dark. A slippery, polished moon sailed slowly onto it and glided smoothly between the clouds.

She surfaced from sleep as from a well. She opened her eyes and found she was lying beside a polar bear. It wasn’t large. But Ada knew that polar bears, even the smallest, are among the most dangerous animals in the world. She turned gently onto her left side and curled up among the droppings littering the sheet. He must have been doing his business in the bed all night, she thought. This would need sorting out at some point — a litter tray or something… The bear heaved itself up, slid to the floor, and began circling the room, sniffing and skirting the snowdrifts.



‘Get up, time for breakfast.’ A pale, dishevelled woman sat up on the other side of the bed. She drew the infant from her breast and laid it on the pillows. ‘Think I can leave her here?’ she asked.

‘Sure.’ Ada was glad Kasia hadn’t noticed the soiled bedding. Otherwise she probably wouldn’t have laid the child down right in the middle of the filth.

‘Sure,’ she repeated. ‘The bear is gentle as a lamb. He adores babies.’

At that moment she was certain she was telling the truth.

They put on their slippers, closed the door behind them, and set off down a dimly lit hallway. The floor of unplaned boards creaked under their feet. The kitchen was narrow, dark, tiled in the colour of tomato soup. A small barred window gave onto a yard fenced in chain link. It smelled of char and rotting rags. Kasia made tea in glasses, prepared sandwiches. Only on the way back to the room did Ada remember that the day before, the bear had eaten two puppies and the neighbour’s cat. Nothing could be done now. She opened the door. The white beast lay on the bed, licking its muzzle. Of the child there remained a bloody stain on the pillow.

‘Kasia,’ she whispered, grabbing her sister by the shoulders. ‘Kasia…’

A desperate cry woke her. Filip-Oleńka stood at the foot of the bed, meowing. Kasia has no children — that was the first thought to come to her. She felt relief. She sat up, ran her fingers through her short, tousled hair, and stroked Filip-Oleńka, then got up and, tripping over the cat as it kept cutting across her path, made her way to the kitchen. The first cold patches of dawn lay on the windowsill. A garbage truck pulled up outside, and somewhere high under the greying sky terns cried. She turned on the light. The kitchen looked nothing like the one from her dream. A bright, clean floor of white-painted boards, wooden cabinets, a large square window — the whole thing straight out of an IKEA catalogue. She took two ice cubes from the fridge and carefully rubbed them over her face, started the coffee machine, and went to the bathroom to shower. Five minutes later she stood clean, wrapped in a towel, before the open wardrobe. Perfectly pressed shirts and trousers hung in colourful rows: beige, grey, navy, green, as in a high-end clothing shop. Even the underwear on the shelves had been folded into neat white cubes. Ada took out slim jeans, an orange T-shirt, and a brown sweater with a wide turtleneck. She dressed quickly and sat down before a dressing table styled to look antique.



She was nearing forty but had kept her slim figure and a light, girlish way of moving. She had grown used to the idea that she still looked like a student. This had been true as recently as ten years ago, but time ran relentlessly on, leaving its merciless mark on her fair, delicate face — a mark she had stubbornly resolved not to notice. The skin on her cheeks had gone slack and sagged slightly around her chin. Her eyes, dark and watchful, had lost their former brightness, giving way to a look of cool, matter-of-fact assurance, and her wide mouth, with its strange, almost inverted contour, had bracketed the first tentative wrinkles. Ada took powder, blush, and mascara from the drawer. With a few short, decisive strokes of the brush she soon took on the look of a person whose age was impossible to place. She swept her dark, ragged fringe to one side, tucked her hair behind her ears, then nodded at her own reflection as if greeting someone coolly, with reserve — paying exactly the respect that was owed.

She returned to the kitchen and turned off the coffee machine. Filip-Oleńka leapt onto the table so suddenly and soundlessly that she flinched, startled, and spilled her coffee.

‘Not allowed,’ she admonished him sharply.

She added milk and sat with her steaming cup. For a moment she gazed at the greying square of the window. The remnants of the dream peeled away from her slowly. What lingered was an unease she could not quite define, and Ada pushed it from her with irritation. The cat meowed ceaselessly.

‘Not now!’ She raised her voice, trying to sound firm, yet she couldn’t hold back a smile, watching the animal shift from paw to paw, arch its back, and strike ever more ingratiating poses.


‘You can’t eat this much or you’ll get fat, and we don’t want that, do we,’ she said, softer now, and went out to the hallway. Filip-Oleńka headed her off with a soft, stubborn trot. She nudged him aside. She put on her coat, gloves, and tall brown leather boots, grabbed her bag and closed the door behind her.

Commissioner Marcin Sawicki wrenched the old, wheezing Opel into a sharp turn.

‘Police, you dickhead!’ he shouted at the honking Nissan driver he’d just cut off, and pulled up onto the pavement right in front of a no-parking sign.

‘A woman,’ he grumbled, wrestling free of his seatbelt. ‘A woman,’ he kept repeating as he walked down the street in a direction he had no business going. He passed the Coffee Bar, a hosiery shop, a newsstand, and pushed open the door of a Starbucks. He shuddered, then against his better judgment ordered a double espresso and a chicken bagel. He paid eighteen złoty and immediately felt an unpleasant prick of guilt. Almost twenty, he thought with horror, waiting for his coffee. Money you’d never get back. He tried to work out how much he spent on eating out each month but stopped the moment it occurred to him that on top of that came the cigarettes, the beer, and the chocolate bars he was addicted to. He was close to a metre ninety tall, but now, leaning against the counter with his elbow wedged between the bowls of white and brown sugar, nervously picking at a paper napkin that read Have a nice day, he seemed to shrink and cave in on himself. He hunched his shoulders, stuck out his belly, and his broad, likeable face took on the miserable expression of a wronged boy. He felt his cheeks quiver.

‘Esspressobagel, here you go!’ called a pretty dark-haired girl cinched into a branded apron.


He flinched, gave the waitress the look of a beaten dog, and picked up his order. He trudged to a table. He hadn’t slept a wink the night before. He had spent long hours at the computer, watching episode after episode of an American series, masturbating during the uncensored sex scenes, and hoping the darkness beyond the window would never be dispersed by the pink streak of dawn. But dawn rose. Not pale pink but corpse-livid, and Sawicki had to haul himself up and face reality.

This is not good, he thought, swallowing the last bites of the bagel from which he had earlier picked out the rocket. He had nothing against women working in the police — as secretaries, for example. Even in the uniformed division. But a woman in criminal investigations… A woman detective… In Terror, no less… A detect-ress, maybe, he laughed sourly, draining his coffee.

Detective, or lady detective — Sawicki was going to have to babysit her for the coming months until whoever up top had lost their mind finally sobered up and transferred her out. That this was temporary, he had no doubt. Surely nobody in their right mind thinks a woman can be a field operative… a lady operative. He had heard of such cases, true enough — women detectives, man-women with no families, no friends, no personal life, possessed by a mania for chasing criminals. Supposedly they were good at it. But at Mostowski Palace no woman detective had worked the field in a long time. And everyone liked it that way.



It was past nine. He set down his cup, wiped his mouth on Wonderful coffee, wonderful life, heaved himself up, and left the café. It was the last day of February. All of Warsaw held its breath for spring, but spring was in no hurry. A pale winter sun rose above the rooftops, bouncing a thousand glinting sparks off the snow like a thousand stinging needles. On the plastic roofs of tram stops, ruffled pigeons settled in, cooing and mincing in place, tracking every passing car with a vacant gaze. Scrawny leafless saplings stood in a disciplined row along the pavements. Across Bankowy Square the crowd streamed by, bundled in down jackets and woollen caps worn that season in a particularly idiotic fashion, pushed back from the forehead to the middle of the head. Sawicki squinted, groaned softly, and wishing every fashionably dressed pedestrian a sinus infection, set off toward Mostowski Palace.

In front of reception several people paced nervously: a young man in a short military jacket with a frighteningly thin girl whose hair was dyed pink, an older gentleman, and two men in overalls. The duty officer had vanished. Sawicki opened the gate with his magnetic card, crossed the inner courtyard, and dragging his feet, climbed to the second floor. The door to the secretariat was open.

“Good morning. Freezing, isn’t it?” Pani Dagmara shook her teased blonde mane from behind her desk and the green fern perched on it. “Un-bear-a-ble.”

“Cold,” Sawicki agreed, rubbing his whitened hands.

The secretary sent him a friendly smile, sniffed, and went back to staring into space. He glanced at the pale blue elephant-shaped clock on the wall just above her head. He was nearly twenty minutes late — that woman must already be waiting. Or maybe she hadn’t come at all, it crossed his mind. Maybe she’d quit, or been transferred somewhere else — to Kraków, say, or Olsztyn, or Białystok? Who knew what might have happened over the past month…

“Any messages for me?” he asked Pani Dagmara.

“No,” she replied, turning her head with cool, contented slowness.

“Anything worth mentioning… today?” he pressed.

“Noooo,” she repeated, pressing her index finger to the tip of her nose. “No.”



For no known reason, her expertly made-up face and the jangling coloured-glass earrings roused in Sawicki an irritation fiercer than usual, an irritation so unexpected and violent that he could barely control his voice.

“Did anyone ask for me? Pani Dagmara, please focus!” he said, decidedly too loudly.

The secretary jumped, shook her peroxide fringe, and widened her eyes with hysterical intensity.

“Oh, yes,” she admitted at last, and her face settled back into its expression of vague contentment. “The deputy chief is waiting with some lady in the briefing room. The new one, because the old one’s being renovated… Has been for a month,” she added by way of explanation.

“Really?” he hissed. “I had no idea.”

“Really,” she agreed cheerfully. “A pipe burst upstairs and—”

“I know, Pani Dagmara, I work here too!” he cut in with undisguised, cold brutality. “What I’d like, however, is for important information to reach me. That’s what we employ you for!”

“But there wasn’t any information,” she protested calmly, lowering her voice like a well-mannered child. “Only that lady came, and she’s waiting with Rogowski. In the briefing room… The new one…” she added uncertainly.

Sawicki sighed, ran a hand through what remained of his ginger hair, and dragged himself down the corridor. Before he reached the door, he regretted his outburst. I’ll have to apologise to her, he decided, and pressed the handle.

Two people were waiting in the room. A slight, dark-haired woman whose face he couldn’t yet make out, and Artur Rogowski, deputy chief of the Criminal Terror and Homicide Division of the Warsaw Metropolitan Police, known to his colleagues as Róg.

“You’re twenty minutes late,” Róg growled, but his face betrayed relief. He’d clearly reckoned with the possibility that Sawicki might not come at all. “This is Pani Adela Rochniewicz…” He coughed and wiped his bald skull with a linen handkerchief that had seen better days. “Introduce yourselves.”



He stood there, flushed and unkempt, squinting his reddened eyes and clearing his throat loudly. He plainly had no idea how to behave. So he shifted from foot to foot and rubbed his forehead with the dirty handkerchief until the silence in the room grew decidedly too awkward. At last he grimaced faintly, as if to say: “Sorry, pal.” But all he said was:

“Right — I’ll leave you to it and head back” — and he walked out, hitching up his sagging trousers, coughing and snuffling loudly, which Sawicki found even more repulsive than usual.

Ada Rochniewicz sat with her side to the door. Slanting rays fell through the window, brightening the small room proudly dubbed "the new briefing room." Trembling patches of sunlight lay on the wall and the grey carpet. Sunlit motes of dust danced in the air. But Ada remained in shadow. Sawicki could see only the delicate, fragile line of her back and bowed head, and this image let him regain his sense of balance for a moment. He walked to the table with a determined step.

She was pretty. Dressed expensively, fashionably, but with restraint, as though she were heading to dinner at a restaurant rather than to a meeting at police headquarters. She was thirty at most, with a short fringe and wide, oddly shaped lips that gave her face a faintly tearful look. And yet he sensed vaguely that something else lay hidden behind that innocent little face. Something he should on no account take lightly.

"Marcin Sawicki," he introduced himself brusquely.

"Good morning," she replied.

"Sorry I'm late," he muttered, sitting down. "Traffic… I understand you'd like to work with us," he began, pulling a sheaf of yellowed documents from his folder. It was a copy of a cable television contract he'd signed a few months earlier, but he felt surer of himself as he laid a pile of papers down in front of him. "Other than this" — he tapped his finger on the contract while covering the company's colourful logo with his elbow — "is there anything else I should know?"



For the first time she looked him straight in the eye. She wasn't smiling, but the corners of her mouth quivered faintly, as though she were stifling amusement. He clenched his teeth.

"What professional experience do you have? Do you consider yourself available on call?" he pressed on, though she hadn't answered a single one of his previous questions.

He was aware that he was being ridiculous, at the very least — playing the role of a future employer in front of this stranger when they both knew perfectly well he had not the slightest say in the transfer decision. Yet suddenly it seemed to him that through willpower and stubbornness he would force her to retreat. He would win this last skirmish, hand back her papers, and say: "I'm sorry, we have better candidates for this position. Maybe next time."

"I was sent here," she said calmly.

"Yes, yes… So you're looking for work in the police. The police," he repeated the last word with emphasis, hoping this would prove the steadfastness of his character and paper over the obvious comedy of the situation.

"I'm not looking." The timbre of Ada's voice didn't change. "I was assigned here."

She must have been older than thirty. At first her slim, girlish figure had misled him. Now he could clearly see the fine wrinkles at the corners of her eyes and the horizontal furrows cutting across her forehead.

“Police work comes with a great many inconveniences,” he went on, counting on finally throwing her off balance. “Especially for women. We work here, ma’am, under conditions that are hard to bear even for a man in his prime. You never know when or where we’ll head out on a call. A young girl like you… How will you reconcile family duties — a husband, children — with—”

“I don’t have children,” she cut in. “And I don’t intend to.”



He sensed a foreboding in those words. He looked up at once but saw nothing in her face he could seize on and use.

“I’m not hiding it, though I know these are questions you shouldn’t be asking,” she said quietly and calmly, but her dark eyes stayed impassive and watchful, like a surgeon’s. “I didn’t come here for a job interview, and you know that perfectly well. You’re not my superior — just my new colleague. I’d rather we finally stopped acting out this strange comedy.”

Sawicki’s resolve began to melt precipitously.

“That depends on whether we can sort things out,” he muttered. He was trying to keep up appearances, but his body refused him, and to his horror he realised he was squirming in his chair, reflexively wiping his sweaty palms on the desktop.

“True.” Ada smiled calmly. “I hope we’ll manage somehow. Now — could I perhaps see the station and meet the rest of the team?”

“Tomorrow!” he nearly shouted. “Yes, tomorrow, because tomorrow is the first — you don’t start until tomorrow—” He was trying to hold on to even the faintest aftertaste of victory.

“Ada,” she cut in quickly. “My name is Ada.”

“Right, Ada,” he cleared his throat. “Today we’ve got renovations going on here, terrible mess. Some of the lads are out in the field… So let’s start tomorrow at eight.”

“Fine. Tomorrow, then.”

She stood, grabbed her bag, and vanished before he could answer.



Dazed, Sawicki stared blankly at the wall. Was that it? All over? The die cast? The whole damn conversation hadn’t even lasted five minutes. He’d been beaten, laid flat with a single gentle gesture by this tiny little woman who had the look of a fading teenager. He ran a hand over his face.



Ada Rochniewicz had spent eight years with the Wrocław police. He’d received this information a month earlier, along with the news that from the beginning of March he would have a woman colleague, a woman cop, a woman detective — neither fish nor fowl — and no chance of appealing his superiors’ decision. He didn’t know much else about her.

At the beginning of February, Rogowski came back to their shared office after talking to the chief and sat down at his desk, coughing and sniffling.

“A chick? You sure?” asked Sawicki without looking up from his computer.

“A chick, a chick. The Old Man’s working on warming up our image. Our political correctness goes right up once we take her on.”

He squirmed in his chair. Sawicki knew Róg was sneaking uneasy glances at him, but kept stubbornly tapping at the keyboard. He had plenty of work, didn't feel like talking. Rogowski snorted and noisily wiped his nose on a checked handkerchief. Sawicki thrust out his lower lip.

"What?" he asked at last, hitting Delete instead of Tab and wiping out a report field he'd painstakingly filled in.

"You're to deal with her," Rogowski explained, and immediately broke into a dry smoker's hack.

Sawicki was dumbstruck. For a moment he stared at the monitor, then slammed his open palm on the desk.

"Have you lost your fucking mind?! Haven't I got enough on my plate without babysitting some broad?" he snapped. "This was your idea, right?" His voice kept rising as Rogowski's coughing got worse. "Knock it out of that bald skull of yours."

His fury was no joke. Rogowski was the deputy chief, but everyone knew he ran the section in all but name. He made most of the decisions. Sawicki jumped up and walked out.

The next day, once his anger had subsided a little, they managed to talk calmly.



"It's not bad," Rogowski explained in a reassuring tone. "I've seen her papers. She finished the officer training course. And before that, nursing school... Apparently she's a good detective. Solved several serious cases — you'll be pleased."

Sawicki shrugged.

"Eight years with the Wrocław police," Róg went on, undeterred. "Enthusiastic letters of recommendation. A commendation for her part in breaking up the BAF organized crime group" — he was declaiming it like a poem at a May Third assembly.

Sawicki recalled hearing something about that at some point. Seven people had been arrested and forty kilos of cocaine seized, meant to be smuggled across the German border.

"But..." the chief added, already less certain, "in 2010 she was transferred to SDS. Desk work..."

Sawicki raised his eyebrows in surprise. A desk jockey? Why send her back to criminal investigations now, of all times? After two years of warming a chair and pushing paper, you don't just walk back onto the streets...

"It doesn't add up," he said.

"I know. Unless..."

"Unless someone high up really wanted to transfer Adela Rochniewicz, the nurse-detective, to Warsaw and pulled the right strings," he finished angrily. "Is she married? Any kids?"

"The 'marital status' field was blank."

"Why doesn't that surprise me," he muttered.

They didn't talk about her again until yesterday, when Rogowski told him to report to the briefing room the next day at nine and see to the new commissioner.

Sawicki sighed, stretched his arms out in front of him, laced his fingers and twisted his palms until his joints cracked, then stood up slowly and went down to reception to buy coffee from the machine. Men in overalls dozed on wooden benches against the wall, a young guy in a military jacket was swearing loudly, and an underfed girl was trying to calm him down. A large blue sign above the counter proclaimed: VISITORS RECEIVED HERE. The duty officer was still nowhere in sight. On his way back upstairs, he picked up forms from the secretariat and decided to spend the day doing nothing but paperwork.








3


On Friday morning Sawicki woke almost rested. He had slept seven hours. He got up a few minutes after six. He dressed quietly and did no more than brush his teeth. He wanted to drink his coffee alone, in peace. He put the kettle on. The thermometer outside the kitchen window read zero. Clouds covered the sky. A heavy, wet snow began to fall. At seven he woke his wife and his little girls. Just before eight he arrived at work. The reception was perfectly quiet, not a visitor in sight. The duty officer was dozing, head propped on one hand. Upstairs, the door to the secretariat was closed. Walking down the corridor, he wondered whether Adela Rochniewicz was already waiting for him. He had no doubt that punctuality was one of her traits. Punctuality, order, common sense, he thought, taking off his jacket and looking uncertainly around the room. Stacks of papers towered on both desks. In the cupboard lay piles of clothes of uncertain origin and sweat-soaked old shoes. On the windowsill a potted flower was withering. In the water cooler, algae had taken up residence. He wiped the dust from the windowsill with his sleeve, cleared the papers from the desk that had been Rogowski’s, and cautiously peered into the drawer. Inside he found a rotting sandwich in a plastic bag and an empty vodka bottle.

Ada came in at three past eight. She shook his hand briefly, firmly, the way a man would. She took off her sailor jacket and a jaunty cap with a colourful trim. Where does she get the cash for gear like that, he wondered. Those little sweaters, those woollen shorts, those elegant boots? She had a good two police salaries on her back. Watching the new commissioner out of the corner of his eye, he recalled with chagrin his own wife’s cheap, colourful clothes.

‘Come on, then — I’ll introduce you to the guys.’ He forced a smile.

He wanted to get it over with as quickly as possible. He felt uneasy leading her down the corridor. He opened door after door. He kept repeating: ‘This is Commissioner Adela Rochniewicz, she’s with us from today.’ Ada greeted them briefly, politely. Her face was cool, official. Not once did she show interest. She didn’t react to the quiet whistles and snickering behind her back. When they entered the largest room on the floor, several cops looked up, astonished. They seemed taken aback. Ada fit in at the station like the Queen of England in a baseball team’s locker room.

‘Got himself a hothouse flower…’ muttered Kapusta, a fair-haired cop with a pockmarked, unhealthy complexion and a reddened forehead. He said it quietly, as if only to himself, but everyone heard him perfectly well. A stifled snicker went round the room. Sawicki clenched his jaw. Hothouse flower? How does that dimwit even know words like that? Ada’s face remained unmoved.

In the secretariat, Dagmara pulled doughnuts from the fridge and rushed to put the electric kettle on.

‘I knew you’d come today, and I’m ready,’ she chattered. ‘You’ve got to sit down for a minute and have coffee with me. You really must come by here often! Sawicki’s such a boor,’ she added, completely ignoring Marcin. ‘It’s just hard working with men. But you know all about that…’

Dark mascara had smudged around her left eye. Heavy breasts swayed under her pink blouse. She hadn’t even got the glasses out when Rogowski’s round, bald head appeared in the doorway.



‘Homicide reported in Upper Mokotów,’ he said. ‘Sounds like some young girl’s got her head smashed in. You two are going. Kapusta and Chlor are questioning the car-smuggling guys. Me and Wiktor are taking the Old Man to Praga.’

‘But she only just got here…’ Sawicki muttered reluctantly.

‘All the better — let her get straight into it. I don’t know about Wrocław, but here we don’t start the day eating cake.’

‘A girl with her head smashed in?’ he protested. ‘Can’t the locals handle it? We’ve got enough of our own.’

‘They’re already there.’ Rogowski gave an ugly cough. ‘District from the Second, and the ambulance. And Modzelewski,’ he added pointedly, then reached out a plump hand, snatched a doughnut, and disappeared through the door.

Ada raised her eyebrows.

‘Modzelewski’s a prosecutor,’ Dagmara explained hurriedly. ‘He always calls in Metropolitan. For every murder. Must be on duty today.’

‘And just our luck,’ Sawicki finished.

It was going on nine when they left the station. The snow had stopped falling; a pale, distant sun filtered through the clouds. The inner courtyard was coated in ice, trickles of brown water running across it. Slipping, they reached the Opel in silence and headed for Mokotów. The morning rush had just cleared. They didn't turn on the siren. Within fifteen minutes they were there. They parked outside a four-storey pre-war tenement and passed through a narrow gateway into the courtyard. From a leafless tree, a pair of ruffled crows watched them. Sawicki pushed open the stairwell door, propped with a brick. Ada felt her skin prickle at the back of her neck. A cold shiver ran down her spine. Suddenly it hit her that she was starting an investigation. She was back in the field. Two years had passed since the accident that had interrupted her settled life in Wrocław. Two years she had spent shut out, walled in by hostility and distrust. Now she was going back to work. Once again she could do what she was best suited for, what she was genuinely good at. A long-forgotten thrill returned — a moment of focus, and finally a fierce, reviving surge of energy, the kind a wrestler feels just before stepping onto the mat. Ada drew a deep breath, passed Sawicki, and was the first up the stairs.



On the landing between the second and third floors, two identical-looking men were waiting, dressed in matching leather jackets and the same knife-creased wool trousers. Both were smoking, raising their cigarettes to their lips with the same quick, nervous gesture. Both took short drags and exhaled smoke loudly through half-open mouths. To Ada it looked as though someone had placed a mirror on the half-landing.

"These are the famous Mokotów twins." Sawicki laughed at her astonishment. "Commissioner Rochniewicz," he said, introducing Ada.

"Hi," they answered simultaneously.

She nodded.

"So you're taking over." The twin on the left rubbed his hands with satisfaction.

"Modzelewski's gone already. Drop the papers at the office tonight," said the one on the right. "Ambulance came too. Pronounced dead. Morgue crew'll be here in forty. Or so they claim."

"Didn't call for the body men," they added in unison, and began zipping up their jackets.

Just then, sobbing reached them. In front of the open door on the third floor sat a pale, dishevelled girl in an unbuttoned coat. Right beside her, a uniformed policeman stood with his back against the wall, leafing through a morning tabloid. The crying evidently made not the slightest impression on him.

"Ah... that's Monika Lewicka. The deceased's friend," the right twin explained.

"She's the one who called it in," the left one added.

Sawicki nodded.

"Right, we're off. Have fun."

They turned as if on command and ran down the stairs. The uniform started after them.

"Just a moment." Ada stopped him mid-stride. "Please buy this lady some tea." She tilted her chin toward the sobbing girl. "From the café downstairs."



The policeman stopped, surprised.

"Is there something wrong with you?" Ada raised her voice.

"A problem?" He goggled at her, his moustache twitching nervously.

"A problem," she repeated sharply. "You've been standing here for an hour reading a newspaper. And my witness is about to faint. Have you lost all human instinct?!"

The policeman turned bright red.

"Tea?" he sputtered. "With my own money?"

Ada opened her wallet.

"Here." She took out ten złoty.

He stepped back, indignant, and opened his mouth to protest, but shut it at once at the sight of her furrowed brow. He sighed, shuffled in place like a scolded dog, then reluctantly took the money.

"And one more thing!" she called after him.

"Yes?"

"Please bring it in twenty minutes, not before. And make sure it's hot. With sugar," she added firmly.

He nodded obediently, glanced at his watch, and went down the stairs.

The sight of the moustachioed man scurrying off in a panic amused Sawicki so much that he bit his lips to keep from snorting with laughter.

"Monika Lewicka?" He turned to the crying girl. "You're the one who called the police?"

"Yes..." she answered, stifling her sobs without success. "She's inside, in the bathroom..." she added with effort, raising her head and pushing the hair from her swollen face.

He pushed the door open. Just past the threshold, he came upon a puddle of vomit.

"I'm sorry," she mumbled. "That was me..."



Ada took a long step, crossed the threshold, and went in first. Sawicki followed. She stopped in the bathroom doorway. He looked over her shoulder, flinched, and lightly touched her back. She turned her head. Their eyes met for a moment. On the tiled floor, a dead woman lay face down. Her arms and legs were splayed limply, as though she had fallen from a great height. Blood had congealed in her long hair. Ada crouched and leaned over the body. He noticed not the slightest hesitation in her movements. She pulled on latex gloves. Did she always carry them in her jacket pocket? — the thought crossed his mind. She gently pushed aside the matted hair and felt the back of the skull. There was something alien and unreal in the sight of those two women: one dead, one alive — as indifferent as the corpse lying at her feet. It was not the first time Sawicki had examined a crime scene. He had grown accustomed to the sight of mangled bodies, too. And yet from his new colleague he had expected something more than a stony face and a pair of white gloves. What, exactly? Tears? Wringing of hands? He knew she was an experienced officer, after all... She straightened up slowly.

"I think we should call the technicians," she said calmly.

Ada sensed Sawicki's tension at once. For a moment something like sympathy stirred in her for this big, strong man who plainly feared her reaction. She didn't want to embarrass him. She pulled gloves from her pocket and crouched over the body. It was a young woman, in her twenties. Slim, athletic. Ada noticed the muscular calf. There was little blood. It had run from a wound at the temple. The skull there was deformed, hideously caved in. Whoever dealt the blow had hit very hard. She gently pushed aside the tangled hair. The wide-open eyes had clouded over, coated in a yellow film. The lips and nostrils had turned a livid brown. She lifted the edge of the sweatshirt to assess the lividity. She stood up.

"She's been dead at least four hours," she stated, and made to slip deftly past Sawicki, but he stopped her and pointed at the floor. Right beside the toilet lay a discarded object.

"The murder weapon..."

"Yes." He nodded. "The killer left it behind."

For a time they stared blankly at the bloodied hammer. The wooden handle bore a clear stamp: "Sklep metalowy AM-NAT."

"That's very strange," she said at last.

Sawicki started and pulled his phone from his pocket.





While he called for the forensics team, Ada surveyed the pleasant, spacious bedroom: clean floor, a carefully made bed, an armchair with a folded tartan blanket on it. She smiled in spite of herself. She liked order. Instinctively she straightened a black-and-white print that hung slightly off the wall. Light curtains hung in the window. No net curtains. She moved through to the kitchen: smooth white tiles, a wide teak table, a sink with a pull-out tap, a dishwasher, an electric grill... The whole place breathed bourgeois, prosperous comfort. She had recently read about wealthy Warsaw residents buying flats in neglected pre-war tenement buildings. Praga and Old Mokotów had become fashionable. On the table stood a porcelain teapot painted with forget-me-nots, two cups, and a plate of biscuits.

She returned to the hallway. In the open doorway she saw Sawicki leaning against the metal stairwell railing, questioning Monika Lewicka.

"Police," he said to the curious neighbours gathering on the stairs. "Please return to your homes and do not interfere with the investigation. If we need your help, we'll come to you ourselves," he added in a tone so sharp and decisive that the stairwell cleared at once.

She headed for the next room.

"Marcin!" she called suddenly. "Come here. I think you'll want to see this."

All the small room contained was a desk with a laptop connected to a large monitor, an armchair, and an old chest of drawers. Every drawer had been pulled out. Papers, folders, and cardboard boxes lay gutted on the floor. Sawicki's eyes went wide.

“This couldn’t have been a simple robbery,” he said, surprised. “No signs of forced entry. The computer and tablet are still on the desk. Together they’re easily worth a good few thousand.”

“The other rooms are in perfect order,” Ada agreed.



“It looks like someone was after something specific and knew where to find it… He must have had his own key and surprised our victim in the bathroom.”

“I don’t think so. I think she let him in herself. At any rate, she was waiting for someone. There are two empty cups and biscuits in the kitchen.”



The forensics team arrived shortly after. Two serious men in protective coveralls, silicone gloves, and blue plastic shoe covers stretched crime scene tape and strode into the flat. They placed numbered markers beside the body, the discarded hammer, the streaks of blood on the bathtub, then set about securing evidence. The camera flash went off every few moments. Ada stood motionless in the bedroom doorway.

“You need to step out now,” said the round-faced technician. “We’ll be done in an hour.”

A door slammed downstairs. Footsteps approached. A little man was clambering up the stairs, a white lab coat poking out from under a short puffer jacket. There was something strange and unsettling about him. His head and hands were disproportionately large for the rest of him; he had stopped right at the edge of deformity, barely reaching a metre and a half. Ada caught the unmistakable smell of formaldehyde and male sweat. Despite herself, she shrank back in disgust. The little man peered into the flat and, without a word to anyone, strode straight to the bathroom.

“That’s Zbok from the morgue,” Sawicki whispered. “Best not bother him — he’s not what you’d call pleasant…”

Five minutes later the little man reappeared on the stairwell.



“No question somebody whacked her on the head,” he said, casting quick, nervous glances from under bristling eyebrows. “No way she did this to herself” — he let out a thin, unpleasant laugh. “This was no accident,” he added, bending back the fingers of his left hand until the joints cracked. “Dead at least four hours. That’s all I can tell you for now. I haven’t got time to mess about with you lot,” he went on, struggling to catch his breath. “I’ve got three stiffs at Oczki waiting for the dance. Call the body men and ring me Monday.”

He left without so much as a goodbye. An awkward silence fell. Sawicki searched for his phone. He finally found it in the inside pocket of his jacket and dialled the funeral home.

Monika Lewicka was still sitting on the stairs. She had stopped sobbing but was very pale, shaking as though in a fit of fever. Sawicki touched her shoulder.

“I’m sorry, but we need to ask you a few more questions,” he said, and Ada caught a gentler note in his voice. “The first hours after a murder are the most important…”

“I understand,” she said with a nod, and began to cry again.
