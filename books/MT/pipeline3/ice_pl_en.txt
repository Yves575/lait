Ice


We do not freeze.


I
WARSAW


"If a soldier fires a shot from his rifle, in that instant the wound begins to exist—the wound the bullet will bore in the enemy some time hence; if a man takes a dose of incurable poison, in that instant the final mortal process begins to exist, even though its hour may not come for days. The ancients spoke rightly: we died at the moment of birth. Over the further course of the bullet and the poison (and, in part, over living itself) we have no power."


CHAPTER ONE

On the Thousand-Rouble Son


On the 14th of July 1924, when the chinovniks of the Ministry of Winter came for me, on the evening of that day, on the eve of the syberiad, only then did I begin to suspect that I did not exist.

Beneath the eiderdown, beneath three blankets and an old gaberdine coat, in fustian drawers and a woollen sweater, in socks pulled over socks—only my feet protruded from under the eiderdown and blankets—after a dozen-odd hours of sleep at last thawed, curled nearly into a ball, my head pressed under the pillow in its coarse casing so that even sounds reached me soft, warm, coated in wax—like ants mired in resin they forced their way deeper, slowly and with great toil, through sleep and through the pillow, millimetre by millimetre, word by word:

—Gospadin Wieniedikt Yerosławski.

—He.

—Asleep?

—Asleep, Ivan Ivanovich.

Voice and voice, the first deep and hoarse, the second deep and singing; before I raised the blanket and an eyelid, I already saw them, leaning over me—the hoarse one at my head, the singing one at my feet—my tsarist angels.


—We have woken young master Wieniedikt, noted Ivan when I heaved up the second eyelid. He nodded to Biernatowa; the landlady meekly left the room.

Ivan drew up a stool and sat; he held his knees together, and on his knees a black bowler with a narrow brim. A tall vatermörder, white as snow in the noonday sun, stung my eyes—the white vatermörder and the white clerk's cuffs, blinding against the solid black of their dress. I blinked.

—Permit us, Wieniedikt Filipovich.

They permitted themselves. The second perched at the foot of the bed, his weight dragging the eiderdown down so that I had to let it go; catching hold of the blankets in turn, I raised myself on the pallet, and so bared my back as well—cold air pressed in under sweater and drawers—I shuddered, awake.

I threw the coat over my shoulders, drew my knees up under my chin.

They regarded me with amusement.

—How is your health?

I cleared my throat. The night's phlegm had gathered there, a burning acid over everything in the stomach — garlic sausage, gherkins, whatever else we'd had yesterday, warm cornelian cherry brandy and cigarettes, a great many cigarettes. I leaned towards the wall and hawked into the spittoon. It bent me double. Bent double, I coughed heavily for a long while. I wiped my mouth on the torn sleeve of my coat.

— Equine.

— Well, that's good, that's good, we feared you wouldn't get out of bed.

I got up. The pocketbook lay on the windowsill, wedged behind a pot with a dead geranium. I drew out the bumaga and thrust it under Ivan's nose. Not a glance.



— Why, gospadin Yerosławski! Are we beat constables! — He drew himself up even straighter on that stool; I had thought it impossible, yet he drew himself straighter still, and now it was the walls that seemed crooked, the wardrobe hunchbacked, the door-frame scoliotic; offended, the chinovnik raised his chin and puffed out his chest. — We most courteously invite you to Miodowa, for a spot of tea and some sweets; the commissar always has sorbets brought in for himself, pastries, cream horns, straight from Semadeni's — a veritable debauchery of the palate, if I may so express myself, eh, Kirill?

— You may indeed, Ivan Ivanovich, by all means — Kirill sang out.

Ivan Ivanovich had great drooping moustaches, heavily pomaded and curled upward; Kirill, by contrast, was shaven perfectly smooth. Ivan drew a pocket watch on a tangled chain from his waistcoat and announced that it was five to five, that Commissar Preiss set great store by punctuality — and at what hour did I go out for supper? They had an engagement with the Major General at the French Confectionery.





Kirill offered Ivan snuff, Ivan offered Kirill a papiros, and they watched me dress. I sloshed ice-cold water into the washbasin. The stove tiles were cold. I turned up the wick in the lamp. The room’s sole window gave onto a cramped courtyard, and the panes had so grown over with grime and frost that even at noon scarcely any sunlight seeps through. When I shaved — when I still used to shave — I had to set the lamp before the mirror with the flame turned up full. Zyga had parted with his razor the moment he arrived in Warsaw; he had grown a beard worthy of a priest. I glanced at his pallet on the far side of the stove. He has lectures on Mondays; must have risen at dawn. On Zygmunt’s bed lay the chinovniks’ black fur coats, their gloves, a walking stick, and a scarf. For the table was crammed to its edges with dirty dishes, bottles (empty), books, periodicals, and notebooks; Zyga dried his socks and underclothes by draping them over the edge of the tabletop, weighed down with anatomy atlases and Latin dictionaries. And at the centre of the table, atop Riemann’s well-thumbed, grease-stained Über die Hypothesen welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen and atop a pile of yellowed Warsaw Couriers — kept for kindling, for patching up the cracks that frost had prised apart, for drying out boots, and for wrapping sandwiches — there rose a double row of candles and stubs, the ruins of a stearine Parthenon. Against the wall opposite the stove, neat stacks of hardbound volumes rose in tiers, arranged by format and thickness, and by frequency of reading. The ryngraf with Our Lady of the Gate of Dawn that hung above them on the soot-blackened wall — the sole remnant of the previous tenants, whom Biernatowa had thrown onto the street for “indecent conduct” — had gone entirely black and now looked rather like a piece of mediaeval armour for Lilliputians. Ivan studied it at length, with great concentration, stiffly planted on his stool, his left hand with the papiros held out to the side at a forty-five-degree angle to his body, his right resting on his thigh beside his bowler, furrowing his brows and nose, bristling his moustache — and then I understood that he was nearly blind, a chancery myope: on his nose and beneath his eye sockets he bore the marks of pince-nez, and without his pince-nez he had no choice but to rely on Kirill. They had come straight in from the frost, and Ivan had had to take them off. My own eyes water here sometimes. The air inside the tenement is thick, heavy, steeped in every odour of human and animal organisms; no one opens the windows, doors are slammed shut at once and the gaps above the thresholds stuffed with rags lest the warmth escape the building — for fuel must be paid for, after all, and anyone with money enough for coal would not be nesting in such dark back wings in the first place, where the air is thick, heavy, you breathe it as though drinking water spat out by your neighbour and his dog, every breath of yours has already passed a million times through the consumptive lungs of peasants, Jews, carters, butchers, and whores, coughed up from black throats it returns to you again and again, filtered through their spittle and mucus, passed through their mould-ridden, louse-infested, and festering bodies — they have coughed it, blown it, retched it straight into your mouth, you must swallow, you must breathe, breathe!

— Ex-excuse me.

The privy at the end of the corridor was, mercifully, unoccupied. I vomited into the hole, from which an icy stench gusted into my face. Cockroaches crept out from beneath the shit-caked board. I crushed them with my thumb as they crawled up towards my chin.

Stepping back out into the corridor, I saw Kirill standing in the doorway — he had his eye on me, keeping watch lest I bolt on them into the frost in my drawers and sweater. I gave a knowing smile. He held out a handkerchief and pointed to my left cheek. I wiped it. When I made to give it back, he drew back a step. I smiled a second time. I have a wide mouth; it smiles very easily.

I put on my only formal outfit, the black suit in which I had sat my final examinations; were it not for the layers of underclothes beneath, it would hang on me now as on a skeleton. The officials watched as I laced my boots, as I buttoned my waistcoat, as I wrestled with the stiff celluloid collar pinned to my last cotton shirt. I took my documents and what remained of my cash, three roubles and forty-two kopecks — scarcely enough for even a token bribe, but a man feels naked with empty pockets in a government office. As for the old sheepskin coat, I could do nothing about it: the patches, the stains, the crooked seams — I had no other. They watched in silence as I forced my arms into the lopsided sleeves, the left one longer. I smiled apologetically. Kirill licked his pencil and scrupulously noted something on his cuff.



We went out. Biernatowa had evidently been peeping through a door left ajar — she appeared at once beside the chinovniks, flushed and all a-chatter, to lead them back down the stairs from the second floor and through both courtyard-wells to the main gate, where the porter Walenty, having straightened his cap with its brass badge and tucked his pipe into his pocket, hastily swept the snow from the pavement and helped the chinovniks into the sleigh, taking the gentlemen by the elbows lest they slip on the icy ground, while Biernatowa, once they were seated and wrapping their legs in travelling rugs, buried them under torrents of complaint about her malicious tenants, about the gangs of Powiśle thieves who break into houses even in broad daylight, and about the cruel frosts, from which the windows grow damp within and warp, and the pipes burst in the walls, so that no plumbing nor sewerage can long endure in the ground; finally she assured them fervently that she had long suspected me of various misdeeds and indecencies, and would without fail have reported me to the proper authorities had it not been for the thousand and one other cares heaped upon her head — until the coachman from his box behind Kirill cracked his whip and the horses jerked the sleigh to the left, forcing the woman to step aside, and so we set off on our way to the Warsaw delegation of the Ministry of Winter, to the former Palace of the Bishops of Kraków, Miodowa 5, corner of Senatorska.

Before we turned from Koszykowa onto Marszałkowska, snow began to sift down; I pulled my shapka over my ears. The officials in their ample furs and bowler hats like nutshells, seated on the low benches of the sleigh — Ivan beside me, Kirill with his back to the izvozchik — resembled beetles I had seen in Zygmunt’s textbook: thick, oval bodies, short little legs, a small head, everything glossy-black, enclosed in the geometrical symmetry of ellipses and circles. A shape so near the ideal of the sphere cuts itself from the world of its own accord. They stared before them with a passionless gaze, lips pressed tight, chins held high by stiff collars, yielding limply to the motion of the sleigh. I thought I would learn something from them along the way. I thought they would begin demanding gratuities for their favour, for their want of haste and diligence. They were silent. I shall ask them — how? about what? They will pretend not to hear. Flakes of sticky snow swirled between us. I tucked my cold hands into the sleeves of my sheepskin.



In the French Confectionery the lights were burning; the electric glow beating through the great windows knitted haloes of wool around the silhouettes of passers-by. The summer sun should still have stood high in the sky, but as always heavy clouds hung over the city, and even the street lamps had been lit — very tall ones, their tops curled into spirals. We turned north. From Ostrowski’s confectionery, at the crossing with Piękna, girls came running out in little red coats and white hooded capes; their laughter broke through for a moment above the din of the street. The unfinished letter to Miss Julia came back to me, and her last cry-question. Next to Ostrowski’s, at Wedel’s, Fredek and Kiwajs and I used to arrange our card evenings. Right here, past the Sokół cinema, at Kalka’s, the Kind Prince rented a room for his night sessions. Had I raised my head and looked to the left, over Ivan’s bowler, I would have made out the window on the second floor of the tenement at number 71 — the window from which Fredek fell.



At the crossing with Nowogrodzka, frozen fast to a lamp post, there hung a fat cow; a sinew of dark ice joined it to the top of the façade of a four-storey building. The cow must have come from the last cattle drive to the slaughterhouse in Ochota; the wintermen had not yet hacked it free. In the perspective of the street, above the roof of the Sfinks building, a blue-black nest of ice loomed — a great clot of frozen mass hard as diamond, linked by a web of ice-threads, icicles, spans, and columns to the tenements on both sides of Marszałkowska and Złota — to the tenements, the lamps, the stumps of frozen trees, the balustrades of balconies, bay windows, spires of domes and turrets, attic parapets and chimneys. The Sfinks cinema had of course long been shut; on the uppermost floors no lights burned.

The sleigh slowed as we passed Nowogrodzka. The driver pointed at something with his whip. A cart ahead of us was pulling onto the pavement. Kirill looked back over his shoulder. I leaned out to the right. At the crossing with Aleje Jerozolimskie two policemen stood, driving traffic from the centre of the roadway with whistles and shouts — overhead, a luty was just then freezing across.



For several minutes we were stuck in the jam it had caused. Ordinarily lute travel above the rooftops, in cities descending to the ground less often. Even from that distance I fancied I felt waves of cold streaming from it. I shuddered and instinctively pressed my chin into the collar of my sheepskin. The Ministry of Winter chinovniks exchanged looks. Ivan glanced at his watch. Across the street, behind an advertising column plastered with bills for a wrestling match at the circus on Okólnik, a man dressed in the English fashion was setting up an archaic photographic apparatus to take a picture of the luty; the photograph would most likely never see print in any case — confiscated by the men from Miodowa. Ivan and Kirill did not so much as notice him.



The luty was uncommonly lively; before dusk it should manage to cross to the far side of Marszałkowska, overnight it would climb above the rooftops, and by Friday it would reach the nest above the cinema. Last year, when a frostnik was crossing from Praga to the Castle by way of the Alexander Bridge, the Bridge was closed for nearly two months. This glacius here, meanwhile — wait a quarter-hour and I could probably make out its movement, the way it freezes from place to place, shifts in ice, by ice, from ice to ice, the way one crystalline thread snaps behind it, then another, and the blue-white ice-shatter crumbles slowly away, one minute, ksztr, two minutes, ksztr, the wind snatched up the lighter particles along with the snow while the greater part froze into the black sheet congealing the street mud behind the luty, ice of ice; and this track of rough frozen mass, like a trail of snail’s slime, stretched for several dozen metres eastward along the Aleje, and across the pavement, and up the hotel façade. The rest the wintermen had already chipped away, or it had thawed of its own accord; yesterday afternoon the thermometer at Szniccer’s had shown five degrees above zero.



The luty did not move in a straight line, nor did it hold to a constant height above the cobblestones (they also freeze beneath the surface of the earth). Three or four hours ago, judging by the crumbled architecture of ice, the luty had begun to alter its trajectory: until then it had been moving barely a metre above the middle of the street, but at that point, three hours earlier, it had set off on a sharp parabola upward, above the tops of the lampposts and the crowns of the frozen trees. I saw the row of slender stalagmites it had left behind; they gleamed with reflected lamplight, glints of coloured neon, light striking through windows and shopfronts. The row broke off above the tramway tracks — the luty hung with its full weight upon a star-shaped web of frost-strings, strung horizontally and reaching upward toward the façades of the corner buildings. One could have walked beneath it, had anyone been mad enough.

Ivan nodded to Kirill, and Kirill clambered out of the sleigh with a reluctant grimace on a face gone ruddy from the nipping frost. Perhaps I shall be lucky, I thought; perhaps we shall be late, Commissar Preiss will already have left for his dinner engagement with the Major General, and they will turn me away from Miodowa empty-handed. Thank You, Lord, for this icicle-monstrosity. I shifted along the bench, leaning my shoulder against the side of the sleigh. A newsboy ran up — “Hirohito laid low!”, “Express special, Mierzov triumphal!” — I shook my head. At traffic jams in the centre crowds form at once, street hawkers appear, sellers of cigarettes and holy water, of holy fire. The policemen were chasing passers-by away from the luty, though of course they cannot keep watch over everyone. A gang of urchins crept up from Briesemeister’s restaurant. The boldest, his face muffled in a scarf, wearing thick, misshapen mittens, dashed to within a dozen paces of the luty and hurled a cat at it. The great brute flew in a high arc, legs splayed, yowling to the heavens… at once the dreadful shrieking broke off. It fell upon the luty, likely dead already, only to slowly roll off it into the snow, frozen to the bone: an ice sculpture of a cat with limbs outspread and tail straightened to wire. The boys ran off, howling with glee. A Jew with sidelocks shook his fist after them from the threshold of Epstein’s jewellery shop, cursing sulphurously in Yiddish.

Kirill meanwhile had caught hold of the senior constable and, seizing him by the elbow lest he dash off after the street urchins, fell to persuading him of something in a hushed voice, though with ample help from sweeping gestures of his free hand. The constable looked away, shrugged, scratched the crown of his head. The junior of the pair kept shouting at his partner — get moving, help! On the Aleje two sleighs had locked runners, causing an even greater commotion; carts were mounting the pavements, pedestrians cursing in Polish, Russian, German, and Yiddish scattered from beneath wheels and hooves, outside the wine merchant’s a matron built like a Gdańsk wardrobe had gone down on the frozen mud, three gentlemen were attempting to raise her, a pot-bellied officer hurried to their aid, and so all four heaved — one — she fell — two — she fell — three — by now half the street was roaring with laughter, while the poor creature, flushed cherry-red, squealed dreadfully, waggling her stout little legs in their dainty boots... Small wonder, then, that only at the sound of tearing metal and the crack of splintering wood did we look back toward the intersection. A motorcar had collided with a coal cart; one horse had gone down, one wheel had come off. The constable shoved Kirill aside and broke into a run toward the wreck. Trapped inside the enclosed machine, the motorist began squeezing the alarm horn; on top of that something went off under the bonnet of the bolide — like a blast from both barrels. That was too much for the grey harnessed to the sleigh alongside. Spooked, it lunged forward, straight toward the luty. The coachman snatched at the reins, but the horse itself must have felt what a wall of frost it had run into — it bucked sideways all the more forcefully, swinging the sleigh around on the spot. Did the runner catch on the kerb? Did the grey slip on the black sheet of ice? I was already on my feet in the ministerial sleigh, watching the accident with Ivan over the row of vehicles ahead of us, but it was all happening too fast, too unexpectedly — too much movement, shouting, lights and shadows. Down went the grey, down went the sleigh it pulled, off fell its cargo — a dozen or so bulging demijohns in baskets packed with sawdust — baskets and bottles went rolling into the middle of the intersection, some must have shattered, for a shimmering celadon liquid spread across the ice — kerosene, I thought — and fire erupted at once; from what — an electrical spark off the motorcar, a dropped cigarette, the strike of a shod hoof on cobblestone — I don’t know. Blue flame danced across the full breadth of the puddle, high, ever higher, a metre, a metre and a half into the air — nearly reaching the luty frozen into the overhead wires.

The photographer, hunched over his apparatus, slowly, methodically burned exposure after exposure. And what would he see on them later, what would preserve itself on glass and print on paper: snow — snow — the pale haloes of streetlamps — dark mud, dark cobblestones, dark sky — the grey façades of tenements down the broad canyon of the city — in the foreground a chaos of angular shapes, vehicles stalled in the snarl — from among them, from among the human silhouettes, surges the blaze of pure fire, so bright the plate appears at that point entirely unexposed — and above it, above the flame of a whiteness whiter than white, in the heart of the hanging arabesque of ice spreads the luty, luty, a massive thunderbolt of frost, a starburst of rime, a living hearth of cold, luty, luty, luty above the fur toques of women, luty above the caps and bowlers of men, luty above the heads of horses and the roofs of carriages, luty above the neons of cafés and salons, of shops and hotels, of confectioneries and greengrocers, luty above Marszałkowska and Aleje Jerozolimskie, luty above Warsaw, luty above the Russian Empire.

As we then rode on towards Saxon Square, along Królewska, past the Garden dead beneath years of permafrost and the colonnade hung with icicles, past the tower and the Orthodox cathedral on Saxon Square smothered under overhangs of snow, towards Krakowskie Przedmieście, that image — the afterimage of vision and imagination — haunted me again and again, an insistent memory of obscure significance, a sight seen but not understood.




The officials exchanged surly remarks in undertones, the coachman shouted at heedless passers-by; the snowstorm had eased, yet it was growing ever colder, my breath froze on my lips, hanging as a white cloud before my face, the sweating horses moved in clouds of clinging moisture — the Royal Castle drew ever closer. Before the turn into Miodowa I saw it above Sigismund’s Column: sealed within a mass of shadowy ice, the Castle — and the great luty-nest above it. A violet-black congelation reached halfway up the roofs of the Old Town. On clear days one can see the waves of frost standing in the air around the Great Tower. No scale on any thermometer can measure that cold. Gendarmes keep watch by bonfires at the edge of Castle Square. When a luty freezes out of the nest, they close the streets. The Governor-General had stationed here a cordon of dragoons from the Fourteenth Little Russian Regiment, but the regiment had since been dispatched to the Japanese front.

The roof of the Palace of the Bishops of Kraków, however, remained free of icy accretion. In the wing facing Senatorska there were still elegant shops — electric spotlights illuminated the advertisements for Nikolai Szelechow’s Exclusive Delicatessen and the teas of the Moscow Trading House of Sergei Bazilovich Perlov — but the main wing on the Miodowa side, beneath the rococo crown and amid pilasters with Corinthian capitals, belonged to the Ministry of Winter. Above both gateways hung black, double-headed eagles beneath the crowns of the Romanovs, inlaid with onyx tungetite.




We drove into the inner courtyard; the sleigh runners grated on the cobblestones. The officials alighted first — Ivan vanished at once through the doorway, settling his pince-nez on his nose; Kirill halted on the steps, before the threshold, and looked back at me. I opened my mouth. He raised an eyebrow. I lowered my gaze. We went in.

An attendant took my sheepskin coat and shapka, and the doorkeeper slid across a great ledger in which I had to sign in two places — the pen kept dropping from my numbed fingers — might he sign for the esteemed gentleman — no, I, I myself. The unlettered common folk, too, frequent the corridors of Winter's nachalstvo.

Everything here gleamed with cleanliness: marble, parquet, glass and crystal and iridescent zimnaz. Kirill led me up the main staircase, through two ante-rooms. On the walls, beneath portraits of Nicholas II Alexandrovich and Piotr Rappacki, hung sunlit landscapes of steppe and forest, of springtime St Petersburg and summer Moscow, from the days when spring and summer still had entry there. The staff did not raise their heads from their desks, but I saw the councillors, desk officers, clerks, and scribes furtively track me with their eyes, then exchange dark looks amongst themselves. When do office hours end? The Ministry of Winter never sleeps.



Extraordinary Commissar Preiss, W. W., occupied a spacious study with an antique stove and a disused fireplace; the tall windows looked out onto Miodowa and Castle Square. When I entered, passing Ivan in the doorway — he had evidently already announced me — the commissar was fussing at the samovar, his back turned. He himself had the figure of a samovar: a plump, pear-shaped trunk and a small, bald head. He moved with frenetic energy, his hands fluttering over the table, his feet never ceasing their dance — a little step to the left, a little step to the right — and I was certain he was humming to himself, smiling to himself, that from a ruddy face merry little eyes gazed out upon the world, no crease furrowed the smooth brow of the commissar of Winter. Meanwhile, since he did not turn round, I stood by the door, hands clasped behind my back, and let the warm air fill my lungs, bathe my skin, thaw the congealed blood in my veins. The study was almost hot; the great, colourfully painted majolica stove never cooled for a moment, and the windowpanes had at last so steamed over that through them I could see chiefly the blurred rainbows of the street lamps, strangely spreading and merging on the glass. It is a matter of great political import that cold should never hold sway in the Ministry of Winter.

— Well now, why don't you sit down, Wieniedikt Filipovich? Sit down, sit down.

A ruddy face, merry little eyes.

I sat down.

With a great sigh he sank back on his side of the desk, cradling a cup of steaming tea in his hands. (He had not offered me any.) He had not been posted here long; the desk was not his; behind it he looked like a child playing at ministers, and would certainly have had the piece replaced. They must only just have sent him — sent him, the extraordinary Imperial commissar — from where? From St Petersburg, from Moscow, from Yekaterinburg, from Siberia?

I drew a deeper breath.

— Your Honour will permit... Am I under arrest?

— Under arrest? Under arrest? How could such a thought have come into your head?

— Your officials —

— My officials!

— Had I received a summons, I should certainly have come of my own —

— Did they not invite you courteously, Mr Gierosławski? — He pronounced the surname correctly at last.

— I had thought —

— Good Lord! Under arrest!

He puffed.

I laced my fingers over my knee. It is worse than I thought. They are not going to throw me in gaol. A high-ranking Imperial official wants a word with me.

He began drawing papers from the desk. He drew out a thick bundle of roubles. He drew out seals. Sweat ran down me beneath my linen.

— Yees. — Preiss slurped his tea loudly. — Please accept my condolences.

— Pardon?

— Your mother died last year, did she not?

— Yes, in April.

— You were left alone. That is hard. A man without family is like... well, he is alone. That's bad, oh, bad. — He turned a page, slurped, turned the next.

— I have a brother — I muttered.

— Yes, yes, a brother at the other end of the world. Where was it he went — Brazil?

— Peru.

— Peru! What does he do there?

— Builds churches.

— Churches! He must write often.

— Well, more often than I do.



— That's nice. He longs for you.

— Aye.

— And you — do you not long?

— For him?

— For your family. When did you last hear from your father? — A page, a page, a slurp.

Father. I knew it. What else could it have been about?

— We do not correspond, if that is what you are asking.

— That's dreadful, dreadful. You have no interest in whether he is even alive?

— Is he?

— Ah! Is Filip Filipovich Gierosławski alive! Is he alive! — He fairly leapt up from behind his grand desk. Against the wall stood a great globe on a slight stand of damp zimnaz; on the wall hung a map of Asia and Europe; he set the globe spinning, slapped the map with the back of his hand.

When he looked back at me, not the slightest trace of his recent merriment remained on that plump countenance; dark boulders regarded me with clinical attention.

— Is he alive — he whispered.

He lifted yellowed papers from the desk.

— Filip Gierosławski, son of Filip, born in the year eighteen hundred and seventy-eight in Wilkówka, in the Kingdom of Prussia, in East Prussia, Lidzbark district, a Russian subject from the year nineteen hundred and five, husband of Eulagia, father of Bolesław, Benedykt, and Emilia, sentenced to death in the year nineteen hundred and seven for participation in a conspiracy against the life of His Imperial Majesty and in armed insurrection; nu, by way of clemency the sentence was commuted to fifteen years of katorga with deprivation of rights of estate and sequestration of property. In the year nineteen hundred and seventeen the remainder of his sentence was remitted, with a prikaz na zhitelstvo imposed within the boundaries of the Amur and Irkutsk Governor-Generalship. He did not write? Never?

— To Mother. Perhaps. At first.

— But now? Recently? Since seventeen. At all?

I shrugged.



— Surely you know perfectly well when and to whom he writes.

— Don’t be insolent, young man!

I smiled faintly.

— Forgive me.

He studied me a long while. On the finger of his left hand he wore a ring set with some dark stone in a mount of precious tungetite, engraved with the emblem of Winter; he tapped the ring against the desktop, truk, trukk, every second stroke harder.

— You graduated from the Imperial University. How do you occupy yourself now?

— I was preparing for my rigorosum —

— What do you live on?

— I give private lessons in mathematics.

— And do you earn much from these lessons?

As I was already smiling, there was nothing for it but to lower my eyes to my clenched hands.

— Much, not much.

— You are a frequent guest of the moneylenders; every Jew on Nalewki knows you. To Abiezer Blumstein alone you owe more than three hundred roubles. Three hundred roubles! Is it true?

— When Your Honour tells me in what matter I am being interrogated, it will be easier for me to confess.

Truk, trukk, truk, trukk.

— Or perhaps you really have committed some crime, that you sit here in such a fearful sweat, eh?

— Your Honour might care to open the windows.

He stood over me; he scarcely needed to stoop to speak directly into my ear — first a whisper, then a soldier’s growl, at the end nearly a shout.




— You are a gambler, my Benedykt, an inveterate card player. What you win, you lose — what you earn, you lose — what you borrow, you lose — what you beg from friends, you lose — you have no friends left — you have nothing left, and even that you lose, you lose everything. Twenty-one, baccarat, zimucha, poker — any way will do. Once you won half a sawmill — you lost it the same night. You must lose, you cannot rise from the table until you have lost so thoroughly that no one wants to play with you any longer. No one wants to play with you any longer, Wieniedikt Filipovich. No one wants to lend any more. You have pawned yourself two years in advance. Bolesław does not write to you; you write to him, begging for money, but he sends no more. You do not write to your father, because your father has no money. You wanted to marry, but your would-be father-in-law set the dogs on you when you pawned and lost your fiancée’s dowry. If only you’d at least blown your brains out, as befits a nobleman — but you won’t, tfu, fine nobleman you are — scum!

I smiled apologetically.

Commissar Preiss caught his breath, then gave my shoulder a friendly pat.



— Come now, don’t be afraid — we’re no strangers to scum. Never mind that your own father concerns you about as much as the gout of the Emperor of Japan — but a thousand roubles, now that concerns you! True? A thousand roubles concerns you like… well, it concerns you very much. We’ll give you a thousand, and perhaps another after that, if you acquit yourself well. You will go and visit your father.

At this he fell silent, evidently awaiting my reply. Since none came, he returned behind the desk, to the steaming tea (it had cooled somewhat, so he now slurped it longer and more loudly), to the papers and seals. With a heavy pen he signed a document, struck it with a seal, adjusted another; satisfied, he rubbed his little hands and sprawled in the leather armchair.

— Here is your passport and a napravleniye to our office in Irkutsk; they will see to you there. They have already bought you a ticket — you leave for Moscow tomorrow, otherwise you will not make the Siberian Express — today is the first of July, your ticket is for the fifth, departure at ten in the evening from Yaroslavsky Station; in Irkutsk you will be on the eleventh, and there they will put you on the Zimna, bound for Kizhma. Here is your thousand — sign the receipt. Buy yourself some clothes, so you look like a human being! And if it should enter your head to take the money and lose it all — but then, lose it if you like, so long as you reach Baikal. Now then, sign!



A thousand roubles. What do they want me to do there? Extract from my father the names he did not divulge at his trial? Only why is the Ministry of Winter turning to me with this, and not Internal Affairs?

— I’ll go, — I said, — I’ll visit my father. And then what? That’s all?

— You will talk with him.

— I’ll talk with him.

— Once you’ve talked — well, it’ll be fine.

— I don’t understand what Your Honour —

— He sent no letters, and of course that did not surprise you. — Commissar Preiss opened a portfolio bound in broadcloth and tortoiseshell. — Irkutsk writes to us... He was a geologist, yes?

— Filip Filipovich studied geology. He never lost his interest in it, not even in Siberia. They report to me here... On What Cannot Be Said



My life is governed by the principle of shame.

One comes to know the world, comes to know the language for describing the world, but one does not come to know oneself. Most people — nearly all, I believe — will not learn, before they die, the language in which they might describe themselves.



When I say of someone that he is a coward, it means I consider that he behaves in a cowardly fashion — it means nothing more, since of course I cannot peer into the depths of his soul and discover whether he is a coward. This language, however, I cannot use to describe myself: within the compass of my own experience I remain the one person for whom my words, deeds, and omissions constitute merely a pale and, at bottom, accidental reflection of what lies hidden beneath them, of what constitutes their cause and source. The existence of that source I experience directly, while of some of my behaviours I am wholly unaware, and all of them I perceive in incomplete, distorted form. We are always the last to learn what an idiot we have made of ourselves in company. We know the intentions behind our acts better than the acts themselves. We know better what we meant to say than what we actually said. We know who we wish to be — we do not know who we are.

The language for describing our behaviour exists, because many people experience this reality and can discuss among themselves someone's ostentatious politeness or someone's faux pas. The language for describing myself does not exist, because no one experiences this reality but me.



It would be a language for one person's use alone, a language unsayable, unwritable. Each must create it for himself. Most people — nearly all, I am convinced — never manage it before they die. At most they repeat to themselves other people's descriptions of their own person, couched in that derivative language — that language of the second kind — or they imagine what they would say about themselves in it, were they granted the chance to look upon themselves from without.

To say anything about themselves at all, they must make themselves strangers to themselves.

My life is governed by the principle of shame. I have no better words with which to tell this truth.

A beggar stretches out his hand in a pleading gesture, I have money, I can give him some, even five, even two, even a single kopeck, no one else is watching, it is only the two of us, I and the beggar — I give nothing, I turn away and walk off quickly, tucking my head into my shoulders.

Who broke the jar of preserves? Mother raises her voice. Who broke it? Not me, and I have no idea whether Bolek or Emilka is to blame, but Mother asks once more, and again, and then I point decisively at Bolek. Him.

Laughter in the company — we are all mocking a mutual acquaintance who is of course not here among us now, one after another, dredging up memories of his disgraces and weaknesses. I rise, I am not laughing, brows furrowed and lips pressed tight, I rise and — have you no shame!



A krasavitsa clings to me, spills across my shoulders, slides down onto my chest, into my lap, as the cab slows and halts before the gateway of the tenement where I rent a room with Kiwajs — Kiwajs is away visiting distant relations in the provinces, the room is free, the drunken girl giggles, biting at a button of my frock coat — the cabman turns round, winks at me, shall I help your lordship? I stare, terrified. I press a crumpled rouble into his fist. Take the whore wherever she wants! I fling the girl off me, leap from the cab, and flee deep into the dark gateway.

In the woods beyond grandfather’s village, among upturned roots above the stream, there lies the carcass of a roe deer. Half-eaten by predators and scavengers, beneath a black canopy of flies. I tell no one; I come every morning and every evening, jab at it with a stick, turn it over, watch how the insects crawl over it afresh, how the flesh changes colour, and dark fluids seep from the body, soaking slowly into the earth. Where do I spend so much time, grandfather asks. I lie. He sees I am lying. Where do I go, he asks. I say nothing. He thrashes me with his belt. I weep. I do not tell the truth, I say nothing. All summer long I wander the woods, searching for dead animals. I carry a thick stick with me, like a cudgel. I beat the carrion with that stick in dull abandon, until the rot comes away from the bone. Take that! Take that! Take that!



Eighteen and seventy-four! Eighteen and seventy-four! The fairground barker tears his throat raw, announcing the winning numbers. We all bought lots on entering the fair. Laughing, red from the frost and the slivovitz, we rummage now through our pockets for the little cards. I find it, draw it out. Eighteen and seventy-four. Playing at great disappointment and fury, they hurl their lots into the mud, curse their luck, and jeer at the fool who does not come to claim his prize. I tear up the winning lot and do as they do.

At night, when no one sees, I practise fearsome grimaces, eyes bulging and teeth bared, too wild to mean anything in the language of human physiognomy; fearsome grimaces, and also the absolute deadness of countenance, the stillness of the smallest muscles of the skull, which in the light of day and among people I can never achieve, for I have no command then over the play of my face. At night, when I sleep, I see beneath my eyelids a diagram, as from Zyga's yellowed plates, an anatomical diagram of that betrayal: dozens of hard twine threads threaded beneath the skin of the cheeks, the brows, the chin, the lips; and the other ends of those threads are held by the people surrounding me, in their hands, between their teeth, knotted around watch chains and cuff links, on rings, on the heads of walking sticks and pipe stems, some have them sewn into their own facial muscles, or even into their hearts, straight through the chest and the breastbone. Then the plate comes alive—it is day—there is movement—people—I—

I smile—I smile—I smile.

…



I recall the behaviours, I employ a second language...

I must become a stranger to myself.

Benedykt Gierosławski was a good child. Who would not wish for such a child? He was well-behaved—he always obeyed his parents, obeyed his elders, did not speak unless spoken to, did not get into mischief, did not fight with other children, said his prayers before bed, had the best marks at school. Other boys roamed the city, spent their time on the streets—he read books. Other boys peeped at girls and teased their sisters—he taught his little sister her letters. He washed his ears. He bowed to neighbours and acquaintances. He did not pick his nose, did not point. He did not fall ill. He gave his parents no trouble. Who indeed would not wish for such a child?

When he turned seventeen and entered the Imperial University, he met others who had been good children.

They recognised one another by their smiles.

Was it already too late? Could I have turned back—turned back to what, to whom, to which me? There is no moment in the past which, miraculously altered, would have reversed the course of my life, no catastrophe that befell me as a result of which I am other than I ought to be; I can point to no such thing.



It is precisely the reverse: however far back I might reach in memory, I always find beneath the thin epidermis of my words and deeds the same principle, a muscle of unease, hope, and revulsion tensed in the same direction when I was three, thirteen, and twenty-three. Not a feeling, something else, not a thought—it flees, slips free, slides away, immaterial, indescribable—stop! look me in the eye!—you, you—I shall call you Shame, though you are not, I shall call you Shame, because there are no better words in the language between human beings.
