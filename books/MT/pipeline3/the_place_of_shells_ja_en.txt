At the Place of Shells

Standing in the shadow of the deserted station building, I kept waiting for the visitor with the half-effaced face. Even when I dredged through memory and somehow pieced an image of that face together, it would at once collapse again like water. Even so, I went on gathering the fragments, pressing them inside the outline to produce a patchwork portrait. The repetition was steeped in the same timid sensation as probing a throbbing tooth with the tongue.

The sunlight at the beginning of July had grown whiter, pouring out heat without stint. Its rays sharpened color even as they reduced everything to black and white. In the station square, glowing white, there was no shadow. On the benches people sat apart from one another like statues that would not melt even in this heat. Set against those motionless figures held fast by the sunlight was a black dog roaming about. The dog twined himself around the transparent water rising from the square. The cobblestones were pierced with fountain jets, and water burst from them and stopped again with no pattern. Slender columns of water, rising to the height of a child, bloomed like glass plants on a rapid cycle, then quietly vanished, leaving behind traces of wet black circles. Delighted, the dog twisted his body in play against the water-snakes, shining white and transparent. Each time the water disappeared, he sprang toward another fountain jet. He alone took pleasure in even that heavy sunlight without a care.

Beside the square of white-scorched cobblestones in front of Göttingen station stood a forest of entangled bicycles. Bicycles had been crammed in with elaborate density, as if stitching up every gap in the parking area, and the metal of their frames gave off a light the sunlight itself could not melt. Even now, three years after coming here, I still do not understand by what means such complicated three-dimensionality manages to keep its balance. Even as I watched, a man braked sharply, twisted the bicycle he had ridden into a seam in that metal forest, and ran in haste toward the station building. Still running, he slipped on his mask in one smooth motion and was drawn in beyond the doors. With another bicycle's worth of complexity added, the forest had already become such a mass of things that it was losing the very meaning of bicycle. In the light tracing over its surface, another image rose up, that of a mass of things from which meaning had come undone, and it began to throb in imitation of toothache.

I checked the time and peered in through the station's main entrance. Perhaps a train had arrived; the hall, almost empty a moment before, was suddenly overflowing with people, and rich color filled the white space. Yet as soon as they passed through the doors and receded into the white sunlight, the travelers who had appeared lost their color once more. When I called to the dog by name, he came bounding to my feet at once, reluctant though he was to break off his play with the water. I fastened the leash to his collar and stepped into the station. In the white hall, the black of his wet coat grew heavier still, vivid as a stake half-driven into the white, and I stood there steadied by that color.

The people thinned out, the voices thinned out, even the announcements of arriving and departing trains fell silent, and for a brief interval the hall filled with blankness. I stepped out from that soundless white and went a little farther down the corridor. Since the station's front and rear entrances are joined by a straight passage, one can see, small in the distance, all the way to the door at the far end, except where a few glass elevators stand along it. Standing at the edge of the quiet corridor, I cast my gaze ahead, and there a vivid green entered my sight as a painting framed by the doorway. For all its sharpness, it did nothing to shorten the distance between it and me. As though I were looking through a telescope from the wrong end, it only flickered before my eyes as a fragment of some far-off world. When I brought it into focus, a human figure slowly took shape. In that corridor, its perspective gone awry, Nomiya stood with the green at his back. Perhaps he had noticed me; the monotonous sound of a suitcase's wheels drew nearer. As the distance fell away, I felt the time that had already receded and the memory that had remained motionless begin to move again, imitating the creak of the wheels. His tall, lean figure seemed to let the oppressive heat slip past him with ease. The proportions of his broad brow and the large eyes beneath it seemed traced from a medieval carving. Perhaps because he stood against greenery trembling in the sunlight, I suddenly thought of a saint depicted in the stained glass of a high church window.

But memory failed me there. When Nomiya came within speaking distance, the lower half of his face, covered by a white mask, blurred into the color of the cloth, and in the shadow of that white, which blurred the contours, I could not clearly make out either the movement of his mouth or even the hint of words. The same was true of me, and at that moment nothing was more welcome than this thing, hot and stifling as it was. I too hid behind what covered my mouth, keeping my words in reserve. Yet once we moved away from the station's main entrance, we would have to take our masks off, and if time was to be joined again, it would be necessary to let the words show.

Nomiya stepped free of that impression of a saint behind glass and at last removed his mask outside the station. His mouth began to shape what seemed to be It has been a long time, but then, as the length of the severed time between us came home to him, it never became sound, and the line of his lips gave way to a small, ambiguous smile. Even so, when I saw that slight bow, light but courteous, memory came apart inside me, piece by piece, with the pain of something catching, and the words I had prepared were lost. This was how he greeted people. My pieced-together, broken memory cries out. More than to his face or figure, the nine years try to fasten themselves to that gesture. At the same time, I felt relief at the sight of a figure carrying enough presence not to turn translucent in the sun. Even at the feet of the dead, shadow pools. I returned the slight bow, awkwardly imitating that substitute for words. We both smiled wryly at the clumsy symmetry of it, but once our lips had moved once, conversation naturally found its starting point. When I said, It was a long journey, wasn't it, Nomiya nodded, allowing for all the phrase's possible meanings, and said with an easy smile, I'm a little tired. In a calm voice he went on to speak of the train's delay and the missed connections, of asking a station attendant whether there might be another train and struggling to make out the answer because the man's rapid speech was muffled by his mask, and of the impression made by the old fortress seen from the train window. From the way he spoke and carried himself, he seemed nothing but an ordinary traveler. All the things I had imagined from what Sawada, Nomiya's friend, had told me melted into white before this reality, and I become capable of nothing more than puppet-like murmurs of assent. Then, over our lopsided conversation, silence came down with an audible drop, as though the cord suspending a curtain had snapped.

There is so much green, and yet no cicadas. Unconcerned by the broken conversation, Nomiya murmurs this, narrowing his eyes and peering through the sunlight, trying to find the voice that scorches summer. His body, where time has stopped, receives the white, heavy light, yet his gaze is not quite anchored anywhere in waking reality. Seeking the cicada voices that fill a dense blank, his eyes are turned toward a more distant time.

The dog kept a slight distance, watching Nomiya. His body language was easy enough to read: his tail, white only at the tip, hung in midair at a halfway height, as if marking a pause. Without straying from his own orbit, he waited for the other to come closer. He probably had no notion that Nomiya was a ghost. His manner toward a stranger was consistent, and there seemed to be no distinction in it between the living and the dead. Relieved on that score, I urged Nomiya on and started toward the street leading to the old town. Seen from a distance, I too, and the dog, and Nomiya as well, would probably have seemed to lose all color and melt away into the white. That white blurred the perspective of memory. It could not, however, bridge the breaks in time or distance.


Göttingen is a city where the seams of time do not show. One can pass with ease from one time into another through memories laid one atop another. Like many German cities, it has at its core an old town thick with accumulated history, and around it the city spreads outward in all directions, as though wrapping itself around and over it. The circular old town is ringed by the old city walls. The walls themselves are already gone; in their place a thin ring of woods encircles it, with footpaths threaded through it and packed hard by lovers of walks in the forest. From one end of the old town to the other takes about half an hour, a size easy to take in on foot. If you trace the streets woven into it, you begin to notice that time there is layered in a many-faceted structure.

I had heard where Nomiya was staying, too, through Sawada. It was north of the old town, near the University of Göttingen's science campus. When I said, "So it would be better to go by bus," Nomiya nodded, and we made for the broad avenue that cuts through the ring of woods where the old city walls once stood. There was a bus stop in front of the station as well, but no bus ran directly from there, so we decided to head for one inside the old town.

I learned of Nomiya's arrival about two weeks earlier. Until the day he arrived, we never exchanged messages directly, relying instead on Sawada's careful, attentive mediation. Because of that, I could not shake the sense that I had come here as Sawada's living proxy.


Dragging our shadows behind us, we had barely reached the edge of the old town when we came upon a golden sphere, glinting dully in the sunlight. On the sidewalk facing a hotel with pale stone walls, it stood there like a bud swollen past measure atop the stem of a dark bronze plaque. Nomiya's gaze slowly traced its outline. This is the Sun. My voice, telling him this, sounded farther away than I had expected, and I felt I might lose my own place.

Embedded in Göttingen is a scale model of the solar system known as the Planetenweg, the planetary path. In a place layered with old memories, the model's own history is still brief, yet it has already settled into the city and become one of its emblems. The Sun on Goethe-Allee, which runs straight east from the station, marks the starting point, and the planetary models from Mercury to Neptune line the street in sequence. Because the layout follows a scale of 1:2,000,000,000, the distances between them are irregular, and unless one is mindful of them, they are easily passed by. From Mercury to Saturn they lie within the old town, but Uranus and Neptune stand quietly in the residential district beyond it. Neptune, at the far edge of the solar system, sits exactly at the boundary with the forest. In this way the place itself deftly overlays a metaphorical sense of distance among the planets of the solar system.

There are said to be roughly fifty Planetenwege in Germany alone, yet I sometimes find myself wondering why a miniature solar system should be set down on the earth at all. Is it because space travel, which once nourished dreams of the near future, has grown more and more remote? Or perhaps we simply need to keep our feet on the ground, to concentrate on the earth so intently that we can do no more than make do with a substitute. Whatever the reasons behind its installation, for walkers and joggers it makes an ideal marker of distance and of the point at which to turn back. With the planets at the edge of their vision, they move toward the Sun and away from it.

At first glance, the bronze Sun and eight planets could almost be taken for tourist information displays. Alongside a simple map of the Planetenweg as it threads through the city, they give the usual information about each planet: its size, orbital period, number of moons, distance from the Sun. Set into each of them, too, is a scale model based on the stated ratio. At the starting point, the Sun is represented by a large metal sphere with a muted golden sheen, its precarious balance supported by the bronze plaque. Like a balloon swollen by sunlight, the sphere looks as though, if one kept staring at it, it might simply drift up into the sky. The planetary models, by contrast, have only the faintest presence. Above each bronze plaque a viewing window has been cut, and in it a planetary object rendered to scale is displayed. Even so, from Mercury to Mars these are scarcely more than metal specks, no larger than pinholes, embedded in thick glass. Through glass scored with fine scratches, planets so tiny slip into the distorted view beyond. Only with the planets that circle beyond Mars do they finally acquire a visible form as objects.

Between the Sun and Mars the intervals are short. Nomiya and I, the dog beside us, followed the stretch from the Sun to Mars as far as the middle of Goethe-Allee. Each time we came upon a bronze plaque, Nomiya stopped, so the rhythm of our walking, and the conversation that tried to keep time with it, kept faltering. Since leaving the station building, we had exchanged few words. I knew the reason was the temporal distance of nine years, yet it also began to seem as though the Planetenweg itself were impeding our conversation. And yet somewhere within me I welcomed this state of affairs. These bronze markers filled the gaps in a conversation that verged on silence.

Once we crossed the river that ran through the town, the street changed its name to Prinzenstraße. It turned from Goethe into princes, though the atmosphere of the street itself showed little change. For a while after that no bronze plaques came into view, and at last, unnerved by the blank where words should have been, I found myself reduced to giving a rambling tour of the town. As we went, I pointed out the antiquarian bookshop, pizza place, music shop, and stationery shop on our left, and, on our right, the library in its silent poise. Eventually, beside a florist and a secondhand bookshop facing the main north-south street through the old town, we finally came upon the bronze plaque for Jupiter.

The dog I had brought with me when I went to meet Nomiya was Agata's. She was my housemate, and for the past year we had shared a place. She had studied economics at a university in Hamburg, worked in an office for a while, and then come to Göttingen to study information science.

Her dog was about the size of a beagle, with a somewhat long coat, black and white and patterned so that the two colors never mingled. Most of its face and back were black, but its belly, legs, chest, nape, and part of its tail were white. Its muzzle was long, and there was something indistinct about its face, as though the image of another animal had been projected onto it.

When people spoke of this dog, everyone except Agata, his owner, was more likely to call him "the truffle dog" than by his name. By then that nickname had grown more familiar to our ears and tongues than his own name, Hektor. A truffle dog is, literally, a dog with a nose for finding truffles. By learning the smell of that singular fungus and being trained to catch the scent buried beneath the soil, it acquires the art of unearthing truffles. Agata's dog had acquired his nose for mushrooms at the hands of Agata's mother, his original owner. She had lived with the dog in a small town far south of Göttingen, taking a free, easy pleasure in her pastime of walking in the woods. Yet a year and a half earlier, I had heard, she had died of breast cancer.

Many of the anecdotes surrounding the truffle dog, too, had come down from Agata's mother. The small town where she had lived also extended partway into the forest, and there, it was said, mushrooms packed with scent lay quietly hidden. Late in the summer three years earlier, while she was out on her daily walk in the woods, Hektor caught the scent of a black truffle. His long nose twitching, he suddenly plunges into the brush and begins scratching at the earth with his nose pressed to the ground. As her mother watched what the dog was doing, a black, round lump was dug up from the soil. The richly scented black is small, but compact and heavy. She slipped it into the pocket of her jacket with a straight face and carried it home. Wild truffles, properly speaking, may not be harvested in Germany for reasons of environmental protection. "If it's only one, they'll let it pass," her mother would say with a laugh. Yet she did not let that discovery remain a mere accident.

When her mother saw the dog rubbing his nose against the pocket still fragrant with truffle, she held the truffle up to his nose again and again, weaving the memory of its scent into him. The forest gives off a whole music of scents. The smell of water held in the green. Rotting leaves, mold on the undersides of leaves. Earth loosening in the sun's warmth. The cold green scent of cypress with its bluish cast. The dry fragrance of deciduous trees layered deep with autumn's colors. Into this, too, a new shade was woven: the scent of truffle. Within the dog's olfactory field of vision, the forest comes into lucid relief. Yet, just as one cannot fully decipher every color woven into an Impressionist painting, it remains a secret shared by the forest and the dog alone. From then on Hektor was the truffle dog, and yet he never found more than three black truffles. "He's so polite to the forest, isn't he." Agata's mother, it was said, would often smile as she buried her hands in the dog's black fur, soft and smoky.

After her mother's death, the truffle dog was taken in by Agata in Göttingen, and little by little he adapted to his new surroundings. This city, too, is wrapped in forest. The remnants of the old city walls around the old town make an ideal walking route, and Agata takes the truffle dog on a neat circuit of the ring of woods. Whenever I saw the owner with the dog bounding around at her feet, I thought of a planet revolving with a satellite in tow. On weekends they often went farther afield, making their way into the forest that spread beyond the green where Neptune's bronze plaque stood. Agata did not seem to expect much from mushrooms, but she would often joke, "If we find one, let's share the secret." Once she showed me a photograph of the truffle her mother had found in the year of her mother's death. Small, enclosed in a hard black rind, it looked very much like a star that had fallen from the sky and burned itself out.

The story of the truffle dog had been repeated so often that the tale itself was already woven through with the taste and scent of that black, spherical fungus. Through this anecdote, my nose and mouth, of their own accord, try to summon the impression of some unknown, meteorite-like thing. The name truffle had taken on a life of its own. It was like a ghost fastened to taste and smell.

From the streets to the dog. Still unable to settle into orbit, I kept voicing little stories about truffles as though we were playing a word-association game. Taking advantage of Nomiya's quiet attention, I let words imitating Agata's way of telling the story spill from my mouth. What my quickened tongue spins is no more than a counterfeit tale passing itself off as storytelling. It was a bad habit of mine. To avoid the heart of the matter, I flee into talk of animals, using it to stitch the conversation together. I had brought in the truffle dog as first aid for when the conversation frayed. When at last the words ran out, Nomiya and I both fell silent, each pretending to be absorbed in the truffle dog's behavior. The dog walks at a rhythm that does not hinder our steps, but it cannot dislodge the catches in our words. Keeping those nine years in view, I still do nothing but circle endlessly around them. Was Nomiya the same? However intently I listened to the silence, I could not sound the depth of his quiet.

When we reached Jupiter's bronze plaque, we turned left at the corner and made our way north through the city. Weender Straße, which divides the old town into east and west, admits no cars apart from postal vans, garbage trucks, and police vehicles; only pedestrians' feet tread the cobblestones.

Leaving Jupiter behind, we would have to step off the Planetenweg. Only when we reached Jupiter and saw the scale model inscribing its presence in the viewing window did Nomiya lightly trace its outline with his fingertip. Something in my memory gives a faint creak: when he described a work, he always used to do this without thinking. He would put a painting or sculpture into words, moving his finger over the surface of a table or through the air to mark out its contours and arrangement, as though his fingers too were trying to inscribe it in memory. "We're heading toward the bus stop, so unfortunately Jupiter is as far as we go." "Is Saturn far from here?" "Much farther on. It's at the edge of the old town, so perhaps you can see it another time." Nomiya withdraws his finger from Jupiter without fuss and goes on without hesitation into the city's clamor, thick with words strange to the ear.

The flow of people along Weender Straße was beginning to return, yet everyone walked with an awareness of the space between them, and more and more people were out walking alone. "Even though it's a main street, it isn't that crowded," Nomiya says quietly, his gaze moving along the street. When I had been in touch with Sawada, I explained the situation in town, but I had no way of knowing how much of it had reached Nomiya. Since March, the shadow cast by the unfamiliar word COVID had gone on changing, the moment one looked away, into something monstrous. "There was a cluster outbreak last month, and things slid backward again because of that. People will be out and about more soon enough," I said, and he nodded. Just when it seemed the lockdown had begun to loosen, a sudden jump in the numbers had an immediate effect on the air of the town. The tension was easing again, and yet I still could not rid myself of the dazed sensation of having had a door shut in my face. That abrupt turn from easing to tension had somehow put a great crack in the feeling of what was real, and even now a sensation like jet lag still rendered this place opaque. I ought to have known well enough how fragile reality was, and yet perhaps I had believed, without question, that its foundations were stable. Perhaps Sawada had explained some of it to him, because Nomiya asks no questions, only moves from sunlight into shade, looking over the town. Without trying to explain any further the unreal reality that had gone on for months, I confined myself to briefly telling him the rules of life under these conditions. What he may need is not words but, rather, some sensory way of narrowing the distance between himself and reality. How is he to grow used to a time lag of nine years? How does this place look in Nomiya's eyes, this quiet life warped into something like the far shore?

Along the way, the truffle dog nudged my leg with his nose to ask for water. From the bag on my back I took out a bottle of water gone lukewarm and a small bowl, and poured him some water while he urged me on. At the sound of it, louder than I had expected, I flinched and stole a glance at Nomiya. He was absorbed in looking up at the buildings ranged along the street. Their façades were plastered with faces that looked down at Nomiya and met his gaze. A man's face wearing a strange smile, a devil's face frozen in outrage: they had something of the effect of masks, stuck fast to the city and casting their gaze down. Buildings bearing faces, face-haunted buildings, were still among the things my senses could not quite reach. Would they, once one had grown used to them, become as much a part of one's own time as the faces of one's family, or were they things from beyond the boundary, treated like unwelcome lodgers, like ghosts haunting a house?
