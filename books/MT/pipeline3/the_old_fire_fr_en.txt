NOVEMBER 6

Because of the rain, I missed the sign for the village. It blurred the hollows, erased the ruts, I ended up inching forward blind and pulling onto the shoulder. All that water hammering on the hood. The storm started yesterday. I haven't seen anyone since I came off the highway. Even though the radio was advising people not to drive, I had no choice. It's five o'clock, anthracite sky. I couldn't get the seatback angle right. I wait, sitting very straight, dulled by the din. At least the van looks solid. It looks like a road-crew vehicle, plasma-colored. I insisted on practicality at the rental desk.

An hour passes. At last the sheets of rain ease. I start up again. The GPS takes me deeper into the forest. Soon neither rain nor light can pierce the canopy. I switch on the high beams. The steering wheel is sticky. I crawl along for kilometers, guessing at the path among the tracks under the brambles, until I come out at the foot of a steep slope. A little farther up, the gate stands open. For the first time, I repeat my father's movements. I shift into first, accelerate, the wheels spin on the loose stones but hold, I kill the engine in front of the house. The automatic light comes on. A rabbit bolts.

The house looks tired, the roof sagging over the bricks like a giant choked by ivy. A car is parked under the hazel tree. Bracken is tearing apart the front steps. Through the window, I can make out light. I press my eye to the peephole, draw back at once. I wasn't expecting my sister's face, forehead huge, brows wide apart, fish eyes, my sister swollen by that lens my father claimed he'd put in backward on purpose. According to him, we had nothing to fear and nothing to hide, our riches were within, and the whole world should know the most beautiful people lived here.

"Hi."

My voice sounded louder than I expected. Véra answers with a smile too big for her mouth. She takes the suitcase from me, sets it down at the foot of the stairs in the kitchen. I recognize the stone floor, the wooden furniture, the bathroom door in the shadow of the fireplace. I had never seen it like this, the hearth blocked with books. Above the table, a birdcage has replaced the light fixture. Cheeses are piled behind the bars.

Véra points to the stairs, then to the countertop, I am to get settled while she finishes making dinner. I've known her far messier. I compliment her. She types on her phone and shows me the screen:

<It's to welcome you properly.>

I answer a little sharply that we're sisters and this is my home too, we can do without that sort of politeness. She turns on the gas with a hurried movement. I can't help adding:

"Especially since we're not keeping any of it."

The stairs hiss beneath my socks. I have to be careful not to slip. Our parents' bedroom is ajar. I stay on the threshold, in the draft from the badly insulated French door. Black parquet. At the heart of the room, the big bed, the naked mattress, no sheets, no blanket. I still wonder how my parents could sleep without a wall behind their heads. I close the door again, vaguely relieved. I don't know what I dreaded more, sleeping in that bed, or sharing our room with my sister now that we're adults.

Her sweet perfume catches in my throat. She kept our bunk beds. Tonight, the sight of them troubles me. The wrought iron looks too thin to hold us. The dresser and desk are still in place, as plump as before, salmon pink. I make sure I can get online. I'll be here for nine days, I need to be able to stay in touch with my colleagues. The signal shows only one bar. Sometimes it disappears.

Leaning out the window, I spot the van. Its orange paint makes me laugh. It looks like a big bumblebee. It stands out as much as my sister and I do, together again for the first time since my father died, five years ago.

Véra has poured wine into the crystal glasses. The ritual makes me tense, I say I don't drink. She raises her eyebrows, pours the wine back into the bottle, it spills, I wipe it up with my sweater then take it off, I'm hot. The bamboo tableware is unfamiliar to me. Proudly, Véra points to the cheese wrapper decorated with chestnuts, then to the fireplace: it's a smoked cheese. I push away the thought that it's made with raw milk. She has made an endive salad with figs and walnuts. I ask whether she's thought about how we're going to handle things over the next few days, I haven't, I've been very busy. She taps on her phone:

<Congratulations on your award.>



I murmur that that's kind.

I don't know what she knows about the films I've written. The latest one just won an award at an Italian festival, I couldn't go, and anyway I hadn't invited her.

"Have you heard from Octave?" I ask in a voice I wish were neutral.

She nods, of course, points to the figs, the walnuts, they came from him... I cut her off. I say that as far as I'm concerned there's nothing I want to keep. She can sort out what she wants, we'll take the rest to the dump. Her fingers tighten around her phone. With her chin she points to the wardrobe, the kitchen, the bathroom. I roll my eyes, we're not really going to go through all that, are we? Her face lights up in the glow of her screen:

<Whatever you want.>



I soften. It's just that I have work to do. I'm behind. I'll have to shut myself away to write. With a simplicity that unsettles me, she shows me the screen again:

<Whatever you want.>



Then asks what I've been up to. I mention my latest assignment, the adaptation of Georges Perec's novel W, or the Memory of Childhood. Véra punctuates my sentences with smiles. I affect nonchalance as I mention the production's prestige, the renown of the actors under consideration, my co-screenwriters' reputations. We have to create six episodes. Shooting is scheduled for two years from now. Véra applauds. I qualify that. Adapting this text isn't simple. And I'm only the dialogue writer. I start to explain Perec, she nods vigorously, she knows, she read A Void.

"You read?"

She signals that of course she does.

"I don't know, my father..."

Silence.

"I mean Dad. He hadn't told me." Without losing her smile, Véra passes me the last figs. I was fifteen, Véra twelve, when I left for the United States. I was supposed to stay through high school, with a host family. Véra had long since stopped speaking. She was learning to read and write, but I hadn't thought her that capable.



"And you, are you okay?" I ask, realizing I haven't asked her a single question since I arrived.

Our last exchanges date back to last year, when she moved to Périgueux. Until then she had lived here with my father, and stayed on even after he died. I helped from a distance. The studio was already furnished, she didn't want to take anything from our childhood home without clearing it with me. Now that the sale papers are signed, Véra was counting on me to come clear out the house.

I read over her shoulder. She gestures impatiently, I need to give her time. I apologize, help myself to more salad.

After a land redistribution, our house is no longer considered a tenant farm attached to the neighboring château, le Pigeon Froid, which belongs to Octave's family. Safety standards have changed. To comply, we'd have to redo the roof, the heating, the electrical system, we don't have the means, we've accepted an offer from a campsite that's going to tear it down. Octave wants to salvage our stone for the dovecote's restoration.

Véra shows me the screen. Working in the shop bored her. She's training in floral preservation.

"Preservation?"

<I make flowers that never wilt, thanks to chemistry.>

I put on an inspired air.

"And does it work?"

<It depends on the flowers.>

I clarify, I mean as a business. She shrugs:

<People don't want the hassle anymore.>

We clear the table.

"It's true, flowers don't grow in November," I say at last, flatly.

Véra soon goes to bed. I stay in the living room, the night seeping into me. The floor lamps cast pools of warm half-darkness but I don't feel at ease. The windows have no curtains. I see my reflection on the sofa ringed with black, in the refrigerator's purr, in the smell of the cheese Véra put back in the cage. I feel oppressed by the blocked fireplace, by Van Gogh sunflowers thumbtacked up by the dozen. My father collected posters for plays and art exhibitions. He never went to any of them and didn't care about the artists' names, but chose the most colorful images to plaster the house with.

I feel worn out at the thought of the big clear-out. If we set fire to the books, only the stones would be left, that would be simplest, since they're all we're being asked to preserve.

I put off sorting the emails until tomorrow and spend the evening idling on my messaging apps, worried by Irvin's silence. It's almost evening in New York. He'd have had time to write to me. I'm tempted to wait for him to make the first move, call myself childish, wish him good night, tell him I got here safely. I hesitate. Add that I miss him.

The bathroom is just as I remember it. It looks like a cave, floor and walls of rough stone. Véra has laid out a towel for me, neatly folded on the sideboard. The sideboard has always disgusted me with its wormholes, even though they've been treated. She's taken off her jewelry. Ethnic style: feathers, shells. I open a drawer. It's full of amber, necklaces, brooches. I push the wet bathmat aside with my foot, step into the shower stall and make Véra's hair disappear down the drain. I hope I don't clog it.

I stay under the scalding water a long time. My hair still bears the mark of the elastic, it dried out when my hormones dropped. I don't know when my period will come back. I turn toward the stone. I still have the reflex, when I'm naked, of looking for something to brace myself against, of not looking down at this lower belly Irvin claims he never sees as swollen. According to him, it's in my head. He wants to reassure me. It isn't his fault. He didn't see it drain out along my legs, redden the water, then disappear into the pipe. He knows nothing about my body.

The less I try to make noise, the more the stairs creak. The bedroom is bathed in the greenish light of our phones as they charge. Only Véra's head sticks out from under the blanket, her hands domed on her chest. Her clothes in a heap on the dresser. The ladder squeaks, the sheets rustle. Lulled by the smell of laundry detergent, I fall asleep at once.

7 NOVEMBER

I sort things by category. Things to destroy, things to give away. I laugh at myself, taking influencers' advice on tidying. The window lets in a cold light. The spiders flee. They do not spin webs, they cling in the corners, around the oven. I clear away the dead ones from the bottoms of the pans. I started with the kitchen, which seemed like the most neutral room. Into a big trash bag I throw the expired food. Mustard, tomato paste. A jar of white stuff, duck fat. An entire shelf is devoted to cheese. The fridge shrinks back under my assault. The freezer compartment needs defrosting. The plastic cracks. In the vegetable drawer, potatoes have sprouted. Véra has only come back once a month since she moved out. The smell of the cheese turns my stomach. My movements are quick. Moldy jam, butter scraps, wilted herbs.

Véra is taking care of our bedroom. I'm uneasy knowing she's nearby. I listen for every sound she makes. A little while ago she came down to make herself coffee, I was smelling a jar of compote, she asked if I wanted some too, and not knowing whether she meant the compote or the coffee, I put the jar in the trash the way a rabbit caught in the headlights throws itself under the wheels.

Véra is making progress too. At this rate, I'm afraid we'll end up with too much time alone together. What am I going to do with her for six more days? I try to slow down.

The kitchen clutter resembles Véra. I prefer it to the neatness of the living room when I arrived. Oil has spilled. I clean the greasy residue, the sticky clumps. As I pass the rag along the back of a cupboard, I feel the panel move. I press harder. It comes loose, comes away easily. It covered a space as big as an apple crate, hollowed out in the cool stone. It is full of packets of sugar and bottles of spirits. I call Véra. She did not know about this nook either. Does she think our father... She shakes her head, probably not. It is true I never saw him drink.

"And Mom?"

Véra stays pensive. I pick up a bottle.

Quince liqueur. I want to throw it away, she stops me, mimes us drinking straight from the bottle. I shake my head no, but set it back down and close the secret compartment. At the bottom of the sink, pieces of jam mix with the grayish water. I have to plunge in my whole arm to fish them out.

Véra seems impressed by my sorting. She looks at me, tugs at her sweater and points to the stairs: I should take a look at my clothes.

"I'm giving them away."

She insists. I can feel her watching me as I go up. That look weighs on me. It makes more of the gesture than I do.

My side of the dresser has stayed as it was before I left for the United States, a few sweaters fewer. All the same, I try on a pair of jeans. Just for fun. My father had mended a hole in them by sticking on a flower-pattern patch with a saucepan of boiling water. I'm surprised to find I've grown slimmer. Even though I've put on weight these past few months, Irvin likes it, which reassures me and unnerves me at the same time. I put the jeans in a plastic bag, do the same with the flannel trousers, a fabric I never wore again, cotton pajamas, T-shirts and wool sweaters. I start rummaging with the growing sense that I'm looking for something.
