You were the one who told me about the city.

On that summer evening we walked upstream along the river, breathing in the sweet smell of grass. We crossed several little falls built to trap the shifting sand, and now and then stopped to watch the slender silver fish swimming in the pools. We had both been barefoot for a while. Clear water washed cool around our ankles, and the fine sand on the riverbed enfolded our feet—like soft clouds in a dream. I was seventeen, and you were a year younger.

You had tossed your low-heeled red sandals into a yellow vinyl shoulder bag, and kept walking a little ahead of me from one sandbar to the next. Wet blades of grass clung to your wet calves, turning them into lovely green punctuation marks. I carried my worn white sneakers, one in each hand.

As if tired of walking, you drop down into the summer grass and look up at the sky without a word. Two small birds dart side by side across the sky above us, crying sharply. In the silence, the blue premonition of dusk begins to gather around us. When I sit down beside you, something strange comes over me. It is as if thousands of invisible threads are delicately tying your body and my heart together. Even the briefest flicker of your eyelids, the faintest tremor of your lips, is enough to shake me.

At an hour like that, you and I have no names. There is only the vivid feeling of summer dusk on the grass by the riverbank, when we are seventeen and sixteen. Before long the stars will begin, little by little, to flicker above our heads, but they have no names either. We sit side by side on the grass by the river in a world without names.

“The city is surrounded by a high wall,” you begin. You draw the words up from the depths of silence, like someone diving into the deep sea with nothing but their body to gather pearls. “It isn’t a very large city. But it isn’t so small that you can take it all in at a glance, either.”

This is the second time you have spoken of that city. With that, the city acquires a high wall all around it.

As you go on speaking, the city acquires a beautiful river and three stone bridges (East Bridge, Old Bridge, West Bridge), a library and a watchtower, an abandoned foundry and simple communal housing. In the pale light just before summer dusk, you and I gaze at that city with our shoulders almost touching. At times we narrow our eyes from far away on a distant hill. At times we open them wide from so close we could reach out and touch it.

“The real me lives inside that city surrounded by a high wall,” you say.

“Then the you in front of me now isn’t the real you,” I ask, naturally enough.

"Yes. The one here now isn't my real self. I'm only a stand-in for her. Just a shifting shadow."

I think about that. A shifting shadow? But for the time being I reserve judgment.

"And what does your real self do in that city?"

"I work at the library," you answer in a quiet voice. "From around five in the evening until around ten at night."

"Around?"

"There, all times are approximate. There's a tall clock tower in the central square, but it has no hands."

I picture a clock tower with no hands. "And can anyone go into that library?"

"No. It isn't open to just anyone. To enter, you need a special qualification. But you can go in. You have that qualification."

"This special qualification—what kind of qualification is it?"

You smile softly. But you don't answer the question.

"But if I can just get there, I can meet your real self?"

"If you can find that city. And if..."

You fall silent there, a faint flush rising to your cheeks. But I can hear the words you leave unspoken.

And if you truly want my real self... Those are the words you deliberately leave unsaid. I gently put an arm around your shoulders. You're wearing a pale green sleeveless dress. Your cheek comes to rest against my shoulder. But the one I held in that summer dusk was not your real self. As you say, she is no more than your stand-in, a shadow.

Your real self is inside the city surrounded by high walls. There is a beautiful island thick with river willows, a few low hills, and everywhere quiet beasts with single horns. People live in old communal housing and lead simple lives, lacking for nothing. The beasts like to feed on the leaves and fruit of the trees that grow in the city, but in the long winters when the snow piles up, many of them lose their lives to cold and hunger.

How much I wanted to enter that city. To meet your true self there.

"The city is surrounded by high walls, and getting inside is very difficult," you say. "Getting out is harder still."

"How can I get in?"

"All you have to do is want it. But wanting something with all your heart isn't so easy. It may take time. Meanwhile you may have to leave many things behind. Things that matter to you. But don't give up. No matter how long it takes, the city will never vanish."

I imagine meeting your real self inside that city. I picture the vast apple grove, growing thick and beautiful outside the city, the three stone bridges over the river, and the cries of unseen nightbirds. And the small old library where your real self works.

"There is always a place waiting for you there," you say.

"A place for me?"

"Yes. There's one open position in the city. You'll be the one to fill it."

What kind of position could that be?

"You're going to become a Dream Reader," you say, lowering your voice, as if confiding an important secret.

The moment I hear that, I can't help laughing. "Look, I can barely remember even my own dreams. For someone like me to become a Dream Reader would be pretty difficult, wouldn't it?"

"No. A Dream Reader doesn't need to dream at all. In the library stacks, all you have to do is read the many old dreams gathered there. But that isn't something just anyone can do."

"But I can?"

You nod. "Yes, you can. You have what's required. And I, in that city, will help you with that work. I'll be beside you every night."

"I'm a Dream Reader, and every night in the stacks of the city library I read many old dreams. And you are always beside me. The real you," I repeat aloud the facts you have set before me.

In my arms, the bare shoulder exposed by your green dress trembles slightly. Then suddenly tenses. "That's right. But there's one thing I want you to remember. Even if I meet you in that city—I, in that city, won't remember a single thing about you."

Why?

"Don't you understand why?"

I do understand. Yes, the girl whose shoulder I am gently holding now is no more than your stand-in. The real you lives in that city. In that mysterious city far away, enclosed all around by a high wall.

And yet the shoulder beneath my hand is so smooth and warm that I cannot help thinking it belongs to the real you.

In this real world, you and I live some distance apart. Not so far that it counts as a great distance, but not so close that I can decide on impulse and go see you at once. If I change trains twice and spend about an hour and a half getting there, I can reach the city where you live. And neither of the cities we live in is surrounded by a high wall. So of course we are free to come and go.

I live in a quiet suburban neighborhood near the sea, and you live in the center of a much bigger, busier city. That summer I am in my third year of high school, and you are in your second. I go to the local public high school, and you go to a private girls' school in your city. For various reasons, we actually see each other only once or twice a month, no more than that. More or less by turns, I visit the city where you live, and you come to the city where I live. When I visit your city, we go either to a small park near your house or to the public botanical garden. The botanical garden charges admission, but beside the greenhouse there is always a cafe that is never very crowded, and that becomes our favorite place. There we order coffee and apple tart, a small extravagance, and lose ourselves in quiet conversation, just the two of us.

Whenever you visit my town, the two of us almost always take a walk by the river or along the shore. There was no river anywhere near your house in the middle of the city, and of course there was no sea, so whenever you come to my town, the first thing you want is to see the river or the sea. All that open water there—you are drawn to it.

"For some reason, looking at water calms me down," you say. "I like listening to the sound it makes."

Last autumn, by chance, I met you, and we have been seeing each other for about eight months now. Whenever we meet, we hold each other somewhere no one is likely to see us and softly press our lips together. But we go no further than that. One reason is that we have not had the time for anything more. There is also the practical matter that we can't find an appropriate place for a deeper kind of intimacy. But more than that, probably, the larger reason is that the two of us are so absorbed in talking, just by ourselves, so intent on making the most of our time, that conversation takes precedence over everything else. Neither you nor I had ever met anyone before with whom we could speak so freely and naturally, saying exactly what we felt and thought. To come across someone like that feels almost like a miracle. So whenever we see each other, once or twice a month, we simply talk, forgetting the passage of time. No matter how long we talk, we never run out of things to say, and when the time comes to part, when we say goodbye at the station ticket gate, it always feels as if we have left so many important things unsaid.

Of course, it is not as if I feel no physical desire. A healthy seventeen-year-old boy standing in front of a sixteen-year-old girl with the beautiful swell of her breasts, especially when he has his arms around her supple body, cannot help feeling sexual desire. But instinctively I feel that such things can wait until later. For now, what I need is to see you once or twice a month, take long walks together, and talk frankly about all sorts of things. To exchange the details of our lives intimately, and come to know each other more deeply. And to hold each other in the shade of some tree somewhere, and press our lips together—in such lovely hours, I do not want to hurriedly bring in any other element. If I did, something important there might be damaged, and we might never be able to go back to what we had before. The physical side can wait until later. That is what I think. Or perhaps that is what my intuition tells me.

But what on earth did we talk about there, with our foreheads pressed together? I can't remember now. Perhaps we told each other so much that I can no longer separate one topic from another. But once you began telling me about that special city enclosed by high walls, it came to occupy the greater part of our conversations.

For the most part it went like this: you'd explain how the city was put together, I'd ask practical questions about it, and with you supplying the answers, the city's concrete details would be decided and written down. The city was something you had made in the first place. Or else something that had long existed inside you. But in bringing it forth as something visible, something that could be described in words, I think I helped more than a little. You talked, and I wrote it down, just as ancient philosophers and religious figures had faithful, painstaking recorders behind them, or people called apostles. As a capable scribe, or a faithful apostle, I even made a small notebook just for recording it. That summer, the two of us were utterly absorbed in that shared work.

In autumn, the beasts' bodies are covered with shining golden fur in preparation for the cold season to come. The single horn on each brow is sharp and white. They wash their hooves in the cool river water, gently stretch out their necks, gorge on red berries, and chew the leaves of broom.

It was a beautiful season.

I stand in the watchtower built into the wall and await the evening horn. A little before sunset, the horn is sounded once long and three times short. That is the rule. Its soft note slips through the cobbled streets as dusk deepens. The sound of the horn has probably been repeated unchanged for hundreds of years, or perhaps longer still. Its tone has seeped deep into the gaps in the stone walls of the houses, and into the stone statues lined along the hedges of the square.

When the horn sounds through the city, the beasts lift their heads toward some ancient memory. One stops chewing leaves, one stops tapping its hooves on the pavement, one wakes from a nap in the last patch of sunlight, and each raises its head to the same angle.

For an instant everything is fixed, like statues. The only thing that moves is their soft golden fur stirring in the wind. Even so, what are they looking at? With their necks turned in one direction and their gaze fixed on empty air, the beasts do not stir. And so they strain their ears to the resonance of the horn.

When the horn's last note is swallowed by the air and disappears, they rise, drawing their forelegs together, or stretch and straighten themselves, and at almost the same moment begin to walk. The brief spell is lifted, and for a while afterward the city's streets are ruled by the sound of their hooves drumming on the stones.

The line of beasts moves through the winding cobbled streets. None takes the lead; none guides the file. With their eyes lowered, their shoulders swaying slightly from side to side, they simply make their way down a river of silence. And yet between each beast and the next there seems to be an intricate bond, too palpable to deny.

After watching them several times, I begin to see that the route they take and the pace they keep are fixed with strict precision. Picking up more of their companions here and there along the way, they cross the gently arched Old Bridge and walk to the square with the sharp steeple (the clock in the tower there has, just as you said, lost both its hands). There they take in a small group that had come down onto the island in the river to graze on the green grass. Then they proceed upstream along the riverside road, pass through the factory district by way of the dry canal that runs north, and gather up a group that had been searching for nuts in the forest. After that they turn west, go beneath the foundry's roofed passageway, and climb the long staircase that runs along the northern hill.

There is only one gate in the wall that surrounds the city. Opening and closing it is the Gatekeeper's duty. It is a heavy, solid-looking gate, plated over with thick iron sheets laid crosswise and lengthwise. Yet the Gatekeeper opens and shuts it with ease. No one but him is permitted to lay a hand on it.

The Gatekeeper is a big man, clearly built for strength, yet intensely faithful to his duty. His pointed head is cleanly shaved, and his face is smooth as well. Every morning he boils water in a large pot and carefully shaves his head and his face with a large, keen razor. There is no telling his age. Blowing the horn morning and evening to gather the beasts is one of his duties as well. He climbs the tower in front of the Gatekeeper's hut, some two meters high, and sounds the horn toward the sky. From what part of this rough, almost uncouth-looking man does so soft, mellow a sound emerge? Each time I hear the horn, I find myself wondering.

At dusk, once he has sent every last beast outside the wall, he pushes the heavy gate shut once more, then lowers the great padlock into place. With a dry, chilly clack.

Outside the north gate there is a place for the beasts. There they sleep, mate, and bear their young. There are woods and thickets there, and a small river runs through it. That place too is enclosed by a wall. It is a low wall, only a little more than a meter high, yet for some reason the beasts cannot cross it. Or perhaps they do not try to.

On the wall to either side of the gate, six watchtowers have been built. Anyone can climb them by old wooden spiral staircases. From the watchtowers, the whole place where the beasts live lies spread out below. But ordinarily no one goes up there. The people of the city seem to take no interest at all in the lives of the beasts.

But during that one week at the beginning of spring, people willingly climb the watchtowers on the wall to watch the beasts fight savagely, or so I am told. At that time of year the beasts grow more savage than their usual bearing would ever suggest, and the males, vying for the females, forget even to eat and fight with every ounce of strength they have. Growling, they try to drive the sharp points of their single horns into a rival's throat or belly.

During that one week of the mating season, the beasts do not enter the city. The Gatekeeper closes the gate so that no danger will come to the people of the city (and accordingly, during that period the horn is not sounded morning or evening). A good many beasts suffer grave wounds in the fighting, and some lose their lives. And out of the red blood spilled on the ground, a new order and a new life are born. Just as the green branches of the willow all burst into bud at the start of spring.

The beasts live within a cycle and an order of their own, one we have no way of fathoming. Everything repeats itself with regularity, and that order is paid for with their own blood. When that savage week has passed and the soft rains of April wash away the spilled blood, the beasts return once more to their former tranquil and gentle selves.

Yet I have never actually witnessed such a scene with my own eyes. I only heard about it from you.

In autumn the beasts remain crouched in their separate places, their golden coats shining in the evening sun, waiting in silence for the horn's echo to be swallowed by the sky. There are probably no fewer than a thousand of them.

That is how a day in the city ends. Days pass, and the seasons turn. Yet days and seasons are only provisional things. The city's true time lies elsewhere.







Neither of us visits the other's home. We never meet each other's families, and we never introduce each other to our friends. Basically, we don't want anyone—anyone in the whole world—getting in the way. You and I are perfectly content just spending time together, and we don't want to add anything else. Besides, even in purely physical terms, there isn't room for anything more. As I said before, there is a mountain of things we need to talk about, and the time we can spend together is limited.

You hardly ever talk about your family. What I know about them amounts to no more than a handful of disconnected facts. Your father used to work for the local government, but when you were eleven some sort of "misstep" forced him to resign, and now he works as an office clerk at a cram school. I don't know what kind of "misstep" it was. But apparently it was the sort of thing you had no wish to speak about. Your own mother died of an internal cancer when you were three. So you have almost no memory of her. You can't even remember her face. When you were five your father remarried, and the following year your younger sister was born. So the mother you have now is your stepmother, but once, only once, you said something to the effect that, compared with your father, she might be someone you could still feel at least a little warmth toward. Like a discreet note printed in tiny type in the corner of a page. About your sister, who is six years younger than you, I never learned anything beyond this: she is allergic to cat hair, so your family can't keep a cat.

As a child, the only person you are able to feel naturally and wholeheartedly close to is your maternal grandmother. Given the chance, you ride the train alone to visit her house in the next ward. During school vacations, you sometimes stay with her for a few days. She dotes on you unconditionally. Out of her meager income she buys you little things as well. But each time you go to see her, you notice the look of displeasure that rises to your stepmother's face. She never actually says anything, but little by little you stop going so often to your grandmother's house. That grandmother too died suddenly of heart disease a few years ago.

You tell me these things in scattered fragments, a little at a time. As if you were scooping some tattered thing bit by bit from the pocket of an old coat.

One more thing I still remember clearly—whenever you talked to me about your family, for some reason you always stared fixedly at the palm of your hand. As if, to follow the thread of what you were telling me, it were absolutely necessary to read the lines in your palm, or whatever they were, with painstaking care.

As for me, there is almost nothing worth telling you about my own family. My parents are perfectly ordinary parents. My father works for a pharmaceutical company, and my mother is a housewife. They behave like ordinary parents and talk like ordinary parents. We keep an elderly black cat. There is nothing in particular worth telling about my life at school, either. My grades aren't bad, but they're not so good that they draw anyone's attention. The place at school where I feel most at ease is the library. I like sitting there alone, reading books and letting time pass in my own imaginings. I have already read most of the books I want to read in the school library.

I remember clearly the first time I met you. It was at the awards ceremony for the High School Essay Contest. The top five winners had been invited. You and I had come in third and fourth, and our seats were next to each other. It was autumn. I was in my second year of high school then, and you were still a first-year. The ceremony was a tedious affair, so in between we talked a little, in low voices, in short bursts. You were wearing your school uniform: a navy blazer and a matching navy pleated skirt. A white blouse with a ribbon, white socks, black slip-on shoes. The socks were impeccably white, and the shoes were polished so clean there was not a spot on them, as if seven kindly dwarfs had carefully polished them before dawn.

I'm not especially good at writing. I've loved reading ever since I was little, and whenever I had free time I always picked up a book, but I never thought I had any talent for writing myself. Still, everyone in my class was made to write an essay during Japanese class for the contest, and out of all those essays mine was chosen and sent to the judging committee, survived the final round, and then, to my complete surprise, ended up placing near the top. To be honest, I couldn't understand what was supposed to be so remarkable about what I'd written. Even reading it over again, it seemed like nothing more than an ordinary composition with no particular merit. But if several judges had read it and decided it deserved a prize, then presumably there was something in it worth noticing. My homeroom teacher, a woman, was delighted that I'd won a prize. Never once in my life had a teacher responded so favorably to anything I had done. So I decided not to say anything unnecessary and accepted the prize with gratitude.

The essay contest is held district-wide every autumn, with a different theme each year, and that year's theme was "My Friend." Unfortunately I couldn't think of a single "friend" I wanted to devote five sheets of four-hundred-character manuscript paper to, so I wrote about the cat we kept at home. I wrote about how that old she-cat and I got along, lived together, and communicated our feelings to each other — though of course there were limits to that. I had plenty to say about that cat. She was a very intelligent cat, and full of character. There were probably a few cat lovers among the judges. Most cat lovers naturally feel sympathy and affection toward other cat lovers.

You wrote about your maternal grandmother. About the closeness between a lonely old woman and a lonely girl. About the small, honest sense of what mattered that took shape there. It is a charming, moving essay. Several times better than anything I wrote. I tell you honestly that I can't understand why mine came in third and yours fourth. You smile and say that, on the contrary, you think what I wrote is several times better than what you wrote. "Really," you add. "I'm not just saying that."

"Your cat sounds wonderful."

"Yes, she's a very smart cat," I say.

You smile.

"Do you have a cat?" I ask.

You shake your head. "My younger sister is allergic to cat hair."

That was the first small piece of personal information I learned about you. Your younger sister was allergic to cat hair.




You are a very beautiful girl. At least that's how you look to me. You are small, with a rather round face, and your fingers are slender and beautiful. Your hair is short, and your neatly cut black bangs hang over your forehead. Like a carefully chosen shadow. Your nose is straight and small, and your eyes are very large. By ordinary standards of facial proportion, your nose and eyes might be said not to balance each other, but for some reason it is precisely that lack of balance that draws me. Your pale pink lips are small and thin, and always dutifully closed. As if they were hiding several important secrets behind them.

We five prizewinners go up onstage one by one and solemnly receive our certificates and commemorative medals. The tall girl who won first prize gives a brief acceptance speech. We also receive a fountain pen as a prize (a fountain-pen manufacturer is sponsoring the contest. I go on to use that pen for many years). A little before the long, tedious award ceremony ends, I write my name and address with a ballpoint pen on a memo page in my notebook, tear it out, and secretly hand it to you.

"If you'd like, maybe you could write me a letter sometime," I say, my voice dry.

I don't normally do anything so bold. I'm shy by nature (and of course timid as well). But when I think that we may part there and never see each other again, it feels like something terribly wrong, something wholly unfair. So I gather up my courage and make a bold move.

You take the slip of paper with a slightly surprised look, fold it neatly into quarters, and tuck it into the breast pocket of your blazer. Above the gentle, mysterious swell of your chest. Then you touch your bangs and blush a little.





"I want to read more of what you write," I say. Like someone who has opened the door to the wrong room and is making a clumsy excuse.

"I would very much like to read the letter you write too," you say, and give several small nods, as if to encourage me.

A week later your letter reaches me. It is a lovely letter. I read it over at least twenty times. Then I sit down at my desk and, with the new fountain pen I have only just been given as part of the prize, write a long reply. That is how we begin corresponding, and how we begin a relationship that belongs to us alone.

Were we lovers? Is it right to call us that so readily? I don't know. But at least during that time, for nearly a year, you and I had our hearts bound together as one, pure and undivided. And before long we created and came to share a special secret world of our own — the strange city surrounded by the high wall.

It was on the evening of my third day in the city that I pushed open the door of that building.

It was an old stone building with nothing especially distinctive about it. It stood a short walk east along the road by the river, beyond the central plaza facing the Old Bridge. No sign was posted at the entrance, and anyone who didn't know would have had no way of telling it was a library. There was only a brass plate engraved with the number 16, affixed there without ceremony. The plate was tarnished, and the numerals were hard to read.

The heavy wooden door opened inward with a deep creak, and beyond it was a dim square room. There was no one there. The ceiling was high, the light from the wall lamps meager, and the air smelled of someone's dried sweat. It was the sort of dimness in which everything seemed faintly blurred, as though it might come apart into molecules and be drawn off somewhere. The worn cedar floorboards let out sharp creaks here and there as I walked across them. There were two tall narrow windows, and no furniture at all.

At the far end of the room, directly ahead, was a door. It was a plain wooden door with a small frosted pane at about eye level, and on it too the number 16 was written in an old-fashioned ornamental script. A faint light showed beyond the frosted glass. I knocked lightly twice and waited, but there was no answer. I heard no footsteps either. After a short pause I steadied my breathing, turned the tarnished brass knob, and gently pushed the door open. The door let out a creak, as though warning everything around it: Someone has come.

Beyond the door was another room, also perfectly square, about five meters to a side. The ceiling was not as high as in the room I had just left. And here too there was no one. There were no windows, only plaster walls on all sides. No paintings, no photographs, no posters, no calendars, and of course no clock. Nothing but blank, bare walls. There was a rough wooden bench, two small chairs, a table, and a wooden coat rack. No coat hung from it. In the center stood an old-fashioned wood-burning stove, its surface spotted with rust. A bright red fire burned inside, and on top of it a large black kettle sent up steam. At the far end was what looked like a lending counter, and on it a ledger lay open, as if someone had been in the middle of working there when some urgent business called them away. Whoever that someone was -- presumably the librarian -- would no doubt return before long.

Beyond the counter was a dark-colored door that looked as though it led to the stacks. If so, then this was indeed the library. Not a single book was in sight, and yet the place still retained the unmistakable air of a library. That special air libraries all over the world possess, whether large or small, old or new.

I took off my heavy coat and hung it on the rack, sat down on the hard wooden bench, and warmed my hands at the stove while I waited for someone to appear. There was no sound at all. It was a silence like being at the bottom of deep water. Once, just to see, I cleared my throat, but it did not sound like I was clearing my throat.

Some fifteen minutes later, you opened the door leading to the stacks and appeared from inside (I think it was about that long. There was no clock, so I could not know the exact time). When you saw me sitting on the bench, your body stiffened for an instant and your eyes opened wide. Then you let out one slow breath and said, "I'm sorry to have kept you waiting. I didn't realize anyone was here."

I could not find the words I ought to say, and only nodded several times in silence. Your voice did not sound like your voice. It was different from the voice I remembered. Or perhaps in this room all sounds and voices took on a different resonance.

At that moment the lid of the kettle suddenly began to rattle, giving a small shiver like an animal waking.

"What can I help you with?" you asked.

I was looking for Old Dreams.

"Old Dreams," you said, pressing your small thin lips into a straight line as you looked at me. Of course you did not remember me.

"But as you know," you said, "only Dream Readers are permitted to touch Old Dreams."

Without a word, I took off my dark green glasses and lifted my eyelids for you to see. They were unmistakably the eyes of a Dream Reader. Eyes that could not go out into the bright light of day.

"I understand. You are qualified," you said, lowering your eyes a little. Perhaps the sight of my eyes had unsettled you. But it could not be helped. To enter this city, I had to alter them in just that way.

"Will you be starting work today?" you asked.

I nodded. "I still do not know whether I will be able to read them properly, but I have to get used to it, even if only little by little."

Not a sound stirred in the room. The kettle too was silent again now. After excusing yourself, you quickly finished the ledger work you were in the middle of. I watched you from the bench. Outwardly, you had not changed at all. You were exactly as you had been on that summer evening. I remembered the bright red sandals you were wearing. And the grasshopper that suddenly sprang up from the grass nearby.

"Have I met you somewhere before?" I asked before I could stop myself, though I knew it was a useless question.

You raised your eyes from the ledger and studied my face for a moment, a pencil still in your left hand--yes, you were left-handed, in this city as well as in the city that was not here--then shook your head.

"No, I do not believe we have," you replied. You answered in that polite tone probably because you were still sixteen, while I was no longer seventeen. To you I was now a much older man. It could not be helped, and yet the passage of time pierced my heart.

When you finished the record-keeping, you closed the ledger, put it away on the shelf behind you, and made herbal tea for me. You took the kettle from the stove and carefully mixed the hot water with crushed herbs, making a deep green tea. Then you poured it into a large ceramic cup and set it before me. It was a special drink prepared for the Dream Reader, and getting it ready was one of your duties.

I took my time drinking the herbal tea. It had a thick, distinctive bitterness and was not easy to drink. But the nourishment in it soothed my still-injured eyes and calmed my mind. That was what this special drink was for. You watched me from across the table. You were probably anxious about whether I liked the herbal tea you had made. I gave you a small nod, as if to say it was all right. At that, a relieved smile came to your lips. It was a long-missed smile. I had not seen it in a very long time.

The room was warm and quiet. Even without a clock, time passed in silence. Like a slender cat padding along the top of a wall without a sound.

We didn't exchange letters all that often. About once every two weeks, give or take. But each one was fairly long. And on the whole, I think your letters were a little longer than mine. Not that their length meant much in our correspondence.

I still have every single letter you wrote to me, but because I never bothered to make copies of the ones I wrote, I can't remember very clearly what I said in them, at least not in any concrete way. Still, I can't have written anything very important. Mostly I wrote about my daily life and the small things that happened around me. I wrote about the books I read, the music I listened to, the films I saw. I wrote about what happened at school too. Since I was on the swim team (only because circumstances more or less forced me into it, and I could hardly be called a dedicated athlete), I think I wrote about practice as well. When I was writing to you, the words came naturally, whatever the subject. I could put what I thought and felt into words exactly as I meant to, with an ease that felt almost mysterious. It was the first time in my life I had ever been able to write with that kind of ease. As I said before, until then I had thought I was no good at writing. You must have been the one who drew that ability out of some hidden place deep inside me. You always appreciated the little bits of humor in what I wrote. "That's probably what my life lacks most," you said.

"Like some vitamin or other?" I said.

"Yes. Like some vitamin or other," you said with an emphatic nod.

I was crazy about you, and when I was awake I was almost always thinking about you. Probably in my dreams too. But in my letters I tried to keep myself in check, as much as I could, so I wouldn't come right out and confess those feelings. Instead I made up my mind to write, whenever possible, only about practical, concrete things. Back then I probably wanted to cling to a world my own hands could actually touch—with a bit of humor folded in, if possible. Because I felt that if I started writing openly about things like love or romance, about what you might call the inward stirrings of the heart, I'd only drive myself deeper and deeper into a blind alley.

Unlike mine, your letters contained less about the concrete details of everyday life than about inward thoughts and feelings. Or dreams you'd had, or little pieces of fiction. Some of those dream stories, in particular, have stayed vividly with me. You often had long dreams, and you could remember them clearly down to the last detail, as if you were recalling something that had actually happened. I found that hard to believe. I myself hardly ever dream, and even when I do I can almost never remember what was in them. The moment I wake in the morning, they all come apart and get sucked away somewhere. Even when a vivid dream startles me awake in the middle of the night, which rarely happens, I soon fall right back asleep, and when I wake the next morning I don't remember a thing.

When I say this, you say:

"In my case, I keep a notebook and pencil by my pillow, and the moment I wake up I write down the dreams I had that night. Even when I'm busy, even when I'm pressed for time. Especially when I have a particularly vivid dream and wake in the middle of the night, no matter how sleepy I am I write down as much as I can, right there, in as much detail as possible. Dreams like that are often important, and they teach me many important things."

"Many important things?" I ask.

"Things about the me I don't know," you answer.

For you, dreams stood on almost the same level as events that actually took place in the real world. They were not the sort of thing that could easily be forgotten or simply vanish. Dreams were like a precious wellspring of the heart, telling you many things.

"That's the result of training. If you make the effort, I'm sure you'll be able to remember your dreams right down to the smallest detail. So try. I really want to know what kind of dreams you have."

"All right," I said. "I'll try."

But although I made a certain effort—without going so far as to keep a notebook and pencil by my pillow—I just couldn't take any interest in the dreams I had. My dreams were too scattered and incoherent, and for the most part impossible to make sense of. The words spoken there were indistinct, and the scenes I saw had hardly anything like a plot. Sometimes, too, they contained disturbing things I couldn't possibly tell anyone about. Rather than have anything to do with such dreams, I preferred to listen closely as you told me about your long, colorful dreams.

From time to time I appeared in your dreams. That made me very happy. Whatever form it took, I was able to take part in the imagined world inside you. And you too seemed happy when I turned up in your dreams. More often than not, though, the me in your dreams had no particular importance, and was cast in no more than a supporting role in the drama.

Do you never have dreams too explicit to talk about in front of me—the kind I so often have myself (dreams that sometimes, against my will, leave a stain in my underwear)? Do you really tell me honestly about every dream you have? Those are the questions I always find myself asking as I listen to you talk about your dreams.

You seem to speak frankly, holding nothing back. But no one can know what is really true. I think everyone in this world carries secrets deep inside. To survive in this world, people need them.

Isn't that true?


"If anything in this world is perfect, it's this wall. Nobody gets over it. Nobody breaks it," the Gatekeeper declared.


At first glance, the wall looked like nothing more than an old brick wall. It seemed as though the next strong storm or earthquake could bring it down with ease. How could he call such a thing perfect? When I said that, the Gatekeeper looked as if someone had spoken ill of his family for no reason at all. Then he took me by the elbow and led me over to the wall.

"Get up close. Have a good look. No mortar between the bricks. And every brick's a bit different in shape. And every last one fits so tight you couldn't slip a hair between them."

It was just as he said.

"Go on, scratch a brick with this knife," the Gatekeeper said, taking a work knife from the pocket of his jacket, snapping the blade open with a click, and handing it to me. At first glance it looked old, but the blade had been honed with great care. "Won't leave a scratch."

He was right. The knife tip made nothing but a dry scraping sound, and not a single white line appeared on the brick.

"See? Storms, earthquakes, cannons—nothing brings this wall down. Nothing even marks it. Never could, never will."

As though he were posing for a commemorative photograph, he kept his palm pressed against the wall, drew in his chin, and looked at me proudly.

No, I murmured inwardly, there is no such thing as perfection in this world. Whatever has a form, whatever shape it may take, is bound to have some weakness, some blind spot, somewhere. But I did not say it aloud.

"Who built this wall?" I asked.

"Nobody built it." The Gatekeeper's conviction was unshakable. "Been here from the start."

Before the first week was over, I took up several of the old dreams you had chosen for me and tried to read them. But those old dreams told me nothing meaningful. All I heard were the vaguest mutterings, and all I saw were disconnected images, blurred and out of focus. It was like listening to a tape pieced together at random from scraps, or being shown a film running backward.

In the library stacks, instead of books, countless old dreams line the shelves. Apparently no one had touched them for many years, and each wore a faint coating of white dust on its surface. Old dreams are shaped like eggs, and no two are alike in size or color. They look like eggs laid by all sorts of animals. But strictly speaking, they are not quite egg-shaped. When you take one in your hand and look at it closely, you see that the lower half swells out more than the upper. Its weight, too, is oddly balanced. Yet it is because of that very distortion that it sits so securely, and even without any support it will not roll off the shelf.

The surface is hard, like marble, and smooth to the touch. But it does not have marble's weight. What sort of material it is made of, or how much strength it possesses, I cannot tell. Would it break if it fell to the floor? In any case, it has to be handled with the greatest care. As one would handle the eggs of some rare creature.

There is not a single book in the library—not one. Once, no doubt, books filled the shelves there, and the people of the city came here in search of knowledge and pleasure. As they would in any ordinary city library. A faint afterscent of that atmosphere still hung in the air. But at some point all the books were removed from the shelves, and old dreams were set there in their place.


There seems to be no Dream Reader here besides me. At least for the present, I appear to be the only Dream Reader in this city. Was there another Dream Reader before me? There may have been. Judging from how carefully the rules and procedures concerning Dream Readers have been devised and maintained, there probably was.

Your duty at the library is to safeguard the old dreams arranged there and keep them properly in order. You choose which dreams are to be read, and record in the ledger that they have been read. Before dusk you open the library door, light the lamps, and in the cold season set the stove going. You make sure there is always enough rapeseed oil and firewood for that. And for the Dream Reader—that is, for me—you prepare a dark green herbal tea. It soothes my eyes and calms my mind.

With a large white cloth, you carefully wipe away the white dust that has gathered on an old dream and place it on the desk before me. I remove my green glasses and place both hands on the surface of the old dream. I cup it in my palms. After five minutes or so, the old dream gradually wakes from its deep sleep, and its surface begins to glow faintly. I feel a pleasant, natural warmth in my palms. Then they begin to spin out the dream. Like cocoons spinning silk, tentatively at first, then before long with a certain eagerness. They have something to tell. Perhaps they have been waiting patiently there on the shelf for the time to come out of their shells.

But their voices are so faint that I cannot fully make out the words. The images they project never quite take shape; they simply fade, crumble, and are absorbed into the air. Or perhaps it is not their fault. Perhaps my new eyes are not yet functioning properly. Perhaps my powers of understanding as a Dream Reader are not yet fully formed.

And then the time comes when the library ought to be closed. There is no clock anywhere, but when that hour draws near, you simply know.

"How is it? Is your work progressing smoothly?"

"Little by little," I reply. "But reading even one leaves me quite exhausted. I may be doing something wrong."

"There is no need to worry," you say, turning the knob and closing the stove's air intake. You blow out the lamps one by one, sit down across the table from me, and, looking straight at my face, say (when you look at me so directly, I become flustered), "There is no need to hurry. There is plenty of time here."

Following the prescribed procedure exactly, one step at a time, you close the library. Your expression serious, you work without haste, with steady calm. As far as I can tell, the order of the tasks never changes. Watching you work, I find myself wondering whether it is really necessary to lock up the library with such precision. In this hushed, peaceful city, who would break into the library in the middle of the night to steal or destroy the old dreams?

"Would it be all right if I walked you home?" On the night of the third day, when we step outside the building, I summon my courage and ask.

You turn and look at my face, your eyes wide. In those dark eyes, a single star from the sky gleams white. You do not seem quite able to grasp the meaning of my offer. Why should you need me to walk you home?
