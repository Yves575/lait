I

I had no belongings, not even a coat. They said none of it was necessary. They let me keep my black sweater: it would do. The shirt I fought for. I said I would give it up gradually. Right there in the passageway, under the belly of the ship, where we stood being jostled, Abs held out his hand to me with a knowing smile:

— Just be careful...

I remembered that too. I did not crush his fingers. I was quite calm. He wanted to say something more. I spared him that by turning away as if I had noticed nothing and went up the steps inside. A stewardess led me between the rows of seats to the very front.

I did not want a compartment to myself. I wondered whether they had warned her. The seat unfolded without a sound. She adjusted the back, smiled at me, and went away. I sat down. The cushions were fathomlessly soft, as everywhere. The backs were so high I could barely see the other passengers. By now I no longer resisted the bright colors of women's clothes, but I still suspected the men, absurd as it was, of being in costume, and still had a quiet hope that I would see some dressed normally — a pathetic reflex. People settled quickly; no one had luggage.

Not even briefcases or bundles. The women had none either. There seemed to be more of them. In front of me — two dark-skinned women in parrot-colored furs, bristling like plumage; evidently bird fashions were in.

Farther on, a married couple with a child. After the glaring selenophores of the platforms and tunnels, after the unbearably shrill, self-luminous vegetation of the streets, the light of the concave ceiling was hardly more than a glow. I put my hands on my knees, because somehow they were in the way. Everyone was seated now. Eight rows of gray seats, a breath of fir, the hush of conversations dying away. I was waiting for an announcement of departure, some signal, an order to fasten seat belts, but nothing came. Across the matte ceiling indistinct shadows began to run from front to back, as though they were silhouettes of birds cut from paper. What the devil was it with these birds — I thought helplessly. — Maybe it meant something? I had gone stiff from concentrating so hard not to do anything wrong.

Like this for four days now. From the first moment. I kept falling behind everything that was happening, and the constant effort to understand even the most casual conversation, any situation at all, turned that tension into a feeling nastily like despair. I was convinced the others felt the same, but we never spoke of it, not even when we were alone. We only joked about our brawn, about all that extra strength still left in us; besides, you really had to watch yourself — at first, when I tried to get up, I would spring toward the ceiling, and everything I picked up seemed made of paper, hollow. But I learned to control my own body quickly.

When I greeted people, I no longer crushed anyone's hand. That was simple. Unfortunately, the least important.

My neighbor on the left, a thickset, tanned man with eyes that shone too brightly (perhaps from contact lenses), suddenly vanished, because his seat swelled out at the sides, and they rose and joined overhead, forming something like an egg-shaped cocoon. A few other people vanished into cabins like that. They resembled swollen sarcophagi. What did they do in them? But I kept running into things like that and tried not to stare, unless they had to do with me directly. A curious thing — I was rather indifferent to the people who gaped at us once they learned what sort we were. Their stupefaction meant little to me, though I saw at once that there was not a trace of admiration in it. It was rather those who looked after us — the employees of Adapt — who aroused my dislike. Most of all, probably, Dr. Abs, because he treated me as a doctor treats an abnormal patient, pretending, and quite well too, that he was dealing with someone perfectly ordinary. When that was no longer possible — he would quip. I had had enough of his directness and joviality. Asked about it — or so I thought — any passerby would have taken me or Olaf for someone like himself; it was not so much we ourselves who were uncanny to him as our past fate: that was what was extraordinary. Dr. Abs, on the other hand, like every employee of Adapt, knew better — that we really were different. That difference was no privilege, only an obstacle to understanding, to the simplest exchange of words — never mind words! — to opening a door, since door handles had ceased to exist fifty or sixty years earlier, I no longer remembered which.

The launch came unexpectedly. Gravity did not change by a hair, no sound penetrated the sealed interior, shadows drifted evenly across the ceiling — perhaps years of routine, some old instinct, told me at one point that we were in space, for it was certainty, not conjecture.

But something else occupied me. I lay half reclined, legs stretched out, motionless. They had let me have my way too easily. Even Oswamm had not opposed my decision very strongly. The counterarguments I heard from him and from Abs could not have convinced — I myself could have come up with better ones. They insisted on only one thing: that each of us had to fly separately. Not even that I had made Olaf rebel (because if it had not been for me, he would certainly have agreed to stay longer) did they hold against me. That was puzzling. I had expected complications, something that at the last moment would frustrate my plan, but nothing happened, and here I was, flying. This last journey was to end in fifteen minutes.

Evidently, neither what I had thought up nor the way I had stood before them to wrest an early departure had surprised them. They must have had that kind of reaction on file already; it was the stereotype of behavior proper to would-be heroes like me, listed in their psychotechnical tables under the appropriate serial number. They had let me fly — why? Because experience told them I would not manage? But how could that be, when this whole "independent" escapade amounted to nothing more than a flight from one station to another, where someone from Adapt on Earth was supposed to be waiting for me, and all I had to do was find that man at the appointed place?

Something had happened. Raised voices reached me. I leaned out of my seat. A few rows ahead of me, a woman shoved the stewardess, who moved backward between the seats with a slow, automatic motion, as though under the influence of that push — not so hard a shove, after all — and the woman repeated: — I won't allow it! Don't let that touch me! —

I could not see the face of the woman who was shouting. Her companion was tugging at her arm, saying something to her soothingly. What did this scene mean? The other passengers paid no attention. Once again an incredible sense of estrangement came over me. I looked up at the stewardess, who had stopped beside me and was smiling as before. It was not the purely outward smile of obligatory courtesy, concealing agitation over the incident. She was not pretending to be calm. She really was calm.

— Will you have something to drink? prum, extran, morr, cider? A melodious voice. I shook my head.

I wanted to say something pleasant to her, but all I could manage was a stock question:

— When do we land?

— In six minutes. Will you have something to eat? There is no need to hurry. You may stay on after landing.

— Thank you, no.

She moved away. In the air, just before my face, against the back of the seat in front, the word STRATO lit up, as if written with the tip of a glowing cigarette. I bent forward to see how the word had appeared, and started. The backrest followed my back and clasped it with a springy give. I already knew that the furnishings anticipated every change of position, yet I kept forgetting it. It was unpleasant — as though someone were following my every move.

I wanted to get back to my previous position — too energetically, apparently. The seat misunderstood me and almost unfolded like a bed. I jerked upright. What idiocy! More self-control.

I sat down at last. The pink letters of STRATO quivered and flowed into others: TERMINAL. No jolt, no warning, no whistle. Nothing. A distant call rang out, like a post-horn; four oval doors at the ends of the aisles between the seats opened wide, and into the cabin burst a hollow, all-encompassing roar, as of the sea. The voices of the passengers getting up drowned in it without a trace. I remained seated while they went out; rows of silhouettes flickered against the outside lights, green, lilac, purple — a veritable masked ball. Everyone was gone now. I stood up. Automatically I tugged down my sweater. Foolish, somehow — empty-handed like that. A cooler draft came through the open doors. I turned. The stewardess stood by the partition, her back not touching it. The same cheerful smile still dwelt on her face, turned toward the empty rows of seats, which now began slowly to curl up and fold, like fleshy flowers, some faster, some a little more slowly — the only movement in that long-drawn roar filling everything, flowing through the oval openings, bringing the open sea to mind. "I don't want t h a t touching me!" Suddenly I found something wrong in her smile. From the doorway I said:

"Goodbye..."

"At your service."

The meaning of those words, so peculiar on the lips of a young, pretty woman, did not register at once, because they reached me when I had already turned and was leaning out through the doorway. I wanted to set my foot on the step, but there was none. Between the metal hull and the edge of the platform, a meter-wide gap yawned. Losing my balance, unprepared for such a trap, I made an awkward leap and, already in midair, felt something catch me as if from below — a current of invisible force — so that I floated across the void and was set down softly on the white surface, which yielded elastically. I must have — flying — looked rather foolish — I felt a few amused glances, or so at least it seemed to me, turned back quickly and walked along the platform. The projectile in which I had arrived lay in a deep cradle, separated from the platform edge by a yawning, unguarded gap. As if by accident I moved closer to that emptiness and again felt the invisible springiness that would not let me step beyond the white edge. I wanted to look for the source of that peculiar force, but suddenly, as if I had come to: I was on Earth.

The wave of people swept me along: jostled, I moved forward in the crush. It was a moment before I really took in the vastness of the hall. Was it even a single hall? No walls: white, gleaming, arrested at the height — a burst of unbelievable wings, and between them columns built not of any material, but of dizzying motion. Rushing upward, immense fountains of liquid denser than water, lit from within by colored spotlights? No; vertical glass tunnels, up which processions of blurred vehicles shot? I no longer knew anything. Continually shoved and buffeted in the swarming haste of the crowds, I tried to force my way into a bit of empty space, but there was no empty space here. A head taller than those around me, I could see the now-empty projectile receding — no, it was we who were gliding forward with the whole platform. From on high, lights gushed forth, and in them the crowd sparkled and shimmered. Now the surface on which we stood packed together began to move upward, and far below I saw the double white strips, already distant, crammed with people, with black gaps yawning along the inert hulls — for there were dozens of ships like ours — the moving platform curved, accelerated, passed under higher levels. Flapping, tossing the hair of those standing with the gale they whipped up, over them flew — as over impossible, because stripped of all support, viaducts — rounded shadows quivering with speed, with the fires of their signal lights drawn out into streaks; then the surface carrying us began to divide, to fork along imperceptible seams, and my lane slid through interiors full of people standing and sitting, surrounded by a multitude of tiny flashes, as though they were setting off colored fireworks.

I did not know where to look. Ahead of me stood a man in something as fluffy as fur, which opalesced like metal when the light touched it. He had a woman in scarlet on his arm. What she had on was covered in great eyes, like a peacock's; those eyes blinked. It was no illusion; the eyes on her dress really did open and close. The walkway on which I stood behind the two of them, among ten or so other people, was still accelerating. Between planes of smoky-white glazing, colored, lit passageways opened, their transparent ceilings ceaselessly trodden by hundreds of feet on the next level up; the all-enveloping din now swelled, now tightened as thousands of human voices and sounds, unintelligible to me, meaningful to them, were choked by yet another tunnel on this journey headed for who knew where; deeper in, farther back, the surroundings were continually stitched by the passing streaks of vehicles — what kind, I could not tell — perhaps flying ones — because at times they slanted upward or downward, drilling into space, so that instinctively I expected some dreadful collision, for I could see no guideways, no rails, if these were overhead railways. When those blurred hurricanes of speed let up even for a moment, behind them huge platforms full of people emerged with majestic slowness, like flying piers, moving in different directions, passing one another, rising, seeming to pass through one another by a trick of perspective. It was hard to rest one's gaze on anything still, because all the surrounding architectonics seemed composed almost entirely of motion, of change, and even what I had first taken for a winged roof was only tiers suspended overhead, which now gave way to others, higher still. All at once, filtered through the glazing of the ceilings, through those enigmatic columns, reflected from silver surfaces, entered every fold of space, the interiors of the conduits we passed, the features of human faces — a heavy, purple dawn, as though somewhere far away, in the heart of that structure miles across, an atomic fire had flared.

The green of the ceaselessly leaping neon went muddy, the milk of the parabolic buttresses flushed pink. There was something of a portent of catastrophe in that sudden suffusion of the air with rust-red — so I took it — yet no one paid the slightest attention to the change, and I could not even have said when it passed.

At the edges of our walkway spinning green circles kept appearing, like neon hoops suspended in midair; then some of the people stepped onto the approaching branch of another walkway or ramp; I noticed that one could pass through the green lines of those lights with impunity, as though they had no substance.

For a while I let the white walkway carry me like that, unresisting, until it occurred to me that perhaps I was already beyond the station and this incredible landscape of warped glazing, forever seeming to wrench itself into flight, was the city itself, and the one I had left existed only in my memory.

— Excuse me — I touched the shoulder of the man in fur — where are we?...

They both looked at me. Their faces, as they looked up, took on an expression of surprise.

I had a faint hope it was only because of my height.

"On the polyduct," said the man. "What's your junction?"

I did not understand a thing.

"Are we… still at the station?"

"Of course…" he replied with some reluctance.

"And… where is the Inner Circle?"

"You have already lost it. You will have to double back."

"The rast from Meridu will be better," the woman put in. All the eyes of her dress seemed to stare at me with suspicious astonishment.

"Rast?…" I repeated helplessly.

— Oh, there — she pointed out an empty rise visible through a green ring drifting toward us, its black-and-silver sides striped, like the hull of some oddly painted ship lying on its side. I thanked her and stepped off the walkway, probably in the wrong place, because the momentum almost swept my legs out from under me. I caught my balance, but it spun me around so that I did not know which way to go. I wondered what to do, but in the meantime my transfer point had moved far away from that black-and-silver rise the woman had pointed out, and I could no longer find it. Since most of those standing near me were stepping onto an incline leading upward, I did the same. Already on it I caught sight of an enormous sign, burning motionless in the air: DUCT CENTR — the rest of the letters on either side eluded the eye — through their sheer size. Soundlessly it carried me onto a platform perhaps a kilometer long, from which a spindle-shaped ship was just pushing off, showing as it rose its underside pocked with lights. Or perhaps that whale-like shape itself was the platform, and I had found myself on a "rast" — there was no one even to ask, for all around me there was emptiness. I must have come to the wrong place. Part of my "platform" was taken up by shallow compartments without front walls. When I drew nearer, I saw something like low, dimly lit bays in which black machines rested in rows. I took them for cars. But when the two nearest slid out and, before I could step back, swept past me, instantly gathering great speed, I saw, before they vanished into the perspective of the parabolic slopes, that they had no wheels, windows, or doors, and were streamlined, like enormous black drops. Cars or not — I thought — at any rate, perhaps this was some kind of parking area? Maybe for those very "rasts"? I decided the best thing would be to wait till someone came and ride with him, or at least find something out. Yet my platform, raised lightly like the wing of an impossible airplane, remained empty; only the black machines slipped out, singly or several at once, from their metal burrows and raced off, always in one direction. I went right to the very edge of the platform, until again I felt that invisible, springy force that kept me safe. It really hung in the air, supported by nothing. Lifting my head, I saw many more like it, gliding just as motionlessly in space with their great lights extinguished; on others they were burning, where ships were docking. But they were not rockets, nor even projectiles like the one that had brought me from Luna.

I stood there a long time, until I noticed, against the background of more halls — I did not know, for that matter, whether they were a mirror image of this one or reality — fiery letters gliding steadily through the air: SOAMO SOAMO SOAMO, a pause, a blue flash, and NEONAX NEONAX NEONAX; perhaps they were station names, perhaps the names of advertised products. They told me nothing.

It was high time I found that fellow, I thought; I turned on my heel and, finding a moving walkway running the other way, rode it down. It turned out not to be the same level, not even the same hall from which I had come up: I knew it from the absence of those enormous columns. Then again, perhaps they had gone off somewhere; by now I was ready to believe anything.

I found myself among a whole forest of fountains; farther on I came into a white-and-pink hall full of women.

As I passed, I idly stuck my hand into the stream of an illuminated fountain, perhaps because it was pleasant to encounter something at least a little homelike. But I felt nothing; the fountain had no water. A moment later I thought I smelled flowers. I raised my hand to my nostrils. It smelled like a thousand bars of toilet soap at once. Reflexively I began wiping it on my trousers. I was already standing before that hall full of women, nothing but women. It did not look to me like the entrance to the toilets, but then again I could not be sure. I preferred not to ask, so I turned back. A young man, dressed as though spreading mercury had frozen on him, puffed out at the shoulders — or perhaps rather foaming there? — tight over the hips, was talking to a fair-haired girl leaning back against the bowl of a fountain. The girl, in a light dress, quite ordinary, which heartened me, held a bunch of pale-pink flowers and, burying her face in them, smiled at the boy with her eyes alone. At the last moment, when I had come up beside them and was already opening my mouth, I saw that she was eating the flowers — and my voice refused to obey me for an instant. She calmly chewed the delicate petals. She looked up at me. Her eyes froze. But I was used to that by now. I asked where the Inner Circle was.

The boy seemed to me unpleasantly surprised, even angry, that anyone should dare interrupt them when they were alone together. Evidently I had committed some impropriety. He looked first upward, then lowered his eyes, as though expecting to discover some stilts as the reason for my height. He did not even say a word.

— Oh, there — cried the girl — rast for wuk, your rast, you'll make it, quick! I set off running in the direction she had shown me, without even knowing where I was going — after all, I still had no idea what that damned rast looked like — after ten steps I saw a silvery funnel descending from above, the base of one of those enormous columns that had astonished me before — could they be flying columns? — people were hurrying there from all sides, when all at once I collided with someone. I did not even stagger, I merely stood as though rooted to the spot, and the other man, a squat gentleman in orange, fell, and something unbelievable happened to him: his fur wilted before my eyes, collapsing like a punctured balloon! I stood over him, dumbfounded, unable to stammer out an apology. He got up, glowered at me from under his brows, but said nothing, turned away and strode off, fiddling at his chest — and his outfit filled out and lit up again...

There was no one left where the girl had pointed. After that adventure I gave up once and for all on trying to find rasts, the Inner Circle, ducts, the junction, and decided to get out of the station. What I had been through so far did not encourage me to accost passersby, so I rode upward at random, following a slanting blue arrow and passing bodily, hardly surprised now, through two more signs blazing in the air: LOCAL CIRCUITS. I came onto an escalator, fairly crowded. The next level was done in muted bronze, veined with golden exclamation marks. Smooth downward curves of ceilings and concave walls. Ceilingless corridors, their upper reaches as though sunk in luminous down. As though I were approaching some kind of living quarters, the place had something of a whole system of gigantic hotel lobbies about it — service windows, nickel pipes along the walls, alcoves with some clerks, perhaps exchange offices, perhaps a post office — I kept walking. I was already almost certain that I would not reach an exit this way and — I judged by how long I had been riding upward — that I was in the elevated part of the station, and yet I kept on in the same direction. Unexpected emptiness, panels of raspberry cladding with sparkling stars, rows of doors. The nearest were ajar. I looked inside. At that very same moment some big, broad-shouldered man did the same from the opposite side. Myself in a full-wall mirror. I opened the door wider. Porcelain, silver pipes, nickel. Toilets.

I felt a little like laughing, but on the whole I was more dazed than anything. I turned back quickly, another corridor, bands white as milk flowing downward. The escalator handrail was soft, warm; I did not count the floors slipping away, there were more and more people, they stopped at enamel boxes sprouting from the wall at every step, one touch of a finger and something dropped into the hand, they put it into their pockets and went on. I do not know why I did exactly what the man ahead of me in loose purple clothing did; a button with a little hollow for the fingertip, a press, and straight into my cupped hand fell a colored, semi-transparent tube, as though heated.

I shook it, brought it up to my eyes, some kind of pills? No. A stopper? It had no stopper, no closure of any kind. What was it for? What did the others do? Put it into their pockets. The sign on the automat: LAHGAN. I stood there; people jostled me. And suddenly I seemed to myself a monkey handed a fountain pen or a lighter; for a fraction of a second blind fury seized me; I clenched my jaw, narrowed my eyes and, slightly hunched, joined the stream of people. The corridor widened; it was a hall now. Letters of fire: REAL AMMO REAL AMMO.

Through the current of hurrying people, over their heads, I caught sight of a window in the distance. The first window.

Panoramic, enormous.

As if all the firmaments of night had been flung flat. To the very horizon, out of a luminous haze — colored galaxies of plazas, clusters of spiral lights, glowing hazes above the skyscrapers, streets: the crawling, wormlike movement of beads of light, and above it, rising sheer, the churning of neon, plumes and lightning bolts, wheels, airplanes and bottles of fire, red dandelions of signal lights on spires, momentary suns and hemorrhaging advertisements, mechanically violent. I stood and stared, hearing behind me the steady murmur of hundreds of feet. Suddenly the city vanished and an enormous face appeared, three meters high. — We have aired a set of chronicles from the seventies in the series "Visions of the Old Capitals."

Transtel now shifts its reach to the cosmolites' studios...

I almost fled. It wasn't a window. Some sort of television set. I quickened my step. I was sweating slightly.

Down. Faster. Golden squares of light. Inside, crowds, foam on glasses, an almost black liquid, not beer, because of its poisonous greenish sheen, and young people, boys and girls, arms around one another, in sixes and eights, blocking the whole width of the passageways, coming at me; they had to tear their linked arms apart to let me through. Something wrenched through me. Before I knew it I had stepped onto a moving walkway. Astonished eyes flashed past, very close — a beautiful dark girl in something that gleamed on her like phosphorescent metal. The fabric clung to her: she was as good as naked. Faces white, yellow, a few tall black people, but I was still the tallest. The crowd parted before me. Overhead, behind convex panes, scattered shadows raced past, invisible orchestras played, and down here a strange promenade went on, in the dark passageways — headless figures of women; the down covering their shoulders shone so that only their protruding necks gleamed within it like white, strange stalks, and a scattered glow in their hair — self-luminous powder? A narrow passage led me into an enfilade of grotesque—because moving, even bustling—statues; something like a broad street with raised sides thundered with laughter, people were enjoying themselves, what amused them so — those sculptures?

Huge figures in cones of spotlights; ruby, honey-colored light poured from them, thick as syrup, with an extraordinary concentration of color. I walked on, my will gone, squinting, losing myself.

A steep green arcade, grotesque pavilions, pagodas reached by little bridges, any number of little eateries, the smell of fried food, sharp, insistent, rows of gas flames behind glass, the clatter of glass, repetitive, metallic, incomprehensible sounds. The crowd that had borne me here collided with another, then it grew looser, everyone was boarding a car open from end to end, no, it was only transparent, as though cast from glass, even the seats were like glass, though soft. Before I knew it I was inside—we were moving. The car hurtled on, people shouting over the loudspeaker repeating: "Meridional level, Meridional level, junctions for Spiro, Blekkr Frosom," the whole car seemed to be melting, pierced by shafts of light, the walls flying past in streaks of flame and color, parabolic arches, white platforms, "Forteran, Forteran, Galee junctions, junctions for the outer rasts, Makra"—muttered the loudspeaker, the car stopped and hurtled on again; I discovered something astonishing: you felt no braking or acceleration, as though inertia had been abolished. How was that possible? I tested it, bending my knees slightly, at the next three stops. Nothing on the curves either. People got out, got in, on the front platform stood a woman with a dog, I had never seen one like it, it was enormous, with a head like a ball, very ugly, its calm hazel eyes reflecting backward-racing, diminished garlands of lights. RAMBRENT RAMBRENT. A fluttering burst from white and bluish fluorescent tubes, stairs of crystalline brightness, black facades, the glow slowly turned to stone, the car stood still. I got out and stood rooted. Above the stop's sunken, amphitheater-like disc rose a familiar multilevel structure; I was still in the station, in another part of the same gigantic hall, distended by the white sweeps of its planes. I headed for the edge of that geometrically exact hollow—the car had already gone—and experienced another astonishment: I was not at the bottom, as it had seemed to me, I was actually high up, some forty stories above the ribbons of walkways visible in the abyss, above the silver decks of platforms still gliding with measured regularity, and between them long, silent hulks were slipping in and people were coming out of them through rows of hatches, as though those monsters, those chrome-plated fish, were depositing, at regular intervals, masses of black and colored roe. Over all of it, far away, through the haze of distance, I saw golden words moving as if along an invisible line:

GLENIANA ROON RETURNING TODAY WITH A RECORDING OF A MIMORPHIC REAL PAYS TRIBUTE IN AN ORATORIO TO THE MEMORY OF RAPPER KERX POLITRA. TERMINAL DAILY REPORTS: TODAY IN AMMONLEE PETIFARGUE ACHIEVED SYSTOLIZATION OF THE FIRST ENZOME, WE WILL BROADCAST THE VOICE OF THE EMINENT GRAVISTICIST AT THE TWENTY-SEVENTH HOUR. ADVANTAGE ARRAKER. ARRAKER REPEATED HIS SUCCESS AS THE SEASON'S FIRST OBLITERAUT AT TRANSVAAL STADIUM.

I walked away. So even the reckoning of time had changed. Struck by the light of gigantic letters that raced above the sea of heads like ranks of burning tightrope walkers, the metallic fabrics of women's dresses flashed with sudden little flames. I walked on without knowing it, and something kept repeating inside me: so even time had changed. That all but finished me. I saw nothing with open eyes. I wanted only one thing: to get out of there, get clear of that hellish station, find myself under the open sky, out in the open, see the stars, feel the wind.

An avenue of elongated lights drew me on; something was writing itself in the translucent stone of the ceiling -- the letters were traced by a sharp flame enclosed in alabaster -- TELETRANS TELEPORT TELETHON; through doors framed by a pointed arch (but it was some impossible arch, wrenched out of shape, like the negative of a rocket's nosecone) I entered a hall roofed with frozen golden fire. In the niches of the walls were hundreds of booths; people ran into them, spilled out in haste, flung torn strips onto the floor, not telegraph tape, something else, embossed with tiny nodules; others trampled those shreds underfoot. I wanted to get out; by mistake I stepped into a dark space, before I could back out something buzzed, a flash, as if from a camera bulb, and from a slit rimmed with metal, like a mail slot, a small folded sheet of glossy paper slid out. I took it, opened it, and a human head emerged from it, with thin lips not quite closed, slightly crooked, looking at me through narrowed eyes: it was me! I folded the paper in half and the solid apparition vanished. I slowly parted the edges -- nothing; wider, and it appeared again, as if it had jumped out of nowhere, a head severed from the body, suspended above the sheet of paper, with a none-too-bright expression. For a moment I stared at my own face -- what was it, a three-dimensional photograph?

I slipped the sheet into my pocket and went out. The golden hell seemed to be descending onto the heads of the crowd, a ceiling of fiery magma, unreal, yet belching real fire, and nobody looked at it; engrossed, they ran from one booth to the next, green letters leaped in the depths, columns of figures descended along narrow screens, other booths with shutters instead of doors shooting up in an instant as someone approached; at last I found the exit.

A curving corridor with a sloping floor, as sometimes in a theater; stylized conches bloomed from the walls, and overhead the words INFOR INFOR INFOR raced on without end.

I had first seen an infor on Luna and taken it for an artificial flower.

I brought my face close to the celadon calyx, which at once, before my lips even parted, froze in expectation.

-- Which way can I get out? -- I asked, half dazed.

-- Where to? -- the warm alto replied at once.

-- To the city.

-- Which district?

-- Doesn't matter.

-- Which level?

-- Doesn't matter, I want out of the station!

-- Meridional, rasts: one hundred six, one hundred seventeen zero eight, zero two. Triduct, levels AF, AG, myths circuit level, twelve and sixteen, the nadir level serves every meridional direction. Central level -- gliders, red local, white long-distance, A, B and W. Ulder level, direct, all escales from the third up... a woman's voice chanted in singsong.

I wanted to wrench the microphone from the wall -- it leaned toward my face with such care.

I walked away. Idiot! Idiot! -- ground away inside me with every step. EX EX EX EX -- the sign gliding overhead kept repeating, rimmed with lemon mist. Could that be exit? A way out?

A huge sign. EXOTAL. I was caught in a violent current of warm air that set my trouser legs flapping. I found myself under the open sky. But the blackness of night fled far into space, driven back by a swarm of lights. An immense restaurant -- tables whose tops glowed in different colors, faces above them lit from below and therefore a little eerie, full of deep shadows. Low armchairs, black liquid with green foam in glasses, lanterns showering tiny sparks -- no, fireflies rather, like swarms of burning moths. The chaos of lights extinguished the stars. When I raised my head, I saw only black emptiness. And yet, astonishingly, at that moment its blind presence filled me with something like comfort.

I stood and looked. Someone brushed past me in the aisle, I caught the scent of perfume, sharp and mild at once, a couple went by, the girl turned to the man, her shoulders and breasts submerged in a fluffy cloud, she slipped into his arms, they danced. They're still dancing — I thought — that's something, at least. The couple took a few steps, a pale, mercury-colored circle lifted them with the other couples, their dark-red shadows moving beneath its huge plate, slowly spinning like a disc; it was supported by nothing, it had not even an axle, it revolved to the music, suspended in the air. I went on between the tables. The soft plastic underfoot ended, and my feet touched rough rock. Through a curtain of light I went inside and found myself in a rocky grotto. As though ten or fifty Gothic naves had been raised out of stalactites, veined deposits of pearly minerals embraced the mouths of caves, people sat in them, their legs dangling into emptiness, unsteady little flames burning between their knees, and below there spread the serenely black sheet of an underground lake, reflecting the converging rock walls. Out there too, on rafts knocked together any which way, people reclined, all facing one direction. I went down to the very water's edge and saw, on the far side, on the sand, a dancer. She seemed naked to me, but the whiteness of her body was unnatural. With small, wavering steps she ran down toward the water; when she was reflected in it, she suddenly spread her arms, bowed her head — that was the end, yet no one applauded, the dancer remained motionless for a few seconds, then went slowly along the shore, tracing the uneven line of the bank. She was perhaps thirty paces from me when something happened to her. Just a moment before I could still see her smiling, exhausted face; suddenly it was as if something had veiled her, her figure shuddered and vanished.

Would you care for a plava? — a courteous voice sounded behind me. I turned around — no one, only a streamlined table striding on comically bent little legs; as it moved, the glasses of sparkling liquid standing in rows on side trays trembled — one arm was politely pressing the drink on me, the other was already reaching for a plate, with a hole for a finger, like a small concave palette — it was an automaton, I could see behind the little central pane the smoldering glow of its transistorized heart.

I brushed past those obsequiously outstretched insect arms, laden with delicacies I had scorned, and hurried out of the artificial grotto, my jaws clenched as though some incomprehensible insult had been offered me. I crossed the full width of the terrace, between the S-shaped rows of tables, beneath avenues of lanterns, showered with the weightless dust of collapsing, dying fireflies, black and gold. At the very edge, rimmed with stone, old stone as if dimmed by yellowish lichen, at last a real wind blew over me, clean and cool. An empty table stood nearby. I sat down awkwardly, with my back to the others, looking into the night. Below, a shapeless, unexpected darkness spread out; only far away, very far away at its edges, thin wavering lights smoldered, strangely uncertain, as though it were not electricity, and farther still rapiers of light rose into the sky, cold and slender; I could not tell whether they were houses or some kind of pylons, I would have taken them for sheaves of searchlight beams if not for the delicate lattice that marked them, as though a glass cylinder had been driven base-first into the earth, reaching the clouds, filled alternately with concave and convex lenses. They must have been unbelievably high; around them a few lights drizzled, pulsing so that now a wreath of orange, now one almost white, encircled them. That was all, that was what the city looked like; I tried to find the streets, to guess at them, but that dark, as though lifeless expanse below spread away on every side, brightened not by so much as a spark.

"Kol?..." I heard the word, perhaps not for the first time, but at first I didn't take it as meant for me. Before I had properly turned, the chair swung me around. A girl stood before me, perhaps twenty, in blue clinging to her as though it had set there; her shoulders and breasts were lost in dark-blue down that grew more transparent lower down. Her slender, beautiful belly was like a sculpture in breathing metal. In her ears she wore something luminous, so large it covered the outer ears; her lips were small, smiling uncertainly — painted, the nostrils too red inside — I noticed that most women wore themselves just that way. She took the back of the chair opposite me in both hands and said, "What's up, kol!"

She sat down. I had the impression she was a little drunk.

"Boring here," she went on after a moment. "No? Wanna take off somewhere, kol?"

"I'm not kol..." I began. She leaned her elbows on the table and moved her hand over the half-filled glass until the end of the gold chain wound around her fingers dipped into the liquid. She bent closer and closer as she did it. I could feel her breath. If she was drunk, it wasn't from alcohol.

"How's that?" she said. "You are. You've got to be. Everyone's kol. Want to? Take off?" At least I would find out what it meant.

"All right," I said.

She stood up. I stood up too from that horribly low chair.

"How do you do that?" she asked.

"What?"

She looked at my legs.

"I thought you were standing on tiptoe..."

I smiled without speaking. She came up to me, took my arm, and was surprised again.

"What've you got there?"

"Where, here? Nothing."

"No way," she said, and tugged me lightly. We went between the tables.

She led me toward a dark-gold wall, where a sign glowed, somewhat like a treble clef. When we were close enough, the wall opened. I felt a blast of hot air.

A narrow silver escalator flowed downward. We stood side by side. She didn't reach my shoulder.

She had a catlike skull, black hair with a blue sheen, a profile perhaps too sharp, but she was pretty. Only those scarlet nostrils... She gripped me hard with her thin hand, her green nails digging into the thick fabric of my sweater. I smiled involuntarily, just with the corners of my mouth, at the thought of where that sweater had been until now and how little it had had to do with women's fingers.

Beneath a round vault that breathed with lights -- from pink into carmine, from carmine into pink -- we came out onto the street. That is, I thought it was a street, but the darkness above us kept brightening, as from an instant dawn. Farther on, long, low shapes glided past, like cars, but I already knew there were no cars. It had to be something else. If I had been alone, I would have gone down that broad artery, because in the distance the letters TO CENTER were glowing, but that surely did not mean the city center at all. Besides, I let myself be led. However this adventure was going to end, I had found a guide, and I thought -- this time without anger -- of the poor devil who, now, three hours after my arrival, must already have been searching for me on every infor in this station-city.

We passed several places emptying out, display windows in which groups of mannequins kept acting out the same scene over and over, and I would gladly have stopped to watch what they were doing, but the girl walked quickly, tapping along in her little shoes, until at sight of a neon face with pulsing blushes that kept licking its lips with a comically stuck-out tongue, she cried:

"Oh, bonses! Want a bons?"

"Do you?" I asked.

"I think so."

We went into a small gleaming room. Instead of a ceiling it had long rows of tiny blazing flames, as if gas-fed; heat rushed down from above -- perhaps it really was gas. In the walls were small alcoves with counters; when we came up to one, seats slid out from the walls on both sides, and it looked as though they had first sprouted from them unopened, like buds, flattened out in the air and, cupping inward, gone still.

We sat down facing each other; the girl tapped a metal plate in the tabletop with two fingers, a little nickel paw sprang out of the wall, threw a small plate down in front of each of us, and with two lightning-fast movements flung onto both a portion of whitish mass, which foamed, browned, and set, while the plate itself darkened at the same time. Then the girl rolled it up—it was not a plate at all—like a crepe and began to eat.

“Oh,” she said with her mouth full, “I didn’t even know how hungry I was!”

I did exactly as she did. A bons tasted unlike anything I had ever eaten. It crackled under my teeth like a fresh-baked roll, but at once crumbled and melted on my tongue; the brown mass inside was sharply seasoned.

I thought I would like bonses.

“Another?” I asked when she had finished hers. She smiled and shook her head.

On the way out, in the passage, she put both hands for a moment into a small tiled niche—something hummed in it. I imitated her. A tickling gale swept over my fingers; when I drew them out, they were already dry and clean. Then we went up on a broad escalator. I did not know whether this was still the station, but I preferred not to ask. She led me into a little booth in the wall—it was dim in there, and I had the impression that some trains were passing overhead, because the floor was trembling. For a fraction of a second it went dark, something breathed deeply beneath us, as if a metal monster had let the air out of its lungs, it brightened, the girl pushed the door open. I suppose it really was a street. We were quite alone on it.

Small clipped shrubs grew on both sides of the sidewalk; a little farther on, flat black machines stood crowded together, a man came out of the shadow and ducked behind one; I did not see him open any door, he simply vanished all at once, and the machine shot off with such force that it must have flattened him into the seat; I saw no buildings; only a roadway smooth as a table, covered with bands of matte metal; at the intersections slit-like orange and red lights moved, suspended above the paving; they looked a little like models of wartime searchlights.

“So where do we take ourselves?” the girl asked. She was still holding my arm. She slowed down. A red streak passed across her face.

“Wherever you want.”

“Then let’s go to my place. No point taking a glider. It’s close.”

We went on. There were still no buildings in sight, and the wind, running out of the darkness from beyond the shrubs, made it seem as though open space stretched out here. Around the station, in the very center? That struck me as strange. It carried a faint scent of flowers, which I drew greedily into my nostrils. Bird cherry? No, it was not bird cherry.
