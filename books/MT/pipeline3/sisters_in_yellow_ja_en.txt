Chapter One: Reunion




1




I had thought that no matter where I ended up living, no matter how old I got, no matter what became of me, I would never forget her.

And yet, until the moment just now when I saw her name in a small article I had happened upon online, it had never occurred to me that I had forgotten all of it: not only that thought, but her name, her very existence, the time we had spent together, and what we had done there.

Kimiko Yoshikawa.

The thought crossed my mind for an instant that it might be someone else with the same name, but I knew instinctively that the person written about in this article was that Kimiko-san.



〈On December 23, the first hearing was held at Tokyo District Court for Kimiko Yoshikawa, 60, unemployed, of Shinjuku, Tokyo, who was charged with assault, intimidation, and unlawful confinement for allegedly confining a woman in her twenties from Ichikawa, Chiba Prefecture, in an apartment in Shinjuku for one year and three months from May of last year, assaulting her, and inflicting serious injuries. At the arraignment, Yoshikawa said, "I will remain silent," and the defense argued she was not guilty.

According to the indictment, from around February 2018 Yoshikawa is alleged to have confined the woman she was living with in a room of an apartment in Shinjuku for one year and three months, assaulted her, and inflicted serious injuries that took one month to heal.

In its opening statement, the prosecution said the victim, who had no fixed address in 2017, began living in Yoshikawa's home. At first they lived together without problems, but Yoshikawa began controlling the victim's belongings and relationships and monitoring her movements. By repeatedly threatening her with statements such as, "Even if you go outside, you can't survive anyway," the prosecution said, she instilled fear in the woman and caused her to lose the will to escape. It also pointed out that Yoshikawa assaulted her multiple times, unlawfully confined her, and kept her under her control, making her obey as she wished. The case came to light after the woman escaped on her own and reported it.〉




I read the article three times through, then let out a breath like a lump from deep in my chest. My heart was pounding hard enough that I could feel a faint tremor in my fingertips. It was Kimiko-san. There was no doubt. That Kimiko-san had been arrested.

When I searched Kimiko Yoshikawa's name, all that came up was one other article with similar content and another report just a few lines long. Both were small items. Other than that there were only pages about name fortune-telling, stroke-count numerology, and recommended names for girls. It seemed there was nothing on the internet about Kimiko-san herself apart from the case I had just seen.

I tried to sort out what I should think about, and where I should start. Then I went back to the first article and checked the date again.

The article had been published on January 10, 2020. That was from about three months earlier. And it said the incident had happened the year before, in May 2019.

But no matter how carefully I read, I couldn't quite grasp what those dates were supposed to mean. I understood that three months had passed since the first hearing, but I couldn't tell what sort of state the victim and the other people involved were in now, or what would happen next with the case and the trial. How was Kimiko now? What was going to happen to her? Where was I supposed to look to find out those things?

I couldn't imagine at all how questioning worked, or the order of things and the rules in a detention center. All I could bring to mind were the flimsy images I'd seen in dramas and news programs: a bleak little gray room, handcuffs, an expressionless judge, portraits drawn by a courtroom sketch artist.

And Kimiko's face.

Kimiko, whom I had lived with for several years when I was still young, about twenty years ago now. The article said she was sixty. Kimiko was sixty? I couldn't believe it. If I thought about it ordinarily, of course it made sense: that much time had really passed, and I myself was already forty. Even so, the age printed under Kimiko Yoshikawa's name simply didn't feel like a real number.





I closed my eyes and told myself I was all right. It went without saying, but I had nothing to do with this case. There was nothing to worry about. For twenty years I hadn't known what Kimiko was doing, I hadn't been in touch with her, and in no sense were we connected anymore. A long time had passed since then, everything was over, and it had ended. So far, other than this case, other than the confinement incident Kimiko was said to have caused last year, nothing had become any kind of public issue. At least, nothing had appeared online. It's all right, I told myself over and over.

When I lifted my face from the phone screen, the room was full of the dark blue of dusk, which until just a moment before hadn't even been there as a presence, and the shadows of everything had deepened. On the low table was a plate of spaghetti with instant meat sauce that I had served up, meaning to eat it. But with the feel of night drawing a little closer bit by bit, for some reason it no longer looked like food.





That night I woke over and over, and morning came before I had slept much at all.

The curtains in the spring morning light made me think of a huge sheet of pure white drawing paper. When I closed my eyes against the brightness, different colors kept bleeding and fading away: dark blue, deep red, yellow - and then Kimiko's face rose in my mind.

Gathering up her mass of pitch-black, unruly hair, which reached to about the middle of her back, Kimiko Yoshikawa said, laughing brightly, See? I’ve got so much hair you probably wouldn’t even notice if there were a whole black cat curled up in it. I laughed too, and everyone laughed. An old house. The rooms were all small, crowded with things and messy, but the entryway was always clean. We were only allowed two pairs of shoes each, and there was a rule that because the entryway was where good fortune came in and the toilet was where bad things went out, both always had to be kept clean.

I closed my eyes and turned over in bed, trying to brush away the things that were rising in my mind. But all sorts of things I could have sworn I had long since forgotten came to me one after another, as if hand in hand. The creaking of the hallway, sagging in places, became our laughter, and the grain of the wood on the ceiling, which I used to stare at for so long before falling asleep, turned into someone’s cigarette smoke, as if it were whispering to me.

The cosmetics left scattered in front of the mirror, the clothes and underwear stuffed into the cubby shelves in the closet, and the cup noodles piled up in baskets in the tiny kitchen all came back to me. They brought back the very smell of those days we had lived there.

After lying there for about half an hour, wrapped in my futon on the bed and worrying over it, I sent a message to the LINE group for work.

<Good morning, this is Itō. I’ve had a slight cough since yesterday. I don’t have a fever, but just to be safe, I’d appreciate it if I could take the day off today. Sorry to ask, and thank you.>

A reply came back right away from the person in charge of the part-timers’ schedule.

<Understood. We should have our policy going forward decided by the start of next week, so I’ll let you know. Given the situation, I’ll report it to head office too, just in case. Take care!>

<Thank you very much! I think it may just be cold symptoms, but if I do develop a fever or anything like that, I’ll contact you immediately. Thank you again.>

Up until the middle of last month, there was still, somewhere, a general feeling of being only half-convinced about the disease. People made all sorts of fuss about it, but in the end it was just the same as influenza, so there was no need to be that afraid, and plenty of people were saying masks were pointless. There was a floating feeling, as though anxiety and an odd excitement had mixed together, but even so, the feeling was still that it was within the normal range of everyday life.

But the reports from overseas gradually grew more frightening, and from around the end of last month into the beginning of this one, rumors started to spread that Japan, too, might finally go into lockdown.

Then, five days earlier, the state of emergency had been declared, and the tension that had been creeping higher and higher until then burst all at once. It wasn’t only in the news: there was actual panic-buying at the supermarket in my neighborhood, masks and hand sanitizer and toilet paper vanished wholesale from the drugstores, people disappeared too, and at my part-time job we ended up scrambling to keep up. I worked as a sales clerk at a prepared-food shop in a shopping street within walking distance of my apartment, part of a major supermarket chain with branches all over Tokyo.




It was a small shop, with about thirty plates of side dishes and salads lined up on counters and in a refrigerated display case, and customers would choose what they wanted from them and we would pack it into bento containers. All we did was sell what was delivered ready-made each morning from the central kitchen, so there wasn’t even a kitchen, and if there were four people in the shop it already felt full. In the three years since I started working there part-time, it had been the same menu over and over, and there were also a lot of regulars who came every day, so I used to wonder if they never got sick of it, but maybe that was exactly what made it reassuring, because business was good and there was always a line at lunchtime and in the evening. But from the end of last month, even our customers finally dropped off sharply, and things visibly went out of whack. When someone did come in, customers would get into arguments with each other over whether to wear a mask, and we started getting complaint calls saying our infection-control measures weren’t enough.

Lying there, I thought maybe it hadn’t been a good idea to tell them on Line earlier that I had a cough. Why had I lied and said I was coughing? Why had I gone and said, at a time like this, that I had a cough? What had I been trying to do? I didn’t know myself.




After a while I picked up my phone, went to the site with Kimiko-san’s article, and read it again from the beginning, slowly, taking my time. Each time my mood darkened again, and my arms and legs felt heavy. And whatever reason I had given, I still thought it was better that I had taken the day off. Even if all I had to do was stand there, there was no way I could have worked in that state.

I got out of bed, took some barley tea from the refrigerator and drank it. Then I took a box down from the closet shelf.

It was a slightly oversized shoebox with crushed corners and a lid that was a little torn, and inside were things like old letters, planners, and notebooks.

The shoebox, worn and so faded it no longer looked like the deep navy blue it had originally been, had once held a pair of high heels my mother bought somewhere long ago. I remembered her putting on those pure white heels in the room and showing them off so happily that, maybe because she was so happy, she sat down on the tatami with them still on and ate instant ramen. I asked my mother for the empty box, and decided to keep my stickers in it, and the little freebies that came with manga magazines, and small notes I traded with friends at school. After that, too, it became a habit to put into it whatever, at that particular moment, I felt I ought to keep. After moving from place to place, leaving things behind and losing things along the way, before I knew it this shoebox alone was what remained. But I normally never looked inside it, or even picked up the box itself. Looking at it now, even though it was mine, something I had somehow never thrown away and had carried with me all this way no matter what happened, I felt as if I were looking at someone's things after they had died.

When I lifted the lid, I saw, in one corner, a small navy flip phone and its charger. I had been the one looking for it, and even so, the moment I found it my heart gave a jolt. I plugged the charger into the phone, not knowing whether it would still work, connected it to the outlet, waited about thirty minutes, and then held down the power button. The little screen slowly brightened, as though breathing back to life, and a ringtone sounded.

Even though I had changed my number and cut everything off so no one could reach me, had I kept the numbers of people I knew back then without deleting them because, somewhere unconsciously, I had sensed that one day I would need them like this?

There were only seventeen contacts in the address book.

In the K section, I could see the characters for Kimiko. I selected the name a little above it, Ran Katō, and brought up the number. Then I went to M. Momoko. Momoko Tamamori. I copied both of their numbers into the notes on the phone I used now.

I had no idea where Ran Katō and Momoko Tamamori were now, or what had become of them.

The last time I saw them was when they left that house. We were twenty, or thereabouts. I had not been in touch with either Ran or Momoko since then. If I had not happened to come across that article about Kimiko yesterday, I might never have thought of them again.

The time all of us spent together in that house came back to me like disconnected images spliced together.

It would suddenly sharpen, then blur again, over and over, as different voices and expressions played back. I did not think these numbers would still work now, and I did not want to contact either of them after all this time. But the only people I could talk to about Kimiko were Ran and Momoko. The only people who could share the unease I was carrying now were those two.

I was afraid that Kimiko might have spoken about the past we had with her.

During questioning, they might have found all kinds of evidence in Kimiko's room from when we were living together, and maybe an investigation had in fact already started under the surface. When I thought that, I felt like I couldn't sit still. No one had come to me yet, but it was possible that Ran or Momoko had been contacted first, called in, and already questioned about things.

If I thought about it calmly, what we'd done might be past the statute of limitations. Maybe we wouldn't be charged with anything that serious. Ran and Momoko and I had all still been young, and we were doing what Kimiko told us to do. But then what about Kotomi? In the end, whose fault was it that Kotomi died, and why did she end up dying? Could we really say it had nothing to do with us?

The more I thought about it, the more an anxiety I couldn't tell apart from fear pressed down on me. I felt a dread as if some huge iron plate were silently crushing my chest, and tears welled in my eyes. What should I do? Should I act as if I hadn't seen the article, not contact Ran or Momoko, and keep quiet? Or should I tell the police what I knew?





My imagination kept swelling toward worse and worse places, until it blocked my vision. What were the two of them doing? Where were they now, what were they doing? Of course the calls wouldn't go through. But if they weren't going to go through anyway, wouldn't it be better to at least try? I set the phone beside me and pulled the futon over my head, as if to blot everything out. The daylight was cut off, and for a while I kept blinking there in the lukewarm spring darkness. Then, just like that, I slept a little.

I had an elusive dream where the only thing I knew was that it was a nightmare. Nothing concrete happened, no people appeared, there was nothing there at all, only time, and yet why was it that with nightmares you could always tell they were nightmares? Dark and merciless as a wave, the nightmare kept bearing down on me without end. When I woke up, my chest and back were drenched in sweat. Then I called Ran Katō's number.





After six rings, I heard a bright voice say, Hello. I could feel my lower jaw trembling with tension.

"Excuse me, um, is this... Ran Katō's number?"

"Yes."

Ran answered, her voice a shade lower. It was Ran. My heart gave a hard thud.

"Um, it's Hana."

"Hana?"

"Yes, um, my name's Hana Itō, and a long time ago, we..."

"Hana," Ran said after a short silence. "...That Hana?"

"Yeah, it's Hana. Sorry, out of nowhere." I shifted the phone to my other hand and pressed it tight to my ear. "I really didn't think it'd go through. Sorry, out of nowhere."

"...What is it? How'd you get my number?"

"It was still saved in my old phone."

"Oh."





I heard Ran let out a small sigh.

"I’m really sorry for surprising you out of nowhere."

"No, that’s fine, but... it just threw me a little. I mean, it was so long ago."

"Yeah, I know. Sorry. The reason I called is, actually, it’s about Kimiko."

Behind Ran, I could hear children making a happy racket. Mixed in with it were women’s voices. Then it all grew a little fainter, and I knew Ran had moved somewhere else.

"...Kimiko? You mean that Kimiko?"

"Yeah."

"What about her?"

"Yesterday, I found something about her."

"What?"

"I found it online."

"What do you mean, something?"

"Kimiko got arrested. I was shocked too. The trial’s already started, and then, maybe, I mean maybe, I thought it might have something to do with us too, and so I wanted to talk to you, Ran."

"Wait a second," Ran cut across me. "What are you talking about? I don’t get any of this. Why was Kimiko arrested? What do you mean, it might have something to do with us too? Did Kimiko say something?"

"No, it’s not that, they said Kimiko kept a girl locked up in her apartment and hurt her, and that’s why she was arrested. Probably... she was doing the same kind of thing she did back then, and that’s why she got arrested. Maybe what happened in the past will become a problem too, and all kinds of things might, you know, come out. I really don’t know at all, but it scares me."

I decided to ask her outright.

"...Nothing’s come your way, Ran? From the police, or anywhere like that?"

"As if it would," Ran said with a laugh, like the idea was ridiculous, but beneath it I could sense the faintest trace of unease.





"I’ve been anxious ever since yesterday, and I’ve been thinking maybe I should go to the police and tell them."

"What?" Ran asked, startled. "Tell them what?"

"About back then, or what I know about Kimiko."

"C’mon, quit joking," Ran said in a lowered voice, but her tone was strong. Just then someone called out to her, and Ran answered in a bright voice, "Okay."

"Listen, I’ve got people over right now."

"Yeah, right. Sorry."

"Hana, where do you live now? Tokyo?"

"Yeah."

"Maybe we should meet and talk. With COVID and all, but... if we can, maybe it’d be better not to do this on the phone."

"Yeah, I think we should talk in person too... Oh, right, Momoko, Momoko was there too, right, I don’t know if her phone still works, but we should probably get in touch with Momoko too, if she can come."

"Not Momoko," Ran said shortly.

"Why not?"

"I’ll tell you when we meet. Hana, anyway, don’t do anything like go to the police, okay? Absolutely not. Got it?"

"Got it."

We fixed a time and place to meet and hung up.





2



Ran Katō, whom I was seeing again for the first time in twenty years, seemed quite different.

She was still small overall, as I remembered, but her body seemed somehow a size larger, and the balance of it felt oddly off. If she hadn’t spotted me first and raised a hand, I don’t think I would have recognized her right away, even in a cafe this empty.





Ran was wearing a soft, loose ecru cotton tunic and a large mask that covered half her face. Her hair, dyed a light brown, was tied back, and at the hairline white strands, as if brushed on in a quick sweep, caught the light and glimmered faintly. In her eyes, with their solid brown shadow and thick mascara, traces of her old self remained.

Her narrow forehead hadn’t changed, and I remembered some midnight long ago when Ran and Momoko and I measured our foreheads and the widths of our eyes and compared them. Come to think of it, Ran had liked makeup even back then, and laughing as she asked how much she thought she could change a face like mine, so clueless about that kind of thing, with her own techniques, she had tried all kinds of makeup on me, and there were times when the three of us cracked up at the finished result. Looking at the Ran in front of me with all that coming back, the realization that a really long time had passed welled up inside me, and it felt as if something deep in my chest gave a small throb.

But we didn’t even exchange the bright greetings people give when they meet an old friend again after a long time, things like Long time no see, or Have you been well, and just ordered our drinks. With her mouth hidden, I couldn’t tell what expression Ran had on her face, and in an atmosphere where nothing seemed to twitch, we sat in silence for a while.





After two iced coffees had been set down on the table, I tried saying, I’m glad you recognized me right away, Ran, and she answered shortly, You were looking around at the entrance. Ran stopped touching her phone and took off her mask; it was smeared thickly with brick-colored lipstick.

"Kimiko," Ran said. "What, what kind of thing did she do? I tried searching, but I couldn’t really tell."

Ignoring how tense I was, Ran spoke as if this were just a continuation of the conversation we’d been having the other day.

"Was it in the news? What, is it bad?"

"Not exactly news," I said, pulling up the article I’d bookmarked and handing her my phone. Ran stared hard at the screen with a serious expression, scrolled a little with her fingertip, then fixed her eyes on it again.

"How did you find this?"

After sliding the phone back across the table to me, Ran asked.

"I was just sort of reading articles about other big cases on Yahoo and places like that, and then farther down more similar cases kept coming up. Because they were related in content. Old ones too, links to tiny articles from local papers I’d never even heard of, lots of stuff like that. I kept going through them, and eventually I came across this article."

"Shinjuku, huh... so it wasn't that place in Sancha." Ran said it with a furrow between her brows. "It said Kimiko was sixty... which means back then she was, what, forty or around that. About as old as we are now, right?"

"Yeah."





"When I think about it now, it was completely insane."

Ran spat the words out.

"Unbelievable. If she was forty, that's like us now rounding up a bunch of twenty-year-olds and living like that with them. Completely deranged."

After she said it, Ran gave a small shake of her head.

"But why are you freaking out over this Kimiko thing, Hana? It's got nothing to do with us, right? The incident was last year — the trial's already started."

I nodded.

"When we were all living together — yeah, sure, we did some stuff that was bad. But we were like twenty, and that was ages ago. There's no way any of that's gonna be a problem now."

"But if there were, say, things from back then still left in Kimiko's house... you know, like those stacks of cards... then from the police's point of view, wouldn't they look at them and go, what is this? And then if Kimiko talked, even if they couldn't actually charge us with anything, they might at least want to ask us about it. There must be statutes of limitation and all that, but with what we were doing, I mean... I don't know which parts of it would count as a problem, or how serious any of it was."

"So you were gonna go to the police and tell them you used to be involved with her?" Ran said, eyes wide, looking exasperated. "You're way overthinking this."

"You think so?"

"Yeah. Your head's going to some seriously dark places. What we were doing wasn't even that big a deal. I really don't think it was. It's not worth losing sleep over twenty years later. Kids these days do way worse."

"You think so?"

"Yeah." Ran said it after a beat, as if thinking. "Besides... we were being made to do it."

"But Kotomi died too, and we still don't know what really happened, and we dragged her into it, or I mean, anyway... it ended up like that."





"Who was Kotomi again...? Oh." Ran gave a small nod. "Right, the hostess at that Ginza club you worked at on those trips."

"Yeah."

"No no no, that's got nothing to do with it. That was totally their own thing... we said the same at the time, didn't we? Sure, it was a shock, but... wait, Hana, you still think that was your fault or something?"

"It's not that, but..." I shook my head.

"No way. Those people were all messed up." Ran gave a small shrug. "So c'mon, calm down a little. Try and cheer up. Don't make that face. There's no point worrying over something from forever ago, something nobody even remembers anymore. I mean, sure, if Kimiko's name suddenly comes up like this, it'd shake you up, right? Like, the whole holding-girls-captive thing, kind of a similar situation. I get it. But it's fine. If there was even the slightest chance something bad was going to happen, the police would've come a long time ago. I mean, that's just a dark past. Youthful indiscretion, plus a dark past."

"Youthful... a dark past."

"Right, right. More importantly, COVID's worse, isn't it? How are you, Hana? At our place it's hell, the kids' school got shut down. COVID's bad, right? How's it been for you?"

"I'm..." I said, letting out a small breath through my nose. "Fine. I live alone."





Ran stirred her iced coffee around and around with her straw and nodded, like, huh. We were at a cafe in front of Jiyugaoka Station. Ran seemed to live somewhere along the Tōyoko Line, and she had picked this place, saying it was convenient because she could get here on one train, and she had other errands too. It was a coffee chain with glass walls all around. Ran hadn't asked where I lived, what kind of work I did, or anything about where or how I had been living since then.

"There's barely anyone here. It's usually packed. This self-restraint thing is rough."

Looking out at the street, there really were only a few scattered pedestrians, and maybe because of the heavy clouds that had hung over everything since morning, the whole town had an atmosphere of darkness and gloom.

"But time comes around, huh. We're almost forty now. Never thought I'd be seeing you again like this, Hana. When we parted, I mean, we kind of just scattered."

"You never changed your phone number, Ran. After that, did Kimiko ever contact you?"

"No," Ran said. "I can barely remember the details anymore, and I don't want to remember them either... back then, we slipped out in the middle of all that chaos, right? And that was it. But you had it the worst, didn't you? You were the one she was craziest about... I mean, Kimiko really was the kind of person who'd get arrested. Scary. Way too out of her mind. We were young, still basically kids, so we didn't get it at all, but she was completely messed up, right?"





"Hey, you mentioned it a little on the phone," I said. "About Momoko... you said Momoko was a no-go. Did something happen?"}

"Ah, Momoko." Ran gave a small shake of her head. "Remember? Momoko had that little sister who was bad news. Drop-dead gorgeous, and her teeth were filthy. She came there too once, right? Apparently she got into some fight over money with her sister and her sister's boyfriend, and in the end she went missing. I don't really know. That's the rumor I heard. After I got out of there, some time passed, and I was in touch with Momoko for a little while, but then she suddenly disappeared."

I let out a sigh.

"Man, everybody around us was out of their minds. It's just that we didn't know anything, that's all."

Ran's voice suddenly grew louder, and rang out so theatrically it was almost like she was practicing lines. The large cafe was sparsely occupied, and the customers scattered here and there were all by themselves, all wearing masks, while soft bossa nova drifted from speakers somewhere in the ceiling, I couldn't tell where. From the washing area behind the counter came the clatter of dishes, mixed with the voices of the staff. Other than us, there were no customers talking. An old man at a table a little way off spread open his newspaper with a loud rustle, and when he glanced over, our eyes met. I took a sip of water and checked the mask hanging from my chin with my fingertips.





"Well, whatever it was, it's over now. Like I keep saying, it was a long time ago. It's just the past. There's nothing for you to worry about, Hana . . . hey, Hana."

Without blinking, Ran fixed me with a direct stare.

"Seriously, absolutely no police or anything like that, okay? There's seriously no point. A hundred percent it'll just make things worse. Seriously, Hana, I'm counting on you there. It'll be fine, right? I sat here and heard you out today. So forget it now. Seriously."

I nodded.

"Really, you'll be okay, right?"

"Yeah."

After that we sat in silence for a long time. Ran sighed and hunched her shoulders, staring at some single point on the table. There was a look of exhaustion on her face. I had the feeling there was something else I should have said to Ran, now that we had met again, something important. And maybe Ran was feeling the same thing too. But I didn't know what it was, or how I could make it come out of my mouth as the right words.

"Well, I should get going soon. Oh, right, Hana."

As if pulling herself together, Ran straightened her back and said, putting on her mask.

"So, about that . . . could you delete my phone number? I'll make sure I delete your calls from my phone too."

When I couldn't say anything and just sat there, Ran picked up the bill and checked it, set her share of the money down on the table, slung her bag over her shoulder, and stood up.

"Okay, Hana, I'm heading out. Watch out for COVID."

Even after Ran left the restaurant with those parting words, I couldn't get up right away. There had to be other things I should have been thinking about, but my head wasn't working properly. I had no appetite, but I hadn't eaten since morning, so I should have been hungry, and I sat there vaguely thinking things like maybe I ought to order something to eat.

As I was looking at the small tabletop menu with photographs on it, I heard a strange sound. Outside, somewhere far off, there was a noise like something huge being rolled slowly along, though I couldn't tell whether it was heavy or light. After a while, I realized it was thunder. The moment I looked up through the glass, rain came down all at once with a bursting sound. The drops were so large it felt as if you could see each one separately, and I saw several people hurrying past with their bags or hands raised over their heads. The rain, growing harder still, lashed the awnings of the shops diagonally across the street and the asphalt, bouncing up and sending off a faint white haze.




Chapter Two  Fortune in Money




1



The first time I met Kimiko was in the summer when I was fifteen.

One morning, just after the last summer vacation of middle school had begun, a woman I didn't know was asleep beside me in my mother's place.

I couldn't see her face, and she was wearing my mother's pajamas, but I knew at once that the woman lying there fast asleep with her back to me, breathing heavily in her sleep, was not my mother.




I drew back a little, propping myself up on one elbow, but almost at once I told myself it was nothing and went back to sleep. My mother worked at a bar, and there had already been a few times when she brought girls from the place, or friends, home and let them stay over like this.

When I woke up again, the woman was gone, and on top of the futon, folded in thirds, her pajamas had been laid out neatly. They were the same worn-out pajamas my mother had been wearing for years, but there was something fresh about seeing them arranged in such a clean shape, the way clothes are when they're being sold in a shop, and I found myself staring at them for a while.

Usually our clothes and underwear were hung on hangers from the curtain rail by the window to dry, and once they were dry we put them straight on from there; the room was full of things, and on top of that it was always a mess, so that spot alone seemed to float up in a soft round patch, like the blank space left when the faint black pencil smudges on a notebook page have been carefully erased.

My mother and I lived in a small, old apartment house in a little town on the outskirts of Higashimurayama, hidden from the main street.

Between two detached houses facing the road there was an unmaintained passage about three meters wide, and if you went down it and turned left at the back, there was a shared entrance. The words Seifūsō were so old and blackened they were barely legible, and it was an entrance that called to mind an ominous cave; the corridor, with only a few low-wattage bulbs hanging from it, was dark even in the middle of a bright day.

It was a two-story wooden apartment building, with four rooms of the same layout on each floor, but apart from us there was no one living there except the middle-aged landlady in the back room upstairs. When you opened the flimsy wooden sliding door, so insubstantial even a child might have been able to kick it in, there was a tiny entryway, and once you stepped up from there, a kitchen of about three tatami mats, and beyond that two four-and-a-half-mat rooms running one behind the other. We used the communal toilet at the back of the first floor as if it were our own. Because the building was hemmed in on all sides, even with the windows open all you could see was the concrete wall next door, and almost no light came in.

We rented two rooms on the first floor. They were the rooms immediately to the left and right when you stood at the entrance to Seifūsō, and my mother and I used the room on the right most of the time.





The room on the left was supposed to be where my father stayed, but aside from a television, a futon, and a few clothes hanging in the closet, it showed no signs of being lived in, and in the first place my father was never one to stay at home.

At the time I never really knew what kind of work he did. He had a solid build, and his skin was always burned dark by the sun, and I think he probably did day labor that sometimes took him away for long stretches, construction work or truck driving or something like that (once, on my way home from elementary school, my father called my name from the cab of a large truck). For a while there was even a period when he brought home workmates dressed in the same kind of work clothes and they cooked hot pot, grilled meat, drank, but that ended quickly enough.

The father I saw only once in a while, as if he had remembered, was always in a good mood. He would give me things like badminton sets or dolls that looked as if he had won them from a crane game, and sometimes he would come home out of nowhere in the middle of the night, wake me up even though I was asleep, and make me eat sushi, saying it was good.

I did not dislike him, but we had spent so little time together, and I never knew what I was supposed to say to him, so whenever he came home I became strangely tense and careful around him, and all the while I would be squirming inside, wishing he would leave soon. Then, when I thought that maybe he had noticed that, my mood darkened again. I knew my father was not what you would call a normal father, and yet I also had some vague sense that maybe it was a sad thing, to have your own child think of you this way, and that brought with it another kind of tension, and self-loathing.

By the time I was in the upper grades of elementary school, my father had stopped coming home for good, and after that I never saw him again. I don't know what he's doing now, or what has become of him. It was only later that I vaguely came to understand that my father seemed to have another home, or rather, other people he was living with.

Living alone with my mother was easy, in a way, but it was the same as having nothing at all. My mother liked having fun, and liked to drink even though she wasn't all that strong, and she had lots of friends and a tendency to get swept along. After graduating from the local commercial high school, she worked as a full-time employee at a stocking factory, and she was proud of the fact that when important people from headquarters came to tour the place, she had once served as a leg model in front of everyone. But that only lasted a few years, and after that she drifted from one local bar to another with her friends, and somewhere along the way, apparently, I was born.

Compared with the mothers of my friends at school, my mother looked much younger, and she was flashy, and even to me, her daughter, she didn't feel like what people would call a normal mother. She was small, with a face that looked childlike in some indefinable way, and she was optimistic and cheerful and laughed a lot, but when she drank she would almost always cry. But there was never any clear reason for it in particular; maybe she was just the kind of person who cried when drunk, and for my mother, drinking and getting happy and making a commotion and crying all seemed almost like the same thing.

She did not seem attached to my father either; I never heard her complain about him or speak badly of him, and even after he stopped coming home she did not seem particularly interested. Every so often she would talk, half joking, about what an awful personality her own mother, whom she had cut off, had had, but even that was limited to times when she had been drinking, things had gotten lively, and she had turned sentimental.

Fundamentally, when my mother was with me, she preferred not to be just the two of us but to have someone else there too. She often spent time aimlessly wandering around with a hostess from the place where she worked, or someone a customer had introduced her to, or local friends she had known for years, and whenever I was alone with my mother, I always felt just a little tense. We were basically living on opposite schedules: while I was at school she slept, she got up in the afternoon, put on her makeup in the evening and went to work, and came home late at night.

There was only one other kid in the neighborhood who came from a family much like mine, and I became close with her. Ever since elementary school, neither of us had anything like a curfew, so the parents of kids from homes where there were places you weren't allowed to go, or where dinner was at a set time, seemed not to like their children spending more time with us than necessary. At the very least, they were forbidden to come play at our houses. There was another friend I liked fairly well who was perfectly normal with me at school but after classes always went off somewhere with a different group, and once I asked her why. Looking a little uncomfortable, she told me, "At your place, Hana, there are all these strange adults coming and going, and it's not a proper home, so they tell me I'm not allowed to go." And because it wasn't some malicious rumor or lie but the plain truth, there was nothing to be done about it.

When I moved up to junior high, my friends' faces and attitudes, and all sorts of other things, came to feel much more distinct. It felt as if I could tell at a glance which kids came from ordinary homes and which didn't, as though they were wearing hats in different colors. In an ordinary home, you didn't wake up in the morning to find an unfamiliar woman asleep next to you. Nor did the mere sight of a futon or pajamas folded up neatly make you feel brighter, or make you want to keep staring at them forever either, probably not, I imagine.




I folded my own futon in thirds the same way and set it beside the one that had already been folded. I was alone in the room, and there was no one in the kitchen either, so I slipped on my sandals, stepped across the hallway, and opened the door to the room opposite. A noisy TV was on, and when I went in I saw the back of a woman lying there watching a daytime talk show. The fan we'd had forever slowly swung from right to left, letting out a loud creak now and then.

When the woman noticed me, she didn't change her posture, only turned her face toward me and gave me a bright smile. The way she smiled was so natural that I almost thought she might be someone I'd met before. But she was a woman I didn't know, and though she might have slept the night before on the futon beside mine in the same room, at least this was the first time we'd come face to face like this. The woman turned back to the screen and laughed as if she were enjoying herself, her shoulders shaking a little with the cheers of the people on TV. I stayed where I was, standing at the border between the kitchen and the room, watching the television, the fan, and the woman.

"How about we eat something?"

When the program ended and it cut to commercials, the woman said it with a stretch.

"You're hungry, aren't you?"

We stood in the cramped kitchen and made instant ramen.

The woman was about a size bigger than I was all over, and her arms and legs seemed much longer too. Her long black hair, dark and wavy and tied back, sagged at the nape of her neck, the ends scattered across the large white T-shirt with English printed on it. I stood a little apart from her, staring hard at the water for two in the aluminum pot as it came to a boil.





The woman tore open the packets with practiced hands, loosened the noodles in the hot water, added the powdered soup and gave it a quick stir, then portioned the ramen into the bowls I had lined up. She tried to catch a wonton stuck on the rim of the pot with the tips of her chopsticks, but it would not come free, and then said brightly, Never mind, I’ll bring it over, and gestured toward the room. I pulled out the bare kotatsu frame that had been propped up there, and we sat facing each other, slurping our ramen.

"It’s summer vacation, right? Not going anywhere?"

I made a vague sound that was almost an answer and almost not. It had not felt all that hot until a moment before, but suddenly I felt sweat begin to bead on me. I reached out, switched on the air conditioner, and pressed the button for high.

"Going anywhere with Ai-san?"

Ai was my mother’s name.

"Not really, there’s not much of that."

"Hmm. What’s your name? I’m Kimiko."

"Hana."

"Hana, huh. Who named you? Ai-san?"

"I’m not really sure," I answered in a small voice.

"I see."

"Um, you’re my mother’s friend, right?"

"Right, right."

The conversation stopped there, and we ate the rest of the ramen in silence. A quiz show was playing on the television, and the old air conditioner’s motor droned on and on. After a while I could hear an ambulance faintly from outside; it seemed to come a little closer, then soon moved away again.

"Does Hana have kanji?"

"Just the usual one. Flower."

"My Kimiko is the ki from yellow, and beautiful child: Kimiko."





I gave another vague nod and drank some of the broth. When I glanced up over the rim of the bowl, my eyes met Kimiko’s, she had already finished eating and was leaning on her elbows at the kotatsu, and I looked away without thinking. After that, from behind the edge of my bowl, I kept stealing glances at Kimiko’s face. Kimiko had a face unlike any woman I had ever seen before.

Of course that was only natural, since every face was different, and yet Kimiko's face had a kind of presence to it, something I couldn't have explained properly in the words I had then. It was a just-woken-up face, and even though she presumably had no makeup on at all, her eyebrows were thick and full, the lashes on her sharply double-lidded eyes were dark and long, and there was little space between her brows and her eyes. Her nose rose firmly from the root, and the hair running from her temples into her sideburns, mixed with sweat, had formed tiny frizzy whorls.

More than pretty or beautiful, Kimiko's face felt strong.

It called to mind the young, commanding pharaoh who was the protagonist of a girls' manga set in ancient Egypt and the present day, wildly popular when I was in elementary school and one that I, too, had been completely absorbed in. Watching Kimiko crack her neck as she watched TV, or shift her posture, I felt as though the pharaoh's lines were floating somewhere diagonally above her head and I could see the borders of the panels, and it made me feel quietly happy.

"I'm in the mood for a soda. Want to go buy one?"

After carrying our bowls to the sink, Kimiko came back to the room and invited me. We went outside just as we were, in the clothes we'd slept in, and walked to the convenience store.

"So even though Ai's place has two rooms, you don't sleep separately?"

"The room where we just ate ramen, we call that the TV room, and the other one's the futon room, and somehow it just sort of..."

"So it's like a living room."

"Not anything that nice," I said, looking down.

"Better than sleeping by yourself, though, right?"

The summer sunlight was sharp enough to make me narrow my eyes without thinking, and each time I breathed, I could feel the heat soaking into my skin.

The apartment block where I lived was just behind the middle school I went to.

If you went about two hundred meters along the long gray wall surrounding the school buildings, then turned left at the corner and kept going for a while, you came to the main gate, and the convenience store was directly across the road from it. We were forbidden to shop there in our uniforms, but hardly any students actually followed that rule. Along the narrow sidewalk, the vivid green of trees planted on the school grounds pressed out toward us, casting dark blue shadows on the asphalt.

As the main gate came closer, I could see a few people hanging around there, making a racket.

Even though it was summer vacation, students still came and went because of club activities, but the ones there were not that kind, they were the students people called delinquents.
