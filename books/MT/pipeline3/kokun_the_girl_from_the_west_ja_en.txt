Orie: The Kōkun. Daughter of a minor noble of the Rigudal Domain Kingdom.

Mijima Orukashuga: A senior scent envoy who serves at the Kōkun's Palace. Rao's second daughter.

Yugiru Kashuga: Son of Iru Kashuga.

Yuma Kashuga: Mashuu's father. He disappeared when Mashuu was seventeen.

Amiru Kashuga: The man who, together with the Imperial Ancestor, brought the first Kōkun back from the holy land of Oaremadzura. Founder of the Kashuga House.

King Keruan: Aisha's grandfather. Once the domain king of West Kantar, he was driven from his throne.

Mircha Keruan: Aisha's younger brother.

Uchai: King Keruan's loyal retainer. The guardian who raised Aisha and Mircha; they call him Jiya.

Taku: Rao's cousin, who lives at the Yugiru Mountain Lodge. He and his wife Raina have twin sons.

Urairi: An inspector of the domain kingdom. Mashuu's colleague.

Oramu: A senior scent envoy with ties to the New Kashuga House.

Oroki Mua: Mashuu's subordinate. A dog handler.

Ariki: Former Head of Pest Control. Greatly trusted by Rao, he still works in the Insect Storehouse.

Oira: Head of Pest Control. Ariki's pupil.

Miria: Queen Mother of Ogoda. Mother of Agua, domain king of Ogoda.

Dyukchi: Domain king of West Kantar.

Odosen: Crown Prince of the Umar Empire. Oruran's heir, later emperor.

Kōkun, Book One: The Girl Who Came from the West

Prologue

Blue Flower

The wind howled in her ears and whipped at her hair.

The passing shower that had come through earlier that afternoon still left the earth damp, and the rock she clung to with both hands and feet was cold as ice.

"...Sister!"

A thin, high-pitched cry reached her, and fine flecks of mud came raining down.

On instinct, Aisha pulled one hand free from the rock and pressed her hand against the heel of her brother's shoe, faintly white in the gathering dusk, to support him.

The instant she did, her body pitched sideways and the other hand gripping the rock nearly slipped free; desperately she seized the rock again, barely stopped herself from tipping farther, and the moment she breathed out, her knees began to tremble.

A fall meant death.

Aisha gritted her teeth and kept supporting him until he planted his foot firmly in one of the hollows cut into the rock and steadied himself.

Even after he had steadied himself, Aisha stayed in that same position, unable to move for a while.

Breathing hard, she waited for the dizziness to pass.

Even if they managed to climb this cliff path to the top, there was probably no future beyond it. She had known that for some time now. From the rocky heights above, the smell of leather, metal, and sweat had been drifting down, so strong it was stifling.

Though she had been told few people knew this cliff path, the warriors lying in wait meant that old retainer Uchai, who had served as a decoy to draw the enemy's eyes away and let them escape, had been captured almost at once.

Aisha could not believe that Jiya had told them. Yet the warriors had, in fact, deduced where she and Mircha were headed and gotten there ahead of them.

*Should I release my grip?*

Dimly, before her, she could see her brother's mud-caked shoes. Remembering the smile on his face when those shoes had been bought for him, Aisha's face twisted.

Suddenly, it grew brighter around her.

Driven by the wind, the clouds had parted. Just before it vanished behind the mountains, the setting sun fell in several shafts across the rocky slope.

In that unexpected brightness, she saw a tuft of grass rooted in a crack just above the rock she gripped, with a tiny blue flower blooming on it. Buffeted by the wind, the flower swayed as though it might tear free, but it did not.

She heard the flower's voice of fragrance. Faint, but steady, its scent drifted on the wind like a thread. Before long, an insect that heard that voice would come flying between the gusts.

"...Sister."

Again the voice drifted down. Riding the cold wind came the scent of fear, like a puppy gone stiff with terror.

"Steady yourself, Mircha!" Aisha shouted.

"Grip the rock and climb! If you start to slip, I'll hold you!"

A little farther, and they would be over the top.

Sensing from the movement above that her brother had begun to climb, Aisha too began climbing, slowly.

Chapter One

Encounter

1. Ritaran

The glow of watchfires that had been invisible until a moment ago now stood out sharply against the blue darkness.

The wind that had been rattling the tents had eased somewhat.

From the countless tents pitched across the encampment, white smoke rose wavering and dissolved into the evening sky. The faint smell of supper cooking drifted over.

"The grunts get to eat first."

The bearded man, sprawled in a folding chair among a knot of hard-faced men, grumbled in a low voice.

Mashuu looked down at his bearded colleague Urairi and smiled faintly.

"You don't have to stay. I can keep my eyes and ears open well enough on my own."

Urairi started to toss back some light remark, then stopped. Turning his gaze toward the plain, he muttered,

"...If I could, I'd do it."

At the foot of the Tenro Mountains, their looming mass sinking into the dusk, the torchlight of a dozen or so riders advancing across the grassland toward them flickered in the distance.

"Oroki."

Urairi craned his neck and looked back at the man standing behind them. The hound at the man's feet raised its head as if it were the one being called. Oroki rested a hand on the dog's head and said,

"Yes."

"The heir was eight or so, and the older girl still only fifteen or sixteen, you said?"

"The heir is nine. The older girl is fifteen."

Urairi nodded and let out a sigh.

"Could've just left them be instead of going to all the trouble of catching them..."

Urairi looked up at Mashuu and went on.

"Don't you think? Unless some faction was trying to use them as figureheads, what power could the descendants of a king hated by his people and driven from the throne possibly have?

That was probably why they'd been left alone all this time. So why now, and in the middle of a war no less, did Dyukchi decide to do this?"

Mashuu did not answer that question. Keeping his eyes fixed on the riders, he murmured,

"So they did come from that side."

Urairi frowned.

"What?"

"The riders. If the men who captured them are coming back from that direction, then the descendants must have been trying to flee into Toura Ira, the Great Collapse Gorge in the Tenro Mountains."

"..."

Urairi rose and came to stand beside Mashuu.

"You said 'so they did.' So you'd already guessed."

Mashuu glanced at him.

"If they were going to run, there was nowhere else."

"That's not so, is it? They'd been hiding out in the woodlands, hadn't they? Easier to run east through the woodlands than climb dangerous mountain paths."

"Even if it meant braving the danger, they must've thought they could survive by fleeing to Tenro."

"Why?"

"You called Keruan a king hated by his people just now, but in Tenro there are people who do not hate that king."

Urairi's face hardened.

"...I'd never heard that. The mountain folk of Tenro? Which ones?"

Mashuu kept his eyes on the mounted warriors, whose figures were now plainly visible.

"You weren't the only one who didn't. If anything, there are probably fewer who know."

Urairi narrowed his eyes.

"When did you learn that?"

Mashuu looked at him and answered quietly.

"Me? I've known since I was a child."

One of Domain King Dyukchi's aides emerged from the great tent he used for official business. Sweeping his gaze around as though trying to see through the dusk, he noticed Mashuu and the others and came toward them at a quick pace.

"Honored Inspectors."

Crossing his arms neatly before his chest, he bowed deeply and then spoke.

"I humbly bring a message from Lord Dyukchi. -- The captives will be arriving shortly, so you are requested to enter the tent. -- That is all."

Mashuu nodded and answered briefly.

"I have received it."

The aide bowed deeply once more, then led the two men to the great tent.

The inspectors stationed in the four domain kingdoms were the eyes and ears of the emperor of the Umar Empire.

The "domain" in "domain kingdom" derived from the Umar word rachi, meaning a fence or enclosure that marked off land and protected it.

The domain kingdoms had once been independent states, and even now, under Umar rule, they retained a measure of autonomy. In return for guarding the borders of the Empire, a domain king was allowed limited authority over the land he had once ruled, but he was no sovereign king. Dyukchi, no more than the domain king of West Kantar, was not permitted to keep anything from Mashuu and the others.

Leaving their guards behind, Mashuu and Urairi walked slowly through the blue darkness toward the entrance of the great tent, bright as though that one place alone had been cut cleanly out of the dark.

* * *

Inside, the great tent was more spacious than it had looked from outside.

The smoke vent overhead stood wide open, and the hearth at the center glowed red with flame. Lamps stood here and there, enough to make out the faces of the senior ministers and clan chiefs seated along the walls.

At the far end, opposite the entrance, stood an altar beautifully painted in vermilion, blue, and gold. With the altar behind him, Domain King Dyukchi sat in a large chair, speaking in a low voice to the aide beside him, but when Mashuu and Urairi entered, he rose, inclined his head, and gestured for them to take the chairs nearby.

The wind outside had been cold, but inside the great tent it was close and stifling. Each small table set before the seats held fruit juice to quench thirst, along with nuts and dried fruit to take the edge off hunger.

Not long after Mashuu and Urairi had taken their seats, the bell announcing the captives' arrival rang out. The murmur died away, and the great tent fell silent.

Dyukchi sat down again and, taking a cloth from his aide, wiped the sweat from his brow and neck.

He was a big man, but there was nothing slack about him, and his skin was dark from the sun. Everything about his build was large, hands and feet included, but what stood out were his brows and eyes. When he fixed someone with those large, bulging eyes, anyone would feel uneasy.

Though he came from no more than a collateral line said to have some tie to the royal blood, he had an uncanny instinct for war. On the condition that he fight as the Empire's bulwark in times of crisis, he had borrowed troops from the emperor. After King Keruan was driven out, he overthrew the man who had taken the throne but failed to quell the skirmishing among the clans, and became the domain king of West Kantar.

One clan still did not recognize Dyukchi, and it could not yet be said that he held all of West Kantar. But if matters continued as they were, Mashuu thought, he would pacify it before long.

The emperor wanted Dyukchi to pacify West Kantar swiftly, and Mashuu hoped for the same.

The moment the door and curtain were hauled high from both sides, a cold night wind blew in.

With that night wind about them, two powerfully built warriors came in, one leading a boy, the other a slender girl.

Neither child was bound, but the warriors gripped the arm each favored. One of the warriors laid a hand to his chest and said in a clear, taut voice.

"As ordered, we have brought the descendants of Keruan."

Dyukchi nodded, spoke a few words of thanks to the warriors, and gestured that they might withdraw.

The warriors, however, hesitated.

"What is it? You may go."

When Dyukchi spoke again, one of the warriors opened his mouth.

"Forgive me for saying so, but the elder girl has shown no sign of submission. If we let go of her, there is no knowing what she may do."

At that, Dyukchi raised his thick brows.

"I see. Very well. I'll be careful. Let go."

The warriors bowed, let go, and stepped back, but they kept their eyes on the girl, ready to seize her again at any moment.

The girl, however, did not move. Not a single brow stirred. With black eyes, dark and still as stone, she only stared straight at Dyukchi.

Dyukchi met that gaze.

"Keruan's grandchildren. Mircha and Aisha, yes?"

The girl opened her mouth.

"I have no name to give a usurper."

Her voice was hoarse, but steady.

Dyukchi sighed.

"I see. You show no sign of submission."

He rose slowly, looked down at the brother and sister, then took up the longsword from the rack at his side and slammed the butt of its scabbard against the floorboards.

The senior retainers started, their faces drawing tight, and the brother and sister flinched as well. The boy's face twisted, and he began to sob.

"Your brother is crying."

Fixing his eyes on the girl, Dyukchi said,

"Know your situation and your place when you speak. Your attitude alone will send your brother's head flying before your eyes."

The girl looked up at Dyukchi, her face pale, and at last said in a thin voice,

"...That is a lie."

Dyukchi raised his thick brows.

"A lie?"

The girl nodded.

"Is it decided by something as slight as my attitude? The course our lives will take?"

Dyukchi's eyes widened slightly.

He stared at the elder girl for a long moment, and then one corner of his mouth twisted.

"If not by your attitude, then what decides your fate?"

For a moment, it seemed there might be a brief pause to think, but the girl answered at once.

"What you gain and what you lose."

The senior retainers stirred ever so slightly.

Mashuu shifted his gaze from the girl to Dyukchi.

*Now then, how would he answer?*

Dyukchi gazed at the girl in silence for a long while. At last, he let out a sigh.

"My own interests, huh. ... Well, you aren't entirely wrong."

And he dropped heavily into his chair.

He lifted a gold-mounted horn bowl from the low table, tipped back the milk wine and drained it with an audible gulp, then wiped his mouth with the cloth an attendant held out to him and used it to mop the sweat from his face and neck.

Then he turned back to the older girl.

"Not off the mark, but not right either. Your existence is less a loss to me than to the Empire as a whole."

The boy looked up at his sister as if to ask a question. She did not look at him. She only kept her gaze fixed on Dyukchi.

"You were trying to flee into the Tenro Mountains. More precisely, into Toura Ira, the Great Collapse Gorge."

Seeing the older girl's expression, Dyukchi added:

"Don't get this wrong. That old codger didn't talk. He is loyalty itself. He put up a fight you would not expect from a man his age, and I had already reckoned he might be a decoy, so I split my soldiers in two from the start."

The girl's face stiffened. Watching her, Dyukchi said, "The one who told us where you might flee was an inspector of the Umar Empire."

Urairi shifted. He made no move to look over, but his surprise could be felt in every line of him.

Mashuu's expression did not change. He only kept watching Dyukchi and the very young girl.

Dyukchi went on in an even tone.

"In other words, your existence serves neither West Kantar nor the Empire. Do you know why?"

The girl's face had gone white. Perhaps she had realized there was no path left open to them anywhere.

Only Dyukchi's voice echoed through the great tent.

"Your grandfather Keruan was a fool. Many people starved to death because of him. Most people in this country hate Keruan from the bottom of their hearts. I'm one of them."

The girl's gaze wavered. Her already bloodless face went whiter still.

Dyukchi had more milk wine poured into the horn bowl and drained that too with a noisy swallow.

"Yet it seems that even in this frontier there are still some such people who revere you.

That lot is called the Makishi, the People of the Hidden Valley. There aren't many of them, and they're only mountain folk out here on the frontier, with no designs on supremacy in this country, so we had left them alone until now. But now that lot is a nuisance. It's the territory they hold that's the problem."

Beside him, Mashuu heard Urairi mutter, "So that's it."

The clan they meant to subdue in this campaign controlled the foothills in the western reaches of the Tenro Mountains, but its authority did not extend into the mountains themselves.

They lacked the strength to hold out for long against the expeditionary army led by Dyukchi, but one chance still remained to them: if they could win the aid of Shinketsu beyond the Tenro Mountains, they might yet reverse the situation.

The peaks of the Tenro Mountains, which ringed West Kantar from the north around to the west, were steep, and there were only a few places where a large army could invade from the Shinketsu side. Fortresses had been built at such points, jointly guarded by imperial troops and the forces of West Kantar.

In Toura Ira, the Great Collapse Gorge in the western reaches of the Tenro Mountains, there was also a mountain path by which a small detachment of warriors seasoned in crossing mountain passes could come over from the Shinketsu side, but there was no fortress there.

Around Toura Ira, the ground was full of places where unexpected hollows lay hidden beneath the surface; if you broke through one, it could cost you your life. No one could follow the mountain path without a guide.

Unless the mountain people who ruled Toura Ira and the lands around it, the Makishi, chose to side with them, Shinketsu's warriors could not descend into West Kantar. And unless a large army could be sent in, it would be impossible to subdue the Makishi, who knew Toura Ira like the back of their hands. That was why, until now, neither West Kantar's domain king nor the Empire had attached much importance to the region.

But if the Makishi still regarded Keruan's descendants as the rightful king's descendants, then the situation might change drastically if the opposing clans put these two forward.

Among the clan chiefs seated along the wall of the great tent were some whose faces showed that they had only now grasped the meaning of what was happening.

The clan chiefs who had come on this expedition from the south and east of the domain kingdom knew little of affairs in the western reaches of the Tenro Mountains. Dyukchi had probably kept the details from them on purpose, so that men who had only recently declared their fealty would not get ideas.

"You are, so to speak, an ant burrow in a levee. Ordinarily it can be left alone without causing trouble. But when a storm comes, the levee breaks from that hole, and calamity spreads through the land.

"I'm not an unfeeling man. It pains me to kill you merely for being that man's grandchildren. But it cannot be helped.

"I could keep you close under my own watch, but even that leaves a certain danger."

With that, Dyukchi suddenly looked tired.

"That is how it is. If you must curse, curse your bloodline and your fate."

At a jerk of Dyukchi's chin, the warriors from before stepped forward and reached for the brother and sister's arms.

The boy, frightened, reached for his sister as if to cling to her. After taking his hand firmly in hers, the girl looked up at Dyukchi.

There was something like hesitation on her pale face. Her lips parted, then closed again, as though she had thought better of it.

Dyukchi frowned in puzzlement.

"Do you have something to say?"

The girl thought for a while. Then, as if she had made up her mind, she said,

"You have been poisoned."

Dyukchi drew his brows tight and stared at her, then once more wiped the sweat from his face and neck with a cloth. But wiping it away did nothing to stop it. Even to an onlooker, sweat was pouring over his skin with alarming intensity.

"What?"

The girl met his gaze with unreadable eyes.

"You smell of chitsuchiyo. Someone has poisoned you."

Murmurs ran through the senior retainers; some half rose from their seats.

Wiping the sweat from his eyes, Dyukchi gave her a defiant smile.

"Last words, and you spit venom? You've got nerve, I'll give you that. But if you're going to lie, make it sound more believable."

"I am not lying."

"Of course you are. Poison brewed from the root of chitsuchiyo has no taste and no smell."

The girl's face said, Ah.

"...Then you may go on thinking so."

With a sigh, the girl put an arm around her brother's shoulders and turned her back on Dyukchi. A warrior hurriedly seized her arm and started leading the two of them toward the entrance of the great tent.

Mashuu sprang to his feet and called after them.

"Wait."

The brother and sister stopped and looked back. Dyukchi and all the senior retainers turned questioning looks on him.

Mashuu addressed the girl.

"Lady Aisha Keruan - is there any scent on me?"

The girl stared at him. Her eyes were black as obsidian.

Just as he thought she would give no answer, she frowned faintly and opened her mouth.

"...Are you Ritaran?"

Mashuu's eyes flew wide. Cold spread from between his brows through his whole head, and his heart began to pound so fast it hurt.

His lips were numb. He parted them and asked in a hoarse voice,

"Why do you think that?"

Still frowning, the girl said,

"Because there is the fragrance of blue scent-herb about you."

Mashuu stared at her, stunned.

"...Hey, what's wrong?"

Without answering Urairi, Mashuu strode toward Dyukchi.

Up close, it was plain that Dyukchi's condition was far from normal. His eyes would not focus.

Mashuu turned to one of Dyukchi's attendants.

"Tell the physician to bring kafuru! Don't get it wrong - kafuru! And bring water, lots of it! Hurry!"

Then he called to the warriors holding the brother and sister.

"Take the two of them away and confine them securely!"

There came a dull thud, like a sack of flour hitting the floor. When he turned, Dyukchi had slid from his chair and was lying on the floor."

In the confusion, with the senior retainers on their feet all speaking at once, Mashuu hurried over to Urairi and said rapidly in a low voice, so the others would not hear, "Stay with the brother and sister. Take the guards with you too. Don't let anyone lay a hand on them. Keep a close eye on their food and even the insect-repelling smoke, so they can't be poisoned. And later, send Oroki to me."

Urairi nodded without a word and headed for the brother and sister. Then, with the warriors, he accompanied them out of the great tent.




II. The Scentless Poison




It was not until dawn the next day that Dyukchi's condition turned the corner.

Throughout that long night, the senior retainers came to the tent one after another, but Mashuu only let them look in from the entrance; he did not allow them inside.

A few showed their anger, but with no way of knowing who had poisoned him, Mashuu's precautions were reasonable, and none tried to force the matter.

The only person Mashuu allowed into the tent was his subordinate Oroki. After giving him detailed instructions, he sent him out into the camp with the dog that followed him like a shadow.

Mashuu sat through the night at the domain king's bedside with the physician. Once he had seen that the domain king's condition had steadied, he spread a bedroll in one corner of the same tent and drifted into a light doze.




He woke a little before noon. The domain king's breathing was deep and quiet, unchanged from before.

Mashuu rose, stepped outside the tent, and drew a deep breath.

The air smelled of rain-wet grass. Come to think of it, he felt he had heard a sudden shower beating on the tent in his sleep. At this season, rain like that fell often in these parts.

The sky was dull beneath a cover of cloud, but the wind was strong, and whenever it tore the heavy clouds apart and drove them on, sunlight streamed through and lit the Tenro Mountains with an unexpected brilliance.

Preparations for the midday meal must have begun; mingled with the smell of smoke was the toasty aroma of rice-flour tapa baking.

At the sound of footsteps on the grass, he turned and saw Oroki approaching with the dog.

"Well?"

When Mashuu asked, Oroki answered in a low voice, "Just as you suspected, sir."

"Was there?"

"There was, sir."

After hearing the detailed report, Mashuu smiled.

"Good work. Get some rest."

Oroki bowed, clicked his heels, and turned smartly away. Mashuu called after him.

"Feed your partner a good meal too."

Oroki looked back and nodded, with the faintest easing in his cheeks. The dog, perhaps aware that he was the one being spoken of, gave his tail a loose wag.

Drawing one deep breath, Mashuu turned away from them.

*...Now then.*

Mashuu murmured inwardly.

*From here, there's another job to do.*

Entering the tent from the bright outdoors, he was blinded for a moment.

As his eyes adjusted to the dimness, he made out Dyukchi lying on bedding spread beside the hearth, with the physician by his side. The smell of a medicinal decoction drifted through the tent, mingling with the smoke.

When Mashuu approached Dyukchi's bedside, Dyukchi opened his eyes a little, perhaps roused by the sound. He made a small gesture asking for water.

The physician lifted and supported Dyukchi, then held a bowl of water to his lips. Dyukchi drank it down audibly.

Seeing that he could drink without choking, the physician's face loosened in relief.

After coughing two or three times, as though trying to clear the phlegm caught in his throat, Dyukchi raised his face and looked at Mashuu. Strength had returned to his eyes.

"...What I was given," Dyukchi said in a hoarse voice. "Was it truly chitsuchiyo?"

Mashuu turned to the physician, and he spoke.

"I cannot say with certainty, but I believe that is so. The symptoms with which you are afflicted are exceedingly like those seen when one is struck by chitsuchiyo, the shadow herb. And the antidotal decoction for shadow-herb poisoning has also proved markedly effective."

Dyukchi said with a sullen look, "But chitsuchiyo is supposed to have no smell or taste."

The physician nodded.

"It does not."

Dyukchi turned his gaze back to Mashuu.

"Then why, I wonder, did that girl say she smelled chitsuchiyo on my sweat?"

Mashuu had spent the whole sleepless watch considering how he should answer if asked that question, yet every answer that came to mind had left some lingering hesitation.

And yet, when the question was actually put to him, he felt no hesitation in speaking the answer he had in mind.

Mashuu turned to the physician.

"Lord Dyukchi's condition seems to have steadied, but will he be all right for a while without you here?"

The physician knelt beside Dyukchi, took his pulse, and nodded.

"For a little while, I believe he will be all right even without me."

"Then rest a while. If there is any change in Lord Dyukchi's condition, I shall send word at once."

The physician bowed and left the tent.

Mashuu waited until his footsteps could no longer be heard, then turned back to Dyukchi.

"As for the answer to what you asked just now--"

"..."

"Might it not be that the girl truly could smell chitsuchiyo? Her nose is likely keener than most people's."

A hard light came into Dyukchi's large eyes.

"You went so far as to clear everyone out, and this is the nonsense you tell me? What I wanted to hear was why that girl made that one needless remark. The girl knew I had been poisoned. That means she is connected to the one who poisoned me..."

Mashuu slowly shook his head. Dyukchi scowled in irritation.

"You say I am wrong? In what way?"

"That girl is not connected to the one who poisoned you."

"What makes you so certain?"

"Because she told you that you had been poisoned, which is precisely what you yourself find puzzling."

"..."

"That was precisely a needless remark, that one. If she had been on the poisoner's side, and believed that if you died she and her brother would both be saved, she would never have said it."

Mashuu let out a small sigh.

"She bore herself bravely, but she is still only fifteen. When she thought that if she kept silent about what she had noticed, someone would die, she likely spoke before she could stop herself."

"It was someone trying to kill her."

"Even so, there are those who cannot leave another person to die."

"But chitsuchiyo..."

Cutting off what Dyukchi was about to say, Mashuu went on.

"You are too preoccupied with the fact that chitsuchiyo is tasteless and odorless. Yet the signs of poisoning appear in many ways. Even I had noticed how heavily you were sweating. That girl, facing you directly, must have seen it plainly."

"..."

"Kings live under constant threat of assassination, and so they become well versed in its methods. That girl is King Keruan's granddaughter. She must have grown up being thoroughly instructed in such matters.

"That is how she judged, from your unnatural sweating and the wavering of your gaze, that you had been dosed with chitsuchiyo."

Dyukchi seemed half convinced, but something still troubled him, and he started to speak.

Before he could get the words out, Mashuu continued.

"Besides, the one who tried to poison you could not have known the value of Keruan's descendants."

Dyukchi's eyes widened.

"You know who did it?"

Mashuu nodded.

"The Rima clan chief."

The area around Dyukchi's eyes tightened.

"Rariha. What proof?"

"His servant had a cloth for polishing bowls. I am told that servant was responsible for polishing the bowls set out in the great tent last night."

Dyukchi sighed and shook his head.

"If that is why you suspect him, you are mistaken. I wash all my bowls myself, polish them myself, and keep them where I can see them. There is no way he could have smeared poison on my bowl."

Mashuu smiled.

"What he smeared on the bowls was not poison."

"...?"

"He coated everyone's bowls with kafuru sap."

In the same calm tone, Mashuu explained how the attempt had been made.

"You fear being poisoned, so you drink only milk wine poured from the same vessel as everyone else's. If poison were mixed into the milk wine, Rariha would have to drink it too.

"He might have chosen to kill everyone in the great tent and leave only himself alive. But even if he succeeded in removing you, that would only earn him the hatred of the other clans."

A look of astonishment spread across Dyukchi's face.

"I see. He turned the fact that I polish only my own bowl separately against me."

Mashuu nodded.

"Yes. The shadow-herb poison, chitsuchiyo, cannot withstand kafuru sap. If it touches that sap, it ceases to be poisonous. The antidote the physician prescribed for you was also made from kafuru sap."

"...He had already rubbed it onto his own cup, and everyone else's."

"Exactly. One of my men spent the whole night finding the one carrying a cup-polishing cloth that smelled of kafuru sap."

Mashuu smiled briefly.

"Not with his own nose, though. With a dog."

Dyukchi grunted and lowered his head, thinking for a while.

Stroking his chin, Mashuu said, "Under the circumstances, punishing Rariha will be no easy matter. We must think through the aftermath with care before we act."

"...That too, but..."

After murmuring this, Dyukchi lifted his head and looked at Mashuu.

"It's what to do about that girl."

"Because she saved your life?"

Dyukchi nodded.

"At least the girl..."

Mashuu shook his head.

"That will not do. If her brother is killed, that girl will direct fierce hatred at you for the rest of her life. It would only leave a lasting grudge."

Dyukchi's face clouded, and Mashuu said, "If you wish to show mercy, have them executed by poison rather than by beheading."

Dyukchi drew his brows together.

"That would make them suffer more."

"No. You need only choose the poison. There are poisons that let a person go as if falling asleep, without pain."

Dyukchi looked at Mashuu with a conflicted expression. Mashuu merely met his gaze in silence.



3. Frost Herb



Aisha and her younger brother, Mircha, were being held captive in a tent pitched at the edge of the encampment.

It stood by itself on the grassland, apart from the other tents, so anyone approaching was immediately conspicuous.

The guards who had come with Mashuu kept strict watch inside and out, and even meals and changes of clothes were not given to the brother and sister until the guards had inspected them.

Once, in the afternoon on the day Dyukchi recovered, Oroki came to the tent, called Urairi outside, whispered something to him, then took three guards and went off somewhere. Other than that, there was almost no coming and going.

Besides the smoke hole overhead, the tent had two small windows to let the wind through. Whenever the shouts of drill and other noises drifted from the camp, the boy would go over to the windows and peer out, but his sister never even approached them. She simply sat inside the tent, passing the time by embroidering the cloth she had been given for that purpose.

By the time darkness fell and the soldiers had finished their evening meal, a late supper was brought to the two of them.

Behind the servants carrying it came Mashuu, accompanied by two of the domain king's aides.

When the guards lifted the tent flap and whispered something inside, Urairi came out. The moment he saw Mashuu and the others standing behind the servants bringing supper, his face went taut.

Mashuu touched a finger to his lips, and Urairi answered with a glance that he understood.

A servant bearing the supper on a large tray ducked his head and went into the tent, and Mashuu and the domain king's aides moved up to the windows.

Night had fallen completely, and the lights of the encampment were far off, so from inside the bright tent it was all but impossible to make out the face of anyone outside the window unless he was standing very close. Even if the brother and sister noticed a figure there, guards had often stood outside the windows in just the same way before. They were not likely to realize that anything was different.

The supper was finer than what had been served to the soldiers. Besides roasted mutton, there was vegetable soup, and fruit as well. The tapa had been spread with butter and sprinkled with precious sugar. There was milk wine too, of course.

The boy must have been hungry. When supper was set out before him, his face lit up with pleasure. The girl, on the other hand, had kept her head bowed since before the servant entered.

Seeing the way she sat, Mashuu narrowed his eyes.

Her shoulders were stiff. She was tense.

*Has she noticed?*

The boy reached first for the tapa and took a big bite of the piece soaked with melted sugar and butter, eating with obvious relish.

The girl too began to eat, quietly. Like the boy, she started with the tapa, then ate the mutton and the soup as well, savoring each slowly. She ate the fruit too, leaving nothing.

When the boy had tasted a little of everything and reached for the milk wine, the girl stopped eating and looked at him.

*...Will she stop him?*

Without thinking, Mashuu leaned forward and stared at her profile.

Her hand was trembling faintly. The tendons stood out in her slender neck.

The boy gripped the handles of the milk-wine jar in both hands and put it to his lips.

She did not try to stop him. She only sat there with her eyes fixed on him.

He drank the milk wine down in eager gulps, his throat working audibly, then set the jar on the floor and reached for the fruit that remained.

Then his head gave a lurch, and he started to topple forward.

She shot out a hand and caught him, lowered him to the floor, and drew his head onto her lap.

Something gleamed on her cheek.

She bent over him as if to cradle the limp head in her arms, but after a moment she straightened and reached for his emptied milk-wine jar.

No sooner had her hand closed around the handle than she hurled it with all her strength.

The jar struck beneath the tent window with a dull thud, and Mashuu instinctively pulled his face back.

Inside, Urairi and the others sprang to their feet in alarm, but the sister did not so much as glance their way. She merely seized her own jug of milk wine and, keeping her face turned toward the window where Mashuu stood, set it to her lips and drank, her throat working.

Her black eyes blazed with a fierce light, fixed on him.

Mashuu tightened his gut and met the girl's gaze.

*That's right. I'm the one who poisoned you.*

From deep in his chest something hot surged up, and his whole body began to tremble.

*You caught the scent on my fingers, the fingers that pinched the poison in--and that the owner of that scent was here as well.*

Before he knew it, Mashuu was smiling. His whole body burned as if on fire.

Mashuu watched without moving as the strength went out of the girl's eyes and she sank forward.

* * *

Dyukchi came out of the tent, accompanied by an aide.

A large woolen blanket had been spread on the grass before the tent. The torchlight the soldiers held aloft cast a dim glow over the bodies of the girl and the boy laid out upon it.

Even in that dim light, their faces were white, and looked noticeably smaller than they had in life.

Dyukchi stood looking down at the bodies for a while. At last he crouched, held a hand to the boy's mouth, and checked that there was no breath.

He did the same with the sister. Then he lightly touched her cheek and drew back his hand in surprise.

"She's quite cold. It hasn't been that long since they died, has it?"

Standing on the far side of the bodies, Mashuu said quietly, "Because I used frost-herb hirin."

Dyukchi nodded, then rose with a sigh and turned to the soldiers.

"Wrap them in cloth and give them a burial."

With only that, he turned his back on the bodies and went back into the tent.

In West Kantar, the bodies of children were buried beneath yugi trees.

According to legend, the yugi tree had sprung from the body of a mother who, after losing her child, had thrown herself into a river in grief and drifted ashore; if the young dead were buried at its roots, the spirit of the yugi tree would gently guide their souls to the heavens.

The soldiers whom Dyukchi had sent out at dusk to look for a yugi tree had already returned after finding a suitable burial place, so the two bodies were quickly loaded onto a wagon, carried across the grassland and into the woods along a narrow river, and buried in the pit that had been dug beneath the yugi tree.

The soldiers too were someone's children, and parents themselves, so they did not throw the bodies into the grave. They wrapped them in wool so they would not be cold, laid them at the bottom of the pit, covered them with earth so that beasts would not disturb them, bowed deeply, and returned to camp.

The moment the hoofbeats of their horses faded into the distance, three figures sprang out from among the dark trees and began digging up the grave with single-minded urgency.

They carried the two bodies out of the pit and laid them on the grass beside it, then hurriedly removed the cloths that covered them from head to foot.

Then they carefully wrapped the bodies in other cloths, and two of the men each took the sister and brother in his arms and disappeared into the depths of the forest.

The one who remained put the cloths that had wrapped the bodies back into the bottom of the pit, arranged them a little, then covered them with earth again, restoring the grave to its former shape.

The sinking moon faintly lit the tips of the forest branches.



4. The Voice of Fragrance



A warm, toasted smell drifted to her.

*...Jiya is baking tapa.*

Just as she thought she ought to get up soon and wake Mircha too, a terrible memory flashed back like lightning, and a sharp pain stabbed through her chest. The pain spread like bitter water from her chest to her throat and head.

Aisha opened her mouth and gulped in air.

A whistle sounded in her throat, and the smell of baking tapa, the smell of smoke, and the scents of two men and a dog all rushed into her nose at once.

In that flood of scent was Mircha's scent. Their arms seemed to be touching, and she could feel his warmth as well.

*Mircha... he's alive!*

She tried to open her eyes and look at her brother, but the world was spinning so violently that she shut them again and waited for the dizziness to pass.

Hearing a man's voice say, "You seem to be awake," Aisha, in her confusion, desperately tried to understand what was happening.

*I drank milk wine with hirin in it...*

She had made sure Mircha drank up all the deadly hirin in the milk wine, and then had drunk it herself as well.

*Then why?*

With her eyes still closed, Aisha felt one of the men rise and come toward her. Even with her eyes shut, she knew which man it was.

"You are awake, are you not, Lady Aisha Keruan?"

Aisha slowly opened her eyes and looked at the man. She was still a little dizzy, but even so she could see his face clearly.

His sun-browned, lean face and black eyes were fixed on her.

"...Why?"

When she murmured it, perhaps hearing the rasp in her voice, the man turned his head and told someone behind him to bring water.

When the water pitcher was brought, he slipped a hand gently beneath Aisha's head and, supporting her, helped her sit up slowly. It was a large, broad, warm hand.

When she took a mouthful of the water poured into a wooden bowl, it smelled of old cedar. It must have been carried in a barrel. Even so, it tasted good to her parched throat.

When she had drained it and returned the bowl, the man took it from her in silence.

Aisha stared at the man.

Whenever he moved, the scent of blue scent-herb grew stronger. He must have kept it tucked inside his clothes all this time. Mixed with the man's own scent, it had changed, but there was no mistaking it: it was blue scent-herb.

"Why..."

She tried to ask again, but the man shook his head.

"There is no time to explain at present. I shall explain tonight, so until then please rest here."

When she tried to look toward her brother, the man rose to his feet.

"Your younger brother is safe as well. A little while ago he woke briefly, then fell asleep again. I wrapped him in blankets myself, so he is not likely to catch a chill. You should warm yourself as well. I will leave one of my men here as a guard, so if anything happens, follow his instructions."

With that, he strode back to the fire, returned the wooden bowl to the man sitting beside it, picked up a tapa from the plate and put it into his mouth, then walked off into the trees.

Beside the man cooking at the fire was a dog. It stayed where it lay, watching her.

"If you are able to rise, would you care to take some tapa here by the fire?"

The man called to her. He had a narrow face, with something of a hunter's sharpness about him.

Aisha set a hand on the grass and slowly rose to her feet. The dizziness came again for a moment, but quickly subsided.

When she sat beside the fire, its warmth sank into her skin, and she realized how thoroughly chilled her body had become.

*Hirin freezes the body.*

Anyone who ate it by mistake should have grown cold in moments and, without even time to suffer, died as though falling asleep....

*Why am I alive?*

The man deftly pinched a sheet of tapa free from the small pan, where it clung in a thin layer and gave off a toasty smell, and laid it on a wooden plate. Then he spread it thick with butter, drizzled honey over it, and handed it to her.

The moment she felt the warmth of the wooden plate in her palms, something hard that had covered her heart split apart, and the sense that she was alive spread through her whole body.

She had thought there was no path left for her. That this was the end of her life.

She had imagined death by beheading, so when she learned the execution would be by hirin, she had thought it would at least be easier than the blade. While she waited for the execution, she had been so frightened her bones shook, but when the moment finally came, and she watched her brother drink the poisoned milk wine, all fear had vanished, and everything in the world had gone black. She and her brother and everything else were shut away inside a pitch-black darkness.

Only, the instant she caught the scent of that man's fingers in the milk wine, a violent rage lanced through her brow.

Because of their bloodline, they had to die—that injustice, and the gall of having to leave this world without even being able to right it, erupted as anger toward something she could not even name, and burned through her.

Slowly, she lifted the tapa from the warm wooden plate, folded it into a small piece, and put it in her mouth. The butter's rich saltiness and the honey's sweetness mingled and filled her mouth.

It was a familiar taste.

The tapa set before her at every meal since she was taken captive had carried the aroma of ingredients she had never tasted before. But this was the familiar tapa she had always eaten, and when its aroma filled her nose, tears welled up.

So the man would not see her tears, Aisha lowered her head and stuffed her mouth with the warm, toasty tapa.

The dog lumbered to its feet and came over. It pressed its cold nose against Aisha's elbow.
