Prologue

Seeing Nothing Anymore

Everything went dark. It was like mourning clothes. Then, here and there, glimmers, like the spots the sun leaves when you stare at it in vain behind eyelids squeezed shut, the way one clenches a fist to resist pain or emotion.

Of course, she had not described it that way at all. For a fresh-faced, anxious ten-year-old, distress comes out bluntly, without flourish or lyricism.

"Mum, it's all black!"

Mona had hurled out the words in a strangled voice. A complaint? Yes, but not only that. Despite herself, she had let a note of shame slip into them, and whenever her mother caught it, she took it seriously. For if there was one thing Mona never feigned, it was shame. The moment it lodged in a word, an attitude, an intonation, the die was cast: an unpleasant truth had broken through.

"Mum, it's all black!"

Mona was blind.

It seemed to have no cause. Nothing in particular had happened; she had been dutifully doing her maths, a pen in her right hand, her notebook pinned beneath her left palm, at the corner of the table where her mother was studding a nice fatty roast with garlic. Mona had been delicately removing from her neck a pendant that bothered her because it kept swinging over her worksheet and because she had fallen into the bad habit of hunching over when she wrote. She felt a heavy shadow crash down over both her eyes, as though they were being punished for being so blue, so large, so pure. The shadow did not come from outside, as it ordinarily does when night falls or the lights in a theatre go dim; it seized her sight from within her own body, from the inside. Inside her, an opaque sheet had crept in, cutting her off from the polygons drawn in her school notebook, from the brown wooden table, from the roast set farther down, from her mother in a white apron, from the tiled kitchen, from her father seated in the next room, from the apartment in Montreuil, from the autumn sky gone grey above the streets, from the whole world. By some spell, the child was plunging into darkness.

Agitated, Mona's mother called the family doctor. She described her daughter's clouded pupils haltingly and added, because the doctor asked, that she seemed to have no language impairment and no paralysis.

"It sounds like a TIA," he said, unwilling to say more.

He immediately demanded large doses of aspirin and, above all, Mona's urgent transfer to Hôtel-Dieu, where he would call a colleague to take charge of her at once. He had not thought of him by chance: he was a wonderful paediatrician, also reputed to be a very good ophthalmologist and, incidentally, a gifted hypnotherapist. Normally, he concluded, the blindness should not last more than ten minutes, and he hung up. A good quarter of an hour had already passed since the first cry of alarm.

In the car, the little girl was crying, beating at her temples. Her mother held her by the elbows, but deep inside she too wanted to beat on that small, round, fragile head, the way one bangs on a broken machine in the foolish hope of making it start again. At the wheel of his rattling old Volkswagen, her father wished he could catch whatever was afflicting his little girl. He was angry, convinced that something had happened in the kitchen and was being kept from him. He kept turning over every possible cause in his mind, from a burst of steam to a bad fall. But no, Mona said it a hundred times:

"It happened all by itself!"

And her father believed none of it:

"You don't go blind like that!"

And yet, yes: people do go blind "just like that". Here was the proof. And today that "people" was Mona, ten years old, her tears of fear streaming down in torrents - tears she perhaps hoped would wash away the soot that had stuck to her pupils on that October Sunday, as evening fell. Hardly had she reached the threshold of the hospital beside Notre-Dame, on the Île de la Cité, when she abruptly broke off her sobs and went still:

"Mum, Dad, it's coming back!"

Standing out in the street, where a cold wind was blowing, she rocked her head back and forth to encourage the return tide of her perception. Like a blind being raised, the veil obstructing her eyes lifted. Lines reappeared, then the edges of faces, the relief of nearby objects, the texture of the walls, and every shade of colour, from the brightest to the darkest. The child saw again her mother's slight silhouette, her long swan neck and frail arms, and then her father's, more solid. At last she made out, in the distance, a grey pigeon taking wing, which filled her with joy. Blindness had seized Mona, then let her go. It had passed through her, as a bullet pierces the skin and comes out the other side of the body, hurting, certainly, yet still leaving the organism to scar over on its own. "A miracle," thought her father, who scrupulously timed how long the crisis had lasted: sixty-three minutes.

At the ophthalmology department of the Hôtel-Dieu, there could be no question of letting the little girl leave before a battery of tests had been carried out, a diagnosis given, and prescriptions issued. The anguish had certainly been deferred, but not entirely dispelled. A nurse directed them to a room on the first floor of the building. It was the consulting room of the paediatrician alerted by the family doctor. Dr Van Orst was mixed-race, and already prematurely bald. His large, radiant white coat stood out against the sickly green of the walls. His immense smile, which etched little jovial wrinkles into his face, made him likeable; he nonetheless carried the burden of many tragedies. He stepped forward:

"How old are you?" he asked in a voice roughened by tobacco.

Mona was ten years old. She was the only child of two parents who loved each other. Camille, her mother, was nearing forty. She was short, with short, tousled hair, and her voice still carried a faint trace of working-class Parisian cheek. Her charm lay in what her man called her "slightly off-kilter" side, though it was underpinned by a fearsome determination: in her, anarchy was forever braided with authority. She worked at a temp agency and was a good employee, committed, zealous. In the mornings, at least. Afternoons were another matter. She wore herself out doing volunteer work. Every cause was a good one, from isolated old people to battered animals. As for Paul, he was past fifty-seven. Camille was his second wife. The first had disappeared with his best friend. He wore a tie to distract from the frayed collars of his shirts and toiled away as a small-time second-hand dealer, above all passionate about 1950s American culture: jukeboxes, pinball machines, posters... And because it had all begun in his teens with a collection of heart-shaped key rings, he owned an impressive array of them, which he had no wish to sell and which, in any case, would have interested no one. With the rise of the internet, his shop, tucked away in Montreuil, had very nearly closed its doors. So he too had gone online, putting his expertise to work on a website he updated incessantly and translated into English. Despite a business sense bordering on the void, he could count on a clientele of attentive collectors who regularly saved him from ruin. The previous summer he had repaired a 1955 Gottlieb "Wishing Well" pinball machine and made the tidy sum of ten thousand euros from it. A godsend of a deal, after months of lean times... And then, once again, the void. The crisis, people told him. Each day at the shop Paul knocked back a bottle of red, then stuck it, like a trophy, on one of those hedgehog bottle racks that had secured Marcel Duchamp's posterity. He raised his glass alone, unable to find it in himself to resent anyone. In his head, he drank to Mona. To her health.

While a nurse led the child through the labyrinth of the hospital for various tests, Dr Van Orst, settled into an enormous armchair, gave Paul and Camille an initial diagnosis:

"TIA, or transient ischaemic attack."

That meant that the blood had temporarily ceased to reach the organs, and they now had to identify what had caused the malfunction. But Mona's case, he went on, was perplexing him: on the one hand, the attack, exceedingly rare in a little girl her age, seemed severe, since both eyes had been affected and it had lasted more than an hour; on the other, it had left her ability to move and speak completely untouched. The MRI would no doubt say more. They had to, he added with an embarrassed look, prepare themselves for the worst.

Mona had to lie down on a narrow platform in a horrible machine and submit to it docilely, without moving. They asked her to remove her pendant. She refused. It was a fine fishing-line chain weighted by a tiny shell that had belonged to her grandmother and brought her luck. She had always worn it, and her adored Dadé wore the same one too. The two talismans bound them to each other, she thought, and she did not want to feel torn away from her grandfather. Since the pendant contained no metal, they let her keep it. Then her head, her pretty head, haloed by shoulder-length brown hair with tawny glints and an adorable little round mouth, was encircled by an ogre-like casing that rang with the noise of a factory. For the fifteen minutes the torture lasted, Mona kept singing to herself without a break, to hold out, to inject a little good cheer and life into that coffin: a slightly soppy lullaby her mother used to hum when, not so long ago, she tucked her in; a pop song, a tune that played on a loop in supermarkets and whose video, full of slick-haired boys, she liked; obsessive ad jingles; and also 'Une souris verte', remembering the day she had screamed its words to exasperate her father, without success.

The MRI results came in. Dr Van Orst summoned Camille and Paul and hurried to reassure them. There was nothing. Absolutely nothing. On the cross-sectional images, the anatomy of the brain showed nothing but uniform areas. No tumour there. More tests were carried out, endlessly. All through the night: from the depths of the pupils to the inner ear, by way of blood, bones, muscles, arteries. Nothing there either. The calm after the storm. Had there ever been a storm?

A clock face lost in a corridor of the Hotel-Dieu showed five in the morning. An image out of a nursery rhyme came to Camille: it was as if, she confided to her husband, exhausted, some evil being had stolen Mona's two eyes before giving them back to her. As if it had chosen the wrong victim, Paul added. Or as if it had sent a sign, a warning, and was preparing to repeat its crime, both of them thought in silence.

In the courtyard, the bell rang. The stream of children, marshalled by Mme Hadji, made its way up to the second floor. The teacher told her Year 5 pupils that they would not be seeing Mona, their classmate, again until school resumed after the All Saints' Day holidays. She herself had only just been informed by Camille, who, on the phone, had explained almost everything about the infernal night, without concealing the gravity of what had happened. Naturally, the children asked questions. Had she been allowed to go on holiday a week before everyone else?

'She's a little sick,' the teacher could only venture, not altogether satisfied with the phrase herself.

'A little sick: lucky!' exclaimed Diego from the third row, and his shrill voice drew agreement from the whole class.

Most children imagine illness as a passport to freedom...

At the back of the classroom, beside the chalk-dusted curtains, Lili and Jade, Mona's two best friends, who knew every corner of her bedroom, were drooling even more. Ah, how they would have loved to be with her! "A little sick"? Yes, all right, thought Lili, but she was bound to spend her days in her father's second-hand shop. And Jade, her eyes fixed on Mona's empty seat, delightedly imagined herself beside her, inventing all sorts of games and stories in that modest little place filled with old things that smelled of America, a whole bric-a-brac of shimmering, amusing and mysterious objects that fired children's imaginations. But Lili protested:

"No, no, when she's sick, it's her Grandpa Dadé who comes to look after her, and he scares me."

Jade forced a mocking laugh to show that nothing frightened her, least of all Mona's grandfather. Yet deep down the little girl admitted it: yes, faced with that old man, huge, gaunt and scarred, speaking in a deep, mineral voice, she wasn't feeling too bold either...

"Hello, Dad, it's me."

It was noon when Camille, her limbs numb, finally made herself call her father. Henry Vuillemin refused to use a mobile phone; he invariably answered calls on his landline with a little "Yes," dry and scorching, that left scant room for enthusiasm. His daughter hated the ritual and, every time, longed for the days when her mother, still alive then, would lift the receiver. She let the syllables fall one by one.

"Dad, I have to tell you: something terrible happened last night."

She told him everything, in order, trying to master her emotion.

"Well?" Henry asked, with a hint of impatience.

But Camille had held back her tears for so long as she told the story that an enormous sob heaved her body and choked her: she was unable to answer.

"Darling, what is it?" her father pressed.

That unexpected "darling" gave her a rush of oxygen; she caught her breath and whispered:

"Nothing! For now, nothing. It's all right, I think."

Henry let out a long sigh of relief, then tilted his neck back, let his head fall, and studied the cheerful patterns of plump fruit, scrollwork and spring flowers in the mouldings of his ceiling.

"Let me say a word to her."

But Mona, curled up in an armchair in the living room under a rust-coloured throw, had drifted off.

The poet Ovid described the phase when consciousness falls asleep as an entry into an immense cave sheltering the languid, indolent god Sleep. He imagined a cavern inaccessible to Phoebus, lord of the Sun. Mona had learned from her grandfather that, on the human scale, there was no journey more regular than the one towards those mysterious, shifting lands... Those lands, through which we travel ceaselessly all our lives, should not be neglected.

In the days that followed, Dr Van Orst, at the Hôtel-Dieu, ordered further tests. They still revealed no particular abnormality. The explanation for those sixty-three minutes of blindness stubbornly refused to emerge, to the point that the doctor now balked at using the term 'transient ischaemic attack', which presupposed a vascular deficiency of which he was no longer entirely certain. In the absence of a clear diagnosis, he suggested hypnosis to Mona and her parents. The idea stunned Paul into silence. As for the little girl, she was not at all sure she knew what it meant. She associated the term with 'the choking game', which she had vaguely heard about at school, and that frightened her terribly. To correct that false impression, Van Orst explained that by putting Mona into a hypnotic state, he could temporarily place her under his influence. Such an experiment would allow him to go back in time and bring her to that original moment when her sight had gone out, make her relive it and, potentially, identify the cause. Paul flared up. There was no question of it; it was dangerous. Van Orst did not press the matter: for a child to be hypnotised effectively, she must give herself over in complete trust. But between Mona's prejudice and her father's angry overreaction, the situation was now a minefield. Camille, for her part, said nothing.

So Van Orst prescribed for the young patient a standard course of treatment: weekly blood tests and blood-pressure checks, visits to the ophthalmologist, ten days of convalescence. He instructed Paul and Camille to watch for 'any appearance of subjective signs of a symptomatic nature', which meant they had to pay extremely close attention to whatever their daughter might be feeling. With that in mind, he suggested a consultation with a child psychiatrist:

- It's everyday preventive care rather than therapy proper, he assured them.

Paul and Camille took in his recommendations only hazily, but at bottom there was only one question preying on them: 'Was Mona, in the long run, at risk of losing her sight?' Curiously, Dr Van Orst never once mentioned the threat of a permanent relapse and, despite the dread it inspired in the parents, they preferred to avoid the subject. They even told themselves that, after all, if the doctor was skirting it, then there was no reason to raise it.

Henry Vuillemin tackled the matter head-on with his daughter. He was not the sort to shy away from questions, even when they opened onto an abyss. Usually so sparing with phone calls, except when he wanted to hear Mona's voice, he made call after call that week. In a warm, impassioned voice, he badgered Camille: yes or no, was his darling granddaughter, the treasure of his life, going to go blind? Henry also insisted on seeing Mona, and Camille could hardly refuse him. She suggested he come on Sunday, All Saints' Day, exactly one week after the episode of blindness. Paul, who could guess the tenor of the conversation, secretly resigned himself and drained, almost in one gulp, a glass of rough Burgundy. In front of his father-in-law, he felt miserably stupid. Mona, by contrast, when she heard the news, bounced with impatience.

This grandfather, with all his years and all his strength, she loved. She also loved watching how everyone who crossed his path fell under the spell of his endless silhouette and his heavy glasses with their thick, almost square frames. In his company, she felt protected. And transported. Henry had always made a point of speaking to her as he would have spoken to an adult. It was she who wanted that imbalance and savoured it, amused by it. She was never afraid she might not understand, and laughed at mistakes and misunderstandings. For she, in turn, kept a close eye on her own language, and it was obviously more sport than import.

Henry had no wish to make her into a little trained monkey. He did not want to be one of those caricatures of grandfathers who hunt down the mistakes of the young only to correct them in a pedantic voice. It was not in his nature. He had never made her do her homework or meddled in her report cards. Besides, he loved the way Mona expressed herself. More than that, he was fascinated by her turns of phrase. Why? There he was at a loss. He could not understand it. He had always been obsessed, haunted, by something in her childish speech. Was it something extra, or some je ne sais quoi missing? A quality or a flaw? The impression was all the more troubling because it was not new: Mona's 'little music' had always harboured an enigma that Henry was fully determined to uncover one day, by listening hard enough.

Camille sometimes conceded her surprise at a relationship she judged "too good to be true," but she recognised that it worked wonderfully and that her daughter was happy that way. And Henry, who readily quoted Victor Hugo's The Art of Being a Grandfather, reminded anyone who cared to listen of one of the cardinal principles of passing things on: it mattered little if one did not immediately understand everything someone said, as though every new word had to be, already, a tree in full bloom in the immense orchard of the brain. The blossomings would know when to come, on the appointed day, provided the furrows had been drawn and the seeds planted.

Those furrows and those seeds, in Henry Vuillemin, it was a flow of rich, assured words that caught you from the first inflection and never let go; it was a way of speaking that was very simple, yet of such breadth that it was exhilarating; it was a storyteller's cadence that often quickened, then slowed and softened with feeling. It was a rolling force of worldly experience and quiet erudition.

So with this 'Dadé,' the relationship was of a kind apart. Between grandparents and grandchildren, grandchildren and grandparents, a miraculous bond sometimes forms, because by a kind of existential curve the elders, from the height of their years, return to the feelings of their earliest youth and grasp, better than anyone, the springtime of life.

Henry Vuillemin lived in a handsome apartment on Avenue Ledru-Rollin, just above the Bistrot du Peintre, a narrow, wood-paneled establishment in an imitation Art Nouveau style. Every morning he went down there and kept to the same routine: coffee and a croissant, reading the national papers, a word here and there with the general run of customers and the waiters on break. He felt he belonged to an older world and, as part of his daily ritual, would walk very slowly to Place de la Bastille; he liked to examine the furniture in the shop windows along Rue du Faubourg-Saint-Antoine, then make his way back toward Place de la République by way of the median of Boulevard Richard-Lenoir before turning onto Boulevard Voltaire. Late in the afternoon, at home, he leafed through the art books piled to the ceiling. Henry, who stood a centimeter taller than General de Gaulle, would lay hands on the most inaccessible volumes without step stool or ladder, and by a curious chance those were regularly the very ones that drew him most. His memory was prodigious, though one had to distinguish between his propensity to talk about what he knew and his personal memories, which he shielded beneath layers of reserve. Mona knew the rule. The only forbidden subject with her grandfather was Colette Vuillemin, who had left him a widower seven years earlier. In tune with her father, Camille never said anything about her either. Much as the child tried from time to time to make an opening, she came up against a leaden silence. Colette was not spoken of. Never. The only exception to the taboo was the talisman Henry wore around his neck in tribute to his late wife. It was a pretty little cerith shell strung on fishing line, which he had picked up with her on the Côte d'Azur in the summer of 1963; he no longer knew exactly which day, but he remembered the scorching heat and a great many things he had sworn to Colette. Mona, as we have said, wore the same pendant, inherited from her grandmother.

We all have our ways of swearing. Henry Vuillemin swore by "all that is beautiful on earth." The phrase surprised Mona, and whenever she heard it she would invariably shrug with a little embarrassed laugh: all that is beautiful on earth was at once a little bit everything and a little bit nothing. And then she wondered whether he, her revered grandfather, was part of it too. Henry had obviously been a handsome young man and remained imposing, charming, spectacular. His gaunt, keen octogenarian face radiated a vigor and intelligence of formidable allure. But he was scarred. A scar slashed the right side of his face, beginning beneath the cheekbone and running up to the eyebrow. The wound must have been very painful. It had torn away not only a strip of skin, but a piece of the cornea as well. It was a war wound. A horrible one: on September 17, 1982, while on assignment in Lebanon for Agence France-Presse, a Phalangist had slashed him with a knife to stop him from going any farther. He was approaching the camp at Shatila. Rumor had it that massacres were taking place there, that Palestinian refugees were being arbitrarily executed, without trial, in reprisal for the assassination of President Bachir Gemayel. He had wanted to verify it, to bear witness. They had blocked his path with inhuman violence. Henry had lost a great deal of blood and the use of one eye. That infirmity, together with his great height and, as the years went by, his increasing thinness, had given his appearance a supernatural air. The handsome reporter, who looked like Eddie Constantine, had become a figure of legend.

On All Saints' Day, Mona was in fine form. Her parents had done their best to cheer up November's leaden atmosphere. Jade and Lili, her two friends, had come over to watch one of the Toy Story films, in which toys come to life. They had been fooling around a little. Jade especially. She was a pretty, impish little girl, with a fine Eurasian cast to her eyes, olive skin, and hair combed to perfection. Yet she was driven by a surprising passion: making faces. She knew how to turn her face into a shifting, deranged theatre where strange, burlesque expressions came racing through like actors in a frenzy. Mona, delighted, always wanted more.

At seven o'clock the intercom buzzed. Paul pursed his lips and raised his eyebrows. Camille pressed the button:

"Dad?"

It was him, punctual to the minute. After greeting him, Paul left to take Jade and Lili back to their respective parents, and for the time it took, Mona, her mother, and her grandfather were alone together in the flat. After an irrepressible outpouring of joy, the little girl, who had kept her mishap from her two friends, launched into a detailed account of her sixty-three-minute ordeal and the trials she had gone through at the hospital. Camille did not interrupt.

Yet while he listened to Mona talk and talk, Henry was examining the surroundings the child lived in with purely clinical detachment. Even her bedroom, despite the decor's enchanting tackiness, struck him as extremely sad. That wallpaper patterned with garlands of flowers, those glitter-studded trinkets shaped like hearts or animals, those pink or brown soft toys, those grotesque posters of stars barely out of adolescence, that plastic jewellery, that furniture modelled on that of cartoon princesses... The acid-bright colours of all that clutter caught at his throat. In that whole collection, dripping with bad taste, there were only two openings onto a kind of beauty. A sturdy American industrial lamp from the 1950s, with an articulated arm, which Paul had unearthed, given to Mona, and fixed to her little writing desk. And then, above the bed, in a frame, an exhibition poster reproducing a painting. It crackled with colours of extraordinary subtlety, cool in tone. They traced a nude woman, in profile and leaning forward, seated on a stool draped with white cloth, her left ankle resting on her right knee. In one corner it read: "Musée d'Orsay Paris - Georges Seurat (1859-1891)".

Despite these exceptions, Henry made the disheartening observation that, for convenience's sake, childhood is mostly filled with futile, ugly objects. Mona was no exception. Beauty, true artistic beauty, slipped into her daily life only by stealth. This was perfectly normal, Henry noted: the refining of taste, the shaping of sensibility, would come later. Except that - and the thought strangled him - Mona had very nearly lost her sight, and if her eyes were to go dark for good in the days, weeks, or months ahead, she would carry with her, into the far reaches of her memory, only the recollection of gaudy, empty things. A whole life in the dark, mentally making do with the worst the world produces, memory offering no escape? It was impossible. It was terrifying.

To his daughter's great annoyance, Henry remained taciturn and distant throughout dinner. When Mona finally went to bed, Camille, with a resolute look, turned up the volume of Coltrane's saxophone playing on an old chrome jukebox, to mask their voices and make sure the little girl heard nothing.

"Papa. Mona seems, for the moment, to be digesting... (she hesitated over the words) ...what just happened fairly well. But the doctor recommends she see a child psychiatrist. It may feel strange to her, and I was wondering if you could take her to the appointment, so she'd feel reassured..."

"A shrink? Is that really what's going to keep her from going blind?"

"That's not the point, Papa!"

"I think it is, and it will be until you dare put the question to the doctor! The doctor, what's his name again?"

"His name is Van Orst, and he's very good," Paul put in awkwardly, trying to join the conversation.

"Papa, wait," Camille went on. "Listen to me. Paul and I are going to do everything we can to make sure nothing happens to Mona, do you hear me? But she's ten, and we can't act as if she's been through nothing. The doctor says her psychological balance is a priority. I'm only asking whether you'd be willing to take care of that, because I know Mona will trust you. Do you hear me, Papa?"

Henry heard perfectly well. But at that precise moment there kindled in his mind, in a fraction of a second, an Apollonian idea, which he jealously kept to himself. He would not take his granddaughter to a child psychiatrist, no... Instead, he would have her undergo a cure of a wholly different kind, one capable of compensating for the ugliness in which her childhood was being steeped.

Mona, who trusted him completely, who credited him as she did no other adult, would have to go with him to the places where the most beautiful and the most human things the world has produced are kept: she would have to go with him to museums. If, by some misfortune, Mona one day went blind forever, she would at least have a sort of reservoir, deep in her brain, from which to draw visual splendors. So the grandfather had his plan... Once a week, following an unchanging ritual, he would take Mona by the hand and lead her to contemplate one work, one alone, first in a long silence, so that the infinite delight of colors and lines might enter his granddaughter's mind, then in words, so that she would move beyond visual ravishment and understand how artists speak to us of life, and how much light they shed on it.

For his little Mona, then, he had something better than medicine in mind. They would go to the Louvre first, the Musee d'Orsay next, and finally Beaubourg. There, yes, there, in those places devoted to preserving the boldest and most beautiful things humanity had produced, he would find a tonic for his granddaughter. Henry was not one of those art lovers who are content, privately and apart from the world, with the sheen of flesh painted by Raphael or the rhythm of a line scored by Degas's charcoals. He loved the almost incendiary qualities of works of art. Sometimes he said, "Art is pyrotechnics, or it's hot air." And he loved it when a painting, a sculpture, a photograph, in its entirety or through a single detail, could fan our sense of existence into flame.

At the moment Camille was asking for his help, Henry was assailed by hundreds of images: the rocky masses behind the Mona Lisa, the monkey sculpted on the back of Michelangelo's Dying Slave, the alarmed expression of the blond-curled child to the right of The Oath of the Horatii; the strange gelatinous loins of Goya's Lamb; and then the clods of earth in Rosa Bonheur's Ploughing in the Nivernais; the butterfly-shaped signature Whistler used in the portrait of his mother; the tottering apse of Van Gogh's church... Or else Kandinsky's colors, Picasso's fractures, Soulages's outrenoir. All of it burst forth like so many signs calling him, asking to be seen, heard, understood, loved. Like a counterfire against the ashes threatening Mona's eyes.

Henry broke into a broad smile:"

- Very well. I shall take Mona with me every Wednesday afternoon. From now on, I, and I alone, will handle this psychological care. It will be between the two of us. Do we agree?

- You'll find someone good, Papa? You'll ask your old friends for advice?

- Do we agree in principle? I shall handle it without questions or interference from anyone.

- But you aren't going to pick a child psychiatrist at random, do you hear me? You'll be very careful.

- Do you trust me, my dear?

- Yes, Paul said with authority, forestalling any hesitation from Camille. Mona admires you and respects you, and she loves you as she loves no one else, so yes, we trust you.

Camille, at her husband's firm words, nodded tenderly without adding anything. Henry felt a faint, moist glimmer pass through his good eye. Coltrane's saxophone made the walls ripple. Mona, in her room, slept, watched over by Georges Seurat.



Part I



THE LOUVRE

Sandro Botticelli



Learn to Receive



The great glass pyramid amused Mona. Raised insolently amid the stone pavilions of the Louvre Palace, its ethereal shape, its transparency, the way it caught the cold November sun, enchanted her. Her grandfather was not saying much. Even so, she could see perfectly well that he was in an excellent mood, for he held her little hand in his with the assured tenderness of happy people and swung their arms lightly as they walked. His joviality, though wordless, radiated in a childlike way.

- What a beautiful pyramid, Dadé! It looks like a giant Chinese hat, Mona pronounced, cutting through the clusters of tourists on the forecourt.



Henry looked at her, a sort of doubtful pout folded into his smile. It made him look so odd that the little girl snickered. They entered the glass structure, passed through security, rode down an escalator, found themselves in the immense hall so little different from those in railway stations or airports, and made their way to the Denon Wing. The bustle around them was stifling. Stifling, yes, because most of the visitors who make up the crowd in a great museum have no idea what they want to do; they create a general drift, steeping the air in the stagnant, hesitant, faintly murky atmosphere peculiar to such places when they become victims of their own success.

In the middle of the hubbub, Henry bent his immense, skinny legs so that he could speak to his granddaughter, looking her straight in the eye. He did this whenever he had something truly important to tell her. His mineral voice, pure and deep, drowned out the surrounding din. It seemed to silence the vain chatter and wearying outbursts of the entire universe.

- Mona, every week the two of us will go and look at one work of art in the museum - one work, no more. The people around us would like to swallow everything in one gulp, and they lose themselves because they do not know how to pace their desires. We shall be much wiser, much more reasonable. We shall look at a single work, first without saying a word, for many minutes, and then, afterward, we shall talk about it.

- Really? I thought we were going to the doctor. (She would have liked to say child psychiatrist, but she was not quite sure of the word.)

- Tell me, Mona, do you want to go to the psychiatrist afterward? Is that important to you?

- Yeah right, like I want that! Anything but that!



- Then listen to me carefully, my dear. There will be no need for it if you look closely at what we are going to see.

- Really? And is it serious if we don't go to the... doctor?

- No, it isn't. I swear it to you by all that is beautiful on earth.

After making their way through a maze of staircases, Henry and Mona found themselves in a small room, one people passed through constantly but where no one, or almost no one, bothered to rest their eyes on the work that held pride of place there. Henry let go of his granddaughter's hand and said to her with infinite gentleness:

- Now look, Mona. Take as many minutes as you need to look, to really look.

And Mona planted herself there, intimidated, in front of a very damaged painting, badly cracked in several places, with pieces missing, a painting that, at first glance, gave off a sense of a past both dimmed and far away. Henry was looking at it too, but he was studying his granddaughter above all, sensing her wavering, her perplexity. There she was, knitting her brows, then stifling a slightly embarrassed laugh. He knew that even in front of a Renaissance masterpiece, a ten-year-old girl, however lively, curious, sensitive, and clever she might be, could not fall into rapture straightaway. He knew that, contrary to what people say, it took time for art to yield its depth, that it was a painstaking exercise and not some effortless rapture. He also knew that Mona, because it was he who was asking it of her, would play along and that, despite her embarrassment, she would scrutinize the shapes, the colors, the texture with the attention she had promised.

The image was simple in composition. At the far left, one could make out a fountain, before which four young women with long, curly hair, astonishingly alike, fanned out like figures in a frieze. They held one another by the arm, intertwining as though they formed a human garland made rhythmic by the variety of their clothes: green and mauve for the first, white for the second, pink for the third, orange-yellow for the fourth. This multicolored procession gave the impression of moving forward and, facing it, on the right-hand side of the work, isolated against a neutral background, stood a fifth woman, young, extremely beautiful, wearing a magnificent pendant and a purple dress. She too seemed animated by a forward motion, as though she were going to meet the procession. She was even holding out toward it a sort of cloth, into which one of the figures, the one in pink, was delicately placing something. What exactly? Impossible to say. The object had faded away. In the foreground, in one corner, there was also a little blond boy, shown in profile, with a hint of laughter on his face. The setting was utterly bare: answering the fountain on the left, there was only a truncated, heavily faded column closing the scene on the right.

Mona played along. But after six minutes, it was already too much. Six minutes in front of a worn-out image were an unusual and painful ordeal. So she turned to her grandfather and struck up a conversation with an insolence no one but her could get away with:

- Dadé, your painting's really battered! Next to it, your face looks brand-new...

Henry looked at the work and all the damage scarring it. He crouched down.

- You'd do better to listen to me instead of saying silly things... A 'painting,' you say! Not quite! First of all, Mona, it isn't a 'painting.' It's what's called a 'fresco.' Do you know what a fresco is?

- Yes, I think so... but I forgot!

- A fresco is a painting that you paint on a wall, and it's very fragile, because if the wall gets damaged -- and walls crumble a great deal over time -- well, the painting gets damaged too...

- Why did the artist paint on that wall? Because it's the Louvre?

- Not at all. It's true an artist might want to paint a fresco in the Louvre, because the Louvre is the largest museum on earth, and it would only be natural for a painter to want to place his work directly on its walls, so that it would be like the palace's very skin. But you see, Mona, the Louvre has not always been a museum. It has only been one for about two hundred years. Before that, it was a castle, where the kings and their court lived. When the artist painted this fresco, it was around 1485. So he did not create it for the walls of the Louvre, but for those of a villa in Florence.

- Florence? (She absently fingered the pendant around her neck.) Me, it sounds like the name of an old fiancee of yours, before Grandma, right?

- Doesn't ring a bell, but it's not impossible! Now listen. Florence is an Italian city. In Tuscany, to be precise. And it is the cradle of what we call the Renaissance. In the fifteenth century - the Quattrocento, as the Italians say - there was extraordinary ferment in Florence. It had about a hundred thousand inhabitants, and the city was prosperous thanks to trade and banking. And you see, religious orders, high political dignitaries, and even citizens high up on the social ladder wanted to put their wealth to use and show their prestige by supporting the artists of their own time. They are known as great patrons. So painters, sculptors, and architects benefited from that trust and from the means they were given to create paintings, statues, and buildings of extraordinary beauty.

- They were made of gold, I'll bet...

- Not quite. In the Middle Ages there certainly were very beautiful paintings lavishly covered in gold leaf. That gave the object value, and it symbolized divine light as well! But in the Renaissance, painting gradually turned away from the gaudy effect of gilding and tried instead to render reality more faithfully as we see it, with its landscapes, the individuality of faces, animals, the movement of living beings, of things, of the sky and the sea.

- People love nature, is that it?

- That's exactly it: people began to love nature. But you know, when we speak of nature, we're not talking only about what grows from the ground.

- Oh, and what else do we mean?

- We are also speaking, in a more abstract way, of human nature. Human nature is what we are deep down, with our dark places and our bright ones, our flaws and our qualities, our fears and our hopes. And you see, it is precisely that human nature the artist is trying to improve.

- And how?

- If you tend your garden, you help nature. You allow it to flourish. This fresco is trying to help human nature by telling it something very simple, yet essential, something you must remember forever, Mona.

But Mona, to provoke the old man, plugged her ears and shut her eyes, as though she wanted neither to hear nor see what he might say to her. After a few seconds, she discreetly cracked one eyelid open to watch his reaction. He was smiling nonchalantly. So she gave up her little game and turned all her attention to him. For she could feel that her grandfather, after those long minutes of silence, contemplation, and discussion, after that little journey through the damaged image unfurling before her eyes, was finally about to reveal to her one of those secrets that remain etched deep in the heart.

Henry motioned for her to turn her gaze to the slightly faded area, where there seemed to be an object the young woman on the right was taking into her hands. The little girl did as he asked.

- That procession of four women on the left is made up of Venus and the three Graces. They are generous deities. And they are offering a gift - we do not know what it is, because some of the paint is missing - to a young girl. The three Graces are what we call allegories, Mona: they do not exist in real life and you will never meet them, but they represent important values. They are said to represent the three stages that make us sociable and hospitable beings, that is to say truly human beings. And this fresco tells us how vital those three stages are; it seeks to root them in each of us.

- Three stages? What stages?

- The first is knowing how to give, the third is knowing how to give back. And between the two there is one without which nothing is possible, a sort of keystone, the keystone that supports all of human nature.

- Which one, Dadé?

- Look: what is the young girl on the right doing?

- You told me: she's lucky because she's getting a present...

- Exactly, Mona. She is receiving a gift. And that is absolutely fundamental. Knowing how to receive. What this fresco says is that we must learn how to receive, that human nature, if it is to be capable of great and beautiful things, must be ready to welcome: the kindness of others, their desire to please, what it does not yet have and what it is not yet. There will always be time for the one who receives to give back, but to give back, that is, to give again, one must first have been capable of receiving. Do you understand, Mona?

- Your story's complicated, but yes, I think I understand...

- I am certain you do! And you see, if these ladies are so beautiful, with such supple, graceful drawing, that unbroken line, without any abruptness or hesitation, it is to express the importance of that continuity, of that chain that must bind human beings to one another and improve their nature: give, receive, and give back; give, receive, and give back; give, receive, and give back...

Mona no longer knew what to say. Above all, she did not want to disappoint her grandfather. She had already tried joking during their conversation and fell silent so as not to add anything too naive, because she knew perfectly well that he was speaking to her and taking her through that immense museum so that she would grow up a little. For the moment, she felt only a tug inside, because this summons to grow up, this heady rush of exploring a new world, had an extraordinary magnetic power, all the more so because it came from Henry, whom she revered. Yet deep in her soul there was already that terrible foreboding that what one gives back, one will never find again. And that regret, sharp though very distant, for a childhood gone forever twisted her heart.

- Shall we go, Dadé? Let's get moving, troublemaker?

- Come on, Mona! Let's go!

Henry took her hand in his again and they left the Louvre slowly, without a word. Outside, night was beginning to fall. Henry knew full well the trouble that had just made his granddaughter waver. But he flatly refused to spare people on the pretext of ensuring that their time together held only good moments, full and beguiling. No: he knew perfectly well that existence is worthwhile only if one accepts its roughness, and that, once time has sifted it, it yields something precious and fertile, something beautiful and useful, that lets life truly be life.

Besides, by that miracle of childhood, Mona's distress did not last long: walking along merrily, she began to hum. Henry never interrupted her at such moments; he found them incredibly touching. Then suddenly, as they neared home, Mona stopped, brought up short by the lie they had agreed on between them to avoid the sessions with the child psychiatrist. She opened her big blue eyes wide and tilted her little mischievous head, laughing at the trick they were playing on her parents.

- Dadé, what do I say if Mum and Dad ask me the name of the doctor I went to see?

- Tell them his name is Dr Botticelli.

The All Saints' Day holidays passed quickly, and Mona went back to school. Camille arrived early, around eight o'clock, under the wan préau that kept off an unpleasant autumn rain. She entrusted her daughter to Mme Hadji, quickly explaining how Mona's convalescence was going and the medical follow-up now in place, punctuated, among other things, by a weekly appointment with a child psychiatrist. She insisted: the teacher should certainly be attentive to Mona, but without showing her any special consideration, nothing that would set her apart from her classmates.
