Fresh rain on blue lawn. Damp summer grass, the must of black earth. Always those August showers on the close-cropped blades, burnt gold. The heavy drops stream down the pane, winding, snaking, twining together in long ribbons of liquid light. How many afternoons spent behind the curtain's gauzy veil, tracing with a fingertip their path, nervous and languid at once. Little flyaway hairs curl around the cheeks, and you marvel that they can be so white when you are so young, haloed with ether beneath the window. And suddenly the gaze drops from the window to the hand drawing the curtain aside, and the hand is old, so old.

There are places that harpoon you. That wind their netting around your dreams, set their claws just so, enough to let you grow, yet leave in your flesh the bruise of their grip.

There are doors whose sound, when you push them open, is like time's cry, still shattering oblivion.

There are staircases whose steps you would so dearly love to climb again, just once, letting the banister's cold smoothness run through your palm.



Now that is the House.



By contrast, there are places that send the clammy unease of the unfamiliar sliding down the back of your neck. Places that strike no chord within you. Places forced on you, that you are made to endure, it's temporary, it's only for now, there's no choice. When the world grows tired of you, they offer you the TV by the bed, the bedside table for your glasses case, the yellow wallpaper. Yellow is cheerful, and besides there's everything you need here, we'll take care of you. They make us trade the House for a room in a medically sanctioned dying-house. That yellow wallpaper: a hammer blow to the back of my old neck.

In any case, I love nothing now but the armchair. It is the only piece of furniture I was able to bring with me. It has always been an old person's armchair: my great-grandfather's, whom I never knew, then Great-Aunt Babel's, when she came to visit us. I understand why we come to love it as we grow old; we recognize ourselves in its dust, in its sagging softness. With the tip of my nail I worry at the scratches in its old-rose velvet.

The comfort of settling your arms into the intimacy of the armrests. The fabric slides close against you like a second skin. It is like slipping back into the embrace of an over-sweet lover, one who would have remained far more constant than all the men I might have known.



Thinking it over, I was the only one who ever truly loved the House. Even my great-great-grandfather, who desired it, imagined it, built it, did not love it as much as I did. I loved it enough to remain there all my life. To abandon my studies in the City because I suffered too much away from the woods of my childhood. To say no to Oktav, when he held out the little ring to me on the Pont-Noir one Wednesday afternoon, because he would never have wanted us to live in the House. He wanted us to move into a large apartment in the old town, not far from the lycée where he would have taught and where our future children would have learned to surpass us. I loved the House enough to wish for nothing else, in all my life, but to remain there, nestled in the hollow of familiar things, letting time lay its patina on me exactly as it did on the banister of the spiral staircase.

I always close my eyes when I want to remember it. I do it every day, every conscious minute, perhaps. I strive, impatiently, violently even, to recreate every room, every corner. I cling to the details: the shape of the light switches, the sound of the doorknobs turning, the thin film of dust on the yellow bulbs. I want to see it all again, feel it all again. I want to hollow out the space of the present and bring surging back, by sheer force of maddened memory, the places I loved so much, knew by heart, trod all my life, and which, now that I am no longer there, are fading, coming apart.



I remember the House as I left it, four months ago, perhaps five. I remember it a little too well, even. The slow decay of the depopulated rooms, the dulled parquet floors, the cold corridors where the paint peels, that much, yes. The broken pane in the stained-glass window through which dead leaves blow in, eddying feebly over the hall floor like an exhausted couple waltzing at the end of the night, I can still see it, very clearly. And the cast-iron radiators too, faded white, icy in the rooms no one lives in anymore, the fireplaces bricked over, the sheets over the furniture in the bedrooms where no one sleeps anymore. That was how it was, at the end.



I want to banish that image of the House. Brighten the colors again, fling wide the bright windows where the intoxicating morning air rushes in, wax the staircase, clean the enormous dining-room table. Lay the table for fifteen or so, as in the heyday when everyone came, without exception.



I want to see again the tall House of white clapboards that sprang into view when we came back from the garden, nestled among the great blue spruces and maples, with the grass breaking into emerald sprays across the sweep of the front steps. To see again the facade with its narrow windows, the veranda jutting out on the left, the roof high and pointed, bristling with chimneys and immaculate white gables, with the attic's bull's-eye window perched like the round golden eye of some reassuring giant, all ringed with carved wood. I want to see again the strange sharp roofs that crowned the bay windows and in winter grew fringes of stalactites. I want to see again the windows glittering in the fresh air, caressed by the branches of the trees that stood too close to the House when a light breeze blew. I want to see again the great House, with the carved wooden columns framing the front door, glazed in a warm varnish of hard caramel, and the stained-glass window of intertwined flowers that let bursts of color filter into the hall when the sun shone through. Painted with immense trellises of tropical foliage, the hall gleamed a soft blue. There the spiral staircase swept upward in a whirl of coppery wood.

Everything was open, the scents from the kitchen circulated freely and invaded the whole House. Voices rumbled from the sitting room to the attic like the murmur of a reassuring torrent that might roll through the walls. Deep in my ear, I still hear. The floorboards creak and breathe out the scent of pine, and even our lightest footsteps as children make the thick planks groan. We run up the stairs because it's more fun to arrive dizzy. The upstairs corridor leads to Uncle's and Aunt's old bedrooms, the ones they had when they were little and still sleep in when they come back to see us with the cousins. They sleep right across from our room, Harriett's and mine. It's better for playing: they only have to cross the corridor and whisper the password at the door so we know perfectly well they aren't annoying adults who would come and scatter everything with their big clumsy legs. At the very end of the corridor is the bathroom, the green one, with the window that sticks a little but through which we can see a bit of the orchard. Opposite is Great-Aunt Babel's room, for when she comes and sets down her camphor-reeking trunks. When everyone leaves again, we have the floor almost to ourselves. The next floor up, we can hardly play there; it's our parents' floor, the serious floor, the one with the two most spacious bathrooms. And then Louisa, that lucky thing, has a private one, with a little ceramic bathtub behind her wardrobe. Because she's the eldest of the girls, she has the biggest bedroom, and all to herself. Still, I wouldn't trade my room for hers for anything in the world, because if I did, Harriett and I couldn't make as much noise, with our parents' room so close by. That's where the closet is too, though we don't even use it for hide-and-seek anymore: we know perfectly well it's the first place you think of when you need to slip away from the world. The best place to hide, of course, is the attic. It's smaller than when Little Father was a child because, since then, they've split it in two to make Klaus's room. I like Klaus's room. It smells of boy, because he doesn't open the window much. Since the adults never come up there, we're left in peace. On stormy evenings we hear every creak of the roof, and there's something indefinably reassuring about this attic room, warm as a wooden cocoon suspended above the forest.

But the images drift past in faded colors, drained of life. The faces are blurred, and that is the most painful thing: realizing that the image of my family, young, alive, is lost forever. I too have lost myself. Who was I, at eight? Now that old age is breaking my back and cracking my fingers, I can feel how irritated I would have been, as a child, by my presence today, encumbered by what is no more. I would loathe the puppet theater I set up in my mind to replay the dead images without end. Who were we, in the woods and in the bedroom, in the kitchen where the soup steams? I shake puppets whose faces are fading. No matter: there comes a point when some people are so familiar to us that we no longer even need their physical presence for them to be there. The only people we can truly say we know are those we grew up with. Our own growth has been faintly tinged by that of the others, like water into which a drop of syrup falls, a single drop, enough to tint the whole glass pale mint. I know exactly what Little Father or Louisa would have replied to this or that remark, I still see Little Mother's smile when she watched us dance on spring evenings. Then it seems to me that, for a time, in this body of today, this painful, creaking body, I relive the days that ended and the days that followed them, deep in the House.

I want to remember the voices of glorious children tangling together, the cold, guarded tenderness of Little Father, the bursts of inspiration in Little Mother when she would suddenly rise from the table, seize the brushes from the jar on the sideboard, and rush to her studio in a corner of the veranda. I want to remember when Klaus, Louisa, and Harriett were still at the House, Klaus already at the edge of adolescence. He was handsome, that prodigy of an elder brother, brilliant, funny, insolently gifted. I would like to grumble again when he ran through his scales at six in the morning and the sweet, hoarse lament of his trumpet woke the whole family. I want to find Louisa again, the prettiest of the girls, as though she had absorbed all our mother's radiance, leaving none for Harriett or me. I remember that when I went past, dragging my feet across the second-floor boards, past Louisa's half-open door, she was neither on her bed nor in her armchair, but always before the mirror in her dressing room, dissolving her eyes into their reflection, watching for flecks of gold deep in the iris.

What I would give to see again the room with the two little beds side by side, Harriett's and mine, with the lamp between them, the cause of so many quarrels between us, because I always wanted to read later than she did! That sly baby face of hers she hid under the covers, yelling that I was keeping her from carrying out her eight hours of sleep… "Then don't get up so early," I would gloat, turning the pages of my novel slowly to make her furious. "You know Klaus is going to wake us up again with his blasted trumpet, so turn it off!" she would protest, indignant, tears brimming in her eyes, pounding her pillow. "I'll turn it off when I've finished my chapter." "Let me see where you are." I showed her the wrong page. "You see, nearly finished," I would assure her. She would start hissing like a snake: I was lying, I was the worst sister in the world, and if she told Louisa… She would let the threat hang and shoot me a furious look from under the sheets. Harriett was always turning to our eldest sister to settle our disputes, because she was so utterly partial and unjust. She always sided with our youngest, who had adorable dark curls that Louisa loved to arrange, whereas she said my thatch was "perplexifying." But Harriett was far from docile, so more often than not Louisa would walk out of the room slamming the door, and shut herself away in her own room to dream of grand chandeliers and dinners with friends.



Even the House of adolescence — I would like to see it again. The one that haunted me every evening in my student room and made me hate the College, curse my studies. They had torn me away from my home, from my life, from my walks in the woods and my afternoons spent reading, curled up on the too-hard window ledge. But what a triumph Friday evenings were! When I would rush onto the train, spring into my seat, stamp my feet with impatience, my lips stretched into an irrepressible smile, and the inexorable machine carried me away from the towers of the City! I loved arriving at the station then, though it still seemed endless to me, and the moment when I saw Little Father by the open trunk waiting for my suitcase, with Harriett already in the car, because on Fridays she finished earlier.



"Madame Aberfletch? Can you hear me? I'm putting your tray on the desk; you can eat whenever you like."



And there is that kind nurse, coming to sweep it all away with her rose perfume. Devil take her, with her white blouse and her insolent youth, her clear voice, the voice of a living girl, a girl who goes home in the evening. She must wait all year for summer, that season of the liquid body, of blond evenings.



Summer



There was nothing I loved more than summers at the House. Everything shone then, in the humid languor of the holidays, which seemed endless, stretched out by the long days of delicious boredom. With the first heat of June, everything began to shimmer, to overflow with life. The maples and the firs seemed swollen with incandescent sap; the grass, an insolent green, was crossed by broad flat washes of sunlight.

Everyone came back from the City, flowed back toward the familiar countryside and the forests haloed in lustral shade. It was enough simply to stand at the edge of the woods to feel the resin-perfumed wind exhale its murmur. When we were children, my father's brother and sister, Uncle Bertie and Aunt Hilde, came every summer with their families. What joy when someone announced, "The cousins are arriving tonight!" Then the day became steeped in an impatient agitation that had us hopping in circles in the sitting room and, scolded by Little Father, we went back upstairs stifling nervous laughter. The afternoon passed in busy preparations; I loved helping my parents, paying meticulous attention to everything. Like a little despot, I made sure the attic was aired out, the toy chests were open, the sheets neatly folded on the beds. I flew down the spiral staircase that had been waxed for the occasion; I whirled through the hall shot through with the crushed light of the stained-glass window. I went into the kitchen twenty times, I bent over the fire, I breathed in the rich aromas as I lifted, with a slightly trembling hand, the lids of the tureens. I knew the nearly ready food was the signal: the cousins would soon be here, and before long we would all be around the table together in an unbearable din of laughter, releasing the frenzy of a long day of waiting.

The evening the cousins arrived, at the start of every summer, was always the same. They were generally exhausted by the drive they had endured all day, by the stifling closeness of the cars, by the hot breath of the tar, by the sun reflected off gleaming bodywork. Even so, we sat down to table in high spirits. When everything seemed to waltz with light and laughter and scents, when we were endlessly interrupted in our stories by a dish going by, or by another story springing up at the other end of the table, and when I leaned back in my chair to furtively study the faces of those we had not seen since Christmas, I felt immersed in a bath of liquid joy, golden and sparkling, perfumed by the honey smell of Uncle Bertie's cigars or by the smell of thyme dissolving in the chicken juices. My gaze went around the table and glided almost automatically over the familiar faces until it caught on the cousins', prettier year after year. I was fascinated above all by their noses, small and round, which came from Bertie's in-laws. He himself had not escaped the family nose, long and hooked, with light gliding over it as over a blade. Amelia and Magda had taken everything from their mother, a kind of fair, full-fleshed grace, with skin all pink below the neck, a skin of fresh flowers beaded with water in crystal vases. Always sitting side by side, carefully passing the pitcher along when it came their way, they laughed freely at every jest of Klaus's or Harriett's. Every summer, the two cousins and Louisa sized one another up the moment the cousins stepped out of the car and set foot on the front steps. In those first moments of their reunion, we could never tell whether Louisa and the cousins were about to tear one another apart or exchange some pretty-girl barb; but no, they fell into one another's arms and went laughing into the House, trailing a scent of precious oils in their wake. Harriett and I spent little time with the cousins, who shared Louisa's temperament and were her age. We were the little nuisances, noisy, a little alarming, I think. Klaus showed no particular interest in the cousins and sat at table beside Aleksander, my Aunt Hilde's cherished son. Aleksander paired the traditional Aberfletch nose with a gaze of steel that cut against the dirty-pond depth of our own eyes. His hair was a little ashier and paler than ours, and his very upright head caught the reflections from the dining-room lamp, which cast its warm halo over the whole table. Aleksander and Klaus did not get on especially well, but, being the oldest of the children, they had learned to stay together, in a kind of wary brotherhood halfway between rivalry and solidarity. They vied in dexterity when they poured themselves water, each trying to raise the carafe highest without the thin stream straying from its course. We clapped our hands, staring at the troubled water quickly filling the glass, and shrieked when it came close to overflowing.

The grown-ups were on edge; they sent us to bed early, telling us not to "carry on till all hours like last year." Then we exchanged a joyful glance with the cousins, our closeness suddenly rekindled by that reminiscence of a shared past. In our eyes, for a brief instant, memories filed past, drowned in summer light, aquiver with laughter and skinned knees. We danced along beside the cousins to their room, all excited to have them back with us, and Klaus let his warm voice, just beginning to break, rumble as he played the grown-up, saying, "I'm going up to my room, be good, children." Aleksander grumbled a little, because he was the same age as Klaus, but had to share a room with the two girl cousins, whom he cheerfully ignored most of the time. Then Louisa and Klaus sank back into the half-dark of the staircase, and we stayed, Harriett, the cousins, and I, in the dark hallway, the neutral ground between our two rooms. Through the half-open door, we saw with pleasure that their suitcases had been carried upstairs, as if by magic, by a devoted relative, and were resting on their respective beds in the yellow halo of the lamp. That room stood empty all year, with its bare, cold bedsteads, except when the cousins came. Then we spread out great cool sheets for them, still fragrant with the sprigs of lavender scattered through the wardrobes. The cousins' eyes were always tired on that first evening, but they seemed truly happy to be back. Harriett and I stayed in their doorway, watching them rush to the window as if to make sure the view had not changed since last summer. Then, satisfied, they rubbed their eyes as they yawned and wished us good night. I took Harriett by the shoulder to force her back into our room, because she was far too worked up to sleep and wanted, that very minute, to play hide-and-seek in the dark, or spy on the grown-ups, or catch Klaus up in the attic by creeping very softly up the stairs so they would not creak. I told her we had every evening of the whole summer for such games, and she followed me with a resignation tinged with pleasure.

In my bed, lying with my eyes wide open, my whole being turned toward the ceiling drowned in blue shadow, I felt the weight of the two floors above me, and I sensed the adults bustling below, clearing the table, with the warm rumble of their hoarse voices. I listened to the sounds of the night getting ready: Louisa upstairs, hurrying from her dressing room to her bed, the cousins across the corridor putting their things away in the wardrobes, arriving, settling in, reclaiming the room they came back to every summer. I felt surrounded by the people I loved, as though I were part of a whole, and the House, at last full of that great family, all of them my own blood, came alive. I knew it did not matter very much if we went to bed a little early today, because there would be other evenings. Harriett's little hoarse voice would always rise at last into the silence, and she would venture: "It's good they're here, the cousins." On other nights I would have told her to be quiet, because I did not feel like talking. But there I was, curled up in my sheets, and the summer wind blew soft against the shutters, outside. So I simply answered, "Yes, it really is." We talked about what we would do tomorrow. In the almost complete darkness of the room, all I could make out of Harriett was a little dark mass against a pillow swollen with pale shadow, enormous next to her head. And it was my favorite moment of the day, full of promises, of plans, of ideas made wilder still because they were already tangled in a half-consciousness full of dreams. We glided into silence like a spark across the surface of the pond, and the waves of sleep closed over our eyes.







We let the days flow by like a thread of liquid light. It was the precious time of elastic hours, of evanescent mornings, of endless afternoons.



Morning was always an every-man-for-himself affair. We scarcely noticed that the House was peopled by anyone besides ourselves, so silent was everything, with the closed doors of the late sleepers, the sitting room empty but still smelling of Bertie's honey-scented cigar.

I always woke before Harriett, because my bed was closest to the window, and a ray of white sunlight always filtered through the gap in the shutters, cutting a warm sliver across my sheets. I lay for a few minutes in the warm sheets, but I could hear the birds outside, the muddled buzz of wasps bumping against the window frame, and suddenly I had a mad longing to look at the garden, to see the sparkling morning lawn spread before my eyes, and to feel the glorious breeze billow my dress. I didn't open the shutters so as not to wake Harriett, and my eyes, used to the dimness, made out without too much trouble the obstacles between my bed and the door. I crept slowly out of the room, careful not to set the floorboards creaking too much. My toes first met the firmness of the boards, then the slightly rough rug that tickles the soles of my feet. I carefully avoided the jutting corners of our two beds, the toy chest, the wardrobe, and stole an anxious glance toward the little ball curled up under her flowered quilt, to make sure she hadn't stirred. Then I set one hand on the handle, the other on the latch, and pulled with one sharp tug so the door wouldn't squeal—for a badly oiled door always makes more noise when you open it slowly. The morning light flooding the corridor made me sway on my feet for a moment. The row of closed doors was daunting, and there was no way to tell whether I was the first one up or not. Still a little dizzy, I went down the stairs. Here again I had to keep the wood from groaning, watch where I stepped, choose certain steps over others, and so take giant strides, clinging to the banister to keep my balance. It was the first adventure of the day, repeated every morning.



When I crossed the empty hall, the foliage loomed, frightening, for the morning light threw a cold gleam across it, and the painted flowers looked blurred, like old stars fading away. The tiles were always icy, and I hurried on into the kitchen. There the clock ticked away, and this was the only time I noticed it, because the rest of the day its mechanical pulse was drowned by the noises of the House, the laughter, the voices, the discordant clatter of pots banging together as we put them away. The kitchen was cool; we had left the ground-floor windows and shutters open to let the night air circulate. In the morning we chased out the bark-colored butterflies that had crept in to nestle in the warmth of the walls. They fluttered about clumsily, their faltering wings batting at the panes without finding the way out.

I shut the windows again, biting my lip when they banged a little too loudly. The dishes by the sink had dried, and a puddle of cold water seeped drop by drop from the rim to the drain. I loved the kitchen windows, with their little panes framed in white, looking out onto the orchard as it woke. I hoisted myself up onto the worktop, cluttered with jars and spatulas, and pressed my nose to the glass. Through the mist of my breath I watched the blurred silhouettes of the fruit-laden trees. The leaves, a shy emerald, flickered in the sun like little fish with green veins.

I would go through the sitting room, through the library, both deserted, and already I was sighing with boredom, telling myself that adults really were too lazy, sleeping like that while the day unfolded without waiting for us. Suddenly I hear movement overhead. The sound of light slippers sets the floorboards creaking; then the game is to guess who it is. If it comes down the stairs fast, it's Harriett or Klaus. The cousins come hurtling down too, but not as fast as we do, especially the first few days: they have to get used all over again to the treacherous gleaming wooden steps. A slow, heavy tread: that's Uncle Bertie. Bertie, in the morning, smells extraordinarily good, because he always makes his toilette before coming downstairs, and once I saw him slapping splashes of eau de cologne onto his freshly shaven neck with the flat of his hand. Then the skin under his chin is red and gleaming, and his wake blossoms with tall grasses swaying in the wind.
