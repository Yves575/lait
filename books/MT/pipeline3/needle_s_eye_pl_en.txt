Chapter 1

It was as I say. He stumbled over his cane. I don't know why he looked back. No one was coming down behind him. Of course I recognized him. Even though he had changed beyond recognition; after so many years it is hard not to change. I let nothing show, though, that I recognized him. Perhaps that was why he thought I did not recognize him. I pretended he was one of those who are always coming down or going up those stairs. I even wondered whether I should warn him not to go down into that old wild, green valley, because it is no longer there. Old people do like visiting places that are gone and no one knows whether they were ever there at all. I do not understand what longing pulls them so. Unfortunately, old-age longings can be dangerous. If only one could get to that valley on the flat, perhaps I would have understood him. Only no valley can be reached on the flat; that is simply the nature of valleys. To come down those stairs at his age and with a cane, when his legs refuse obedience and his eyes no longer come together in seeing, not only the world but every step he sets before himself, is the same as in youth imagining that instead of hands one has wings and can glide down into that valley.

It was not him I expected, yet unexpectedly it was him. A shudder came over me, I drew into myself, at that moment I wanted to be neither here nor anywhere else. After feeling out the first step with his cane, he set his foot on it uncertainly, then dared to place the other. With the same effort he came down to the next, and the next. Feeble and stubborn, I thought. Why are old people so stubborn, can they really not forgive those who will live after them?

He paused on every step, for a shorter while or a longer, looking down at his feet, as if unsure whether these were the stairs he had always gone down and up, or whether his memory was failing him. At his age it happens that memory gives up on remembering, for it saddles a man with duties he is tired enough of already, after living so many years. He felt out each step below with his cane, making sure he could come down safely. First he set his left foot on that step, then his right. And with the same caution, bringing his right foot down beside his left, he went down to the next step. The cane he held in his right hand struck me as uncertain; it trembled when he touched the step with it.

I was afraid he might grow dizzy from looking down at his feet on every step like that. I wondered whether to move out of his way, or rather not let him pass when he reached the Needle's Eye.

He was not surprised when he saw me; perhaps he had even expected me to get here before him, before he came here from the park through town. He stopped on the step above me and, without looking me in the eyes, said:

– I went by the park too, but for the last time. I don’t know whether you remember that sometimes she used to come home from school through the park. I sat down on a bench and decided to wait. A Gypsy woman passed by with a child at her breast. Do you think she stopped as she did then? Of course not. She didn’t even ask whether I wanted my fortune told, though the child whimpered when she passed by me. Plainly she decided that the future was not ahead of me but behind me, and she wasn’t going to add to my years either; where from? I had already been sitting there a long time and was just about to get up and go through town, because perhaps she too would be coming back through town today, if she wanted to buy something, but some old woman sat down beside me, asking whether she might.

– Unless you’re waiting for someone?

– No, please, I say. I used to wait once, but that was long ago.

– Long ago, not long ago, but if you’ve loved, you go on waiting.

– And did you love someone?

– What a question. Is there anyone who never loved? You’ve lived a good many years, I can see, same as I have, and you don’t know that? I still love him, though by now probably for nothing. It pains me that I never told him.

– And why ever didn’t you?

– I was ill, and the doctors weren’t giving me much life, so I didn’t want to burden him with my love. Especially since he left after taking his matura. I didn’t even know where, and now he may not even be alive. Would you have said it, if you weren’t sure you were going to live?

– I don’t know, especially after so many years. Perhaps I wouldn’t have wanted to burden her either with the guilt of abandoning my love, if she were to die.

– Does that mean you loved someone?

– I still love her, though I’m not sure she is alive either. But if she were, I don’t think I’d tell her even now if we met, because neither would she recognize me nor I her. We are no longer the same as we were in our youth.

– If you love, you are always the same. If a person knew that in youth, they’d live differently. As it is, they waste many things. Most often love. Well, I’ll be off now. I have to feed the cats.

– Do you have many of them?

– It varies. As many as gather.

– I’ll walk you home.

– No, thank you. I live nearby. And where are you headed? Because perhaps I should walk you home. You with a cane, and I, thank God, still on my own two feet.

– I’ll manage. I’m much closer than you.

He tested the next step with his cane, but it was as though he did not trust it, because he ran its tip over the step to the left and to the right before he set his foot down. I wanted to ask him why, in that case, he was going down into the old wild, green valley, but he forestalled me:

– I feel sorry for you, that you still have to go on living. But perhaps you’ll have more luck. I wish you that. Though in my opinion wishes have never yet done anything for anyone. – He went down to the next step, testing it with his cane in the same way. – Truth be told, every life is a repetition of someone else’s life. The past outstrips us, you must know that; we drag along behind it. For who could ever keep up with his own life? – He stopped on the next step. – Oh, how many feet must have passed this way. Have you noticed how worn these steps are? It takes centuries for that. Who knows whether they aren’t still passing this way. Perhaps I’ll join them. What, does that surprise you? It’s always easier in a crowd. – And as he felt for the step below with his cane, he swayed, though it was as if someone steadied him. – When I was in secondary school... But what am I telling you for, you’ll live through it yourself, because you must live through it. Without that, you couldn’t ask yourself whether it was worth it. Every question we ask ourselves in life comes down to that one, whether it was worth it. Well, not at your age. Years from now, only years from now. Now you wouldn’t know what answer to give yourself. Only please remember that youth can mislead for a long time. Don’t let it deceive you. It may suddenly turn out that you were young many years ago. Sadly. Forgive these bitter words, but they are the only ones I have left. In return I give you my life. It will come in useful when emptiness overtakes you. Emptiness is the worst thing that can happen to us. And the only remedy for it is to live in someone else’s life. No one, after all, begins his own life from himself. You still don’t understand that. But in time, when you begin to visit it... I’d like to know what impressions you’ll have. I had better ones and worse ones, as with life. Once, I ran away from my childhood, but perhaps you will never want to leave yours. It happens. I’ll tell you this, though: it will do you no good. Life is wandering after yourself without any hope of ever finding yourself. Well, enough, because they’ll pass me by. And there are still so many steps left.

So I stepped out of his way. And then the cane slipped from his hand and rolled down, and he staggered and plunged after it. He fell all the way to the road at the foot of the stairs, striking his head on the cobbles. Just then nobody was coming down or going up, so nobody saw it. I rushed after him. I tried to lift him in some mindless hope that he might still be alive. But he was heavy. Tall in life, and in death limp as well, and so all the heavier. Then I heard someone’s voice above me:

“Leave him. The ambulance is already on its way.”

So I laid his bloody head back on the cobbles, and only then did I see that several people were standing nearby. Where they had suddenly come from, I do not know. Maybe that is how it is at every accident, that people as if sprout from the ground or flutter down from the sky, but while he was falling down those stairs I could have sworn there was not a living soul there. So there is no point listening to people, because no one saw that he caught on the cane. They can say all sorts of things, and each one will say something different anyway, as always when no one wants to be a witness. And as it turned out later, no one wanted to. The one who had told me to leave him was just then talking about a similar incident, pointing to some house not far away:

“There, in that house. Two friends, not used to guns, found a revolver from the war. And because it was rusty, even the trigger would not pull, so one of them started banging the barrel on the table. Well, and it fired into the other one’s belly. He collapsed, and the other one started lifting him, I think he even straightened him out. The doctor said afterwards that if he hadn’t lifted him, he would have lived.”

At that moment his words were cut off by the ambulance drawing up with its siren on. Two orderlies jumped out with a stretcher and a doctor, who pulled back his eyelids, then, just to be safe, put his stethoscope to his chest and pronounced him dead. Right after the ambulance, likewise with its siren on, the militia arrived. Two men in uniform got out of the car, one an officer, with stars, the other younger, with bars. After them, from the back seat, a third man clambered out, so fat he looked almost ridiculous in uniform.

The officer began questioning the people standing around the body, asking what each of them had seen and how it had happened, while the younger one, the one with bars, wrote it down, though he had nothing to write, because although they all talked over one another, no one had seen anything. One of them had only seen me running down the stairs, when the body was already lying on the cobbles. And the one who had forbidden me to lift him had come up just as I was trying to lift him, and said:

“Leave him.” And he had already started saying that there, in that house..., but the officer cut him short:

“Did anyone know him?”

Someone began wondering whether maybe, if he had seen him alive... at which the officer waved a hand.

“Search his pockets,” he said.

The fat one tried to bend over the body, but could not manage it, so he dropped to one knee and pulled a wallet from the inside pocket of the jacket, handed it to the younger one, and he, without looking inside, passed it to the officer. The wallet had many compartments, but apart from money and a photograph of some young girl there was nothing in it, not even a receipt, from a laundry for instance.

“No identity card?” the officer said in surprise. “Search him again, thoroughly, maybe it’s somewhere.” “No?” he said, even more surprised.

“A citizen without an identity card, it’s as if he didn’t exist,” said the fat one.

“The body is here, so the identity card ought to be too,” said the younger one with bars. “Without an identity card, it’s as if this body didn’t exist either. And how are we supposed to conduct an inquiry?”

“Maybe it fell out and is under the body. Lift the body,” the officer ordered.

The orderlies lifted the body, but beneath it there was nothing but a wide pool of blood.






“Put it back. The way it was lying.”

“And this girl in the photograph?” The junior one peered into the wallet lying open in the officer’s hands. “Pretty girl.”

The officer nodded.

“Maybe a daughter, someone would recognize her sooner. Show them?” He took the photograph from the officer and held it up to each person’s eyes.

They shrugged, saying they did not recognize her. Only one man said:

“It must be an old one. Scalloped edges. They don’t make them like that these days. I’ve got similar ones from my grandparents.”

“Well then, and you,” he said, turning to me, “do you recognize her?”

Yes, it was her, I recognized her. I did not admit it, though. And so that not even the shadow of suspicion might fall on me, I drew the officer’s attention to the cane:

“Maybe someone would recognize him by the cane. Canes get attached to people. And almost everyone carries a different one. One of the teachers at my lyceum...”

The officer did not let me finish. He looked at me mistrustfully, virtually drove his gaze into me.

“We know well enough what we’re to recognize someone by. Leave that to us.” And he ordered: “Just in case, look for that cane, and put the money in deposit. Outline the body as well. For all we know he may be some tourist; he could have left his identity card with his guide, in case he got lost.”





They outlined the body in chalk, the ambulance drove off with it, and the one with the bars and the fat one began looking for the cane. That outline of his body in white chalk on the paving stones, with the bloodstain where his head had struck, seemed to me as though it were still breathing with his breath, as though it were even whispering something in his words, and it was more harrowing than when the body itself had been lying there, before the orderlies took it away.

“We’ll have to drive round the town and question wherever a coach is standing,” the officer ordered, when in spite of the search, on the stairs and all around them, they still had not found the cane. “Saturday, so they’ve all come here today. They park below the castle, they park by the granary, they park in the new town and wherever else they park, question them. Luckily there’s only one hotel, question there too. And question in private lodgings, get a list from the town hall, they have to register after all.” He took off his cap and scratched his head, as if his thoughts had started itching. “It would be useful to put up a notice as well, that a man aged... only we don’t know how old he was, we didn’t find an identity card. Damn it, we didn’t even bring the camera, we could have taken his photograph.”

“And how were we supposed to know he’d killed himself?” the fat one broke in. “They reported an accident, and an accident could mean he’d broken an arm or a leg. Haven’t plenty of arms and legs been broken on these blasted stairs?”





“A photograph could be put up,” the one with the bars threw in. “Only not here, better in town, because afterwards they’ll be afraid to walk this way, thinking it’s haunted. Though with a photograph of a corpse, who’s going to admit they knew him. Better his daughter’s. Someone would be more likely to recognize the daughter.”

“And how do you know it’s his daughter and not his granddaughter?” the officer rebuked him. And the other one actually blinked, that he hadn’t thought of it himself.

“From this it appears that in you we have our only witness,” the officer said, turning to me. And as if hesitating, he added, “Or perhaps the only starting point from which the investigation should proceed, since we have neither the deceased’s identity card, nor a photograph, nor any other witnesses. You’ll come with us. We’ll draw up a statement.”

At the station he sat me down at the desk opposite him, asked whether I’d have some tea, and lifted the telephone receiver.

“Two teas,” he ordered. “Do you take sugar? Then I’ll have mine sweet today too. And sugar. Ah yes, and have Bronka come take it down.”

A woman skinny as a slat, who turned out to be the cleaner, brought two glasses of tea, sugar in some tin that had once held hard candies, and a single teaspoon. He sweetened his tea, stirred it, then handed me the spoon.

“I suppose I’ll have to bring a teaspoon from home. Those aluminum ones snap, and there are hardly any left in the whole station,” he said, as if by way of excuse for there being only one spoon.

Bronka came in too, threw me a contemptuous look, sat down at the typewriter, and spread some colourful magazine out in front of her.

“Before you finish your tea, I’ll have a look and see what the autumn fashions are.”

“Write. What’s tea got to do with the questioning?”

“How would I know?”

He waited until I had taken at least that first sip, while he himself had already taken, I think, a third, then he pushed his chair back from the desk, settled his back against it, laced his hands, plainly relaxing, and I think he stretched his legs out under the desk as well, because he touched me.

“Well then, go ahead.”

“What am I to say?”

“What you know.”

“I don’t know anything more than what I’ve said.”

“You know, you know, we already know that you know. Everyone under questioning says the same thing, gives his first name, surname, date of birth, and says he knows nothing more. Experience teaches us, however, that a man himself does not know what he knows unless someone helps him. There are various forms of such help. But I use ordinary conversation, so to speak, the kind that makes a man aware. I proceed on the assumption that everyone has to find out for himself what he knows. So?”

Plainly I still had not understood anything, because I decided to remind him what he had told me when he was taking me to the station:

“I beg your pardon, but this must be some misunderstanding. I was only supposed to be a witness.”

"That is true, I don't deny it, you are still a witness. Only you're the only one. We have no other witnesses with whom we might confront you in order to confirm your credibility. And when there is nothing certain, the question arises whether being a witness is your only role in this incident, or whether some other role might also be ascribed to you. I don't yet know what that might be, but we must try to make it clear. The two of us. So I would like you to understand that I have to question you in different roles. The boundaries between roles, besides, are fluid. Circumstances determine them. To find some comparison, a liquid can pass into a gaseous state or a solid one, depending on the temperature. Water, steam, ice. The same thing, and not the same. We cannot allow ourselves helplessness. That would disqualify us. We would be unnecessary. So sometimes we have to probe to the point of pain. Truth, as you know, carries the highest price and is never marked down, even after years. So I would not advise counting on that. Truth is not subject to a statute of limitations. No matter how long a case may drag on. If only we had at least a second witness. But in your case that could only be the deceased. Unfortunately, we cannot question him. Though it would be interesting to know what he would have to say. We can only surmise it. And that is not so groundless. The search for a lead usually begins with surmise, since hardly anyone can be caught in the very act. Take into account, for example, all those infidelities of husbands and wives. Rarely does one catch the other, as they say, in flagrante. Most infidelities begin with surmise. And only then does surmise make one look for proof. So it is not impossible that the deceased would testify against you, just as you testify that he had only himself to blame, because he stumbled over the cane. Only where is the cane? We looked for it, you saw that. If it had been there, we would have found it. A cane has no wings to fly away." He laughed, but it was such a laugh that it chilled me. "All right, we shall look again. But in the meantime tell me which hand he was holding it in, the left or the right, when, as you say, he stumbled over it. You don't remember. Pity. Are you surprised that such a detail may matter? Then I will tell you that a detail may tell us more than we would suspect. The merest detail can overturn all testimony, gainsay all witnesses. A detail is a whole cosmos. The world may seem large, yet it is details that decide that someone met someone here, not there, that an apple fell from an apple tree, thanks to which Newton discovered the law of gravity, that some mould unexpectedly killed off the bacteria Fleming was cultivating, and in that way penicillin was invented. And so on, and so on. Have you written that down, Bronka? Then throw that page away."

He dragged the questioning out, and I must say that at times he surprised me with his perceptiveness:

"You said that no one was coming down behind him, yes? And yet he looked back. Perhaps the sun struck him so sharply, because it is fierce today, and seeing nothing, he turned his head and then you, let us say, wanted to help him down to the lower step?"

Often, in the middle of a sentence, he would break off the interrogation and go out, as if giving me time to think. Or he would sink into thought and then tell Bronka to cross something out, though it seemed important to me. Plainly his thoughts were following a different trail. And sometimes it was as if he wanted to soften the interrogation and give it a more confiding tone, because he would drop in something about himself, that he had two children, a son and a daughter, by his first wife, who had died, and that he had had to bring them up by himself.

"You know how it is. Service isn't friendship."

He had married again, and now they were expecting a child. Sometimes he would ask me questions that on the face of it had nothing to do with the case:

"Father and mother alive? Even if you're young, all sorts of things can happen. I barely remember my own mother, she died of tuberculosis. And my father passed away two years ago."

And suddenly he surprised me again:

"When did you take your matura? And why didn't you go on to university?"

"They didn't admit me."

"What, you failed the exam?"

"I passed."

"How's that? You passed and they still didn't admit you? That ought to be looked into too. Interesting. And what do you do now?"

"I work."

"Where?"

"At the fruit and vegetable processing plant. Jams, marmalades, juices."

"We know that plant. We conducted an investigation there once. Five railcars of cherries stood unloaded in barrels on the siding until they rotted. And where do you live?"

"With my parents."

"And what did you come here for? Had you arranged to meet him?"

"Whom?"

"Why, the deceased."

"I didn't know him."

"You didn't know him, and then you did. That's what you said, that you came to know him. Or perhaps he only thought you didn't recognize him. Well, we can't ask him. Still, what puzzles me is that if it was a chance meeting, why was it in the Needle's Eye of all places, and not in the street, in the park, in a cafe, a restaurant, anywhere, as happens in chance meetings? Think it over. I'll be right back." And he went out.

Oh, he wouldn't be easy, I thought. And I couldn't very well tell him that I had been waiting for her, and he had turned up unexpectedly. I had even thought he was already dead, since a year had passed since, according to the Gypsy woman's prophecy, he should have been dead. At once he would have asked me, and who is she, first name, surname, where does she live? And what, did she come? How is it, you knew she wouldn't come, and you were waiting for her? You wait for someone who may be late or may not come that day at all because something unforeseen has held them up, but who will come another time.

After a short while he came back, and from the doorway he threw an impatient question at me:

"And do you have anything to tell me? Let us assume, then, that you didn't know him. We'll follow another trail. You saw that he was going down, yes? Going down where?"

"Down."

"Obviously, one goes down, not up." He sat behind the desk, staring into my eyes with a strange intensity. "Down, you say? And if he could barely get down, how was he supposed to come back out, didn't you stop to think about that? At his age, and with a cane."

"I don't know. Maybe he had no intention of coming back out."

"He had no intention, you say. And how do you know he had no intention?"

"I suppose."

"Then I must inform you that what you suppose may itself be evidence. And we may use it against you, regardless of the fact that you are supposing in favor of your own innocence. Everything has two sides, every thought and every word. Nothing has only one meaning, much as you no doubt imagine it does. A single meaning is an illusion. The very fact that you only suppose betrays you, that something lies behind it. And that is the most important thing, what lies behind something. True, you covered yourself with that 'maybe.' But people sometimes toss out a 'maybe' like that, though they do not toss it out without a reason either. Words rule us, you ought to know that. You do not need a matura for that. Many a time a man would rather not say a certain word, and it says itself. So then, how about it, because something here does not add up." He fell silent, as if waiting for my answer.

It unsettled me that he was silent so long, staring at me, and silent. In those eyes fixed on me, it seemed to me, I could see a question he would have liked to ask me, only he did not know whether he might not entangle himself in something. I was afraid of that question and waited tensely for the moment when he would ask it. He did not. He only cleared his throat:

"Hmm..."

And that was the only sound that came out of his mouth for quite a while. After a moment he cleared his throat again:

"Hmm..."

At that "hmm" I shrank into myself. In an "hmm" like that, God knows what may be lurking, even an accusation for which there is as yet no proof, but in it there already rings the conviction that proof will be found, if not sooner than later, it will be found. Later it may even be easier to find. Investigative techniques will improve, methods of examination will be refined, new apparatus will appear that reads on its own, without asking questions. And without Bronka, because there will be nothing to write down, the suspect will simply come up on a screen. If he has a soul, his soul too will be visible, plain as day. Patience, what has drawn away will draw near as well. And in the meantime there is no harm in dreaming. By then years will have passed for the suspect too, he will have been through more than one thing, understood more than one thing, perhaps one day he will even come of his own free will and confess, there will be no need to detain him, go anywhere, track anything down. They will sit behind a desk and receive the guilty the way people are received when they file applications for relief or flats. If only one could get at consciences, because so far consciences have rarely opened of themselves, and if they do, only in old age. Perhaps instead of the militia there will be only offices, bureaus, departments, and salaries will rise too. The militia has a hard time of it, especially with the young. The young are the hardest material to question: they twist, tangle things up, deny everything, codes and paragraphs mean nothing to them, they think youth lets them do anything. The militia's nothing to them. Well, they will find out, when we are the ones showing the world the way.

At that moment an officer one star above him came in and laid a note on his desk.

"Got him in your grip yet?" he asked, glancing at me.

"Almost."

"What, that tough? Maybe pass him to Edek."

"No, I can handle him."

"Suit yourself."

After he left, he threw a glance at the note the other officer had brought him, set it aside with suppressed fury, and with that same fury turned on Bronka:

"Leave the penmanship alone now, Bronka! Write!"

"That's what I'm doing all the time," Bronka bristled. "Only I've got nothing to write."

He bored into me with the same furious eyes, as if expecting at least my hands to start shaking.

"Let's get back to the cane."

"Let's get back to the cane, I've written," Bronka repeated.

"What did the cane look like?"

"What did the cane look like?" Bronka again.

"Stop, for God's sake, reading me what you're writing!" Oh, he was plainly furious. "So then, what did the cane look like?"

"Ordinary. Like a cane."

"Exactly. What color was it?"

"I think black."

"You think, or black?"

"It could have been black."

"'Could have been' is not an answer. Listen — you are under interrogation in connection with what may well be murder. You could just as easily have stuck a leg out and tripped him as he could have caught his foot on the cane. Because so far we haven't found the cane."

"Black," I said, a little frightened. I felt as if he were slowly beginning to get at my thoughts.

"Well then, we've got the color. What next?"

"The rest of it was black too."

"Think you can make a fool of me, is that it? And here I am trying to give you a chance."

No, I wasn't making fun of him, I wouldn't have dared. Maybe I did say it stupidly, but that was because of the sudden confusion in my head, because he had dropped the polite address and started talking to me like someone he already had in his grip, and what lay behind that.

"And the handle?"

"What about the handle?"

"Well, what shape was it? They come in all sorts. One fellow here, young too, like you, used to walk with a cane with a snake's-head handle."

"No, semicircular, smooth."

"And what else did it have?"

"It also had a non-slip tip on the end."

"Meaning he couldn't have slipped, because it held him. And he fell?"

"Maybe because it held him, and he wanted to go lower. And caught on it."

He looked at me as if he were thinking, you're sly, brother, but you won't lead me astray.

"All right. Let's leave the cane till we find it."

He began drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desk, as if wondering whether after all he shouldn't pass me on to that Edek the officer one star higher in rank, the one who had brought him the note, had suggested. I didn't know who Edek was, a name like any other, but the very idea of passing me on to some Edek already struck me as threatening. Until then he had been mild, at times friendly; I had even had the impression it pained him that he had to question me. And fear swept over me. True, he hadn't agreed to this Edek, but maybe there was some Edek sitting inside him too, since he had said he could manage on his own.

"You're aware, I take it, that confessing guilt is a mitigating circumstance," he said, as if with weariness in his voice. I think he even yawned.

"But what am I supposed to confess to?"

That's not my concern. I'm not the one who's supposed to confess. Truth be told, everyone would have something to confess to. Even if we were hauling in the first people off the street for questioning. Maybe one day that's how it will be. But what was he supposed to confess to, for it to be easier on him? Nothing had ever been easier for him again since he had let a Gypsy woman tell his fortune, and she had predicted a long life for him, though that meant nothing to him; but when he heard that today she would not be coming home from school this way through the park, that she would go through town instead because she had to buy something for herself, hooks and eyes or snaps, he almost flew off the bench.

The park was usually empty at that hour, and besides it was small, one of those little parks in the middle of town, and on top of that badly thinned out by the almost apocalyptic storm that had struck the town a few years earlier and was still remembered with horror. So it no longer had the same pull as before. There was no hiding there from people's eyes, so hardly anyone arranged to meet there now. The young preferred to go to the grove, down by the river, and the old were put off walking there or sitting on the benches by sorrow for the park as it had once been. Once, at this time of year, the trees rang with birdsong; now only rarely did anything disturb the silence, so he was sure he would hear her footsteps as she came near.

He half closed his eyes against the glaring sun, for in that silence no one would escape him. He heard every footstep from far off and did not have to open his eyes each time to make sure whether it was her or not. Besides, he knew her steps by heart, as though they only lightly touched the ground; one might have thought she was wearing sandals, although she always walked in high heels, she could not stand flat shoes. She set her feet down as if from the toes, not the heels; he would have known those steps even in starless darkness.

So beneath those half-closed lids he gave himself over to imagination, all the more since he did not expect her until the last lesson was over. He had the advantage of that one lesson over her, for he had slipped out of Latin class to be waiting for her already in the park when she came back, and more often than not she came back through the park. More than once he had slipped away, not only from Latin, which he disliked, but from physics and mathematics too, subjects in which he was among the best pupils. When he went out, as she did, after the last bell, it happened that he lost her in the several classes coming out at once. There were several classes in the same year, and each of them was large, so that when lessons ended a crowd poured outside. And when he found her, almost always she was already walking with someone. Oh, there were many of them, boys from the older classes too, lying in wait for the chance at least to walk that stretch with her or see her to her very door.

What he might have been dreaming of is perhaps not worth recalling, since dreams at that age are common enough and known to all, so anyone can reach for his own. Just then he heard footsteps drawing near, or rather a rustle as if of bare feet. No, those were not her steps, he would have known them at once. These were unhurried, stealthy rather, so he would not have let go of his dreams had not a child suddenly whimpered close by, almost within arm's reach. He flung his eyelids open, then, so abruptly that the brightness of the sun blinded him, and in that brightness he saw before him a Gypsy woman with an infant at her breast.

She was directly in front of the bench he was sitting on, and plainly would have gone on, for one could not read in her steps any intention of stopping beside him. Evidently experience had told her there was no point telling the fortune of someone like him, she would not squeeze so much as a zloty out of him. If he had any money at all, he was keeping it for the cinema, for the circus. Only what girl would let herself be invited by him to the cinema or the circus? The prettier ones looked around for those who had a future. And what future could he have? Let him even live a long time, but does a long life guarantee a future? Pale, gaunt, as though caved in on himself, perhaps even ill, only not yet aware of it. The only fortune she could tell him was that he would live a long time, because there is no way to verify that except by living a long time.

So perhaps it was no accident that when she came level with him and was already about to take that next step, her baby whimpered, because the nipple had slipped from its mouth; she pushed it back in and stifled the cry. But she did have to stop for a moment, and then her eyes met his. Only briefly, for his gaze chilled her and she lowered her eyes to the little head of the suckling infant, even stroked it tenderly. She was used to mastering people’s mistrust, even their contempt for her, yet his eyes seemed older than his years. A kind of sad gleam streamed from them. She had never yet seen so much suffering in young eyes; in old ones you often do not see that much. Proof enough that he had opened them only when he heard her baby cry. Could it be he had inherited that suffering from someone? After all, everyone is an endless inheritance. Yet since she had stopped beside him, she could not simply go away without a word. Her baby’s cry had already been that first word to him. So, as if carrying on that cry, she said:

‘Let him have his fortune told, and I’ll say whether she’ll be coming back this way.’

And without waiting for his consent, she sat down on the bench. With a practised movement she drew out her right breast from under her blouse and shifted the infant to it; the baby, pulled away from sucking, whimpered again, but did not have time to burst into tears, for it already had the other nipple in its mouth.

‘When something hurts, the best thing is to have your fortune told. There hasn’t yet been a wise man who didn’t believe things would get better. Fortunes can comfort, and they take no one’s hope away. They can change more than one thing too. And his whole life is still ahead of him. If he gave a little for a rattle, I’d foretell his whole life for him.’

And without waiting for his assent or refusal, she freed her left arm from the shawl that, tied at the nape and around her waist, supported the tiny child she carried at her breast.

‘Give me your hand,’ she said. ‘Not that one, the other.’

And she pulled that hand onto her knees, then took it in both of hers, turned it palm up, and began to trace it with her forefinger.

‘He has a long life line. Oh, long. From here to here.’ But he did not even look to see how long. ‘He’ll live and live.’ She named the number of years, which came out to exactly last year. ‘Now let him look, from here to here. If he had a bigger hand, he’d live even longer. But it’s long enough as it is. He’ll have his fill of life.’ She must have noticed the distrust in his eyes, for she folded his hand shut and whispered, almost fearfully: ‘There’s another line here too, the line of the heart. A Gypsy woman is never wrong, a Gypsy woman always tells the truth.’

But this second line meant nothing to him either. A longer or shorter life had no meaning for him. He was sunk in that one moment for which he had been waiting, sitting in the park by the main avenue. He would have given the longest life for that moment. There are moments that, like the sun in a lens, gather a whole life into a single point, burning into us a mark that stays forever. Who knows whether it is not only for such moments that one lives, and not for so many years.

He scarcely listened to what she was predicting for him, for in his thoughts he was just then leaving the school with her after the last lesson, coming out through the doors of the building, then through the gate from the schoolyard, crossing to the other side of the street, walking a little way along the pavement, then turning toward the church, he was already by the church, in a moment he will enter the park, he is already in the park, so that her footsteps coming toward him should reach him any moment now. He already thought he could hear them, still very faint, she must be somewhere near the beginning of the side path, but they were her footsteps, how could he fail to know them, when suddenly the Gypsy woman's words struck him like a club to the head:

– She won't be coming back this way today. She was going to, but then she remembered she had something to buy, hooks and eyes, snaps, and she'll go through town.

She had not finished when he tore his hand from her grasp, sprang up, and ran toward the town.

– And won't he give for a rattle?! – she shouted after him, and the nipple slipped from the baby's mouth, for it had burst out wailing.

He turned back, fumbled a coin out of his pocket, and threw it onto her lap.

– He won't buy a rattle for that! – she cried in disappointment. – May he be cursed!

If the Gypsy woman had told the truth, that she would be coming back through town, then the way to her house led down the steps through the Needle's Eye into that old wild, green valley which separated the hill on which the town stood from the opposite one, much lower, where her house was.
