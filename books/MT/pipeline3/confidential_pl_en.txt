The Commotion of the Past



Unless you can outpace events and, conjuring the future, write a good ending. You can’t — I know what I’m talking about. You’re stuck, your legs tangled in dried-out roots. The drought isn’t doing you any favors, what you’d really need is months of downpour, the roots would turn slippery, you’d scramble free that much faster. In the meantime, I encourage you to practice saying goodbye to memories. You’ve been running late for years now. You need to reckon with linear time. Memory loops are the domain of the old, for whom the passing years cut off retrospection. Time to set time free. Do it slowly — the body plays tricks, and sudden changes can send it into shock. Turn the circle into a timeline, not a line segment — it sounds like a strange geometry problem.

I can’t promise that when time finally moves forward, life will get some color in its cheeks. Everything has its drawbacks and few advantages. Raise your head out of the commotion of the past. That’s where you’re from, but there’s no one left there but you. Look around and throw out the things your children won’t want to inherit. Do the same inside your head, though that’s much harder. Whatever you can — put into laments. It works sometimes, pushes a subject far enough away that you find room to think about your own life. Have you ever thought about this? Are you living well? Whose life are you living? It’s no longer about whether they’d be proud of you — that’s over. Think of tomorrow from today.









The English



They were nearly there — all that was left was to get off the square and walk two hundred meters.

“If he starts screaming for the whole street to hear again, you’re going without me this time,” she said.

Ever since the boys were born, Sunday dinners at her in-laws’ had become mandatory. Not every week, thankfully, but at least once a month. The script repeated itself one hundred percent.

They would come around the corner and look toward the same spot — the balcony on the second floor. He’d be standing there waiting, even though it was only a quarter to two. He was visible from a distance, a red-haired older gentleman gesticulating energetically. The closer they got, the clearer it became that he wasn’t just waving his arms.

“How old are you, for God’s sake?! Can’t you people show up on time?! I’ve been standing here for fifteen minutes dying of fear that something’s happened to you! How dare you put me through this?!”

“I said it and I’ll do it. I won’t put up with this anymore.”

“Come on, Mum, don’t worry about it. Grandpa’s just like that.”

She looked at her husband. He spread his hands helplessly and smiled pleasantly, raising his bushy eyebrows. She didn’t want to come across as hysterical, but she’d had enough of these balcony scenes long ago.

“He acts like a madman — maybe this time you’ll actually say something to him?” she asked, though she knew the answer perfectly well.

“What can I say to him? He’s telling the truth — he’s dying of fear and there’s nothing we can do about it. Let’s agree that when he dies, we’ll stop coming to visit.” He looked at her with a warm smile.

“Do you think Grandpa will die today?” asked one of the sons.

“Not yet, I hope.”

“What about you, Mum? Would you want Grandpa to die today?”

“I’d rather not. We’ll have plenty of excitement as it is.”



Ignoring the ever-louder shouts from the balcony, they reached the stairwell, passed the mailboxes, and started up the stairs. They never took the elevator — it was so small there was no way all four of them could fit.

Grandfather would be waiting in the open doorway. First he’d bend down to the older grandson, hug him too hard and a little too long.

“My firstborn, my firstborn. Let me look at you — don’t you look smart. Who’d have thought you’d ever be born!”

Then it would be the younger one’s turn. He’d hug him too hard as well.

“What a black mop of hair you’ve got — maybe it’ll lighten. Good thing you’re not ginger.”

Without taking his eyes off the boys, he’d hug his son, and last of all greet his daughter-in-law.

“Beautiful grandsons you’ve given me. Come in, come in.”

“Dad, could you spare us the screaming from the balcony next time? We’d arranged for two, we got here fifteen minutes early, and you’re yelling that we’re late?”

“Everything will be fine, don’t worry. I was worried a car had hit you, or some other disaster… The most important thing is we’re all alive and together. By the way, I wonder if I’ll live to see great-grandchildren. Well, boys, can I count on you?”

“Maybe you’ll be dead by then,” said the younger grandson.

“Well, what a comedian!” said Grandfather, smiling.



After the greetings they would go to the dining room, where a large round table stood. A white tablecloth, plates with gold trim, and silver cutlery — everything already laid out. At the table sat an old woman, tiny and shrunken. She’d had Alzheimer’s for years and showed no reaction to the noise that filled the room.

“Boys, say hello to Grandma,” their father would say.

They didn’t like going near her; they didn’t understand what was happening to her. She rarely spoke, and never in Polish. They’d go up to her, quickly kiss her on the cheek, and take their seats. Sometimes a barely perceptible smile would cross her wrinkled face — hard to say whether it was conscious.

Grandfather’s housekeeper would cook dinner. She’d set the pots and pans on the windowsill and leave, despite sincere invitations to sit with everyone at the table. Chicken soup with homemade noodles, meat patties, mashed potatoes, and lettuce with sweet cream, which no one ate but Grandfather. The youngest grandson would wolf down his entire meal to get a second helping of soup before it vanished into the kitchen. Then he’d politely ask to be excused and lie down on the floor, where he’d play with his toy cars.

The rhythm of these dinners never changed. Everything lasted about an hour and a half. Then Grandfather would take a nap, and they’d leave, no one stopping them.

“A bodybuilder from Dachau picked up a brick and wore himself out,” the younger grandson announced one Sunday, lying on the floor with a belly full of soup.

“Oh God!” Grandmother groaned in Polish.



Silence fell, and parental eyes turned to the older son.

“I didn’t teach him that!”

“My friends at school taught me,” replied the youngest member of the family, completely unfazed.

“Why did you decide to share this with us right now?” asked the father.

“It just came back to me.”

“Would you look at him — it just came back to him! And do you know that some people never managed to forget?” asked Grandfather, agitated.

“So I know.”

“And from where, may I ask?”

“From you. You talk about it all the time. I don’t understand why you always talk about Jews.”

“Because we are Jews,” Grandfather replied.

“All of us?”

Grandfather looked around and smiled.

“Yes, all of us.”

“Me too?”

“You too.”

The boy went back to his toy cars for a moment.

“I’d rather be English,” he said after a long silence.

“Want me to tell you something?” asked Grandfather.

“So tell.”

“I’d rather be English too.”

Grandfather reached for his glass of tea, but it had already gone cold enough that he lost interest. He got up from the table and went for a nap.



I Tell Everyone



This guy was the prankster type. He loved medicine and women, in precisely that order. Half the city loved him back. The half he treated. The other half split between those he rattled and those who didn’t care. Red-haired and freckled — which is to say, hot-tempered. Could hit you in the face out of nowhere, put away a bottle of vodka, and walk on as if nothing had happened.



He’d made it to the second half of the twentieth century, decided to seize the chance and live without giving up any pleasures. He reckoned his share of drama spent. His wife took on raising the child; he only stepped in when the situation called for a doctor. He thought a father’s lot thankless, like that joke about the son who wouldn’t eat. The whole family worried — skinny, wouldn’t touch a thing. Finally the father couldn’t take it anymore and asked what his little boy would most like to eat. The little one thought it over and said: shit. What do you mean, shit? Just shit. Shit on a plate. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, then he got up and went to the bathroom. Came back with shit on a plate. Set it down in front of his son. The boy looked it over and said he wanted the shit fried. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, then he got up and went to the kitchen. Came back, set it down in front of his son. The son looked it over and said he wanted Daddy to try it. The mother at the father, the father at the mother. He tried it, said it was exquisite, and urged his son to finally start eating. I won’t eat — Dad ate the best piece.

The household ticked along, the son grew like a weed, and the red-haired doctor kept meeting new women and spending pleasurable moments in their company. When acquaintances asked how he was doing, he'd tell the joke about a Jew who came to a priest to confess. The priest asks if the penitent is Catholic. He isn't. Then why has he come? Because he really cares. The priest enters the confessional, and the Jew starts telling him about all the women he's been sleeping with. This blonde, that one with the curly hair, and yet another had such small breasts... Why did you pick me of all people to tell this to? the priest asks. I tell everyone.



His wife pretended not to hear. After what they'd been through, she knew life was capable of worse. Sometimes she'd complain to a friend, and the friend, once home, would ask her husband to have a word with the doctor. They'd arrange to meet, go out for vodka, and the family friend would gently reveal the mission he'd been sent on. The redhead would look at him and say that just at that moment he'd been reminded of a joke — the one about the tax collector who comes to the Jewish community. What do you do with the wax from your melted candles? We collect it and make new candles. I see. And what do you do with the matzo crumbs? Oh, we make delicious matzo balls from those. I see. And when you circumcise the little boys, what do you do with the, well, you know? Oh, those. We collect them, send them off to the tax office, and they send us back pricks like you, sir.

He smothered friendly impulses in an instant. If his companion persisted, he'd call the waitress over and ask her to open a window. Nothing to worry about, he'd say — my friend here is no eagle, he won't fly out.

His son married a petite brunette, a graduate of the art academy. The doctor had had other plans for him, to be sure, but he took note and stopped looking for the ideal match.



The red-haired doctor's wife developed rapidly progressing Alzheimer's. He took excellent care of her. He brought a young girl from the provinces into the house. She cooked, looked after the ailing wife — and got on well with the doctor too.

To the grandchildren he became a wonderful doctor and a strange grandfather who had his moments. Once a year he'd take them shopping. To everyone's surprise, he'd go and buy a billiard table or a bicycle. Sometimes he'd take them to a restaurant, and after placing the order he'd promptly vanish. Only as they grew older did the grandchildren realize that the real point of these outings was not their company but the waitress in the short skirt. He didn't know how to be a grandfather, but he was proud that the family was growing.

A year after his wife's death, he invited the older grandson to dinner. After they ordered, he didn't leave the table as usual. While they waited for their food, the grandfather recalled a joke about Rosenzweig, who paces around the house all night. Around five in the morning his furious wife gets up and asks him why he's pacing. I'm pacing because I can't sleep, Rosenzweig answers. Why can't you sleep? Because I owe Hozenduft money. A lot? A lot. So call him and tell him you're not paying him back. The husband thinks for a moment and calls. This is Rosenzweig. Yes, I know what time it is. I just wanted to say I'm not paying back the money. Good night. And he hangs up. So now what? he asks his wife. Go to sleep — now he's the one who can't sleep.



The grandson, trained in joke-telling since childhood, doesn't understand why his grandfather is telling him this right now. A shapely waitress brings their food. The grandfather smiles at her but stays at the table.

“Listen, there’s a Jewish law that says a healthy man shouldn’t be alone. This law also says that when a wife dies, the husband may marry her sister. Your grandmother’s sister perished when everyone did.”

What now? the grandson thinks. The grandfather draws a deeper breath and announces that he’s getting married. He adds right away that his son won’t be happy with his choice of wife.

“How do you know?” the grandson asks.

“Something tells me. People rarely take to their stepmothers. My half-sister couldn’t stand my mother.”

“Did she have reasons?” the grandson asks.

“Oh, did she ever!”

The grandson worries he’s been dispatched to break this strange news to his father. He senses it won’t be easy or pleasant. Reprieve comes unexpectedly.

“But let’s not tell your father for now...” the grandfather decrees, trailing off. “A second wife, one for your old age — you choose her without thinking of anyone else. You look for someone who’ll understand you. Even if you know she speaks before she thinks, that she’s difficult, that she hurts everyone around her. You go for it because you want to drown out the loneliness. You tell yourself you’ll sort out the mismatch later.”

The grandson is in his teens and senses that later might be too late, but these are grown-up matters, after all. He doesn’t yet know that in his family, the stepmother type will turn out to be hereditary.








Underwater Odyssey



A lie doesn’t have short legs. Father always said it did, but he didn’t know how wrong he was. It only floats to the surface when you don’t pay enough attention to keeping it hidden.

When he was little, he didn’t lie — they said he made up stories beautifully. Once he became a teenager, they called it lying. He didn’t overthink it; he managed life in his own way.

He forged his values to suit the moment.

The first rule: calm parents, calm life. The system had its gaps, but over time it became an integral part of the cycle: keep the parents calm between parent-teacher conferences, survive their return from a conference with dignity and hope for a better tomorrow. Find solace in the quiet at home after the uproar, and don’t take the reproaches or the anger personally. After a few days, give them something to lift their spirits — it didn’t have to be true, just enough to tide them over. Remember that “calm parents, calm life” is the priority, and that the best times come between conferences. Don’t push it, but don’t forfeit either.

He was a sunny child, the first in his family born after the war. He sensed they couldn’t stand dishonesty. He developed his modus operandi naturally. You could say he took his cues from nature. He flowed like a stream — where there was less resistance, not where anyone expected him. A principle as simple as it was frowned upon.
