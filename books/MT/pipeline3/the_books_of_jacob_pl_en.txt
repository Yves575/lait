PROLOGUE

A swallowed slip of paper lodges in the gullet somewhere near the heart. Saliva soaks through it. The black ink, specially prepared, slowly spreads, and the letters lose their shape. Inside the human body a word splits in two, into substance and essence. As the first vanishes, the second, left without form, lets itself be absorbed by the body's tissues, for essence is forever seeking a material bearer, even if that should become the cause of many misfortunes.

Yenta wakes, though she has been nearly dead. She feels it clearly now: it is like pain, like the current of a river, a trembling, a pressure, a movement.

Near the heart a delicate vibration returns; the heart beats faintly but evenly, sure of itself. Warmth begins to flow once more into Yenta's dried, bony chest. Yenta blinks and with difficulty raises her eyelids. She sees Elisha Shorr's worried face bent over her. She tries to smile at him, but she has no such command of her face. Elisha Shorr's brows are drawn together, he looks at her reproachfully. His lips move, but no sound reaches Yenta's ears. Hands appear from somewhere, old Shorr's large hands, reaching for her neck and slipping under the blanket. Shorr awkwardly tries to turn her limp body onto its side and peer beneath it, at the sheet. No, Yenta does not feel his efforts; she feels only warmth and the presence of the bearded, sweating man.

Then suddenly, as if by some blow, Yenta sees everything from above: herself, and the balding crown of Shorr's head, for in his struggle with her body his cap has fallen off.

And from then on it remains so: Yenta sees everything.



I. THE BOOK OF FOG

1

1752, Rohatyn

It is the end of October, early in the morning. The dean stands on the rectory porch and waits for the carriage. He is used to rising at dawn, but this time he feels half awake and does not really know how he has come to be here, alone before a sea of fog. He remembers neither rising nor dressing, nor whether he has eaten yet. He looks in surprise at his sturdy boots protruding from beneath the cassock, at the somewhat frayed skirts of his faded wool coat, and at the gloves he is holding in his hand. He slips his left hand into one; inside it feels warm and fits his hand perfectly, as though hand and glove have known each other for years. He breathes easier. He touches the bag slung over his shoulder, mechanically tracing its rectangular edges, hard, thickened, like scars beneath the skin, and slowly remembers what is inside it: a heavy, familiar, pleasing shape. Something good, something that has brought him here, certain words, certain signs; all of it is deeply bound up with his life. Ah yes, now he knows what is in there, and that knowledge begins little by little to warm his body, and the fog seems somehow more transparent for it. Behind him is the dark opening of the door, one leaf shut; the cold must already have set in, perhaps a frost has even blackened the plums in the orchard. Above the door is a dim inscription; he sees it without looking, because of course he knows what is written there; after all, he commissioned it himself. Two craftsmen from Podhajce spent a whole week carving the letters in wood, for he had ordered them done with flourishes:

TODAY IT IS, TOMORROW GONE.
WHAT HAS FLED, WE SHALL NOT REGAIИ

In the word "regaiИ," which vexes him greatly, the letter N is written backward, as though in a mirror.

Irritated by it for the hundredth time, the priest jerks his head sharply, and that wakes him at last. That reversed letter is an И... What slovenliness! One has to watch their hands all the time, keep after them at every step. And since those craftsmen are Jews, they have made the inscription somehow Jewish; the letters are too curly, too wavering. One of them even argued that such an N was acceptable too, indeed prettier, because the diagonal runs upward and from left to right, in the Christian manner, whereas the other way round would be the Jewish way. This petty irritation restores his senses, and now Father Benedykt Chmielowski, dean of Rohatyn, sees why he had the feeling he was still asleep: he is standing in a fog the color of his bed linen, grayish; a broken white already touched by dirt, immense reserves of gray that are the lining of the world. The fog is motionless, tightly filling the whole yard; behind it loom the familiar shapes of the great pear tree, the low wall, and farther off the wicker cart. It is nothing but an ordinary cloud from the sky that has fallen to earth and pressed its belly to the ground. He read about that yesterday in Comenius.

Now he hears the familiar creak and rattle that unfailingly, on every journey, carries him into a state of creative meditation. Only in the wake of that sound do Roszko, leading the horse by the bridle, and the dean's chaise emerge from the fog. At the sight of it the priest feels a surge of energy, slaps his glove against his palm, and clambers up onto the seat. Roszko, taciturn as ever, adjusts the harness and casts a long look at the priest. The fog grays his face and makes him seem older than ever before, as though he has aged in the night, though in truth he is only a young lad.

At last they set off, yet it is as though they stand still; only the swaying of the chaise and its soothing creak bear witness to motion. They have gone this way so many times, through so many years, that there is no longer any need to look at the landscape, nor will any landmark be required. The priest knows they are now on the road that runs along the edge of the forest, and so they will keep moving until the crossroads, where a little chapel stands, put there, after all, by the priest himself years before, when he took the parish in Firlejów. For a long while he wondered whom he should place in that chapel, and Saint Benedict, his patron, came to mind, or Onuphrius the hermit, whom a palm in the desert miraculously fed with dates, while every eighth day angels brought him the Body of Christ from heaven. Firlejów too was to be such a desert for the priest when he came here, after years spent tutoring Dymitr, the son of His Highness Jabłonowski. Yet after thinking the matter through, the priest decided the chapel was not to be built for him and the satisfaction of his own vanity, but for simple folk, so that at the crossroads they might have somewhere to rest and from there lift their thoughts to heaven. So upon a whitewashed brick pedestal stands Our Lady Queen of the World with a crown upon her head. Beneath her little pointed slipper a serpent writhes.

But today she too vanishes in the fog, the chapel and the crossroads with her. Only the tops of the trees are visible, a sign that the fog is beginning to sink.

"Look, sir, Kaśka won't go on," Roszko says darkly when the chaise comes to a stop. He climbs down from the box and crosses himself several times with sweeping gestures.

Then he bends and peers into the fog as though into water. His shirt shows beneath his Sunday red jerkin, though the color has already faded a little.

"I don't know which way to drive," he says.

"How can you not know? We are on the Rohatyn high road already," says the priest in astonishment.

And yet it is so. He gets down from the chaise and follows his servant; helplessly they circle the carriage, straining their eyes into the whiteness. They think they can make something out, but eyes that cannot fasten on anything begin to play tricks. To think such a thing should happen to them! Why, it is like getting lost in one's own pocket.

"Hush," the priest says suddenly, lifting a finger and listening. And indeed, somewhere off to the left, out of the billows of fog, comes the faint murmur of water.

"Let us drive toward that murmur. That is running water," the priest decides.

Now they drag along very slowly beside the river called Gniła Lipa. The water guides them.

Soon the priest relaxes in the chaise, stretches his legs out before him, and lets his eyes wander over the sea of fog. At once he falls into a traveling reverie, for a man thinks best in motion. Slowly, reluctantly, the mechanism of his mind comes to life; cogs and pinions engage, setting the driving wheels in motion, just as in the clock that stands in the entry hall of his rectory, bought in Lwów; he paid dear for it. In a moment it will chime bim-bom. He begins to wonder whether the world itself did not arise from just such a fog. After all, the Jewish historian Josephus maintains that the world was created in autumn, during the autumnal aequinoctium. One may well think so, for there were fruits in Paradise; if an apple hung on the tree, then it must have been autumn... There is something in that. But another thought comes to him at once: what sort of argument is that? Could not Almighty God have created such paltry fruits into the bargain, at any season whatever?

When they reach the main road leading to Rohatyn, they join the stream of people on foot, on horseback, and in carts of every description, all emerging from the fog like little figures molded out of bread for Christmas. It is Wednesday, market day in Rohatyn, and peasant wagons roll along laden with sacks of grain, cages of poultry, and every kind of produce. Among them traders march briskly with every imaginable article for sale; their stalls, cleverly folded for now, can be borne on the back like a yoke, and in a moment they will turn into tables full of bright cloth, wooden toys, eggs bought up in the villages for a quarter of the price... The peasants lead goats and cows for sale as well; the animals, frightened by the uproar, balk in the puddles. A fast wagon covered with a tarpaulin riddled with holes rattles past them, full of noisy Jews coming to the fair in Rohatyn from all the surrounding countryside, and behind it a rich carriage forces its way through, unable to preserve its dignity in the fog and the crush on the road: its pale varnished doors are black with mud, and the coachman in a blue cape wears a wretched expression; plainly he did not expect such confusion and is now desperately scanning for a chance to get off this devilish road.

Roszko is obstinate and will not let himself be pushed off into the field; he keeps to the right and, with one wheel on the grass and the other on the road, makes good progress. His sad, long face flushes, and an infernal grimace spreads over it. The priest glances at him and remembers the engraving he was looking at only yesterday; it showed the denizens of hell, and they had the same grimaces on their faces as Roszko has now.

"Let his reverence, the good priest, through. Nu, move! Aside, folk!" shouts Roszko.

The first buildings rise up before them, suddenly, without warning. Plainly the fog alters all sense of distance, for even Kaśka herself seems taken aback by it. She gives a sudden start, wrenching at the shafts, and were it not for Roszko's quick hand and his whip, she would overturn the chaise. Perhaps it is the sparks showering from the forge that frighten Kaśka, or the unease of the horses waiting their turn to be shod...

Farther on there is an inn, mean and poor, like a village hut. Above it a well-sweep juts up like a gallows, piercing the fog on its way upward, its end vanishing somewhere high above. The priest sees a dust-covered carriage standing there; the weary coachman lets his head hang almost to his knees and does not climb down from the box, nor does anyone step out from within. Already a tall, gaunt Jewish man stands before it, and beside him girls with tangled hair. That is all the dean sees, for the fog swallows every sight they pass; it vanishes somewhere, seeping away like a melted snowflake.

Here, then, is Rohatyn.

It begins with clay huts roofed with straw thatch, which seems to press them down into the earth; yet the nearer one comes to the market square, the more slender the cottages grow, the finer the thatch becomes, until at last it gives way to the wooden shingles of town houses built of unfired clay brick. There are also the parish church, the Dominican monastery, Saint Barbara's church by the square, and farther on two synagogues and five Eastern churches. Around the square houses crouch like mushrooms, and in each some business or other. Tailor, rope-maker, furrier, all Jews; and beside them a baker named Bochenek (Loaf), and this always pleases the dean, for it reveals a hidden order, one that might be plainer and more consistent, and then people would live more virtuously. Beside him is the workshop of a swordsmith called Luba; its frontage bespeaks prosperity, the walls newly painted blue, and above the entrance hangs a great rusty sword; plainly this Luba is a good craftsman, and his customers' purses are full. Farther on a saddler, who has set a wooden trestle before his door, and on it a handsome saddle, the stirrups perhaps silvered, so brightly do they shine.

Everywhere there is the sickly smell of malt, and it seeps into all the goods set out for sale. One might eat one's fill of it as of bread. On Rohatyn's outskirts, in Babińce, there are several small breweries; it is from there that this sustaining smell spreads through all the countryside. Many stalls sell beer here, and the better shops keep spirits and mead in stock, chiefly trójniak. The shop of the Jewish merchant Wakszul offers wine, true Hungarian wine and true Rhenish wine, and also a somewhat tart sort brought all the way from Wallachia.

The priest moves along the stalls run up out of anything at hand: planks, scraps of coarse-woven cloth, wicker baskets, even leaves. Some good woman in a white kerchief sells pumpkins from a cart, and their bright orange color draws the children; beside her another cries up the rounds of cheese laid out on horseradish leaves. Farther on stand many other market women, driven to trade because they are widows or have drunken husbands: oil-sellers, salt-sellers, linen-sellers. The priest usually buys this pie-seller's wares and now sends her a pleasant smile. Behind her are two stalls decked with a green bough, which means fresh-brewed beer is to be had there. And here a rich stall of Armenian merchants: beautiful light fabrics, knives in ornamented sheaths, and beside them dried fish, whose faint smell seeps into the woolen Turkish hangings. Farther on, from a box hanging from his narrow shoulders, a man in a dusty caftan sells eggs packed by the dozen in baskets braided from grass. Another offers eggs in lots of sixty, in large baskets, at a competitive price, almost wholesale. Bagels hang all over the baker's stall; one has fallen into the mud and now a little dog is eating it with gusto.

They sell whatever can be sold here. Flowered fabrics too, kerchiefs and shawls straight from the bazaar in Istanbul, little shoes for children, fruit, nuts. A man under the fence has a plow and nails of every size, from ones as small as pins to huge ones for building a house. Beside him a buxom woman in a starched cap has spread out clappers for the night watch: small ones, whose sound is more like the night music of crickets than any summons to sleep, and large ones which, on the contrary, would wake the dead.

Time and again the Jews are forbidden to trade in things connected with the Church. Priests and rabbis alike thunder on the matter, but nothing comes of it. So there are beautiful prayer books here, with a ribbon between the pages, with wonderfully silvered letters stamped upon the cover, letters that seem warm and alive when the pad of a finger passes across them. A clean, almost elegant man in a fur cap holds them like relics, wrapped in thin paper, in cream tissue, so that this dirty, foggy day may not stain their innocent Christian pages, fragrant with printer's ink. He has wax candles too, and even little pictures of saints with halos.

The priest goes up to one of the wandering booksellers, hoping he may find something in Latin. Yet they are all Jewish books, for beside them lie objects whose purpose the priest does not know.

The farther the gaze strays into the side streets, the more misery shows through, like a dirty toe through a torn shoe; a coarse, silent poverty bent to the earth. These are no longer shops or stalls, but hovels fit for dogs, knocked together from thin boards scavenged off rubbish heaps. In one of them a cobbler mends shoes already sewn, resoled, and patched over many times. In another, hung all over with iron pots, sits a tinker. He has a thin, hollow face, and his cap covers a forehead blotched with brown marks. The dean would be afraid to have pots mended by him, lest some terrible sickness pass to others through the touch of this poor wretch's fingers. Beside him an old man sharpens knives and every kind of sickle and scythe. His whole workshop consists of a stone wheel slung round his neck. When he is given something to sharpen, he sets a primitive wooden frame on the ground; a few leather straps make of it a simple machine whose hand-driven wheel licks the metal blades. Sometimes a few genuine sparks fly out of this contraption and fall into the mud, much to the delight of the dirty, itch-ridden children. He earns pennies by his trade. With the help of that wheel he may yet drown himself in the river; that too is a benefit of the trade.

Women in rags gather shavings and dung from the street for fuel. From those rags it is hard to tell whether the poverty is Jewish, Orthodox, or Catholic. Yes, poverty has neither faith nor nationality.

Si est, ubi est? the priest asks himself, thinking of paradise. Certainly not here in Rohatyn, nor, so it seems to him, anywhere on Podolian soil. And if anyone thinks matters are better in the great cities, he is grossly mistaken. To be sure, the priest has never in his life got as far as Warsaw or Krakow, but he knows this and that from the tales of the Bernardine friar Pikulski, more travelled than he, or from what he overhears here and there in manor houses.

Paradise, that garden of delights, is transported by God to a beautiful and unknown place. And as it is written in Arca Noe, paradise lies somewhere in the land of the Armenians, high in the mountains; while Brunus, for his part, maintains that it lies sub polo antarctico, beneath the southern pole. The signs of paradise's nearness are four rivers: Gebon, Philon, Euphrates, and Tygris. And there are authors who, unable to find room for paradise on earth, place it in the air, some fifteen cubits above the mountains. But this strikes the priest as rather foolish. How so? Would those living on earth beneath paradise see it from below? Would they be gazing at the saints' heels?

Yet on the other hand one cannot agree with those who would spread the false opinion that the sacred text concerning paradise has only a mystical meaning, that is, that it is to be taken in a spiritual or allegorical sense. The priest, not only because he is a priest but from his own deep conviction, believes that Holy Scripture must be taken literally.

He knows almost everything there is to know about Paradise, for only last week he was finishing a chapter of his exceedingly ambitious book, a chapter compiled from all the volumes he has in Firlejów, and there are a hundred and thirty of them. For some he has ridden to Lwów, and even to Lublin.

There it is, the modest corner house; this is where he must go. So Father Pikulski advised him. The low double doors stand wide open; from within comes a spicy scent, unheard of amid this stench of horse dung and autumn damp, and yet another pungent aroma, already known to the dean: coffee. The dean does not drink coffee, but in the end he must make closer acquaintance with it.

The dean glances back, looking for Roszko; he sees him turning over sheepskin coats with gloomy concentration, and beyond him the whole marketplace engrossed in itself. No one is looking at the dean, all are absorbed in haggling. Noise and uproar.

Above the entrance to the building there hangs a rather clumsily made sign:

SHORR GENERAL GOODS

Then come Hebrew letters. By the door hangs a metal plaque, and beside it some signs, and the dean remembers that Athanasius Kircher says in his book that when a wife has been brought to bed and the Jews are afraid of a witch, they write on the walls the words: "Adam, Hawa. Huc - Lilit," which is to say: "Adam and Eve, come here, and you, Lilith, witch, be gone." This must be it. Surely a child has lately been born here as well.

The dean steps over the high threshold and is wholly immersed in the warm spicy smell. It takes a moment for his eyes to grow accustomed to the darkness, for the light here is let in only by a small window, and that too is cluttered with flowerpots.

Behind the counter stands a stripling whose moustache has only just begun to sprout; he has full lips, which at first tremble slightly at the sight of the priest, and then attempt to shape themselves into some word. He is utterly taken aback.

"What is your name, boy?" the dean asks boldly, to show how much at his ease he is in this dark, low little shop and to encourage the stripling to conversation, but the other does not answer. "Quod tibi nomen est?" he repeats in an official tone, but the Latin, which ought to serve understanding, suddenly sounds too solemn, as though the dean had come here to perform an exorcism, like Christ in the Gospel of Saint Luke, who addresses the possessed man with that very question. But the boy only opens his eyes still wider and repeats "bh, bh," and then suddenly scurries off behind the shelves, knocking against a braid of garlic hanging from a nail.

The dean has behaved foolishly; he ought not to expect Latin to be spoken here. He gives himself a critical glance: beneath his coat protrude the black haircloth buttons of his cassock. That must be what frightened the boy, the dean thinks, the cassock. He smiles to himself, and the biblical Jeremiah comes to mind, who nearly lost his head as well and stammered: Aaa, Domine Deus ecce nescio loqui! - Ah, Lord God, behold, I do not know how to speak.

From that moment on the priest thinks of the boy as Jeremiah. He does not know what to do when the lad vanishes so suddenly, so he looks round the shop, fastening the buttons of his coat. It is Father Pikulski who has persuaded him to come here; he follows the counsel, but now it no longer seems a good idea.

No one enters from outside, for which the priest gives thanks to God in his mind. It would not be an ordinary spectacle: a Catholic priest, the dean of Rohatyn, in a Jew's shop, waiting to be served like some townswoman. Father Pikulski advises him to go to Rabbi Dubs in Lwów; he himself goes there and learns much from him. And the priest does go, only old Dubs seems already to have had enough of Catholic priests plying him with questions about books. He is disagreeably taken aback by the request, and the very thing that most interests Father Benedykt he does not have, or pretends not to have. He assumes a polite expression and shakes his head, clicking his tongue. And when the priest asks who might help him, Dubs flutters his hands and, turning his head over his shoulder as though someone were standing there behind him, makes it plain that he does not know, and if he did know he would not tell. Afterwards Father Pikulski explains to the dean that this has to do with Jewish heresies, and though they themselves boast that among them there are no heresies, for this one, it seems, they make some exception and hate it outright.

In the end Pikulski advises him to go to Szor. A large house with a shop on the market square. Yet as he says it he gives the priest a queer sidelong look, ironic perhaps, unless the priest only imagines it. Perhaps he ought to have gone about these Jewish books through Pikulski, though the dean does not much like him. He would not have to stand here ashamed and sweating now. But there is a good deal of contrariness in the priest, so he comes alone. And there is something else too, not altogether rational: a little play on words has insinuated itself into the whole affair; who would believe such things have any sway in the world. The priest has been working diligently over a certain passage in Kircher where mention is made of the great ox Szorobor. And perhaps it is the likeness of the words that has drawn him here: Szor and Szorobor. Strange are the Lord's decrees.

But where are those famous books, where that figure who inspires respectful dread? The shop looks like an ordinary stall, though its owner is said to be descended from the famous rabbi, the greatly revered sage Zalman Naftali Shorr. And here there is garlic, herbs, crocks of spices, jars big and small, and in them every sort of spice, pounded, ground, or still in its natural form, like those vanilla pods, the clove buds, the whole nutmegs. On the shelves too, laid out on hay, are bales of cloth, silk and satin perhaps, bright enough to catch the eye. The dean wonders whether he might not need something himself, but already his attention is caught by the clumsy inscription on a sizeable dark-green jar: “Herba the”. He knows at once what he will ask for when at last someone comes out to him: a little of that herb which puts him in a better humor, which, for the dean, means he can work without tiring. It aids the digestion besides. He will buy some cloves as well, to spice his evening mulled wine. Of late the nights are so cold that his frozen feet will not let him concentrate on his writing. He looks about for some bench, and then everything happens at once: from behind the shelves there emerges a well-built, bearded man in a long woolen robe, with pointed Turkish boots showing beneath it. A thin dark-blue cloak is flung over his shoulders. He narrows his eyes as though he has just come up out of a well. From behind his back peer curiously that Jeremiah who took fright before, and two other faces much like Jeremiah’s own, rosy and inquisitive. And on the other side, in the doorway from the market square, a slight breathless boy, or else a young man, has just halted, for a fair goatee is already sprouting thick on his chin. He leans against the jamb and pants; plainly he has run here as fast as he could. He bores into the dean with insolent eyes and smiles roguishly, showing sound teeth set wide apart. The dean is not quite sure the smile is not in fact a mocking one. He prefers the dignified figure in the cloak, and it is to him that he turns with extraordinary politeness:

“Pray forgive me this intrusion, sir...”

The other man looks at him tensely, but after a moment the expression on his face slowly changes. Something like a smile appears there. The dean suddenly understands that the man does not understand him, so he begins again in another way, in Latin, joyfully certain he has found one of his own sort.

The Jew slowly shifts his gaze to the breathless boy in the doorway, and the latter boldly steps inside, tugging down his dark-cloth jacket.

“I’ll translate,” he announces in an unexpectedly low voice, with a soft Ruthenian lilt, and, pointing a finger at the dean, says with great feeling that this is a real priest, the truest of priests.

It never occurs to the priest that a translator will be needed; somehow he does not think of it. He is embarrassed and does not know how to get himself out of it, for the whole affair, meant to be delicate, is suddenly becoming public and in another moment will bring the whole market crowd in here. Most of all he would like to step back out into the cool fog smelling of horse dung. He begins to feel hemmed in inside this low room, in the air thick with the smell of spices, and besides, someone from the street is already peering in curiously to see what is going on.

"I should like a word with the esteemed Elisha Shorr, if he permits," he says. "In private."

The Jews are taken aback. They exchange a few words among themselves; Jeremiah disappears and only after a long moment of unbearable silence does he return. Evidently the priest has been granted permission, and now they lead him behind the shelves. It is accompanied by whispers, the light patter of children's feet, stifled giggles, as though behind the thin walls there were crowds of other people who are now peering through cracks in the wooden partitions at the dean of Rohatyn wandering through the recesses of a Jewish house. And it turns out as well that the little shop on the market square is only the foothold of a far more elaborate structure, a kind of beehive: rooms, little passageways, little flights of stairs. The whole house is larger and built around an inner courtyard, which the priest catches only out of the corner of his eye through a small window in the room where they pause for a moment.

"I am Hryćko," says the bearded boy as they go along. The priest realizes that if he wanted to withdraw now, he would not even know how to get out of this beehive of a house. The thought starts him sweating, and then one of the doors opens with a creak, and in it stands a lean man in the prime of life, with a fair, smooth, inscrutable face and a grey beard; he wears a gown that falls below the knees, and on his feet are wool socks and black slippers.

"This here is Rabbi Elisha Shorr," Hryćko whispers with solemn excitement.

The room is small, low-ceilinged, and very sparsely furnished. In the middle stands a broad table; on it lies an open book, and beside it, in several stacks, others. The priest's gaze runs greedily along their spines, trying to read the titles. The priest knows very little about Jews in general, and the Jews of Rohatyn only by sight.

Suddenly it seems agreeable to the priest that they are both men of small stature. Before tall people he always feels somehow disconcerted. They stand facing one another and for a moment the priest has the impression that the other man too takes satisfaction in this resemblance. The Jew settles softly, smiles, and with a movement of his hand indicates the bench for the priest.

"With your leave, and in these extraordinary circumstances, I come here before Your Worship quite incognito, having heard much of your great wisdom and erudition..."

Hryćko stops him halfway through the sentence and asks the priest:

"In-co-gnito?"

"Indeed; which is to say that I supplicate for discretion."

"And what is that? Sup-pli-cate? Dis-cre-tion?"

The priest falls silent, unpleasantly taken aback. A fine interpreter he has been saddled with; he can see the fellow does not understand him. How are they to talk to one another, then? In Chinese? He will have to try to speak plainly:

"Please keep this matter secret, for it is no secret that I am the dean of Rohatyn, a Catholic priest. But above all, an author." He underscores the word "author" by raising a finger. "And today I should prefer to speak here not as a clergyman, but precisely as an author, who labours assiduously over a certain opusculum..."

"O-pu-scu-lum?" Hryćko's doubtful voice cuts in.

"...a little work."

"Ah. You must forgive me, Father, I'm not schooled in Polish, only in the ordinary speech people use. I know only what I've overheard around horses."

"From horses?" the priest asks, astonished beyond measure, annoyed with this wretched interpreter.

"Well, I work with horses. Trading."

Hryćko speaks, gesturing with his hands. The man regards him with dark, unreadable eyes, and it occurs to the priest that perhaps he is dealing with a blind man.

"Having already read several hundred authors from cover to cover," the priest goes on, "borrowing here, buying there, I feel that many books have escaped me, and I can gain no access to them at all."

Now he breaks off and waits for the other man to speak, but Shorr merely nods with an ingratiating smile that leads nowhere.





"And since I heard that Your Worship keeps here a very fine library, not wishing for all the world to incommode..." he at once corrects himself reluctantly, "...to trouble or bother you, I plucked up courage, against all custom, yet for the good of others, to come here and..."





He falls silent, for suddenly the door flies open and a woman enters this low-ceilinged room without the least warning. Behind her, whispering, faces peer inside, half visible in the gloom. A small child whimpers for a moment and then abruptly falls quiet, as though all attention were meant to settle on the woman: bareheaded, with luxuriant curls around her face, she strides in boldly, her eyes fixed somewhere straight ahead, not looking at the men at all; on a tray she carries a jug and a plate of dried fruit. She wears a loose flowered gown, and over it an embroidered apron. Her pointed shoes clack. She is slight, but shapely; her figure draws the eye. Behind her patters a little girl carrying two glasses. She stares at the dean in such terror that she runs heedlessly into the woman before her and falls over. The glasses roll across the floor; fortunately they are of thick glass. The woman pays the child no heed, but throws the dean a single glance, quick and brazen. Dark, shadowed eyes flash, large and somehow bottomless, and in an instant a blush rises into skin so white it startles. The dean, who has no dealings with young women, is taken aback by this sudden intrusion; he swallows hard. The woman sets the jug and the plate down loudly on the table, along with the glasses lifted from the floor, and, still looking straight ahead, goes out. The door slams. Hryćko, the interpreter, looks disconcerted too. Meanwhile Elisha Shorr springs up, picks the child up, settles her on his lap, but the little girl wriggles free and vanishes after her mother.

The priest would wager his head that the whole business with the woman and child is only so they can look him over. What a sight! A priest in a Jewish house! Exotic as a salamander. And what of it? Does not a Jewish doctor treat me? And is it not a Jew who grinds my medicines as well? The matter of books is also, in its own fashion, a matter of hygiene.

"Books," says the dean, pointing a finger at the spines of the folios and Elzevirs lying on the table. On each of them two signs have been painted in gold, which the dean takes for the owner's initials; he can make out the Hebrew letters.

He reaches for his ticket for this journey to the people of Israel and carefully lays before Shorr the book he has brought with him. He smiles triumphantly, for it is Athanasius Kircher's Turris Babel, a work great both in substance and in format, and the dean has risked a good deal hauling it here. What if it had fallen into that stinking Rohatyn mud? What if some rogue had snatched it from him in the market? Without it the dean would not be who he is, but would instead become some small-minded parish priest, a Jesuit tutor at a nobleman's court, a vain Church official, be-ringed and ill-disposed toward the world.

He nudges the book closer to Shorr, as though he were presenting his wife. Now he taps the wooden covers gently.

"I have more. But Kircher is the best." He opens it at random and together they look at a drawing of the Earth, shown as a sphere, with the long, slender cone of the Tower of Babel upon it.

"Kircher proves that the Tower of Babel described in the Bible could not be as high as it is shown here. A tower reaching all the way to the sphere of the Moon would disrupt the whole order of the cosmos. Its base, resting on the globe itself, would have to be enormous. It would blot out the sun, which would have catastrophic consequences for all creation. Men would have to use up all the wood and clay on Earth..."

The priest feels as though he were uttering a heresy, and does not really know why he is saying this to the silent Jew. He wants the other man to take him for a friend, not an enemy. But will that be possible? Perhaps people can come to an understanding even without knowing each other's languages or customs, or each other, or each other's things and objects, or smiles, or the signs made by their hands, or anything at all; so perhaps people can come to an understanding through books? Is that not in fact the only possible way? If people read the same books, they would live in the same world, whereas now they live in different ones, like those Chinese Kircher wrote about. And there are others too, a great many, who do not read at all; their minds are asleep, their thoughts simple, animal, like those peasants with empty eyes. If he, the priest, were king, he would decree that one day of corvee be set aside for reading, he would drive the whole peasant estate to books, and at once the Commonwealth would look different. Perhaps it is even a matter of the alphabet - that there is not only one, but many, and each shapes thought differently. Alphabets are like bricks: from some, fired and smooth, cathedrals are built; from others, rough and clayey, ordinary houses. And though the Latin alphabet is certainly the most perfect, the other man, it seems, knows no Latin. So he points to one engraving, then another, then another, and sees him bend over them with growing curiosity, until at last he draws from somewhere a pair of spectacles neatly set in metal wire - Father Chmielowski would like such a pair himself; he must ask where to order them. The interpreter too turns out to be interested, so the three of them bend over the engraving.

The priest glances at them, pleased to have hooked them both, and sees golden and chestnut hairs in the Jew's dark beard.

"We could exchange books," he suggests.

He says that in his library at Firlejów he has two more works by the great Kircher, Arca Noe and Mundus subterraneus, kept under lock and key, too precious to be taken down every day. He knows there are other titles as well, though only from mentions here and there. And he has gathered many other thinkers of times past besides, including - he adds, to flatter him - the Jewish historiographer Josephus.

They pour him compote from a jug and slide a small plate of dried figs and dates toward him. The priest puts them into his mouth with reverence; he has not eaten them in a long time - their unearthly sweetness at once lifts his spirits. He understands that he should lay out his business now, it is high time, so he swallows the sweets and comes to the point; yet before he is done, he already knows he has spoken too soon and will achieve little here.

Perhaps he can tell from the sudden change in Hryćko's manner. He would wager his head that the boy is adding something of his own to what he is translating. He only does not know whether these are warnings of some kind or, on the contrary, something meant to support the priest. Elisha Shorr draws back slightly in his chair, tips his head backward and half closes his eyes, as if retiring to confer with the darkness inside him.

This goes on until, against his will, the priest exchanges a knowing glance with the young interpreter.

"The rabbi listens to the voices of the elders," the interpreter whispers, and the priest nods as if in understanding, though he understands nothing. Perhaps this Jew truly has some magical commerce with assorted little devils; there are enough of them among Jews, all those lamias and liliths. Shorr's hesitation and his closed eyes persuade the priest that it would have been better had he never come here at all. The situation is delicate and singular. May he not expose himself to infamy.

Shorr rises and turns toward the wall, lowers his head, and stands so for a moment. The priest grows impatient - is this a sign that he is to leave? Hryćko too now has his eyes half closed, and his long youthful lashes cast a shadow on cheeks covered with soft down. Perhaps they are asleep? The priest clears his throat softly; their silence has robbed him of the last of his self-assurance. He already regrets having come.

Suddenly Shorr, as though nothing at all has happened, moves toward the cabinets and opens one of them. Reverently he draws out a thick folio, marked with the same symbols as all the other books, and lays it on the table before the priest. He opens the book from the back and turns a leaf. The priest sees a beautifully wrought title page...

"Sefer ha-Zohar," he says with reverence, and puts the volume back into the cabinet.

"If only somebody could read that to you..." Hryćko says in a consoling tone.

The priest leaves two volumes of his New Athens on Shorr's table, by way of encouragement for some future exchange. He taps them with his forefinger, then points to himself - straight at the middle of his chest: I wrote this. They should read it, if they knew the language. They would learn a great deal about the world. He waits for Shorr's reaction, but Shorr only raises his brows a little.

Father Chmielowski and Hryćko go out together into the cold, unpleasant air. Hryćko keeps chattering on about one thing and another, while the priest studies him carefully - his young face with the first promise of a beard, the long upturned lashes that give him a somewhat childish look, and finally his peasant clothes.

"Are you a Jew?"

"Eh, no..." Hryćko answers with a shrug. "I'm from here, from Rohatyn - there, from that house. Orthodox, more or less."

"Then how do you know their tongue?"

Hryćko edges closer and walks almost shoulder to shoulder with the priest, plainly emboldened to such familiarity. He says his father and mother died of the plague in 1746. They had dealings with the Shorrs; his father was a craftsman, a tanner, and when he died, Shorr stood up for Hryćko, his grandmother, and his younger brother Ołeś, paid off his father's debts, and took the three of them under his protection. And so they live side by side; now he has more to do with Jews than with his own people, and somehow he cannot himself say when he learned their tongue; he speaks it as if it were his own, fluently, which often proves useful in all sorts of matters and business, because the Jews, especially the older ones, do not much care for Polish or Ruthenian. Jews are not as people say, and the Shorrs least of all. There are a good many of them and they keep a warm, welcoming house; there is always something to eat there, and a glass of vodka when it is cold. Now Hryćko is learning his father's trade, so that he can carry on tanning after him; there will always be a need for that.

'And haven't you any Christian kin?'

'I do, but they're far off and don't care much about us. Oh, and this is my brother, Ołeś.' A boy of perhaps eight comes running up to them, freckled all over. 'Don't fret over us for nothing, sir,' Hryćko says cheerfully. 'God made man with eyes in front, not in the back of his head, which means a man should mind what will be, not what was.'

The priest does indeed take this for proof of God's forethought, though he does not remember where in Scripture this is said.

'Learn the language from them, and you'll translate those books.'

'Oh no, sir, books aren't for me. Reading bores me. I'd rather go into trade, that's what I like. Horses best. Or, like the Shorrs, vodka and beer.'

'Oh, you've gone to the bad among them already...' says the priest.

'And what of it, are they worse than other wares? People need to drink; life is hard.'
