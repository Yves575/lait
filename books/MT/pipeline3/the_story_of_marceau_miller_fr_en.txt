Prologue





A flash drills through my brain, a violent blow to my skull throws me off balance. I am two hundred metres above the void, climbing a rock face I know well. My nails scrape at the granite, my hands lose their hold. In that instant I understand that it is all over. For the thrill of the challenge, out of pride perhaps, I have always climbed barehanded, never roped in. I knew that one day I would pay the price.

First I see the sky. Then a hundred metres of rock face rush past in the space of a few blinks. I can barely make out the silhouette disappearing behind the summit I was just about to reach. That silhouette is familiar to me. It has just caused my fall, into the void, and I am going to die. One thought calms me, strangely enough: I am going to join my sister. She has been gone for twenty years; when I got up this morning, I was a long way from imagining that we were to meet today.

My name is Marceau Miller, and I am a bestselling novelist. Tomorrow, on my Wikipedia page, you will be able to read, just below the line marked "Born": "Died: May 16, 2021 (aged 40)." The irony of fate is that I have spent years writing as though each day might be the last. I leave behind the most important manuscript of my life. As if some part of me had sensed this moment, anticipated this unforeseeable appointment with destiny. The Grim Reaper does not send you a registered letter to warn you of the day and hour of your death. I leave it to them, to Sarah, to the others. They can do with it what they can. I owed them the truth.





My body floats in the air; I no longer control anything. The ground is rushing toward me at a terrifying speed. My heart tightens for my children, Hermione, Benjamin, and my wife, Sarah, with whom I have built everything.

I catch sight of my pickup parked at the foot of the rock face and understand that I am going to crash right beside it. Nothing else crosses my mind. And yet they say your whole life flashes before your eyes in a moment like this. We have all had that awful nightmare: you are falling into the void, with nothing left to hold you back. You wake with your body drenched in sweat and your heart on the verge of bursting, with a feeling of unease that follows you for hours.

I have always wondered whether our brain disconnects before the fatal impact. In a few hundredths of a second, I will know.

Fifty metres.

Ten metres.

Darkness.









FIRST PART

THE FALL





Chapter 1

Saturday, May 15, 2021

The Day Before the Death of Marceau Miller





Settled in the rocking chair on the chalet terrace, I lift my head from the novel that has absorbed me completely. My tea is still steaming. This is where I love to be, facing Lake Geneva, my lake, as if it belonged to me. In every one of my husband's stories, nothing heralds the storm about to break over his characters. It is often stealthy; then it strikes head-on. I take a sip of my Saint James, a full-bodied black tea with chocolate notes, and set down my cup. It is his twentieth book, and he still manages to surprise me. I do not grasp everything about Marceau, but I have to grant him one thing: his success is deserved. He handles suspense like no one else. Too much, even. Everywhere, all the time.

The drone of an engine pulls me out of my reading for good. A boat is approaching the dock at the far end of the garden. I serviced it last week; it is part of the fleet of boats Karen, my partner, and I own. This little motorboat is nothing now but a patched-up nutshell, something we would never dare offer our clients. But it makes our children happy. Sometimes I take it to the agency, following the shoreline as far as Yvoire. It takes a quarter of an hour longer than driving, but it is just as practical as Marceau's old pickup for hauling equipment.

Benjamin is at the helm of the motorboat. At ten, he already handles it well, but he is coming in a little too fast for my liking as he nears the dock. Marceau waves his arms. He must be telling him to slow down. I let out an involuntary breath when the boat glides alongside the dock without a bump. They have spotted me. All three of them wave broadly. I smile and wave back. Hermione, our eldest, stationed at the bow of the boat, jumps onto the dock and catches the line her father throws her. I keep an eye on the manoeuvre. The dock on piles is not very wide; its long wooden legs sink into the depths and take root there in concrete footings embedded in a mixture of silt and green-stained pebbles. At this time of year, when the sun is still so warm, the planks are not slippery. Everything is fine. Benjamin cuts the engine and joins his father and sister.

I set my book down without even slipping in a bookmark. I have memorized the page number. Forty — Marceau's age, and mine. And by page forty, things are already going badly for the characters. It is only the beginning, I can see that.





I get up and stretch. The shadow of the pergola is cool across this terrace built by my grandfather, a cabinetmaker, his last piece of work before he suddenly disappeared about ten years ago. The chalet's exterior, partly covered in vegetation, gives the place the feel of a holiday home. The garden stretches for nearly eighty metres, bordered by woodland left in a wild state and a hedge that has also been left to itself. In fine weather, thanks to the lake nearby, the lawn stays green for a long time. It was mown not long ago, edged along the shore by a fringe of gray sand and pebbles. I head toward the children. Benjamin is already starting to tell me about his adventures as he runs toward me, but he is too far away; I cannot hear a thing. Hermione is coming back up with her father, the two of them laden with diving gear.

"I went down five meters with Dad! I saw a féra this big!"

My boy's outstretched arms seem too short for the enormous fish he spotted in the lake. Marceau reaches me and kisses my forehead. His lips still hold the coolness of Lake Geneva. Benjamin tugs at his sleeve so he will confirm what he says about discovering the miracle with shining scales. Hermione catches him off guard by pulling down onto his head the cap he dropped behind him. In that instant I feel how fragile happiness is, as fleeting as a fish vanishing into the depths. It gives me vertigo; I become aware of everything I hold most dear, and of the pain I would feel if I lost any one of them. Happiness reaches out its hand without warning. It can take it back at once.





"Sarah?"

Marceau smiles at me, then goes off to unload the diving gear a little farther away. I catch his eye; I have the impression I can read in it what I am feeling. But he knows the price and impermanence of these moments better than anyone. He has already put so many pieces back together after broken happiness. He makes me think of that Japanese art that repairs broken ceramics, kintsugi.

I can almost trace with my finger the golden scars his memories have left in him.





Chapter 2

Saturday, May 15, 2021

The day before Marceau Miller's death

The big hand on the living-room clock has not yet reached eight. Its familiar ticking fades, drowned out by a noise from outside. The daylight is ebbing; I catch the headlights of several vehicles filtering through the maples in the driveway. The low, unmistakable purr of Alexis's sports car engine makes the panes of the old bookcase I inherited from my grandmother Louise tremble. Through the window, I soon see him climb out of his sports car, wearing a wide grin. Alexis, true to himself as ever. Benjamin is already circling the gleaming vehicle. Alexis grabs him and holds him upside down like a rag doll. I hear their bursts of laughter; I smile too. Then they tear off in a mad race, disappearing toward the back of the garden, shouting.

Marceau comes down the staircase, repainted white, the dominant color in our home. I like light. The broad openings onto the lake, and the reflection of the mountains, mean we never have to take our eyes off it. I have arranged the chalet like a loft on the ground floor, a modern open-plan kitchen blended with the old, opening onto the living room and the library corner. A spacious living room, perhaps a little overfilled with furniture, I will admit that, but comfortable and warm. Marceau joins me by the sofa. He takes me in his arms; I know he loves my smile. He smiles back without taking his eyes off me.

"Are you ready?"

"Not really, for evenings like this, you know that. I'd rather slip under the sheets with you."

He melts me, the idiot. This is the phase I love most: the stretch between the moment he finishes one novel and the moment he vanishes into a new one. That's when I get him back, when we are closest.

"Then why are you so set on organizing this ritual every year for your novels' release?"

"Ritual, Sarah, is the key to a writer. A writer without ritual is a dead writer."

"You've become superstitious? Seriously, what are you afraid of? That it might stop? That your readers won't love you anymore?"

"The second one, beautiful lady: a writer who isn't afraid is a writer who's finished. You always have to write with passion layered over fear."

"That's a good line. You should save it for your next interview... And speaking of which, there's your publisher. Early, as always. He adores this evening. And especially my fresh goat cheese empanadas. Soon we won't be able to see his belt anymore."

Édouard Payet, with his genial paunch, lets his gaze wander over the chalet's facade, as if he were hesitating to come in. I know he is taking stock of the whole house. The woodwork, the openings, the roof's supporting timbers, the little upstairs balcony, the terrace, the carelessly tended greenery. He has always loved this place, the lair of his star author. He knocks on the door and we go to welcome him. In the publisher's wake, Freud, his Welsh Corgi, waddles into the small hall I've cleared of the things Benjamin always leaves lying around for the occasion. The little dog's claws skid on the shining floorboards. Short-legged and rather plump, barking nonstop, he looks like his master. The two of them are already ogling the victuals within sight, farther off on the kitchen island. I don't believe it: Édouard already has a handful of peanuts in his hand. I was just wondering where I had put the little bowl. He's obviously found it. With one motion, he lifts them to his mouth and swallows them without chewing, like pills. An ogre, but one with a jovial smile and disarming patter.

"Sarah, Marceau! I look forward to this day every year. Nothing changes here! And it shouldn't, that's the secret of my star writer."

His hearty embrace is as vigorous as ever, and on my shoulder I can feel the grains of salt from the peanuts his fingers have left behind; Freud didn't have time to lick them off. Édouard suddenly leans toward Marceau, a conspiratorial look in his eyes.

“Did you get my little present?”

I look at Marceau, who seems embarrassed. I don’t know what he’s talking about.

“Listen, Édouard, it’s magnificent, but... I’m not a watch guy, and... I’d hate myself if I damaged it.”

“A Breitling Aviator is made for you! You take your old Savage Bobber up almost every week. Marceau, loosen up a little! You work like a dog and never treat yourself enough. With all you make, you can afford it!”

“The house by the lake and my plane are enough for me. You know me. I don’t want to be caught off guard if one day everything stops, just like that, all at once, with no warning.”

“I could publish you with my eyes closed, Marceau.”

“Never do that.”

“Don’t worry, I’m not senile yet. And if the text wasn’t good, Marceau, rest easy, I’ve got good ghostwriters.”

Marceau’s face changes imperceptibly. He has just slipped into his own world.

“A mishap with a text... for me, that would be a fatal crash, Édouard.”

“Come on, I’m teasing. I’m always impatient to see what you’ll come up with, you know that.”

I hold out the tray of empanadas to Édouard while Marceau slips away. Freud lifts his snout and waits for the crumbs, but Benjamin comes running over and distracts him from his goal. My son strokes the dog’s head and leads him off, calling to him cheerfully.

Édouard wolfs down his last bite with undisguised satisfaction before heading toward the terrace with an almost ceremonial step. The publisher’s ritual can begin, the one he always performs far from my living room, where I can barely stand those heavy curls of smoke. From the inside pocket of his tweed jacket, he reverently draws out a Cohiba Behike 56, the Holy Grail of aficionados, nestled in its Spanish cedar case.

His gestures are precise, almost liturgical. He first takes out his Xikar pocket hygrometer to check the wrapper leaf’s ideal moisture level. Then, between his expert fingers, he rolls the cigar beneath his nostrils, breathing in the scents of leather and precious wood. The engine-turned silver cigar cutter, a gift from Marceau, makes a clean cut, releasing the first earthy notes. Édouard studies the end with care, like an oenologist scrutinizing the colour of a grand cru.

His S.T. Dupont lighter produces that distinctive blue flame. The toasting ritual begins: the flame delicately licks the foot of the cigar, preparing the tobacco for ignition. Then Édouard slowly turns the Cohiba above the flame, in a choreography measured to the millimetre. The first puffs rise, carrying the promise of the complex aromas to come: notes of roasted coffee, bitter cocoa and Eastern spices.

A smile of contentment takes shape on the publisher’s lips as he draws those first puffs, his eyes half-closed, savoring this moment the way one tastes an instant of eternity. The coming success of Marceau’s new novel is already mingling with the ribbons of smoke rising into the evening air, dancing above the lake’s calm waters.

I turn up the hi-fi; we use the power of these systems far too rarely. The sound fills the house and carries as far as the lake. Some evenings, I like to break the silence, bathe the chalet in joy and noise. I catch sight of Karen dancing on the terrace with her daughter, Zoé, and mine. I hadn’t seen them arrive. Karen is wearing a loose burnt-orange jumpsuit with butterfly sleeves, belted high at the waist. A light fabric with a plunging neckline that flatters her slender figure. The whole thing with ankle-strap wedges that lift her to nearly six feet. At that moment, I regret not choosing my linen trousers, the ones that show off my curves. Up on tiptoe, I kiss Karen and give her a wink, brushing her outfit with my fingertips; she smiles. The girls sweep her away again as I ask where Rollin, her husband, is. Between two dance steps, she points an arm toward the garden. Farther off, on the lawn, Alexis is drawing Marceau and Rollin, his closest friends of twenty years, along with him, ready to set sparks flying tonight. Alexis loosens them up, glass in hand, and they go from one series of suggestive hip sways to the next. Rollin, broad and muscular, is as awkward as ever, but he goes along with it. He is contemplative, warm by nature, the band’s gentle dreamer, unbeatable when we quiz him about flora and fauna on our outings. A nonconformist to his fingertips, he has never given up his freedom and gets by with his food truck. From the front steps, Édouard exhales heavy curls of smoke after drawing on his glowing cigar. Bright face, eyes riveted on Marceau. The truth, in moments like this, is that I am always afraid something will happen and break everything apart, as in my husband’s novels. There are false alarms, and real tragedies.

10:00 p.m. The sprinkler system kicks in. I’d forgotten about it; my heart leaps. A few drops hit me, cries of surprise and amusement burst out. Alexis holds Rollin under the artificial rain, while Marceau runs in long strides to the garden shed to stop the system. And while the three friends put on a show, my son tugs at my sleeve and pulls me under the fine refreshing droplets on this warm evening with its hint of summer. I sober up slightly, my heart thudding, but I breathe, and I let go.

The magic ends as suddenly as it appeared. The sprinkler system is stopped, the boys are soaked. Alexis takes off his clothes and tries to lure Rollin toward the lake. The latter resists the tipsy provocateur and lets him go. He has had enough already, soaked from head to toe. Alexis, mocking, almost naked in the middle of the vast garden at nightfall, in the sweep of the chalet lights, sends out one last call to the others. Rollin shakes his head while Alexis takes off across the grass and disappears in a dive into the lake turned to night. Several seconds go by, then he bursts up from the depths in a loud splash, smirking. The lapping of the water around him makes the surface shimmer, where the points of light from the lamps dance across the vastness of the lake. In the distance, the Swiss shorelines trace themselves like a lit serpent. The water is startlingly cold, I know that. Even in the grip of several drinks, you do not escape its cold bite. The most important thing is that it must not take the lives of those I love; it already spat me back out once just before I drowned. I owe it my being a seasoned swimmer, with a few medals to my name as well. It is him and me, like the forests and the mountains around us, always.

Rollin’s clothes cling to his skin, revealing his thickset shoulders.

“I think I need a bath towel now.”


"Idiot, that won't be enough," I say, laughing. "You know the house, run to the bathroom and grab some things from Marceau's wardrobe. They'll just be a bit tight on you."

Karen bursts out laughing with me as Rollin heads into the house. We have known each other for a long time, through our husbands. Our friendship, and the trust between us, have allowed us to imagine going into business together and creating this boat-rental agency, which is doing pretty well by now. A few yards from shore, Alexis whistles and throws his soaked underpants toward the garden. Freud needs no encouragement and catches the dripping fabric on the fly, bringing it back to his master. Alexis moves toward the bank and backs out of the water, turning his white backside toward us. Karen is the first to react:

"Alexis, the children! You're going too far!"

Alexis keeps showing off as he rejoins the dock, which he uses as a springboard to dive back into the lake again, with exaggerated clumsiness. Benjamin starts undressing too, but I stop him dead. Disappointed, he goes back to the shore and backs Alexis up, and from a distance Alexis tries to talk me round. It's hopeless, boys.

Rollin reappears a little later, dry, in Marceau's ill-fitting clothes. Something about him strikes me as strange. Is he tired? He has lost his smile and strides over, a large towel in hand, to rescue his naked friend, who is out of the water and starting to shiver. Alexis's anatomy hardly merits anything so large, but he will be more civilized draped in it for the rest of the evening.

They move a few steps away together, toward the back of the garden, under cover of the foliage, at the edge of the woods, not far from the moored boat still dancing in the wake of Alexis's antics. They seem deep in some hushed huddle. I hope Alexis is not drawing Rollin into another twisted idea. These evenings have this peculiar quality: they seem to plunge us twenty years back, into the carefreeness of those days. Marceau comes out of the house in turn, looking serious, as if he is already somewhere else, with that worried expression I know from time to time. Why tonight? Will I ever have an answer to that mystery? The rare times I have wanted to know more about the feelings moving through him, I have found myself as if on the edge of a bottomless precipice. That is the limit of our marriage. A rule we never speak of, a safe distance, his safeguard.

It is after midnight, the bathing episode has changed the mood. The buffet withstands the last assaults. Freud's barking draws everyone's attention. His fur is bristling and he stands facing the little wood beside the garden. Édouard whistles to him, to no effect. In the end he goes over to bring him back. The area around the chalet is full of nocturnal animals. The children sometimes stumble across them when they venture into the woods.

Édouard comes back. In his master's arms, tongue hanging out, Freud already seems to have forgotten everything. Édouard thanks me for the evening and reminds Marceau of the schedule for the next seventy-two hours, especially the television appearance on the big literary talk show and the studio recording for a radio spot. The publicist will send him his updated schedule for the bookstore signings. What I have learned over the years is that being a writer is only a kind of parole. I share my husband with Édouard, the journalists, the readers... But also with a great many fictional characters. If they could sometimes step aside and leave a little more of Marceau to me, I would like that better. Having him close his computer just long enough for the machine to digest the manuscript he is working on is not enough for me. But I am not the one who decides, and I am not sure he really decides it himself either."

At 1:45, everyone has gone home. Hermione and Benjamin are finally in their room, crushed with sleep. I tap discreetly at the door of Marceau's study, his sanctuary. I hear his muffled voice just behind it.

"Five more minutes, I'm finishing a message. I'll be there."

In my head, I translate... half an hour or an hour. I dart into the bathroom and swallow a Dafalgan. Fatigue, alcohol, the headache won't be long in coming. I'm exhausted but satisfied, the evening was a success. As I slip under the sheets, exhaustion and the slight floating feeling from too much alcohol carry me away. I don't like the idea of falling asleep without the warmth of my husband beside me, I resist. I need him. I resist a little longer, but my consciousness disconnects.









Chapter 3

Sunday, May 16, 2021

The day of Marceau Miller's death





Blurred light, 5:10 on the alarm clock display. Marceau isn't beside me. Damned writer. I frown, but my eyes close again. I sink back down.





8:15, a slight headache, I can sense the fine weather through the shutters. The light assaults me. Beside me, the sheets aren't even rumpled. Did he sleep in his study or what? Soft-footed, I cross the hallway without waking Hermione and Benjamin. What mother doesn't protect her children's sleep? Marceau isn't in his study. I don't go in, I hardly ever set foot there: he doesn't like it.

My memory struggles in the fog of waking, I search my recollections, nothing comes back to mind. He should be here, with me. The living room is a mess, I pick up a few remnants of last night's party here and there and scan the garden: no one. I step outside, barefoot on the terrace, facing the lake, calm, not a breeze disturbing it. Our little motorboat is moored, solitary, at the dock. The air is mild, almost like it is in the chalet. In the distance, opposite the hangar, I can't make out our old pickup. I thought it was still parked there yesterday. I go around the chalet, in the silence of the cool grass absorbing my footsteps. I feel the cold grass tickling the soles of my feet. I move closer to the hangar, wide open, as usual. The Savage Bobber is there, with its glittering propeller and broad wings. I'd have heard it taking off from the meadow next door anyway. Still no sign of the pickup. I turn around and glance toward the woods and the track leading to the road in front of our house. In a voice still half-extinguished, I call Marceau, but only a bird nearby answers me with a flap of wings and rises above the chalet. For a moment I envy its position, above the world, and its view, over the lake, over the mountains. Can it see Marceau from up there?





Back in the house, I open the cupboards in the entrance hall. I find the sneakers, Marceau's put away beside mine, and above all I check his climbing bag: empty. His climbing shoes and chalk bag are no longer there. I start, then I remember: he got rid of them, the day he promised me he'd stop free soloing — that thrill of climbing without a rope, without protection... and above all, to my mind, without any sense. Jackets and coats hang on the hooks or in the wardrobe. If he hasn't taken anything with him, he'll be back soon. I send him a text, reminding him in passing that we need to coordinate our schedules for the days ahead, between his obligations tied to promoting his novel and, on my side, the boat rentals that never let up at this time of year. Not forgetting that the children still don't manage on their own.

Noon. I peel potatoes irritably for the fries Benjamin is demanding. And for the tenth time, at least, I answer the children that no, I don't know where their father is. In the land of promises, Marceau is the first to desert. Hermione was counting on him to install her smart lighting system, Benjamin on him for the complicated assembly of his new remote-controlled plane. I advise them to negotiate compensation. On my phone, no new message. I call for the third time, still no dial tone: "Hello, you have reached Marceau Miller..." I cut it off. By the fifth text, my tone changes.

Fuck, where are you?









Chapter 4

Monday, May 17, 2021

The day after Marceau Miller's death




8:00.

I call Benjamin one last time. Otherwise it'll be school without breakfast. Hermione is on edge. Asking her to clear the table is enough for her to storm off cursing and slam her bedroom door. A normal Monday morning, well, almost normal. Usually, Marceau gets on Hermione's nerves before I do.

Last night, I served them my first lie: Dad is away for the launch of his new novel, I forgot, he'd told me about it. I still haven't started the series of phone calls to friends and acquaintances; though I don't believe it, I cling to the idea that Marceau is going to turn up with an explanation I hadn't thought of, to the idea that everything will start up again, normally.




9:15.

A bitterness in my throat, it's stress, my stomach can't handle it. In the bathroom medicine cabinet, I search desperately for medication. I find nothing, half of it is out of date, it'll need sorting. It'll be fine. As soon as I hear something, everything will go back to normal. Feverish, I get my things together, trying not to forget anything for this workday that's already looking bad.




Late to the agency, I don't have the headspace for the usual emergencies. Karen is already handling the first customers. Probably because of the stunned look on my face, she asks me if everything is all right, and what I did with the rest of the weekend after Saturday night's party. And in the end I crack. I admit I haven't had any news of Marceau since the end of the evening, in fact. Does she have any idea where he could be? I read the bewilderment in her eyes. I must look like an idiot. She hesitates, searching for a reassuring explanation. Could it be linked to the release of his book? It doesn't hold up, he would have left me a note, even to be alone, as he sometimes does. I go around the agency as if I were naively looking for him here too. Karen pours me some tea and checks the list of our available boats while I count them one by one, moored at the agency dock. On rare occasions, Marceau has borrowed one, just as Alexis or Rollin have, for that matter. Karen calls Rollin, while I try my luck with Alexis. My heart starts racing as all the holds I'm clinging to give way. They know nothing. I move on to his publisher. Édouard can't think of anything better than to remind me of the urgency of the TV appearance and the radio interviews over the next few days. Failure after failure, even after contacting acquaintances of acquaintances. My knuckles turn white from clenching my phone. An alarm goes off in my mind. I read the same worry, barely veiled, in Karen's eyes. She assures me she can handle the agency alone and practically pushes me out the door; I have better things to do, I need to find Marceau.

I have always been able to count on Karen. We understand each other perfectly, laughing at the same things, keeping snap judgments at bay, dealing with our flaws. Real partners, with boundless trust between us. So much so that it is almost dizzying: she could take everything from me if she wanted to; I would keep my eyes closed. Truly, going into business with her to set up the agency on the lake has been one of the best decisions I have ever made. Choosing to be independent was too, and to live in keeping with my values. Finding my place, on the lake, surrounded by forests and mountains, my own ground, my roots.

But Marceau is my roots too. Leaving without warning, without answering his phone, especially at the launch of his new book, is not normal. It is as if someone is asking me to move forward without light, with nothing to hold on to. My heart leaps at every text, every call I get, more and more of them, from the people around us, as worried as I am, asking for news, sometimes suggesting ideas... and no doubt making up their own minds too, in silence.




15:20.

I am back from a long drive along the lakeshore, from Yvoire to Meillerie, all the way to the Swiss border. I stop everywhere: restaurants, sports clubs, every place Marceau goes, sometimes very isolated ones, when he wants to write far from everything. Act, I have to act. I need help. I park in front of the gendarmerie in Thonon-les-Bains, the nearest and the biggest.




A knot tightens in my stomach as I walk in: what am I even doing here? This is where I am, with my dread as baggage. They are my last option. A young gendarme listens to my distress and writes down everything I tell him. He tries to reassure me and convinces me there is nothing alarming for the moment. He starts asking me questions about my marriage, conflicts, possible infidelities... My face hardens. I remind him that my husband has disappeared and that I am here precisely because I have no explanation. He tells me to calm down. Did I raise my voice?

Eventually, a Captain Robin Delmas comes to see me. An arrogant type who looks down on me. In his office, I have to start all over again, step by step, explain everything again, again: the evening, the alcohol, yes, no other substances, obviously not. I can barely keep my voice under control. On the verge of losing my temper, I stammer. Who does he take us for? Among celebrities, illegal substances are common, he insists. That is when I really get angry. No, Marceau does not touch coke. Our first and last joints go back to high school. I cannot stand his condescending look. Marceau left no note, nothing is missing from the house? No. No warning signs, no particular changes in his habits? I have already gone over all these questions myself. I let out yet another sharp "No," short of breath. He left in the pickup, that is all.




Delmas concludes that since Marceau has been gone for less than forty-eight hours, he cannot initiate any action for a forty-year-old adult in full possession of his faculties, with no particular conflicts or worrying reasons. And that is that. He stares at me, with an almost satisfied smile thrown in for good measure. I feel the urge to hit him rising inside me. Clearly, I am the only one who understands that nothing about this situation is normal. I feel that whatever I say, he will hear nothing. As if I am up against a wall, my anger comes right back in my face. They walk me out; I am to keep him informed, case closed.

I come out feeling even worse than when I went in, struggling to breathe, a sense of emptiness, of abandonment. Misunderstood, scorned, but no less determined. He will be seeing me again, Delmas. That much he can be sure of. My brain is running flat out. There have to be solutions... And then a flash. An idea. I climb into my vehicle and tear out of there.




Chapter 5

Monday, May 17, 2021

The day after Marceau Miller's death




Thonon-les-Bains is in my rearview mirror. Captain Delmas's exasperating voice is still echoing in my head, as if he were hanging off my bumper. I want to speed up and leave him far behind me, but traffic on the main road is at a standstill. I turn onto the side roads, closer to the lake. It won't save me any time, but at least I can take the curves. The view of the lake opens up between breaks in the greenery, ash, beech, maple. At least it doesn't betray me. I race along the narrow roads between houses with tiled roofs worn smooth by time, sometimes tinged with the pale green of moss. The wood-clad homes blend into the landscape. At last I near our place. A familiar landscape, comforting and cruelly empty at once. My heart beats in time with the last bends in the road, wavering between the nagging hope of finding Marceau and the icy fear of his absence.




When I reach the chalet, I spot a vehicle parked in front of the house. The hedge trees barely let me make it out. My heart is pounding fit to burst. I accelerate down the dusty track, a mix of shell gravel and dirt where the holes and stones are hard on both tires and suspension. In my wake, a brown cloud rises and whitens the leaves all around. Maybe Marceau has finally come home? I have no room for anger, so much does anguish flood through me. Pins and needles race over my body. Fear feeds on me, as if everything were slipping away from me. Like that strange sensation before something happens.

A figure bursts out from the trees and waves both arms at me. I realize I'm going much too fast. Instinctively, with adrenaline surging through me, my right foot slams on the brake pedal, my tires skidding for several yards. I stop level with the man who, a little shaken, is batting air and dust away from his face. It's old Reynaud. I understand then that the 4x4 is his. Despite my wild hope being dashed, seeing him here comforts me. Yves Reynaud knows me as well as Marceau does, ever since we were teenagers. He is also, above all, a retired gendarme, solid as a rock, a man who doesn't look his seventy years. We don't see each other often, but with him it's as if time swallows up the gaps and the distance. He is always there, faithful, loyal. Paternalistic. He comes around the car and climbs into the passenger seat. The engine is still running, I can't speak, my face is undone. He leans toward me and takes me in his arms. I suddenly feel overwhelmed, bite my lips so I won't cry. On his shirt I can smell the warmth of summer and the scent of the forest, just like on Marceau when he comes back from his long walks. Reynaud was born here; this region is his. I cling to him like a providential lifeline.

— Tell me everything from the beginning, Sarah. What's going on with Marceau?




I can see in him the solicitude of a friend, eager to reassure me, ready to do anything to help, and also the instincts of the former gendarme, more worried. At least he listens to me. The old fox knows this region like the back of his hand. I go through it again. That's always how it goes in this kind of case. Just saying the word case frightens me. All of this sends us back twenty years. My brain refuses to remember. I know Reynaud is thinking about it. I start up again more calmly and park in front of the house.

We get out, he doesn't leave my side, he watches me. I lead him toward the garden shed; I hadn't yet thought to inspect it.

— What are you looking for in here, Sarah?

I open the plastic storage bins and catch sight of our diving gear. I rummage through the clutter on the makeshift workbench that serves as a desk. I know Marceau comes here from time to time. I find nothing but papers, tools, nothing that catches my attention. I slam my fist down and send a set of screwdrivers flying. Reynaud pulls out wooden bins set on high shelves. I tell him to be careful, they're full of nails, screws, points. Reynaud handles them carefully; they're heavy. One of the bins is empty, but before he puts it back, I rub my fingers across the bottom: a fine film clings to the pads of my index and middle fingers. I smell it. It's chalk. The powder you coat your palms with to keep them dry and make sure of your grip while climbing. Reynaud meets my eyes.

"He'd promised me he would stop."

A sob cuts me off and keeps me from going on. Reynaud takes me in his arms. Right then, I want to cry, but nothing comes, as if shedding a tear would confirm that the worst has happened. I'm afraid, as if I already know. All it takes is a faint trace of powder, almost imperceptible. No secret can really hide. I feel Reynaud's heart pounding in his chest. He lays his calloused hands on my shoulders, his face changes. Then he lowers his eyes before he speaks. I look for them; he raises them to me.

"Listen, Sarah, I know Marceau well, we happen to see each other from time to time."

"What do you mean? Without me? Why? He never told me."

I pull free of his grip.

"We all have old demons."

My blood turns to ice. The door of my worst memory creaks on its hinges. Flashes make me shudder. I refuse those images. That pain is like the needle of the syringe I dreaded so much when I was a child — I would thrash and scream. She was my best friend. It was twenty years ago. She was Marceau's sister. Born the same year as him, less than a year between them, we were inseparable.

I stare at Reynaud for a long time before asking him:

"You're talking about Jade, is that it?"

"It was my case back then. I don't know where we fucked up..."

The ground feels as if it's giving way beneath my feet.

"Where is Marceau right now? Talk, for God's sake!"

"I don't know, Sarah, I don't know... but it's happened that I've caught sight of him near..."

He crushes the silence, as if to let me digest it. But I don't want to waste time, I want to know.

"Near the fucking place where Jade disappeared, up in the mountains, is that it?! Why stir all that up again? Do you know how much it hurts?"

Everything comes back. For me too, it doesn't go away.









Chapter 6

Monday, May 17, 2021

The day after Marceau Miller's death

Reynaud's 4x4 devours the mountain road towards the border between France and Switzerland, less than forty kilometres away. On the narrow D25, between hedgerows and forests, we pass through peaceful villages in bloom, where the houses with their exposed timbers and broad roofs seem to have always been there, like the postcards of our grandparents. Near Sciez, we continue on the D1005, the main road that leads all the way to Switzerland. Reynaud speeds up and overtakes one car after another on the stretch of road that passes through Evian. He is silent, focused. Near Evian, we drive along the lake, with the Alps on the horizon, their summits capped with white. As we go on, the hillsides, with houses clinging to them, impose their dominance. With each passing kilometre, the land around us steepens into mountain. We pass Lugrin, then the border at Saint-Gingolph. I send Karen a text, asking if she can take care of Benjamin until I return. I have not been back to the exact place we are heading for in years. And yet Marceau and I sometimes hike in the surrounding area. We have always shared that irrepressible need for nature, for forests, for mountains, for escape. But at this hour, the immensity around us suddenly makes me dizzy.

The road gives way to a track that grows more and more stony. Shaken inside the vehicle, I hold on. The tyres bite into the mountain as we gain altitude, my eardrums popping. On the track, narrowing like the source of a stream, we crawl along past the Planche car park, a tiny place covered with dirt and stones, isolated in the middle of the trees, the last one still accessible by car. Reynaud keeps going along the track as it becomes less and less passable. Then, on a bend, a few hundred metres from Switzerland, he cuts through the trees. The branches crack under the tyres. He pushes the 4x4's engine, staying in first gear, until we can go no farther and come to a stop. We are at almost eighteen hundred metres above sea level, about a forty-minute walk from Lake Neuteu. We exchange a glance, Reynaud takes out a water bottle, puts it into a backpack, and picks up his walking poles. This is the first stage, the simplest one. Around us, the forest, not just any forest, not for me, not for Reynaud. My heart is unsettled, I breathe out and set off. Reynaud already has dozens of metres on me. This ascent, with the air of a hike, overwhelms me. I watch where I put my feet, avoiding holes, the mountain's traps. I begin to feel the heat in my thighs and calves. A veil of sweat on my face. Then it appears before us, Lake Neuteu, surrounded by jagged massifs, forests as far as the eye can see, and oppressive memories. I chase away the flashes that blind my mind, but the dread persists, clings on. The varnish of the years has never truly covered the tragedy deep within my whole being.

I arrive first, galvanized by fear. I have overtaken Reynaud, who has lagged behind. My gaze scans the edges of the lake, isolated in these mountains, like a laser. Nearly two hundred metres long and barely a hundred wide, bordered by short grass running towards the summits and giving way to bare rock. Absolute desert, not a soul on the horizon. The only landmark truly worthy of a solitary man like Marceau rises there, about a kilometre away. The highest rocky peak in the landscape, a monster of verticality made of rock and challenge. Not an easy walk, more a route for an experienced mountaineer. Reynaud comes up beside me. I think of the chalk found in the garden shed; a shiver runs down my spine. Reynaud names it for me.

"The Dent du Velan."
