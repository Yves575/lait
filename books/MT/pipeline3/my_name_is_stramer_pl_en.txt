I

NATHAN

He came back from America for Rywka. He said he'd thought about that girl every day for four years, until at last he bought passage home. He never said he'd come back without a penny, and that even for the ticket he had to borrow money from his older brother. But he still liked to drop American words into conversation, words no one in the family understood.

He brought back from America a leather belt with a brass buckle. A farewell present from his brother for the trip back to Poland. On the New York docks they hugged and never saw each other again. By looking at the wear on one hole after another, you could see how his shape had changed over the years. Nathan himself could hardly believe he'd once been so thin.

Like his father and grandfather, he looked like a wrestler. An old wrestler. Broad shoulders, barely any neck, and a chest thrust forward like a rooster's. He was proud that people in town called him Boss.

When people on the street asked how things were, he would answer:

"Good, but not hopelessly so."

He touched the brim of his hat and walked on.

Every few months a letter would come from New York. Ben wrote that unfortunately, for the time being, he had to put off the trip to Tarnów. He couldn't leave the stationery shop just now. The school year was starting and he had too many orders. He had to let a dishonest employee go, and he still hadn't found a new one he could trust. Soon it would be the Christians' Christmas, time for presents, and over here it wasn't only Jews who shopped! His wife had fallen ill again. The crisis had begun; if he left the shop now, there would be nothing to go back to in America. But he missed them and was planning for next year. He could hardly wait to meet Rywka, and their sons and daughters too.

As parents, you know best what they need, so buy it for them from me.

Inside the letter, written on company stationery (BEN STRAMER GENERAL MERCHANDISE, 33 Grand St, NY) and folded in thirds, he tucked green bills. He also wrapped them in purple tissue paper so it wouldn't show what was inside. The tissue paper stained the bills every time, so when they came out of the envelope they were green and violet, and Rywka had to wash them gently and then dry them over the stove in the kitchen.

Nathan gave the children only the American stamps from the envelopes. The dollars he put away.

"I work all the time, so I don't have time to make money," he explained to his wife.

And he waited. Waited, waited, waited. Until the right deal came along. A deal that would change the Stramers' lives. Lift them out of the one-room apartment with the little kitchen on the ground floor at the very end of Goldhammer Street, carry them high up and set them down only on the top floors of tenement houses with stained-glass windows in the stairwells, in the elegant Polish-Jewish district, preferably near a stop on the tram line Tarnów was so proud of. In one of those spacious, high-ceilinged apartments with many rooms, with a toilet and a bathroom with running water, electric light, and a balcony full of flowers.

For a brief while it seemed it would come true. He had bought a whole freight car of rosin cheap. They told him violinists used it on their bow hair. How was he supposed to know there were so few violinists in Tarnów? In the end he bought a violin and bow himself and had Salek, who, Rywka said, had beautiful, long fingers, play the violin.

Though he had never been to the philharmonic or the opera, he could already see his seven-year-old son there in his mind's eye, on the stages of Kraków, even Vienna. Rywka too most easily pictured him in a little tuxedo, with a bow tie and a storm of curls. Unlike his brothers, he didn't like fighting or getting dirty.

"Here," said Nathan, handing him the instrument. "A famous violinist never did any family any harm."

And everybody knew how much money there was in ticket sales.

But nothing came of that either.



True, he did send him a few times to violin lessons with a Polish teacher. But did Salek really make it there? Because every time guests came and Nathan asked him to give a "little concert," Salek found an excuse. Until after half a year Nathan stopped asking and ordered him to "bring the violin this minute."

After the performance he said nothing.

Only after the guests had left did he say, shaking his head on what little neck he had:

"Goddammit!"

No one understood, so he added:

"Money down the drain."

That evening, for the first time, instead of reaching for his belt, he reached for the bow. At least it would be good for something! And indeed it was. He didn't have to hit hard at all, and Salek made sounds much like the ones he himself had been making on his violin just moments before, during the "little concert."

At work, behind the register in the Jewish slaughterhouse, Nathan too waited for the deal of his life. That was why, swept up in his thoughts, his plans, and the future profits he had calculated down to the last detail, he sometimes gave customers the wrong change.

"Shit," he would say if there was money missing from the register at the end of the day.

But sometimes there was too much money. Then he said nothing, only discreetly slipped the difference into his trouser pocket.

I have children, he thought.



Whenever that right moment finally came, he felt like running home from work. On the way he picked up groceries. Once home he didn't sit down for a moment. He helped Rywka get the food ready. Over supper he quizzed the children about school. Mind you, he had no patience to listen. But at least he asked, Rywka thought. Then he asked her to sit down and rest. He cleared the plates himself, rolled up his shirt sleeves, and washed the dishes in the basin.

Only when he was finished, and the children were asleep, did he take the dollars rolled into a little tube out of the metal leg of the bed.

"I've got a feeling this time it'll work. That this will be it."

Rywka said nothing. But Nathan knew what she was thinking:

Why, with that last deal, hadn't it occurred to you to take a proper look at even one candle?

He didn't want to waste time; he was afraid other merchants would beat him to it, that someone would offer a higher price. But what he feared most was that the seller, who had come all the way here from Kolomyia, would realize how much candles cost in Tarnów and understand that he was selling his for next to nothing. Nathan had to act fast. An opportunity like this was sure never to come again. Candles four times cheaper than those from the famous Jewish factory Pszczółka, which supplied all the churches in Tarnów! Maybe even in Kraków. That was what he'd heard, but he didn't know whether it was true or just publicity. Besides, he'd also heard that the owner, Mr. Szpilman, quietly gave money to the socialists. But that in turn might have been negative publicity spread around by Pszczółka's competitors.

As he paid, Nathan even felt like a swindler for a moment. Maybe he should throw in a little extra for that poor seller from Kolomyia, or at least invite him to dinner? But that feeling at once gave way to regret that the man had no more goods to sell. So maybe it wasn't worth it after all.

That was how Nathan answered, in his head, the question Rywka had not asked him.

They sat facing each other at the slightly wobbly kitchen table; the floor was uneven, and the little piece of wood under one leg must have slipped out again. As usual, Nathan tried to push it back with his shoe. It didn't work, so he bent down, found the sliver, and put it back in place. Tomorrow morning, when it was light, he would finally clean it and glue it down, he decided.

They both knew none of his explanations would convince her, and that was probably why they were talking without words. Or maybe they simply didn't want to wake the children? They only looked at each other, but all the same, for a moment it seemed to Nathan that in Rywka's light eyes, instead of the one little candle burning on the table, he could see the thousand he had bought without wicks.

What difference did it make that as soon as he realized, he ran straight to the station and spent his last money on a platform ticket? The train to Kolomyia, with the merchant on it and Nathan's dollars still faintly purple, had long since left. Nathan stood among the people on the platform for a long time, repeating incomprehensible American words before he admitted to Rywka what had happened. And that evening, despite the closed window, his children's crying could be heard on Goldhammer Street.

Then he went to bed and didn't get up for work for a week. At the slaughterhouse, Rywka said the same thing she had said to the children:

"Nathan's got stomach trouble."

This time it would be different, his eyes were saying.

It made no impression on Rywka that his eyes had lit up again, as they had when she first met him. Sometimes she only wondered where that energy of his came from, coming and going in waves. Once she even asked Nathan.

"I brought it back from America," he said.

Then she thought of the sea he had told her about after he came back to Tarnów. It too was tossed by waves; there were tides coming in and tides going out. It was rarely calm - like her.

"More likely you caught it at sea," she said with a smile.

In any case, she definitely preferred stories about the sea and great ships to stories about New York. They soothed her. She liked to imagine them before falling asleep.

RYWKA

Nathan had promised many times to take her to the sea, but that was before the wedding and the children. The farthest she had ever been was Krakow.

Of course, when she was young, she had been offered passage to America. What girl hadn't been? A pleasant-looking, graying man with a signet ring on his little finger accosted her in the street. A Jew, of course. He introduced himself as a businessman, the owner of a thriving factory in Nebraska, and "above all an inconsolable widower who had returned to Europe in search of love."

His search even took him to her parents. Rywka's father threatened to go to the police. It wasn't for nothing that Galicia was called Scandalicia in those days. Everyone knew what it was really about and who they were really looking for. Even so, two girls she knew from Nisko, Nesa Schnur and Lila Flaum, agreed. Or maybe their parents talked them into it and pushed them out of the house? She heard they ended up in Argentina or Brazil. She never saw them again.

In Nisko people said pimps called pretty girls "silver spoons," "bolts of silk," and "Smyrna carpets." The less pretty ones were "barrels of flour," and the ugly ones "sacks of potatoes." She still remembered those terms. Maybe because the girls called one another those names. She wondered which category they would have put her in.

She knew she was too short to be a silver spoon. But she had light eyes darkly framed, thick black hair, and a slim waist. So she was hardly a barrel of flour, much less a sack of potatoes.

Now, on Goldhammer Street, those days seemed unreal to her. She got married, moved to Tarnów, had children. Much had changed in her life. But had the world changed too? You still heard about pimps trafficking in "tender meat." After all, it hadn't been so many years. Even now she still sometimes began a letter by mistake with the date eighteen hundred... Somehow she couldn't get used to this twentieth century.

Maybe that was because she rarely read newspapers. Who had time for that with six children?

In fact, she had given birth seven times. Her first son, Kuba, was born too early, without eyelashes or fingernails. She blamed herself: while she was pregnant, she had needlessly helped Nathan paint and furnish their first apartment, smaller even than the one on Goldhammer Street. Once she even slipped and fell by the entrance. She should have stayed in bed. Anyway, it no longer mattered.

It was winter, they wrapped him in the warmest feather quilt, but it did no good. He lived not quite three weeks. It was so unnatural to arrange a funeral for her own child, to walk through the snow behind her own son's coffin. She forced herself to cry. She couldn't, so she covered her face with her hands. So the others wouldn't see she wasn't crying.

The little coffin wasn't expensive, but it still cost them everything they had. They buried Kuba by the cemetery wall.

The night after the funeral, muffled sobbing woke her. She nestled against Nathan, and only then did she burst into tears herself.

She didn't know whether Nathan missed their little one as much as she did. She didn't know whether he too was afraid Rywka might never get pregnant again. Like his sister-in-law Pepi, who before leaving for America had also given birth too soon and ended up in the hospital with the baby, but came out alone. From what Nathan said, that loss was what decided them to leave. Then again, everyone knew America had the best doctors for such things.

But Ben and Pepi, in letters written in an even hand on business stationery, never mentioned a pregnancy or a child. It was in the ones sailing the other way (and perhaps they passed each other somewhere at sea, Rywka thought) that Nathan, in his nervous handwriting, announced the birth of yet another Stramer every time and described the others. Maybe that was exactly why they never managed to come to Tarnów. Even though they were their closest family. But they never once forgot to slip money into the envelope.

It was easier for Rywka to understand than for Nathan. After all, what if it had been the other way around? What if we were the ones left childless, and from across the ocean, instead of dollars, news kept coming about more and more American nephews and nieces? How would we feel?

So she thought of Ben and Pepi with fondness. And whenever she thought of them, Kuba would come to mind.

"The first child is for God," Nathan had once said.

"Don't talk like a rabbi," she had answered.

Who would he have taken after? Would he, like the eldest, Rudek, never ask their advice about anything and spend his days racing around town? What did he actually do, and where did he get the money he slipped her for the household?

She could only guess.

RUDEK

One Sunday Nathan happened to spot him among a Polish-Jewish gang of half-grown boys who, fooling around, were following a regiment of Austrian soldiers marching through Tarnów's main streets in tight dress uniforms. Possibly to mark the first anniversary of Emperor Franz Joseph's death, since there was even a band playing a march. That was what Nathan had come to see. Or rather, to get a look at the violinist. At that point he was still predicting a musical career for Salek; he had only just bought him a secondhand Höfner violin. He wanted to see what a real violinist looked like. But why couldn't he spot him anywhere?

Like everyone else, he found himself staring at the drum major conducting the band, at his white staff with a brass ball on the end. Now the ball was up, now it was down, now it was spinning around its own axis, and at the finale it flew into the air.

I wonder how much they pay him for that, Nathan thought, and froze in anticipation: maybe for once the drum major wouldn't catch his fancy staff.

He'd sooner have expected that than to run into Rudek here. At first he couldn't believe his son was with the others, picking cigarette butts up off the ground, making insulting faces and gestures at the soldiers. And on top of that, bawling for all of Sobieski Square to hear:

At Ulm, at Ulm, at Austerlitz

We took it up the ass and never said a word.

Nathan happened to know that song from his own youth. He started shoving his way toward Rudek.

Because we Austrians have this habit,

that we take it up the ass and don't say a word.

He didn't let him get through the next verses. He hauled his son out of the crowd by the collar. And in front of everybody he thrashed him with his American belt. A few Austrian officers with twirled mustaches turned their heads without breaking step; some of them lifted their pince-nez to their eyes.

Nathan threaded the belt back through his belt loops and asked:

"Will we ever have it better than we had it under Emperor Franz Joseph?!"

And added:

"And if I ever see you again with a cigarette in your gob, I'll rip it out along with your lungs."

That was less than a year ago, but apparently it worked, because lately Rudek could be found every day by the wall of the military barracks, across Goldhammer Street, where the Austrian garrison was stationed, and his cigarettes were not in his gob but in his pockets.

For the past week he had been taking Salek there with him after school. Did Rudek look much older, or Salek much younger, than they really were? Or maybe it was just the contrast between them. A tall, broad-shouldered boy in a flat cap and knickerbockers bent over a small boy — crammed into a tight school uniform with a high collar, really still just a child.

In any case, more than thirty centimeters or a year and a half separated them. Salek did not play football, not on the right wing in a blue jersey and white shorts like Rudek for Samson Tarnów, not in any other position on any other team. He never played a thing. He never learned to whistle through his fingers or spit properly. Whenever they shared a bottle of lemonade outside the shop, he soaked his uniform. And worst of all, he still sometimes pissed on his trouser legs under a tree, even though Rudek had shown him a few times how to avoid it.

"You can shake it for two weeks, a drop still sneaks into your pants," he lectured him. "Just not on your pants."

Boxing and wrestling were much the same. Rudek made Salek practice on their younger brothers, Hesio and Nusek, again without much success. In the end he more or less taught him one move, a headbutt to the nose. It was after that one that Hesio got blood on his shirt, and all three of them ended up getting the American belt.

In any case, when somebody picked on him, the most Salek did in anger was clench his fists and windmill his arms.

He's in no danger anyway, everyone knows whose brother he is — Rudek felt warm inside. But he also prepared him for the possibility that in another neighborhood they might not know, and somebody might try to hit Salek. He was to say that he knew Omega, Zachar, or Bänder. There was probably no one in Tarnów who thought of them without fear.

"Nobody'll touch you."

He did not admit to Salek that he didn't know them himself either. Why does anybody else need to know?

What everyone did know was that Omega, Zachar, and Bänder, if they were not in prison, could be found in the evenings in the company of prostitutes outside the Hotel Polski on Dworcowa Street. But everyone preferred to avoid that. People said they feared nobody.

As if by accident they shoulder into people, shove passersby off the sidewalk, and knock their headgear off. School caps, peaked caps, hats, yarmulkes, and the fur shtreimels of Hasidim land in mud and puddles that fill the place year-round after even the slightest rain.

And that's only the beginning. Then the knife-men wait for a reaction. Will the victim muster even the slightest objection, if only in a look, the tiniest gesture of displeasure, visible in the dark only to them?

"So you're a tough guy?"

And then they come so close you can smell what they ate and drank that day in the third-class restaurant at the railway station.

Of the three, only Dawid Bänder didn't hit right away. First he played with the knife. That was what people feared most, because Bänder was unpredictable. He might let one go free and suddenly stick the knife into another.

"Circumcise you, tough guy?" he was said to have once asked a terrified Prince Roman Sanguszko, whose car had broken down near the station one night.

Rudek understood the importance of such an encounter. At school they taught that the Sanguszko family had once owned Tarnów, and on the street they taught that Bänder was the great-grandson of Idele Muc, the legendary leader of Tarnów's thieves. And because Rudek was one of the most diligent pupils in both places, he understood that the descendants of the city's rulers had stood face to face.

"Circumcise you, little tough guy?" Rudek and his brothers acted out that scene as a joke many times in the courtyard on Goldhammer Street.

"Anything but that! Mercy on His Princely Grace!" Salek, in the part of Sanguszko, would cower behind his hands and swoon against the wall of the wooden outhouse.

SALEK

He had picked that up at the theater. For his birthday Rudek had given him a standing-room ticket. They were performing Samson and Delilah, directed by Dante Baranowski, who was also playing the male lead. During the performance, in the cool, rather empty hall of the Sokół, he could not concentrate on the actors hurling themselves about the stage, now roaring with rage, now weeping and fainting. He recognized Delilah at once as Jadwiga Geron, the elegant wife of the gymnasium's Polish teacher, to whom he bowed in the school corridors. Should he at least bow to her discreetly from a distance, as he usually did? He did not want her to feel offended. He also did not know whether he had the right to sit down, though more than half the red seats were empty. Who was he supposed to ask? Besides, all the while he kept wondering whether Dante Baranowski had really been born Dante Baranowski.

Maybe earlier he too had been just an ordinary Salek or Jurek. And only as an adult, to appear on a poster and step onto the stage, he first had to change his first name to something more artistic-sounding. That made him think of Goldhammer Street, which had still been called Zdrojowa Street when they moved there.

And how would this sound: Dante Stramer?

After the performance Rudek was waiting outside the Sokół.

"Rudziu, why is Baranowski named Dante?" Salek asked on the way home.

“Baranowski’s mother was simply Italian, and Dante is a typical Italian name.”

How did he manage to know everyone, and so much about them? Salek looked up at his brother with admiration.

But he could impress Rudek too.

He became popular at the gymnasium very quickly, already in his first year, when during recess, at the sight of Father Wątorek, the Catholic religion teacher, and Izaak Bleiweiss, the Mosaic religion teacher, walking arm in arm across the uneven courtyard, he called out to his classmates:

“The Old Testament is walking arm in arm with the New.”

Some did claim somebody from one of the older classes had come up with it, but Rudek did not believe them. After all, there was an unimpeachable witness to Salek’s priority: their friend and fellow student at the same gymnasium, Romek Brandstaetter.

Before long the joke had taken over the whole clamorous courtyard and the school’s brick building. And the bolder ones began shouting it at the religion teachers.

“Do you hear that, Professor?” Wątorek finally asked.

And Bleiweiss replied:

“Why shouldn’t I hear it, if you please, Father Monsignor?”





At school all sorts of rumors circulated about them. People whispered, for instance, that they so often walked arm in arm because neither of them had a wife.

Salek neither listened nor took notes in Mosaic religion class. He did his math homework, wrote Polish compositions, prepared for the tutoring he gave after school. And when he was done, he looked through the window at the people walking along the street. He thought that after classes he too would be walking there, and those who saw him through the window then would simply see a boy walking down the street. There was nothing unusual or extraordinary in that, and he himself did not know why he so often returned to the thought that in a moment he would be for others what they were now for him.

He also watched the trash bin by the intersection across the street. He counted how many people between one bell and the next would bend over it and put a hand inside. Now there was already a fourth, though not yet a quarter of an hour had passed since the bell: an old woman was pushing the trash aside, probably looking for scraps of food. Before her there had been a boy, no older than he was, who with a quick motion had gathered bent cigarette butts into the pocket of his coat. Rooks came to the bin too, and they not only made the most noise and the biggest mess, they also gave the others a bad name, because they scattered trash onto the pavement. But Salek was not able to count them.





The Hebrew alphabet now seemed sad, gloomy to him. It reminded him of the poorest part of Tarnów, the old Jewish quarter. The letters hung down like the sidelocks and beards and the too-long black coats of Grabówka’s inhabitants, forever muddy at the bottom.

He knew everything Bleiweiss taught anyway. Once it had been interesting, he might have liked it. Fairy tales for the three-year-old boy he had been when his father first took him by the hand to cheder. Not the one nearest home, where Rudek had gone the year before, but one much farther away. Salek’s legs hurt; he was almost running to keep up with his father. He did not notice the curb, stumbled, fell on his hands, but got his trousers dirty too, and on top of that they tore at the knee.

“Goddammit!” his father jerked him up.

Salek burst into tears. He didn't want to go to cheder anymore.

"Because you'll go to the one near home."

A year earlier Rudek had come running back from there with a fever, determined never to go back. It took a long time before Mama got out of him what had happened. Only after she promised not to repeat it to anyone did Rudek tell her that the melamed didn't just yank boys by the ears and beat them with a switch for getting Hebrew words wrong. He had made Rudek strip naked and stand in the corner of the room, while the other children were supposed to come up and spit in his face. That was when he ran away.

"What did he make you do that for?" she asked.

"Because I didn't believe the Jews crossed the Red Sea on dry land."

Without a word Salek went on after his father. All day in cheder he kept his hand on his knee and repeated passages from the Torah after the melamed with the others.

True, the old teacher never raised a hand against the children, or even his voice, but to make up for it he always had one unchanging answer to every question and doubt:

"You little fool, that is what is written in the Five Books of Moses."

Salek heard the same thing when, after a year of study in that damp room, which was also the melamed's entire apartment, he finally dared ask the first question he had been carefully preparing for days:

"Why, when children sat on a wall and laughed at the bald head of the prophet Elisha as he passed beneath it, did God let two crazed she-bears out of the forest to tear forty-two children apart?"

Only Rudek explained to him that you simply had to respect your father and both your grandfathers, even if they were bald under their hats.

In any case, fortunately God never repeated anything so cruel in cheder. Like the melamed, he kept silent at the sight of the oldest boys quietly playing cards, button-toss, and slap-hands under the table. He remained just as unmoved when they stood behind the teacher and pretended to be waltzing with him, or, holding their noses in disgust, took his old patched long johns and socks out of the cupboard with two fingers, or imitated a dog that had mistaken the old melamed for a she-dog.

Maybe it was because the melamed was far removed from the power, holiness, and clarity of mind of the prophet Elisha, Salek thought. Or maybe because he wasn't bald: long, matted gray hair stuck out from under his yarmulke.

II

NUSEK

In Tarnów people said the farther you got from Grabówka, the cleaner the air. Nusek breathed there only through his mouth. The smell of that neighborhood might remind you of the outhouse on Goldhammer Street, or of a Friday night when, after Shabbat dinner and too much cholent, the children lay awake for a long time.

"PKP?" Salek or Hesio would ask from time to time.

"RKM," Rudek would answer.

"No!" if she was still awake, Rena would shout. "I won't allow it. Go out to the courtyard."

At night the flat on Goldhammer Street turned into a bedroom. Three beds filled the room and a fourth the kitchen. The chairs and the table stood in the middle, and you had to remember they were there, especially in the dark, on the way to the outhouse. But Nusek almost always tripped, or at least bumped into something.

Rywka made sure each child had as much room as possible. She had decided that Rudek, the tallest and oldest son, would sleep in a bed with Nusek, the youngest; beside them Salek with Hesio, who was only a little smaller; and a little farther off, behind the screen, the girls, Rena and Wela, the youngest of them all. Only Suchard the dog and Milka the cat got to choose for themselves each evening at whose feet they would spend the night.

"What does PKP mean?" Nusek asked. "What does PKP mean?" he repeated, as if he thought Rudek hadn't heard.

"You'll feel it soon enough."

"Why do you never tell me anything? Why?"

"For the common good. Go to sleep."

"Just tell me what PKP is."

"Polish State Railways."

"But really?"

"Really. Go ask at the station."

Nusek wondered why they never took him seriously. And how many times had he begged his older brothers to take him with them down by the barracks? No point even talking about it.

Surely they couldn't still be on about that stupid business with the bow. Especially since he'd done everything Rudek told him to do. He had meant well, the best proof being how hard he'd tried, too hard even.

Nusek closed his eyes, but he couldn't fall asleep. He could hear his father's steady snoring. Salek mumbling something indistinct in his sleep. And the bed creaking when Rudek turned over and settled his head on their shared pillow.

He could feel his warm breath on his face.

Why do I always get the least of everything? he thought. Room in bed, the pillow, even the quilt that Rudek tears away from me every night?

"You asleep?" he heard Hesko ask.

He opened his eyes.

"What?"

"Want to know what PKP and RKM mean?"

"Yes. Tell me."

"And will you carry the wood and coal for me for a week from the storage shed in the courtyard?"

Nusek still couldn't fall asleep, but now for a different reason. Every so often he looked admiringly at Rudek asleep beside him, his mouth open. Maybe he really did deserve the whole pillow they shared, even the quilt? The very thought of PKP and RKM made Nusek want to laugh. They were smart! That way they could say it at school right in front of the teachers, or at home right in front of their parents. He would never have thought of that himself. It took someone like Rudek or Salek to come up with it. All you had to do was call something by a different name and that was that.

"That head isn't just for wearing a cap," his father used to say whenever he was cooking up another deal.

But that saying didn't suit his father, and not only because Nusek had never seen him in a cap. When he went out, he usually took one of two old crumpled hats from the rack, alternating between them.

It suited the head lying beside Nusek's on the pillow far better.

Again he remembered all the times he had heard them say PKP and RKM. He had suspected they were strong swear words, or some mysterious code connected with cigarette dealing, with girls, or at least with communism. But he giggled, because it sounded so elegant: PKP? — Pozwoli Kolega Pierdnać? — Would the fellow permit a fart? And RKM: Raz Kolega Może — The fellow may, just this once.

He fell asleep smiling. As always, he wanted to catch that moment when he was falling asleep, and as always, he failed at the last moment.





RYWKA

At night some of them dreamed one another. Rywka was almost sure, too, that sometimes they dreamed the very same dream at the same time. Apparently there were solitary dreams and shared ones, she thought, like graves in a cemetery, rooms in a hotel, or cabins on the ships Nathan talked about.

She could have sworn that once she heard the children playing football together in their sleep.

"Pass it to me," Nusek asked.

"Hesio, to you," Rudek said. "Shoot for the top right corner."

"Goal!" Salek shouted.

Rywka waited to hear what would happen next, but there was only silence.

"Nathan," she said, nestling up to her husband and kissing his broad nape. "You won't believe what I heard."

"All the kids in all the squares and courtyards in Tarnów play it," Nathan said with his eyes closed, "and you don't have to wake me up over it right away."

He knew what he was talking about. The day before, on the front page of a newspaper at the kiosk, he had seen a long headline: A ball-kicking epidemic is spreading through our city at an alarming rate. Through the dirty pane, the last word was especially hard for him to make out. Not like the first, whose big broad letters had drawn him to the kiosk.

As usual, he did not buy the paper, but tried to read at least a few lines right there.

There is not a courtyard in our town where a dozen brats, little Jews, or street urchins do not at once start kicking a ball through the mud, whatever the weather. We only fear that our ladies may take up kicking too, for then the end of the world would come.

Maybe he hardly ever cleaned the window on purpose — Nathan looked approvingly at the old toothless vendor — so people wouldn't read for free.

You don't understand! Rywka wanted to cry out. They were playing in their sleep!

But instead she said:

"Sorry. Sleep in health, wake in health."

By morning she had forgotten about it. She had to get everyone out the door. To work, to school, to cheder. In the room, in the dark, she laid out the clean clothes they were to put on. In the kitchen she got the fire going under the stove plate. On the table she set before each of them an evenly cut slice of dark bread with butter and poured chicory coffee. Nathan, as head of the family, had the right to an extra breakfast roll.

"Daddy, lend me your roll," Nusek said. "I'll give it right back, I'll just eat it first."

At first Nusek did not know what they were laughing at. Only after a moment did he join in, and then he laughed loudest of all, as if he wanted to make up for the time it had taken him to understand his own joke.

"I'll give it right back, I'll just eat it first," he repeated proudly, pointing to himself. "I'm smarter than I thought."

Before they went out, Rywka would wash the children's faces, straighten their collars, comb their hair.

In the doorway, saying goodbye, she would first stand on tiptoe beside Rudek, then bend her knees and go lower and lower, stopping in turn by Salek, Rena, and Hesio on her way down to Nusek and Wela, finally crouching by Wela.

"Down you go like a New York elevator." Nathan had promised not to say it anymore, but again he could not help himself.

Rywka always kissed the children in the same spot. Her favorite. Where the forehead ends and the nose begins. She did not know what it was called, but she called it "the little saddle."

Every morning, the same thing. That way, as she kept telling herself and Nathan, there was no time to be bored. This morning routine had become deep-rooted. Sometimes it was only while eating her slice of bread or washing the dishes that she realized Nathan and the children had already gone. As if someone else had woken them that day, made sure they put on warm underwear, drank coffee, and did not leave the house late.

III

NATHAN

"I bought it," Nathan announced in the doorway, proud as if he were talking about the Nitrogen Works, not a sideboard from the neighbor on the second floor, who was selling off his last pieces of furniture before leaving for America.

"For next to nothing," he added once the door was shut.

That was probably what he liked best about the sideboard. And at once he sent Rudek, Salek, and Hesio upstairs.

"And Nusek?" Hesio asked.

"Too small to be a porter."

Rudek went down the stairs first. So fast, as if he were carrying the sideboard on his own and not with Salek and Hesio, who could barely keep up, gripping the oak legs tighter and tighter so the sideboard would not come crashing down on their brother.

After they scraped the wall for the third time, Rudek spoke in Anszel Koszcz's manner:

"Gentlemen... is this what they pay you for?"

Salek asked if they might take a short break so he could tie his shoe. Three steps later, when Rudek set the sideboard down on the first floor, his hands were shaking.

Anszel, the tallest and broadest-shouldered of all Tarnów's porters, was their unofficial boss. He could probably have carried down an even heavier piece of furniture by himself, with no trouble and no one to help him. Quite recently Rudek had watched him milling around by the Pilzno Gate. Looking for work, he circled slowly, with patient steps, among the stalls.

Like the Earth around the Sun, Rudek thought.

And indeed Anszel almost never stopped. If the porters wanted to talk to him, they had to walk beside him, circling in his orbit. He was the one they went to with everything. He was their highest authority; on earth, at least on the uneven, muddy earth by the Pilzno Gate, there was no appeal beyond him.

With the black patch over his left eye and the rope slung over his shoulder, Anszel looked like one of the pirates their father used to scare them with, but he kept order. He usually spoke calmly, even with a smile, but everyone knew what would happen to a porter who, say, stole a parcel, or, God forbid, to a customer who did not pay. Rudek had often seen Anszel, in one quick motion, pull an iron shaft from his handcart.

And how had he lost his eye? Tarnów knew so many versions. Maybe even Anszel himself was no longer sure how it had happened, and that was why he told everyone something different.

"Besides, there may be even more versions than listeners," Rudek explained to his brothers. "Because some people heard a different one every time."

Rudek liked the heroic one best. Years earlier, one July Saturday, hooligans had surrounded the Jewish quarter. They assaulted people and broke into shops closed for Shabbat. They must have planned it well, because besides clubs and scrap iron they had sacks into which the stolen goods went.

"It was their bad luck," Anszel would say, without stopping his rounds among the stalls, "that they also attacked the porters' families coming out of the synagogue on Fish Square."

Anszel's men ran to the Pilzno Gate, where during the holidays they left their carts fastened to one long chain. They pulled the iron shafts from them. Jewish workers with crowbars and planks joined the porters. And at the head walked Anszel.

"We drove them out of the quarter. We smashed the wagons they tried to escape in and turned the horses loose. We got everything back. Only I lost something." Anszel would point to the black patch.

"But you saved the town from a pogrom," people answered him with undiminished admiration. Maybe because the cities and small towns around Tarnów had not been so lucky.

"They just got a little whack on the paws." Every time the story came up, Anszel would modestly shrug his shoulders, as wide as the sideboard the three Stramer brothers were struggling to carry from the second floor down to the ground floor.

Salek had heard a little less heroic version at school. Before an older boy repeated it to him, he asked for discretion. Earlier, the boy's father had asked the same of him, after one of Tarnów's rope merchants had in turn sworn him to secrecy.

"I'm telling this only to you," Anszel was supposed to have said in a low, hushed voice to that trustworthy man walking at his side.

Before he became a porter, Anszel had done a few years in prison after catching his wife with a lover. The only thing unclear was whether he lost the eye while serving his sentence, or already in his victorious fight with the lover he killed.

Salek had heard yet another account of the incident from Hesio, one with no mention of prison at all.

"Turns out it was a jealous husband who attacked a sleeping Anszel with a knife. He got home earlier than usual from his job at the slaughterhouse and found him in his wife's arms."

The thought of a bloodbath, of a butcher's knife driven into an eye, quieted the shrieking courtyard on Goldhammer Street for a moment.

Nathan, who had heard through the open ground-floor window what they were talking about, said at supper that back in his younger days he remembered a drunk Anszel crashing his bicycle. He rode into a ditch. He must have impaled himself on something there. A dry branch, a piece of wire, broken glass. The next day he woke up without an eye, drenched in blood, and remembered nothing.

What impressed the children most about the story was that their father knew Anszel personally.

"I've met plenty like him in my life." Nathan gave a dismissive wave.

He also showed the children the Jewish porter's shove. He called Hesio over and, without warning, shoved him with his protruding belly. Little Hesio went flying into the wall, where luckily they had not yet put the elegant sideboard with the horse heads jutting out at the top, but tears still sprang to his eyes. When Salek timidly objected that the porters at the Pilzno Gate and on Fish Square were thin and wouldn't have anything to hit with, Nathan did not even look at him, only shook his head.

"In America," he added quietly, half to himself.

"I mean the Jewish porter's shove in America," he repeated loudly, as if he had realized that it was not at all obvious to the rest of the family that the real porters, craftsmen, traders, and all the potbellied rest were there, where his brother Ben was. Among really tall buildings with real elevators. On really wide, really long streets with sidewalks, full of real shops, cafes, and customers paying for everything with the realest dollars.

"Even the sky is higher there," he had once let slip.

At times Rywka had the feeling that Nathan had not really come back from there yet. She guessed that he often imagined how his life might have turned out across the ocean. And that it seemed more real to him than their life in Tarnów, which was why he neglected it.

Gently but thoroughly, she wiped the little carved wooden columns with the horse heads jutting out at the very top. This elegant oak sideboard did not fit with the rest of the furniture. Not with the bench Nathan had knocked together from three pine boards, not with the chest of drawers with four drawers that barely opened, not with the table that always limped, or the wardrobe beside it with the cracked back panel. It did not fit their cramped ground-floor apartment, or rather the apartment did not fit it.
