You were the one who told me about that town.

That summer evening, with the sweet smell of grass in our nostrils, we made our way upstream along the river. We crossed several little waterfalls formed by check dams, and every so often we stopped to watch the slender silver fish swimming in the pools. We had both been barefoot for some time. The clear water washed cold around our ankles, and the fine sand of the riverbed enfolded our feet—like soft clouds in a dream. I was seventeen, and you were a year younger.

You had carelessly shoved your red low-heeled sandals into a yellow vinyl shoulder bag and kept walking a little ahead of me, from sandbar to sandbar. Wet blades of grass clung to your wet calves, making lovely green punctuation marks. I carried my worn white sneakers, one in each hand.

As if the walking had worn you out, you drop down carelessly into the summer grass and look up at the sky without a word. Two small birds cut swiftly across the air side by side, crying sharply. In the silence, the blue forewarning of dusk begins to gather around us. When I sit down beside you, a strange feeling comes over me. It is as if thousands of invisible threads were delicately binding your body to my heart. Even the slightest flicker of your eyelids, the faintest tremor of your lips, can set my heart quivering.

At such an hour, neither you nor I has a name. There is only this vivid feeling on the grass by the river, at summer dusk, when we are seventeen and sixteen. Soon the stars will begin to appear one by one above us, but the stars too have no names. We sit side by side on the grass by the river in a world without names.

“The town is surrounded by a high wall,” you begin to say. You draw the words up from the depths of silence, like someone diving alone into the deep sea for pearls. “It isn’t a very big town. But it isn’t so small that you can take in everything at a glance, either.”

This is the second time you have spoken of the town. And so the town comes to have a high wall around it.

As you go on speaking, the town acquires a beautiful river and three stone bridges—the East Bridge, the Old Bridge, and the West Bridge. It acquires a library and a watchtower, an abandoned foundry, and plain apartment houses. In the pale light just before summer dusk, you and I sit close together, almost shoulder to shoulder, gazing at that town. At times we narrow our eyes from a distant hilltop; at others we open them wide from so near we could almost reach out and touch it.

“The real me lives inside that town enclosed by the high wall,” you say.

“Then the you sitting here in front of me now isn’t the real you,” I ask, naturally enough.

“Yes. The me who is here now isn’t the real me. I’m only her substitute. Something like a shifting shadow.”

I think about that. Something like a shifting shadow? For the moment, though, I decide to withhold judgment.

“So what does the real you do in that town?”

“I work in the library,” you answer in a quiet voice. “My hours are from around five in the evening until around ten at night.”

“‘Around’?”

“All the times there are approximate. There’s a tall clock tower in the central square, but it has no hands.”

I picture a clock tower with no hands. “And can anyone go into that library?”

“No. It isn’t open freely to just anyone. To enter it, you need special qualifications. But you can go in. You have them.”

“Special qualifications—what kind of qualifications?”

You smile faintly, but do not answer.

“But if I can get there, I’ll be able to meet the real you, right?”

“If you can find the town. And if . . .”

There you fall silent, your face flushing lightly. But I can hear the words you do not voice.

And if you truly seek the real me . . . Those are the words you deliberately leave unspoken. I slip an arm gently around your shoulders. You are wearing a pale green sleeveless dress. Your cheek comes to rest against my shoulder. And yet the one I am holding that summer evening is not the real you. As you say, she is no more than a shadow standing in for you.

The real you is inside that town enclosed by the high wall. There is a beautiful willow-thickened island in the river there, and a few gently rising hills, and everywhere quiet beasts with a single horn. People live in old apartment houses and lead lives that are simple but without want. The beasts like to eat the leaves and nuts of the trees that grow in the town, but in the long winters, when the snow lies deep, many of them die of cold and hunger.

How fiercely I wished to enter that town. To meet the real you there.

“The town is enclosed by a high wall, and it’s very hard to get in,” you say. “It’s even harder to get out.”

“How can I get inside?”

“You only have to desire it. But it isn’t so easy to desire something with all your heart. It may take time. And during that time, you may have to cast various things aside. Things that matter to you. But don’t give up. No matter how long it takes, the town will not disappear.”

I imagine meeting the real you inside that town. I picture the vast apple orchard, lush and beautiful, outside the town, the three stone bridges across the river, the voices of unseen night birds. And the small old library where the real you works.

“There will always be a place there waiting for you,” you say.

“A place for me?”

“Yes. There is exactly one vacant position in the town. You will fill it.”

What sort of position could that be?

“You will become a 〈Dream Reader〉,” you say in a lowered voice, as if confiding an important secret.

Hearing that, I can’t help laughing. “Listen, I can barely remember even the dreams I have myself. For someone like me to become a 〈Dream Reader〉 would be awfully difficult, wouldn’t it?”

“No. A 〈Dream Reader〉 doesn’t need to dream himself. All he has to do is read the many 〈Old Dreams〉 gathered in the library stacks. But not just anyone can do that.”

“But I can?”

You nod. “Yes, you can. You already possess the qualifications. And the me who is there will help you in that work. I’ll stay beside you every night.”

“So I’ll be a 〈Dream Reader〉, and every night I’ll read many 〈Old Dreams〉 in the library stacks of the town. And you’ll always be beside me. The real you.” I repeat aloud the facts that have been set before me.

In my arms, your bare shoulder above the green dress trembles faintly. Then suddenly goes rigid. “Yes. But I want you to remember one thing. Even if I do meet you in that town, the me who is there won’t remember a single thing about you.”

Why?

“You don’t understand why?”

I do understand. Yes—the one whose shoulder I am gently holding now is no more than your stand-in. The real you lives in that town, in the mysterious town far away, encircled by a high wall.

And yet the shoulder in my hand is so smooth and warm that it can only seem to me the shoulder of the real you.

In this actual world, you and I live some distance apart. Not so far that it could be called very far, but not so near that I could decide on a whim and go to see you. If I changed trains twice and traveled for about an hour and a half, I could reach the city where you lived. And neither the place where I lived nor the place where you lived was surrounded by a high wall. So of course we were free to come and go.

I lived in a quiet suburban neighborhood near the sea, and you lived in the center of a much bigger, busier city. That summer I was in my final year of high school, and you were in your second. I went to a local public high school; you went to a private girls’ school in your city. For various reasons, we saw each other in person only once or twice a month. We more or less took turns: I would visit the city where you lived, and you would come to the town where I lived. When I visited you, we would either go to a small park near your house or to the public botanical garden. There was an admission fee for the botanical garden, but beside the greenhouse there was a café that was never very crowded, and it became our favorite place. There we would order coffee and apple tart—a small extravagance—and lose ourselves in our own quiet conversation.

When you visited my town, the two of us almost always walked along the riverbank or the shore. There was no river near your home in the middle of the city, and of course no sea, so when you came to my town the first thing you wanted to do was see either the river or the sea. You were drawn to all that natural water.

“Looking at water calms me down for some reason,” you said. “I like listening to the sound it makes.”

We had met the previous autumn, by chance, and by then had been close for about eight months. Whenever we met, we embraced in places where we would not easily be seen and gently pressed our lips together. But our relationship never went further than that. One reason was that we simply didn’t have the time. Another was the practical fact that we couldn’t find a suitable place to become more deeply intimate. But more than either of those, the larger reason was probably that we were, above all, absorbed in talking to each other alone, intent on making use of every minute. Neither of us had ever before met anyone with whom we could speak so freely and naturally, saying exactly what we felt and thought. To come across such a person seemed almost miraculous. So whenever we met, once or twice a month, we would simply talk and talk, forgetting the passage of time. No matter how long we talked, our subjects never ran out, and when the time came to part and say goodbye at the station gate, we always felt that we had forgotten to say many important things.

Of course it wasn’t that I felt no physical desire. When a healthy seventeen-year-old boy stands before a sixteen-year-old girl with beautifully swelling breasts, and all the more when he has his arms around her supple body, there is no way he will not feel sexual desire. But instinctively I felt that such things could wait until later. For the time being, what I needed was to see you once or twice a month, to take long walks with you, and to talk candidly about all sorts of things. To exchange the intimate details of our lives and come to know each other more deeply. And then to hold each other somewhere in the shade of a tree and press our lips together—I did not want to hurry any other element into those lovely hours. If we did, something important there might be damaged, and we might never be able to return to the way things had been. Physical things could be kept for later. That was what I thought. Or perhaps that was what intuition was telling me.

But when we sat there with our foreheads close together, what in the world did we talk about? By now I can’t remember. Perhaps because we talked about too much, I can no longer identify each topic one by one. But once you began telling me about the special town enclosed by a high wall, it came to occupy the greater part of our conversations.

Mainly you told me how the town was made, I asked practical questions about it, and you supplied the answers. That was how the town’s concrete details were fixed and recorded. The town was something you had made. Or perhaps something that had always existed inside you. But in bringing it forth as something visible, something that could be described in words, I think I helped no little. You spoke, and I wrote it down—just as ancient philosophers and religious teachers had behind them faithful, painstaking recorders, or people called apostles. As an able scribe, or a loyal apostle, I even made a small notebook for the sole purpose of recording it. That summer, the two of us were completely absorbed in that collaborative work.

In autumn, the beasts’ bodies are covered with splendid golden fur in preparation for the coming cold season. The single horns rising from their foreheads are sharp and white. They wash their hooves in the chilly river water, stretch out their necks to devour red berries, and chew the leaves of the broom shrubs.

It is a beautiful season.

Standing in the watchtower built into the wall, I wait for the evening horn. A little before sunset, it is sounded once long and three times short. That is the rule. The soft sound of the horn slips through the darkening cobbled streets. For perhaps hundreds of years—or perhaps for longer still—that sound must have gone on repeating itself unchanged. Its tone has seeped deep into the cracks of the stone walls of the houses, into the stone statues lined up along the hedges in the square.

When the horn resounds through the town, the beasts lift their heads toward some ancient memory. One stops chewing leaves, another stops clacking its hooves on the paving stones, another wakes from its doze in the last patch of sunlight, and each one raises its head to the same angle.

Everything is fixed for an instant, like sculpture. The only thing moving is their soft golden fur, stirring in the wind. Even so, what in the world are they looking at? With their necks bent in one direction and their gaze fixed on empty space, the beasts do not stir. They listen intently to the sound of the horn.

When the final note of the horn is swallowed by the air and vanishes, they rise, drawing their forelegs together, or stretch and compose themselves, and almost at the same moment they begin to walk. The brief spell is broken, and for a while afterward the streets of the town are ruled by the sound of their hooves striking the ground.

The line of beasts proceeds through the winding cobbled streets. No one stands at the head, no one leads the procession. With eyes lowered, their shoulders swaying faintly from side to side, the beasts simply move down the river of silence. And yet between each one and the next there seems to exist an intricate bond that cannot be denied.

After watching them several times, I come to understand that the routes they take and the speed at which they move appear to be fixed with exact precision. Gathering others into the herd here and there, they cross the Old Bridge in its gentle arch and walk to the square with the pointed spire there (the clock on the tower, just as you said, has lost both its hands). There they collect the few small groups that had gone down to the island in the river to graze on green grass. They proceed upstream along the road by the river, pass through the factory district along the dry canal that runs north, and gather up another group that had been foraging for nuts in the woods. Then they turn west, pass beneath the foundry’s roofed passageway, and climb the long stairway that runs along the northern hill.

There is only one gate in the wall surrounding the town. Opening and closing it is the gatekeeper’s duty. It is a heavy, solid-looking gate, reinforced with thick iron plates set both vertically and horizontally. But the gatekeeper opens and closes it with ease. No one but him is permitted to touch it.

The gatekeeper is a large, powerfully built man, yet extremely faithful to his work. His pointed head is shaved clean, and his face is smooth as well. Every morning he boils water in a large pot and carefully shaves his head and face with a large sharp razor. His age is impossible to guess. Blowing the horn morning and evening to gather the beasts is one of his duties too. He climbs the small tower in front of the gatehouse, perhaps two meters high, and sounds the horn toward the sky. From what part of this rough, almost boorish-looking man does such a soft, lustrous sound emerge? Every time I hear the horn, I find myself wondering.

At dusk, once he has sent every last beast outside the wall, he pushes the heavy gate shut again and finally drops the great lock into place. It makes a dry, cold clack.

Outside the north gate there is a place for the beasts. There they sleep, mate, and bear young. There are woods and thickets there, and a small river runs through it. That place too is enclosed by a wall. It is a low wall, only a little over a meter high, yet for some reason the beasts cannot cross it. Or perhaps they simply do not try.

On the wall to either side of the gate there are six watchtowers. Anyone can climb them by way of old wooden spiral staircases. From the watchtowers, one can see the beasts’ dwelling place at a glance. But ordinarily no one climbs them. The people of the town seem to have no interest at all in the beasts’ lives.

But for one week at the beginning of spring, people willingly climb the wall’s watchtowers to watch the beasts fight savagely. At that season the beasts become ferocious beyond anything their usual behavior would lead one to imagine, and the males, competing over the females, forget even to eat and fight with all their strength. Growling, they try to drive the sharp tips of their single horns into their rivals’ throats or bellies.

Only during that one week of mating season do the beasts not enter the town. The gatekeeper closes the gate so that the townspeople will not be put in danger (and for that reason the horn is not sounded morning or evening during that period). No small number of beasts are grievously wounded in the fighting, and some lose their lives. And from the red blood spilled on the earth, a new order and a new life are born—just as the green branches of the willows all bud at once in early spring.

The beasts live within a cycle and an order of their own, one we cannot fathom. Everything is repeated with regularity, and that order is paid for in their own blood. When that savage week has passed and the gentle April rains wash away the spilled blood, the beasts once again return to their former quiet, gentle existence.

But I have never actually witnessed such a scene with my own eyes. I have only heard about it from you.

In autumn, the beasts remain crouched in their various places, their golden coats shining in the evening sun, silently waiting for the sound of the horn to be swallowed up into the air. There are probably no fewer than a thousand of them.

That is how a day in the town comes to an end. The days pass, and the seasons change. But days and seasons are only provisional things. The town’s true time lies elsewhere.

You and I do not visit each other’s homes. We do not meet each other’s families, and we do not introduce our friends to one another. Put simply, we do not want anyone—anyone at all in this whole world—to interfere with us. You and I are fully satisfied just spending time together, and we do not want to add anything else. And even from a purely physical point of view, there is no room there for anything more. As I said before, there are mountains of things we have to say to each other, and the time we can spend together is limited.

You hardly ever talk about your family. All I know of them are a few disconnected facts. Your father had been a local civil servant, but when you were eleven, some kind of “blunder” forced him to resign, and now he worked as an office clerk at a cram school. I don’t know what sort of “blunder” it was. But apparently it was the kind of thing you did not want to put into words. Your natural mother died of cancer of the internal organs when you were three, so you have almost no memory of her. You can’t even remember her face. When you were five, your father remarried, and the following year your younger sister was born. So the woman who is now your mother is, in fact, your stepmother; but once, just once, you said something to the effect that you might still be able to feel at least a little affection toward her—more, at any rate, than toward your father. Like an unobtrusive footnote printed in tiny type at the corner of a page. About your sister, who is six years younger than you, I learned nothing beyond this: “My sister is allergic to cat hair, so we can’t keep a cat.”

When you were a child, the only person toward whom you could feel genuine, natural affection was your maternal grandmother. Whenever you had the chance, you would ride the train alone to visit her house in the neighboring ward. During school vacations, you would sometimes stay there for a few days. Your grandmother doted on you unconditionally. Out of her meager income, she even bought you little things. But each time you went to see her, you would notice a displeased expression on your stepmother’s face, and although she never actually said anything, little by little you stopped going so often. That grandmother, too, died suddenly of heart disease a few years ago.

You tell me these things in scattered fragments, as if drawing something tattered and worn from the pocket of an old coat, a little at a time.

And there is one more thing I still remember well—when you talked to me about your family, for some reason you always stared fixedly at your own palm. As though, in order to follow the thread of what you were saying, it were absolutely necessary to read the palm lines there—or something of the sort—with the utmost care.

As for me, there was almost nothing I could think of to tell you about my own family. My parents were utterly ordinary parents. My father worked for a pharmaceutical company, and my mother was a housewife. They behaved like ordinary parents and spoke like ordinary parents. We kept an old black cat. Nor was there much worth telling about life at school. My grades were not bad, but neither was I so exceptional as to stand out. The place at school where I felt most at ease was the library. I liked sitting there alone reading books and passing the time in reverie. Most of the books I wanted to read, I had already read in the school library.

I remember well the first time I met you. It was at the awards ceremony for a “High School Essay Contest.” The top five winners had been invited, and you and I had placed third and fourth, with seats next to each other. It was autumn. I was in my second year of high school, and you were still in your first. The ceremony was a tedious affair, so during the pauses we spoke to each other in low voices, little by little. You were wearing your school uniform: a navy blazer and a matching navy pleated skirt, a white blouse with a ribbon, white socks, and black slip-on shoes. The socks were white as could be, and the shoes were polished without a single stain—as though seven kind dwarfs had carefully shined them before dawn.

It wasn’t that I was especially good at writing. I had loved reading ever since I was little, and whenever I had free time I would pick up a book, but I had never believed I possessed any talent for writing. Yet everyone in my class had been forced to write an essay during Japanese class for the contest, mine had been selected and sent to the committee, it had survived the final round, and then, to my astonishment, had placed near the top. To be honest, I couldn’t understand what was so good about it. Even rereading it, it seemed to me no more than a plain, unremarkable composition. But if several judges had read it and decided it deserved a prize, there must have been something in it. My homeroom teacher, a woman, was delighted that I had won. Never in my life had a teacher responded so warmly to anything I had done. So I said nothing unnecessary and accepted the prize with gratitude.

The essay contest was held every autumn on a district-wide basis, with a different theme each year, and that year the theme was “My Friend.” Unfortunately, I could not think of a single “friend” I wanted to write about over five pages of manuscript paper, so I wrote about the cat we kept at home. About the way that old female cat and I related to each other, lived together, and communicated our feelings to each other—within limits, of course. There was plenty I could say about that cat. She was very intelligent and full of character. Probably there were a few cat lovers among the judges. Most cat lovers naturally feel affection and sympathy for other cat lovers.

You wrote about your maternal grandmother. About the emotional bond between a lonely old woman and a lonely girl. About the small, unfeigned sense of values that took shape there. It was a charming, moving essay—several times better than anything I had written. I tell you so frankly. I can’t understand why mine placed third and yours fourth. Smiling, you say the opposite is true: that what I wrote was several times better than what you wrote. Really, you add. I’m not lying.

“Your cat sounds wonderful,” you say.

“Yeah, she’s really smart,” I say.

You smile.

“Do you have a cat?” I ask.

You shake your head. “My sister is allergic to cat hair.”

That was the first small piece of personal information I learned about you: your sister was allergic to cat hair.

You are a very beautiful girl. At least that is how you appear to me. Small, with a rather round face, and long, lovely fingers. Your hair is short, and your neatly trimmed black bangs fall across your forehead like carefully chosen shading. Your nose is straight and small, and your eyes are very large. By ordinary standards of facial beauty, one might say that the balance between your nose and eyes is off, but for some reason I am drawn to precisely that lack of symmetry. Your pale pink lips are small and thin and always dutifully closed, as though they were hiding several important secrets inside.

The five prizewinners go up onto the stage one by one and are solemnly presented with certificates and commemorative medals. The tall girl who won first prize gives a brief speech of acceptance. The extra prize is a fountain pen as well—the fountain-pen company was sponsoring the contest. I would go on using that pen for many years. A little before the end of the long, boring ceremony, I write my name and address in ballpoint pen on one of the memo pages in my notebook, tear it out, and secretly hand it to you.

“If you’d like, maybe you could write me a letter sometime,” I say in a dry voice.

I don’t ordinarily do things that bold. I am shy by nature—and of course a coward too. But thinking that we might part there and never see each other again makes that seem terribly wrong, utterly unfair. So I gather up my courage and take a reckless step.

You take the slip of paper with a slightly surprised look, fold it neatly into quarters, and tuck it into the breast pocket of your blazer, above the gentle, mysterious swell of your chest. Then you touch your bangs and blush a little.

“I want to read more of what you write,” I say, like someone making a clumsy excuse after opening the door to the wrong room.

“I would very much like to read a letter from you too,” you say, giving a few small nods, as if encouraging me.

Your letter reaches me a week later. It is a wonderful letter. I read it over at least twenty times. Then I sit down at my desk and, using the brand-new fountain pen I have just won as a prize, write a long reply. That is how we begin writing to each other, and how our private relationship begins.

Were we lovers? Is it all right to call what we had by so simple a name? I don’t know. But for at least that period, for nearly a year, our hearts were joined in a pure, unalloyed way. And before long we created and came to share a special secret world belonging to us alone—a strange town surrounded by a high wall.

It was at dusk on my third day in the town that I pushed open the door of that building.

It was an old stone building with no distinguishing features. It stood a little way east along the riverside road, beyond the central square facing the Old Bridge. There was nothing posted at the entrance, so that anyone who did not already know would not realize it was a library. There was only a brass plate, curtly affixed, engraved with the number 16. The plate had tarnished, and the numerals were hard to read.

The heavy wooden door opened inward with a deep creak, and beyond it was a dim square room. No one was there. The ceiling was high, the light from the wall lamps was poor, and the air smelled of someone’s dried sweat. It was the kind of dimness in which everything seemed faintly veiled, about to break down into molecules and be drawn away somewhere. The worn cedar floorboards gave off sharp sounds here and there beneath my feet. There were two tall narrow windows, and not a single piece of furniture.

At the far end of the room was a door. A plain wooden door, with a small pane of frosted glass at about face level, and there too the number 16 was written in an old-fashioned decorative script. A faint light showed through the glass. I knocked lightly twice and waited, but there was no answer. Nor did I hear footsteps. After pausing a moment to steady my breathing, I turned the tarnished brass knob and gently pushed the door open. It creaked, as if warning the surroundings: Someone has come.

Beyond it was another room, also perfectly square, perhaps five meters on a side. The ceiling was not as high as in the first room. Here too there was no one. There were no windows at all; plaster walls enclosed the room on every side. No paintings, no photographs, no posters, no calendar, and of course no clock—only blank naked walls. There was a crude wooden bench, two small chairs, a table, and a wooden coat rack. No coat hung from it. In the middle of the room stood an old-fashioned rust-speckled woodstove, burning brightly, with a large black kettle on top of it giving off steam. At the far end there was what seemed to be a lending counter, and on it a ledger lay open, as if someone had been in the middle of working and then had suddenly been called away by some urgent business. Presumably that someone—presumably the librarian—would return to the room before long.

Behind the counter was a dark-colored door that seemed to lead to the stacks. If so, then this really was a “library.” Not a single book was in sight, and yet there remained in the place something unmistakably library-like: that special air possessed by libraries everywhere in the world, large or small, old or new.

I took off my heavy coat and hung it on the coat rack, sat down on the hard wooden bench, and warmed my hands by the stove while I waited for someone to appear. There was no sound at all. A silence like the bottom of deep water. Once, experimentally, I cleared my throat, but it did not sound like throat-clearing.

It was some fifteen minutes later—probably about that long, though without a clock I couldn’t know for certain—that you opened the door leading to the stacks and emerged. Seeing me sitting on the bench, you gave a little start, your body tensing for an instant, your eyes opening wide. Then you let out one slow breath and said, “I’m sorry to have kept you waiting. I had no idea anyone had come.”

I could not find the words I ought to say, and merely nodded several times in silence. Your voice did not sound like your voice. It was different from the voice I remembered. Or perhaps in that room every sound and every voice resonated differently from the way they ordinarily did.

At that moment the lid of the kettle suddenly rattled and gave a small shiver, like an animal waking.

“And what is it you require?” you ask.

What I seek is 〈Old Dreams〉.

“〈Old Dreams〉,” you say, drawing your small thin lips into a straight line as you look at me. Of course you do not remember me.

“But as you know,” you say, “only 〈Dream Readers〉 are permitted to touch 〈Old Dreams〉.”

Without a word, I remove my dark green glasses and lift my eyelids for you to see. There is no mistaking them: the eyes of a 〈Dream Reader〉. Eyes that cannot go out into the dazzling light of day.

“I understand. You possess the necessary qualifications,” you say, lowering your gaze slightly.

Perhaps something in the state of my eyes has unsettled you. But it cannot be helped. In order to enter this town, I had to alter them into just that condition.

“Will you begin your work today?” you ask.

I nod. “I still don’t know whether I’ll be able to read well, but I have to get used to it, little by little.”

Once again there is not a sound in the room. The kettle too has fallen silent. After excusing yourself, you quickly finish the ledger work you had left half done. I watch you from the bench. Outwardly, you have not changed in the least. You are exactly as you were on that summer evening. I remember the vivid red sandals you wore. And the grasshopper that suddenly sprang up from the nearby grass.

“I wonder if we’ve met somewhere before,” I find myself asking, though I know it is a pointless question.

You lift your eyes from the ledger and gaze at my face for a moment, still holding the pencil in your left hand—yes, you are left-handed, both in this town and in the town that is not here—and then you shake your head.

“No, I don’t believe we have,” you reply. You speak in such polite language perhaps because, though you are still sixteen, I am no longer seventeen. To you I am now a man much older than you. It cannot be helped, yet the passage of time pierces my heart.

When you finish the record-keeping you had been working on, you close the ledger and put it away on the shelf behind you, then prepare an herbal tea for me. You take the kettle from the stove and carefully mix its hot water with crushed herbs to make a dark green tea. Then you pour it into a large earthenware cup and set it before me. It is a special drink provided for the 〈Dream Reader〉, and preparing it is one of your duties.

I take my time drinking the herbal tea. It has a thick, peculiar bitterness and is by no means easy to drink. But its nourishment heals my eyes, still damaged, and calms my heart. That is what the drink is for. You watch me from the far side of the table. Perhaps you are worried whether I like the herbal tea you have made. I give you a small nod, as if to say, It’s fine. At that, a smile of relief appears at your lips. It is a familiar smile. I have not seen it in a very long time.

The room is warm and quiet. Even without a clock, time passes in silence, like a slender cat walking along the top of a wall with soundless steps.

We did not exchange letters all that frequently. Roughly once every two weeks. But each one tended to be quite long. And on the whole, I think the letters you wrote were somewhat longer than mine. Not that the length of our letters had any particular significance in our exchange.

I still have every last letter you wrote me, but since I never kept copies of my own, I can no longer remember clearly what I wrote in them. Still, I’m sure it can’t have been anything so important. Mostly I wrote about daily life and the small things that happened around me. I wrote about books I had read, music I had listened to, and films I had seen. I also wrote about things that happened at school. I was on the swim team—only because circumstances left me no choice, and I could hardly be called an enthusiastic athlete—but I think I wrote about practice and the like as well. When I was writing to you, words came naturally, whatever the subject. I could say what I thought and felt almost uncannily just as I wished. It was the first time in my life that I had ever written with such fluency. As I said before, until then I had always believed I was bad at writing. You must have drawn that ability skillfully out of some deep place in me. You always delighted in the little touches of humor in what I wrote. “That’s probably what my life lacks most,” you said.

“Like some kind of vitamin?” I said.

“Yes. Like some kind of vitamin,” you said, nodding emphatically.

I was infatuated with you, and I think that whenever I was awake I was almost always thinking about you. Probably in my dreams as well. But in my letters I tried to restrain myself as much as possible from confessing those feelings directly. I had decided to write, as far as I could, only about practical and concrete things. At the time, perhaps I wanted to cling to the world I could actually touch with my own hands—with a certain amount of humor mixed in, if possible. Because if I began writing directly about love and romance, about inner movements of the heart, so to speak, I felt I would only drive myself farther and farther into a blind alley.

Your letters, by contrast, contained less about concrete matters around you than about inward feelings of one kind or another. Or dreams you had seen, or short pieces of fiction. Several of those dreams in particular have remained vividly with me. You frequently had long dreams, and you could remember them clearly down to the smallest detail, as though you were recalling things that had actually happened. That was hard for me to believe. I myself hardly ever dreamed, and when I did, I could almost never remember the content. The moment I woke in the morning, my dreams came apart completely and were sucked away somewhere. Even if a vivid dream startled me awake in the middle of the night—which was rare—I would soon fall back asleep, and by the next morning I would remember nothing at all.

When I said that, you replied:

“In my case, I keep a notebook and pencil by my pillow, and the moment I wake up I record the dreams I had that night. Even when I’m busy and short of time. Especially if I’ve had a particularly vivid dream and woken in the middle of the night—no matter how sleepy I am, I write down as much of it as I can, right then and there. Dreams like that are often important, and they teach me many important things.”

“Many important things?” I asked.

“Things about me that I myself don’t know,” you answered.

For you, dreams existed on almost the same level as events that actually took place in the real world, and they were not things that could easily be forgotten or vanish without a trace. They were like a precious spring of the heart, telling you many things.

“That’s the fruit of practice. If you try, I’m sure you too will become able to remember your dreams in detail. So try it. I very much want to know what kind of dreams you have.”

“All right,” I said. “I’ll try.”

And I did make some effort, in my own way—though not so far as keeping a notebook and pencil by my pillow—but I simply could not work up any interest in the dreams I had. My dreams were too scattered and incoherent, and for the most part impossible to understand. The words spoken in them were indistinct, and the scenes I saw had almost no semblance of a plot. Sometimes they contained disturbing things I could never have told another person. Rather than involve myself with such things, I preferred to listen closely to your long, colorful dream stories.

From time to time, I would appear in your dreams. Hearing that made me very happy. In whatever form, I had been able to take part in the imagined world inside you. And you too seemed pleased that I had appeared in your dreams. In most cases, though, the me in your dreams bore no especially important meaning; I played only a supporting role, like a secondary character in a drama.

Did you never have the kind of explicit dreams that would be hard to tell me about—the kind I myself often had, the kind that sometimes, despite myself, left my underwear soiled? Were you really telling me honestly about every dream you had? Listening to you talk about your dreams, that was something I always found myself wondering.

You seemed to speak frankly, holding nothing back. But what was really true, no one could know. It seems to me that there is no one in this world who does not carry secrets in the heart. That is something necessary for people to survive in this world.

Isn’t it?

“If anything perfect exists in this world, it is this wall. No one can get over this wall. No one can break this wall,” the gatekeeper declared.

At first glance the wall looked like nothing more than an old brick enclosure. It seemed as though the next strong storm or earthquake would bring it down with ease. How could such a thing be called perfect? When I said that, the gatekeeper looked like a man hearing his family spoken ill of for no reason. Then he seized me by the elbow and led me up to the wall.

“Take a close look. There’s no mortar between the bricks, is there? And every single brick is shaped a little differently. Yet every one of them fits together so snugly there isn’t room for even a hair between them.”

He was right.

“Try scratching the brick with this knife,” the gatekeeper said, taking a work knife from his jacket pocket, snapping the blade open with a click, and handing it to me. It looked old at first glance, but the blade had been sharpened with meticulous care. “It won’t leave so much as a mark.”

He was right about that too. The tip of the blade made only a dry scraping sound. Not even a single white line appeared on the brick.

“You see now? Storms, earthquakes, cannons—nothing can bring this wall down. Nothing can damage it. Nothing ever has, and nothing ever will.”

Keeping his palm pressed to the wall as if posing for a commemorative photograph, he drew in his chin and looked at me proudly.

No, I murmured to myself, nothing perfect exists in this world. Anything that has a form of some kind must have, somewhere, a weak point or a blind spot. But I did not say it aloud.

“Who built this wall?” I asked.

“Nobody built it,” was the gatekeeper’s unshakable opinion. “It was here from the beginning.”

By the end of the first week, I had taken up several 〈Old Dreams〉 that you selected for me and tried to read them. But those old dreams told me nothing meaningful at all. What I heard were only vague, rustling murmurs, and what I saw were nothing more than scattered images out of focus. It was like being made to listen to a tape spliced together at random from fragments, or to watch film running backward.

In the library stacks, instead of books, countless old dreams are lined up. It seems no one has touched them for many long years, and each is covered with a faint veil of white dust. 〈Old Dreams〉 are shaped like eggs, and each differs in size and color. They resemble eggs laid by various animals. But strictly speaking, they are not quite egg-shaped. When one takes one in hand and looks at it closely, one sees that the lower half bulges more than the upper. The balance of weight is uneven too. Yet that very asymmetry gives them stability, and even without support they do not roll off the shelf.

Their surfaces are hard as marble and smooth to the touch. Yet they do not possess marble’s weight. I do not know what material they are made from, nor how strong they are. If one were dropped on the floor, would it break? In any case, they have to be handled with the greatest care, as one would handle the egg of some rare living creature.

There is not a single book in the library—not one. Once, I imagine, books must have stood in long rows there, and the people of the town must have visited in search of knowledge and pleasure, as in any ordinary town library. A trace of that atmosphere still lingers faintly in the air like a remnant scent. But at some point all the books were removed from the shelves, and old dreams were lined up in their place.

There seems to be no 〈Dream Reader〉 here besides me. At least for now, I appear to be the only 〈Dream Reader〉 in this town. Was there another before me? Perhaps there was. Given how minutely the rules and procedures concerning 〈Dream Readers〉 have been devised and maintained, there probably was.

Your duties in the library are to guard the old dreams lined up there and manage them properly. You choose which dream is to be read and record in the ledger that it has been read. Before evening, you open the library doors, light the lamps, and, in the cold season, light the stove. You make sure that the rapeseed oil and firewood do not run out. And for the 〈Dream Reader〉—that is, for me—you prepare a dark green herbal tea. It heals my eyes and calms my heart.

With a large white cloth, you carefully wipe the white dust from an old dream and place it on the desk before me. I remove my green glasses and lay both hands on the surface of the old dream, enclosing it in my palms. After about five minutes, the old dream gradually wakes from its deep sleep, and its surface begins to glow faintly. A pleasant, natural warmth is transmitted to my palms. Then they begin to spin the dream—hesitantly at first, like a cocoon letting out silk, then with due fervor. They have something to tell. They must have been waiting patiently on the shelf for the time when they could emerge from their shells.

But their voices are too faint, and I cannot fully make out their words. The images they project never attain sufficient clarity; they simply fade, crumble, and are drawn away into the air. Or perhaps it is not their fault. Perhaps my new eyes still are not functioning properly. Perhaps my powers of comprehension as a 〈Dream Reader〉 are not yet in place.

And then the time comes when the library ought to be closed. There is no clock anywhere, but when that hour approaches, you somehow know.

“How is it? Is your work going well?”

“A little at a time,” I reply. “But reading even one leaves me terribly exhausted. Maybe I’m doing it wrong somehow.”

“There’s no need to worry,” you say, turning a knob to close the stove’s air vent. Then you blow out the lamps one by one, sit down across the table from me, and say, looking me straight in the face—when you look at me that directly, I become flustered—“There’s no need to hurry. There is all the time in the world here.”

Following the prescribed procedure exactly, step by step, you close the library. Your expression is serious, but you are unhurried and perfectly composed. As far as I can tell, the order of those tasks never changes. Watching you, I find myself wondering whether it is really necessary to lock up this library so meticulously. In this quiet, gentle town, who in the world would break into the library at night to steal or destroy old dreams?

“Would it be all right if I walked you home?” On the third night, when we stepped outside the building, I finally summoned the courage to ask.

You turned around and looked at my face, your eyes wide. In those black pupils, a single star from the sky was reflected white. You seemed unable quite to understand the meaning of what I had offered. Why should I need to be escorted home by you?
