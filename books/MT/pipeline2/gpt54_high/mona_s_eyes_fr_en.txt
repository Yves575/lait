Prologue


Seeing Nothing Anymore


Everything went dark. It was like mourning clothes. Then, here and there, little scintillations, like the spots the sun makes when you try in vain to look at it through tightly shut eyelids, just as you clench a fist to resist pain or emotion.

Of course, that was not at all how she had described it. On the lips of a fresh, anxious ten-year-old, distress comes out bluntly, without flourish or lyricism.

“Mom, it’s all black!”

Mona had uttered the words in a strangled voice. A complaint? Yes, but not only that. Despite herself, she had let a note of shame slip into it, and whenever her mother heard it, she took it gravely. For if there was one thing Mona never feigned, it was shame. The instant it lodged in a word, a posture, an intonation, the die was cast: an unpleasant truth had got in.

“Mom, it’s all black!”

Mona was blind.

The effect seemed to have no cause. Nothing in particular had happened; she had been dutifully doing her math, a pen in her right hand, an exercise book pinned beneath her left palm, at the corner of the table where her mother was studding a nice fatty roast with garlic. Mona was delicately lifting from her neck a pendant that bothered her as it swung over her worksheet—she had got into the bad habit of hunching over to write. She felt a heavy shadow fall across both her eyes, as if they were being punished for being so blue, so large, so pure. The shadow had not come from outside, as shadows ordinarily do, when night falls or the lights in a theater go down; it had seized her sight from inside her own body, from within. Somewhere inside her, an opaque film had spread, cutting her off from the polygons drawn in her school notebook, the brown wooden table, the roast set farther down, her mother in a white apron, the tiled kitchen, her father sitting in the next room, the apartment in Montreuil, the autumn sky gone gray above the streets, the whole world. By some spell, the child was plunging into darkness.

In a fever, Mona’s mother phoned the family doctor. Confusedly she described her daughter’s veiled pupils and specified, because the doctor asked her to, that she did not seem to have any trouble speaking or moving.

“It sounds like a TIA,” he said, without wishing to commit himself further.

He ordered large doses of aspirin immediately and, above all, Mona’s urgent transfer to the Hôtel-Dieu hospital, where he would call a colleague to take charge of her at once. He had not thought of him by chance: he was a marvelous pediatrician, also reputed to be a very good ophthalmologist and, incidentally, a gifted hypnotherapist. Normally, he concluded, the blindness should not last more than ten minutes, and he hung up. A good quarter of an hour had already passed since the first cry of alarm.

In the car, the little girl was crying and beating at her temples. Her mother held her by the elbows, but deep down she too would have liked to pound that small, round, fragile head, the way you hit a broken machine in the stupid hope of making it start again. At the wheel of his jolting old Volkswagen, her father wished he could take on whatever affliction his little girl had fallen victim to. He was angry, convinced that something had happened in the kitchen and that it was being hidden from him. He kept turning over the inventory of all possible causes, from a burst of steam to a bad fall. But no. Mona said it a hundred times:

“It came on all by itself!”

And her father would not believe it:

“You don’t just go blind like that!”

And yet yes, one does go blind “just like that”: there was the proof. And that “one,” today, was Mona, ten years old, with frightened tears streaming down her face—tears from which perhaps she hoped the soot sticking to her pupils would be washed away, that Sunday in October, as evening fell. Barely had she reached the threshold of the hospital beside Notre-Dame, on the Île de la Cité, when she abruptly stopped sobbing and froze.

“Mom, Dad, it’s coming back!”

Standing in the street, where a cold wind was blowing, she rocked her head back and forth to help her sight return. Like a blind being raised, the veil obstructing her eyes lifted. First lines reappeared, then the angles of faces, the contours of nearby objects, the texture of the walls, and every shade of color, from the brightest to the darkest. The child recovered the slight silhouette of her mother, her long swan’s neck and frail arms, and then the bulkier one of her father. Finally, far off, she made out a gray pigeon taking flight, and that filled her with joy. Blindness had seized Mona, then let her go. It had passed through her, the way a bullet tears through skin and comes out the other side of the body, causing pain, certainly, but still leaving the organism to heal. “A miracle,” thought her father, who meticulously timed how long the attack had lasted: sixty-three minutes.

In the ophthalmology department at the Hôtel-Dieu, there was no question of letting the little girl go home before they had run a battery of tests, reached a diagnosis, and written prescriptions. The anguish had been postponed, certainly, but not entirely dispelled. A nurse pointed them toward a room on the first floor of the building. It was the office of the pediatrician alerted by the family doctor. Dr. Van Orst was mixed-race and prematurely bald. His large, radiant white coat stood out against the sickly green of the walls. His immense smile, which dug little jovial creases into his face, made him likable; he was no less the repository of many tragedies. He came forward.

“How old are you?” he asked in a voice roughened by tobacco.

Mona was ten. She was the only child of two parents who loved each other. Her mother, Camille, was nearing forty. She was short, wore her hair cropped and tousled, and still had in her voice a faint trace of streetwise cheek. Her charm lay in what her man called her “slightly ramshackle” side, though it was backed by a fearsome determination: in her, anarchy was forever braided with authority. She worked at a temp agency, the model employee, committed and conscientious and zealous. In the mornings, at least. The afternoons were another matter. She wore herself out doing volunteer work. Every cause seemed worth taking up, from lonely old people to beaten animals.

As for Paul, he was past fifty-seven. Camille was his second wife. The first had disappeared with his best friend. He wore a tie to distract from the frayed collars of his shirts and scraped a living as a small-time dealer in bric-a-brac, with a particular passion for 1950s American culture: jukeboxes, pinball machines, posters... And because it had all begun in adolescence with a collection of heart-shaped key rings, he owned an impressive array of them, which he had no wish to sell and which, in any case, would have interested no one. With the rise of the internet, his shop, tucked away in Montreuil, had very nearly closed for good. So he had got online himself, putting his expertise to use with a website he was forever updating and translating into English. Despite a business sense close to nil, he could count on a clientele of attentive collectors who regularly saved him from ruin. The previous summer he had repaired a 1955 Gottlieb Wishing Well pinball machine and made the tidy sum of ten thousand euros on it. A lifesaving transaction, after months of lean times... And then, once again, nothing. The crisis, people told him. Each day at the shop Paul knocked back a bottle of red wine, then planted the empty on one of those hedgehog-shaped bottle dryers made famous by Marcel Duchamp, like a trophy. He raised his glass alone, unable to find it in himself to blame anyone. In his head, he drank to Mona. To her health.

While a nurse led the child through the hospital maze for various tests, Dr. Van Orst, settled deep in an enormous armchair, gave Paul and Camille an initial diagnosis.

“TIA, or transient ischemic attack.”

It meant that the blood had temporarily stopped irrigating the organs, and that they now had to detect the reason for the malfunction. But Mona’s case, he went on, baffled him: on the one hand, the attack, exceedingly rare in a girl of her age, struck him as violent, since both eyes had been affected and the episode had lasted more than an hour; on the other, it had entirely spared her ability to move and speak. The MRI would no doubt tell them more. They had to prepare for the worst, he added awkwardly.

Mona had to lie down on a platform inside a horrible machine and yield herself to it docilely, without moving. They asked her to take off her pendant. She refused. It was a fine chain of fishing line strung with a tiny shell that had belonged to her grandmother and brought her luck. She had always worn it, and her adored “Dadé” wore the same one on his side. The two fetishes connected them to each other, she thought, and she did not want to feel torn away from her grandfather. Since the pendant contained no metal, they let her keep it. Then her head—her pretty head haloed by shoulder-length brown hair with tawny highlights and adorned with an adorable round mouth—was enclosed in an ogreish casing resounding with factory noise. For the fifteen minutes the torture lasted, Mona sang to herself without pause, to hold out, to inject a little cheer and life into that coffin. She sang herself a slightly mawkish lullaby her mother used to hum when tucking her in, back in the old days; she sang herself a pop song, one of those tunes that looped in supermarkets and whose video, full of slick-haired boys, she liked; she sang herself nagging ad jingles; she also sang herself “Une souris verte,” remembering that day she had shouted the words at the top of her lungs in hopes of annoying her father, without success.

The MRI results came in. Dr. Van Orst called Camille and Paul in and hurried to reassure them. There was nothing. Strictly nothing. On the cross-sectional images, the anatomy of the brain showed only homogeneous areas. No tumor on that front. Other tests followed, endlessly. All through the night: from the depths of the pupils to the inner ear, by way of the blood, bones, muscles, arteries. Nothing there either. Calm after the storm. Had there ever been a storm?

The face of a clock lost in one of the Hôtel-Dieu corridors showed five in the morning. An image from a nursery rhyme came to Camille’s mind: it was as if, she confided to her exhausted husband, some malevolent being had stolen both of Mona’s eyes before giving them back. As if it had mistaken its victim, Paul added. Or as if it had sent a sign, a warning, and was preparing to repeat the deed, both of them thought in silence.

In the schoolyard, the bell rang. The stream of children, marshaled by Mrs. Hadji, made its way to the second floor. The teacher informed her CM2 class that they would not be seeing Mona, their classmate, again until school resumed after the All Saints’ break. Camille herself had just called to tell her, and over the phone she had explained everything, or almost everything, about the infernal night, without concealing the seriousness of what had happened. Naturally, the children asked questions. Was Mona really allowed to go on vacation a week earlier than everyone else?

“She’s a little ill,” the teacher said, settling for the phrase without being entirely satisfied by it herself.

“A little ill—lucky her!” exclaimed Diego, in the third row, and his shrill voice won the assent of the whole class.

For most children imagine illness as an open sesame to freedom...

At the back of the room, right beside the curtains powdered with chalk, Lili and Jade, Mona’s two best friends, who knew every corner of her bedroom, were drooling even more. Ah, how they would have loved to be with her! “A little ill”? All right, thought Lili, but surely she would be spending her days in her father’s bric-a-brac shop. And Jade, her eyes turned toward the empty place Mona had left, delightedly pictured herself with her, inventing all sorts of games and stories in that modest little shop full of old objects that smelled of America—a whole jumble of shimmering, amusing, mysterious things that made children dream. But Lili objected:

“No, no, when she’s sick it’s her Grandpa Dadé who comes and looks after her, and he scares me.”

Jade forced a scornful laugh to show that she, at least, was afraid of nothing, and certainly not Mona’s grandfather. Yet deep down the little girl admitted it: yes, faced with that old man—huge, gaunt, scarred, speaking in a deep, mineral voice—she wasn’t exactly fearless either...

“Hello, Dad, it’s me.”

It was noon when Camille, her limbs numb, finally decided to call her father. Henry Vuillemin refused to use a cell phone; he invariably answered calls on his landline with a dry, searing little “Yes?” that left scant room for enthusiasm. His daughter hated the ritual and each time regretted the days when, still alive, her mother used to pick up the receiver. She let the syllables fall one by one.

“Dad, I have to tell you: something terrible happened last night.”

She told him everything, in order, trying to control her emotion.

“Well?” Henry asked, with a hint of impatience.

But Camille had held back her tears for so long while telling the story that a huge sob rose through her body and choked her: she was unable to answer.

“Darling, well?” her father pressed.

That unexpected darling gave her a rush of oxygen; she caught her breath and said:

“Nothing! For the moment, nothing. I think she’s all right.”

Then Henry exhaled a long sigh of relief, threw back his head, and gazed up at the moldings on his ceiling, at the cheerful patterns of plump fruits, scrolls, and spring flowers.

“Let me say a word to her.”

But Mona, curled up in an armchair in the living room beneath a russet throw, had fallen asleep.

The poet Ovid described the phase in which consciousness falls asleep as an entry into an immense cave sheltering, languid and indolent, the god Sleep. He imagined a cavern beyond the reach of Phoebus, lord of the Sun. Mona had learned from her grandfather that, on the human scale, no journey is more regular than the one toward those mysterious, ever-changing lands... Those were territories, then, that ought not to be neglected, since we travel through them ceaselessly, all our lives.

In the days that followed, Dr. Van Orst, at the Hôtel-Dieu, ordered new tests. They still revealed no particular abnormality. The explanation for those sixty-three minutes of blindness stubbornly refused to appear, to such a degree that the doctor was now reluctant to use the term “transient ischemic attack,” which implied a vascular deficiency of which he was no longer entirely sure. In the absence of a clear diagnosis, he suggested to Mona—and to her parents—that they try hypnosis. The idea was met by mute astonishment from Paul. As for the little girl, she was not at all sure she knew what it meant. She associated the word with the choking game she had vaguely heard about at school, which terrified her. To correct that misunderstanding, Van Orst explained that by putting Mona into a hypnotic state he could temporarily bring her under his influence. Such an experience would allow him to travel back in time and guide her to that original moment when her sight had gone out, make her relive it and potentially identify the cause. Paul protested. Out of the question; it was dangerous. Van Orst did not insist: for a child to be effectively hypnotized, she must surrender in complete trust. But between Mona’s preconception and her father’s angry overreaction, the ground was now mined. Camille, for her part, had said nothing.

So Van Orst prescribed a standard medical regimen for the young patient: weekly blood and blood-pressure checks, visits to the ophthalmologist, and ten days of convalescence. He ordered Paul and Camille to watch for “any appearance of subjective signs of a symptomatic nature,” which meant they were to pay extremely close attention to their daughter’s perceptions. In that connection, he suggested consulting a child psychiatrist.

“It’s everyday prophylaxis rather than therapy properly speaking,” he assured them.

Paul and Camille took in his recommendations only confusedly, but really there was only one question preoccupying them: “Is Mona eventually in danger of losing her sight?” Strangely, Dr. Van Orst never once mentioned the threat of a definitive relapse, and despite the dread they felt at the thought, the parents preferred to avoid the subject. They even told themselves that if the doctor was skirting it, then perhaps it did not need to be raised.

Henry Vuillemin raised it head-on with his daughter. He was not the kind to shy away from questions, even when they were abysses. Usually stingy with telephone calls, except to hear Mona, he multiplied them that week. In a warm, passionate voice, he harried Camille: yes or no, was his cherished granddaughter, the treasure of his life, going to become blind? Henry also insisted on seeing Mona, and Camille could not decently refuse him. She suggested he come on the Sunday of All Saints’ Day, exactly one week after the attack of blindness. Paul, who could guess what the conversation was about, secretly resigned himself and almost tossed back a glass of harsh burgundy in one gulp. In front of his father-in-law, he felt miserably stupid. Mona, on the other hand, stamped with impatience when she heard the news.

This grandfather, full of years and strength, she loved. She also adored observing how everyone who met him let themselves be charmed by his endless silhouette and his heavy glasses with their large, almost square frames. In his company, she felt protected. And transported. Henry had always made a point of speaking to her as he would have spoken to an adult. It was she who demanded that imbalance and savored it, amused by it. She was never afraid of not understanding, and she laughed at mistakes and misunderstandings. For she, in turn, monitored her own language, and it was obviously more a game than anything serious.

Henry had no desire to make of her a little performing monkey. He did not want to be one of those parody grandfathers who hunt down the faults of youth only to correct them in a pedantic voice. It was not in his nature. He had never made her do her homework, never meddled in her report cards. Besides, he adored the way Mona expressed herself. More than that, he was fascinated by her turns of phrase. Why? There he had no key. He failed to understand it. Ever since the beginning, he had been obsessed, haunted, by something in her child’s language. Was it some extra element or some je-ne-sais-quoi missing? A quality or a flaw? The feeling was all the more troubling because it was not new: Mona’s “little music” had always carried an enigma Henry was determined one day to uncover, by dint of listening.

Camille sometimes admitted her astonishment at a relationship she deemed “too good to be true,” but she recognized that it worked wonderfully and that her daughter was happy in it. And Henry, who readily quoted Victor Hugo’s *The Art of Being a Grandfather*, reminded anyone who would listen of one of the cardinal principles of transmission: it matters little if we do not immediately understand everything someone says, as though every new word had to be already a flourishing tree in the immense orchard of the brain. Blossoming would come in due time, provided furrows had been drawn and seeds planted.

What those furrows and seeds meant, in Henry Vuillemin’s case, was a flow of rich, assured words that caught hold of you from the first intonation and never let go; it was speech at once very simple and of such breadth that it became exhilarating; it was a storyteller’s cadence that often quickened, before slowing and taking on the color of gentle feeling. It was a steamroller of worldly experience and tranquil learning.

And so, with this “Dadé,” the bond was of a different kind. From grandparents to grandchildren, from grandchildren to grandparents, there sometimes comes into being a miraculous tie, owing to the fact that, by a sort of existential curve, the old return, from the height of their years, to the feelings of their earliest youth and grasp, better than anyone, the springtime of life.

Henry Vuillemin lived in a handsome apartment on Avenue Ledru-Rollin, just above Le Bistrot du Peintre, a narrow wood-paneled establishment imitating the Art Nouveau style. Every morning he went down there and kept to his habits: a coffee and a croissant, the national papers, a word here and there with whoever happened to be around—the ordinary run of customers and the waiters on break. He felt himself to belong to an old world and, ritually, walked at a very slow pace to Place de la Bastille, liked studying the furniture in the windows along Rue du Faubourg-Saint-Antoine, headed back toward Place de la République along the median of Boulevard Richard-Lenoir, then cut across to Boulevard Voltaire. Late in the afternoon, at home, he pored over the art books piled to the ceiling. Henry, who stood one centimeter taller than General de Gaulle, could reach the most inaccessible of them without a step stool or ladder, and by a curious chance those were regularly the ones that attracted him most. His memory was prodigious, though one had to distinguish between his readiness to talk about what he knew and his personal memories, which he protected beneath layers of reserve. Mona knew the rule. The only forbidden thing with her grandfather was to mention Colette Vuillemin, who had left him a widower seven years earlier. In tune with her father, Camille never said anything about her either. The child might try now and again to open a breach, but she always ran up against a leaden silence. Colette was not spoken of. Ever. The sole exception to this taboo was the fetish Henry wore around his neck in homage to his dead wife. It was a pretty little cerith shell threaded on fishing line, which he had picked up with her on the Côte d’Azur in the summer of 1963—he no longer knew exactly which day, but remembered the blazing heat and having sworn many things to Colette. Mona, as has been said, wore the same pendant, inherited from her grandmother.

We all have our own ways of swearing. Henry Vuillemin swore by “all that is beautiful on earth.” The phrase puzzled Mona, and whenever she heard it she invariably gave a little shrug with a confused laugh: all that is beautiful on earth was a bit everything and a bit nothing. Besides, she wondered whether he himself—her revered grandfather—belonged to it. Henry had obviously been an attractive young man and remained imposing, charming, spectacular. His octogenarian face, gaunt and sharp, radiated a vigor and an intelligence of formidable allure. But he was scarred. A scar slashed the right side of his face, beginning beneath the cheekbone and running up to the eyebrow. The wound must have been terribly painful. It had torn away not only a strip of skin but a piece of the cornea. It was a war wound. A horrible one: on September 17, 1982, during a photo assignment in Lebanon for Agence France-Presse, a Phalangist had knifed him to keep him from going any farther. He was approaching the Shatila camp. Rumor had it that massacres were taking place there, that Palestinian refugees were being arbitrarily executed without trial, in reprisal for the assassination of President Bachir Gemayel. He had wanted to verify it, to bear witness. His way had been barred with inhuman violence. Henry had lost a great deal of blood and the use of his eye. That infirmity, combined with his height and, as the years passed, an ever more pronounced thinness, had given his appearance a supernatural air. The handsome reporter, who had looked like Eddie Constantine, had become a legendary figure.

On All Saints’ Day, Mona was in good form. Her parents had done their best to make the leaden November atmosphere cheerful. Jade and Lili, the two friends, had come by to watch an installment of *Toy Story*, the animated film in which toys come to life. They had romped around good-naturedly. Jade especially. She was a pretty, impish little girl, with fine Eurasian eyes, olive skin, and hair combed to perfection. Yet she was driven by a surprising passion: making faces. She could transform her harmonious features into a mobile, deranged theater where strange and burlesque expressions passed like actors in a frenzy. Mona, delighted, always wanted more.

At seven o’clock the intercom buzzed. Paul pursed his lips and raised his eyebrows. Camille pressed the button.

“Dad?”

It was indeed him, arriving to the minute. After greeting him, Paul set off to take Jade and Lili back to their respective parents, and for the duration of the trip Mona, her mother, and her grandfather found themselves alone together in the apartment. After an irrepressible outpouring of joy, the little girl, who had kept her mishap to herself in front of her two friends, launched into a detailed account of her sixty-three minutes of torment and the ordeals she had endured at the hospital. Camille did not interrupt her.

And Henry, while listening to Mona talk and talk on, examined with wholly clinical detachment the places where the child lived. Even her room, despite the enchanting kitsch of the decor, struck him as deeply sad. That wallpaper with its garlands of flowers, those glitter-encrusted trinkets shaped like hearts or animals, those pink or brown stuffed toys, those grotesque posters of celebrities barely out of adolescence, those plastic jewels, that furniture imitating the furniture of cartoon princesses... The acid-bright colors of all that clutter caught him in the throat. In the whole of that ensemble, dripping with bad taste, there were only two openings onto a form of beauty. A sturdy 1950s American industrial lamp, with an articulated arm, that Paul had found, given to Mona, and attached to her little desk. And then, above the bed, in a frame, an exhibition poster reproducing a painting. It crackled with extraordinarily subtle, cool-toned colors. They drew a nude woman, in profile and leaning forward, seated on a stool covered with white fabric, her left ankle resting on her right knee. In one corner one could read: “Musée d’Orsay Paris – Georges Seurat (1859–1891).”

Despite these exceptions, Henry came to the dispiriting conclusion that the time of childhood is, for convenience’s sake, steeped for the most part in trivial and ugly objects. Mona was no exception to the rule. Beauty, real artistic beauty, entered her daily life only clandestinely. That was perfectly normal, Henry noted: the refinement of taste, the shaping of sensibility, would come later. Except that—and the thought choked him—Mona had very nearly lost her sight, and if her eyes were to go out forever in the days, weeks, or months to come, she would carry with her into the farthest reaches of memory only the recollection of gaudy, vain things. A whole life in the dark, having mentally to compose with the worst the world produces, with no escape for memory? It was impossible. It was terrifying.

To his daughter’s great annoyance, Henry remained taciturn and distant throughout dinner. When at last Mona went off to bed, Camille, with a resolute air, turned up the volume on the Coltrane saxophone playing on an old chrome jukebox, to mask their voices and make sure the little girl would hear nothing.

“Dad. Mona seems, for the moment, to be processing...” She hesitated over the words. “...what’s just happened fairly well. But the doctor recommends follow-up with a child psychiatrist. It may feel strange for her, and I was wondering whether you could take her to the appointment, so she feels reassured...”

“A shrink? Is that really what’s going to stop her from going blind?”

“That’s not the point, Dad!”

“I think it is, and it will be as long as you don’t dare ask the doctor! Doctor what’s-his-name again?”

“His name is Van Orst and he’s very good,” Paul put in awkwardly, trying to take part in the conversation.

“Dad, wait,” Camille resumed. “Listen to me. Paul and I are going to do everything we can so that nothing happens to Mona, do you hear me? But she’s ten years old and we can’t act as if she’s been through nothing. The doctor says her psychological balance is a priority. So I’m only asking you to see whether you’d be willing to take charge of that, because I know Mona will trust you. Do you hear me, Dad?”

Henry heard perfectly well. But at that precise moment, in a split second, an Apollonian idea flashed alight in his mind, and he kept it jealously to himself. He would not take his granddaughter to a child psychiatrist, no... Instead he would put her through a cure of an altogether different kind, one capable of compensating for the ugliness her young life was being fed.

Mona, who trusted him completely, who gave him a credence she granted no other adult, would have to accompany him to where what the world has produced that is most beautiful and most human is kept: she would have to accompany him to museums. If, by some misfortune, Mona were one day to become blind forever, she would at least possess a sort of reservoir deep in her brain, a place from which to draw visual splendors. And so the grandfather had his project... Once a week, according to an unchanging ritual, he would take Mona by the hand and lead her to contemplate one work—one only—first in a long silence, so that the infinite delight of colors and lines might sink into his granddaughter’s mind, and then in words, so that she might move beyond mere visual rapture to understand how artists speak to us about life, and how much light they cast upon it.

For his little Mona, he imagined something better than medicine. They would go first to the Louvre, then to the Musée d’Orsay, and finally to Beaubourg. There, yes, there, in those places devoted to preserving what humanity had produced that was most daring and most beautiful, he would find a tonic for his granddaughter. Henry was not one of those art lovers content, inwardly and apart from the world, with the polished sheen of flesh painted by Raphael or the rhythm of a line marked out by Degas’s charcoals. He loved the almost incendiary qualities of works of art. Sometimes he said, “Art is fireworks or it’s hot air.” And he loved it when, in their entirety or through a single detail, a painting, a sculpture, a photograph could inflame our sense of existence.

As Camille was asking for his help, Henry was assailed by hundreds of images: the rocky masses behind the Mona Lisa, the monkey carved on the back of Michelangelo’s *Dying Slave*, the alarmed expression of the blond-curled child to the right of *The Oath of the Horatii*; the strange gelatinous loins in Goya’s *The Lamb*; then the clods of earth in Rosa Bonheur’s *Ploughing in the Nivernais*; the butterfly-shaped signature Whistler used in the portrait of his mother; the wavering little apse of Van Gogh’s church... Or again Kandinsky’s colors, Picasso’s fractures, Soulages’s outrenoir. All of it burst forth like so many signs calling him, asking to be seen, heard, understood, loved. Like a counterfire against the ashes threatening Mona’s eyes.

Henry broke into a broad smile.

“Agreed. I’ll take Mona with me every Wednesday afternoon. From now on, I—and I alone—will handle her psychological follow-up. It will be our business, the two of us. Is that all right?”

“You’ll find someone good, Dad? You’ll ask your old friends for advice?”

“Do we agree on the principle? I’ll take care of it without questions or interference from anyone.”

“But you’re not going to pick a child psychiatrist at random, do you hear me? You’ll be very careful.”

“Do you trust me, darling?”

“Yes,” said Paul firmly, to head off any hesitation from Camille. “Mona admires and respects you, and she loves you as she loves no one else, so yes, we trust you.”

At her husband’s firm words, Camille nodded gently, adding nothing. Henry felt a slightly moist gleam pass through his good eye. Coltrane’s sax made the walls sway. Mona, in her room, slept, watched over by Georges Seurat.

Part I


LOUVRE

1

Sandro Botticelli


Learn to Receive


The great glass pyramid amused Mona. Set insolently in the middle of the Louvre’s stone pavilions, its ethereal shape, its transparency, the way it caught the cold November sun enchanted her. Her grandfather did not say much. She could see perfectly well, however, that he was in an excellent mood, because he held her little hand in his with the sure tenderness of happy people and swung their arms loosely. His good cheer, though mute, radiated in a childlike way.

“The pyramid is so beautiful, Dadé! It looks like a huge Chinese hat,” Mona pronounced, as she threaded her way through clusters of tourists on the forecourt.

Henry looked at her and let a kind of doubtful little pout mingle with his smile. It gave him such an odd expression that the little girl snickered. They entered the glass structure, passed through security, rode down an escalator, found themselves in the immense concourse so little different from those of train stations or airports, and headed toward the Denon Wing. The bustle around them was stifling. Stifling, yes, because most visitors in the crowd of a great museum have no idea what they want to do; they create a general drift, infusing the air with that stagnant, hesitant, slightly murky atmosphere peculiar to such places when they fall victim to their own success.

In the midst of the hubbub, Henry bent his huge, thin legs to speak to his granddaughter eye to eye. He did that whenever he had something truly important to tell her. His mineral voice, pure and deep, rose above the surrounding din. It seemed to reduce to silence the idle chatter and exhausting clamor of the whole universe.

“Mona, each week the two of us will go and look at one work—one work only, no more—in the museum. These people around us would like to swallow everything at once, and they lose themselves because they do not know how to husband their desires. We will be much wiser, much more reasonable. We will look at a single work, first without saying a word, for long minutes, and afterward we will talk about it.”

“Oh? I thought we were going to the doctor.” She would have liked to say “the child psychiatrist,” but was not entirely sure of the word.

“Tell me, Mona, do you really want to go to the psychiatrist? Is that important to you?”

“As if! Anything rather than that!”

“Then listen to me carefully, my darling. There won’t be any need if you look closely at what we’re going to see.”

“Really? And is it bad not to go to the...” She stumbled over the word again and opted for a simpler phrasing. “...doctor?”

“No, it isn’t bad. I swear it to you by all that is beautiful on earth.”

After threading their way through a maze of staircases, Henry and Mona found themselves in a room of modest dimensions, one with a great deal of foot traffic but where no one, or almost no one, bothered to fasten their eyes on the work enthroned there. Henry let go of his granddaughter’s hand and said with infinite gentleness:

“Now look, Mona. Take all the minutes it takes to look, to really look.”

And Mona planted herself there, intimidated, before a very damaged painting, badly cracked in several places, with pieces missing, a painting that at first glance gave off the feeling of a distant, altered past. Henry was looking at it too, but above all he was studying his granddaughter, sensing her wavering, her perplexity. There she was, frowning, then stifling a slightly embarrassed laugh. He knew that even in front of a Renaissance masterpiece, a ten-year-old girl, however lively, curious, sensitive, and clever she might be, could not fall at once into ecstasy. He knew that, contrary to received opinion, time was needed to penetrate the depth of art, that it was a tedious exercise and not an easy rapture. He also knew that Mona, because it was he asking it of her, would play along and that, despite her embarrassment, she would scrutinize with the promised attention the forms, the colors, the material.

The image was simply composed. All the way to the left a fountain could be made out, before which, like a frieze, four young women with long, curly hair, astonishingly alike, were arranged standing. They held one another by the arm, interlacing as if they formed a human garland whose rhythm came from the variety of their dresses: green and mauve for the first, white for the second, pink for the third, orange-yellow for the fourth. This multicolored procession gave the impression of moving forward and, facing it, to the right of the work, isolated against a neutral background, stood a fifth woman, young, extraordinarily beautiful, wearing a magnificent pendant and a purple dress. She too seemed animated by a forward movement, as if she were going to meet the procession. Indeed, she was extending toward it a sort of cloth into which one of the figures—the one in pink—was delicately placing something. What? Impossible to say. The object had been effaced. There was also, in the foreground, in one corner, a small blond boy, in profile, faintly smiling. The setting was utterly stripped down: echoing the fountain on the left was only a truncated, much-faded column closing the scene on the right.

Mona played along. But after six minutes it was already too much. Six minutes in front of a decrepit image made for an unusual and painful trial. So she turned to her grandfather and began the conversation with an insolence only she could permit herself:

“Dadé, your picture’s really battered! Next to it, your face looks brand-new...”

Henry looked at the work and at all the damage scarring it. He bent his knees.

“You’d do better to listen to me instead of talking nonsense... A ‘picture,’ you say! Wrong. First of all, Mona, this isn’t a ‘picture.’ It’s what’s called a fresco. Do you know what a fresco is?”

“Yes, I think so... but I’ve forgotten!”

“A fresco is a painting done on a wall, and it’s very fragile, because if the wall gets damaged—and walls crumble a great deal over time—well, the painting gets damaged too...”

“Why did the artist paint on this wall? Because it’s the Louvre?”

“Not at all. It’s true that an artist might want to paint a fresco in the Louvre, because the Louvre is the biggest museum on the planet, and it would be perfectly natural for a painter to want to place his work there directly, so that it might become like the skin of the palace. But you see, Mona, the Louvre has not always been a museum. It’s only been one for about two hundred years. Before that, it was a palace, where kings and the court lived. When the artist painted this fresco, it was around 1485. So he did not make it for the walls of the Louvre, but for those of a villa in Florence.”

“Florence?” She absently fingered the pendant around her neck. “That makes me think of the name of one of your old girlfriends, before Grandma, right?”

“Doesn’t ring a bell, but it’s not impossible! Now listen. Florence is an Italian city. In Tuscany, to be precise. And it is the cradle of what we call the Renaissance. In the fifteenth century—the Quattrocento, as the Italians say—Florence experienced an extraordinary effervescence. There were around a hundred thousand inhabitants, and the city was prosperous, thanks to trade and banking. And, you see, religious orders, high political dignitaries, and even private citizens, provided they were high enough on the social ladder, wanted to put their wealth to good use and display their prestige by supporting the creation of their contemporaries. They are known as great patrons. So painters, sculptors, and architects took advantage of their trust and the means they were given to create paintings, statues, and buildings of astonishing beauty.”

“They were made of gold, I bet...”

“Not exactly. In the Middle Ages there were indeed very beautiful paintings lavishly covered in gold leaf. It gave the object value, and it symbolized divine light, into the bargain! But in the Renaissance, painting gradually does without the flashy effect of gilding and seeks instead to render reality better as we see it, with its landscapes, the singularity of faces, animals, the movement of living beings, of things, of the sky and the sea.”

“So people start loving nature, is that it?”

“That is exactly it: people begin to love nature. But you know, when we talk about nature, we aren’t talking only about what grows out of the earth.”

“Oh? And what else are we talking about?”

“We also mean, in a more abstract way, human nature. Human nature is what we are deep down, with our shadows and our light, our faults and our qualities, our fears and our hopes. And, you see, it is precisely that human nature the artist seeks to improve.”

“And how does he do that?”

“If you cultivate your garden, you do nature good. You allow it to flourish. This fresco is trying to do human nature good by telling it something very simple, but essential, something you must remember forever, Mona.”

But Mona, just to provoke the old man, plugged her ears and shut her eyes, as if she wanted neither to hear nor to see anything he might tell her. After a few seconds, she discreetly cracked one eyelid open to observe his reaction. He was smiling nonchalantly. So she gave up her little game and summoned all her attention. For she sensed that her grandfather, after those long minutes of silence, contemplation, and discussion, after that little journey through the damaged image spread before her eyes, was finally about to reveal one of those secrets that stay engraved at the bottom of the heart.

Henry motioned for her to look at the slightly faded area where there seemed to be an object the young woman on the right was taking in her hands. The little girl obeyed.

“The procession of those four women on the left is made up of Venus and the Three Graces. They are generous divinities. And they are offering a gift—we don’t know what, because some of the paint is missing—to a young girl. The Three Graces are what are called allegories, Mona: they do not exist in real life and you will never come across them, but they represent important values. They are said to embody the three stages that make us sociable and hospitable beings, in other words truly human beings. And this fresco says just how crucial those three stages are; it seeks to anchor them in each of us.”

“Three stages? Which stages?”

“The first consists in knowing how to give, the third in knowing how to give back. And between the two there is one without which nothing is possible, one like a keystone, the keystone that supports all human nature.”

“Which one, Dadé?”

“Look: what is the girl on the right doing?”

“You told me: she’s lucky because she’s receiving a gift...”

“Exactly, Mona. She is receiving a gift. And that is what is absolutely fundamental. Knowing how to receive. What this fresco says is that one must learn to receive, that human nature, if it is to be capable of great and beautiful things, must be ready to welcome: welcome the goodwill of others, their desire to give pleasure, welcome what it does not yet have and what it is not yet. There will always be time for the one who receives to give back, but in order to give back—that is, to give in turn—it is indispensable first to have been capable of receiving. Do you understand, Mona?”

“Your story is complicated, but yes, I think I understand...”

“I’m certain you do! And you see, if these ladies are so beautiful, with such supple and graceful drawing, that unbroken line that allows no jolt, no hesitation, it is in order to express the importance of continuity, of the chain that must bind human beings to one another and improve their nature: give, receive, and give back; give, receive, and give back; give, receive, and give back...”

Mona no longer knew what to say. Above all she did not want to disappoint her grandfather. She had already relied on humor during their conversation and now fell silent so as not to add anything too naive, while knowing very well that he was talking to her and bringing her into this immense museum so that she might become a little more adult. For the moment she simply felt a pulling inside herself, because this summons to grow up, this intoxicating thrill of exploring a new world, had an extraordinary magnetic power, all the more so because the call came from Henry, whom she revered. And yet, deep in her soul, there already existed that terrible presentiment that what one gives back, one never gets back again. And that keen though still very distant grief for a childhood vanished forever twisted her heart.

“Shall we go, Dadé? Move along, you lot?”

“Come on, Mona! Off we go!”

Henry took her hand in his again, and they left the Louvre at a slow pace, without a word. Outside, night was beginning to fall. Henry was fully aware of the turmoil that had just made his granddaughter waver. But he categorically refused to spare people on the pretext of ensuring himself nothing but pleasant, full, seductive moments in their company. No: he knew perfectly well that existence is worthwhile only on condition that we shoulder its roughness, and that once those roughnesses have been sifted by time they reveal a precious, fertile matter, a substance both beautiful and useful, that allows life truly to be life.

Besides, by that miracle of childhood, Mona’s trouble did not last long: as they walked along gaily, she began humming to herself. Henry never interrupted her at such moments, which he found incredibly touching. And then suddenly, as they approached home, Mona stopped, overtaken by the conspiratorial lie they had agreed upon in order to avoid the sessions with the child psychiatrist. She opened her big blue eyes and turned her mischievous little head, laughing at the trick they were playing on her parents.

“Dadé, what do I say if Mom and Dad ask me the name of the doctor I went to see?”

“Tell them his name is Dr. Botticelli.”
