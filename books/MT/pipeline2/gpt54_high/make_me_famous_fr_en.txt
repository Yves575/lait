Fame is my life. The fame I knew I would have, the fame I made sure I had. Was I prepared for that kind of success? Of course I was. I have always felt that what awaited me was not a mere life but a destiny. My path would be exceptional, my trajectory out of the ordinary.

When people talk about fame, they think of glitter, glamour, money, devoted fans, prestige, recognition. But they should also mention the constant sense of superiority, the heady rush of wealth, the endless commentary, vanity, hypocrisy, impunity. Fame is a hard drug, a ferocious monster. And I went after it with my rage, with my nails, with my teeth.

Fame on that scale set the beast in me free, merciless and cruel. I may as well say it from the outset: I got my hands dirty. At my level, everyone has bodies in the closet. Anyone who says otherwise is lying. Fame is spoils of war—no one is ever ready to give it up.

At thirty-two, I stand at the top of a castle I built on my own with my songs. I don’t believe in luck. I don’t believe in a network of influential friends. I don’t believe in the glass ceiling. I owe my success to nothing but my talent, my character, and meritocracy. So if I could have been honest on the night of my last awards ceremony, there would have been only one person for me to thank in my long speech: myself.

Three weeks on a desert island in the middle of the Pacific Ocean. A place with no mains water or electricity, no contact whatsoever with the outside world. It’s the kind of fantasy fame lets you buy. When you have everything, you have to show at least a little imagination when it comes to summer vacations.

Yesterday, I spent ten hours on a private jet. We landed somewhere near Tahiti or Fiji, unless it was Hawaii. I boarded a second plane, then a seaplane. An hour later, the island appeared: an atoll lost in the immensity.

Ocean as far as the eye can see, white sand, a magnificent lagoon: I’m walking through a postcard. Flocks of seabirds stop off on the rocks, the vegetation is lush, you can tell the downpours are frequent, the rainwater cistern is full. Coconut palms, banana trees, orange trees—I’m not about to starve. My eyes on the open sea, I look for land on the horizon, some fixed point to rest my gaze on. There is none.

My only shelter for the next three weeks is a shack on the beach. The place is charming, built on stilts, with a terrace facing the ocean. Its high, sloping wooden frame supports a roof covered in woven tropical leaves. Inside, there is a single room with sparse furnishings: a single bed, a chest of drawers, a table, two chairs. The provisions are stored in a cupboard: rice, fruit, dried fish, tubers, canned food, a hundred liters of mineral water. I open the drawers to complete my inventory and gather my meager haul on the floor: a camp stove, two gas cylinders, flippers, a diving mask, a flashlight, a machete, a box of matches, a mosquito net, a water purifier, a fishing net, a globe, a Bible. Nothing on the walls, not the slightest decoration, no clock, no mirror. Rustic, for a $500,000 vacation. But it’s simple: the more it costs, the less Wi-Fi there is. I’m paying top dollar to be in the middle of nowhere, out of reach of eyes, cell phones, paparazzi lenses—and the incessant demands of my teams. This year, I’m giving myself the best gift of all: for everyone to leave me the hell alone.

I first hear about this island six months ago. That evening, I make a much-anticipated appearance at the party that closes out an awards ceremony where I’ve once again swept the board and reeled off one acceptance speech after another. A little earlier, trophy in hand, I paid tribute to my audience four separate times, with tremors in my voice and tears in my eyes. That’s the first thing you have to learn: how to cry on command. Same old song and dance. You have to seem moving and fragile, be modest in triumph, explain that you make music for your fans, acknowledge the people behind the scenes by rattling off a long list of names that mean nothing to anyone.

Natalie Holmes is standing by the bar at the after-party, a champagne flute in her hand. I haven’t run into her in more than a year, and this past year has been eventful: breakup, betrayal, media storm, drama, the triumphant release of my third album. Natalie Holmes and I used to have a handful of mutual friends, but those friends have turned into enemies, so I’m ready to bet she’ll pretend she hasn’t seen me, eyes lowered to her Jimmy Choos. Wrong. She comes over with a big smile. It’s amazing how success makes people forget their grudges. My new assistant whispers in my ear the title of the latest movie she was in, the director’s name, the release date. I’m willing to make conversation, but I need a minimum of material.

Natalie begins by congratulating me on my album (not the most original opening) and on the four trophies I’ve won tonight (what a platitude), then tells me she often thinks back on our conversations at John Cutler’s in Los Angeles (I have no memory of them). That dress really squashes her arms; I love a strapless gown, but it doesn’t flatter everyone (what the hell was her stylist thinking?). Jaw clenched so I don’t yawn, I would rather be anywhere but here talking to a bland actress. I’m just about to slip away politely when the conversation takes an unexpected turn. Natalie Holmes lays a conspiratorial hand on my wrist and starts telling me about her latest vacation.

—It wasn’t really a vacation, more an experience, she murmurs.

—What do you mean?

—I’m not allowed to say any more, but I swear it changed my life.

—That much?

—You can’t imagine. I wasn’t expecting to be so shaken… I can give you the number if you want.

—Sure, why not.

—I can’t promise anything, though. It only works by word of mouth, and places are extremely limited. I heard Selena Gomez has been waiting for months to go.

If Natalie Holmes is to be believed, all of Hollywood’s elite file secretly through this “top secret” and “very exclusive” place. Obviously, that is exactly what you say to make me want in. No mega-yacht, no villa in the Bahamas, no castle in Tuscany, but three weeks on a private island in the Pacific. “A Robinson Crusoe adventure,” “a spiritual retreat,” “an end-of-the-world experience,” “a place so remote that nothing else exists.” I have a hard time imagining Natalie Holmes spending the summer without air-conditioning and spearfishing, but why not. “That’s where Christopher Nolan got the idea for Interstellar”; “Taylor Swift drops her bags there once a year, she recharges and finds inspiration for new songs”; “Apparently the island belonged to F. Scott Fitzgerald, and he wrote the first chapters of The Great Gatsby there”—I have my doubts; as far as I know, the American writer never got rich, but Natalie Holmes knows exactly what will seduce me. Fitzgerald is my favorite writer. She adds, stars in her eyes and a smile heavy with insinuation:

—Those in the know call it “the island of masterpieces”… Word will get out eventually, but for the moment the place is still unspoiled, so you have to enjoy it while you can. It’s absolutely magical.

—If you say so.

My pride is stung. I ran into Selena and Taylor yesterday—how come they didn’t mention it? And besides, why does Natalie Holmes, an actress with neither my aura nor my influence, know about it before I do? As she moves away, she actually makes me promise to keep my mouth shut; she signed an ironclad confidentiality agreement. Secret location, no information online, ultra-selective co-optation. Mystery creates desire.

One evening, dead tired, I dial the number on a whim. In the middle of promoting my third album, I’ve spent the whole day being harassed by idiots, not one of whom has been capable of carrying out my orders properly. Everyone around me is either incompetent or lazy, and the week is ending in an atmosphere of terror. They say music soothes the savage beast—don’t make me laugh. Speed and pressure make me merciless. Alone in a hotel room in Las Vegas, I study myself in the mirror while I wait for someone to pick up, to no avail. I leave a message on an enigmatic answering machine that quotes a verse from the Bible.

Fine sand, turquoise water, absolute calm. I swim for a long time, slowly, breaststroke, backstroke. I think about the currents that could carry me out to sea, the heavy swells near the coral reef, the fish whose dorsal fins contain deadly venom. The thrill is part of the pleasure.

After my swim, I untie my bathing suit under the outdoor shower, a Spartan setup behind the shack. The rainwater warmed by the sun is lukewarm, the soap lathers against my skin, and I let out a sigh of happiness. For once, no paparazzo is hiding behind a rock to photograph my breasts. I don’t even need to suck in my stomach.

Building a sandcastle takes up the rest of my day. As when I was a child on the beaches of Île d’Oléron, I pack down the wet sand, dig moats, shore up my fortress, peaceful and focused, before setting off in search of shells to decorate my towers. A futile, accidental activity, just for me. The pleasure of applying myself without trying to excel. Is it two in the afternoon or five? I haven’t the faintest idea. Automatically, I slip my hand into my pocket to grab my phone. It will take me several days to suppress that reflex.

The day before I left, a man in a white polo shirt ceremoniously searched my bag. Bringing a phone, a computer, a camera, or any electronic object whatsoever was out of the question. He confiscated my watch as well. The watchword: disconnect. Be unreachable. Drop off the radar. No calls from my assistant, no social media, no obligations. Nothing but silence and serenity.

The only exception to my chosen isolation is an emergency satellite phone, black with an antenna, tucked away in the cupboard next to the first-aid kit. I have no trouble imagining celebrities paying for this singular experience and cracking after forty-eight hours. Plenty of them must ask to leave early, terrified by some nasty spider or stricken with mortal boredom. Because I have no doubt these three weeks will be an ordeal too. Solitude is another sort of hell.

But for now, I’m in paradise. For months I haven’t been longing for another trophy; I’ve been dreaming of a break. The people around me can see that I’ve become irritable, impatient, insufferable. They don’t realize it, no one can understand what I’m going through. The closest image I can find is this: I’m in a washing machine, and the cycle has been going on for seven years.

I will never admit it publicly, but I am exhausted. I was ready for any war to become famous, never imagining I would have to give so much to remain so.

The sun is setting over the ocean for the first time. My suitcase is open at the foot of my bed, the fire I lit with dead wood crackles on the beach. My guitar across my knees, I fumble out the first words of a verse, a notebook within reach. This paradisal retreat will certainly give me material for my fourth album—I’ve isolated myself on an island, I haven’t taken a vow of silence. At nightfall, the sea gives way entirely to the sky, where I discover thousands of constellations whose geometry I do not recognize. I am somewhere in the Southern Hemisphere.

The flames throw sawtoothed shadows across the sand; I shiver as I roll the globe I found in the hut beneath my fingertips. It dates from the second half of the twentieth century; it shows vanished countries, acronyms from another age, and I look for borders that do not yet exist. I run my index finger along the latitudes and meridians, trying to locate myself on the sphere. In all likelihood, the island does not even appear on it. I am nothing but a dot in the immensity of the Pacific, but where? The outlines of states take shape before my eyes; the nearest continental coasts are probably more than four thousand kilometers from here, somewhere in South America or Oceania.

What am I doing here? More than that, how did I get here? To calm the vertigo rising in me, I stroke the scars on my thighs, my scalp, my hands, my arms, my ankles. Each one traces the path of my life, each one another milestone on the road to glory.

There is a price to fame, and it has to be paid every day.

Part One

Faith

My name is Cléo Louvent Johnson. My mother is French, my father American; I move easily between the two languages. My mother is a statistician, my father an Egyptologist; I build myself between numbers and hieroglyphs. I grow up in an apartment full of books in a quiet corner of Paris’s fourteenth arrondissement. My father, who fondly calls me Cleopatra, pins my drawings of pyramids and sphinxes to the kitchen wall with magnets. On Saturdays, my mother takes me to the neighborhood library; on the way home, we stop to say hello to the gray herons in Parc Montsouris.

I am four years old, and my father is telling me a story in my bedroom. My favorite books are the ones lit up by his commentary. A specialist in Egypt, passionate about every kind of antiquity, he tells me about the misfortunes of Isis and Osiris, their struggle against Seth, but also the wanderings of Ulysses, the childishness of the Olympian gods; I drink in his words when he details the exploits of Ramses II, Pericles, Alexander the Great. Every story becomes a pretext for detours through history and its epics, which swarm with characters on extraordinary quests and marvelous animals. It is almost time to sleep; my father tucks me in before turning off the light, when suddenly he senses that I’m troubled. When he presses me a little, I finally confess: “Daddy, I want to be as famous as Céline Dion.”

I have no memory of it, but for years my parents took pleasure in reminding me of that anecdote, which made them laugh a great deal. I sense tenderness in it, mixed with surprise. Where on earth did she get that from?

My parents live in the microcosm of their academic research, their publications, their conferences. They don’t know who the fashionable figures are—they would be incapable of recognizing the idols of their century if they ran into them in the street. Red carpets, celebrity couples, Hollywood, private jets, gossip magazines: it is an exotic country where they have no desire to spend a vacation.

At the school gates, my parents look older than the others. My mother explains to me that her pregnancy lasted not nine months but nine years. I will be their only child. Their difficulty conceiving is no secret; my Wikipedia page says I was born through in vitro fertilization; for some obscure reason, that information only adds to the magic surrounding my birth: did it really take science getting involved to create a being as exceptional as me?

I am a little girl with short hair, well-behaved and solitary, who slips plane-tree leaves into the pockets of her coat to make salad for her dolls. My parents hear me talking to myself in my room, notice that I always shut the door to play.

They have no idea that their darling child spends her Wednesday afternoons filling notebooks in search of a star-quality signature. At seven, I practice signing stacks of autographs as fast as I can. The following year, I give my first interviews. The questions come naturally to me; I work out my answers methodically, in French and in English. At my feet, my stuffed hedgehog, whale, ladybug, and squirrel listen intently. My menagerie is a captive audience, the best I could dream of.

I sing along in unison with my favorite stars too, playing their music on the CD player on the floor near my desk. I know every lyric by heart; I read them in the liner notes, sitting cross-legged on the pale blue carpet in my room. As a teenager, the website paroles.net, with its thousands of cataloged songs, takes over. I spend hours there, captivated.

I have explained my path to journalists so many times. I have recounted my past in interviews so many times. I have played that dangerous game so many times without understanding how serious it was.

I have romanticized my life, prettified my answers to make myself seem more brilliant, more impressive. I have removed from my story, and little by little from my memory, the episodes that didn’t fit my legend. I have manufactured false memories for myself. In search of the juicy detail or the moving anecdote, I have dipped into other people’s pasts, into my reading, into films, until I forgot what truly belonged to me.

Memories are fragile; to convince yourself of that, all you have to do is tell the same event several times. By the third or fourth telling, the past begins to rearrange itself. Gently, you alter the lighting, erase certain details, heighten the dramatic force to captivate your audience. You turn the past into a story, and with each retelling, it is the memory itself that you damage. There are anecdotes I have repeated so many times that I no longer know whether they are true or false.

It is tempting to rewrite your life; I have done it often. The signs heralding my destiny are legion: the early interviews with my stuffed animals, my extraordinary mezzo-soprano voice, my fascination with song lyrics, my musical ear, my ease at the piano, my guitar teacher’s encouragement, my solid grounding in music theory, my passion for choir. It is so much simpler to walk the road backward.

I start music at seven, but don’t be fooled. Where I come from, the conservatory is the after-school activity of good students. My parents do not dream of turning me into a celebrity by encouraging me to learn an instrument—we are not in Los Angeles. In my world, music is a bourgeois reflex, not encouragement to become a global pop star.

My desire for fame comes not from my family but from my fault lines. All my life, I will go looking for the admiration of millions of strangers, force the respect of everyone who has crossed my path. The baker, the pharmacist, my kindergarten teacher, the neighbor on the third floor. I have only one aim: that they should all recognize my superiority.

People talk a great deal about celebrities’ mental disorders. How many become alcoholics, suffer from addictions, sink into depression, kill themselves? Fame drives you mad—and you have to be mad to want to be famous. Public life is a pot of honey that attracts narcissists, perverts, sociopaths. Balanced people have no need to vampirize love on such a scale.

I think back to that four-year-old girl confiding to her father how sad she was not to be famous. In that thirst to be admired, congratulated, applauded, there was a need that had always exceeded normal proportions.

“When I met Cléo Louvent for the first time, I knew immediately she would become famous.” In articles about me, there is always some former teacher or classmate claiming that my potential was obvious to everyone. In reality, no one would have bet on me. I am an ordinary child, not especially pretty, shy.

My first report cards are excellent, but I am encouraged to speak up more in class. I hide behind my grades—they owe everything to my anxious perfectionism. As soon as I make a single crossing-out in my school notebook, I tear out the page and start again.

At eight, I skip a grade and find myself sitting next to beautiful Juliette Marchand. My new best friend is my exact opposite: popular, talkative, loud. Her ease impresses me. My mother thinks she has an unfortunate tendency to make a spectacle of herself; my father feels the same aversion toward people who talk loudly or try to be the center of attention. If I behaved at home the way Juliette does, I would get a curt answer: stop showing off.

Juliette divides her affection among Lydia, Clémentine, and me. In other words, I am one of her ladies-in-waiting, and at every recess I fear I will stop being her favorite. When Juliette holds forth on how important it is to kiss a boy before starting middle school, I silently nod along behind her. She starts calling me Cléou; I hate it, but once again I say nothing.

One Monday in October, Juliette decides to organize a fashion show with several girls from the class. The performance will take place at her house the following Sunday, in front of her parents and their friends. We spend the week talking about it in the schoolyard. At ten, I swear nothing matters more.

The big day arrives; my father drops me off at Juliette’s, my prettiest clothes folded in my backpack. To guarantee my place in the show, I have claimed to own a pair of flared pants; once I’m there, I pretend I forgot them. In the bathroom, I overhear Lydia saying I’m a dirty little liar: those bell-bottoms don’t exist. Clémentine agrees with her—she adds that I have a deep voice like a boy and hair on my thighs. Juliette comes to my defense firmly: the queen protects her favorites and decrees peace. On the other side of the door, I swallow back my tears.

Juliette orchestrates the event, chooses the order of appearances, starts the music. Her bedroom serves as backstage; we change in a rush, and we each get four turns. She opens the show in a ruffled skirt and an orange tank top, her blond hair loose down her back. Half an hour later, jealousy leaves me breathless as she closes the show in a midnight-blue dress with thin straps. Juliette walks with assurance to the applause, spins around, winks at the captivated adults. On Monday at recess, I steal her snack and spit in her schoolbag.

To become famous, you have to pile up enough resentments, boil with frustration for months, simmer for decades. The years spent in my best friend’s shadow will give me the endurance I need to withstand the years spent in the light. It is no accident that, on the eve of my fame, I decide to dye my hair blond like Juliette. I have a score to settle, and it isn’t only hair-related.

Lying in bed at night, I resent my parents for giving me this ridiculous, too-short name. Why Cléo? I would rather have been called Juliette, Mathilde, Émeline, or Éléonore. A girl’s name, a princess’s name, pretty, important (minimum three syllables). I am jealous and petty; I hope no one comes to Juliette’s birthday. Or better yet, that she tells me her mother is dead. And then I imagine my revenge, caress it with my fingertips. When I reach the top, I won’t spare a glance for those who underestimated me. I won’t be content just to spit in their bag, I’ll spit in their faces, reduce them to ashes.

I wish my parents had explained it to me sooner: the children who draw all the looks rarely go on to have the most glittering adult lives, the closest-knit families, the most impressive careers. The star of elementary school never becomes the global superstar. Since then, I’ve had occasion to observe it dozens of times: fame isn’t a victory, it’s revenge.

I still have very vivid images of the summer I was fourteen: bike rides among the pines with my cousins, snacks on the beach, a roller-skating race. I go with my aunt to buy oysters at the market, I take sailing lessons, I secretly pluck my eyebrows. My diary is hidden in my underwear drawer; I suspect my cousin Violette of reading it while I’m in the shower and taking sadistic pleasure in quoting passages from it in our conversations; doubt slips into my gaze, she revels in it. Or so I think.

My mother’s sister owns a vacation house on the island of Oléron, and I have spent part of my holidays there for as long as I can remember. The rest of the time, I stay with my grandparents in Aix-en-Provence, camp in Auvergne with my cousins and uncle, or fly to Boston to visit my father’s family. That summer, my father joins us on Oléron for a week. One afternoon, I get the urge to sit down beside him on the couch while he watches the Olympic Games on television.

In front of the screen, I am first seized by a burning intuition, almost a revolt: I hate team sports. Handball, volleyball, basketball: depending on someone other than yourself for victory strikes me as insane. How could you not want to burn your teammate alive after a bad performance?

Swimming seems to me a more reasonable discipline (except relays, obviously). Yesterday, a seventeen-year-old French girl sprang a surprise by winning the 400-meter freestyle. Our new national star is only three years older than I am, and this morning at the newsstand she is on every front page. In her interviews, I discover, fascinated, the years of sacrifice, the pre-dawn wakeups, the sixteen kilometers of swimming a day, the smell of chlorine clinging to the skin until it makes you nauseous, the hard labor required to win a hundredth of a second. Everything rings true: strength of character, tolerance for pain, the need to win. In it I recognize the jagged outline of my own ambition.

I too have something to prove. I too am determined to do ten times more than the others. I too am ready to fight. I too am a perfectionist with an enormous capacity for work. I too want to live in a simplified world where the best wins. It’s not for nothing that I am always first in my class, I have never yielded that place to anyone; I am also the most gifted at the conservatory, the best in my piano, guitar, and singing classes. If the goal is ten, I accomplish a hundred. An overachiever, as my father says. Overachieve or nothing.

And yet I cannot explain this oddity. Why am I dying so badly to become famous? To feel special, powerful, loved? Of course I believe I am different from the others, out of step, misunderstood, but isn’t that the most universal feeling in the world when you’re growing up? So how am I supposed to interpret this rage? Did my parents not look at me enough? Or, on the contrary, is it the effect of ambition transferred onto an only child too long awaited and too cherished? Is my thirst for fame the consequence of an innate disposition, an acquired experience, neglect, bullying, inheritance? I don’t know. I have no cause to offer, no psychological, familial, sociological, astrological, mystical, existential explanation. I can’t explain it, and yet I burn. I grew up in the posh neighborhoods of Paris, but I’m a junkyard dog.

—Swimming is a dog’s sport, my father says, just as the 100-meter backstroke starts.

A minute later, the French swimmer wins her second gold medal.

—Dad, do you think she knew she was going to win?

—Of course. You have to have big dreams to do big things.

In September, I plaster the faces of my new idols on the walls of my bedroom: swimmers, boxers, fencers. I’ve found my clan: determined, disciplined women who are like me. As a model for life, Céline Dion had never really done the trick—and besides, Juliette thinks she’s corny. I still intend to become a singer, but I do not recognize myself in the discourse of my favorite artists. In creation, it is more a matter of passion, inspiration, and dissipated lives. I want to be a star of music because I want to be a machine. And my rise will run through my body too.

Alongside my music classes, I sign up for tennis with Juliette and for dance. I learn the love of gesture, repetition, performance. At night, I do planks; in the morning, I step on the scale. I meticulously weigh my food, count calories, check my protein intake. Every week, I plot my weight on a graph drawn on graph paper. As soon as I put on 500 grams, I run until I’m exhausted.

Even the best athletes need recovery time. So I rest on Friday evenings by turning on my computer. My favorite game is called The Sims.

Charlotte, my current avatar, plays guitar and piano. She has a social life, a fiancé, a Dalmatian. Yesterday she was burgled, and she spent her evening repairing a leak in her bathroom. To build her charisma, Charlotte talks to herself in the mirror for hours. I also impose workout sessions on her, a healthier diet, frequent makeovers. Because I am not moving forward blindly, gratuitously, for pleasure. I have a goal in mind: to make her a star. To have any hope of getting there, she absolutely has to become friends with other famous people and always stay in a good mood.

Charlotte works hard. She runs from auditions to concerts to photo shoots. I could have chosen another career; around her, her friends are acrobats, bank robbers, dog trainers, secret agents, fighter pilots. But I won’t be satisfied until Charlotte is finally famous. I want a limousine to come pick her up outside her house, strangers to ask her for autographs around town. Only then will I buy her a more comfortable bed, old master paintings for her bedroom, a shower stall with brand-new silicone seals, a new oven.

As long as I remain first in my class, my parents don’t forbid me from spending so much time on my computer, but they do not approve of it either. “Wouldn’t you rather pick up a book?”

My game lasts several weeks. Despite her efforts, my avatar does not become famous. Failure is not an option. It never will be. I lead Charlotte into the garden and build four walls around her. She will die like a medieval recluse: walled in.

Juliette has been arguing with her boyfriend in the bathroom for an hour. Tonight we’re celebrating my birthday at her father’s apartment on Rue Princesse. Everyone at school knows that my best friend throws the best parties. Earlier, we made a chocolate cake and asked her older brother to buy alcohol.

While Juliette settles her romantic scores, I’m talking to Nathan, a friend of her brother’s who goes to Henri-IV. He is shy and blushes easily; it’s the first time we’ve exchanged more than three sentences.

—Nathan is short for Nathanaël. It means “gift of God” in Hebrew. Do you know where Cléo comes from?

Using etymology as a pickup line—no doubt about it, I’m at a party in Saint-Germain-des-Prés. And while he talks, I smile inwardly because Nathan makes me think of a stork. He has long thin legs and a tiny little head.

—Cléo is a nod to Cleopatra.

—Right, your father specializes in Egypt, doesn’t he?

—Yes. He wanted to call me Cleopatra, my mother forbade it, and they settled on Cléo. But he calls me Cleopatra all the time anyway.

—Cleopatra is pretty, it must mean something.

—Yes, I think it means “the father’s pride,” or anyway that’s what my father keeps repeating. Patros means “father” in Greek.

—But do you know if Cléo means anything on its own? Pride?

—I’ve never really looked.

I search on my phone. I have bad reception, so we keep chatting while I wait; Nathan pours me another drink, a mix of vodka and orange juice. At the same moment, Juliette comes out of the bathroom red-eyed; as I went past the door, I heard shouting and crying. How can she waste so much time on this guy? Doesn’t she have anything better to do?

In the living room, girls are dancing to the latest hit. There are warm bare bodies, the lyrics sung in unison when the chorus comes around, conversations in the dark, the smell of beer and cannabis, dirty glasses piling up in the sink, crumbs on the herringbone parquet, Nathan’s gray eyes. The answer appears on my phone screen; I put my hand on his shoulder to keep from falling, I have such trouble believing it. In Greek, Cléo comes from kleos, which means “glory.” Renown. Fame.

My life is an adventure movie. On the evening of her fifteenth birthday, the heroine learns the secret of her origins. Your name means “fame” because your destiny is to become a global star. I grew up hating my name, and I had no idea it contained my prophecy. In all modesty, this discovery makes me understand that I am the chosen one.

For a long time I sensed that I was different from everyone else without managing to explain that strange intuition. Tonight, everything becomes obvious. It isn’t that I admire fame or fantasize about it like a teenage girl ill at ease in her own skin—it’s that I’m going to do it. Holy shit, I’m really going to do it.

Fame is not just a sweet childhood dream; an illustrious horizon awaits me. I picture myself spinning the wheel of fortune and winning the jackpot, climbing onto the top step of the podium, collecting every honor, a laurel crown on my head. I was born in the wrong cradle, in the wrong body, in the wrong life. And soon it will be up to me to right that injustice.

Without thinking, I grab Nathan’s face and kiss him. I drain my glass in one gulp, slam it down on the table, then take Nathan by the hand and lead him to the bathroom. I lock the door, undo his fly, slide his pants down his thighs before squatting. I’m going to be famous. No question of being passive, I am no longer the good little girl who keeps her mouth shut. This time, I open it wide.

In return, I very much want him to finger me, but the insolent fool doesn’t budge. The oaf. The goofball. The kook. I finish him off and go back to dancing.

I take my place in the middle of the dance floor, haloed with glory, absolute sovereign. Eyes closed, at the center of the world, I rock my shoulders, roll my hips, the alcohol doubles my boldness, all eyes are on me, I feel them sliding over my silhouette. I dance in time, magnetic, imperial. I am the queen bee.

I leave the party without saying goodbye to anyone; it isn’t even midnight, I haven’t blown out my candles, they will go on celebrating my arrival on earth without me. Certainty. Certainty. Feeling. Joy. Peace. This night is my Night of Fire, the night of revelation—and what a flash of lightning. Drunkenness multiplies my exaltation, the icy wind burns my face, I ride up Boulevard Saint-Michel on my bike, pedaling through the night, standing on the pedals to climb the hill. I pass the Luxembourg Gardens, straight on to Avenue de l’Observatoire, my heart fluttering with happiness, my birthday ought to be a national holiday. In my headphones, pure jubilation: “Samba de Janeiro,” that wildly famous World Cup victory song. I don’t need to speak Portuguese to understand the euphoria; I let the percussion reverberate inside me. I run one red light, then another, weave between the cars around Place Denfert-Rochereau, close my eyes, invincible, let go of the handlebars with one hand, then the other, lift my arms, fly like a bird. I ignore the motorists honking; you don’t kill off the main character at the beginning of the movie. In the freezing night, I shout, louder and louder, the four prophetic letters that trace my destiny: C-L-É-O.

When I get home, I spit onto the sidewalk the traces of semen still stuck to my gums. My phone rings; Nathan asks where I went. I’m not surprised—he’s already in love. I don’t answer his message. What’s the point? We can’t be together—it will be too complicated when I’m famous.

The apartment is silent, my parents are already in bed, and yet I am home before curfew. I drink a glass of water in the kitchen, gather a few provisions. The night ahead is going to be long, the longest of all.

At fifteen, I have just received irrefutable proof of my future fame; it is time to find out how my life’s mission will actually take shape. I open a graph-paper notebook, my handwriting hasty, broken up by arrows, underlined words, sentences in capital letters, numbered ideas. Half drunk, I carry out my first skills assessment.

1. My rigor, my perfectionism, my need to be the best, my refusal of laziness and flatness: all of it makes sense. I will be a high-level celebrity, not some cheap little starlet. I aspire not merely to be a singer but an illustrious figure. Like Cleopatra, I am on this earth to mark my era. Tutankhamun, Homer, Plato, Aristotle, Alexander the Great, Caesar, Charlemagne, Shakespeare, Napoleon. There aren’t enough women on that list; I volunteer.

2. I have a dazzling voice. Hard to ignore, people compliment me as soon as I open my mouth to sing. And besides, I am not just another soprano with a smooth, high timbre like a nightingale. My voice is deep, husky, gravelly; it is cracked, complex, distinctive—my vocal signature will be easy for the public to identify, recognizable above all others. Everyone is going to adore me.

3. I am passionate about poetry, excellent in literary subjects, capable of writing songs in French and in English, which will guarantee me international reach. Pretty voices can be found on every street corner: I am worth more than that. I will not be a mere performer, I will use my pen. My destiny is to become famous by singing my own songs.

4. Puberty is rarely a gift. For me, it is a blessing. The features of my heart-shaped face are softening, my breasts are rounding out, sport is sculpting my body. I am slimmer, more graceful—the cramped little girl is disappearing in favor of a star’s physique. There is still work to do: change my haircut, find the right lipstick, buy new clothes. But with all that, we should be able to make a beautiful poster.

Now that my case is laid out before me, all that remains is not to procrastinate. My action plan is to compose, right now, the song that will make me famous. I search for a melody, murmuring so as not to wake my parents sleeping in the next room. When I come to the chorus, I think of Céline Dion: her mother and brother wrote her first song when she was thirteen. Unfortunately, I cannot turn to my family for help. If they can’t be potential lyricists, at least they might have opened the doors of show business for me. I would have loved to have a mother who was a director, a father who was an actor. How can they settle for lives so small? Nobody knows who they are, nobody will remember their names—and you do not become a millionaire with your back bent over your research. My parents are ordinary people, squares, failures, hicks. On Saturday nights they are not invited to premieres: they watch a documentary about abstract impressionism or Turkish ambitions in the Mediterranean, they read on the living-room couch and go to bed at eleven. I love my parents, but they are of no use to me. Even so, I refuse to think like a victim. There are always more reasons to fail than to succeed. I plunge back into my song. If Thérèse and Jacques Dion managed it, I can too.

I spend the next few hours shaping and polishing my song. Inspired, determined, illuminated, I embroider around a story of impossible love. With that I-know-what-I-want look in my eyes, I prepare my coup d’état. I am in the process of composing a masterpiece in my bedroom, and everyone will be stunned to hear this miraculous anthem for the first time. “You wrote that all by yourself?” “We had no idea we had a musical genius among us!” “This song is incredible, I’m sending it to my aunt right away, she works in television, I’m sure she can find you a producer!”

The first rays of sunlight signal the end of my Night of Fire. Footsteps in the hall, the sound of keys, the apartment door slamming shut with a metallic crack. As he does every Sunday morning, my father goes down to the bakery to buy croissants. Meanwhile, my mother gets ready in the bathroom listening to the radio. She hums in the shower and I have finished my song.

I have repeated so many times that I started writing songs at the age of eight that I ended up believing my own lies. The truth is more banal: I wrote my very first song at fifteen. And however much I may have been struck by the fire of inspiration, what I produced is pathetic.

When you’ve had a 19.75 overall average on the baccalauréat, when you’re top of your class at Sciences Po, you dream of a prestigious career in the civil service, not of appearing on television and signing autographs. At twenty, I have everything going for me, I am pretty as an angel, the future is reaching out to me; and yet I feel thwarted, embittered, I have the sense of living a life that is not mine and I blame the whole world. Everyone says I am succeeding, but that is not how it feels to me. I end the semester with one certainty: I am giving far too much of myself to this degree.

Obviously, I am not about to interrupt my brilliant higher education to play opening sets in shitty bars. I deserve better than performing in dingy venues, going to auditions, becoming a backing singer. But my model-student cover takes up a crazy amount of time; it swallows me without inspiring me. Composed at night after a day of grinding away, my songs are without poetry, without substance, without nuance. They are everything I refuse to be: mediocre.

So I remain in the shadows, preparing my grand entrance. I am not motionless, I am paralyzed. I wait for the right idea, I wait to write better songs, I wait until I can play the piano better, I wait until I can afford a new guitar, I wait to complete my bachelor’s degree, I wait to find an internship, I wait for my singing teacher’s advice, I wait until I am thinner, I wait to buy myself a corduroy jacket, I wait for Saint Never’s Day, the Greek calends, February 30th. For the moment, I don’t need anyone to slow my progress toward glory. I do a very good job of that all by myself.

“Could you be a little quieter? We’re in a library.”

First warning. I cut in sharply before plunging back into my studying, and I unlock my laptop with the password I’ve been using for years: Cléo_célèbre!

Despite my intervention, the group of four idiots sitting at my table is still just as rowdy. I come here to surround myself with focused faces, not washouts playing on their phones instead of studying. Second warning. I shoot them several pointed looks to make them shut up—if only my eyes could spit fire. Unfortunately, my dear tablemates go on talking.
