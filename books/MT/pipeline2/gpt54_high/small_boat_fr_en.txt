I

I didn’t ask you to set out, I’d said.

You were the one who wanted it, and if you didn’t want to get wet, pal, you shouldn’t have climbed aboard. I didn’t push you into the water and I wasn’t the one who came to fetch you from your village or your field, from your crumbling suburb, to drag you out of there and stick you in your goddamn leaking boat, and now you’re floundering and I can well believe you’re frightened, and you’re calling for help as if it were my fault, you’re asking me to save you and you’re getting impatient. You’re counting on me. But I never asked anything of you. So let me do my job and be patient.

And it seems I must have thought all that so hard that I said it out loud — the first sentence at least, if the recordings were anything to go by, and there was no reason not to go by them, I’m willing to admit.

To which, while we were at it, we might have added sentences like I’m not God either, because I must have been thinking that too, and I might have said it. I might have said There are people like you everywhere tonight, forty Titanics all supposedly sinking in the Channel at the same time and I can’t deal with everyone at once. So you’re going to have to wait, pal, or else tell the others, the ones sinking too, to calm down and hang up so I can deal with you and you alone, you can just ring all the others yourself since you’ve got a mobile, all the others who every night climb into rotten dinghies, with no compass, no lights, thirty of them on a floor that could barely support five, with no instruments, no bearings, no knowledge of the sea, with women and children.

But what I’d actually said to Julien, beside me, was: These people are unbelievable, they throw themselves into the water and then they practically start giving you hell because you don’t chuck the lifebuoy quickly enough, they’ve got some nerve. He had smiled before burying himself again in his book. Rather flat, in this case, he’d remarked. And at three in the morning, that was the kind of joke that made you laugh.

That joke wasn’t recorded, I think it wouldn’t have looked too good, and Julien can sleep easy: he won’t be the one who gets taken for a monster for making a joke about inflatable boats going flat. Besides, on the station recordings, you hear only me, or almost. Just my luck.

After that I’d gone back to my monitors, my computer, my microphone, probably telling myself that, after all, they ought to be pleased, that lot: since they wanted to go to England, there they are, in British waters, and now on a British vessel, with British emergency blankets, wrapped up like sweets in gold paper, and they’ll be able to go on with their conversation in English for as long as they like.

But in the end the currents had carried their bodies back into French waters.

And now they were floating on the investigator’s desk, in the offices of the maritime gendarmerie. There were twenty-seven of them, if you counted right, a little girl among them, scattered between the pens, the notepads, the files, floating round the gendarmerie captain’s computer, and among them too the body of the man who had called me fourteen times that night and whom, of course, you could no longer hear now. The sea was calm on the desk, no wind and no swell, and beside the bodies there were only neatly arranged papers on the surface.

While she was playing me the recordings, the gendarme would alternately stare at me and look at I don’t know what through the window, from which you couldn’t see the sea, and that was just as well, because I saw the sea all the time from my semaphore station and, given the choice, I preferred to see, as I did today, a stretch of street with a building site opposite, workmen, African for the most part but at least alive and not wet and frozen with cold, and no women and no children, so I was happy enough to look at that while on the recording you could hear Please, please and I could hear myself saying Calm down, rescue is on the way.

The gendarme had her hair pulled back and tied in a severe ponytail, exactly like mine, I thought, and her back very straight like mine, military in a way, coat-hanger shoulders as Éric used to say, perhaps even the same face as mine, for all I knew, but ten years older, she in her blue gendarmerie sweater and me obviously in civilian clothes, I’d put on a ridiculous sweatshirt and a pair of trainers as if I’d just come back from my morning run, so that I looked like a kid despite my strict ponytail, like a sulky little girl, face double-bolted shut, summoned to the headmaster’s office, and it was no doubt this ridiculous and vaguely humiliating resemblance, by virtue of which I had sitting opposite me a caricature of myself, or the pitilessly dismal portrait of what I must really look like, it was no doubt that resemblance that made her immediately unsympathetic to me, even downright antipathetic, although in any case this was hardly a situation in which sympathy was the point.

As she showed me into her office, she’d said Thank you for coming of your own accord, your director had so far refused to give us your contact details. She’d added Your colleague, it seems, the one who was on duty with you that night, did not have the, the, the, but she had hesitated over the word that should come next, it might have been Scruple, or Courage, or why not Moral Conscience or Sense of Duty, but she couldn’t find the right word, so she corrected herself, saying Has not taken this step, for the time being.

And luckily she hadn’t asked me why I, myself, had taken this step, as she ended up calling it, and why I had come like this of my own accord, before, for all I knew, the judge and the gendarmes came knocking directly at my door and carted me off in front of my little girl and planted me in the dock on a charge of failure to assist a person in danger or something else, in short luckily she hadn’t asked me why, that morning, after dropping Léa at my parents’, I had got in the car to drive to the maritime gendarmerie in Cherbourg, four hours on the road from Boulogne, on the motorway a tasteless coffee on whose surface I saw little dinghies rocking though they were biscuit crumbs, and around me people silently pointing at me behind my back, a phone call to my parents to make sure everything was all right with the little one worried about not going to school, another phone call too to the gendarmerie to say I’m coming, I’m on the road and me amazed not to hear English spoken at the other end of the line, not to hear Please, please and shouting behind, but no, simply a We’re waiting for you, as sensible castaways should have said, bearing it patiently since there wasn’t much else to do apart from pray and keep watch for a boat in the vicinity, We’re waiting for you they would have said calmly, instead of multiplying useless pleas, instead of failing to understand the difference between being in French waters and being in British waters, instead of calling fourteen times in two hours to say they were sinking, instead of irritating me by repeating that as if I were the one who didn’t understand, but I understand perfectly well that you’ve got your feet in the water, only they’re English waters, not French waters, even if they’re just as cold, I can well believe it, so look over there instead if you still know which way to look, where north is, where south is,

luckily, then, that she hadn’t asked me why I had finally decided, because I wouldn’t have known what to answer her, in any case I don’t think I would have used words like Scruple or Sense of Duty, and certainly not Courage, perhaps rather words like Fatigue or Nausea, but more surely Anger,

that is to say Shame.

It was also that I wanted to hear myself. That is, hear myself rather than read myself, rather than read my words in the papers, hear my voice rather than the voices of journalists on television repeating what I said, alongside expressions like Migrant Drama, Migrant Tragedy, as if every night at eight o’clock we were at the theatre, putting on the same play again,

and indeed the same play, and me in the front row, you could say I never miss a single performance, every night I come to watch the Migrant Drama, I have a free seat and I’m even paid for it, there are those in the boxes, those in the balcony, but for me they’ve installed a semaphore in the front row so I can see properly, so I’m both ringside and in the front balcony over the sea, and even if sometimes it has a different title, sometimes Migrant Drama, sometimes Migrant Tragedy, Drama in the Channel after Drama in the Mediterranean, it is always the same play of course, and always the same character too, and at a certain point the character picks up a phone to address the audience, it’s interactive, he calls out, it begins with Please please, but it isn’t really the audience he’s addressing, while the audience is busy giving out dismayed ohs and ahs and sometimes sighs of indignation, it’s me, me alone he’s addressing, and then it’s up to me to answer, and answer properly, otherwise the audience isn’t happy and then the sighs of indignation are for me, and the next day the theatre critics descend on me, because the direction the performance takes depends on my answers and if I handle it well the Migrant Drama is a little less of a Migrant Drama, not to mention that meanwhile I also have to deal with what is happening on other stages where they’re performing the Drama of the Drifting Yachtsman or the Tragedy of the Capsized Trawler.

So, hear myself, hear my voice, in that office of the maritime gendarmerie, to be really sure of what I’d said, rather than hear myself ventriloquised by a dolled-up journalist saying that I’d said this and that, and that among the things I’d said were horrible, scandalous, or else appalling things, before going on to say that I had or hadn’t done things I should or shouldn’t have done.

But on the recordings it was mostly him you heard, I don’t know what to call him: the Migrant, the Man on the Phone, the Man Who’s Sinking, but his voice, his I knew by heart, and what he said too, because really it’s always the same one who calls me every night, the same voice and the same pleas, because that idiot, you can pull him out of the water all you like, back he goes, once, ten times, a hundred times: one night we haul him out in an emergency, safe and sound, he can at least say thank you, and the next night it’s him calling again, because he’s back in it as if he hadn’t learnt a thing and he says Please please, every day we put him back on dry land, every night he starts again, and then the feeling that this is what it will be till Judgment Day: the same bloke, always, going back into the water and beginning to sink in the middle of the Channel and waiting for someone to come and get him so he can start over again the following night, and after a while it gets wearisome.

But my own voice too, all the same, though not really my voice either, not because recordings inevitably distort tone, but because it was my professional voice, the one that briskly manages disaster every night with phrases like Calm down and Send me your location on WhatsApp or Rescue is on the way. So what you mainly heard were phrases like that, at 2.05, at 2.36, at 3.12, at 4.22 and up to 4.32 when, after that, there was nothing more to hear, among those phrases there were others that apparently I ought not to have uttered, like the one that said, then, I didn’t ask you to set out and the investigator asked me how that sentence was to be interpreted.

So I told the investigator that that sentence, since plainly it was a problem though I still didn’t know whether they were going to throw me in a cell because I had spoken an obvious truth, namely that it still wasn’t me who had asked them to set out, if that was one of the faults they were holding against me but what fault exactly, that sentence anyway, in my opinion, shouldn’t be given excessive importance, because after all I could have said it to any irresponsible pleasure-boater, the moron who puts to sea without giving a thought to the weather conditions or who is incapable of manoeuvring his crappy little yacht properly and ends up dead in the water and panicking and then the whole French Navy has to be mobilised to stop him going under. Him too, if he came and told me I wasn’t moving fast enough as if I were responsible for what was happening to him, I’d tell him in just the same way that it wasn’t me who had asked him to set out, after all.

But she looked at me as if she didn’t believe me, and I had my doubts too. But why?

Then she took advantage of it, delighted to be able to ask me with feigned innocence You don’t see the difference between an irresponsible pleasure-boater and a migrant dinghy? The question was probably not very well-intentioned, but in the end it was easy enough to answer, because my job meant precisely not making that distinction, which is what I said then, and I ought to be congratulated for not making it, that distinction: we go to everyone’s aid, we don’t make distinctions. Was I supposed to be more prompt with one than the other?

But of course that wasn’t what she meant, because I seemed to be thinking then that it was all the same thing, some yokel taking to sea in his pleasure boat and people fleeing misery and war, as if they had all set out in the same boat for a fishing trip. The distinction to be made was not between one drowning man and another drowning man, but between the reasons, the causes that had put him in such a situation. The distinction to be made is why people leave, and that explains why they leave thirty to a nutshell and not alone on a First 24. So the problem would be that I wasn’t making the distinction, that I didn’t seem to understand that the situations were different, that people do not set out in the same way and above all not for the same reasons and therefore not in the same conditions either.

And yet the insidious question implied that, on the contrary, I was making a distinction all right, only not in the right direction, namely that in the end I would have been quicker to save some blowhard showing off on his floating palace on a night of high winds than to mobilise the rescue services for a handful of Iraqi Kurds who stubbornly kept on paddling about in the Channel night after night. In other words I wasn’t making distinctions when I ought to have made them and when I ought not to have made them I was making them, and all that could only be explained by one reason, the same one, and since sooner or later she was going to ask me whether I had something against migrants, or whether I thought a migrant’s life was worth less than that of a pleasure-boater or a fishing skipper, then, to head off a question of that kind, I said that I had no opinions about migrants, no more opinions about migrants than about migration policy or the right to asylum, about relations between North and South, about the problems, the solutions, about the misery of the world, about injustice, that that was not what was being asked of me, to have opinions about migrants.

No, I don’t have opinions about migrants, I think I have even fewer than other people, and besides I’m asked not to have any at all and that suits me just fine. If anyone has opinions about migrants, it would be my ex, for example, Léa’s father, he has opinions, and in fact they’re pretty much the same as the baker on the corner and the cashiers in the supermarket next door, who I talk to from time to time when I’m off for a week, pretty much the same, really, as half of Calais, Boulogne or Dunkirk. And I heard Éric’s opinions about migrants all the more because I did that job, at the CROSS rescue centre, because not only could he not see why we went to so much trouble there to help those parasites, but why, as soon as we saw one, we didn’t just pitch them straight overboard, with a good whack of the oar as well if possible, just to make quite sure they didn’t come back up to the surface.

It had started with all the Calais stories and the Jungle, because Éric was from there, from Calais, and in the end he simply couldn’t stand the idea of me doing that job, helping trawlers or cargo ships yes, migrants no, and every time I came home I’d get it, and how many have you hauled out of the drink this time, and how much is all this bullshit costing us, and why don’t you shove them into English waters and let them sort it out since they want to go there at any price, and why, once you’ve fished them out, don’t you send them straight back to Africa, and don’t you think we’ve got something better to do than look after their little aches and pains, let them sort themselves out or stay where they are, etc. I’d heard that a thousand times, until, for reasons that had nothing to do with it, I asked him to leave, once and for all, but I wasn’t there to tell the story of my life with Léa’s father and the way we had ended up separating.
