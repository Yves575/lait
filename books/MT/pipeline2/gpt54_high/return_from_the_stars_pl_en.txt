I

I had no things at all, not even a coat. They said I wouldn’t need one. They let me keep my black sweater: that would do. And I fought for the shirt. I said I’d wean myself off it gradually.

Right there in the passageway, under the ship’s belly, where we stood being jostled, Abs held out his hand with a knowing smile.

“Just be careful…”

I remembered that too. I didn’t crush his fingers. I was perfectly calm. He wanted to say something more. I spared him by turning away as if I hadn’t noticed anything and went up the steps inside. A stewardess led me between the rows of seats all the way to the front.

I didn’t want a separate cabin. I wondered whether they had warned her. The seat opened out soundlessly. She adjusted the back, smiled at me, and went away. I sat down. The cushions were bottomlessly soft, as everywhere. The backs were so high I could barely see the other passengers. I was already able to accept the gaudiness of women’s clothes without resistance, but I still suspected the men, absurdly, of masquerade, and still cherished the faint hope that I would see some dressed normally—a pitiful reflex. People were settling in quickly; nobody had luggage.

Not even briefcases or bundles. The women didn’t either. There seemed to be more of them. In front of me two mixed-race women in parrot-colored little furs, fluffed up like feathers; evidently bird fashion was in.

Farther on, some married couple with a child. After the glaring selenophores of the platforms and tunnels, after the unbearably shrill self-luminous vegetation of the streets, the light of the concave ceiling was almost a mere glow. I put my hands on my knees, because somehow they were in the way. Everyone was seated now. Eight rows of gray armchairs, a waft of fir, the hush of conversations dying away. I expected an announcement of departure, some sort of signal, an order to fasten belts, but nothing happened. Across the matte ceiling vague shadows began to run from front to back, as if silhouettes of birds cut from paper. What the devil was it with these birds? I thought helplessly. Maybe it meant something? I was stiff with overattention, afraid of doing something improper.

It had been like this for four days already. From the first moment. I was constantly lagging behind everything that was happening, and the continual effort to understand the merest conversation, the simplest situation, was turning that tension into a feeling disgustingly close to despair. I was convinced the others felt the same, but we never spoke of it, not even when we were alone. We only joked about our sturdiness, about that excess of strength left in us; besides, you really had to watch yourself—at first, when I wanted to stand up, I would shoot toward the ceiling, and every object I picked up felt like paper, hollow, empty. But I learned to control my own body quickly.

By now I no longer crushed anyone’s hand when I shook it. That was simple. Unfortunately, it was the least important thing.

My neighbor on the left, stout, sunburned, with excessively shiny eyes—perhaps from contact lenses—suddenly vanished, because his seat swelled at the sides, which rose and joined, forming something like an egg-shaped cocoon. A few more people disappeared into such cabins. They looked like bloated sarcophagi. What did they do in them? But I kept encountering things like that, and I tried not to stare, unless they concerned me directly. A curious thing: the people who goggled at us when they learned what sort of beings we were, I treated with rather indifferent coolness. Their astonishment mattered little to me, though I realized at once that there was not a trace of admiration in it. It was rather those who took care of us—the Adapt people—who aroused my dislike. Most of all, perhaps, Dr. Abs, because he treated me as a doctor treats an abnormal patient, pretending—quite skillfully, for that matter—that he was dealing with someone perfectly ordinary. When that was no longer possible, he joked. I had had enough of his familiarity and joviality. Asked about it—so at least I thought—any passerby would have taken me or Olaf for someone like himself; it was not so much we who seemed uncanny to him as our past fate: that was what was extraordinary. Dr. Abs, on the other hand, like every Adapt employee, knew better—that we really were different. And this difference was no distinction, only an obstacle to understanding, to the simplest exchange of words—why, even to opening a door, since door handles had ceased to exist, I couldn’t remember whether fifty or sixty years ago.

The takeoff came unexpectedly. Gravity did not change by a hair, no sounds entered the hermetically sealed interior, shadows moved steadily over the ceiling—perhaps long habit, an old instinct, told me at a certain moment that we were in space, because it was certainty, not supposition.

But something else occupied me. I lay half reclined, legs stretched out, motionless. They had yielded to me too easily. Even Oswamm had not opposed my decision very strongly. The counterarguments I heard from him and from Abs could not persuade me—I myself could have come up with better ones. They insisted on only one point: that each of us had to travel separately. They did not even blame me for setting Olaf against them—because if it hadn’t been for me, he would certainly have agreed to stay longer. That was puzzling. I had expected complications, something that at the last moment would thwart my plan, but nothing happened, and here I was, flying. This final journey was to end in fifteen minutes.

Clearly what I had invented, and also the way I had stood before them to fight for an early departure, had not surprised them in the least. They must have had that sort of reaction catalogued already; it was a behavior pattern typical of cocky fellows like me, provided in their psychotechnical tables with the proper serial number. They let me fly—why? Because experience told them I wouldn’t manage? But how could that be, since this whole “independent” escapade consisted of a flight from one station to another, where someone from Earth Adapt was supposed to be waiting, and all I had to do was find that man in the agreed place?

Something happened. Raised voices reached me. I leaned out of my seat. A few rows in front of me some woman had shoved the stewardess away, and the stewardess, moving backward between the seats in a slowed, automatic way, as though under the effect of that shove—not so strong, after all—while the woman repeated: “I won’t allow it! I won’t let it touch me!”

I couldn’t see the face of the woman who was shouting. Her companion was tugging at her arm, saying something soothing. What did the scene mean? The other passengers paid no attention. Once again I was overcome by a sense of incredible foreignness. I looked up at the stewardess, who had stopped beside me and was smiling as before. It was not a merely outward smile of obligatory politeness covering irritation after the incident. She was not pretending calm, she really was calm.

“Would you like something to drink? prum, extran, morr, cider?” A melodious voice. I shook my head no.

I wanted to say something pleasant to her, but all I managed was the stereotyped question:

“When do we land?”

“In six minutes. Would you like something to eat? There’s no need to hurry. You can stay on after landing.”

“No, thank you.”

She moved away. In the air, right in front of my face, against the back of the seat ahead, the word STRATO lit up, as if written with the tip of a glowing cigarette. I bent forward to see how the word had appeared, and started. The back of my seat moved with me and clasped me elastically. I already knew that furnishings anticipated every change of position, but I kept forgetting it. It was not pleasant—as though someone were following my every movement.

I wanted to go back to my previous position, apparently too energetically. The seat misunderstood and almost unfolded like a bed. I jerked upright. What idiocy! More self-control.

At last I sat down. The letters of the pink STRATO trembled and flowed into others: TERMINAL. No shock, no warning, no whistle. Nothing. A distant sound came, like a postilion’s horn; four oval doors at the ends of the aisles opened wide, and into the interior burst a muffled, all-pervading roar, like the sea. The voices of the passengers rising from their seats drowned in it without a trace. I remained seated while they went out; rows of silhouettes flickered against the outside lights—green, lilac, purple—a veritable masked ball. By now they had all gone. I stood up. Automatically I tugged my sweater down. It felt somehow foolish, standing there with empty hands. A cooler draft was drawing through the open doors. I turned around. The stewardess stood by the partition, not quite touching it with her back. On her face there still rested the same serene smile, directed toward the empty rows of seats, which were now slowly beginning to curl up, fold in on themselves, like fleshy flowers, some faster, some a little more slowly—that was the only movement in that long-drawn roar filling everything, flowing through the oval openings, bringing the open sea to mind. I don’t want it to touch me! Suddenly I detected something bad in her smile. From the exit I said:

“Goodbye…”

“At your service.”

I did not grasp the meaning of those words at once, so singular on the lips of a young pretty woman, because they reached me when, already turned away, I was leaning out through the door. I wanted to set my foot on the step, but there was none. Between the metal hull and the edge of the platform yawned a gap a meter wide. Losing my balance, unprepared for such a trap, I gave an awkward leap and, once already in the air, felt a stream of invisible force catch me from below, so that I floated across the void and was set down softly on the white surface, which yielded springily. I must have had a rather foolish expression as I flew—I felt several amused glances, or so at least it seemed to me—I turned quickly away and walked along the platform. The projectile in which I had arrived rested in a deep berth, separated from the edge of the platforms by a gap with nothing guarding it. As if unintentionally, I moved closer to that emptiness and again felt the invisible springiness that would not let me step beyond the white brink. I wanted to look for the source of that peculiar force, but suddenly it was as if I had come awake: I was on Earth.

The wave of people swept me up: pushed along, I moved forward in the crowd. It was a moment before I properly saw the vastness of the hall. Was it even one hall, for that matter? No walls: high above, a white, gleaming explosion of incredible wings arrested in midair, and between them columns built not of some material, but of dizzying motion. Enormous fountains of a liquid denser than water, rushing upward, lit from within by colored reflectors? No; glass, vertical tunnels in which processions of blurred vehicles shot upward? I knew nothing anymore. Continually pushed, jostled in the antlike haste of the crowds, I tried to get to some empty place, but there were no empty places here. Being a head taller than those around me, I could see, thanks to that, that the emptied projectile was moving away—or rather, no, it was we who were gliding forward together with the whole platform. Lights blazed down from above, and in them the crowd sparkled and shimmered. Now the plane on which we stood pressed together began to go upward, and far below I saw already distant double white bands choked with people, with the black yawning slits of gaps along the inert hulls—for there were dozens of ships like ours—the moving platform turned, accelerated, went under higher levels. Flapping, disheveling with the wind they raised the hair of the people standing there, rounded shadows quivering with speed flew across them, as though upon impossible viaducts, impossible because deprived of any support at all, with the lights of their signals stretched into streaks; then the surface carrying us began to split, to fork along invisible seams, my strip sliding through interiors full of standing and seated people, surrounded by a multitude of small flashes, as if they were engaged in setting off colored fireworks.

I didn’t know where to look. In front of me stood a man in something fluffy as fur, which under the touch of light opalesced like metal. He had on his arm a woman in scarlet. What she wore was covered in huge eyes, peacock-like, and those eyes were blinking. It was no illusion, the eyes on her dress really were opening and closing. The walkway on which I stood behind the two of them, among a dozen other people, was still accelerating. Between planes of white-smoky glaze opened colorful, illuminated passages with transparent ceilings, ceaselessly trodden by hundreds of feet on the higher, next level; the all-pervading roar now spread, now tightened, when another tunnel of this journey aimed who knew where stifled the thousands of human voices and sounds, incomprehensible to me, meaningful to them; in the depths, farther off, the surroundings were constantly stitched by flying streaks of I knew not what vehicles—flying, perhaps—for sometimes they slanted upward or downward, boring into space, so that instinctively I kept expecting a terrible collision, since I could see no guides, no rails, if they were aerial trains. When those blurred hurricanes of speed broke off even for a moment, there emerged from behind them with majestic slowness huge planes full of people, like flying landings, moving in different directions, passing one another, lifting, seeming to interpenetrate through an illusion of perspective. It was hard to rest one’s eyes on anything motionless, because all the architecture around seemed composed, really, of movement itself, of change, and even what I had first taken for a winged ceiling was only suspended tiers, which were now giving way to others higher still. Suddenly, filtered through the glazed ceilings and those enigmatic columns, reflected from silver surfaces, there entered all the recesses of space, the interiors of the passages we were passing through, the lines of human faces—a heavy purple dawn, as though somewhere far away, in the heart of that structure stretching for miles, an atomic fire had flared up.

The green of the endlessly leaping neon lights turned dirty, the milk-white of the parabolic buttresses pinkened. There was something in that sudden saturation of the air with russet that seemed a forewarning of catastrophe—that was how I took it—but no one paid the slightest attention to the change, and I couldn’t even have said when it passed.

At the edges of our walkway spinning green circles would appear, like neon hoops suspended in the air; then some of the people stepped onto the approaching offshoot of another walkway or ramp; I noticed that one could pass through the green lines of those lights with impunity, as though they were immaterial.

For some time I let myself be borne along helplessly by the white walkway, until it occurred to me that perhaps I was already beyond the station and that this incredible landscape of warped glaze, forever seeming on the point of taking flight, was the city itself, while the one I had left behind already existed only in my memory.

“Excuse me”—I touched the arm of the man in fur—“where are we?…”

They both looked at me. Their faces, as they raised them, took on an expression of surprise.

I had a faint hope it was only because of my height.

“On the poliduct,” said the man. “What styk do you have?”

I understood nothing.

“Are we… still in the station?”

“Of course…” he answered, with a certain reluctance.

“And… where is the Inner Circle?”

“You’ve already missed it. You’ll have to double back.”

“A rast from Merid would be better,” the woman put in. All the eyes of her dress seemed to be staring at me with suspicious amazement.

“Rast?…” I repeated helplessly.

“Oh, there”—she pointed, through the approaching green ring, to an empty rise with black-and-silver striped sides, like the hull of a strangely painted ship lying on its side. I thanked her and stepped off the walkway, probably in the wrong place, because the momentum nearly took my legs out from under me. I caught my balance, but it spun me round so that I no longer knew which way to go. I was wondering what to do, but in that time the place where I was to change had moved a long way from the black-and-silver rise the woman had indicated, and I could no longer find it. Since most of those standing beside me were moving onto a ramp that went upward, I did the same. Once on it, I noticed an enormous sign burning motionless in the air: DUKT CENTR—the rest of the letters on both sides escaped the eye because of their size. Soundlessly it carried me onto a platform perhaps a kilometer long, from which a spindle-shaped ship was just pulling away, showing as it rose its underside riddled with lights. Then again, perhaps that whale-like shape was the platform, and I had found myself on the “rast”—there was no one even to ask, because emptiness stretched around me. I must have got off in the wrong place. Part of my “platform” was built over with flattened rooms without front walls. Coming closer, I made out a sort of dimly lit low bays in which black machines rested in rows. I took them for cars. But when the two nearest slid out, and before I could step back passed me, at once developing great speed, I saw before they vanished in the perspective of the parabolic slopes that they had no wheels, no windows, no doors, streamlined as gigantic black drops. Cars or not, I thought, in any case this must be some sort of parking place? Perhaps for those “rasts”? I decided it would be best to wait until someone came and ride together with him, or at least find something out. Yet my platform, lightly poised like the wing of an impossible airplane, remained empty, only the black machines slipped singly, or several at once, from their metal burrows and raced off, always in one direction. I went right to the edge of the platform, until once more that invisible springy force assuring safety made itself felt. It really hung in the air, unsupported by anything. Lifting my head, I saw many others like it, hovering just as motionlessly in space with their great lights extinguished; on others the lights were burning, where ships were docking. Yet they were not rockets, nor even projectiles like the one that had brought me from Luna.

I stood there a long time, until I noticed, against the background of some further halls—I couldn’t have said, for that matter, whether they were a mirror reflection of this one or reality—fiery letters moving evenly through the air: SOAMO SOAMO SOAMO, a pause, a blue flash, and NEONAX NEONAX NEONAX; perhaps they were the names of stations, perhaps of advertised products. They told me nothing.

High time I found that fellow, I thought, turned on my heel, and finding a walkway flowing back the other way, rode down on it. It turned out to be not that level and not even that hall from which I had come up: I knew it by the absence of those enormous columns. Then again, perhaps they had moved elsewhere; by now I was ready to believe anything possible.

I found myself among a whole forest of fountains; farther on I came upon a white-and-pink hall full of women.

As I passed, I idly thrust my hand into the stream of an illuminated fountain, perhaps because it was pleasant to encounter something even a little familiar. But I felt nothing; that fountain had no water. A moment later it seemed to me that I smelled a floral scent. I lifted my hand to my nostrils. It smelled like a thousand toilet soaps at once. Reflexively I began wiping it on my trousers. I was already standing before that hall full of women, women only. It did not look to me like the anteroom to a lavatory, though in the end one could not be sure. I preferred not to ask, so I turned back. A young man, dressed as though spilt mercury had congealed on him, puffed—or rather foaming—at the shoulders, tight over the hips, was talking to a fair-haired girl leaning with her back against the bowl of a fountain. The girl, in a light dress, quite ordinary, which filled me with heart, held a bouquet of pale pink flowers and, burying her face in them, smiled at the boy with her eyes. At the last moment, when I had come up to them and was already opening my mouth, I saw that she was eating those flowers—and for an instant my voice refused to obey. She was calmly chewing the delicate petals. She raised her eyes to me. They went still. But by then I was used to that. I asked where the Inner Circle was.

The boy seemed unpleasantly surprised, or even annoyed, that anyone should dare interrupt their tête-à-tête. Evidently I had committed some impropriety. He looked first upward, then lowered his eyes, as though expecting to discover some stilts as the cause of my height. He didn’t say a word.

“Oh, there!” cried the girl. “Rast na wuka, your rast, you’ll make it, quick!” I broke into a run in the direction she had indicated, without knowing where I was going—for I still had no idea what that cursed rast looked like—after ten steps I saw a silvery funnel descending from above, the base of one of those enormous columns that had astonished me before—could they be flying columns?—people were hurrying there from all directions; suddenly I collided with someone. I didn’t even stagger, I merely stopped dead, while the other man, a squat fellow in orange clothing, fell, and something unbelievable happened to him: his fur wilted before my eyes, caved in like a punctured balloon! I stood over him, dumbfounded, incapable of even spluttering an apology. He got up, looked at me darkly from under his brows, but said nothing, turned away and walked off with long strides, manipulating something at his chest—and his outfit filled out and lit up again…

There was no one left in the place the girl had indicated. After that adventure I gave up for good looking for rasts, the Inner Circle, ducts, styks, and decided to get out of the station. What I had experienced so far did not encourage me to accost passersby, so I followed at random a slanting blue arrow upward, passing with my own body, without much surprise, through two more words blazing in the air: LOCAL CIRCUITS. I came upon an escalator, fairly crowded. The next level was kept in a tone of subdued brown veined with golden exclamation marks. Flowing descents of ceilings and hollowed walls. Corridors without ceilings, seeming at the top to be immersed in luminous down. As though I were drawing near some residential spaces, the surroundings had something of a system of gigantic hotel halls—little windows, nickel pipes along the walls, recesses with officials of some sort, perhaps exchange booths, perhaps a post office—I went on. I was already almost certain that I wouldn’t find an exit this way and—judging by the length of the upward ride—that I was in the aerial part of the station, but I still kept to the same direction. Then sudden emptiness, panels of raspberry facing with sparkling stars, rows of doors. The nearest were not quite closed. I peered inside. At that same moment some huge broad-shouldered man did the same from the opposite side. My own self in a wall-sized mirror. I pushed the door wider. Porcelain, silver pipes, nickel. Toilets.

I felt a little like laughing, but all in all I was rather stupefied. I turned back quickly, another corridor, milky-white bands flowing downward. The escalator handrail was soft, warm, I did not count the floors going by, there were more and more people, they stopped at enameled boxes that projected from the wall every few steps, one touch of a finger, something dropped into the hand, they slipped it into a pocket and went on. I don’t know myself why I did exactly what the man in the loose violet garment ahead of me did; a key with a little hollow for the tip of a finger, a press, and straight into my waiting palm dropped a colored, half-transparent tube, as though warmed.

I shook it, lifted it to my eyes, some kind of pills? No. A stopper? No, it had no stopper, no closure at all. What was it for? What did the others do? They put it in their pockets. On the automaton: LAHGAN. I stood there; people jostled me. And suddenly I seemed to myself a monkey handed a fountain pen or a lighter; for a fraction of a second blind fury came over me; I clenched my teeth, narrowed my eyes, and merged, slightly hunched, with the stream of people walking on. The corridor widened, it was a hall now. Fiery letters: REAL AMMO REAL AMMO.

Through the current of the hurrying people, over their heads, I saw from afar a window. The first window.

Panoramic, enormous.

As though all the firmaments of night had been flung flat. To the horizon, out of a luminous mist—colored galaxies of squares, clusters of spiral lights, glows drizzling above skyscrapers, streets: the creeping, wormlike movement of beads of light, and above it all, in verticals, the boiling of neon, plumes and lightning, wheels, airplanes and bottles of fire, red dandelions of signal lights on spires, momentary suns and hemorrhages of advertisement, mechanically violent. I stood and watched, hearing behind me the steady murmur of hundreds of feet. All at once the city vanished and an enormous face appeared, three meters high.

“We have just transmitted a selection of newsreels from the seventies in the series ‘Visions of Old Capitals.’ At present transtel is extending its range to the studios of the cosmolites…”

I almost fled. It was not a window. Some sort of television set. I quickened my pace. I had begun to sweat a little.

Down. Faster. Golden squares of light. Inside, crowds, foam on glasses, an almost black liquid—not beer, with its poisonous greenish sheen—and young people, boys and girls, arms around one another, in sixes and eights, blocking the full width of the passages, coming toward me; they had to tear their arms apart to let me pass. Something jolted me. Before I knew it I had stepped onto a moving walkway. Astonished eyes flashed past quite close—a lovely dark girl in something that gleamed on her like phosphorescent metal. The fabric clung to her: she was like a naked woman. White faces, yellow faces, a few tall black men, but I was still the tallest. They made way for me. Above, behind bulging panes, streaming shadows sped by, invisible orchestras played, and down here a peculiar promenade went on, in dark passages—

headless figures of women; the down covering their shoulders glowed, so that only the emerging necks shone in it like white, strange stems, and scattered light in their hair—self-luminous powder? A narrow passage led me into an enfilade of grotesque statues, grotesque because they moved, because they were busy even; something like a broad street with raised sides roared with laughter, people were amusing themselves, what amused them so much—those sculptures?

Huge figures in cones of reflectors; ruby light, honey light poured from them, thick as syrup, with an extraordinary concentration of color. I walked on helplessly, squinting, losing myself.

A steep green passage, grotesque pavilions, pagodas reached by little bridges, full of tiny places, the smell of frying—sharp, insistent—rows of gas flames behind glass, the clatter of glass, repeated metallic incomprehensible sounds. The crowd that had carried me here collided with another, then it grew looser, everyone was boarding a carriage open straight through—no, it was only transparent, as if cast from glass, even the seats were like glass, though soft. Before I knew it I was inside—we were moving. The carriage raced on, people shouted over the loudspeaker repeating, “Meridional level, Meridional level, styks for Spiro, Blekkr Frosom,” the whole carriage seemed to be melting, pierced by shafts of light, the walls flying past in streaks of flame and color, parabolic arches, white platforms, “Forteran, Forteran, styks Galee, styks of the outer rasts, Makra,” muttered the loudspeaker, the carriage stopped and raced on; I discovered an astonishing thing: one felt no braking and no acceleration, as if inertia had been abolished. How was it possible? I tested it, bending my knees slightly, at three successive stops. Nothing on the curves either. People got off, got on, on the front platform stood a woman with a dog, I had never seen such a dog, it was enormous, with a head like a ball, very ugly, and in its calm hazel eyes were reflected the garlands of lights racing backward, diminished. RAMBRENT RAMBRENT. There was a flutter of white and bluish fluorescent tubes, stairways of crystalline light, black fronts, the brightness slowly hardened, the carriage stood still. I got out and was dumbstruck. Above the amphitheatrically sunken disc of the stop rose a familiar multilevel structure; I was still in the station, in another part of the same gigantic hall, swollen with white sweeps of planes. I moved toward the edge of that geometrically precise hollow—the carriage had already gone—and experienced another astonishment: I was not below, as it had seemed to me, I was in fact high up, some forty stories above the ribbons of walkways visible in the abyss, above the silver decks of the steadily gliding platforms, and long silent bodies were moving among them, and people were getting out of them through rows of flaps, as though those monsters, those chrome fish, were laying at regular intervals masses of black and colored roe. Above all this, far off, through the haze of distance, I saw golden words moving as if along an invisible line:

GLENIANA ROON RETURNING TODAY WITH A RECORDING OF MIMORPHIC REAL PAYS IN AN ORATORIO HOMAGE TO THE MEMORY OF RAPPER KERX POLITRY. TERMINAL DAILY REPORTS: TODAY IN AMMONLEE PETIFARGUE HAS BROUGHT ABOUT THE SYSTOLIZATION OF THE FIRST ENZOME, THE VOICE OF THE EMINENT GRAVIST WILL BE BROADCAST AT THE TWENTY-SEVENTH HOUR. ARRAKER IN THE LEAD. ARRAKER HAS REPEATED HIS SUCCESS AS THE FIRST OBLITERATOR OF THE SEASON IN THE TRANSVAAL STADIUM.

I walked away. So even the reckoning of time had changed. Struck by the light of the giant letters, which sped over the sea of heads like rows of burning tightrope walkers, the metallic fabrics of women’s dresses felted into sudden little flames. I walked without knowing it, and something inside me kept repeating: so even time had changed. That as good as finished me. With open eyes I saw nothing. I wanted only one thing: to get out of there, out of that infernal station, to find myself under the open sky, in free space, to see the stars, to feel the wind.

I was drawn by an avenue of elongated lights; in the translucent stone of the ceilings something was being written—the letters traced by a sharp flame enclosed in alabaster—TELETRANS TELEPORT TELETHON; through pointed-arch doors—but it was some impossible arch, wrenched from its foundations, like the negative of a rocket’s nose—I entered a hall roofed by a frozen golden conflagration. In the wall recesses—hundreds of booths; people ran into them, came hurrying out, threw torn strips onto the floor, not telegraph tape, it was something else, embossed with little nodules, others trampled those scraps. I wanted to leave, by mistake stepped into a dark interior, before I could withdraw something buzzed, a flash, as though from a photographer’s lamp, and from a metal-rimmed slit, like a letter box, there slid out a little folded sheet of glossy paper. I took it, opened it, a human head rose out of it, with thin lips not quite closed, slightly twisted, looking at me with narrowed eyes: it was I myself! I folded the paper in two and the plastic phantom vanished. I slowly parted the edges, nothing, wider, and it appeared again, as though sprung from nowhere, a head cut off from the torso, suspended above the sheet of paper, with a not especially intelligent expression. For a moment I looked at my own face—what was it, a three-dimensional photograph?

I put the sheet in my pocket and went out. The golden hell seemed to be sinking onto the heads of the crowd, a ceiling of fiery magma, unreal and yet breathing real fire, and nobody looked at it; preoccupied, they ran from one booth to another, green letters leapt in the depths, columns of digits descended narrow screens, other booths had, instead of doors, roller shutters that shot up at someone’s approach; at last I found the exit.

A corridor, curved, with a sloping floor, as sometimes in a theater, stylized shells blooming from the walls, and above words raced on: INFOR INFOR INFOR, without end.

The first time I saw an infor was on Luna, and I took it for an artificial flower.

I brought my face close to the pale-green calyx, which at once, before I had parted my lips, grew still in expectation.

“Which way can I get out?” I asked, not fully present.

“Where to?” a warm alto replied at once.

“To the city.”

“To which district?”

“It doesn’t matter.”

“To which level?”

“It doesn’t matter, I want to get out of the station!”

“Meridional, rasts: one hundred six, one hundred seventeen zero eight, zero two. Triduct, level AF, AG, circular myt level, twelve and sixteen, nadir level leads in every meridional direction. Central level—gliders, red local, white long-distance, A, B and W. Ulder level, direct, all escales from the third upward…” recited the woman’s voice singingly. I felt like tearing the microphone from the wall, the one bending toward my face with such solicitude.

I moved away. Idiot! Idiot! something ground in me with every step. EX EX EX EX repeated the sign moving overhead, edged with lemon mist. Could that be exit? A way out?

A huge sign. EXOTAL. I entered a violent current of warm air that set my trouser legs flapping. I found myself under the open sky. But the blackness of the night had retreated far into space, thrust back by a swarm of lights. An enormous restaurant—tables whose tops glowed in different colors, above them faces lit from below, and therefore a little uncanny, full of deep shadows. Low armchairs, black liquid with green foam in glasses, lanterns from which tiny sparks showered—no, rather glowworms, like swarms of burning moths. The chaos of lights blotted out the stars. When I lifted my head, I saw only black emptiness. And yet, a strange thing, in that moment its blind presence somehow filled me with reassurance.

I stood and looked. Someone brushed me in passing, I caught the scent of perfume, sharp and gentle at once, a couple passed, the girl turned to the man, her shoulders and breasts sunk in a fluffy cloud, she went into his embrace, they were dancing. They still dance, I thought. That was something at least. The couple took a few steps, a pale mercurial ring lifted them together with the other couples, their dark-red shadows moved beneath its huge slowly rotating disc; it rested on nothing, had no axis even, it turned in the music, suspended in the air. I went on among the tables. The soft plastic beneath my feet came to an end, rough rock touched it. Through a curtain of light I went inside, and found myself in a rocky grotto. As though ten or fifty Gothic naves raised from stalactites, veined deposits of pearly minerals embraced the mouths of caves, people sat in them, legs hanging into the void, wavering little flames burning between their knees, and below there stretched the undisturbed black surface of an underground lake, reflecting the convergences of rock. There too, on makeshift little rafts knocked together any which way, people were lying, all turned in one direction. I went down to the very water’s edge and saw on the far side, on the sand, a dancer. She seemed naked to me, but the whiteness of her body was unnatural. With small, wavering steps she ran down toward the water, when she reflected in it, she suddenly spread her arms, bowed her head—that was the end, but no one applauded, the dancer remained motionless for a few seconds, then went slowly along the shore, circling the uneven line of the bank. She was perhaps thirty paces from me when something happened to her. A moment before I could still see her smiling, exhausted face, suddenly it was as though something covered her, her figure shuddered and vanished.

“A plava for you, sir?” a polite voice sounded behind me. I turned around, nobody, only a streamlined little table walking on absurdly bent legs; it moved, the glasses of sparkling liquid standing in rows on its side trays trembled—one arm politely offered me that drink, the other was already reaching for a plate with a finger-hole, like a small concave palette—it was an automaton, I saw the smolder behind its central pane, the glow of its transistor heart.

I slipped past the insect arms stretching out so loyally, laden with delicacies I had disdained, and quickly left the artificial grotto, jaw clenched, as though I had suffered some incomprehensible insult. I crossed the whole width of the terrace, among the S-curves of tables, under avenues of lanterns, showered with the weightless dust of disintegrating, dying glowworms, black, golden. At the very edge, lined with stone, old stone, as if dimmed by yellowish lichen, at last a real wind touched me, clean, cool. Beside it stood an empty table. I sat down awkwardly, with my back to the people, looking into the night. Below there stretched a darkness shapeless and unexpected; only far away, very far away at its edges, thin wavering lights smoldered, strangely uncertain, as though it were not electricity, and farther still rapiers of light rose into the sky, cold, slender; I did not know whether they were buildings or some sort of pillars, I would have taken them for sheaves of searchlights if not for the delicate mesh that marked them—like a glass cylinder planted by its base in the ground, reaching the clouds, filled alternately with concave and convex lenses. They must have been of incredible height, around them a few lights drizzled, pulsing in such a way that now a wreath of orange, now of almost white radiance encircled them. That was all, that was what the city looked like; I tried to make out streets, to guess them, but that dark and somehow dead expanse below spread on all sides, not brightened by so much as a spark.

“Kol?…” I heard, perhaps not for the first time, but at first I didn’t take it as meant for me. Before I had properly turned around, the chair did it for me. A girl stood before me, perhaps twenty years old, in a blue that clung to her like something congealed, her shoulders and breasts disappearing in dark-blue down, which lower down grew ever more transparent. Her slim beautiful belly was like a sculpture in breathing metal. In her ears she had something shining, so large it covered the whole curve of them, small lips, smiling uncertainly—painted, and the insides of her nostrils red too—I had noticed that that was how most women wore themselves. Taking the back of the chair opposite me in both hands, she said:

“What’s up with you, kol!”

She sat down. I had the impression she was a little drunk.

“It’s dull here,” she went on after a moment. “Isn’t it? Shall we take ourselves somewhere, kol?”

“I’m not kol…” I began. She leaned her elbows on the table and moved her hand over the half-filled glass until the end of the gold chain wound round her fingers dipped into the liquid. As she did so she bent closer and closer. I could feel her breath. If she was drunk, it wasn’t with alcohol.

“How’s that?” she said. “You are. You must be. Everyone is kol. Want to? Shall we take ourselves?” So that at least I would know what it meant.

“All right,” I said.

She stood up. I stood up too from that horribly low chair.

“How do you do that?” she asked.

“What?”

She looked at my legs.

“I thought you were standing on tiptoe…”

I smiled without speaking. She came up to me, took my arm, and was surprised again.

“What have you got there?”

“Where, here? Nothing.”

“You’re singing,” she said, and tugged me gently. We went off among the tables, and I wondered what “you’re singing” could mean—maybe “you’re kidding”?

She led me toward a dark-gold wall, where a sign was glowing on it somewhat like a treble clef. When we were close to it, the wall opened. I felt a gust of hot air.

A narrow silver escalator flowed downward. We stood side by side. She did not reach my shoulder.

She had a catlike skull, black hair with a blue sheen, a profile perhaps too sharp, but she was pretty. Only those scarlet nostrils… She held me firmly with a thin hand, green nails digging into the thick material of my sweater. I smiled in spite of myself, with the corners of my mouth only, at the thought of where that sweater had been so far and how little it had had to do with women’s fingers.

Under a round vault that breathed lights—from pink to carmine, from carmine to pink—we came out onto the street. That is, I thought it was a street, but the darkness above us kept brightening now and then as if from an instantaneous dawn. Farther on long low silhouettes glided by, like cars, but I already knew there were no cars. They had to be something else. If I had been alone, I would have gone along that broad artery, because in the distance the words TO THE CENTER were glowing, but that surely did not mean the center of the city. In any case I let myself be led. However this adventure was to end, I had found a guide, and I thought—this time without anger—of the unfortunate fellow who now, three hours after my arrival, was surely looking for me by every infor in this station-city.

We passed several places that were emptying out, shop windows in which groups of mannequins kept acting out the same scene, over and over, and I would gladly have stopped to watch what they were doing, but the girl walked quickly, clicking in her little shoes, until at the sight of a neon face with pulsating blushes, endlessly licking itself with a comically protruding tongue, she cried:

“Oh, bons! Want a bons?”

“And do you?”

“I think so.”

We went into a small gleaming room. Instead of a ceiling it had long rows of blazing little flames, as if gas flames; heat burst down from above, so perhaps it really was gas. In the walls were small recesses with consoles; when we approached one, seats slid out from both sides of the wall; it looked as though they had first grown out of it still undeveloped, like buds, then flattened in the air and, hollowing, came to rest.

We sat opposite each other, the girl tapped with two fingers on a metal plate in the table, a nickel paw sprang from the wall, tossed a small plate before each of us and with two lightning movements hurled onto both portions of a whitish mass which, foaming, browned and set, while the plate itself darkened at the same time. Then the girl rolled it up—it was not a plate at all—like a pancake and began to eat.

“Oh,” she said, her mouth full, “I hadn’t even known how hungry I was!”

I did exactly as she had. A bons tasted like nothing I had ever eaten. It crackled under the teeth like a freshly baked roll, but at once crumbled and melted on the tongue; the brown mass inside was sharply seasoned.

I thought I would like bons.

“Another?” I asked when she had finished hers. She smiled, shaking her head.

On the way out, in the passage, she put both hands for a moment into a small tiled niche—something hissed in it. I imitated her. A tickling wind blew over my fingers; when I took them out, they were already dry and clean. Afterward we went up on a broad escalator. I did not know whether this was still the station, but I preferred not to ask. She led me into a small booth in the wall—it was not very light there, I had the impression some trains were running overhead, because the floor trembled. For a fraction of a second it went dark, something breathed deeply beneath us, as though a metal monster had let air out of its lungs, it grew light, the girl pushed the door. This perhaps really was a street. We were entirely alone on it.

Small clipped shrubs grew on both sides of the sidewalk; a little farther on low flat black machines stood crowded together; some man came out of the shadow, hid behind one, I didn’t see him open any door, he simply vanished suddenly, and the machine moved off with such force that it must have flattened him into the seat; I saw no houses at all; only a roadway smooth as a table, covered with bands of matte metal; at the intersections slitlike lights moved, suspended above the paving, orange and red; they looked a little like models of wartime searchlights.

“Where shall we take ourselves?” the girl asked. She was still holding my arm. She slowed. A red streak passed over her face.

“Wherever you want.”

“Then let’s go to my place. It isn’t worth taking a glider. It’s close.”

We went on ahead. There were still no houses in sight, and the wind running in from the darkness beyond the shrubs was such as if open space stretched here. Around the station, in the very center? That struck me as strange. It carried a faint scent of flowers, which I greedily drew into my nostrils. Bird cherry? No, it was not bird cherry.
