I

The white sedan speeding down the Koshu Highway as dusk began to fall was thick with the smell of cigarettes and blood.

In the back seat, wedged between a man in a gaudy tie and another in a patterned shirt, a long-haired woman sat slumped forward, head hanging. She wore nothing but torn jeans and a cheap T-shirt, and the hand flung limply at her side was grimy. When the car bounced slightly, a thick, sticky drop of blood plopped down onto the stomach of her yellow T-shirt. The men kept glancing at it, visibly uncomfortable. The young man driving shot uneasy looks at the woman in the rearview mirror every time he stopped at a light.

The sedan was following a Ford just ahead. Its polished black body caught the streetlamps and neon signs beginning to glow and shone with an oily luster. It was one of those violent sunsets. The glass windows of every building in Shinjuku had turned a blazing red, as if the whole district were bleeding from every inch of itself.

Before long the two cars entered a quiet residential neighborhood in Setagaya. Neat, tranquil streets stretched out around them, hard to believe this was the same Tokyo as crowded, chaotic Shinjuku. But after two or three turns, a high stone wall appeared abruptly, looming over everything around it. The white Shirakawa stone was stacked without the slightest gap, higher than a man, with barbed wire strung along the top. The cars stopped before the broad perimeter of wall, laid out as if to conceal something within, and after a moment an iron gate fitted with imposing surveillance cameras swung open.

Inside the wall stood a vast one-story mansion and a magnificent Japanese garden. In one corner there was even a small five-story pagoda. The cars moved slowly along the stone-paved drive and came to a stop near the tower.

At some point, men had appeared from nowhere and gathered around the cars as if surrounding them. All of them were young, hard-faced, and dressed alike in white shirts and matching ties.

A man leaped out of the Ford’s driver’s seat and opened the rear door. Gleaming shoes slid out first, followed by the long, narrow legs wearing them. The man who got out wore a black suit like he was coming back from a funeral. He was tall, with hollowed cheeks and a flattened ear that stood out sharply.

He jerked his chin, and at once the doors of the white sedan opened. The men inside began dragging out the still-limp woman. She had long arms and legs, and though she was not fat, there was a heavy solidity to her build; even with two of them, hauling her out did not look easy.

They dropped her onto the paving stones as carelessly as a piece of baggage. Her back gave a violent jerk. But she did not get up. She stayed face down.

“Hey. She alive?”

At the black-suited man’s words, the one in the patterned shirt hurriedly drove a hard kick into the woman’s shin.

A rasping groan rose from her. Still sprawled on the ground, like a crocodile, the woman slowly lifted her face. Hair clotted stiff with blood was pasted filthily to her cheek. Dark red blood ran from her nostrils, and beside her right eye the skin was split and swollen a deep, ugly black.

“God, she’s ugly.”

The man in the black suit gave a snorting laugh, and the men around him tittered along in eager agreement.

“Get her up.”

The man in the patterned shirt nodded, grabbed her left arm, and hauled her upward. She clung to him obediently and rose unsteadily to her feet. Then—

“Bwegh.”

A sound like a toad being crushed burst out. The patterned-shirt man’s body lifted clear off the ground, spun once, and slammed down onto the stone paving on his back.

The other men stirred. He let out a thin whistling breath and rolled up his eyes, then lay still.

This time the woman did not sway at all. She planted herself squarely where she stood. Opening her palms, spreading her arms, she looked the men over with a thick, contemptuous stare, then gaped her mouth open. Blood-smeared teeth showed.

One of the white-shirted men realized it was a grin, a smile meant to provoke them, and with his face twisted in rage he lunged straight at her.

“Sst.”

The sound slipped from between the woman’s teeth like air being torn apart. She bent her knees, dropped her head, and drove forward. The man took the full force of a boar-like headbutt to the stomach and went flying, crashing shoulder-first into the hard ground before he could even break his fall. A dull pop sounded as a bone came out of joint. A scream rose, upside down and broken.

There was a brief hesitation. Then the other men rushed her too.

“Sst.”

The man coming from her right took her fist straight in the Adam’s apple. Unable to cry out, unable even to breathe, he dropped onto his backside and thrashed his legs. The man charging from the left got the steel toe of her safety boot driven into his kneecap. Even through all the flying curses, the sickening crack of the joint giving way rang out clearly. Another man’s fist smashed hard into her cheek. She staggered, taking two or three stumbling steps, but recovered at once and brought both arms up to guard her head like a boxer.

“Sst.”

She blocked the next punch with her arm and at the same moment drove the metal-capped toe of her boot into the man’s groin. A scream. Shouting. A mass of white-shirted men came pouring in from the front of the garage.

Their cheeks were flushed red with anger and tension, but there was something dreamlike in their faces too, as if they still could not take in what was right before their eyes, could not believe what was happening. Amid all that rage and bewilderment, only the blood-smeared woman was smiling, teeth bared. Smiling as she punched and kicked without pause.

Through the thick bellow of curses came the barking of a dog. A black-and-tan mass shot through a gap in the crowd of white shirts. A huge Doberman in a thick black leather collar lunged straight at the woman. Hit head-on by forty kilos of charging dog, she went down. The men swarmed her at once, moving in to pin her. Human growls, canine growls, the sound of cloth tearing.

“Hey. Don’t kill her. She’s a gift for the old man. Handle her carefully.”

The man in the black suit watched, amused. The sun went down. Night fell.

In the middle of the elegant gravel garden, the woman was pinned to the ground by four forked restraining poles. The men holding them had not come away unscathed themselves; scowling resentfully, they glared down at the woman crawling in the dirt. Her clothes and hair were even more torn up now, but she did not move, did not groan. Still lying prone, she only breathed, quietly. Her shoes had been taken off, and the black underwear bared beneath her ripped T-shirt made her look like a wounded tiger. In the evening hush, the splash of koi in the pond echoed clearly.

“Yanagi, what’s that? A woman?”

A voice roughened by drink rang out. A man in a striped yukata sat on a leather sofa on the veranda facing the garden. Mid-sixties, perhaps. Bald, with a great belly jutting out and a short, boar-like neck sunk into his sloping shoulders.

“Seems to be female, at least. I thought she might be good for that job, so I brought her in.”

The black-suited man called Yanagi took an old orange leather wallet from his pocket and pulled out the driver’s license inside.

“Shindo, Yoriko... twenty-two. From Hokkaido.”

“What is she?”

“There was a commotion out front of the office, so I sent some young guys to see what was going on. This thing was out there raising hell. Seemed like a rare creature, so I scouted it.”

At that, the man on the veranda let out a grating laugh. Bright tattoo work showed through his loosened collar.

“Scouted it, did you.”

“We had to crack her two or three times with a beer bottle before she’d agree to accompany us. We’ll dig into who she is properly later, but the strength she’s got in a brawl is real. She can take on ten pros at once and still stand there like it’s nothing. If she weren’t female, I’d want her as one of my own.”

“She really is a woman, right? These days there are plenty that look like women and still have cocks.”

“We’ll check that too later. Thoroughly.”

A low, vulgar laugh rose from the men.

The woman—Shindo Yoriko—listened to the exchange in silence, her gaze fixed on the man on the veranda. She was not glaring at him, nor pleading with him. She was simply looking.

“While you’re checking, wash her right down to the depths and clean her up. Don’t put her in front of that one looking like this.”

With that, the man on the veranda rose with obvious reluctance and disappeared into the house. Young men in white shirts appeared at once like stagehands and began clearing away the ashtrays and chairs.

“Hey. Bring the hose. Turn on the water.”

Grabbing a hose dragged over from somewhere in the garden, Yanagi began splashing Shindo with jets of cold water.

“What’s this, quiet now? Finally given up?”

The hard stream struck her bloodied face full on. Shindo opened her mouth and gulped it down greedily, as if it tasted good.

Yanagi laughed.

“You’ve got some guts. Or are you too stupid to understand what’s happening?”

The water stopped. The blood had been washed from her face, but the fresh bruises she had taken in the fight had swollen so badly it was hard to imagine what her features had looked like before.

“If you’re stupid, so much the better. Listen, Yoriko-chan. Starting today, you work at this mansion. If you don’t like it, I’ll kill you right here. Understand?”

Still silent, Shindo gave a small nod.

“Good. Everything up to now was just play. From this moment on, if you go against me, you die. Got it?”

Yanagi eased open his suit jacket. A dagger in a plain white-wood sheath was tucked into his waistband. Shindo nodded again.

“You lot, back off.”

At Yanagi’s signal, the men holding the restraining poles slowly drew the metal prongs away from Shindo’s body.

“Stand up.”

Without even swaying, Shindo rose cleanly to her feet. The skin visible through her torn clothes was covered in fresh cuts and bruises, but she showed no sign of enduring pain.

“You heard what I said. Strip. Show me if you really are female.”

In silence, Shindo pulled off the T-shirt, already no better than a rag, and dropped it on the ground.

“The underwear too.”

Without the slightest hesitation, she removed her black bra and tossed it aside. A few of the men around her grinned, but more imposing than the breasts laid bare were the beautifully split abdominal muscles beneath them. The arms the T-shirt had concealed were packed with muscle too, thick and bulging. It was a body like one of the guardian kings at Todai-ji’s Great South Gate, as if carved from the trunk of a giant tree.

“The bottoms too. That’s the important part.”

Without taking her eyes off Yanagi, Shindo began undoing her button-fly jeans. The large curve of her rear wrapped in black underwear came into view, along with taut thighs. The men’s eyes all fixed there.

In the next instant, Shindo swung the wet jeans up and lashed them across Yanagi’s face like a whip.

“Shit!”

The wet, heavy cloth struck his head hard. In the split second before Yanagi could knock it away, Shindo launched herself straight forward. She threw herself against his chest as if clinging to him, caught the lapel of his suit with her left hand, and with her right pulled the dagger from his waist. At the same time, Yanagi moved. He seized her by the hair, yanked her in, and with a smooth outer-leg reap slammed her to the ground. He immediately tried to kick away the hand holding the dagger, but Shindo rolled clear and came back up at once.

“Get her!”

At someone’s shouted command, the Doberman from before obeyed its master, baring white fangs as it lunged at her half-naked body. Shindo instantly snatched up the jeans from the ground, whipped them around her arm, and thrust it out in front of the dog.

With a growl, the dog clamped down on her arm through the wet denim. It bit with enough force to tear flesh and break bone in an ordinary person, jaws locked with the fierce resolve never to let go. The more she tried to shake it off, the more stubbornly the dog held on.

The beast met the eyes of the beastlike woman.

Shindo bared her teeth and barked back at it, a harsh, savage sound, asserting which of them was stronger as a living thing. The dog did not release her, but for an instant fear flickered in its large wet black eyes. Then, with forty kilos of dog hanging off her and slowing her movements, restraining poles came thrusting in from every direction at her torso, forcing her back onto the ground. At the command to stop, the dog immediately let go and returned to its master.

Straightening his disheveled hair with his fingers, Yanagi spat on the ground. Then he planted a leather shoe on the wrist of the hand still gripping the dagger.

“Are you out of your mind? Everybody here is real yakuza. Aren’t you scared of dying?”

Yanagi’s cruel-looking face wore the smile of a man enjoying himself immensely.

“Don’t talk to me like you know me, you piece of trash.”

“Oh? So you can talk.”

Yanagi ground his shoe down harder on her wrist. The dagger clattered from her palm.

“You... why didn’t you stab the dog?”

Shindo twisted her face and spat out a gob of blood-tinged saliva.

“Nishi. Bring that dog over here.”

A white-shirted man called Nishi nodded and led over the Doberman, now attached to a leash clipped to its collar. Yanagi picked up the dagger.

“I’ll say it one more time. Starting today, you work here. If you say no, I’ll kill you right now.”

He pointed the tip of the blade at Shindo’s face.

“Do what you want. Better dead than doing what you say.”

“I see.”

With that, Yanagi grabbed the collar of the dog, which had been sitting there meekly wagging its tail, and pressed the blade to its throat. Shindo’s eyes flew wide.

“—Stop.”

A thin whine rose from it. A strong creature that, if it chose, could kill a human with a single bite now trembled, its own life under threat, unable even to resist.

“Can’t hear you.”

The Doberman’s tail tucked tight, and its high, fearful whine grew louder.

“Stop!”

Its great ears flattened as if crushed, and its black eyes opened wide and round.

“So it’s fine if I kill it, then. This one will die because of you. Poor thing.”

He pressed the blade in. The dog’s wet eyes stared fixedly at Shindo.

“I’ll do what you say.”

“What was that?”

“I’ll do what you say!”

At once, Yanagi let go of the collar. The dog gave a small whine, then sank sadly to the ground and looked up at him from beneath its brow. There was no resentment in those eyes, no anger.

“So you can crush a man’s balls, but the poor doggie’s too much for you? You’re kind, Yoriko-chan. Listen carefully: if you ever go against me again, or try to run from here, I’ll cut its belly open and skin it alive. Right in front of you.”

With that, Yanagi slipped the bloodless dagger back into the sheath at his waist.

On the connecting corridor, polished bright enough to reflect a face, Shindo sat alone in seiza. Her reddish-brown hair had been pulled back with an office rubber band, and she wore baggy black pants and a white shirt that did not fit. Gauze and adhesive bandages had been plastered all over her face, covering almost everything but her eyes, nose, and mouth, so that she looked like a botched mummy. It was less treatment for her injuries than a way of simply hiding them.

While she changed into clothes borrowed from one of the white-shirted men—a live-in junior member—Yanagi had forced a one-sided explanation on her. This was the residence of Genzo Naiki, chairman of the Naiki-kai, a direct affiliate of the Okitsu-gumi, the largest yakuza syndicate in Kanto, and Yanagi served as one of the Naiki-kai’s assistant underbosses. From cleaning to cooking, the estate was run entirely by carefully selected young men from subordinate organizations, but for one particular job they absolutely needed a woman, and so they had “scouted” Shindo.

He had told her he would explain what kind of job it was later, and to sit and wait. More than thirty minutes had passed. It was dark around her, and there was not another person in sight. The nearest stone wall was about twenty meters away. If she wanted to, she could escape at once. But Shindo could not move.

The Doberman’s thin cry still clung to her ears, despairing, resigned.

The dog had done nothing wrong. No matter how vile the human who kept it, the dog had done nothing wrong. Those eyes... those eyes, full of despair, and yet offering no resistance to the one holding its life in his hands. That pitiful whimper. Dogs were all like that. For some reason, they gave their loyalty even to hopeless human beings.

She let out a breath, and pain twinged faintly around her ribs. It did not feel broken, but there might be a crack. She went back over everything that had happened that day.

One of her two part-time jobs was delivering meals to individual customers, so she had ridden her bicycle into Shinjuku and worked until evening. Then she went back to her apartment in Shin-Okubo, left the bicycle, washed off her sweat, changed clothes, and headed out to Shinjuku again, this time on foot. If all she wanted was food, she could have stayed near home, but she had thought she might catch a movie too. But almost as soon as she entered Kabukicho, an obviously drunk punk, laughing loudly, slapped her on the ass as he brushed past. She spun around at once, seized him from behind by the collar, swept his legs, and drove him face-first into the asphalt. It was almost funny that his buddy walked several meters before noticing, but he soon came hurrying back in a panic and threw a wide, looping punch. She could have dodged it if she had wanted to, but a crowd was already gathering, and she figured it would be less trouble if she let herself get hit once, so she deliberately took it in the face. Whether because of the alcohol or because he had no skill to begin with, the punch barely even stung. She caught his wrist from below and twisted it sharply inward toward his body. A nasty grinding sensation ran through her hand, and neatly enough, the wrist joint came out in one go. A scream. Panicking, the man started hammering wildly at her stomach with his good hand, so she let go, kicked him away, and opened some distance. The onlookers had multiplied in no time. The first man, the one she had dropped on the asphalt, was writhing and groaning with blood pouring from his nose. She still wanted to hit them a few more times, but they were unsatisfying opponents, and it would be a nuisance if someone called the police, so she decided to leave. But then several men who were obviously bad news emerged from the crowd and blocked her path. More, she thought—and at that very moment a kick came flying at her. It was a high kick, a lot better than the drunk punk’s. Yelling, “Fucking bitch! Pig!” he threw kick after kick at her; she slipped the first two or three and caught the fourth. He was short. She stamped on his supporting foot to pin it in place, hoisted the trapped leg onto her shoulder, and dropped hard into a crouch, forcing the hip joint open into a one-hundred-and-eighty-degree split. The scream that burst out of him sounded like a newborn baby’s. When she straightened up, she felt a shock in her back. Probably a flying kick. When she turned, the man who had kicked her was stumbling as he landed, and she immediately drove a high kick into the wide-open side of his neck. It made a nice sharp sound. Then pain flared in her side, and when she looked, she realized she had been struck across the ribs with an empty wine bottle. That was probably the injury throbbing now. In a place this cramped, once someone brought in a weapon, the odds turned against her. Someone among the spectators might get hurt too. She looked for a way out, but the man with the bottle drew back to swing again, so she slammed her fist straight into his face. Through the gaps in the ring of gawkers around her, she could feel murderous intent closing in from every side. More were coming. More, coming to be beaten by me. A smile came naturally to her face. If only this had been a wider place, if nobody got in the way, she could have taken every last one of them. She cursed the city’s narrowness, its crowds. Then a hard blow struck the back of her head. If Yanagi’s earlier words were to be believed, it had been a beer bottle. Another blow followed immediately, and everything blacked out.

A night wind, chilly for May, swept through. Sitting motionless on the cold, hard corridor, Shindo drew one more deep breath and ran a hand over her throbbing ribs and the back of her head.
