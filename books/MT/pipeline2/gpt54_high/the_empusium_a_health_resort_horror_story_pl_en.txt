1.

Guesthouse for Gentlemen

The view is obscured by billows of steam from the locomotive, now drifting across the platform. We have to peer out from beneath them if we are to see everything, let ourselves be dazzled for a moment by the grey mist, until the sight that emerges from this trial is sharp, piercing, all-seeing.

Then we shall see the slabs of the platform, squares threaded through with blades of puny little plants—a space bent at any cost on preserving order and symmetry.

A moment later a left shoe appears on them, brown, leather, no longer new, and at once another joins it, the right; this one seems even more worn—its toe slightly scuffed, the leather in a few small places showing lighter specks. For a moment the shoes stand there undecided; then the left one moves forward. The movement briefly reveals a black cotton sock beneath the trouser leg. The black recurs in the skirts of an unbuttoned napped-wool coat; the day is warm. A brown leather suitcase is held by a small hand, pale and bloodless; under the weight the veins have tightened and now point back to their sources, somewhere deep in the guts of the sleeve. Beneath the coat a flannel jacket flashes into view, not of the best quality and, what is more, somewhat rumpled by the long journey. It bears tiny pale specks of indeterminate dirt—the scales of the world. The white collar of the shirt, one of those detachable ones, has evidently been changed quite recently, for its whiteness is fresher than the shirt’s own and contrasts with the earthy colour of the traveller’s face. Light eyes, brows, and lashes make that face look unhealthy. Set against the intensely red western sky, the whole figure gives the disquieting impression of having come here, to these melancholy mountains, from the world beyond.

The traveller makes for the main hall of a station surprisingly large for such a mountain district, together with the other arrivals; what sets him apart from them is that he walks without haste, even reluctantly, and that no one greets him, no one has come to meet him. He sets his suitcase down on the worn tiled floor and pulls warm gloves onto his hands. After a moment one of them—the right—rises to his mouth, the hand inside rolled into a little tube, to receive a parade of short, dry coughs.

The young man hunches over and searches his pocket for a handkerchief. For a moment his fingers touch the place where, in the pocket beneath the fabric of the coat, the passport lies hidden. If we focus our attention for a moment, we shall see the fanciful handwriting of some Galician official who has carefully filled in the blanks on the document: Mieczysław Wojnicz, Catholic, student at the Lwów Polytechnic, born 1889, eyes blue, height متوسط, face long, hair fair.

This Wojnicz is now crossing the main hall of the station at Dittersbach, not far from Waldenburg, walking uncertainly through the lofty, gloomy room where echo surely lodges on the highest cornices, and he can feel himself being carefully scrutinised by someone’s eyes from behind the ticket windows in the waiting room. He checks the time on the big clock—it is late; this is the last train from Breslau. He hesitates for a moment, then steps out in front of the station building, to let himself at once be embraced by the broad arms of the uneven, ragged line of the mountain horizon.

It is mid-September, yet here, as the newcomer notes with surprise, summer has long since passed and the first fallen leaves lie on the ground. The last few days must have been rainy, for a light mist still fills the landscape almost completely, sparing only the dark lines of the streams. He can feel in his lungs that he is high up; that is good for his body, worn out by illness. Wojnicz stands on the station steps, eyeing his shoes with their thin leather soles suspiciously—he will have to think about winter boots. In Lwów the asters and zinnias were still in bloom, and no one was thinking of autumn at all. Here, by contrast, the high horizon makes everything darker, and the colours seem more vivid, almost vulgar. At that moment he is seized by a familiar melancholy, the kind peculiar to people convinced of their own imminent death. He feels that the world around him is scenery painted on a paper screen, that he could thrust a finger into this monumental landscape and bore a hole through it, straight into nothingness. And that it—that nothingness—would begin to pour out of there like a flood and in the end reach him too, seize him by the throat. He has to shake his head to rid himself of the image. It shatters into droplets and falls onto the leaves. Fortunately, a clumsy conveyance like a trap comes trundling towards him along the road. In it sits a skinny, freckled boy in strange attire. He is wearing something like a military jacket of hard-to-place provenance—for it resembles neither a Prussian uniform, which in a place like this would have been understandable, nor any other—and a military forage cap set at a jaunty angle on his head. Without a word he stops in front of Wojnicz and, mumbling something under his breath, takes his luggage.

“How do you do, good man?” Wojnicz asks politely in his schoolroom German, but waits in vain for an answer; the boy pulls his cap down over his eyes and impatiently points him to a seat in the trap.

And at once they set off. First through the little town over the cobbles, then along a road that, in the falling dark, leads them on a winding track through the forest between the steep mountain slopes. All the while they are accompanied by the murmur of a nearby stream and by its smell, which always unsettles Wojnicz so: the scent of damp undergrowth, rotting leaves, stones forever wet, water. He tries to ask the driver something, anything that might start a connection—how much longer they will be riding, how he recognised him at the station, what his name is—but the other does not even look back and says nothing. The gas lantern fixed to the boy’s right half-lights his face, which in profile resembles the muzzle of a mountain rodent, a marmot, and Wojnicz concludes that the driver must be either deaf or insolently rude.

At last, after some three-quarters of an hour, they emerge from the forest’s shadow and drive into a surprisingly flat valley, an unexpected plateau here among the wooded mountains. The sky is fading, but you can still make out that high, unsettling horizon, which to every newcomer from the lowlands seems to rise to the throat.

“Görbersdorf,” says the driver suddenly, in a boyish, unexpectedly high voice.

But Wojnicz can see nothing, nothing except a dense wall of darkness peeling away in whole sheets from the mountain slopes. Only when his eyes grow used to it does a viaduct suddenly loom before them, and they pass beneath it into the village; beyond it appears the enormous bulk of a red-brick building, then other, smaller buildings, and a street, and even two gas lamps. The brick building turns out to be a colossus, growing before his eyes, and the motion of the trap draws rows of lit windows out of the dark. The light in them is a dirty yellow. Wojnicz cannot take his eyes off this unexpected triumphal sight and for a long while keeps turning to look back at it, until it sinks into the darkness like a great steamer.

Now the trap turns into a narrow side lane along the stream and crosses a little bridge where the wheels raise a clatter like gunshots. At last it stops before a large wooden building with a most peculiar architecture, calling to mind a house made of matchsticks—so many verandas, little balconies, and terraces. A pleasant light burns in the windows. Beneath the first-floor windows gleams a beautiful inscription in Schwabacher letters, cut from thick sheet metal:

Gästehaus für Herren

Wojnicz gets down from the trap with relief and draws deep into his lungs a great draught of this new air, said to cure even the gravest conditions. Perhaps he does it too soon, though, for he is suddenly seized by a coughing fit so severe that he has to brace himself against the railing of the bridge. And then, as he coughs, he feels the cold and the unpleasant slipperiness of rotting wood, and the good first impression vanishes. He cannot stop the violent spasms of his diaphragm, and an overpowering fear comes over him—that in another moment he will suffocate, that this will be the last attack. He tries to fend off panic, as Dr Sokołowski advised him, to think of a meadow full of flowers, of warm sunshine. He tries very hard, though his eyes are watering and his face has flushed with blood. It seems to him he is about to cough out his soul.

But then he feels a grip on his shoulder—and a tall, solidly built, grizzled man holds out a hand. Through his tears, Wojnicz catches sight of his rosy, healthy face.

“There, there, sir. Let us pull ourselves together,” the man says with such confidence and such a broad smile that the guest, half-dead from coughing, feels an urge to cling to him and let himself be taken to bed like a child. Yes, exactly. A child. A bed. Somewhat abashed, he flings his arms around the man’s neck and lets himself be led through a hall smelling of spruce smoke and up the soft stairs cushioned with a runner. All this evokes some distant association with wrestling, with that manly sport in which hard bodies press against one another, rub, strike, not to do each other harm but, quite the contrary, under the pretext of struggle to show one another tenderness and attachment. He yields to the strong hands, lets himself be led to a room upstairs, sat on the bed, stripped of coat and sweater.

Wilhelm Opitz—for that is how the man introduces himself, pointing a finger at his own chest—covers him with a woollen throw and receives from a pair of hands that appear for a moment in the crack of the door a mug of hot, savoury broth. As Mieczysław drinks in small sips, Wilhelm Opitz raises a finger (and at that moment Wojnicz realises what an essential part of Wilhelm that finger is) and says in soft, faintly comical German:

“I wrote to Professor Sokołowski telling him to have you stop over in Breslau. The journey is too long and tiring. I said so.”

The broth spreads a pleasant warmth through Wojnicz’s body, and the poor fellow does not even notice when he falls asleep. We stay with him a little while longer, listening to his calm breathing, glad that his lungs have quieted.

Our attention is drawn now to a streak of light, thin as a blade, entering the room from the corridor and coming to rest on the porcelain chamber pot beneath the bed. The gaps between the floorboards draw us—and there we disappear.

At a quarter to seven, the sound of a trumpet woke Wojnicz, and for a long moment he could not tell where he was. The melody was unmistakably being played out of tune, which amused him and put him in a good mood. It seemed familiar, but in the way of things so simple they are almost genius. Things that seem to have always been there, and always will be.

Mieczysław Wojnicz was made up of ailments, and it was not even he himself who knew them best, but his father, January Wojnicz, a retired civil servant and landowner. He managed these ailments with great expertise, gravity, and tact, treating the property entrusted to his care in the form of a son with the utmost responsibility and—obviously—love, though a love devoid of all sentimentality and all those “womanish feelings” he so detested.

One of those ailments, in the shaping of which he himself had played a certain part, was his son’s excessive fear that he was being watched. Thus the younger Wojnicz devoted a great deal of attention to the gaze of others, checking whether it might not be following him from around a corner, from the corner of a room, through a window where the curtain had folded back, through the keyhole. The father’s caution and suspicion had turned into the son’s obsession. He felt as if another person’s gaze were something sticky, clinging to him like the soft, hideous mouth-opening of a leech. That was why, in every room where he was to spend the night, he carefully inspected the window curtains, plugged the keyhole with a pellet of paper, checked for possible holes in the wall, gaps between the boards, even looked behind pictures. In boarding houses and hotels, being spied on is not so implausible after all—once, when he and his father were staying in a Warsaw hotel during one of those health visits to a specialist, the younger Wojnicz discovered a neat little peephole in the wall, clumsily disguised by the rich pattern of the wallpaper, and of course sealed it with a pellet of bread; and when in the morning he tried to figure out from where, and by whom, the guests might be observed, he discovered that behind the wall there was a service staircase used by the hotel staff. And yet! So it was no obsession. People spy on one another. They adore it, looking at a person when he is unaware. Judging, comparing. A person being watched like that becomes defenceless, becomes an unsuspecting, helpless victim.

On waking, he immediately set about writing a message to his father to reassure him. It was only a matter of a few simple words, and yet it did not come easily; his hand was numb and weak. So he focused all his attention on his hand as it guided the tip of the pencil across the cream paper of his leather-bound notebook. This movement fascinates us; we like it. It recalls the same winding lines and spiral ornaments that earthworms burrow underground and bark beetles gnaw into tree trunks. Wojnicz sat up in bed, propped against two enormous pillows. Before him he had an ingenious contrivance—a kind of little table without legs. Its underside was a cushion stuffed with peas, so that it easily settled onto the writer’s lap.

First came two digits, forming “13”, then a straight stroke and a cross—“IX”—and after them four more digits making up “1913”. Then, out of a series of loops, the word “Görbersdorf” emerged, traced over several times to make it bolder. The umlaut was treated with special reverence. Then the pencil set off across the paper, steady and tireless. The graphite rasped, the paper yielding beneath the rounded shapes of the letters.

The room was modest but comfortably furnished. Two windows looked out onto the street and the little river in front of the house, though the view was screened by crocheted thread curtains. Beneath one of the windows stood a round table and two comfortable upholstered chairs, already somewhat worn, almost armchairs—a pleasant little reading corner, for anyone who fancied it. To the left of the door stood a bed with a beautifully decorated wooden headboard; beside it, a wardrobe. The dressing table had been placed to the right of the door. The walls were covered with broad pale-blue striped fabric wallpaper, which made the room seem taller and more spacious than it really was. On the wall hung prints from exotic parts: a group of hares and a pack of hyenas.

Mieczysław Wojnicz briefly described in Polish his impressions of the journey, converting one thousand nine hundred feet into metres (it came to almost five hundred) and marking the numbers on a sketch map tracing his route from Lwów all the way here. His brief comments dealt mainly with the meals en route. Beside the place marked “Wrocław/Breslau” he added: “Yellow pumpkin soup; for the second course, purée with cracklings, cabbage, and a cutlet exactly like our schabowy. For dessert, vanilla pudding with meringue and blackberry compote, very tasty.” Underneath he added: “Payment 5 marks.” He had promised his father that he would write him a few words every day, preferably about how he was feeling, but in truth he did not know what he felt, so he preferred sending recipes or geographical information back to Lwów.

There was a soft knock at the door, and before he could say “come in,” a leather boot slipped into the gap between frame and door and gently pushed it open; behind it appeared the black folds of a skirt, the lace of an apron, and a breakfast tray, quickly set down on the table. The boot, the lace, and the apron vanished as quickly as they had appeared, and the dazed Wojnicz had time only to pull the throw up over himself and stammer some greeting and thanks. He was so hungry that the only thing that interested him was the food.

In a moment he would describe it in his notebook: two hard-boiled eggs in lovely faience egg cups, covered with little hen-shaped caps; slices of smoked sheep’s cheese garnished with parsley; a little ball of the yellowest butter served on a horseradish leaf; a bowl of fragrant lard with a little spreading knife; sliced radishes; a basket of rolls of various kinds, light and dark; apricot jam in a glass dish; a mug of thick cocoa and a little pot of coffee.

After the full stop, the notebook snapped shut, and Wojnicz ate with relish everything on the tray; fortified by the meal, he got up. Draping the throw over his shoulders, he padded across to his suitcase, from which he took a neatly folded set of underwear, then set about his ablutions. And as he wiped his face with the towel, steeped in the guesthouse’s omnipresent scent of conifers, the image of their house in the country rose before his eyes as vivid as life itself, and with it the laundry dried in winter in the attic, which Gliceria carried up there in buckets whenever it was pouring outside. Back came the attic, always full of dust, and the view from its little windows known as bull’s-eyes—the fields and the little park—and the bitter smell of rotting stalks of tomatoes, maize, pole beans. And by the laws of some not entirely intelligible synaesthesia, that image passed into bodily sensation: the roughness of clothes, the stiffness of collars, the angularity of freshly ironed trousers, and the constriction of a hard leather belt. And it was there, in the attic, whenever he could, whenever he was left alone and for a moment far from his father’s drill, that he stripped naked and wrapped himself in a satin tablecloth edged with soft fringe, and felt how deliciously that fringe brushed his calves and thighs, and thought how wonderful it would be if they could, like the ancient Greeks, go about in such tablecloth chitons. And now, remembering that satin toga, he dressed and was glad that at last he felt strong and rested.

We are witness as clothes appear layer by layer on his slender body, until at last his figure—altogether unlike yesterday’s, coughing and grey-faced—stands with a hand on the doorknob, eyes closed, imagining how he would look to someone who might be watching him now. He looks good—a slim young man with fair hair and delicate features, in grey dress trousers and a brown wool jacket. A moment later he opens the door decisively.

No, we do not consider this an obsession, at most a harmless oversensitivity. People should get used to being watched.

Wojnicz came downstairs around ten, for he had an appointment at the Kurhaus for examinations.

Because the windows were small and set far apart in the walls, a half-light reigned throughout the house—typical of mountain architecture. There stood an oval table covered with a thick patterned cloth, a sofa, and several chairs; against the wall was a piano, whose infrequent use was betrayed by a few finger marks on the gleaming lid and a stack of yellowed sheet music. A small hanging shelf beside it was full of books about the region, local ski routes, and monuments. In the enormous glass-fronted cabinet, a beautiful porcelain service gleamed white, decorated with sentimental cobalt-blue scenes—shepherds and sheep.

“Gemütlich,” Mieczysław whispered to himself, pleased to have recalled a certain German word he was especially fond of. His own language lacked anything like it. Cosy? Nice?

Doctor Sokołowski’s words, too, came back to him, from the time when the doctor had first begun treating him and wrestling with his apathy: life must be made appetising. Yes, appetising—that was a better word than “gemütlich,” Wojnicz thought, because it applied not only to surroundings, but to everything else as well—to someone’s voice, the way they spoke, the way they settled into an armchair, tied a scarf at the throat, the way biscuits were arranged on a plate. He ran a finger over the table covered in soft olive-green plush, and only after a moment did he notice, to his alarm, a slim man in the armchair by the window, with pronounced birdlike features and wire-rimmed spectacles on a prominent nose. He was enveloped in a cloud of cigarette smoke. Wojnicz’s hand sprang away from the plush as if burned and, embarrassed, vanished into the clasp of the other. The man, equally disconcerted at having his solitude discovered, rose and introduced himself rather formally, in German, with a curious Silesian accent:

“Walter Frommer. From Breslau.”

Wojnicz slowly and distinctly pronounced his own first and last name, perhaps hoping the other man would remember them at once. They spoke for a while, and Frommer managed to tell him that he came to Görbersdorf regularly for treatment and had been here, on and off, for three years now. Every so often he went back briefly to Breslau, but as soon as he did, his condition worsened again.

“You know, the city of Breslau lies on water. In spring swarms of mosquitoes hang above the buildings—small, but extraordinarily venomous—and people get rheumatism. In summer it’s impossible to sit in the garden, which is why government officials stay there only for a short spell of a few years, and then return to better places. Breslau is a city one passes through”—a sadness entered his voice, as if he felt sorry for the city—“It’s all that water, it creeps everywhere... I don’t bear it well...” He began to cough. “There, you see? I cough at the very thought of it.”

Wojnicz’s gaze strayed towards the window, beyond which a merry party was just passing, every few moments bursting into laughter. He thought they were laughing in Polish, though he could not quite explain the impression to himself. From that distance no words could be heard.

“Are you also thinking of moving into the Kurhaus?” he asked Frommer.

He had thought the question might bring at least a faint smile to the man’s face, but Frommer took it seriously.

“God forbid,” he protested. “There are too many people there. You can’t see anything from there. You won’t find anything out, and you won’t learn anything. Life in a crowd is worse than prison.”

Well, by now Wojnicz thought he had probably formed an opinion of Walter Frommer already: an oddball.

They both seemed equally shy, for for a moment they stood facing each other in awkward silence, each waiting for the other to utter some conventional phrase. They were rescued from the impasse by Wilhelm Opitz, their host.

“I hope I’m not interrupting an animated conversation,” he said, and for a moment Wojnicz wondered whether Wilhelm was mocking them or really was that inattentive. But Wilhelm took him firmly by the arm and led him toward the door.

“Forgive me, but I must hand the young man over to Doctor Semperweiß’s watchful eye. Our guest arrived here in a lamentable condition.”

Frommer muttered something indistinct, went back to his place by the window, and sat down in exactly the same position as before. As if he were employed there as a smoking piece of furniture.

“Doctor Frommer is a little odd, but he’s a decent man. Like everyone in my guesthouse,” Wilhelm said in that dialect of his which was becoming more and more agreeable to Wojnicz’s ear, as they paused on the steps outside the house. “The boy will take you to Doctor Semperweiß. Be careful with him—he doesn’t like people from the East. In fact, he doesn’t like anyone. It’s a great pity there’s nobody here like Doctor Brehmer,” he added thoughtfully when, a moment later, they were standing by the little bridge.

Wojnicz watched the fog gather itself into strange ribbons and drift upward like smoke.

“Do you happen to know Doctor Sokołowski?” he asked.

Wilhelm’s face brightened at once.

“Of course I knew him when I was a child. He was friends with my father, who worked for him. Everyone here works at the Kurhäuser. How is he getting on?”

Well, Wojnicz couldn’t really say. He knew only that Sokołowski worked at a clinic in Warsaw and lectured in Lwów from time to time. His father had taken him for consultations whenever Sokołowski was in town. And it was thanks to him that he had ended up here.

“Is he still so thin?” Willi asked.

Thin? No, not thin. Professor Sokołowski was a stocky, heavyset man. But Wojnicz did not have to answer that surprising question, because out of the wisps of mist emerged Rajmund, the coachman from the day before, a gangling boy in his teens, whom Wilhelm greeted in a rather peculiar way: with a light smack on the head. The boy accepted it as a perfectly natural, friendly gesture.

The two of them now walked down along the stream toward the centre of the village. Rajmund chattered away eagerly, but he spoke in such a strange dialect that Wojnicz understood very little. He looked with interest at the handsome houses lining the road and the workmen repairing the electric lines. Rajmund asked Mieczysław whether he knew what it was—electricity.

Then they bowed to two elderly women in wide skirts sitting on a bench in front of one of the houses.

“Frau Weber and Frau Brecht,” Rajmund said with an ironic smile, and that, at least, Wojnicz understood.

A little later the boy proudly pointed out Doctor Brehmer’s sanatorium, the same building Wojnicz had seen the previous evening, though now it seemed even more imposing, especially as the fog had almost disappeared and, somewhere high above the valley, the September sun shone generously.

Rajmund vanished as soon as he had led Wojnicz to the right door in the wide corridor. Now a nurse took charge of him, her eyes set off by red puffiness. A brief, polite smile exposed for a moment her large, yellowed teeth, whose colouring matched the worn gilt of the watch on a chain pinned to her apron. Above the pocket her name was embroidered: Sydonia Patek.

Wojnicz had to sit out his time in the waiting room outside the doctor’s consulting room; the doctor had not yet returned from his rounds. So his fingers reached for the illustrated magazines laid out for patients, but his eyes found no comfort there; they could not settle on the blackletter. To his surprise, though, he found a booklet in Polish, and at once, as if by a miracle, his gaze relaxed upon the words of his native tongue:

In Prussian Silesia, a quarter of a mile from the Czech border and eleven miles south-west of Wrocław, in a long valley stretching from east to west between the Riesengebirge and the Adlergebirge, there lies, in the district of Waldenburg, on the little river Sztejna, the charming village of Görbersdorf, famous for several decades as a mountain climatic resort for sufferers from chest ailments.

Görbersdorf stands 570 metres above sea level, in the belt which medical science calls “free from consumption.” The mountains surrounding it rise to 900 metres. They shelter the village and its healing establishments from the winds, which reach it already weakened; hence the stillness of the air in Görbersdorf, such as is rarely found in any valley.

He read no further, but folded the booklet in half and slipped it into his pocket. His attention was now drawn to a glass-fronted case where a wooden human torso stood—without head, arms, or legs, its chest and belly open, displaying internal organs painted in different colours. Wojnicz went up to the little wooden trunk to look at the lungs. They were smooth and clean, polished, gleaming with varnish. They resembled the fleshy petals of some monstrous flower, or fungi growing on tree bark. How wonderfully they fitted the chest, how they had reconciled their airy nature with the cage of ribs. He studied them carefully, trying to peer beneath the pointed little corner where they joined the other organs, all curled up and painted in various colours. And yet he was disappointed. Perhaps he had expected something new, something he did not yet know. The answer to the mystery. Why he was ill. And why others were not.

When he returned to his seat, a familiar unease came over him, that irritation which always brought on the same bodily response—sweating. He would have to undress and expose his body to a stranger’s gaze. And panic: how he was to hide his shameful condition from the doctor. What he would have to say so as not to touch on all those matters so painfully sensitive to him. How to slip away. He had practised it so many times already.

When the doctor burst into the waiting room, he did not even glance at him—he crossed it in quick strides, the skirts of his white coat flying behind him. With a motion of his hand he signalled for the patient to get up. Mieczysław followed him, almost at a trot, into a large consulting room with an enormous window, full of glass-fronted cabinets, assorted medical apparatus, and strange chairs. It somehow did not surprise Wojnicz to see a gun propped against the doctor’s desk—a large one, not a hunting gun at all, more like a Winchester, with a beautifully polished stock. Without turning round, the doctor told him to sit down, and that way Wojnicz felt safely hidden behind the desk, as if in a trench.

He handed the doctor Professor Sokołowski’s letter of introduction, but the doctor merely glanced at it, plainly more interested in the body sitting before him. The young man felt uneasy, and the reason was the way the other man looked at him. As though he did not see Mieczysław Wojnicz, a patient from far-off Lwów, but only the body itself—an object, something mechanical. First, without the slightest embarrassment, he pulled down Wojnicz’s lower eyelid, carefully examining the colour of the mucous membranes and the eyeball. Then his gaze travelled from chin to temples; finally he told him to undress to the waist, looked critically at his chest, and began pressing the patient’s nipples with a finger.

“Slightly enlarged, as are the lymph nodes,” he said. “Have they always been like that?”

“For several years,” Wojnicz replied, abashed.

The doctor took him by the chin and ran a finger over the uneven, very sparse stubble of two days’ growth. He meticulously felt his lymph nodes; then his bold fingers tapped along his back, drawing out a dull sound, a rumbling as though from underground. He did it very carefully, centimetre by centimetre, like a sapper searching for a hidden bomb. All this lasted half an hour or so, until at last the doctor sighed and told him to dress. Only then did he reach for the letter. Looking over the metal rims of his spectacles, he said:

“Phthisis.” It sounded as though he had whistled. “Tuberculosis, consumption, and nowadays, fashionably, Morbus Koch. You know all that, young man, don’t you?”

Wojnicz buttoned up his shirt and nodded.

“Not very far advanced, to tell the truth. Oh, something small, a little grain of something. ‘Phthisis’ means disintegration, you know?” He pronounced the word—“Zerfall”—with evident pleasure, lingering on the r. “But here we know how to deal with disintegration.”

“Yes, Doctor Brehmer’s method...” Wojnicz began, but the doctor, impatient, got to his feet and waved a raised hand.

“Oh yes. Brehmer observed that travelling to Italy with tuberculosis makes not the slightest sense. Only mountain air truly cures. Air like what we have here. You see?” The doctor went over to the window and for a brief moment stood there, lost in thought. “We are in a basin here”—he said this while making circular, extravagantly wide sweeps with his arms, as though he wanted to impress the nature of the phenomenon more forcefully on his listener—“Beneath us lies a vast underground lake, which is why it is warmer here than elsewhere. The air is rich in oxygen, and yet there is no wind. Lung diseases and epidemics have never been known among the local people, would you believe it? No one here has ever had trouble with the lungs. And what is more, the altitude remains within the limits necessary for the treatment of pulmonary disease, because it does not accelerate the heart too much, as it does in places lying above nine hundred metres above sea level. There is a fir forest here, saturating the air with ozone, and ozone plays a key role in renewing the blood and the whole organism. Breathing alone will arrest the process of decay in your young lungs. Every breath is curative; that is how you must think of it. Imagine that with every breath pure light is flowing into your lungs.” The doctor looked at Mieczysław through the lenses of his spectacles, which magnified his dark eyes in an unsettling way. “And besides, we have other attractions here as well. You need only fall into line, submit to the regimen of treatment. Think of yourself as being in the army.”

He went over to the window and, with a tilt of his head, indicated the patients strolling in the park.

“Those are your comrades-in-arms.”

Wojnicz suddenly realised that he was not going to like this doctor. He thought of gentle, kindly Dr Sokołowski.

“That is clear to me, doctor,” he replied, tugging down the cuffs of his shirt. “I should only like to know whether I stand any chance.”

“Of course you do. Otherwise you would not have come here at all, young man. You would never have dared come here if you had not felt that you had one. You would have gone on being ill in peace back in the East. It’s flat there, isn’t it?”

Wojnicz was now to learn many interesting things about the brilliant Dr Brehmer, who had bought the village of Görbersdorf and the whole surrounding countryside—more than a hundred hectares of woods and fields in all—to found this sanatorium. Brehmer had long since noticed that autopsies and examinations of living patients suffering from tuberculosis invariably revealed a disproportion between the heart and the lungs: the lungs were relatively large, the heart small, with thin, flaccid, weak walls. Before him no one had paid any special attention to this correlation, and no one had thought of linking this disproportion between the organs of the chest to the aetiology of tuberculosis. Yet it seemed obvious that a small, weak heart led to slower blood circulation, and through that to chronic under-supply of blood to the lungs and pulmonary epithelium. Tuberculosis was the result. Moreover, Brehmer studied the geographical distribution of the disease, and this only strengthened his conviction about that aetiology. Travellers’ reports showed that there were places and regions where tuberculosis did not occur: the high mountains in every climatic zone, Iceland, the Faroe Islands, the Kirghiz steppes.

For one thing, the special features of the high-altitude climate were decisive. Lower atmospheric pressure makes the body respond with heightened cardiac activity and a quicker pulse, defending itself in this way against oxygen deprivation—and that leads to faster metabolism and a rise in body temperature. Secondly, lifestyle and diet were important: plenty of food, especially fat, kumiss with its alcohol content, hard physical labour.

A rapid pulse and heightened cardiac activity cause the heart muscle to increase in volume and the organ to develop a powerful musculature; often, among the inhabitants of the regions mentioned, this even leads to cardiac hypertrophy, a phenomenon exactly opposite to that found in tuberculosis patients.

“My dear young man,” Dr Semperweiß concluded his lecture. “That is our whole prescription. In Central Europe the tuberculosis-free zone begins at an altitude of roughly four hundred and fifty metres. Add to that the constant supervision of a doctor, who regulates the diet. And exercise in the fresh air. Nature itself heals us.”

Dr Semperweiß took out a sheet of paper and set down the recommendations point by point, commenting on each aloud in a voice already faintly bored:

“At least six weeks, preferably several months. Walks tailored to the individual patient, and by all means along routes of varying incline; there must be benches at short intervals, so you do not tire yourself. Moderate cold-water treatment. That is what will help you. Medicaments in moderation. In the event of violent coughing fits, I recommend suppressing the cough as far as possible, and when it cannot be suppressed, taking tiny sips of cold water or soda water with hot milk. Should bleeding from the lungs occur—God forbid—we apply ice bags to the heart and lungs and administer injections of morphine. In severe attacks, with breathlessness and weakness, we first give a strong stimulant, champagne for example. Yes, do not be afraid of champagne, or of alcohol in general. But absolutely in small quantities. Drunkenness is strictly forbidden! In the case of fever, temperature readings are first taken every two hours, to confirm it. Night sweats are effectively combated by drinking milk in the evening with two or three teaspoons of cognac or liqueur. Nurse Sydonia Patek will explain everything and show you.”

As he delivered this whole speech, he was writing down the instructions, and Wojnicz marvelled that he could do both at once.

“You are staying with Herr Opitz, are you not? Every day you will come to the Kurhaus for treatments and lying out, and as soon as a place opens up in the sanatorium I shall let you know; everything here is fluid. Fluid,” he stressed. “For the time being, Herr Opitz’s guesthouse is just as good for your health as we are or Dr Römpler’s sanatorium, and the short daily walks will put some colour in your cheeks.”

The doctor rose briskly and handed Wojnicz the sheet of instructions. And that was that—he had been accepted.

Now he sat in the waiting room while the ugly nurse prepared his treatment booklet and the other necessary documents. He took from his pocket the leaflet, folded into quarters, and finished reading:

In general it must be admitted that, from the therapeutic point of view, the most effective cure hitherto has been held to be a stay in such places as Meran in Tyrol, Görbersdorf in Silesia or Davos in Switzerland, laid out on the model of Görbersdorf. Dr Römpler’s sanatorium, founded in 1875, lies almost directly at the foot of the mountains and consists of a suitable number of buildings in the form of elegant villas. A water main 1,140 metres in length brings down from the mountain at whose foot the sanatorium lies crystal-clear mountain spring water, issuing directly from porphyry rock, into the elegantly appointed bathing rooms of the shower building.

Patients do not lack occupations and amusements. The cure itself, dinners, etc., take up a large part of the day, and the charming surroundings of Görbersdorf offer countless excursions. The aim of the cure is that the patient should strive to combat the illness himself. By strengthening the organism, he must be made resistant. In this way the progress of the disease is first arrested; then the affliction gradually begins to recede and health to return. Through continual exercise the affected lungs learn to function properly, and the fresh mountain air stimulates the heart. The results of the Görbersdorf cure may be counted among the most favourable. Nearly 75 percent of patients recover.

How wonderful it would be to believe that one belonged to that seventy-five percent.


2.

Schwärmerei

Wojnicz was walking back to the guesthouse with a small notebook in which a record of his treatment would henceforth be kept, and he was thinking over what Dr Semperweiß had told him. The most important thing in the cure is regimen. One gets up early, very early. Temperature taken. Written down in the notebook. Before breakfast, eaten between seven and eight, gymnastics are obligatory; after breakfast, a walk and, along the way, perhaps baths according to Father Kneipp’s method and other treatments. Walks follow a prescribed route. Second breakfast at ten—always fresh bread and butter and milk. Lying out on one of the many terraces. Dinner between half past twelve and half past one (soup with meat, a hearty meat dish with vegetables, then dessert and compote; on Sundays, instead of compote, something sweet is served, cake or a sweet flour dish). After dinner, coffee in the winter garden or the pavilions, without fail. Lying out again, another walk, but the afternoon route must be different from the earlier one. Afternoon tea around four or half past four, and supper at seven—hot meat with potatoes, and, absolutely, a glass of milk. In the evening, the thermometer again, and a few sentences in the notebook about how one feels. Plenty of sleep. No excitement. Good, hearty food. Lots of meat, milk, and sheep’s cheese. Wojnicz decided to take breakfast and dinner at the Kurhaus; he would eat supper at the Guesthouse for Gentlemen. That was what he had been advised to do. Once he moved into the Kurhaus, he would take the rest of his meals there as well. Guests are summoned to meals by a trumpet call.

He was brimming with excitement and good intentions; it was that kind of euphoria that comes with beginning something new, something that irrevocably heralds a fresh start, when one cuts oneself loose from the old and lets the past slip into oblivion. Now even the stern, ironic Semperweiß seemed to him a herald of change.

As he walked, he tried to memorise the layout of the houses and guesthouses. He looked at the somewhat bizarre building of the astronomical observatory, where Dr Brehmer was said to study the influence of the cosmos and the weather on the treatment of tuberculosis. Then he came to the imposing bulk of Villa Rosa and turned back.

The full, golden September sun was shining. Mieczysław Wojnicz aimed his feet at the centre of the large, flat stones paving the road.

Two elderly women were still sitting on the bench in front of the house, silently shelling broad beans, snapping the dry, crackling pods. One of the beans suddenly flew out of one of those wrinkled hands and landed right by his shoe. He picked it up carefully between two fingers and was about to hand it back, but for some sudden, inexplicable reason they sprang up from the bench and, taking their bowls and baskets with them, vanished into the house. Only their black, glossy skirts flashed in the sun. Still, nothing had happened. Wojnicz rubbed the brown bean on his sleeve; it looked perfect. He tossed it into the air and caught it. Not knowing what else to do with it, he slipped it into his pocket.

He was surprised to find the guesthouse door standing wide open; then he saw a prayer book on the ground, flung straight into a puddle. Its cream pages were already soaked through with dirty water. He picked up the prayer book and went inside with a sudden sense of unease.

The downstairs parlour was empty—presumably all the guests were still away at their treatments. He set the mud-stained prayer book down on a little table and was just about to go upstairs when the half-open dining-room door caught his eye, and beyond it the shoes—on the table—somehow familiar. Without thinking, as if hypnotised, he went over and pushed the door open to look more closely.

The shoes were the lower part of an elongated bundle of indeterminate shape, which turned out in the end to be a human body. It lay in the dining room on the table where meals were eaten. It seemed to be carefully wrapped in folds of cloth—Wojnicz had the impression it was wearing any number of skirts, shifts, corsets, wraps. Wojnicz had never seen a woman from so close up and so still; they were always slipping past, always in motion. It was impossible to focus on them and notice all the details. But now he had such a body before him, and there was no doubt it was dead. He looked at the black lace-up boots protruding from beneath the skirts and petticoats. The latter were finished with some kind of embroidery, though the lace had already been washed thin, its edges beginning to fray. The bootlaces had been carefully tied into double bows; strange that someone who by afternoon was no longer alive had laced her boots so meticulously that very morning. The outer skirt, made of a faintly lustrous fabric in thin black-and-grey stripes, lay in neat folds. Higher up there was something like a tight-fitting jacket of dark, almost black broadcloth, fastened with round buttons, the kind Polish priests have on their cassocks. From beneath this garment a white shirt protruded, rather open at the neck, with a button torn off, only the thread remaining; the collar of the shirt, however, had now been pulled high right up beneath the chin, though not carefully enough to hide from Mieczysław the bluish-red streak on the neck, shocking against the white skin.

At last he had to do it—to raise his eyes to the face. With horror he saw the half-closed eyes, and beneath the lashes a tiny glistening strip of eyeball. Her twisted head was turned toward him, as though she wanted to confess something. On her lips, narrow and already slightly bluish, he noticed the trace of a smile—it seemed utterly out of place, almost ironic. The tips of her teeth protruded from beneath her upper lip, quite dry. And there was something else: her face was covered with a fine pale down, like fluff.

He stood petrified, hardly breathing.

He recognised her almost at once as the woman who had brought him breakfast that morning. At the time he had noticed only the boot that had pushed the door open. And the ample, corset-pinched curves. Nothing more. Only here, in death, could one see the woman whole.

“She hanged herself,” said Willi Opitz, stopping in the doorway.

Wojnicz started, startled by his host’s low, resonant voice. Opitz said it in a tone as though he were pointing out some disgraceful dereliction, something that simply ought not to have happened. But his voice trembled.

“Don’t upset yourself. The men from the mortuary will be here in a moment to take the body away. Rajmund has gone for them.”

Wojnicz did not know what to say. His tongue had gone dry as a chip; he felt a band around his throat.

“When did it happen?” was all he asked.

“When? Just now—well, an hour ago. I went upstairs to her when she didn’t come down to collect the vegetables from the delivery man. She was hanging. I cut her down. Go up to your room, boy. Ah, the men from the mortuary are here already.”

“She brought me breakfast this morning,” said Wojnicz, and despite himself the agitation showed in his voice. “She was your maid, wasn’t she?”

“Oh no, no. That’s my wife.”

Opitz waved a hand as if shooing away a wasp and opened the door to the grim men from the mortuary, who began speaking to him quietly, in dialect. Mieczysław withdrew from the dining room—as he hurried upstairs, he could hear their muffled voices, but he could not understand what they were saying. The whole exchange seemed to him like the murmuring of people who had no need of words to understand one another.

Wojnicz sat down in the russet armchair with the crocheted headrest. He was shaken. Strange—it had not occurred to him that the kindly Willi Opitz had a wife. He should have known that men generally have wives who, not always clearly visible, support the family business from the kitchen or the laundry. Absorbed in himself, in his arrival and his illness, he had not even noticed her. And now she was dead.
