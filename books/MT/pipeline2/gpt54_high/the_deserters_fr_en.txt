I

He sets down his rifle and struggles out of his boots, whose smell (excrement, mildewed sweat) only adds to his fatigue. His fingers on the frayed laces are dry twigs, slightly singed here and there; his nails are the color of the boots, he will have to scrape them with the tip of the knife to get the filth off, mud, dried blood, but later, he has no strength for it; two toes, flesh and dirt, stick out of the sock, great smeared worms crawling from a dark trunk, knotted at the ankle.

All at once, as he does every morning, every evening, he wonders why these boots stink of shit, it is inexplicable,

you can rinse them in every puddle you come across, rub them on the rasping tussocks of grass, nothing helps,

and yet there are not that many dogs or wild beasts, not that many, in these stony heights dusted with holm oaks, pines and thorn scrub, where the rain leaves a fine pale mud and a smell of flint, no shit, and it would be easy for him to believe that the whole country reeks, from the sea, the hills of orange groves and then olive groves to the farthest reaches of the mountains, of these mountains, even himself, his own smell, not the boots’, but he cannot bring himself to it and flings the boots against the edge of the ravine that hides him from the path a little higher up the slope.

He lies down on his back on the gravel, sighs, the sky is violet, the afterglow lights the swift clouds from below, a canvas, a screen for fireworks. Spring is almost here and with it come the rains, often torrential, turning the mountains into cans punched through by bullets, disgorging from the slightest hollow a powerful spring, when the air smells of thyme and fruit-tree blossom, white flakes strewn between the low walls by the violence of the cloudburst. It would be the very devil if it started raining now. Then again it would wash the boots. The boots, the fatigues, the socks, both pairs he owns just as stiff, cardboard-like, tattered. Betrayal begins with the body,

how long since you washed?

Four days now you’ve been walking near the ridgelines to avoid the villages,

the last water you splashed over yourself smelled of petrol and left your skin greasy,

you are a long way from purity, alone under the sky eyeing the comets.

Hunger forces him to straighten up and swallow, without pleasure, three army biscuits, the last of them, hard brown slabs, no doubt some mixture of sawdust and glue made from an old mare; for a moment he curses war and soldiers,

you are still one of them, you still carry weapons, ammunition and memories of war,

you could hide the rifle and the cartridges in some corner and become a beggar, leave the knife too, beggars don’t have daggers,

leave the shit-stinking boots and go barefoot,

leave the misery-colored jacket and go bare-chested,

when he has finished eating he drinks the last of his canteen and plays at pissing as far as he can toward the valley.

He lies down again, this time right against the rock face, the bottom of the pack under his head; he is invisible in the shade, never mind the creatures (red spiders, tiny scorpions, centipedes with teeth as sharp as remorse) that will scamper over his chest, slide over his nearly shaven skull, wander through his beard as rough as brambles. The rifle against him, the butt under his shoulder, the barrel toward his feet. Wrapped in the piece of tarred canvas that serves him as blanket and roof.

The mountain rustles; a little wind comes over the summits, drops into the hollow and vibrates among the shrubs; the cries of the stars are glacial. There are no clouds left, it will not rain tonight.

Angel, my holy guardian, protector of my soul and my body, forgive me all the sins committed this day and deliver me from the works of the enemy, for all the warmth of prayer the night remains a beast fed on anguish, a beast with blood on its breath, ruined cities through which mothers roam brandishing the mutilated corpses of their children before disheveled hyenas who will torture them, then leave them naked, defiled, their nipples torn off with bites before the eyes of their brothers, themselves raped in turn with truncheons, terror spread across the country, plague, hatred and the night, that night that always envelops you to drive you toward cowardice and betrayal. Toward flight and desertion. How long is he going to have to walk? The border is a few days from here, beyond the mountains that will soon become hills of red earth planted with olive trees. It will be hard to hide. Many villages, towns, peasants, soldiers,

you know the region,

you are at home here,

no one will help a deserter,

tomorrow you will reach the house in the mountains,

the hut, the hovel, you will take refuge there for a while,

the hut will protect you with its childhood,

there you will be caressed by memories,

sometimes sleep comes by surprise like the bullet of a hidden sniper.

II

More than twenty years ago, on September 11, 2001, near Potsdam on the Havel, aboard that cruise boat, a little river steamer grandly and beautifully named Beethoven, summer seemed to falter.

The willows were still green, the days still warm, but before dawn an icy mist rose from the river and immense clouds drifted over us from the far-off Baltic Sea.

Our floating hotel had left Köpenick, east of Berlin, very early on the morning of Monday the 10th. Maja was still brisk, still spry. She would go up to the upper deck to walk, a stroll among the showers, the deck chairs and the deck games. The green domes and golden spire of Berlin Cathedral captivated her from afar as we passed. She imagined, she said, all those little gilded angels leaving their prison of stone to fly off in a cloud of acanthus leaves blown loose by the sun.

The water of the Spree was now a dark matte blue, now a glowing green. In the preceding weeks all Germany had been shaken by storms whose spasms had swelled all the way to the Havel and the Spree, though at that end of summer they were usually rather low.

We sailed amid the eddies.

I remember the confluence of the Spree, the wooded islets, the salt light dusting the tall black poplars and the muddy flow of the canal that the ship’s wake was mixing with the waxed waters of the river.

Maja and I were sitting, each in a canvas chair, in the sun on deck, aft, at the stern as one should say, and we watched everything flee: the landscape widened as if the ship’s prow were opening wide the green substance of the foliage.

A few months late, we were celebrating the tenth anniversary of Paul’s refounding of the Institute while paying tribute to the founder himself. Or rather, we were celebrating the tenth anniversary of the Institute’s “unification,” in the spring of 1991, and the fortieth anniversary of its creation in 1961. But above all it was a celebration of Paul’s work. I don’t think anyone was missing — of the old guard, those from the East, all were there; the newer members, the colleagues from Berlin and elsewhere, had almost all answered the call. Some, among them Linden Pawley, Robert Kant and a few French researchers, had even come from abroad. This floating conference was called the Paul Heudeber Days; two sessions a day were planned, on number theory and algebraic topology, as well as a session on the history of mathematics in which I was to take part.

The only one absent was Paul himself.

Maja had just celebrated her eighty-third birthday.

Maja drank litres of tea.

Maja was cheerful and sad and silent and talkative.

We all knew she had no business being there, aboard the Beethoven for a mathematics conference; we all knew she was indispensable.

Paul

Most train travelers prefer to sit facing the direction of travel.

A historian is a traveler who chooses not to sit facing the direction of travel.

A historian of science is a historian who, seated facing the opposite direction, turned toward the rear and, unlike most historians, does not look out the window.

A woman historian of mathematics is a historian of science who, seated facing the opposite direction, eyes closed, tries to prove that the Arabs invented trains.

No one laughed.

It must be said that I was the only historian at the conference. All the others were mathematicians, female and male, physicists, female and male, or, worse still, logicians. All of them sitting facing the direction of travel. Looking toward innovation, invention, discovery. I was the only one interested not so much in the glorious proofs and inventions of tomorrow as in the syrupy meanders of the past. Meanders of the past that cast their light into the farthest reaches of the future, and I felt, during this session of the Paul Heudeber Days on the Havel, that this audience of learned women and men would listen to my paper on Nasiruddin Tusi and irrational numbers with nothing more than formal respect, full of consideration for me and for my mother, who despite her great age would not miss a morsel of the talks, between two walks on deck.

Maja was behind the idea of this river conference; if memory serves, Jürgen Thiele, the secretary general, had proposed “an afternoon excursion on the Spree or the Havel” to conclude the Days, which were initially to be held at the Institute in Berlin; she had made a face, the Spree or the Havel, that was still Berlin at best, Brandenburg at worst, why not the Danube, and Jürgen Thiele had opened his eyes wide, the Danube, but that’s very far, and I imagine Maja had burst out laughing, all right, the Havel then, but let the whole conference at least be on a boat, and Jürgen Thiele was very embarrassed (he explained it to me later), because he wanted to refuse my mother nothing for those tribute days but his funds were limited — this whole business of a river conference still seemed absurd to him, an old person’s whim.

Thiele was nevertheless surprised to receive two letters on the same day, a few weeks before the call for participation in the Days was published: one letter informing him that the faculty of mathematics at the University of Potsdam was proposing to co-organize the Paul Heudeber Days with our Institute, and another informing him that the Georg Cantor Foundation was awarding (though Thiele had asked for nothing) an enormous subsidy for the conference, making it possible, though no less aberrant, he thought without saying so, to organize it on the water.

Paul’s tragic death a few years earlier had stirred intense emotion in the scientific community; everyone was ready to take part and even if most of the organizers — Jürgen Thiele first of all, I thought — had no idea why Maja wanted this, no one wished to disappoint her. These two letters arrived at just the right moment and Jürgen could only suspect, no doubt rightly, that Maja had taken up her pen or picked up her telephone: though in theory retired from politics since the federal elections of 1998, she still had the power to draw “benevolent attention” to projects in the cradle. The money from the Georg Cantor Foundation was welcome; Jürgen Thiele got in touch, as co-organizer, with the University of Potsdam, which was celebrating its tenth anniversary and whose founding Paul had helped along: many of its mathematics teachers had been his students.

The Paul Heudeber Days would therefore take place on the Havel, aboard a cruise ship capable of accommodating, in its conference room, the fifty or so participants, those who were not Berliners being for the most part lodged in a hotel opposite Peacock Island, thus technically in Wannsee — a hotel with the name of a medieval or Alpine inn, the White Owl, an inn Maja assured me (I wondered where on earth she got such certainty) had existed since at least the sixteenth century, though the current building — Doric columns supporting a monumental balcony, windows with green shutters, climbing rosebushes, as in a fairy tale, softening the facade with their innumerable flowers of a very dark red, almost black — had been rebuilt by Karl Schinkel in the first third of the nineteenth century. The White Owl stood lost in the middle of the forest, on the edge of the immense lake crossed by the Havel. Only the Key Speakers and other VIPs of the conference were lodged on the Beethoven, since there were only a few cabins; the daytime “sailings,” on the other hand, were open to everyone: Potsdam-Elbe on Wednesday, the actual day of tribute, around Paul’s work, then Peacock Island-Köpenick by way of Spandau on Thursday to close the festivities. Only a few distinguished guests had arrived on Sunday to enjoy the boat’s “positioning” from Köpenick to Wannsee and thus an extra day of cruising through Berlin on Monday.

Jürgen Thiele was full of empathy, disorder and good will. Jürgen Thiele, though he was still the Institute’s secretary general, only carried out that task anymore out of loyalty to Paul, whose student he had been thirty years earlier; he readily admitted he was tired of organizing, setting up, putting in order — arranging a Christmas lunch panics me, he confessed. So imagine a conference with fifty people! The University of Potsdam had assigned him a co-organizer, a young doctoral student in number theory named Alma Sejdić, whose dissertation sought to prove a corollary to Paul’s first conjecture. The addition proved as disastrous as it was hilarious: instead of accumulating, these two forces seemed either to combine uselessly or to cancel one another out. Omissions were omitted twice, blunders repeated twice over. It was like a drawing traced with two ballpoint pens fastened together by an elastic band, parallels never meeting, despite all their efforts, constrained by Euclid himself.

Jürgen Thiele had had to deploy all his diplomacy so as not to offend the University of Potsdam, which could not understand why, only a few kilometers from its own buildings, it had to finance the hire of a luxurious cruise ship — but Jürgen Thiele had produced the Georg Cantor Foundation subsidy as if from his sleeve, and everyone found the idea of a floating conference exhilarating.

And so, after a few months of this ballet in chaos, Maja and I embarked, as planned, on Monday, September 10, at Köpenick, in the company of Linden Pawley, whose flight from New York had landed at Tegel that very morning, the inevitable Robert Kant of Cambridge, and Jürgen Thiele — there were indeed five luxurious cabins prepared for us.

III

Every morning since he left, the cold wakes him shortly before dawn. He shivers. No sudden movement, so that the dew, black beads on the canvas, will not run. Patiently, by folding his canvas into a gutter, he manages to fill his canteen with a few centilitres and drink that sweat of dawn, icy, which will be his only meal of the morning.

He sets off, once his reluctant feet are wrapped in that knitted sponge of a miserable green, still damp, in the direction of destiny, toward the north, for one must call rout and oblivion by their names. Once again he hesitates to abandon the rifle, it is heavy and its strap is awkward, too short since he cut it to make himself a belt, with that knife still so sharp, itself another mark of a dangerous solitude, drunk on blood, he no longer thinks, he is already walking while the first rays of the sun probe the shadows of the rocks. These stings of light set the sparrows, warblers and tits in motion, their wingbeats following the train of the morning song.

If he thinks so much about birds, if their presence and their song keep him so taut, it is because they sharpen his hunger — it would be so easy to settle into an ambush, nose to the wind, rifle in hand, wait for one of those little birds to betray itself, shoot it down and then eat it, but the power of a weapon of war would leave nothing but feathers, the shot would echo far into the heights and even if a fat pheasant or a partridge strayed into his sights, it would have to be cooked, and he has no intention of interrupting his march for long, of exposing himself with fire or smoke.

He has resolved to reach the house.

You’d find it even on a moonless night,

the hut,

the path goes on into the day between holm oaks scattered thin by drought; a few lentisks shelter among the rocks, releasing, as the walker passes, their apothecary scent, like some forgotten pharmacy; he searches for the fresh wild savory that spring multiplies in the mountain, so he can chew a bunch of it for a long time, bitter, sour, peppery — strawberry-tree berries have survived the winter still, like forgotten Christmas decorations, red and rough, they taste of overripe strawberries, of the blandness of oblivion.

These fruits are tiny stars, planets within reach,

little moons reddened by desire and the Evil One,

at every step the sun kindles the petals of the cornel blossoms, their vivid yellow dimmed by no leaf, on their still bare branches the first crack in winter opens by magic.

He walks like the last man alive, amid the mountain’s obsessive rustling.

He envies the black specks of airplanes or distant birds of prey.

Numb yourself with memory, ass on a stone — one of those blue-gray outcrops that warm up very quickly in the sun and smell of metal and flint, as smooth as they are hard: was there some first shiver, some hoarse wind, premise of the logic of brutality, some bellow preceding the sovereign rut of war, it seems to him not,

it was surprise that sat you down there,

soon the black snakes will come out of their holes and the males will begin searching for females,

he unlaces his boots, unbuckles the straps and takes them off. The leather is eaten away by wear, water and cold. The smell of shit has not left him. His hands are rough; his pale palm is starred with darker calluses, stiffened by gripping wooden handles too hard. His tobacco-stained fingers end in yellowed nails rimmed with dark filth, you can see the trace of the veins, at the thumb and along the wrist; his cheeks are harsh with a stubborn beard, his hair is greasy and stuck together in clumps, matted into duller strands by dried blood,

you will reach the house before nightfall,

the house, the hut, the hovel — it lies very deep in his memories and his hopes. A cairn from the country of childhood. High enough in the mountain that no one ventures there. Hidden enough from the mountain world that he can shut himself away there. For a while. The roof may have partly fallen in, the round cypress beams, still gleaming, will stand alone without tiles among the uneven stones. The door so low. The porch in front, its wooden braces recalling his father’s arms, its two posts of badly squared stone the columns of a brutal God’s temple. The facade of unrendered rubble. The covering of old yellow clay tiles,

you’ll be able to carve faces in the beams with the knife as before,

you are hungry enough to frighten,

you are hungry to the roots of your hair,

imagining the little hearth under the porch of the hut and some fowl there crackling on the embers, twisted with furious pain,

you are thirsty,

he drains his metal flask. The lovely March sun takes on an orange hue. A wind blows from the sea,

you keep going,

you have to keep going even if you stagger a little, clumsy with dizziness. He lets thoughts escape as soon as they are born. He drives them out through his feet, distances them by walking. He passes his thoughts into his boots, which scatter them among the stones. Then inner silence, until the return of hunger, that great fixed star.

The treachery of illusion, the scent of spring on its way.

The sea edging its violet plains with white.

So high in the mountain the sea is only a menacing line, a horizon of sorrow.

His fever carries it away from him: the more he walks, the farther the house recedes.

You crunch too loudly,

you’ll have to beware of the scree slope above the hut,

lie in the sunset and watch for unknown movements — abandoned dogs gone feral in the war, deserters, villagers, distant cousins, all of them far from their relics, on the road to the hermitage, to escape suffering, to be done with the long Lent of blood,

spring suddenly takes his breath away. A spring of wingbeats, rock flowers, thornbushes, white and blue rosemary, the humming of wing cases — the path he had been following has descended a few dozen metres toward the sea; he takes off his clothes stiffened by filth, stained with grease and dried blood, finds himself bare-chested, now licked by the breeze, now blinded by the force of the sun whose burn he feels on his shoulders, on the long scar that bars his back, before the cloth of the pack covers it again. Tired of the strap that is too short, he carries his weapon in his arms like a hunter, his left hand around the stock, his right around the grip as one closes on a bird’s neck, with force and casualness; the bolt is open, he sees the brass of a casing shining in the magazine, once again he wants to rid himself of the accursed object,

it weighs more in your arms than a child,

you should abandon it, hide it there in a thicket, a few hours’ walk from the hut,

he works the well-greased bolt, impossible to get rid of it,

destiny before you and all these things, the remains, the traces and the great mourning ahead,

you will be what the Lord wishes,

strength or forgiveness, or nothing, like that yellow arachnid beneath your boot, crushed despite its power to kill, crushed despite its sting, all that one does not know of oneself, we bend beneath yesterday’s world, we bend beneath our faults, we bend beneath the prospect of tomorrow, our Father give us this day our daily oblivion, in the too many footsteps that sand the soul away, metre after metre, road after road, path after path, this sudden emotion comes from the nearness — a day’s walk — of the village below, halfway down the slope, where orange trees little by little invade the plains, where olive trees grow discreet on the terraced walls of stone, among the houses with their gentle arches, their broken vaults among the loquat trees so green, which light up with orange fruit in June, among the noble fig trees bent by the years whose figs rustle with insects in autumn, just as the vine trellis shaded the terrace in front of his father’s house, where they pressed a wine that quickly stung the tongue, violet, cloudy and intoxicating — the green demijohns braided in straw piled up in the darkest, coolest storerooms, until they were cleaned in September to receive the new harvest, and with a wire brush the red and black clouds of tannin clinging beneath their glass shoulders were scrubbed away,

you’ll have to hide, they are no doubt looking for you,

you must not cross anyone, hide yourself from men and beasts, from shepherds, from dogs, swallow your own name,

the closer your steps bring you to the hut, to the house in the mountain, the greater the danger grows, in the village everyone knows, no doubt, rumors swell like war itself, everyone knows, or thinks they know,

the afternoon swells like thirst and reddens like hunger.

He stops in the shade of a holm oak. He sits on a root. The sun drenches the valley before him. He dreams of rain. He shakes his canteen over his tongue once again. He loosens his boots, hesitates to take them off, he is so tired he will not put them back on if he removes them. For an instant the smell seems to have vanished, but it returns, stronger still, without warning,

you stink of blood and shit,

you stink of sleep and hunger,

a child could kill you with a single punch,

he counts the days since leaving the city. Since fleeing the barracks. Four days since he drove the vehicle into the ravine,

you have walked nearly a hundred kilometers in the mountains,

the holm-oak root is hard under your ass,

your drawn-up knees hurt,

he leans back against the black trunk, stretches out his legs, eyes on the valley — almond trees, hazels, prickly pears — that he knows so well. He has plowed these terraces, weeded around the bases of the trees, taken away the countless stones. The sun he knows. The strip of sea beyond the hills he knows. The fear he carries with him.

At the next bend in the path, once he has passed the old retention basin of the stream, now dry, he will be two hours’ walk from the house. He will reach it almost an hour before sunset,

you know where you’re going to hide out,

behind the big rock and check, without being seen, that no one is hanging around the hut. Behind the rock and watch. Watch the last insects in the twilight. Listen to the birds and the stones in the twilight.

He takes out the knife. The blade is as gray as it is blue. He dreams of a hare, springing from a hollow, suddenly within reach of the dagger. He cuts a cross in the tree root. A thin short cross. A sign. He would have been capable of drinking the hare’s warm blood if it had appeared,

you are feverish like these parts in your memory,

for hours now he has been searching for an orange tree, or even a lemon tree still carrying a few forgotten fruits on its branches. Opposite the hut stands a vast lemon tree planted by his grandfather, which bears (or rather which bore — it has been so long since he has seen it) dozens of yellow, juicy, thick-skinned fruits that leave on the hands a scent of linen and flowers, a fragrance of purity, purity pleases the Lord,

there is an orange tree too, they used to weave crowns from its blossoms for weddings,

you are the least pure of creatures,

he finds the strength to set out again, knees aching, thighs hard as stones, feet skinned raw; the farther the war recedes, the more his body comes apart, an old mechanism that habit used to keep turning. He is almost incapable of climbing the few kilometers that still separate him from the hut, from the house, from the violet mists and hollows of cloud. It is his rifle that carries and guides him, the oversized needle of a magic compass, the dowser’s rod of death,

you walk badly, you stagger, you make too much noise,

he brushes away the tiny flies that pursue him only to catch up with him again. The sun burns his skin, fragile now from emerging from the cold of war, he is a lizard brought back to life by heat; everything in him is taut between fear and exhaustion.

His footsteps suddenly trigger — stones rolling, branches trembling, wingbeats — the flight of a pigeon a few metres away. He closes the bolt to cock the rifle and shoulders it — he does not fire,

you are too close to the villages, you must not draw the attention of some shepherd passing that way,

he watches the bird disappear behind a stand of holm oaks to rejoin its mate,

these birds always go in pairs,

they are the mountain’s inseparables, the inevitable birds of spring, along with the nightingales. He puts the safety on. At the top of the pass between these two hills blotched with rock the hut will come into view. He watches banks of cloud suddenly massing, gray above the line of sea. A cloud veils the sun. The wind turns the drops of sweat on his shoulders and chest into so many icy pins. He had forgotten the dexterity of cold — he forces himself to stop and put his jacket back on, stiffened by all the fluids clogging its fibers, along with pain and terror,

you stink of slaughterhouse, that’s the smell coming off you, guts and the jet of water over blackened tiles,

a smell of meat,

he passes his left hand over his face, his beard is rough as bark. With the sun gone, the altitude makes itself felt again, and the shadow too: he shivers. Behind him, a little lower down, a cottony mist spreads between two folds of hills, a white fog on the red earth, the sea has vanished. Steel gnaws at the horizon. He hurls his last strength at the stones to get over them, at the slopes to climb them. The pass bellows, the pass freezes his face. The wind presses his face and shoulders. He clings to his rifle and leans forward. From one loss of balance to the next he reaches the shelter of a rock a few dozen metres lower down. He leans against it,

the house is below you on your right,

he looks, the tiled roof, more yellow than red, is there, a single-pitched roof with its back against the mountain, he catches sight of the porch, the short chimney, the partition wall of rubble stone, the low walls around the abandoned garden, not a living creature in sight, far away a bird of prey circles, tiny solitary speck in the sky gone milky, the enclosure to the right of the garden is empty, the great almond tree in front of the house still has no leaves, the lemon tree is green with that grave green of citrus, eternal, a sepulchral green with yellow glints in the wavering light of the absent sun, no smoke from the chimney, in the air there floats a smell of thyme and snow,

if you had binoculars you would look for traces,

signs that someone is there, shepherds, peasants, refugees, creatures, angels, demons,

there is only the short wavering plain breaking away toward the sea, nothing is heard but the wind crossing the low walls, back to the rock, hands around his knees, the rifle to his right, the bag at his feet like a motionless dog, he waits, he waits the amount of time he had planned, the two hours remaining before full dark, reassured by the presence of the hut, by the lemons in the lemon tree, by the old orange tree invisible to him beside the hazel beyond the hut to the right of the wall,

all urgency abolished by the sudden presence of childhood,

by wingbeats,

backwards,

you await the apparition, Lord, your invisible face, you await Sirius, you await Orion, you await your face, Lord,

your ass is frozen by the scraps of winter the mountain still contains,

the mountain keeps winter, the orange trees and the lemon trees keep winter — December’s fruit still hangs in the branches when the flowers open in April, he forces his eyes into the gathering darkness, he strains his gaze, he sees nothing, not a movement, not a shadow except the almond tree’s shadow growing, the hovel’s shadow growing, a blackbird sings in the evening, a blackbird Lord one of your creatures sings your glory, all creatures sing your glory, hope infuses his chest, it is the presence of the hovel and the blackbird’s voice,

hope Lord is born of You,

you will find again a little childhood and a little rest in the hovel,

the hovel where you used to go with your father, where your father went with his father, went to cultivate, went to gather, went to cultivate,

the house is before his eyes and there is no movement, no presence, it is night now or almost, the bird’s voice has fallen silent, he will go down to the hut, the hovel, the house, whatever name one gives it,

you limp, you stumble, the bag and the rifle are heavy,

the path unwinds like a snake along the mountainside, there is no longer a single star in the sky and the wind is still at war.
