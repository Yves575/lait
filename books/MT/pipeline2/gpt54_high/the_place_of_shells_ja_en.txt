At the Place Leading to the Shell

Standing in the shadow of the deserted station building, I kept waiting for a visitor whose face had half vanished. Even when I dredged through memory and somehow managed to piece together an image of that face, it immediately collapsed again like water. Even so, I gathered up the fragments and pressed them inside an outline, making a patchwork portrait. The repetition was filled with a timid sensation, like probing a throbbing tooth with my tongue.

The sunlight at the beginning of July had turned whiter, pouring out heat without stint. Its rays heightened every color even as they reduced everything to black and white. The square before the station glowed white; there was no shadow there. On the benches, people sat spaced apart like statues that would not melt even in this heat. In contrast to those still figures pinned in place by the sun, a black dog moved restlessly about. The dog kept after the clear water rising out of the square. The stone paving was pierced with fountain jets, and from them water spurted and stopped again without pattern. Stretching in slender columns to about a child’s height, it flowered like glass plants in a rapid cycle, then quietly vanished, leaving dark wet circles behind. Delighted, the dog twisted and writhed after the white, transparent water-snakes. Each time the water disappeared, he sprang toward another jet. He alone was enjoying even this heavy sunlight without a care.

Beside the white-scorched cobbled square in front of Göttingen station stood a forest of tangled bicycles. Bikes had been shoved in, intricately threaded through every gap, and the metal of their frames gave off a light that would not melt even in the sun. Even now, three years after I came here, I still do not understand how such a complicated three-dimensional structure manages to keep its balance. Even as I watched, a man braked hard, screwed his bicycle into a seam in that metallic forest, and came hurrying toward the station. Even the motion of pulling on his mask as he ran was smooth; he was swallowed up beyond the doors. With one bicycle’s worth of complexity added, the forest had already become such a mass of things that it was close to losing the meaning of bicycle. In the light tracing its surface, another image rose up apart from it—the image of a mass of things from which meaning had come undone—and began to throb like toothache.

I checked the time and peered in through the station’s main entrance. A train must have arrived: the hall, which until a moment before had been almost empty, was suddenly overflowing with people, and the white space filled richly with color. Yet once the travelers slipped through the doors and receded into the white sunlight, they lost their color again. I called the dog’s name, and though he was reluctant to leave off playing with the water, he came bounding at once to my feet. I clipped the leash to his collar and stepped into the station. In the white hall, the black of his wet fur took on even more weight, vivid as though it were a stake half driven into the white, and I stood there relying on that color.

The stream of people broke off, voices broke off too, and even the announcements of arriving and departing trains closed their mouths; for a brief moment the hall filled with blankness. I stepped out of that soundless whiteness and went a little farther down the passage. The station’s front and rear exits were joined by a single straight corridor, so that, aside from the few glass-walled elevators set along it, I could see all the way to the small doors at the far end. Standing at one end of the quiet corridor, I cast my gaze down it, and there a vivid green caught my eye, a painting framed by a doorway. For all its sharpness, though, it did nothing to narrow the distance between us. Like looking through the wrong end of a telescope, it merely flickered before me as a fragment of a far-off world. When I brought it into focus, a human figure slowly resolved. In that corridor of warped perspective, Nonomiya stood with the green at his back. Perhaps he had noticed me; the monotonous sound of a wheeled suitcase drew closer. As the distance fell away, I could feel time, already far receded, and memory, which had remained still, beginning to move again, imitating the creak of the wheels. His tall, lean figure seemed to let the oppressive heat slide past it. The balance of his broad forehead and the large eyes beneath it was as though traced from a medieval sculpture. Perhaps because he stood against greenery trembling in the sunlight, I suddenly thought of a saint depicted in the stained glass of a church’s high windows.

But that was where memory stopped working properly. By the time Nonomiya had come within speaking distance, his mouth was covered by a white mask, and everything below his nose blurred into the color of the cloth. In that white shadow, which made his outline vague, I could barely make out the movement of his lips, or even the sign of words. The same was true of me, and just then I was grateful, more than for anything else, for this hot thing that made breathing difficult. I too was hiding behind what concealed my mouth, holding my words in reserve. But once we moved away from the station’s main entrance, we would have to take it off, and then, to reconnect time, we would need to let words show themselves. Stepping out of the impression of a saint enclosed in glass, Nonomiya finally removed his mask outside the station. His mouth started to move into the shape of what looked like It’s been a long time, but then, struck by the length of the severed time between us, he made no sound, and his lips crumpled into a small ambiguous smile. Even so, when I saw that light yet courteous bow, memory came loose in fragments, with the pain of something catching inside me, and the words I had prepared vanished. So this was how he greeted people. The broken, cobbled-together memory inside me cried out. More than in his face or figure, it was in that gesture that the nine years were trying to join themselves. At the same time, I felt relieved by a presence solid enough not to let the sunlight pass through it. Even the dead have shadows pooling at their feet. I returned the gesture, imitating his wordless bow. We both gave wry smiles at that clumsy symmetry, but once our lips had moved even a single time, the starting point of conversation settled itself naturally into place.

“It was a long journey,” I said. Nonomiya nodded, taking in the several meanings the words might carry, and formed a natural smile. “I’m a little tired.”

In his even tone he went on to tell me about the delayed train, how the connections had not worked out, how he had asked a station attendant about finding another train but had struggled to catch the answer because the man spoke so quickly and his mask muffled his voice, and about the old fortifications he had seen from the train window. From the way he spoke and carried himself, he seemed nothing more than an ordinary traveler. Everything I had imagined from what Sawada, Nonomiya’s friend, had told me melted white away before the reality of him, and I could do no more than nod and murmur along like a puppet. Then, over our lopsided conversation, silence came down with a sound, like the cord holding up a curtain snapping.

“For all the greenery, there are no cicadas.”

Without paying any mind to the broken-off conversation, Nonomiya murmured this, narrowed his eyes against the sunlight, and seemed to search for the voices that sear the summer. His body, where time had stopped, received the heavy white light, but his gaze was not wholly fastened to the waking world. Seeking the cicada cries that would fill that dense blankness, his eyes were turned toward a more distant time.

The dog kept a little distance, watching Nonomiya. His body language was easy enough to read: the tail, white only at the tip, was caught in midair at an undecided height, tracing the sign of something held in reserve. Without straying from his own orbit, the dog waited for the other to approach. He probably had no idea that Nonomiya was a ghost. His attitude toward strangers was always the same; there seemed to be no distinction there between the living and the dead. Reassured by that, I urged Nonomiya on and started toward the street that led to the old town. From a distance, the dog and I, and Nonomiya too, would probably look as though we were losing our colors, melting into the white and disappearing. That whiteness blurs the perspective of memory. But it could not bridge the severance of time and distance.

Göttingen is a city whose seams of time do not show. One can slip with ease from one time into another, moving through layers of memory. Like many German cities, it has at its core an old town thick with accumulated history, and around that core the rest of the city spreads outward in every direction, as though bulging out to surround and cover it. The circular old town is ringed by the trace of the old city wall. The wall itself has already been removed; in its place a thin belt of woods now encircles the town, stitched through with little paths packed firm by the feet of people who love woodland walks. From one edge of the old town to the other takes about half an hour, a size easy to grasp on foot. If you trace the streets woven into it, you begin to notice how time there layers itself in many-sided structures.

I had heard through Sawada where Nonomiya would be staying. It was to the north, away from the old town, near the natural sciences campus of the University of Göttingen. “So it’s better if we go by bus,” I said. Nonomiya nodded, and we headed toward the broad avenue that cuts through the woods on the line of the old wall. There is a bus stop in front of the station too, but no direct bus runs from there. So we decided to make for one inside the old town instead.

I learned of Nonomiya’s arrival about two weeks earlier. Until the day he came, we exchanged no messages directly, relying instead on Sawada’s careful, attentive mediation. Because of that, I could not quite shake the feeling that I had come here somehow as Sawada’s flesh-and-blood stand-in.

Dragging our shadows behind us, as soon as we came to the edge of the old town we came upon a golden sphere, gleaming dully in the sunlight. On the sidewalk facing a hotel with pale stone walls, it stood atop a stalk of dark bronze, like a bud swollen out of all proportion. Nonomiya’s gaze slowly traced its outline. “That’s the sun,” I said. My own voice sounded farther away than I had expected, and for a moment I nearly lost my bearings.

Göttingen has built into it a scale model of the solar system known as the Planetenweg, the Planet Path. In a place layered with old memories, the model’s own time is still shallow, yet it has already settled into the city and become one of its several emblems. The model sun placed on Goethe Street, which runs straight east from the station, marks the starting point; from there, the planet models, from Mercury to Neptune, are arranged one after another along the route. Because the layout follows a scale of one to two billion, the distances between them are irregular, and unless you are paying attention you walk right past them. Mercury through Saturn are inside the old town, but Uranus and Neptune stand quietly in the residential district beyond. Neptune, at the outer edge of the solar system, lies exactly on the boundary with the woods. In that way the city neatly overlays, by metaphor, the distances among the planets that make up the solar system.

There are said to be roughly fifty Planet Paths in Germany alone, yet I sometimes wonder why a miniature solar system should be laid out on the earth at all. Is it because the journey into space, once the source of so many near-future dreams, has receded farther and farther from us? Or perhaps because we need, simply, to keep our feet on the ground and focus on the earth, and have no choice but to make do with a substitute. Whatever the reasons for its installation, the path serves lovers of walking and jogging as a set of ideal markers—measures of distance covered and points at which to turn back. With the planets at the edge of their vision, they move nearer to the sun and farther from it.

At first glance, the bronze sun and eight planets look like guide signs for visitors. Along with a simple map of the Planet Path threading through the city, they bear standard facts about each planet—its size, period, number of moons, distance from the sun. They also display models of the planets made to that scale. At the starting point, the sun is represented by a large metal sphere, its surface shining a muted gold, its precarious balance supported by a bronze plate. Like a balloon swollen by the heat of sunlight, the sphere seems, if you keep looking at it, as though it might simply drift up into the sky. The planet models, by contrast, have only the faintest presence. Near the top of each bronze plate a little viewing window is cut, and in it a planet object made to scale is mounted. But from Mercury through Mars, that amounts to no more than a speck of metal the size of a pinprick embedded in a thick plate of glass. If you look through the glass, scored with fine scratches, the too-small planets disappear into the distorted scenery beyond. Only with the planets orbiting farther out than Mars do they finally acquire a visible form as objects.

The distance from the sun to Mars is short. With the dog, Nonomiya and I went from the sun to Mars before even reaching the middle of Goethe Street. Every time we came to one of the bronze plates, Nonomiya stopped, and the rhythm of our walk—and of the conversation matched to it—kept catching. Since leaving the station, we had exchanged very few words. I knew the reason was the nine-year distance between us, yet it began to feel as though the Planet Path itself were obstructing our talk. And somewhere inside, I welcomed this. These bronze markers filled the gaps in a conversation bordering on silence.

Once we crossed the river that runs through the city, the street changed its name to Prinzenstraße. It changed from Goethe to Prince, but little in its atmosphere seemed to change. For a while after that no bronze plates appeared, and at last, unnerved by the blankness between our words, I found myself giving a rambling tour of the town. On our left were an antiquarian bookshop, a pizza place, a music shop, a stationer’s; on our right I pointed out the library’s silent presence. Eventually, beside a florist and a used-book shop facing the main street that runs north to south through the old town, we finally came upon the bronze Jupiter.

The dog I brought along when I went to meet Nonomiya belonged to Agata. She was my housemate, and we had been renting a place together for the past year. She had studied economics at a university in Hamburg, worked in an office for a while, and then come to Göttingen to study informatics.

Her dog was about the size of a beagle, with somewhat long black-and-white fur lying in distinct markings, the colors never blending. Most of his face and back were black, but his belly, legs, chest, neck, and part of his tail were white. He had a long muzzle, and his features were oddly ambiguous, as though another animal had been projected onto them.

When people spoke of him, everyone except his owner Agata tended to call him not by name but “the truffle dog.” The nickname had already grown more familiar to our ears and tongues than his own name, Hektor. A truffle dog is, literally, a dog with a nose for truffles. By learning the smell of that special fungus and being trained to detect the scent hidden beneath the soil, such a dog acquires the skill of unearthing truffles. Agata’s dog had acquired his nose for mushrooms from her mother, his original owner. She had lived with the dog in a small town far south of Göttingen and taken free delight in her hobby of walking in the woods. But a year and a half earlier, I had heard, she had died of breast cancer.

Many of the stories about the truffle dog, too, had come down from Agata’s mother. The little town where she had lived merged almost into the forest, and there, it was said, mushrooms dense with fragrance lay quietly hidden. In the late summer three years earlier, on one of the daily walks that had become their habit, Hektor sniffed out a black truffle. Working his long muzzle, he suddenly plunged into the undergrowth and began scratching at the soil with his nose pressed to the ground. As Agata’s mother watched him, a round black lump emerged from the earth. Richly fragrant, it was small but densely heavy. She slipped it into her jacket pocket and brought it home as though nothing had happened. Strictly speaking, gathering wild truffles is forbidden in Germany on environmental grounds. “If it’s only one, they’ll turn a blind eye,” her mother used to say with a laugh. But she did not leave that excavation as a mere accident.

When she saw the dog rubbing his nose against the pocket still carrying the truffle’s scent, she held the truffle up to his nose again and again, weaving the memory of the smell into him. The forest makes music of many scents. The smell of water held in green things. Rotting fallen leaves, and the mold on their undersides. Earth loosening in the warmth of the sun. The cool green scent of cypress tinged with blue. The dry fragrance of deciduous trees layered deep with autumn color. And into all of this a new shade was woven: the scent of truffle. In the dog’s field of smell, the forest must have stood out with perfect clarity. Yet, just as one cannot read every color woven into an Impressionist painting in full detail, it remained a secret between the forest and the dog. From then on Hektor was a truffle dog, but he never found more than three black mushrooms. “He’s such a polite dog with the forest, isn’t he?” Agata’s mother would say, smiling as she buried her hand in his soft black, smoky fur.

After her mother’s death, the truffle dog was taken in by Agata in Göttingen, and he adapted to his new surroundings. This city, too, is wrapped in woods. The trace of the old wall around the old town makes an ideal route for walks, and Agata would take the truffle dog around that ring of forest. Whenever I saw her with the dog bounding around her feet, I thought of a planet revolving with its satellite in tow. On weekends they often went farther afield, venturing into the woods that spread beyond the green space where Neptune’s bronze plate stood. Agata did not seem to expect much where mushrooms were concerned, but she would often joke, “If we find one, let’s share the secret.” Once she showed me a photograph of a truffle her mother had found in the year she died. Small and encased in a hard black skin, it looked very much like a star that had fallen from the sky and burned itself out.

The story of the truffle dog had been repeated so often that the taste and aroma of that black, ball-shaped fungus had already been woven into the tale itself. Through the anecdote, my nose and mouth, of their own accord, try to give off the impression of some unfamiliar meteorite-like thing. The word truffle had begun to walk on its own. It was like a ghost haunting taste and smell.

From the streetscape to the dog—still unable to find any orbit, I found myself voicing little stories about truffles as if we were playing an association game. Taking advantage of Nonomiya’s quiet attentiveness, I let words spill from my mouth in imitation of Agata’s way of telling things. All my quickened tongue could spin was the semblance of a tale—a counterfeit passing under the name of storytelling. It was a bad habit of mine. To avoid the heart of the matter, I would flee into talk of animals, using it to keep a conversation stitched together. I had brought the truffle dog along as an emergency measure, a stopgap for when the conversation began to fray. When at last the words ran out, both Nonomiya and I fell silent, each pretending to be absorbed in the truffle dog’s behavior. The dog kept a rhythm that did not interfere with our walking, but it did nothing to shake loose the catches in our speech. Keeping the span of those nine years always in view, all I still did was circle around it. Was Nonomiya doing the same? However closely I listened to the silence, I could not gauge the depth of his quiet.

When we reached the bronze plaque for Jupiter, we turned left at the corner and made our way north through the town. Apart from postal vans, garbage trucks, and police vehicles, no cars enter this Weender Street, which divides the old town into east and west; only pedestrians’ feet strike the cobblestones.

After Jupiter, our route took us off the Planet Path. Not until we reached Jupiter did the scale model in the little viewing window acquire any real presence to the eye, and Nonomiya lightly traced its outline with a fingertip. A faint creak ran through memory: whenever he described a work, he always did this unconsciously. He would render a painting or sculpture in words, moving a finger across a tabletop or through the air to trace its contours and arrangement, as though he meant to impress the memory into his fingers as well.

“We’re heading toward the bus stop, so unfortunately Jupiter is where we stop.”
“Is Saturn far from here?”
“Much farther on. It’s at the edge of the old town, so you should have a look another time.”

Nonomiya lifted his finger from Jupiter without fuss and went on without hesitation into the city’s bustle, full of the sound of an unfamiliar language.

The crowds on Weender Street were beginning to return, but people now walked mindful of the distance between them, and more of them were walking alone. “For such a major street, it isn’t all that crowded, is it?” Nonomiya murmured, his eyes moving over the avenue. When I had been in touch with Sawada, I had explained the state of the city, but I had no idea how much of it had reached Nonomiya. Since March, the shadow cast by the once-unfamiliar word corona had kept turning into something monstrous whenever you took your eyes off it. “There was a cluster last month, and it pushed things backward again. Soon enough, more people will be out and about,” I said, and he nodded. Just when it had seemed the restrictions were easing, a sudden spike in the numbers had an immediate effect on the city’s atmosphere. The tension was beginning to loosen again, but I still could not shake the dazed feeling of having a door shut right in front of my nose. That abrupt turn from easing back to tension had put a deep crack in my sense of reality, and even now a feeling like jet lag still rendered this place opaque. I should have known all too well how fragile reality was, and yet I must have believed, without question, that its foundations were stable. Perhaps Sawada had already explained some of it; Nonomiya asked no questions, only moved from sunlight into shadow, taking in the city. Rather than try to explain any more of this unreal reality that had gone on for months, I limited myself to telling him the basic rules for living under the circumstances. What he needed, perhaps, was not words but a way of narrowing the distance to reality through the senses. How was he to acclimate himself to a nine-year time lag? How did this place look through Nonomiya’s eyes—this quiet life, warped as if it lay on the other shore?

Partway along, the truffle dog nudged my leg with his nose to tell me he was thirsty. From the bag on my back I took out a plastic bottle of water gone tepid and a small bowl, and poured some out while he hurried me along. The splash of the water rang out louder than I had expected, and I flinched, glancing over at Nonomiya. He was distracted, gazing up at the buildings that lined the street. Their facades were plastered with faces, all of them looking down at him and meeting his gaze. The face of a man wearing a strange smile, the indignant face of a devil—there was something masklike about them, clinging to the city and sending their stares down into the street. Buildings that wore faces, houses with faces of their own: those too were still beyond the reach of my senses. As I grew used to them, would they become as much a part of my time as the faces of my family? Or were they things from beyond the boundary—like ghosts haunting a house—treated as inconvenient lodgers?
