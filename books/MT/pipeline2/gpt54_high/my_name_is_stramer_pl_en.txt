I


NATHAN

He had come back from America for Rywka. He used to say that for four years he had thought about that girl every day, until at last he bought a ticket home on a ship. What he did not say was that he had come back without a penny, and that he had even had to borrow the money for the fare from his older brother. But he still liked to drop American words into conversation, words no one in the family understood.

From America he brought back a leather belt with a brass buckle. A farewell present from his brother for the journey back to Poland. They embraced on the New York dock and never saw each other again. If you looked at the wear on the successive holes, you could trace on that belt how his figure had changed over the years. Nathan himself could hardly believe he had once been so thin.

Like his father and grandfather, he looked like a wrestler. An old wrestler. Broad shoulders, hardly any neck, and a chest thrust forward like a rooster’s. He was proud that people in town called him “Boss.”

In the street, when people asked how things were, he would answer:

“Things are good, but not hopeless.”

He would touch his hat and walk on.

Every few months a letter came from New York. Ben wrote that unfortunately, for the time being, he had to put off his trip to Tarnów. He couldn’t leave the stationery store just now. The school year was beginning and he had too many orders. He had to let a dishonest employee go, and he still hadn’t found a new one he could trust. Christian Christmas was coming soon, the season of presents, and here it isn’t only Jews who buy! His wife had fallen ill again. The crisis had begun; if he left the store now, there’d be nothing for him to come back to in America. But he missed them and was planning for next year. He could hardly wait to meet Rywka, their sons and daughters.

As parents, you know best what they need, so buy it for them on my behalf.

Into the letter, written on company stationery (BEN STRAMER GENERAL MERCHANDISE, 33 Grand St, NY) and folded in three, he slipped green banknotes. He wrapped them as well in purple tissue paper, so that no one could see what was inside. Each time the tissue paper stained the notes, which came out of the envelope green and purple, and Rywka had to wash them gently, then dry them over the stove in the kitchen.

Nathan gave the children only the American stamps from the envelopes. The dollars he put away.

“I work all the time, so I don’t have time to earn money,” he explained to his wife.

And he waited. Waited, waited, waited. Until a good deal came along. A deal that would change the life of the Stramer family. Lift them out of the one-room apartment with its little kitchen, on the ground floor at the very end of Goldhammer Street, carry them high up and set them down only on the top floors of town houses with colored stained-glass windows on the stairwells, in the elegant Polish-Jewish district, preferably near a stop on the tram line Tarnów was so proud of. In one of those spacious, high-ceilinged apartments with many rooms, with a toilet and a bathroom with running water, electric light, and a flower-filled balcony.

There was a brief time when it seemed it might come true. He had happened to buy a whole railcar of rosin at a low price. Someone told him violinists used it to rub the horsehair on their bows. How was he supposed to know there were so few violinists in Tarnów? In the end he even bought a violin and bow himself and made Salek—of whom Rywka said he had beautiful long fingers—play it.

Although he had never been to the philharmonic or the opera, in his mind’s eye he could already see his seven-year-old son there—on the stages of Kraków, even Vienna. Rywka, too, found it easiest to picture him in a little tuxedo, with a bow tie and a cloud of curly hair. Unlike his brothers, he didn’t like fighting or getting dirty.

“Here,” Nathan said, handing him the instrument. “A famous violinist never hurt any family yet.”

And everybody knows how much money there is in ticket sales.

But that came to nothing too.

True, he did send him for violin lessons a few times to a Polish teacher. Only did Salek really get there? Because every time guests came and Nathan asked him to give a “little concert,” Salek made excuses. Until after half a year Nathan stopped asking and told him to “bring the violin here this instant.”

After the performance he said nothing.

Only when the guests had left did he say, shaking his head on his barely visible neck:

“Goddammit!”

No one understood, so he added:

“Money down the drain.”

That evening, for the first time, instead of reaching for the belt, he reached for the bow. At least it would be good for something! And indeed it was. He didn’t have to hit hard at all, and Salek made sounds much like the ones he himself had only just been drawing from his violin during the “little concert.”

At work, behind the till in the Jewish slaughterhouse, Nathan was also waiting for the deal of a lifetime. Which was why, in the heat of his thoughts, his plans, and his meticulously calculated future profits, he sometimes gave customers the wrong change.

“Shit,” he would say if at the end of the day there was money missing from the till.

But sometimes there was too much money. Then he said nothing, only discreetly slipped the difference into his trouser pocket.

I have children, he thought.

When the right moment finally came, he felt like running home from work. On the way he did the shopping. At home he did not sit down for a moment. He helped Rywka prepare the meal. During supper he asked the children what was happening at school. It was another matter that he had no patience to listen. But at least he asked, Rywka thought. Then he told her to sit down and have a rest. He cleared the plates away himself, rolled up his shirt sleeves, and washed the dishes in a basin.

Only when he was done, and the children were already asleep, did he take the dollars, rolled into a little tube, out of the metal leg of the bed.

“I have a feeling this time it’ll work. This is the one.”

Rywka said nothing. But Nathan knew what she was thinking:

Why didn’t it occur to you, with the last deal, to inspect at least one candle properly?

He hadn’t wanted to waste time; he was afraid other merchants would get there before him, that someone would offer a higher price. But what he was most afraid of was that the seller, who had come all the way from Kołomyja, would find out how much candles cost in Tarnów and realize he was selling his for next to nothing. Nathan had to act fast. A chance like this would never come again. Candles four times cheaper than those from the famous Jewish Pszczółka factory, which supplied all the churches in Tarnów! Perhaps even in Kraków. That was what he’d heard, though he didn’t know whether it was true or just advertising. Then again, he’d heard as well that the owner, Mr. Szpilman, quietly gave money to the socialists. But that, in turn, might simply have been anti-advertising spread by Pszczółka’s competitors.

At the moment of paying, Nathan even felt like a swindler for a second. Maybe he ought to add something for that unfortunate seller from Kołomyja, or at least invite him to dinner? But the feeling at once gave way to regret that the man had no more goods to sell. So perhaps it wasn’t worth it after all.

That was how Nathan answered in his thoughts the question Rywka had not asked him.

They sat opposite each other at the slightly wobbly kitchen table; the floor was uneven, and the little piece of wood must have slipped out again from under the table leg. As usual, Nathan tried to shove it back with his shoe. That didn’t work, so he bent down, found the splinter, and put it back in place. Tomorrow morning, when it was light, he would finally clean it and glue it on, he decided.

They both knew that none of his explanations would convince her, and perhaps that was why they spoke to each other without words. Or perhaps they simply didn’t want to wake the children. They only looked at each other, but even so for a moment Nathan thought that in Rywka’s pale eyes, instead of the one candle burning on the table, he saw the thousand he had bought without wicks.

What did it matter that the moment he realized it, he ran straight to the station and with his last money bought a platform ticket? The train with the merchant and Nathan’s dollars, still faintly purple, had long since left for Kołomyja. Nathan stood for a long time among the people on the platform, repeating incomprehensible American words, before he admitted to Rywka what had happened. And that evening, even though the window was shut, the crying of his children could be heard out on Goldhammer Street.

After that he took to his bed and for a week did not go to work. At the slaughterhouse Rywka said the same thing she told the children:

“Nathan has stomach catarrh.”

This time it will be different, his look was saying now.

Rywka was not impressed that his eyes had lit up again, the way they had when she first knew him. Sometimes she only wondered where that energy of his came from, arriving and departing in waves. Once she even asked Nathan.

“I brought it back from America,” he said.

Then she thought of the sea he had told her about after coming back to Tarnów. It too was tossed by waves, there were tides coming in and tides going out. Rarely did it lie still—like her.

“More likely you caught it at sea,” she said, smiling.

In any case, she definitely preferred stories about the sea and great ships to stories about New York. They soothed her. She liked imagining them before she fell asleep.


RYWKA

Nathan had promised many times to take her to the sea, but that was still before the wedding and the children. So far, the farthest she had ever been was Kraków.

Of course when she was young they offered America to her too. What girl hadn’t been offered it? A pleasant, graying man with a signet ring on his little finger once stopped her in the street. A Jew, of course. He introduced himself as an entrepreneur, owner of a thriving factory in Nebraska, and “above all an inconsolable widower returned to Europe in search of love.”

That search even took him to her parents. Rywka’s father threatened to go to the police. It was no accident Galicia was called “Scandalicia” in those days. Everyone knew what it was really about and whom they were really looking for. Even so, two of her friends from Nisko, Nesa Schnur and Lila Flaum, decided to go. Or perhaps their parents had persuaded them and pushed them out of the house? She heard they ended up in Argentina or Brazil. She never saw them again.

In Nisko they said pimps called pretty girls “silver spoons,” “bales of silk,” and “Smyrna rugs.” The less pretty ones they called “barrels of flour,” and the ugly ones “sacks of potatoes.” She still remembered those terms. Maybe because the girls used to call each other them. She also wondered which category they had put her in.

She knew she was too short for a silver spoon. But she had light eyes in a dark frame, thick black hair, and a neat waist. So she was probably no barrel of flour, let alone a sack of potatoes.

Now, on Goldhammer Street, those times seemed unreal to her. She had got married, moved to Tarnów, borne children. A great deal in her life had changed. But had the world changed too? One still heard about pimps trafficking in “delicate flesh.” After all, it wasn’t so many years ago. She still sometimes caught herself beginning a letter by mistake with the date eighteen hundred and... Somehow she could not adjust to the twentieth century.

Maybe because she rarely read newspapers. Who had time for that with six children?

Actually she had given birth seven times. Her first son, Kuba, was born too early, without eyelashes or nails. She reproached herself for needlessly helping Nathan paint and fix up their first apartment while she was pregnant—smaller even than this one on Goldhammer. Once she had even slipped and fallen in front of the entrance. She should have stayed in bed. Though in the end that no longer mattered.

It was winter, they wrapped him in the warmest quilt they had, but it did no good. He lived less than three weeks. How unnatural it was, arranging the funeral of your own child, walking through the snow behind the coffin of your own son. She forced herself to cry. She couldn’t, so she covered her face with her hands. So the others would not see that she wasn’t crying.

The little coffin was not expensive, but even so they spent everything they had on it. They buried Kuba by the cemetery wall.

The night after the funeral, muffled weeping woke her. She pressed herself against Nathan, and only then did she herself begin to cry.

She did not know whether Nathan missed their tiny baby in the same way. She did not know whether he too was afraid Rywka would never get pregnant again. Like his sister-in-law Pepi, who before leaving for America had also given birth too early and gone to hospital with the baby, but came out of it alone. From what Nathan told her, that loss had decided their departure. On the other hand, everyone knew that in America they had the best doctors for such things.

But in the letters Ben and Pepi wrote in an even hand on company stationery, they never once mentioned a pregnancy or a child. It was in the letters traveling in the opposite direction—and it was possible they passed one another somewhere at sea, Rywka thought—that Nathan, in his nervous handwriting, each time informed his brother of the arrival of another Stramer and described the rest. Maybe that was precisely why they never managed to come to Tarnów, though after all they were the closest family. But not once did they forget to put money in the envelope.

Rywka found that easier to understand than Nathan did. Because what if it had been the other way around? What if we were the ones left childless, and instead of dollars news kept coming across the ocean of one American nephew and niece after another? How would we feel?

So she thought of Ben and Pepi with sympathy. And whenever she thought of them, Kuba came back to her mind.

“The first child is for God,” Nathan had said once.

“Don’t talk like a rabbi,” she answered.

Whom would he have resembled? Would he, like the eldest, Rudek, never ask their advice on anything and spend whole days running about town? What did Rudek actually do, and where did he get the money he secretly gave her for the household?

She could only guess.


RUDEK

One Sunday Nathan happened to spot him among a Polish-Jewish gang of half-grown boys who, clowning around, were trailing behind a regiment of Austrian soldiers marching in tight dress uniforms through Tarnów’s main streets. Possibly it was for the first anniversary of Emperor Franz Joseph’s death, because there was even a band playing a march. That was what Nathan had come to see. Or rather, to get a look at the violinist. At that time he was still prophesying a musical career for Salek, and had only just bought him a secondhand Höfner violin. He wanted to see what a real violinist looked like. But why couldn’t he spot him anywhere?

Like everyone else, he stared at the drum major conducting the band, at his white staff with a brass ball at the end. One moment the ball was up, the next it was down, then it spun around its own axis, and in the finale it flew into the air.

I wonder how much they pay him for that, Nathan thought, and stood motionless in expectation: maybe just once the drum major would fail to catch his ornamental staff.

He would sooner have expected that than to run into Rudek here. At first he couldn’t believe his son was there with the others, picking up cigarette butts from the ground, making insulting faces and gestures in the soldiers’ direction. And on top of that bellowing across all of Sobieski Square:

At Ulm, at Ulm, at Austerlitz  
we took it up the arse and said not a word.

Nathan happened to know that song from his own youth. He started shoving his way toward Rudek.

For we Austrians have this custom,  
we take it up the arse and say not a word.

He did not let him sing the next verses. He yanked his son out of the crowd by the collar. And in front of everyone thrashed him with his American belt. A few Austrian officers with waxed little mustaches turned their heads without breaking stride, some of them lifting binoculars to their eyes.

Nathan threaded the belt back through the loops of his trousers and asked:

“Will things ever be better for us than they were under Emperor Franz Joseph?!”

And added:

“And if I ever see you again with a cigarette in your gob, I’ll rip it out together with your lungs.”

That was less than a year ago, but evidently it had worked, because lately Rudek could be seen every day by the wall of the military barracks, on the other side of Goldhammer Street, where the Austrian garrison was stationed, and the cigarettes were no longer in his mouth but in his pockets.

For the past week he had been taking Salek there with him after school. Did Rudek look much older, or Salek much younger, than he really was? Or was it simply the contrast between them that created that impression. A tall, broad-shouldered boy in a flat cap and knickerbockers bent over a slight little boy—besides, squeezed into a tight school uniform with a high collar—really still more child than anything else.

In any case, more than thirty centimeters and a year and a half separated them. Salek did not play football—neither at right wing, in a blue shirt and white shorts, like Rudek in Samson Tarnów, nor in any other position on any other team. He didn’t play anything, anywhere. He never learned to whistle on his fingers or spit properly. When they drank lemonade from a bottle together outside the shop, he spilled it over his uniform. And worst of all, even now it still happened that under a tree he wet the legs of his trousers, although Rudek had shown him a few times how to avoid it.

“You can shake for two weeks and a drop will still fall into your underpants,” he instructed him. “But not on your trousers.”

It was the same with boxing and wrestling. Rudek made Salek practice on their younger brothers, Hesio and Nusek, also without much success. In the end he more or less taught him one blow—head to the nose. After that Hesio stained his shirt with blood, and it ended with the American belt for all three of them.

In any case, when someone picked on him, all Salek did in anger was clench his fists and windmill his arms.

He’s in no danger anyway, because everyone knows whose brother he is, Rudek felt a warmth inside. But he had prepared him as well for the possibility that in another district they might not know, and someone might try to hit Salek. He was to say that he knew Omega, Zachar, or Bänder. There was probably no one in Tarnów who didn’t think of them with fear.

“No one will lay a finger on you.”

He didn’t admit to Salek that he himself didn’t know them either. Why should others know that?

What everyone did know was that Omega, Zachar, and Bänder, if they were not in prison at the time, could be found in the evenings in the company of prostitutes outside the Hotel Polski on Dworcowa Street. But everyone preferred to avoid that. People said they feared no one.

As if by accident, they bumped others with a shoulder, shoved passers-by off the pavement, and knocked the coverings from their heads. School caps, flat caps, hats, yarmulkes, and the fur shtreimels of Hasidim landed in the mud and puddles that were always there after even the smallest rain, no matter the season.

And that was only the beginning. After that the knife-men waited for a reaction. Would the victim dare the slightest objection, even if expressed only by a look, the tiniest gesture of displeasure, visible in the darkness only to their eyes?

“So you’re such a tough guy?”

And right away they would come so close you could smell what they had eaten and drunk that day in the third-class restaurant at the railway station.

Of the three, only Dawid Bänder did not strike at once. First he played with his knife. And that was what people feared most, because Bänder was unpredictable. It happened that he let one go free and suddenly stuck the knife into another.

“Want me to circumcise you, tough guy?” he was once said to have asked the terrified Prince Roman Sanguszko, whose car had broken down near the station one night.

Rudek understood the weight of such an encounter. At school they taught that the Sanguszko family had once owned Tarnów, and on the street that Bänder was the great-grandson of Idel Muc, the legendary chief of Tarnów’s thieves. And because Rudek was one of the most diligent pupils in both places, he understood that descendants of the rulers of the city had stood facing one another.

“Want me to circumcise you, little tough guy?” Rudek and his brothers acted out that scene many times for fun in the yard on Goldhammer Street.

“Anything but that! Mercy on His Princely Grace!” Salek, in the role of Sanguszko, would cover himself with his hands and faint against the wall of the wooden privy.


SALEK

He had picked that up at the theater. As a birthday present, Rudek had given him a standing-room ticket. They were playing Samson and Delilah, directed by Dante Baranowski, who also took the leading male role. During the performance, in the cool, half-empty Sokół hall, he could not concentrate on the actors flinging themselves about the stage, alternately roaring with rage, crying, and fainting. He immediately recognized Delilah as Jadwiga Geron, the elegant wife of the teacher of Polish at the gymnasium, to whom he bowed in the school corridors. Should he now, as usual, at least incline his head discreetly from a distance? He did not want her to feel offended. Nor did he know whether he had the right to sit down, even though more than half the red seats were empty. Whom was he supposed to ask? Besides, all the while he kept wondering whether Dante Baranowski had really been born Dante Baranowski.

Maybe once he too had been an ordinary Salek or Jurek. And only as an adult, in order to appear on posters and go onstage, had he first had to change his name to one with a more artistic sound. That made him think of Goldhammer Street, which when they moved there was still called Zdrojowa.

And how would this sound: Dante Stramer?

After the performance Rudek was waiting outside the Sokół building.

“Rudek, why is Baranowski called Dante?” Salek asked on the way home.

“Baranowski’s mother was simply Italian, and Dante is a typical Italian name.”

How did he do it, that he knew everyone and knew so much about them? Salek looked up at his brother with admiration.

But he too could impress Rudek.

He became popular quickly at the gymnasium, already in the first class, when during recess, at the sight of Father Wątorek, who taught Catholic religion, and Izaak Bleiweiss, who taught the Mosaic faith, strolling arm in arm across the uneven courtyard, he said to his classmates:

“The Old Testament is walking arm in arm with the New.”

True, some claimed someone from an older class had thought it up, but Rudek didn’t believe them. After all, the indisputable witness to Salek’s priority was their friend, a pupil of the same gymnasium, Romek Brandstaetter.

A moment later the joke had spread through the whole shouting courtyard and the brick school building. And the bolder boys began calling it out at the religion teachers.

“Do you hear that, Professor?” Wątorek finally asked.

And Bleiweiss answered:

“Why shouldn’t I hear it, Reverend Prelate?”

All sorts of rumors went round the school about them. For example, people whispered that they liked walking arm in arm so often because neither of them had a wife.

Salek did not listen or take notes in Mosaic religion class. He solved his math homework, wrote Polish essays, prepared for the tutoring he gave after school. And when he was finished, he looked out the window at the people passing in the street. He thought that he too would be walking there after classes, and those who saw him through the window then would simply see a boy walking along the street. There was nothing strange or extraordinary in that, and he himself did not know why he so often returned to the thought that in a moment he would be for others what they were now for him.

He also watched the trash bin at the intersection across the street. He counted how many people between one bell and the next bent over it and put a hand inside. Now there was already a fourth—even though not a quarter of an hour had passed since the bell—an old woman was brushing the rubbish aside, probably looking for scraps of food. Before her there had been a boy, no older than Salek himself, who with a quick motion gathered bent cigarette butts into the pocket of his coat. Rooks came flying to the bin too, and they made not only the most noise and mess, but spoiled other people’s reputation as well, because they scattered rubbish onto the pavement. But Salek was unable to count them.

The Hebrew alphabet now seemed sad to him, gloomy. It reminded him of the poorest part of Tarnów, the old Jewish district. The letters drooped downward like the sidelocks, beards, and too-long black coats, forever muddy at the hem, of Grabówka’s inhabitants.

And anyway he knew everything Bleiweiss taught. Once it had been interesting, he might even have liked it. Fairy tales for the three-year-old boy he had been when his father first led him by the hand to cheder. Not the nearest one to home, where Rudek had gone the previous year, but one much farther away. Salek’s legs hurt, he was almost running to keep up with his father. He did not see the curb, stumbled, fell on his hands, but he dirtied his trousers too, and on top of that they tore at the knee.

“Goddammit!” his father yanked him up.

Salek burst into tears, he no longer wanted to go to cheder.

“Or you’ll go to the one near home.”

The year before, Rudek had come running back from there with a fever and the decision never to go back. It took a long time before Mama got out of him what had happened. Only when she promised she would not repeat it to anyone did Rudek tell her that the melamed not only yanked ears and beat boys with a switch for Hebrew words repeated wrong. He had made him strip naked and stand in the corner of the room, and the other children were to come up and spit in his face. That was when he ran away.

“What did he do that for?” she asked.

“Because I didn’t believe the Jews crossed the Red Sea with dry feet.”

Without a word Salek went on after his father. All day in cheder he kept a hand on his knee and together with the others repeated passages of Torah after the melamed.

The old teacher did not raise a hand against the children, or even his voice, but for every question and every doubt he always had the same unchanging answer:

“Silly boy, it’s written so in the Pentateuch.”

Salek heard the same thing when, after a year of study in that damp room, which at the same time was the melamed’s whole apartment, he dared ask his first question, one he had been carefully preparing for many days:

“Why, at the sight of children sitting on a wall and laughing at the bald head of the prophet Elijah passing below it, did God let two maddened she-bears out of the forest, and they tore forty-two children to pieces?”

It was only Rudek who explained to him that one simply had to respect one’s father and both grandfathers, even though they were bald under their hats.

In any case, luckily God did not repeat anything equally cruel in cheder. Like the melamed, he kept silent at the sight of the oldest boys calmly playing cards, buttons, and slap-hands under the table. He remained equally unmoved when they stood behind the teacher and pretended they were dancing a waltz with him, or when, with disgust, holding their noses, they took his old darned long johns and socks out of the wardrobe with two fingers, or imitated a dog that had mistaken the old melamed for a bitch.

Maybe it was because the melamed was far from the power, holiness, and clarity of mind of the prophet Elijah, Salek thought. Or maybe because he wasn’t bald, and long, stuck-together gray hair stuck out from under his yarmulke.

II


NUSEK

In Tarnów people said that the farther from Grabówka, the cleaner the air. Nusek breathed there only through his mouth. The smell of that district could remind one of the privy on Goldhammer Street, or of the night from Friday to Saturday, when after the Sabbath supper, from overeating cholent, the children could not fall asleep for a long time.

“PKP?” Salek or Hesio would ask every so often.

“RKM,” Rudek would answer them.

“No!” if she was still awake, Rena would shout. “I won’t allow it. Go into the yard.”

At night the two-room apartment on Goldhammer turned into a bedroom. Three beds filled the room, and the fourth the kitchen. Chairs and a table stood in the middle—you had to remember they were there, especially in the dark, on the way to the privy. But Nusek almost always tripped or at least bumped into something.

Rywka had tried to make sure each of the children had as much room as possible. She decided that the tallest and oldest son, Rudek, would sleep in a bed with the youngest, Nusek; next to them Salek with Hesio, only a little smaller than he was; and a little farther off, behind a screen, the girls, Rena and Wela, born last of all. Only the dog Suchard and the cat Milka chose for themselves every evening at whose feet they would spend the night.

“What does PKP mean?” asked Nusek. “What does PKP mean?” he repeated, as though he thought Rudek hadn’t heard.

“You’ll feel for yourself in a minute.”

“Why do you never tell me anything? Why?”

“For the common good. Go to sleep.”

“Just tell me what PKP means.”

“Polish State Railways.”

“But what does it really mean?”

“It really does. Go ask at the station.”

Nusek wondered why they never took him seriously. And how many times had he begged his older brothers to take him with them to the barracks? Not worth talking about.

Surely they still couldn’t be on about that stupid business with the bow. Especially since he had done everything Rudek told him to. He meant well, the best proof being how hard he tried—even too hard.

Nusek closed his eyes, but he couldn’t fall asleep. He heard his father’s steady snoring. Salek saying something indistinct in his sleep. And the bed creaking when Rudek turned over and laid his head on their shared pillow.

He felt his warm breath on his face.

Why do I always get the least of everything? he thought. The least room in bed, the least of the pillow, even the quilt that Rudek tears away from me every night?

“Asleep?” he heard Hesio.

He opened his eyes.

“What?”

“You want to know what PKP and RKM mean?”

“Yes. Tell me.”

“And will you carry wood and coal for me from the shed in the yard for a week?”

Nusek still couldn’t fall asleep, but now for another reason. Every so often he looked with admiration at the sleeping Rudek beside him, his mouth open. Maybe he really did deserve the whole shared pillow, even the quilt? Nusek felt like laughing at the very thought of PKP and RKM. Clever devils! That way they could say it at school in front of the teachers or at home in front of their parents. He would never have thought of it himself. It took someone like Rudek or Salek to invent something like that. All you had to do was rename something—and that was that.

“That head isn’t for wearing a cap,” as Father said when he was hatching another deal.

But the saying didn’t fit Father, and not only because Nusek had never seen him in a cap. Usually, when he left the house, he took one or the other of two old crumpled hats from the peg.

The saying definitely fit the head lying beside Nusek’s on the pillow much better.

Again he remembered the various times he had heard them say PKP and RKM. He suspected it was some sharp curse or a secret code connected with the cigarette trade, with girls, or at the very least with communism. And meanwhile he giggled, because it sounded so elegant: PKP? — Pozwoli Kolega Pierdnąć? “May a colleague fart?” And RKM — Raz Kolega Może, “This once a colleague may.”

He fell asleep smiling. As always, he still wanted to catch that moment when he was falling asleep, and as always, at the last second, he failed.


RYWKA

At night some people dreamed one another. And Rywka was almost certain that sometimes they dreamed the same dream at the same time. Evidently there were single dreams and shared dreams, she thought—like graves in a cemetery, rooms in a hotel, or cabins on the ships Nathan had told her about.

She could have sworn she once heard the children playing football together in their sleep.

“Pass it to me,” Nusek asked.

“Hesiu, to you,” Rudek said. “Shoot for the top right corner.”

“Goal!” Salek shouted.

Rywka waited to hear what would happen next, but there was only silence.

“Nathan”—she pressed herself to her husband and kissed his broad nape—“you won’t believe what I heard.”

“All the children on all the squares and in all the yards in Tarnów are playing it,” Nathan said with his eyes closed, “and there’s no need to wake me on that account.”

He knew what he was talking about. The day before, on the front page of a newspaper at the kiosk, he had seen a long headline: BALL-KICKING EPIDEMIC SPREADING ALARMINGLY IN OUR CITY. It was the last word that had been especially hard to read through the dirty pane. Unlike the first, whose big broad letters had drawn him to the kiosk.

As usual, he did not buy the newspaper, he tried to read at least a few lines on the spot.

There is no yard here where a dozen brats, little Jews or street urchins do not, regardless of the weather, start kicking a ball in the mud. Our only fear is that our ladies may take to kicking too, for then the end of the world would come.

Maybe he hardly ever washes the windows on purpose, Nathan thought with admiration, looking at the old toothless seller, so people can’t read for free.

You don’t understand! Rywka wanted to cry out. They were playing in their sleep!

But instead she said:

“Forgive me. Sleep well, wake well.”

By morning she had forgotten about it. She had to get everyone out of the house. To work, to school, to cheder. In the dark room she laid out the clean clothes they were to put on. In the kitchen she lit the stove. On the table she put before each of them, on a plate, an evenly cut slice of brown bread with butter and poured chicory coffee. Nathan, as head of the family, additionally had the right to a roll for breakfast.

“Daddy, lend me your little roll,” said Nusek. “I’ll give it right back, I just have to eat it first.”

At first Nusek did not know what they were laughing at. Only after a moment did he join in, and in the end he himself laughed the loudest, as if he wanted in that way to make up for the time it had taken him to understand his own joke.

“I’ll give it right back, I just have to eat it first,” he repeated proudly, pointing to himself. “I’m smarter than I thought.”

Before they left, Rywka washed the children’s faces, straightened their collars, combed their hair.

Already in the doorway, to say goodbye, she first stood on tiptoe by Rudek, then bent her knees and gradually came lower and lower, stopping in turn at Salek, Rena, and Hesio on the way down to Nusek and Wela, by whom she crouched.

“You go down like a New York elevator.” Nathan had promised not to repeat that anymore, but once again he couldn’t help himself.

Rywka always kissed the children in the same place. Her favorite one. The place where the forehead ends and the nose begins. She did not know what it was called, so she called it the little saddle.

Every morning the same. Thanks to that—as she kept telling herself and Nathan—she had no time to be bored. These mornings had sunk deep into habit. It even happened that only when she herself was eating her slice of bread or washing the dishes did she realize Nathan and the children had already gone. As if someone else had woken them that day, made sure they put on warm underpants, drank their coffee, and did not leave the house late.

III


NATHAN

“I bought it,” Nathan announced in the doorway, as proud as if he were talking about the Tarnów Nitrogen Works and not a sideboard from the neighbor on the second floor, who was selling off his last pieces of furniture before leaving for America.

“For dirt cheap,” he added once the door was shut.

That was probably what he liked most about the sideboard. And he immediately sent Rudek, Salek, and Hesio upstairs.

“And Nusek?” Hesio asked.

“Too small to be a porter.”

Rudek came down the stairs first. As fast as though he were carrying the piece of furniture down by himself and not with Salek and Hesio, who could barely keep up with him, gripping the oak legs tighter and tighter so the sideboard wouldn’t crash down on their brother.

After the third time they knocked it against the wall, Rudek spoke in the style of Anszel Koszcz:

“Gentlemen... is this what you’re being paid for?”

Salek asked whether they couldn’t take a short break so that he could tie his shoe. When three steps later Rudek set the sideboard down on the first floor, his hands were shaking.

Anszel, the tallest and broadest-shouldered of all Tarnów’s porters, was their unofficial chief. He could probably have brought down an even heavier piece of furniture by himself, without trouble and without help. Not long before, Rudek had watched him hanging about by the Pilzno Gate. In search of work, he moved slowly, with patient step, among the stalls.

Like the earth around the sun, Rudek thought.

And indeed Anszel almost never stopped. If the porters wanted to speak to him, they had to walk beside him, circling in his orbit. It was to him they turned with everything. He was for them the highest authority, from whose judgments on earth—at least on that uneven, muddy earth by the Pilzno Gate—there was no appeal.

Even though, with the black patch over his left eye and the rope slung over his shoulder, he resembled the sea pirates their father used to frighten them with, Anszel kept order. He usually spoke calmly, even with a smile, but everyone knew what threatened a porter if, for example, he stole a parcel, or God forbid a customer who failed to pay for services. More than once Rudek had seen Anszel pull a metal shaft from his handcart with a quick movement.

And how had he lost the eye? Tarnów knew so many versions. Maybe even Anszel himself was no longer sure how it had happened, and that was why he told each person something different.

“In fact there may have been more versions than listeners,” Rudek explained to his brothers. “Because some people heard a different one every time.”

Rudek liked the heroic one best. Years ago, one Saturday in July, hooligans surrounded the Jewish district. They attacked people and broke into shops closed for the Sabbath. They must have planned it well, because besides sticks and pieces of iron they had sacks, into which the stolen goods were going.

“It was their bad luck,” Anszel would say, without ceasing to circle among the market stalls, “that they also attacked the porter families coming out of the synagogue on Fish Square.”

Anszel’s men ran to the Pilzno Gate, where for the holidays they left their carts fastened to one long chain. They pulled the metal shafts out of them. Jewish laborers with crowbars and planks joined the porters. And at the head went Anszel.

“We drove them out of the district. We smashed the wagons they tried to escape in and let the horses loose. We got everything back. Only I lost something.” Anszel would point to the black patch.

“But you saved the city from a pogrom,” people answered him with undiminished admiration. Maybe because neighboring towns and townlets had had less luck.

“They got only a light rap on the paws.” Every time he recalled it, Anszel modestly shrugged shoulders as wide as the sideboard the three Stramer brothers were struggling to carry from the second floor to the ground floor.

At school Salek heard a somewhat less heroic version. Before a boy from an older class repeated it to him, he asked for discretion. Earlier his father had asked the same of him, having in turn been bound to silence by one of Tarnów’s rope merchants.

“I’m telling this only to you,” Anszel was supposed to have said in a low, hushed voice to that trustworthy man walking beside him.

Before he became a porter, Anszel had spent a few years in prison after catching his wife with her lover. It was only not clear whether he lost the eye later, while serving his time, or already during the victorious fight with the lover, whom he killed.

Salek heard from Hesio yet another version of the event, one with no prison in it at all.

“It turns out it was the sleeping Anszel who was attacked with a knife by a jealous husband. He came back earlier than usual from work at the slaughterhouse and found him in his wife’s arms.”

The thought of a bloody slaughter, of a butcher’s knife stuck into an eye, silenced the shouting yard on Goldhammer Street for a moment.

True, Nathan, who through the open ground-floor window had heard what they were talking about, said over supper that he remembered from his youth how a drunken Anszel had had a bicycle accident. He fell into a ditch. He must have gotten himself caught on something there. A dry branch, a bit of wire, broken glass. The next day he woke up without an eye, covered in blood, and remembered nothing.

What impressed the children most in that story was the fact that their father knew Anszel personally.

“I’ve met plenty like that in my life,” Nathan said, waving a hand.

At the same time he demonstrated the Jewish porter’s blow for the children. He called Hesio over and unexpectedly shoved him with his jutting belly. Little Hesio flew into the wall, where luckily they had not yet placed the elegant sideboard with the horse heads sticking out at the top, but even so tears sprang to his eyes. When Salek timidly protested that the porters by the Pilzno Gate and on Fish Square were skinny and would have had nothing to hit with, Nathan did not even look at him, only shook his head.

“In America,” he added quietly, to himself.

“I’m talking about the Jewish porter’s blow in America,” he repeated loudly, as if he had realized that for the rest of the family it was not at all obvious that the real porters, craftsmen, merchants, and all the rest of the pot-bellied world were over there, where his brother Ben had stayed. Among truly tall buildings with real elevators. Truly wide, long streets with sidewalks, full of real shops, cafés, and customers paying for everything in the most real dollars imaginable.

“Even the sky is higher there,” he had blurted out once.

Sometimes Rywka had the impression that Nathan had not really come back from there at all. She guessed that he often imagined how his life would have turned out across the ocean. And that it seemed more real to him than their Tarnów life, which he therefore neglected.

Gently but thoroughly she wiped the carved wooden columns with the horse heads jutting out at the very top. That elegant oak sideboard did not match the rest of the furniture. Not the bench Nathan had knocked together from three pine boards, not the chest of drawers with its four barely opening drawers, not the table forever limping, not the wardrobe standing beside it with its cracked back panel. It did not suit their cramped ground-floor apartment—or rather, the apartment did not suit it.
