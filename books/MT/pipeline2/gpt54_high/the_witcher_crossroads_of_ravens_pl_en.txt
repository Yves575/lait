Kaedwen is a land wedged between the Dragon Mountains to the north, the Blue Mountains to the east, and impassable forests to the west. Hereditary kings of the House of Topp rule there. Their original capital was ancient Ban Ard, but in the year 1130 post Resurrectionem King Dagread granted that stronghold to the sorcerers for a school and moved the capital to Ard Carraigh, which lies in the middle of the realm.

Other principal towns of Kaedwen are Ban Fearg, Daevon, Ban Fillim and Ban Gleann.

The arms of the House of Topp and of the whole realm have since time immemorial been the unicorn—d'or, licorne effrayée de sable.

Around Kaedwen’s royal lands lie frontier territories belonging to the commonwealth, called marks or marches. These are entrusted to margraves, that is to say counts, whether hereditary or appointed by the king. The name comes from the fact that those lands are, as it were, the vanguard and, by pushing ever farther on, are meant to carry conquest onward and wrest new lands for Kaedwen from the elves, shift the borders, and plant their signs—id est, their marks—ever farther afield. These marches are the Western, the Upper, the Lakeland and the Lower.

Chapter One


Despite the sincerest of intentions—and for rather weighty reasons—Geralt simply could not concentrate on the reeve’s chatter. All his attention was absorbed by the large stuffed crow standing on the reeve’s table. The crow, eyeing the witcher with its glass eye, stood on a base of clay painted green, both its legs sunk into that clay. Thus, despite its utterly lifelike appearance, the crow could not possibly be alive; of that there was no question. Why then, Geralt could not stop wondering, had it already winked that glass eye at him several times? Could it be magic? Probably not, for his witcher’s medallion had not twitched or vibrated, not once, not in the slightest. So then was it a hallucination? A delusion? Brought on, say, by the fact that he had been hit on the head a few times?

“I’ll repeat the question,” repeated Reeve Bulava. “I’ll repeat it, though repeating myself is not my habit.”

Reeve Bulava had already assured Geralt several times that repeating himself was not his habit. Even so, he kept repeating himself. Apparently he liked it, though it was not his habit.

“I’ll repeat my question: what was it really about? What did you have against that deserter, to chop him up so horribly? Some old grudge? Because I simply don’t believe, you see, that it was over that peasant and the maiden honor of his little daughter. That you rushed to the rescue. Like some shitty knight-errant.”

The crow gave him another sidelong look. Geralt moved his hands, tied behind his back, trying to get the blood flowing. The rope was cutting painfully into his wrists. Behind him he could hear the heavy breathing of a village lout. The lout was standing right behind him, and Geralt was sure he was only waiting for a pretext to punch him in the ear again.

Reeve Bulava snorted, spread himself in the chair, and thrust out his belly and velvet doublet. Geralt stared at the front of the doublet and identified what the reeve had eaten today, yesterday, and the day before. And that at least one of those meals had been in tomato sauce.

“I’d thought,” said the reeve at last, “that I’d never have to look at any of you witchers again. No witcher has been seen for years. People said that after the year ninety-four hardly any of you came out alive, up there in the mountains. Then word went round that the rest had died off too, of hunger or plague. And now look at this, one turns up right in my village. And the first thing he does is commit murder. Then, caught red-handed, he has the gall to cite some fucking ukases.”

“By virtue of the personal ukase of the year eleven hundred and fifty,” Geralt rasped, clearing his throat, “issued by Dagread, King of Kaedwen and the Border Marches, primo: witchers are permitted to practice their trade freely within the territories of the Kingdom and the Marches and are exempt from the jurisdiction of local authorities...”

“First primo,” Bulava cut in sharply, “it’ll soon be half a century since Dagread turned to dust, and with him his autocratic personal ukases. Second primo, no king is going to exempt anybody from anything for me, because the king is in Ard Carraigh, far from here, and here the local authority rules. Meaning me. And third primo, my lad, you were not arrested for practicing your trade but for killing a man. Catching werewolves and slaying leshens, that’s your witcher business. But as for butchering people, no king ever granted you that privilege.”

“I acted in defense—”

“Daryl!”

The lout obediently punched Geralt, this time in the back of the neck.

“That thing of yours, repeating yourself”—the reeve looked up at the ceiling—“is irritating. Do you know what irritation can lead to? Even in a calm man like me?”

The crow glared with its glass eye. Geralt said nothing.

“You,” said Bulava at last, “are no witcher. You are a defect. You need repair. You ought to be sent back to that mountain den of yours people talk about. I don’t know how things are done there among your lot. It’s possible a botched article like you gets taken apart into component pieces, to be used in the making of new, better witchers. Because that’s how it’s done up there, isn’t it? Witchers are made out of different human bits, stitched together or glued, or however it goes. People say all sorts. So then, not to talk for nothing... I’ll dispatch you back to the mountains, beyond Gwenllech. In a week.”

Geralt remained silent.

“You won’t even ask why in a week?” The reeve bared his yellow teeth. “You like invoking ukases and laws. Well, I’m for the law too. And the law says strangers aren’t allowed to carry weapons here in the district. And you came in here armed.”

Geralt wanted to object that he had not come in at all, he had been dragged in. But he did not get the chance.

“The punishment,” pronounced Bulava, “is twenty strokes of the whip. The punishment will be administered by Daryl here, and he has a heavy hand. You won’t be back on your feet before a week is out. Come on then, take him. Out to the square with him, tie him to the post...”

“Hold on, hold on,” said the man entering the room, stopping the louts. He wore a brown cloak, heavily soiled at the hem. “What’s your hurry, Bulava, with the post and the lash? You want to cripple my witcher? Nothing of the sort. I need my witcher whole and healthy on the works.”

“And what are you doing here”—the reeve planted his hands on his hips—“sticking your nose into executions, Blaufall? It’s enough I have to put up with you forever taking my villagers off for corvée. But don’t meddle in my jurisdiction. That’s not your affair. My jurisdiction. A crime must be punished...”

“Pshaw, no crime,” Blaufall interrupted. “There’s no offense here at all, only self-defense and helping folk in danger. Don’t pull faces, don’t pull faces, because I’ve got a witness here. Come on then, goodman. Go on, don’t be afraid. Tell it how it was.”

Geralt recognized the peasant. The very one he had saved from robbery the day before, and who had fled into the forest instead of thanking him. The father of the little girl he remembered stripped down to her shift.

“I testify...” the peasant stammered, pointing a finger at Geralt. “I give my word that this young man there hastened to rescue me from brigands... saved my property... saved my daughter from dishonor... freed the innocent maid from robbers’ claws...”

“And that deserter,” prompted Blaufall, “set on him with an axe, and the young man did no more than defend himself. Self-defense! Confirm it, goodman, that that was how it was.”

“That’s how it was... Exactly so! Master Reeve, that young man is not guilty!” The peasant was pale and unnaturally loud. “Master Reeve! Release him, I beg you. And here... Please, take this... by way of, hmm... perhaps there were costs, or losses... I’m willing to make them good...”

Bowing obsequiously, the peasant handed the reeve a purse. Bulava quickly slipped it into the pocket of his baggy breeches, so deftly the purse did not so much as jingle.

“Self-defense!” he snorted. “He slashed a man to ribbons with a sword. Innocent young man... Oh, I’d...”

They went out onto the square. The louts shoved Geralt forward without untying his hands.

“And you, Blaufall,” asked the reeve, “what’s got you in such a tearing hurry that you even dragged in a witness? You need that witcher so badly?”

“What, don’t tell me you don’t know. We’re laying a road, the Great Road. It’s to run from Ard Carraigh through the forests all the way to Hengfors. And not just any road, not some track, but a proper highway, dry and level, laid with logs and fascines so carts and teams can pass. The Great Road’s an important thing. Heavy trade will go that way, between our lands and the North, I mean. They say the king himself ordered haste. And there are monsters in the woods and marshes, every other moment some laborer gets killed or carried off by a monster...”

“And since when do you care about laborers? You always used to say a laborer doesn’t matter; if there isn’t one, there’ll be another...”

“To hell with the laborers, most of them are corvée hands anyway. But every now and then a monster does in one of my foremen, and that throws the work into chaos, the whole job goes to hell. Ah, what’s the use talking. I’m telling you, I need the witcher. I’ll miss the deadline. Not only will the devil take my bonus, they may even send an inspection. And an inspection...”

“Always finds something,” Bulava nodded knowingly. “Materials sold off on the side, padded estimates, this and that...”

“Don’t stray off the point,” Blaufall grimaced. “And let the witcher out at once, without delay. I’m taking him straight to the works with me... Well I’ll be... What’s going on?”

“Men from the outpost.” The reeve shaded his eyes with a hand. “Captain Carleton’s men.”

A dozen or so horsemen galloped onto the square, throwing up dust and scattering chickens. Armed. Dressed in bright, gaudy colors, and rather raggedly at that. Only the two in front were better turned out. The commander, a mustachioed man in a moosehide jerkin, with a gilt pendant and a hat decked with a spray of ostrich feathers. And a long-haired elf with a band around his brow, in the green uniform of a scout.

“Captain Reisz Carleton,” Bulava greeted him, stepping forward. “Welcome, welcome. To what do we owe the honor?”

Captain Reisz Carleton leaned forward in the saddle and spat fiercely. Then he gave a sign to the scout. The elf rode up to the post with the crossbeam and deftly flung a rope ending in a noose over it.

“Oho.” Bulava planted his hands on his hips and checked that his louts were standing behind him. “Looks like the captain’s come to my village for a hanging. Ah, and I can even see whose lot is the noose today. I can, I can, those two there in bonds... Ha! So the captain’s caught deserters from his own outpost! The ones who’ve been assaulting my peasants and girls in the woods?”

“Those,” Captain Reisz Carleton twirled his mustache, “I’ve no intention of hanging. They’ll both be whipped raw, made to run the gauntlet through a lane of switches. So they remember. And that’s all. I’ve too few men to hang them for every little thing. Or to let some stray murder them with impunity.”

The captain straightened in the saddle and raised his voice, speaking not only to the reeve but to the village louts, Blaufall, his lackeys, and the knot of peasants already gathering.

“What am I supposed to punish my soldiers for? For what? For going absent without leave? Because they wanted to fuck a girl? We’re out there at the outpost like it’s the end of the world, like exile, like a punishment posting. You get neither beer nor women there... Why be surprised if the lads slip off on the sly now and then and grab themselves some...”

“What the hell,” Reisz Carleton raised his voice still higher, “are women doing wandering through the woods? And this goodman here, what, he had to go that way with his young miss? Couldn’t she have stayed at home? So why be surprised the lads wanted to... I don’t approve of it! I don’t approve, but I understand! Aelvarr? Ready there?”

“Ready, captain.”

“Then give me that witcher, Bulava. He killed one of my soldiers; he’ll swing for it. An example of terror must be set. And don’t cut him down, reeve. Let him hang, as a warning to the others.”

Blaufall stepped forward, looking as though he wanted to speak, then thought better of it. The louts seized Geralt, but uncertainly. As it turned out, quite rightly.

All at once it grew strangely quiet. And as though something cold had passed over the square.

From behind the barns a coal-black horse stepped very slowly into view. It bore a rider. White-haired, in a black leather jerkin studded on the shoulders with silver nails. Over his right shoulder protruded the hilts of two swords.

Very slowly, even gracefully, the black horse passed the peasants and the reeve. Then it stopped before Captain Carleton’s cavalry.

For a moment there was silence. Then the black horse tossed its head. The bit-rings jingled.

“Reeve Bulava,” said the white-haired rider into the silence, “you will release the young witcher at once. You will return his horse, his weapons, and his belongings. At once.”

“Yes...” coughed the reeve. “Yes indeed, Master Holt. At once...”

“Captain Carleton.” The rider inclined his head slightly. “Greetings.”

“Witcher Preston Holt.” Reisz Carleton touched the brim of his hat. “Greetings.”

“Captain,” the rider raised his voice, “be so kind as to take that elf, his rope, and the rest of your subordinates away from here. You are no longer needed. Today’s lynching is cancelled.”

“Is that so?” The captain drew himself up in the saddle and rested a hand on his sword hilt. “Are you that sure of yourself, witcher?”

“Yes. That sure. Farewell. Reeve, has the boy been released? Have his things been returned?”

“Why, you son of a bitch!” one of Carleton’s horsemen roared, wrenching his sword from the scabbard and spurring his mount. “I’ll—”

He did not finish. The rider called Preston Holt raised a hand and made a short gesture. The air howled and whistled, the peasants clapped their hands over their ears. The horseman screamed and flew from the saddle as if shot from a sling, crashing down heavily and limp straight under his comrades’ hooves; their horses shied, snorted, stamped, flung up their heads, one of them reared. The fallen man’s horse bolted off between the cottages, bucking and kicking.

It grew very quiet.

“Anyone else?” Preston Holt raised a gloved hand. “Anyone eager to make a stand? Play the hero? No? Thought so. Farewell, gentlemen soldiers. Is the young witcher in the saddle yet?”

“Yes,” replied Geralt.

“Then ride. After me.”

The Upper March has set up the mark of its frontier on the River Gwenllech. It is known that the ambition of its margraves is to press farther into the valleys of the Dragon Mountains, and thus they are wont to call their domain cismontane, as though it should soon be enlarged by a transmontane part, once the elves are driven farther into the depths of the mountains. Yet the years pass, and no such thing comes to pass.

Chapter Two


The kingdom of Kaedwen was known throughout the inhabited world for weather that was cold and capricious. Shut off to the north by the barrier of the Dragon Mountains and to the east by the mighty massif of the Blue Mountains, the land suffered from the frequent, unpredictable wandering of air masses, which resulted in long and bitter winters, cold springs, and short, rainy summers. As for autumn, that varied—sometimes sunny, warm, and pleasant, and sometimes not at all.

Now, in the month of March, called Birke by the elves, snow still lay here and there in ravines and uprooted hollows, white patches lingering in depressions on the bare slopes. Yellowing sheets of ice still covered some puddles and ditches. The little sun there was seemed to warm things, but when the wind blew from the mountains it burned with frost no less than in January.

Geralt had left Kaer Morhen the day before the Equinox. That was witcher custom. Experience had shown that after winter monsters were at their hungriest and so aggressive that people in villages and settlements were willing to fork out to hire a witcher, even though by the hungry spring time they had eaten through their stores and were practically without means of living. But Geralt had no chance to be hired. Things had turned out as they had turned out: barely two days’ ride from the mountains, bang, a peasant and his daughter, marauders, a bald lout with rotten teeth, slash, hack, and there you had it. He had landed before Reeve Bulava’s court in Neuhold, and from that court, and later from the threat of lynching at the hands of soldiers from the nearby outpost, he had been rescued by that strange white-haired individual with two swords on his back and a coal-black horse under him, whose trail Geralt was now following.

“I suggest,” the strange individual said, turning in the saddle, “that we ride together for a while. Captain Carleton may still want to hang you, it was plain he very much wanted to. He isn’t foolish enough to come after me, but you, alone, may be an easy target. So if my company does not inconvenience you...”

“Not at all,” Geralt assured him hurriedly, urging on his buckskin mare. “Gladly... I... I am...”

“I know who you are. Did your hair go white after the mutations? After the Changes? Loss of pigment, like mine?”

“Well... Yes. But how...”

“How do I know who you are? Because I keep track of what goes on up there in your fortress. And I heard that they’d trained one wunderkind there, called Geralt, and that he was soon to take to the Path.”

“But Vesemir...”

“Never mentioned me? The name Preston Holt never slipped out? Let me explain: Vesemir and I have for some time now been moving on different orbits, so to speak. If you know what I mean.”

Geralt did not really know what an orbit was, but he nodded with a wise expression.

For a while they rode in silence. Side by side.

“So you have left Kaer Morhen and taken to the Path,” Preston Holt said at last. “Your beginning may not have been the best, but that is how beginnings are. I have no intention of reprimanding you, quite the contrary, I examined the corpse of that marauder, and your cuts must be deemed impeccable. Unnecessary, perhaps. Ill-considered, perhaps. Inelegant, perhaps. But, all in all, impeccable.”

They fell silent again, watching a herd of cattle driven out onto the upland pasture and the little cowherd running from cow to cow to warm his frozen feet in fresh, warm cowflops. The flops themselves probably did only a middling job of warming him, but the running certainly did.

“They’re turning the cows out,” Holt observed, “though the grass is barely poking up from the earth. Which means the season has begun and you’ll find work easily enough, Geralt. The villages will soon be willing to pay for the protection of their herdsmen and livestock. Let’s urge the horses on, there, into the birchwood, along the roznos.”

“Along the what?”

“The roznos. This ditch here is a roznos, a channel by which water used to be carried off from the mine. We are, as you no doubt know, in the part of the kingdom of Kaedwen called the Upper March. The wealth of the Upper March is minerals: chiefly salt, but also silver, nickel, zinc, lead, lapis lazuli, and suchlike. At least that was so once. Nowadays most of the minerals seem to have begun to run out. Nothing lasts forever.”

Geralt made no comment.

“See that hill ahead of us? It’s called Podkurek, that’s how it appears on the official maps. The name came about because, close on a hundred years ago, a peasant by the name of Podkurek happened to dig up a lump of silver there the size of a fairish cabbage. A mine sprang up here in no time, biting into the slope of the hill. Large quantities of silver were extracted there, and galena too, lead ore. But the deeper the miners burrowed, the more trouble they had with water. There are more such roznosy than this one, you’ll see them. In the end the cost of drainage made the whole operation unprofitable. The miners moved off elsewhere. They left behind a labyrinth of corridors and workings, partly flooded. And now the best part: the abandoned and flooded mine was occupied and taken into the possession of wandering zatrawce. You do know what zatrawce are, I trust?”

“Zatrawce,” Geralt recited, drawing breath, “are small creatures resembling dog-headed monkeys. Gregarious, they live underground, in darkness. In a pack they are dangerous...”

“They are dangerous as hell,” Preston Holt cut short the recitation. “And they quite often make themselves felt by amateur miners who come creeping here to burrow in Podkurek’s slopes in search of silver, which can still be dug up here. Ah, and there’s an illustration of my discourse: what’s shining over there is the canvas of wagons and tents. We shall ride straight into the camp of the valiant diggers. The first this spring.”

The valiant diggers first greeted them with a delegation armed with shovels and clubs. The threatening faces of the greeters conveyed a simple message: clear off, strangers, we were here first. But their expressions softened quickly enough—inspection showed that the newcomers were not unwanted rivals. And suddenly joy itself appeared on faces that only moments before had been hostile.

“Good gods, thanks be to the gods!” cried the chief digger, hiding behind his back the mattock he had only a moment ago been brandishing in warning. “Thanks be to the gods! Good gods, why, it’s the illustrious master witcher! And word had reached us that you were in the area, and we were just about to send for you! And meanwhile here you are, dropped out of the sky for us, dropped out of the very sky!”

“That is a habit of mine.” Preston Holt straightened in the saddle. “Dropping out of the sky to those in need. For I am a witcher.”

By then several wailing women had joined the diggers. It was getting louder and more incoherent by the moment. With voice and gesture Preston Holt began demanding order. It took some time before it was possible to make out what the matter was and what it was the diggers and their women were actually after.

“We need a witcher, a witcher!” the chief digger cried, waving his mattock. “Those underground creatures that live under the mountain carried off our boy! They attacked him, snatched him, dragged him into the depths! Who’ll save him now if not you?”

“It can’t be two weeks,” said Holt, “since I warned you to keep away from the shaft and the adit. What? Didn’t I say it? To dig on the other side of the mountain? You say the zatrawce took the boy? How old? Five? Long ago? Ah, the day before yesterday? You did not exactly hurry to send for me. Dismount, Geralt.”

Preston Holt dismounted. Geralt could not help noticing that it was not easy for him. And that he limped badly on his left leg. He waved away the women crowding around him and lamenting. He talked for a while with the elder digger, then tugged Geralt by the sleeve.

“Well then, young witcher,” he said. “We must help people in need, harmed by monsters. For we have just been hired.”

“If the zatrawce took the boy the day before yesterday,” Geralt muttered, “there’s little chance he’s still...”

“Alive? Yes, that is doubtful. But perhaps at least we shall find... Hmm... Give the mother the chance to bury what remains... What are these hesitations, young Geralt? A moment ago you rush in without thinking to defend a maiden’s honor and slash a man to death, and now hesitation?”

“Did I say anything?” Geralt shrugged. “Or that I was hesitating? I’m not hesitating.”

On the slope of the hill there still remained traces of wooden structures, probably part of old drainage machinery. A shaft yawned there, partly collapsed, opening on the abyss.

“What remains of the mine,” Holt explained, “is the shaft and the adit, there, farther on. Both lead to the upper workings. The workings wind because they run along the vein, which has been completely exhausted. From there an incline runs down to the lower workings, even more winding, with numerous branches. Below that there is yet another adit, but completely flooded. The zatrawce live in those lower galleries. There may also be holes there, connections with natural caves.

“The plan is this: with my leg I’m not going down, so I’ll go to the adit and make a racket there, draw the zatrawce onto me. Then all hope rests with you. You’ll go down the shaft to the gallery, and from there down the incline. There you may have a chance of finding... what needs to be found. With a bit of luck. Which I wish you, boy. See you back up top.”

“Maybe first,” Geralt ventured, “we could scope things out...”

“Do what?” Holt grimaced. “Ah, I understand. There is no point in scoping anything out. Drink your elixir, medallion in hand, and get down that shaft.”

“With all due respect,” the chief digger standing nearby blurted out, “but that youngster there seems awfully young-like... Will he manage? We’d thought it’d be you, Master Holt, going down. Your own self...”

Preston Holt turned and looked at him. The digger shrank, mumbled, and did not finish.

The gallery was high; Geralt could walk upright with ease, and there were still several handspans between him and the roof. Water dripped from the walls everywhere; apart from the sound of the falling drops there was nothing to be heard. He quickened his pace. He wanted to reach the incline as quickly as possible, before Holt, true to his promise, began raising a racket at the adit.

In the walls of the workings there were deep cavities, traces of ore deposits gouged out long ago. In one such cavity he noticed a strange brick structure, or rather the remains of one. He had no way of knowing that they were the remnants of an ancient chapel.

Miners, which Geralt did not know either, clung to belief in the gods longer than any other social group. Working under conditions of constant danger, they had to believe that some providence watched over them, and that prayers addressed to it would ensure their safety. As may easily be guessed, experience soon proved that prayers were good for nothing, that cave-ins and methane explosions happened just as often to the pious as to unbelievers. But the miners believed all the same, built chapels, burned candles, and prayed. For a long time. But not forever. Reason, as usual, triumphed in the end.

The corridor suddenly began dropping away; he was on the incline now. He listened, but still heard nothing except dripping water.

He seemed to be almost at the lower gallery when it began.

A large stone whistled out of the darkness and grazed his hair. More followed, some of them striking home. One hit Geralt on the head and staggered him, and the zatrawce rushed him from all sides, howling and barking wildly, scratching and biting. There was no telling how many there were, they were darting around too fast. Geralt wrenched his sword from its scabbard only to lose it at once—two creatures pinned him, a third knocked the weapon from his hand with a blow from a great stone. The creature raised the captured sword overhead and roared triumphantly, opening its dog-like maw to the widest stretch. The triumph was premature—Geralt flung off both attackers, seized a stone and hurled it, striking the zatrawiec straight in the teeth. The zatrawiec dropped the sword; Geralt leapt, caught it before it fell, hacked down the creature grappling with the stone wedged in its jaws. With two quick cuts he split open two more. Then he bolted toward the shaft. Behind him broke out wild barking and a rain of stones—again some of them accurate. One struck him in the back of the head—everything flashed, a spray of stars lit the workings. Another stone hit him in the small of the back and nearly knocked him down. Twice the zatrawce caught him, sinking their teeth into his calves; leather gaiters saved him from serious injury. One sprang up and bit him painfully above the knee, where the gaiter did not protect him. Geralt did not slow to kill it.

Under a hail of stones he reached the shaft, scrambled up the remains of the ladder, and hauled himself out to the surface. There he fell and lay. For some time.

“Well, well,” he heard. “You came out. And even without major injuries. I see blood is running from a few places. But it is not pouring from anywhere, and that is already something. I am full of admiration.”

Preston Holt stood over him, gnawing at a roast chicken leg.

“Oh, come on,” Geralt groaned, still lying flat. “Oh, come on! You were supposed to be at the adit... Make some noise... Draw them away...”

“Really?” Holt threw away the bone. “Ah yes. Forgive me, I completely forgot.”

Geralt cursed. With his mouth still against the ground.

“There is more,” Holt said, licking his fingers. “They won’t pay us anything. Because the boy turned up. Came back. He was simply wandering somewhere, and the diggers, as usual, blamed the zatrawce. Get up, young Geralt. Witcher Geralt. Let me help you. Can you walk? Then let us go. As I said, there’ll be no pay. But they will feed us and let us stay the night. The girls will dress your wounds. If you ask nicely, perhaps one of them will be kind to you.”

They went toward the camp and the smoking cookpots. Witcher Geralt found it hard to put one foot before the other.

The round-faced girls dressed him in several places. And fed him. And the diggers allowed them to stay the night. Holt in a tent, Geralt on a wagon.

One of the girls came to Geralt in the night and was kind. But only kind, only a little, and nothing more. Then she went away at once.

At dawn Geralt clambered down from the wagon and began saddling the mare, still hissing with pain. Holt came upon him in the middle of it.

“What’s the hurry?” He rubbed one eye. “Wait. They’ll give us breakfast, then we’ll ride on.”

“Oh, come on,” Geralt ground out. “Maybe I’m not at all sure I want to go anywhere with you. Maybe I’d rather ride alone.”

Holt leaned against a birch trunk and watched the sky for a while. Clear, without a single cloud.

“I fully understand,” he said, “your attitude. But I had to, and I stress had to, put you through a preliminary test, to see what you were like in action.”

“I might not have come out of there.”

“But you did.”

“Not thanks to your help. Which is why now...”

“I ask you,” Holt interrupted, “to ride with me at least until noon. Reckoning roughly, and allowing for the time of day, that comes to five or six miles. Time and distance enough, I think, for you to blow off the anger you feel toward me and look at the world more soberly. And then I shall have a proposition for you.”

“What proposition?” Geralt narrowed his eyes.

“Six miles. At noon.”

It was indeed close to noon when the sky suddenly blackened with wings and rang with the flapping of feathers and one vast croaking. Dozens, if not hundreds, of black birds rose from the ground and the branches of the surrounding trees and flew off.

“Ravens,” Geralt sighed. “So many ravens! Impossible! Ravens don’t fly in flocks! Never!”

“Undoubtedly,” Holt conceded. “So many ravens at once is unheard of; I am surprised myself. Undoubtedly we are dealing with something unusual. And we also happen to be in a somewhat unusual place. If you have noticed.”

“A crossroads.” Geralt looked around. “A place where roads part.”

“A crossroads. A symbolic place. Four roads, to the four quarters of the world. A place of choice and decision. A choice you must now make, Geralt. Witcher Geralt.”

The ravens settled on the higher branches of the trees. They croaked, watching the riders.

“Three of the roads, including ours, are your roads as a solitary witcher, the fate you chose when you set out from Kaer Morhen onto the Path. If you take one of those three roads, if that is the decision you make, we shall part. But if you choose the fourth road, you will hear my proposition.

“I am, as you have no doubt noticed, advanced in years. Let me add on my own account that I am very advanced in years. You would be surprised how very. I am also, and that is equally plain and impossible to hide, a cripple. My days of witcher glory are already a song of the past. I shall not take to the Path with swords again; the brightness of my blades, if I may put it that way, will no longer scatter darkness. But darkness remains, monsters still come battering in the night. You can face them and defeat them. People threatened by monsters await your aid.

“Yes, yes, here I am waxing poetic, and you are waiting for the proposition. Here it is: I propose cooperation. I was known here in Kaedwen, and still am. I never lacked for clients, and even now people ask me for help I can no longer give them. But you can. I have looked you over, and I say: be my successor, Geralt. Instead of wandering the roads hungry, come live with me. Make use of my reputation, and you will not lack for work. And after work you will have somewhere to return to. And somewhere to winter. As for me... It will make me happy that someone continues my work. And also, I do not conceal it, that someone will help support me in my old age.

“You do not have to decide at once. For the time being it is enough if you go on not alone but with me. Keep me company a while longer. Agreed?”

“Well... all right.”

“Then let us ride. Let us leave this crossroads to the ravens.”

The ravens bade them farewell with croaking.

“Do not take offense,” Holt turned in the saddle, “but I insist that in my company you try to speak more or less properly. In particular, do not say ‘scope things out’ and ‘oh, come on.’”

A dispute arose over it, and bloodshed threatened. Hengfors, though small, had a mighty protector in Kovir, thus the margraves had to hang their ears and agree to peace.

Chapter Three


As usual, the first to notice them were cats and children. The cats—of which there were no end on the edge of town—reluctantly yielded the way; as they slunk off they turned their heads and hissed. The children fled home crying and wailing, abandoning whatever they had been playing with, mostly clods of dried mud.

Aside from cats and children, none of the inhabitants of the little town of Spynham paid the incoming witchers the slightest attention. One might have thought riders in black cloaks with swords on their backs rode into Spynham so regularly they had ceased to arouse even the least curiosity.

Preston Holt knew the place; he knew in which stable to leave the horses. They went on foot from there, along a muddy street, scattering more cats and more snot-nosed brats.

“Great lord,” a beggar woman squatting by a wall with an infant in her lap called out to Geralt. “Great lord, spare a coin... for milk for the baby...”

Before Holt could react, Geralt pulled a mark from his purse and tossed it to the old woman. She thanked him effusively. Holt kept silent for some fifty paces, then finally stopped.

“You are no great lord,” he said through his teeth, looking Geralt in the eye. “You are, however, a great fool. For a mark one can buy the milk of twelve cows. As you will soon learn, they will want to pay you little more than that for killing a monster. And killing a monster is a somewhat greater effort than whimpering for alms and baring rotten teeth. And that baby was probably borrowed from a crony sitting in the tavern waiting for a customer to suck off in the privy for half a mark. I warn you, don’t come down this street again. There’ll be a great laugh if you show your face here once more.”

For a moment Geralt had the urge to stand up to Holt and answer back, tell him to stop nagging and mind his own business. He did not. Somehow, almost reflexively, he had taken Holt for a senior, almost for a master. Perhaps because Holt resembled Vesemir extraordinarily. In behavior and in speech. Physically he was very different.

Ahead of them gleamed the whitewashed colonnades of a temple, beneath which squatted a whole army of beggar women and men. The temple had been closed and abandoned for years, but the beggars still squatted there. And still stretched out their hands for alms. From habit.

Holt prudently drew Geralt over to the opposite side of the street.

“Do you know why we are called witchers?” he asked. “Because we are children of witches.”

“Oh, come on...” Geralt faltered. “I mean, what a notion. Witchers from witches. Give it a rest.”

“It may strike you as amusing, but that happens to be true. The first witchers were children of women with uncontrolled magical abilities, called witches. They were not right in the head, and lusty youths often used them as sexual playthings. The children, the result of such amusements, were abandoned. Or left on doorsteps. And from orphanages and shelters they sometimes found their way to witcher schools.”

“Oh sure. You made that up. It wasn’t like that.”

“It was. All of us witchers descend from feeble-minded girls. Doesn’t that amuse you?”

“Not in the least. Because it wasn’t like that at all.”

“It was, it was. But long ago! These days you won’t find witches under temples. Sorcerers exterminated them all. Ha! Nothing lasts forever.”

The closer they came to the market square, the more crowded it grew. For Geralt this was something new. He was not used to crowds and felt bad in them. The noise frayed him. He was beginning to lose his breath, and the stink was growing more and more oppressive. It smelled of smoke, scorching fat, rotting fruit, dung, and the devil knew what else besides.

In the market square they had to enter a lane of stalls and a crush of customers. Here too all that awaited Geralt was novelty and surprise. And at last wonder. Never in his life would he have thought there existed in the world so many trades and such an abundance of wares for sale. And so many willing to buy. Straps and leather goods, glazed and unglazed clay pots, fur hats, little sheepskin jackets, rawhide shoes, embroidered kerchiefs, copper frying pans, rakes, pitchforks, hoe handles—and bagels, bagels, bagels.

Holt paid not the slightest attention to the marvels on the stalls. But at one point he stopped pushing his way through and caught Geralt by the sleeve.

“Eyes down,” he hissed. “Eyes down, don’t gape.”

“Why?”

“Sorceresses.”

Geralt obediently looked away. Reluctantly. The two women at the stall with amber jewelry drew the eye with almost magnetic force. By the richness of their dress. And by beauty as if from a picture.

“Those two,” Holt explained once they had moved away, “regard ordinary people as cattle, and hate witchers. They are quick to pick a quarrel; they may take staring for provocation. Best, too, that they do not sense our medallions.”

Holt stopped before an imposing house above whose door a sign had been nailed. The picture on the sign, and by no means a bad one, showed a mermaid with an ample bosom. The inscription beneath read: “Lorelei.”

Holt seized the knocker and knocked energetically. Very energetically.

The massive door opened, and in it stood a broad-shouldered bruiser with a jaw like a loaf of bread. For a long moment the bruiser measured the witchers with his eyes. Then he stepped back and made room for them with an inviting gesture.

Holt, evidently a regular, removed both swords from his back without being asked and handed them to the bruiser. Geralt hurried to follow his example.

Disarmed, they entered an antechamber lit by several lamps. Geralt had difficulty drawing breath; the room was thick with the scent of perfumes and incense. On the wall hung another painted mermaid. Equally busty. There was no inscription.

“Master Preston Holt,” said the woman entering the room.

“Madam Pampinea Monteforte.” Holt bowed. Geralt bowed too. And closed the mouth that a moment before had fallen open. From admiration.

Unversed in the art of ladies’ dressmaking, Geralt could not know that the fabrics of Pampinea Monteforte’s black gown were sheer chiffon, muslin, and crêpe. Nor did he know that it was a great art to sew a dress so that it concealed by revealing. And the other way round.

Nor did Geralt know what the quintessence of femininity was.

But what did it matter that he did not know, when he could see it.
