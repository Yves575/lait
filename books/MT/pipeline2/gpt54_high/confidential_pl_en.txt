The Clamour of the Past

Unless you know how to get ahead of events and, by casting a spell over the future, write a good ending. You don’t; I know what I’m talking about. You’re bogged down, your legs tangled in dried roots. The drought doesn’t suit you; what you really need is months of downpours, the roots would turn slippery and you’d scramble free faster. For now, I recommend exercises in bidding memories farewell. You’ve been behind for years; you need to reckon with linear time. Memory loops are the preserve of old people, whose retrospective vision the passing years cut short. Time to set time free. Do it slowly: the body can play tricks, and may react to violent change with shock. Turn the circle into a timeline, not a segment—that sounds like a strange geometry problem.

I can’t promise that once time finally starts moving forward, life will take on colour. Everything has its faults and few virtues. Lift your head out of the clamour of the past. That’s where you come from, but there’s no one left there except you. Look around and throw out the things your children won’t want to inherit. Do the same in your head, though that is much harder. Whatever you can, tell as threnodies. Sometimes that works; it lets you put the subject at a safe distance. Safe, meaning the kind of distance that opens you to thinking about your own life. Have you ever thought about that? Are you living well? Whose life are you living? It isn’t about whether they’d be proud of you anymore; that’s over. Start thinking about tomorrow today.

The English

They were already very close; all that remained was to leave the square and walk two hundred metres.

“If he starts bellowing again for the whole street to hear, this time you can go by yourselves,” she said.

Ever since the boys were born, Sunday lunches at her in-laws’ had become obligatory. Thankfully not every week, but at least once a month. The script repeated itself exactly.

They would come round the corner and look to the same spot: the balcony on the second floor. He stood there waiting, though it was still a quarter to two. You could see him from far off, an elderly red-haired man energetically waving his arms. The closer they got, the clearer it became that he wasn’t limiting himself to frantic arm-waving.

“How old are you, for God’s sake?! Can’t you stick to an agreed time?! I’ve been standing here for fifteen minutes, scared to death something’s happened to you! How dare you put me through nerves like this?!”

“I’ve said it, and I mean it. I’m not putting up with this any longer.”

“Mum, come on, don’t worry. Grandad’s just like that.”

She looked at her husband. Helplessly, he spread his hands and gave her a pleasant smile, lifting his bushy eyebrows. She didn’t want to seem like a hysteric, but she had long since had enough of these balcony scenes.

“He behaves like a madman—maybe this time you’ll actually say something to him?” she asked, though she knew perfectly well what the answer would be.

“What can I say to him? He’s right: he’s scared to death and there’s nothing we can do about it. Let’s agree that when he dies, we’ll stop coming to see him.” He looked at her with a warm smile.

“Do you think Grandad will die today?” one of the boys asked.

“Not yet, I hope.”

“And you, Mum? Would you like Grandad to die today?”

“I’d rather he didn’t. We’ll have more than enough excitement as it is.”

Ignoring the ever louder shouting from the balcony, they reached the stairwell, passed the letterboxes, and started up the stairs. They never took the lift; it was so small the four of them had no chance of fitting in.

Grandad was waiting in the open doorway. First he bent down to the older grandson, hugged him too hard and a little too long.

“My firstborn, my firstborn. Let me look at you—my, don’t you look clever. Who’d have thought you’d be born at all!”

Then it was the younger one’s turn. He hugged him too tightly as well.

“You’ve got black curls—maybe they’ll lighten. Good thing you’re not ginger.”

Without taking his eyes off the boys, he hugged his son, and only at the end greeted his daughter-in-law.

“What beautiful grandsons you’ve borne me. Come in, come in.”

“Dad, could you spare us the shouting from the balcony next time? We agreed on two o’clock, we got here fifteen minutes early, and you’re yelling that we’re late?”

“Everything’s fine, don’t upset yourself. I was worried a car had hit you, or some other misfortune had befallen you... The important thing is that we’re all alive and together. By the way, I wonder whether I’ll live to see great-grandchildren. Well, boys, can I count on you?”

“No saying whether you’ll live that long,” said the younger grandson.

“Well, listen to him—what a wit!” said Grandad with a smile.

After the greetings they went into the dining room, where a large round table stood. A white tablecloth, plates with gold edging, silver cutlery—everything was already laid out. At the table sat an old woman, tiny and shrunken. She had suffered from Alzheimer’s for years and did not react to the noise filling the room.

“Boys, say hello to Grandma,” their father said.

They didn’t like going near her; they didn’t understand what was happening to her. She rarely spoke, never in Polish. They went over, quickly kissed her on the cheek, and sat down in their places. Sometimes a barely visible smile passed across her wrinkled face; it was hard to tell whether she was aware of it.

Grandad’s housekeeper did the cooking. She set the pots and pans on the windowsill and left, despite the heartfelt invitations to sit down at table with everyone. Chicken broth with homemade noodles, minced-meat cutlets, mash and lettuce with sweet cream, which no one ate except Grandad. The youngest grandson would wolf down the whole meal so he could get a second helping of soup before it vanished back into the kitchen. Afterwards he would politely ask if he might leave the table and lie down on the floor to play with his toy cars.

The pattern of those lunches never changed. It all lasted about an hour and a half; then Grandad went for a nap, and they left, no one stopping them.

“The bodybuilder from Dachau picked up a brick and got winded,” the younger grandson said one Sunday, lying on the floor with a belly full of soup.

“Oh God!” Grandma groaned—in Polish.

Silence fell, and the parents’ eyes turned to the older son.

“I didn’t teach him that!”

“The boys at school taught me,” replied the youngest member of the family, entirely unruffled.

“Why did you decide to share that with us just now?” his father asked.

“It came back to me.”

“Look at him, it came back to him! And do you know that some people never managed to forget?” his agitated grandfather asked.

“Yes, I do.”

“And how would you know that, if I may ask?”

“From you. You talk about it all the time. I don’t understand why you’re always talking about Jews.”

“Because we’re Jews,” his grandfather answered.

“All of us?”

Grandad looked around and smiled.

“Yes, all of us.”

“Me too?”

“You too.”

For a moment the boy went back to his toy cars.

“I’d rather be English,” he said after a long silence.

“Shall I tell you something?” Grandad asked.

“Go on.”

“I’d rather be English too.”

Grandad reached for his glass of tea, but by then it had cooled enough to stop interesting him. He got up from the table and went to take a nap.

I Tell Everyone

He was the prankster type. He loved medicine and women, in exactly that order. And half the city loved him back—the half he treated. The other half was divided between those he drove out of their minds and those who were indifferent. He was red-haired and freckled, which is to say temperamental. He could smack someone in the face out of nowhere, drink half a litre of vodka, and carry on as though nothing had happened.

He had lived long enough to reach the second half of the twentieth century, so he decided to make the most of the chance and live without giving up his pleasures. He considered his quota of tragedy used up. His wife took charge of bringing up the child; he got involved only when the situation called for a doctor. He thought a father’s lot was a thankless one, like in that joke about the little boy who wouldn’t eat. The whole family worried over him: he was skin and bone, he ate nothing. At last the father couldn’t stand it any longer and asked what his little boy would most like. After thinking it over, the boy said, “Shit.” “What do you mean, shit?” “Just shit. Shit on a plate.” The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, then he got up and went to the bathroom. He came back with shit on a plate. Set it in front of the boy. The boy inspected it and said he wanted fried shit. The mother looked at the father, the father at the mother, then he got up and went to the kitchen. He came back and set it in front of the boy. The boy looked at it and said he wanted Daddy to taste it. Mother at father, father at mother. He tasted it, said it was excellent, and urged his son to start eating at last. “I’m not eating it, Daddy ate the best bit.”

The household ran, the son grew like a weed, and the red-haired doctor kept meeting new women and spending blissful hours with them. When friends asked how he was, he would tell the joke about the Jew who came to a priest to confess. The priest asks whether the penitent is Catholic. He isn’t. Then why has he come? Because it’s very important to him. The priest goes into the confessional, and the Jew starts telling him about all the women he’s slept with lately: that blonde, the one with curly hair, another had such tiny breasts... “Why did you choose me in particular to tell this to?” asks the priest. “I tell everyone.”

His wife pretended not to hear. After what they’d been through, she knew life could bring more serious problems. Sometimes she complained to a friend, and when the friend got home she would ask her husband to talk to the doctor. They would arrange to meet, go out for vodka, and the family friend would gently explain the mission he’d been sent on. The doctor would look at him and say that just then a joke had come to mind about a tax collector who comes to the Jewish community. “What do you do with the wax from melted-down candles?” “We collect it and make new candles from it.” “Aha. And what do you do with matzah crumbs?” “We make delicious matzah balls from those.” “Aha. And when you circumcise little boys, what do you do with those, well, you know?” “Ah, those. We collect them, send them to the tax office, and they send us back pricks like you.”

He smothered friendly impulses in an instant. If his interlocutor persisted, he would call the waitress over and ask her to open the window. “Nothing to be afraid of,” he would say. “My friend here isn’t an eagle. He won’t fly out.”

The son married a tiny brunette, a graduate of the Academy of Fine Arts. The doctor had had other plans for him, true, but he accepted it and stopped looking for the perfect match.

The red-haired doctor’s wife fell ill with rapidly advancing Alzheimer’s. He took excellent care of her. He brought a young girl from the provinces into the house. She cooked, looked after his sick wife, and got along very well with the doctor too.

To his grandchildren he became a marvellous doctor and an odd grandfather who had his moments. Once a year he took them shopping. To everyone’s surprise, he was capable of buying them a billiard table or a bicycle. Sometimes he took them to a restaurant and, as soon as the order was in, disappeared. Only years later, as they were growing up, did his grandchildren understand that the main reason for those outings had by no means been any desire to spend time with them, but a waitress in a short skirt. He didn’t know how to be a grandfather, but he was proud the family was growing.

A year after his wife’s death, Grandad invited his older grandson to lunch. Once they’d chosen their dishes, he didn’t leave the table as he usually did. While they waited for the food, Grandad was reminded of the joke about Rosenzweig, who walks around the house all night. Around five in the morning, his furious wife gets up and asks him what he’s pacing for. Rosenzweig answers that he can’t sleep. “Why can’t you sleep?” “Because I owe Hozenduft money.” “A lot?” “A lot.” “Then call him and tell him you won’t pay him back.” The husband thinks for a moment and calls. “Rosenzweig here. Yes, I know what time it is. I just wanted to say I’m not paying you back. Good night.” And he puts the receiver down. “And now what?” he asks his wife. “Go to sleep. Now he’s the one not sleeping.”

The grandson, though trained in the art of joke-telling from childhood, doesn’t understand why his grandfather is telling him this particular one now. A pretty waitress brings the food. Grandad smiles at her, but stays at the table.

“Listen, there’s a Jewish law that says a healthy man shouldn’t be alone. It also says that when a wife dies, the husband may marry her sister. Your grandmother’s sister died back then, when everyone did.”

What now? the grandson thinks. Grandad takes a deeper breath and announces that he’s getting married. He immediately adds that his son won’t be pleased with his choice of wife.

“How do you know?” the grandson asks.

“Something tells me. People rarely like their stepmothers. My half-sister couldn’t stand my mother.”

“Did she have reason to?” the grandson asks.

“Oh, she had nothing but reasons!”

The grandson worries that he has been deputised to pass this strange news on to his father. He can tell it will be neither easy nor pleasant. Rescue comes unexpectedly.

“But for now let’s not say anything to your father...” Grandad decrees, then trails off. “A second wife, one for your old age, you choose without thinking of anyone else. You look for someone who’ll understand you. Even if you know she speaks before she thinks, that she’s difficult, that she hurts everyone around her. You do it because you want to drown out the loneliness. You think you’ll deal with the mismatch later.”

The grandson is in his teens and feels that later may be too late, but these are adult matters, after all. Back then he still doesn’t know that in his family the stepmother type will prove hereditary.

Underwater Odyssey

A lie does not have short legs. Father always said it did, but he had no idea how wrong he was. It floats to the surface when too little care is taken to hide it.

When he was little, he didn’t lie—they said he made up wonderful stories. Once he became a teenager, they started calling it lying. He didn’t dwell on it much; he managed life to suit himself.

He forged his system of values to fit the needs of the moment.

The most important rule: calm parents, calm life. The system had its gaps, but over time it became an integral part of the cycle: keep his parents calm between parents’ evenings; survive, with dignity and hope for a better tomorrow, their return from parents’ evening. Find solace in the silence after the row and not take their reproaches or anger personally. After a few days, give them something to lift their hearts; it didn’t have to be true, it just had to work for the time being. Remember that “calm parents, calm life” was the priority, and that the best moments came between parents’ evenings. Don’t go charging in, but don’t give them a walkover either.

He was a cheerful child, the first postwar child in his family. He could feel that they did not take unreliability well. Naturally, he developed a modus operandi. You could say he took his cue from nature. He flowed like a stream—where the resistance was less, not where he was expected. A principle as simple as it was frowned upon.

The door opened and a shaft of light shot into the cinema.

“Is he here?” someone called, using his surname.

He thought he must have misheard.
