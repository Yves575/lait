1 The Ark

I

Leaving the walking path near the highway, the seven of us pushed into the scrub woods, stepped over rotten logs and fallen leaves along a mountain trail, and passed through a field thick with dead grass in the middle of who knew where.

By the time we reached an old wooden bridge spanning a ravine some ten meters deep, the sun had already vanished behind the mountain ridge.

Ryuhei gripped the railing—made of lashed-together logs—with his thick arms and shook it.

When the bridge answered with a creaking groan, he twisted his wrestler’s rugged face into a grimace and said to Yuya beside him, “Hey, we’re seriously crossing this? You didn’t say anything about that. This thing’s not gonna drop us, is it?”

“Nah, it’ll be fine. I crossed it before, and it wasn’t that bad. Totally doable. See?”

Yuya stepped out onto the bridge and spread his arms, rocking his body to show us.

There wasn’t any other way anyway. The six of us had no choice but to follow him.

Once we were across, I pulled my phone from the pocket of my windbreaker and checked the time: 4:48 p.m.

Seeing the phone in my hand, Hana, in her gaudy fluorescent hiking clothes, quickened her pace and came up beside me. Holding up her own phone in her right hand, she asked, “Shuichi, you getting a signal?”

“No. Been out of range for about an hour.”

“Yeah, same. So we’re not getting back to the villa tonight, right?”

No one answered. We all knew that was impossible.

Beyond the bridge, the ground opened into a rough field hemmed in by steep mountains. A few hundred steps farther on, Yuya shouted loudly.

“There, there! I see it, I see it! Just a little farther. It’s right there.”

All the way here he had our dissatisfied, distrustful looks boring into his back, and there was relief in his voice now. Even so, I still couldn’t see anything that looked like the entrance to a building.

This had started that morning. After we’d all gone boating on the lake, Yuya had suddenly said:

“There’s this really interesting place you can walk to from here. Wanna go? Deep in the mountains there’s this huge underground structure. Sounds like it used to be for some pretty sketchy shit or whatever, but I bet by now nobody even knows it’s there anymore.”

Since the day before, we had been staying at a villa in Nagano owned by Yuya’s father.

Yuya was a friend from college, and he was the one who had proposed we all get together. It was a small reunion of the six of us who used to hang out all the time back in our student days.

I hadn’t seen any of them in two years. Yuya used to have bleached hair and black piercings; now he’d stopped dyeing it, and only the piercings were left, and my eyes still hadn’t quite adjusted.

I had my own reasons for bringing my cousin along, so there were seven of us staying at the villa.

A huge underground structure in the mountains. Even hearing it put that way, none of us really knew what to make of it.

An underground structure sounded troublesome enough as it was, and apparently this one was enormous—but who would build something like that, deep in the mountains, and for what? It was hard to believe, but Yuya said he’d seen the place himself about six months earlier.

It was intriguing, and if it wasn’t too far, there seemed no reason not to go.

But things hadn’t been as advertised. We walked and walked and still didn’t reach it. Yuya, who had made it sound like an easy twenty- or thirty-minute stroll, spent the whole time staring anxiously at the map on his phone.

Naturally, the underground structure wasn’t on the map. Yuya said he had saved the location in his map app when he’d visited before, but apparently the pin was quite a long way off. By the time we finally got our bearings after losing our way, it was already beginning to get dark.

“So, Yuya? This underground structure—you mean we’re spending the night there, right? There’s no time to head back now. You sure that’s okay? Didn’t you say it was a pretty bad place?”

“No, I said it might’ve been used for bad shit a long time ago. A long time ago. It’ll be fine if we just use it for a bit. It’s not like anyone’s there. It’s basically just exploring ruins.”

Ryuhei and Yuya kept walking ahead, leaving the rest of us some ten meters behind.

It had been like that the whole way here. In a way that made it sound as though he were speaking for all of us, Ryuhei kept up a stream of complaints to our guide, Yuya.

Seeing the state of him, Mai, who was walking on my right, turned a smile toward me that was either troubled or conciliatory. In the deepening dusk, her pale face made her long-lashed eyes stand out all the more clearly.

I started to answer, but if I spoke, Ryuhei would surely hear, and I couldn’t bring myself to say anything. Mai didn’t seem to want a reply either; before Ryuhei could notice, she looked away.

When I glanced back, Sayaka, who had fallen a little behind, jogged up to us. Her warm-brown hair was tied in a bun, and there was sweat on her brow.

She voiced something she seemed to have been worrying about the whole time.

“Um, with this underground structure... what about toilets? And for sleeping, are we talking about lying on the floor with our backpacks as pillows? Is everybody okay with that?”

Yuya hadn’t told us anything like that—nothing like the sort of information you’d get in a guide to an inn. We hadn’t come intending to stay the night.

My cousin Shotaro, who was walking a step ahead of me, answered her.

“Best not to expect too much. It’s probably a building with quite a history behind it. Still, it has to be better than spending the night out in the open. Being underground, it probably won’t get that cold.”

“I guess that’s true. It does get pretty cold at night.”

Sayaka made a point of agreeing politely with Shotaro.

I had only introduced my college friends to my cousin the day before. Shotaro was fitting in with them better than I had expected.

Five years earlier, Shotaro had inherited a substantial fortune from my aunt, and ever since then he had drifted through life without taking a regular job—traveling for a while, then getting absorbed in geological research, and so on. It wasn’t that he meant to coast forever on his inheritance, either. Sometimes he would take a million yen or so abroad and come back with several times that.

He was my cousin, so I had known him longer than any of my friends. He was the person I relaxed most around, and yet I still felt I had never grasped the whole of him.

I had brought Shotaro along because I had sensed the possibility of trouble hanging over this gathering. He said he had always been interested in the geography of the area, which made it easy enough to bring him.

So far, none of the trouble I had worried about had materialized. Instead, we had ended up heading for an unexpectedly enigmatic underground structure. It was reassuring to have him with us; he seemed like someone who could sort things out if anything happened.

II

In the middle of a wasteland ringed by precipitous mountains, Yuya suddenly stopped. Then he pointed at the ground and shouted.

“Found it! Look, this is it. The entrance.”

Yuya crouched down, thrust a hand into the dead grass, and lifted a round hatch about eighty centimeters across, like a manhole cover.

When I peered inside, the shaft dropped straight down into the earth. The sides were concrete, with iron bars set into the wall as a ladder.

“So you go in from here—”

“What? No way. Creepy. It’s so narrow.”

Hana shone her phone’s light down into the hole. It wasn’t bright enough to reach the bottom.

I felt the same. Of course, out in mountains like these, it was only natural that the place would be something a bit mine-like, but from the way Yuya had talked about it, I had imagined something more civilized.

“No, I mean, sure, the entrance looks like this, but once you’re in it’s fine. It’s seriously huge inside. It goes down three levels. We can make it through one night.”

Hana and the others were clearly reluctant, and I wasn’t exactly eager myself.

Ryuhei was the first to move.

“Whatever. I’ll give it a try. I can get through like this, right?”

Watching out so his backpack wouldn’t scrape, Ryuhei started down the ladder. After glancing at the expressions of the three women, Yuya followed behind him, not wanting to let him get too far ahead alone.

After whispering to one another—“What do we do?” “Who’s going first?”—Hana, Sayaka, and Mai climbed down in turn. Shotaro and I brought up the rear.

After climbing down seven or eight meters, my feet touched bottom.

Beyond that, a cave-like horizontal passage stretched away. It was wide enough to walk through without stooping.

It sloped gently downward. We advanced by the light of our phones.

A little farther on, a gigantic rock lay in the middle of the passage.

It was far too large for anyone to move by hand. For some reason, it had been wound round and round with a thick chain.

“What’s this? Did they try to dig it out and give up?”

“Hard to say.”

Shotaro said it in a way that suggested more than the words themselves.

Once we had slipped past the huge rock, we came to an iron door.

Just before it, the natural rock beneath our feet gave way to a dirty plank floor. Beyond this point, it was unmistakably a man-made structure.

Yuya opened the door and shone his light inside.

“Whoa. You were right. This is... something.”

From Ryuhei’s voice, it was impossible to tell whether he was impressed or unnerved.

Beyond the door stretched a broad corridor. The ceiling was low. A little farther ahead the corridor bent, hiding what lay beyond, but the echo of the iron door opening told us the underground structure was a large one.

“Wow. ...Smells moldy.”

Mai murmured.

A stale smell hung in the air. The air had the dampness of the depths of a forest where sunlight never reached, tinged with something faintly chemical.

“So what about lights in here? There’s no power, is there?”

“No power, but there was this huge generator. It kind of looked like it might still run. If not, I guess we may have to do our best with phone lights. I brought a battery pack.”

Ryuhei and Yuya passed through the iron door and stepped into the pitch-dark corridor.

Each of us held a light, and we followed timidly behind them in a line like glowworm larvae.

The floor was finished in cheap, worn vinyl. Doors lined both walls like a hotel corridor.

Yuya pointed to the door of the room on the right just before the corridor turned left. A plate on it read 107.

“The generator’s in here. It doesn’t really look broken, but...”

Yuya turned the doorknob and directed his light inside.

It seemed to be some sort of machine room. Black cables crawled across the walls and gathered at the back, where they connected to the generator.

It was a generator about the size of a bathtub, like the ones I had seen at the hospital where I once worked part-time. An exhaust pipe ran from the wall up through the ceiling. The machine itself looked older than I was, but the LP gas cylinders feeding it looked comparatively new.

The gauges on the cylinders showed there was still gas left. Yuya and Ryuhei ran their hands over the generator, trying to figure out how to start it.

Realizing neither of them knew how to operate it, Shotaro spoke up quietly.

“For now, check that the cylinders and hoses are properly connected. Then I think you turn on the engine switch and pull the starter grip.”

When Yuya did as he was told, an engine noise like a motorcycle’s filled the room.

The next instant, the fluorescent lights overhead flickered. Then bluish-white light flooded not only the machine room but the corridor beyond, filling the underground structure.

“What a relief. No lights at all would’ve been rough.”

Sayaka said it while looking around at everyone’s faces.

With a general feeling of relief, we left the cramped machine room.

This underground structure was full of mysteries. Now that it was lit, I felt like exploring it, but at the moment my fatigue outweighed my curiosity.

Taking us with him, Yuya went a little way back down the corridor toward the entrance and opened the door opposite the machine room, number 106.

“This is the dining hall. Want to take a break for a bit?”

It was a large room, several dozen tatami mats in size. Long tables ran through the long, narrow room, with chairs lined up tightly along them. It was a cafeteria that could seat dozens.

Hana pulled out a nearby chair.

“Ugh, gross. ...This thing okay?”

The chair was a cheap one, the sort you’d find in a school. Worse, left underground for so long, its backrest was black with mold and starting to rot.

After dusting off the seat, Hana cautiously lowered herself into it. It held.

Looking more closely, the long tabletops were cheap too, just veneered plywood. They were worn as well, and looked dangerous to stand on.

At the back of the dining hall was a sink. When I turned the faucet, it gurgled and then spat out reddish-black water. After running it for a while, the water eventually turned clear.

“Huh. The water works.”

I muttered it without meaning to.

There was a cupboard above the sink. It was packed with thick old plates and cups.

Sayaka was kneeling by the wall, fiddling with something.

“Oh, wow. The outlets work too. Look.”

She had plugged her phone charger into a wall socket with exposed wiring.

For a while we stayed in the dining hall, stretching and yawning.

There was hardly any conversation. It was like arriving at a mountain hut on a long ridge traverse and doing nothing but work the fatigue out of your body. Since we’d all gathered the day before, this was the moment that made me feel most nostalgic for our student days.

And yet the place had too ominous an air to let us sprawl around carefree for long. After a while, Shotaro tapped me on the shoulder.

“Shuichi, shall we look around the building a little? This is a very interesting structure.”

I had just been starting to think I should eat something, but I was curious about the building too.

At that point Yuya, who had been looking a little sheepish, cut in.

“Oh, Sho-san, if you want, I can show you around a bit. When I was here last time, I looked over quite a lot of it.”

So he came with us, and the three of us left the dining hall and began exploring the underground structure.

The low ceilings, the dim fluorescent light, the grimy floors, the walls made of cheap, drab materials, the wiring strung everywhere—the whole place had the feel of an old cargo ship.

Not just the atmosphere, but the size and structure too resembled a freighter. The building had been made by welding steel plates to a framework of steel beams laid lengthwise and crosswise. It was long and narrow and arranged in three levels, with what seemed to be storerooms and rooms fitted with plain two-tier pipe bunk beds lining the corridors on either side.

Each door had a room-number plate, like in an apartment building. When we went back near the entrance and stood with the iron door behind us, the room on the right was 101 and the one on the left 102. The farther down the corridor you went, the numbers continued 103, 104, and so on. Every room was numbered, regardless of whether it was a storeroom or living quarters. Every door, too, sat oddly in its frame, leaving crooked gaps between the door and the wall. The whole place was crudely built.

Room 104, next to the dining hall, turned out to be the toilet. It had four stalls, like a public facility. There was even a shower booth attached, though I had no desire to use it.

The smell was trapped in there, but not enough to be truly unpleasant. Apparently, because it had been left unused for so long, the waste had already decomposed considerably.

“There’s no sewer out here, right? How did they handle this?”

“They probably collected it in a tank first and then pumped it up to the surface. The household wastewater was probably handled the same way.”

Peering into one of the squat toilets, Shotaro answered my question.

That was enough of the toilet. We went back into the corridor.

Once we passed the machine room, 107, the corridor turned left. At the corner there was a metal staircase leading down to the second basement.

We ignored the stairs for the moment and kept going down the corridor. About five meters on, it turned right almost immediately. Doors lined both sides there as well. Starting with 108, the rooms continued all the way to 120 at the far end.

In places, the wall on the outer side exposed blackish rock. It had the same feel as the cave we had come down through from the entrance. Water seemed to be seeping out here and there; it was damp.

Apparently this underground structure had been made by shaping a natural cavity in the earth, cutting it into levels, and setting partition walls inside until it became a building. The reason the corridors bent at such strange angles seemed to be that the original terrain had been respected.

When we reached the dead end, Shotaro said, with the air of someone who had just finished looking over a museum exhibit:

“Twenty rooms. It must have cost quite a bit. But well—they certainly built it. Spectacularly illegal, of course.”

“And that’s not all,” Yuya said. “There’s still the second basement.”

Yuya took the lead and headed back toward the stairs.

The second basement was laid out much the same as the floor above. Doors ran endlessly along both sides of a lightning-bolt-shaped corridor. This level didn’t have a large room like the dining hall, but the space directly below the first-basement toilet had been taken up by a sewage tank, occupying the area of one whole room. There were twenty rooms here too, Yuya explained, numbered 201 through 220.

The corridor on the second basement was lit by fluorescent lights as well. But the stretch to the left at the foot of the stairs, where the room numbers were lower, was dark. The light fixtures were there, so perhaps a wire had been cut somewhere. Since the light was on at the far end, that area might have been on a different circuit.

When we reached the bottom of the stairs, before we started looking into the rooms, Shotaro asked:

“Yuya, how was it again that you found a place like this?”

“Uh, well, I kind of just stumbled on it about half a year ago. I was gonna do some solo camping, and I wanted to go somewhere absolutely nobody would ever come, so I came way out into the mountains. Then I found that hatch. And when I went in, it was incredible.”

Standing in the middle of the corridor, Yuya spread both arms.

“So what is this place, anyway? Who built something like this, and why? Honestly, doesn’t it obviously feel like it was used for some really bad shit?”

After thinking for a moment, Shotaro answered:

“This is probably a radical group’s hideout from fifty years ago.”

“—Seriously? A radical group? Like seventies stuff?”

“At a glance, it looks about that old. Partway in from the entrance, there was that fairly large rock, right? The one with the chain wrapped around it. That was obviously left there on purpose, so it could be used as a barricade in an emergency. They’d use that rock to block the iron door.

“But after the radicals, it looks like some other criminal group used the place too. Some of the wiring seems to have been put in fairly recently—at most twenty years ago. Radicals wouldn’t have still been holed up in a place like this that late.”

I had half suspected as much myself.

Why else would you build a structure like this, deep in the mountains and underground at that, if not to keep it hidden from sight? But hearing it said aloud made the place feel all the more unsettling.

Shotaro said brightly, “Well, let’s have a closer look and see what kind of place this is.”

We tried room 208, directly in front of us. It seemed to be a storeroom for scrap.

We rummaged through the things inside. Used work gloves, a rusted grass-cutting sickle, an old speaker, lengths of copper pipe, scraps of wood—there was no end of junk. Some of it looked very old, some of it less so. It was all the sort of stuff you would see piled up at a neighborhood dump site.

“Oh? So even a criminal hideout has straw hats.”

Keeping a straight face, Shotaro showed me a battered, broad-brimmed straw hat.

“Yeah. I kind of thought maybe we’d find pistols or white powder or something, but I guess it’s not that kind of place.”

“If the people who used this place dealt in things like that, they’d have taken them when they pulled out. Though if we searched every last inch, we might still turn something up.”

Shotaro carelessly tossed the straw hat onto a battered wooden crate.

Next, we opened the door to room 209, diagonally across from it.

At first glance, that too looked like a room where junk had been dumped. There was less of it than in the last room, but refuse-like items had been swept into piles in the corners.

But when we switched on the light, what was there was nothing like the ordinary, peaceful items in 208. When I heard the place had been used by a criminal organization, I had imagined weapons or drugs. What we actually found was something darker still.

The first thing that caught my eye was a restraint: a long chain with handcuffs and shackles attached. At the very back stood a pitch-black iron chair with an oddly pointed seat.

There was also a club made from a thick piece of wood wrapped in leather, and a metal frame big enough for a human head, fitted with something like a vise. There were rusted nails and concrete blocks too.

Yuya, Shotaro, and I looked at one another with the awkwardness of people who have glimpsed someone else’s secret.

Yuya walked over to a corner of the room, crouched, and let out a groan without touching the implements.

“No way. Holy shit. These are torture tools, right?”

“That’s certainly what they look like.”

Apparently Yuya hadn’t looked in this room the last time he was here.

“Do you think they were really used?”

“I don’t know, but doesn’t it look that way? I’ve only ever seen this kind of stuff in museums. It doesn’t even feel real.”

The torture implements were old and rusted. There were no traces of dried blood on them, but they were too badly worn to have been nothing more than tasteless decorations. I looked around at the floor. The vinyl flooring bore gash-like tears. They looked as though someone writhing in agony had clawed at it.

I had heard of incidents in which internal feuds among radical groups in the seventies had escalated into murder. If the history of this underground structure was what we imagined, then finding torture tools here was not especially strange.

The inorganic underground structure suddenly seemed to exude a bloody, sinister presence.

“—But there’s no proof they were actually used, right?”

“No. And even if they were, it would have been a very long time ago. They’re historical artifacts now.”

That seemed to reassure Yuya a little.

I too decided to take Shotaro’s words as something to hold on to and not think too much about what had happened here in the past. Needless to say, torture devices had never had anything to do with my life, and whatever happened, they surely never would from here on out either.

After that we peered into several nearby rooms, but nothing more alarming than the torture tools turned up.

“By the way, Yuya, you said this place goes down to a third basement, didn’t you? Where do you go down from here?”

Shotaro asked. There was no staircase in the corridor leading to the third basement.

“Uh, well, the way down is at the far end, but there are, like, reasons you can’t go down there now. Still, let’s go close and take a look. You’ll get it right away.”

Yuya went on ahead to guide us.

We walked along the corridor toward the lower-numbered rooms. The corridor lights were out along this stretch. It wasn’t too dark to walk, but we turned on our phone lights anyway.

At the end of the corridor stood an iron door. It was like the one at the entrance on the first basement, but smaller and narrower.

Pointing at it, Yuya said, “See? I’m pretty sure the entrance we came in from is right above this.”

Tracing back the route we had taken to get here, he was probably right.

Yuya slowly opened the iron door.

The space beyond was unlike the other rooms. Just inside it narrowed like the neck of a bottle, and beyond that, no matter where you looked, the chamber was bare black rock. Only the part of the ceiling near the entrance was planked over, and conspicuously low. Everywhere else it was rock as well. This room alone had the aspect of a natural cave.

And against the far wall there stood a hoistlike machine, the sort of thing that looked as though it might once have been used on a sunken ship.

“What the hell is this? Rusted as hell.”

A thick chain was wound around the hoist. Following it with my eyes, I saw it branch, pass through pulleys, and run up through the plank-covered section of ceiling near the door to the first basement.

“Oh! Is that the chain that was wrapped around the big rock?”

“Exactly. I told you that rock was a barricade.”

If you turned this hoist, the huge rock would be pulled into place to block the iron door on the first basement.

“Oh, right—now that you say it, that’s obviously a barricade. I wasn’t really thinking that deeply. So that’s why the ceiling here is only planked over near the entrance. To run the chain through.”

“Well, that’s one reason,” Shotaro said meaningfully. “There may be others.”

“And yeah, the stairs down to the third basement are in this room. But you can’t go down. Look.”

Yuya pointed to the back right corner of the room.

A square opening had been cut into the floor. The stairs were there.

When I went over and looked down, I understood immediately what he meant.

The third basement was flooded. The water had risen to the fourth step—almost brushing the ceiling of the third basement. Crouching and stretching my arm down as far as it would go, I managed to touch the black, glossy surface with my fingertips.

“Cold. Seriously? It’s flooded?”

“It’s underground,” Shotaro said. “And since it was amateur construction, a little flooding was probably inevitable. Surrounded by natural rock like this, it would be. The drainage system must be broken too. This may be why the place was abandoned.”

He was right: even on the outer wall of the first basement, I had seen water running down here and there.

“Yeah, well, I guess this isn’t the point where we get to call it a mansion with a pool. Actually, it’s kind of scary. I mean, doesn’t this mean the whole place is eventually going to wind up underwater, sooner or later, if it keeps going like this?”

Yuya answered.

“Well, in theory, yeah, but it’s gotta take a long time, right? It doesn’t feel like the amount of water’s changed that much since I was here six months ago. Maybe it’s gone up a little. If it gets that bad, wouldn’t it be like five years from now or something?”

That was a fair point.

Shining my phone light into the water, I could make out what looked like lengths of rebar fixed in concrete, steel beams, and other construction materials lying around in a jumble on the third basement.

There was nothing else to see, so we went back out into the corridor and started back the way we had come.

When we got near the stairs, on the opposite side of the corridor I saw Sayaka from behind, holding up her phone camera. Apparently the place was strange enough that she had decided to take pictures.

“Yuya, is that hole we came through really the only way in and out? It doesn’t seem likely there’d be just one entrance.”

“No, technically there’s one more, but it’s unusable. It’s down on the third basement. From there a narrow shaft, kind of like a trash chute, goes up to the surface, but it’s flooded, so there’s no way through at all.”

“I see.”

“Oh, right. There was a sort of floor plan in the machine room. That’ll be quicker.”

The three of us went back to the machine room.

Yuya opened a desk drawer. It was crammed with old adhesive bandages, nail clippers, pencils and ballpoint pens, and various other odds and ends.

He scooped them out onto the desktop. At last, mixed in among them, he found a large sheet of paper, A2 size, folded into quarters.

“Oh, this is it. Guess I shoved it pretty far back last time I looked at it.”

It wasn’t quite the kind of thing you would call an easy-to-read map, but it did show the structure of the building as a whole. The paper was badly yellowed, apparently from the time the place was built.

At the top of the blueprint, as though added later, someone had written “Ark” in ballpoint pen. It seemed to be the name of the underground structure.

As we had seen, the Ark was long and narrow, three levels deep, bent in the middle into the shape of a backward Z. According to the plan, the third basement, unlike the first and second, was not divided into small rooms; it seemed to consist only of a few large chambers. The entrance we had come through lay on the west side, and on the east side of the third basement there appeared to be another exit leading to the surface.

“This is probably some kind of emergency exit. It goes up to the surface with a hatch just like the other entrance. Actually, on the way here we passed the hatch for the emergency exit. Probably nobody noticed it, though.”

Apparently there had been another hatch just past the bridge. Naturally, I hadn’t noticed it, and neither had Shotaro. It had already started getting dark by the time we passed that way.

“Yuya, did you actually check the emergency exit? Is this blueprint accurate?”

“Uh, yeah. I did go down from the emergency exit, just to check. I climbed down this ladderlike thing and got all the way to near the ceiling of the third basement, but the whole third floor was full of water. So I just went back up.”

“Speaking of that emergency exit...”

Something had been bothering me for a while.

“What are these?”

I pointed at the desk.

There were two LCD monitors sitting there—old fifteen-inch ones, the kind that used to be in an elementary school library. Scrawled across the bezels in permanent marker were the very words we had just been talking about: “Entrance” and “Emergency Exit.”

“Oh! Yeah, those caught my eye last time too. They look like surveillance-camera monitors. I mean, they’re labeled ‘Entrance’ and ‘Emergency Exit,’ right? When I was here in daylight, I saw what looked like cameras near the hatches. They’re mounted up in trees. There’s one at the west entrance and one at the east emergency exit.

“So these probably show those feeds. But last time there wasn’t any power, so I couldn’t check.”

Rattling on like that, Yuya switched on the two monitors one after the other.

“Oh, maybe they’re still alive? ...Whoa, nice, they work.”

With a small electronic hiss, the old monitors came to life with surveillance-camera images.

Night had already fully fallen, so the images were indistinct, like woodcuts. The cameras, like the monitors, seemed old too, and the picture was not very sharp.

Even so, it was clear they showed the dead field above ground. By moonlight, a dim hatchlike shape could be seen near the center of each screen. If anyone approached either the entrance or the emergency exit, we would know at once.

Tracing the hatches on the two screens with his finger, Yuya said, “Right, so this one’s the entrance we came in through. And this one here is the emergency exit by the bridge.”

“Then the two of them must be about a hundred meters apart.”

“Yeah, about that. East and west. Maybe the idea was that if outsiders found one, you could escape through the other?”

That was how Yuya answered Shotaro.

Installing the cameras alone must have taken quite a bit of work. A little appalled, I said, “They were serious. What kind of people were using this place, anyway?”

“It could just as easily have been some new religious sect carrying out special forms of training. If the cameras were for keeping out intruders, the way they’re positioned is odd. They look more like they were meant to guard against escape.”

That was Shotaro’s reply.

It was a fairly convincing theory. I studied once more the word Ark written across the top of the yellowed blueprint.

“I guess the name really does come from Noah’s ark in the Old Testament.”

“I can’t think of any other origin.”

I remembered idly leafing through the Bible in a cultural anthropology class in college. The story of Noah’s ark, famous enough to need no introduction, appeared near the very beginning of the thick Old Testament.

When the world had fallen into disorder and the earth was full of violence, Noah, a righteous man, received a revelation: God had decided to destroy mankind. Noah was told to build an ark and prepare for the flood. Once the ark was complete, and Noah and his family had entered it, along with the male and female of every living thing, the flood came upon the earth. That was the story. The account in the original text is spare as a narrative, but in sermons and in novels and films based on it, people are often shown mocking Noah and his family as they build an ark on a mountain, refusing to believe a flood could ever come.

A structure like a ship, built deep in the mountains. The name Ark might have been added later, but it made sense. To the radicals—or the members of some new religion, whatever they had been—this may have been a place to wait for salvation.

To me, it was no more than a bad joke. There was no salvation to be found in this ominous underground structure. The only thing we had found was torture devices.

Among those who had come with me, there was no one particularly devout and no one with strong political convictions either. Apparently we were not Noah’s family but the people laughing at those who had built the place.

“Huh? What are you doing?”

Hearing us making noise in the machine room, Sayaka poked her head in through the door we had left open.

Then, right after her, Hana came over too, apparently looking for her.

“Oh, Sayaka, here you are. Were you taking pictures or something?”

“Ah, yes. I probably won’t ever come to a place like this again, so I thought I’d take some for the memory.”

Apparently she had been peeking into the different rooms and photographing them.

“I don’t really care, but you should probably not put them online or anything. It could be a pain if the people who used this place found out.”

“Oh, true. I won’t. I’ll just take them, that’s all.”

While Sayaka and Hana were having that conversation, Mai and Ryuhei arrived together.

It seemed the other four had each been exploring the inside of the place on their own as well. All seven of us ended up together in the machine room.

They all looked surprised to see the monitors turned on.

Yuya repeated for them the same explanations he had given Shotaro and me: about the flooding on the third basement, the emergency exit, and the surveillance cameras.

“Yeah, okay, I get it, but—”

Hana cut him off.

“Actually, I kind of want to go outside for a bit. My boyfriend got in touch earlier today, and if I don’t reply before the end of the day, he might come over to my place while I’m away.”

As she said it, Hana stroked the phone in her hand.

Yuya scratched his head.

“Well... I don’t know if you’re gonna get much signal around here.”

“Yeah, that’s why I just want to try. If it doesn’t work, I’ll give up. Could somebody come with me?”

Out here in the pitch-black mountains, anyone would be afraid to go out alone.

“In that case, want me to come with you?”

But Hana looked uncomfortable at the idea of going outside alone with Yuya.

Sensing that, Sayaka immediately came to the rescue.

“Then maybe I’ll go too, just to try. I probably have work messages anyway. Is that okay?”

“Oh, Sayaka, you’re coming too? Thanks. Yeah, that’s better.”

That settled it. Hana, Sayaka, and Yuya would go outside for a while.

Once the three of them had gone, Ryuhei stared at the dark image on the monitor over my shoulder.

“That thing really showing anything?”

From a distance it looked like a blank screen, as if the connection were bad.

But before I could answer, something moved on the monitor.

“Oh—that’s Hana and the others.”

On the camera covering the entrance, the three of them appeared, lifting the hatch and coming out as they went off in search of a signal.

The first figure waved at the camera. That was Yuya; behind him, in the flashy jacket, was Hana. Sayaka came after her. Naturally, in the dark their faces were impossible to make out.

A little later, the camera at the emergency exit showed the three of them walking along with their phone lights in hand. They crossed the wooden bridge and headed for higher ground to see whether they might get a signal there.

“Huh. So it does work.”

Ryuhei muttered it, sounding convinced at last.

After that, he and Mai idly handled the things Yuya had dumped out onto the desk. Before long, when they grew bored, Ryuhei took Mai by the hand. The two of them left the machine room.

Shotaro and I stayed there, absentmindedly watching the monitors.

At a loss for anything else to do, I asked my cousin a pointless question.

“Do you think everyone’s eaten anything?”

“Who knows.”

Everyone had seemed too absorbed in looking around the underground structure. Maybe no one had gotten to food yet.

A little over half an hour later, Yuya and the others came back. Thanks to the surveillance cameras, we knew immediately that they were returning.

But something about the group was strange.

“Huh? Aren’t there more of them now? ...That can’t be right.”

The figures on the entrance monitor had gone from three to six.

It was like something out of a horror movie. Shotaro and I left the machine room and went to meet the party—who had clearly run into something—at the iron door.

When we opened it, Yuya came in first. Hana followed. There was mud on her jacket; apparently she had fallen.

Then Sayaka.

And behind her, a frightened family of three.

The father, with close-cropped hair graying at the temples and thick black-rimmed glasses, looked to be in his fifties. The mother was somewhat heavyset and wore her hair short. The son, with slightly thick lips, looked about middle-school age.

The moment Sayaka saw our faces, she hurried to explain.

“Um, these people said they got lost, and... so we brought them with us. Since, well, there’s at least a place with a roof here. You were out picking mushrooms, was it?”

The father answered.

“Yes, something like that. I’m sorry.”

It was mushroom season, true, but even so they had come remarkably deep into the mountains.

This was hardly our home, exactly, but we invited them into the dining hall all the same.
