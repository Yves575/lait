For Berenice

There are thirty-two of them. Thirty-two still living in the abbey on this autumn day in 1986, at the end of a road enough to make anyone who takes it go pale. In a thousand years, nothing has changed. Neither the steepness of the way nor its vertigo. Thirty-two stout hearts—you have to be stout-hearted when you live perched on the edge of the void—thirty-two bodies that once were too, in their youth. In a few hours, they will be one fewer.

The brothers form a circle around the one who is leaving. There have been many circles, many farewells, since the Sacra first reared its walls above them. There have been many moments of grace, of doubt, of bodies braced against the coming shadow. There have been and there will be other departures, so they wait patiently.

This dying man is not like the others. He is the only one here never to have taken vows. Yet he has been allowed to stay for forty years. Every time there has been debate, questions, a man in purple robes has arrived—never the same one—to settle the matter. He stays. He is part of the place, as surely as the cloister, its columns, its Romanesque capitals, whose state of preservation owes much to his talent. So let us not complain: he pays his way in kind.

Only his fists jut out from the brown wool blanket, one on either side of his head, an eighty-two-year-old child in the grip of a nightmare. The skin is yellow, at breaking point, vellum stretched over angles too sharp. His forehead gleams, waxed by a clammy fever. It had to happen one day: his strength would fail him. A pity he never answered their questions. A man has a right to his secrets.

Besides, they feel they know. Not everything, but the essentials. Sometimes they disagree. To relieve the boredom, they discover in themselves the zeal of gossips. He is a criminal, a defrocked priest, a political refugee. Some say he is being kept here against his will—the theory does not hold; they have seen him leave, and come back—others insist he is here for his own safety. And then there is the most popular version, and the most secret, for romance gets in here only as contraband: he is here to watch over her. She who waits, in her marble night, a few hundred meters from the little cell. She who has been waiting for forty years. Every monk of the Sacra has seen her once. Every one of them would like to see her again. All they need do is ask Padre Vincenzo, the superior, for permission, but few dare. Perhaps for fear of the impious thoughts that come, they say, to those who draw too near her. And impious thoughts—the monks have quite enough of those already, when in the heart of the dark they are pursued by dreams with an angel’s face.

The dying man struggles, opens his eyes, closes them again. One of the brothers swears he saw joy there—he is mistaken. A cool cloth is laid gently on his forehead, on his lips.

The sick man stirs again, and for once they all agree.

He is trying to say something.

Of course I’m trying to say something. I have seen man fly, faster and faster, farther and farther. I have seen two wars, seen nations sink, picked oranges on Sunset Boulevard—don’t you think I have something to tell? Forgive me, I’m ungrateful. You clothed me, you fed me when you had nothing, or next to nothing, when I decided to hide among you. But I’ve kept silent too long. Close the shutters, the light hurts me.

He stirs. Close the shutters, brother, the light seems to bother him.

The shadows watching over me, backlit against a Piedmont sun, the voices growing muffled as sleep draws near. It all happened so fast. Barely a week ago they still saw me in the vegetable garden, or on a ladder; there was always something to fix. Slowed by age, yes, but given that no one would have bet on me at birth, it was enough to force admiration. Then one morning I couldn’t get up. I read in their faces that it was my turn, that soon they would toll the bell and carry me to the little garden facing the mountain, where poppies grow above centuries of abbots, illuminators, cantors, and sacristans.

He is very near the end.

The shutters creak. Forty years I’ve been here, they have always creaked. Darkness at last. Darkness like in the cinema—which I saw being born. An empty horizon, first nothing. A blinding expanse that, by dint of staring at it, my memory peoples with shadows, with silhouettes that become cities, forests, men and beasts. My actors move forward, take their places at the front of the stage. I recognize some of them; they haven’t changed. Sublime and ridiculous, cast in the same crucible, inseparable. The currency of tragedy is a rare alloy of gold and tinsel.

It’s only a matter of hours now.

A matter of hours? Don’t make me laugh. I’ve been dead a long time.

Another cool compress. He seems calmer.

But since when have the dead been forbidden to tell their story?

Il Francese. I always hated that nickname, though I was given worse. All my joys, all my dramas are Italian. I come from a land where beauty is always at bay. Let it fall asleep for five minutes and ugliness will slit its throat without mercy. Geniuses spring up here like weeds. People sing as they kill, draw as they cheat, make dogs piss on church walls. It is no accident that an Italian, Mercalli, gave his name to a scale of destruction, the one that measures the intensity of earthquakes. One hand demolishes what the other has built, and the feeling is the same.

Italy, kingdom of marble and garbage. My country.

But it is a fact: I was born in France in 1904. My parents had left Liguria in search of fortune fifteen years earlier, scarcely married. Instead of fortune, they were called Ritals, spat on, mocked for the way they rolled their r’s—or, so far as I know, the word roll does indeed begin with an r. My father had only just escaped the racist riots at Aigues-Mortes in 1893; two of his friends stayed behind there: brave Luciano and old Salvatore. They were never again spoken of without those adjectives.

Some families forbade their children to speak the language of the old country, so they wouldn’t “sound like Ritals.” They scrubbed them with Marseille soap in the hope of whitening them a little. Not in the Vitaliani household. We spoke Italian, ate Italian. We thought Italian—that is to say, in volleys of superlatives, with Death often invoked, tears abundant, hands rarely still. We cursed as casually as we passed the salt. Our family was a circus, and we were proud of it.

In 1914 the French state, which had shown so little zeal in protecting Luciano, Salvatore, and the others, declared that my father was without the shadow of a doubt a good Frenchman, worthy of conscription—especially as some clerk had, by mistake or as a joke, made him ten years younger when copying out his birth certificate. He left long-faced, with no flower in his rifle barrel. His own father had lost his life in the Expedition of the Thousand in 1860. Nonno Carlo had conquered Sicily with Garibaldi. It was not a Bourbon bullet that killed him, but a prostitute from the port of Marsala whose hygiene was open to question, a detail the family preferred to pass over in silence. He was certainly dead, and the message was clear: war killed.

It killed my father. One day a gendarme came to the workshop above which we lived in the Maurienne valley. Every day my mother opened up in case some order came in that her husband might fill when he returned; one day or another they would have to get back to cutting stone, restoring gargoyles, carving fountains. The gendarme put on the proper expression, looked more stricken still when he saw me, cleared his throat, explained that there had been a shell, and that was that. When my mother, very dignified, asked him when the body would be brought home, he stammered, explained that there had been horses on the battlefield, other soldiers, that a shell did a lot of damage and that as a result they didn’t always know who was who anymore, or even what was man and what was horse. My mother thought he was about to burst into tears, had to offer him a glass of Amaro Braulio—I never saw a Frenchman drink it without grimacing—and only cried herself many hours later.

Of course, I don’t really remember any of it, or remember it badly. I know the facts; I restore them with a little color, colors that are slipping through my fingers now in the cell I have occupied for forty years on Mount Pirchiriano. Even now—or rather a few days ago, when I was still capable of it—I speak French badly. No one has called me Francese since 1946.

A few days after the gendarme’s visit, my mother explained to me that in France she would not be able to give me the education I needed. Her belly was already rounding with a brother or sister—who was never born, not alive anyway—and she drowned me in kisses as she explained that she was sending me away for my own good, sending me back home because she believed in me, because she could see my love of stone despite my age, because she knew I was destined for great things, and that was why she had given me my name.

Of the two burdens of my existence, my first name was no doubt the lighter to bear. I hated it passionately all the same.

My mother often went down to the workshop to watch her husband work. She realized she was pregnant when she felt me twitch at a blow of the chisel. Until that moment she had spared no effort, had helped my father move enormous blocks, which perhaps explains what followed.

“He’ll be a sculptor,” she announced.

My father grumbled that it was a lousy trade, one in which hands, backs, and eyes wore out much faster than stone, and that if you weren’t Michelangelo, you might as well spare yourself all that.

My mother nodded and decided to give me a head start.

My name is Michelangelo Vitaliani.

I discovered my country in October 1916, in the company of a drunk and a butterfly. The drunk had known my father, had escaped conscription thanks to the state of his liver, but the way things were turning suggested his cirrhosis would not protect him much longer. They were drafting boys, old men, cripples. The newspapers claimed we were winning, that the Boche would soon be history. In our community, news that Italy had joined the Allies the year before had been greeted as a promise of victory. Those who came back from the front sang a different tune, for those who still felt like singing. Carmone the engineer, who like the other Ritals had scraped salt at Aigues-Mortes, then opened a grocery in Savoy where he consumed a good part of his wine stock, had therefore decided to go back. If he had to croak, he might as well do it at home, lips reddened by Montepulciano to wash down the fear.

For him, home was the Abruzzi. He was kind, and agreed to drop me at Zio Alberto’s on the way. He did it because he felt a little sorry for me and also, I think, because of my mother’s eyes. A mother’s eyes can be a powerful thing, but mine had irises of a strange blue, almost violet. They had sparked more than one fistfight, until my father put a stop to all that. A stonecutter has dangerous hands—I should know. The rivals backed down quickly.

My mother shed great violet tears on the platform at Modane. My uncle Alberto, a sculptor too, was going to take care of me. She swore she would join me soon, as soon as she had sold the workshop and made a little money. A matter of weeks, a few months at most—it took her twenty years. The train hissed, spat black smoke whose taste is still in my mouth, and carried off the tipsy engineer and my mother’s only son.

Whatever anyone says, at twelve sadness doesn’t last long. I had no idea what this swaying train was heading toward, but I knew I had never been on a train before—or didn’t remember it. Excitement quickly gave way to nausea. Everything was going too fast. The moment I fixed my eyes on some detail—a fir tree, a house—it vanished. A landscape isn’t made to move. I began to feel sick, wanted to tell Carmone, but he was snoring with his mouth open.

Fortunately there was the butterfly. It flew in at Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne and settled on the window, between the rushing mountains and me. After a brief struggle with the glass, it gave up and did not move again. It was no beautiful butterfly, none of those glories of color and gold I would see later in spring. Just a mediocre butterfly, gray, a little bluish if you squinted hard, a moth stupefied by daylight. For a moment I thought of torturing it, like any boy my age, then realized that if I stared at it, the only still thing in a frantic world, my nausea went away. The butterfly stayed there for hours, sent by some friendly power to reassure me, and that was perhaps my very first intuition of the fact that nothing is really what it seems to be, that a butterfly is not just a butterfly but a story, something enormous lurking in a very tiny space—something the first atomic bomb would confirm a few decades later and, perhaps more than that still, what I am leaving behind as I die in the foundations of the most beautiful abbey in the country.

When Carmone woke up, he laid out his project, because he had one. He was a communist. Do you know what that is? I had heard the insult several times in the community back in France; people were always wondering whether so-and-so was one. I said, “Pfff, of course. It’s a man who loves men.”

Carmone burst out laughing. In a way, yes, a communist was a man who loved men. “Besides, there is no bad way to love men, do you understand that?” I had never seen him so serious.

The Carmone family owned a piece of land in the province of L’Aquila, to which geography had done two injustices. One: it was the only province in the Abruzzi without access to the sea. Two: earthquakes ravaged it at regular intervals, like the Liguria of my ancestors, except that bitch Liguria, at least, had access to the sea.

Their land offered a pleasant view over Lake Scanno. Carmone meant to build there a tower mounted on a gigantic ball bearing and house the local proletarians in it, all for a moderate rent that would allow him to live decently—especially since, being a good communist, he was reserving the top floor for himself. Thanks to two teams of horses relieving one another every twelve hours, the building would turn on itself over the course of the day. Its inhabitants would thus all enjoy, without exception, without profiteers or exploited, a view of the lake once a day. Perhaps electricity would someday replace the horses, though Carmone admitted it would probably never reach that far. But he liked to dream.

The bearings would also have the advantage that if an earthquake struck, they would decouple the structure from the ground. In the event of a grade-XII earthquake on the Mercalli scale—he was the one who taught me that name—his building stood a thirty percent better chance of surviving than an ordinary one. Thirty percent may not sound like much, but since grade XII was not exactly a joke, he explained, rolling his eyes wide, it was enormous.

I surrendered to a half-sleep, eyes fixed on my butterfly, and we entered Italy while Carmone spoke to me tenderly of devastation.

Italy and I embraced like old friends the first time we met. In my haste to get off the train at Turin station, I tripped on the step and landed arms spread on the platform. I lay there for a moment, with no thought of crying, with the beatitude of a priest at his ordination. Italy smelled of gunflint. Italy smelled of war.

Carmone decided to take a cab. It was more expensive than walking, but my mother had entrusted him with money in an envelope and, just as wine was meant to be drunk, he declared, money was meant to be spent; besides, let’s go buy ourselves a little quarter-liter of red from the Po before we set off, if you don’t mind.

I didn’t mind, dazzled as I was by what I was discovering: soldiers on leave, soldiers about to depart, porters, train drivers, and a whole crowd of shady people whose function or ambitions seemed mysterious to the boy I was. I had never seen shady people in my life. I had the impression they returned my insistent stares with kindness, as if to say: you’re one of us. Perhaps they were simply looking at the blue bump growing right in the middle of my forehead. I moved through a forest of legs, blissful, enthralled by other smells: creosote and leather, metal and cannon, scents of half-dark and battlefields. And there was the noise, a forge’s din. It grated, squealed, banged, a musique concrète played by illiterates, far from the halls where jaded notables would one day crowd in to pretend to appreciate it.

Without knowing it, I was entering Futurism. The world was nothing but speed: the speed of footsteps, trains, bullets, of fortunes changing or alliances shifting. Yet all those men, that whole mass, seemed to be digging in their heels. Bodies exulted, pressed toward the train cars, the trenches, a barbed-wire horizon. But something, between two movements, two surges, was howling: I want to live a little longer.

Later, when my career took off, a collector proudly showed me his latest acquisition, Luigi Russolo’s Futurist painting Revolt. It was in Rome, around the turn of the thirties, I think. The man considered himself an enlightened amateur, passionate about abstract art. He was an idiot. Unless you were there that day at Porta Nuova station, no one can understand that work. No one can understand that there is nothing abstract about it. It is a figurative painting. Russolo painted what was exploding in our faces.

No twelve-year-old puts it that way, obviously. At the time I merely looked around me, eyes wide, while Carmone quenched his thirst in a dive at the end of the platform. But I saw all of it. A sign, if one were still needed, that I was not quite like everyone else.

We left the station under a light fall of snow. We had barely stepped outside when a carabiniere blocked our way and asked to see my papers. Not my companion’s, only mine. His fingers numbed by the cold and the little red from the Po, Carmone handed him my pass. The other man looked at me suspiciously, a look he must have put on every morning to go to work and taken off again in the evening, unless he had been born with it.

“You’re a little Francese?”

I didn’t like being called French. I liked being called little even less.

“Little Francese yourself, cazzino.”

The carabiniere nearly choked. Cazzino was the favorite insult of the back courtyards where I had grown up, and carabinieri do not choose a profession with such handsome uniforms in order to have the size of their virility insulted.

Being an engineer through and through, Carmone pulled my mother’s envelope from his pocket and greased the gears that had jammed. Before long we were on our way again. I refused to get into a cab and pointed to a tram. Carmone grumbled, consulted a map, asked a few questions, and determined that the tram would let us off not too far from where we had to go.

Sitting on a wooden bench, I crossed the first big city of my life. I was happy. I had lost my father, I had no idea when I would see my mother again, but yes, I was happy, drunk on all that still lay before me, that mass of future to climb, to carve to my own measure.

“Tell me, Signor Carmone?”

“Yes?”

“What’s electricity?”

He stared at me in astonishment, then seemed to remember that I had spent the first decade of my life in a village in Savoy I had never left.

“That, my boy.”

He pointed to a streetlamp topped by a handsome golden globe.

“So it’s like a candle, then?”

“Except it never stops. Electrons move between two pieces of carbon.”

“What’s an electron? Some kind of fairy?”

“No. That’s science.”

“What’s science?”

Snowflakes swirled, light as a girl’s dress. Carmone answered my questions without impatience or condescension. Before long we passed an immense building site: the Lingotto, where Fiat cars would a few years later climb a spiral ramp to the roof and make their first turns of the wheel after assembly—a mechanical Sacra di San Michele. The outskirts thinned out, roads gave way to tracks, the tram stopped in what looked like a field. We had to walk the last three kilometers. I am grateful to that man, Carmone, for having come so far with me, despite the cold, despite the times. We walked through mud and I imagined that already my mother’s eyes were beginning to fade in his memory, beginning to seem less violet. But he led me without fail to Zio Alberto’s door.

We had to manhandle the bell and knock several times before Alberto deigned to open, wearing a filthy undershirt. The same cloudy eyes as Carmone, cracked with red veins: the two men shared an immoderate love of the bottle. My mother had written to announce my arrival, so there was not much to explain.

“Here is your new apprentice, Michelangelo, Antonella Vitaliani’s son. Your nephew.”

“I don’t like being called Michelangelo.”

Zio Alberto lowered his eyes to me. I thought he was going to ask what I preferred to be called, to which I would have answered “Mimo.” The nickname my parents had always given me, the nickname I would still be given seventy years later.

“I don’t want him,” Alberto said.

Once again, I had forgotten one detail. Because yes, that’s all it is: a detail.

“I don’t understand. I thought Antonell… that Mrs. Vitaliani had written to you, and that it had all been agreed.”

“She wrote. But I don’t want an apprentice like that.”

“And why not?”

“Because nobody told me he was a midget.”

C’è un piccolo problema, old Rosa had remarked, the neighbor who delivered my mother on a stormy night. The stove rattled, whipped by an adverse wind; a hellish draft reddened the walls. A few matrons from the neighborhood, come to witness the event, curious to glimpse the firm flesh their husbands fantasized about, had long since fled, crossing themselves and murmuring il diavolo. Old Rosa, unflappable, kept on humming, sponging, encouraging. Cholera, cold, plain bad luck, a knife that would not have been drawn if there had been less drink: these had taken children, friends, husbands from her. She was old, she was ugly, she had nothing left to lose. So the devil left her alone; he knew how to recognize trouble when he saw it. He had easier prey.

C’è un piccolo problema, then, she said, wrenching me from Antonella Vitaliani’s entrails. Everything hung on that word, piccolo; it was obvious to anyone who saw me that I would remain more or less piccolo all my life. Rosa laid me on my exhausted mother. My father came bounding upstairs; Rosa said later that he frowned when he saw me, looked around as though searching for something else, his real son rather than this rough draft, then nodded, I see, so that’s how it is—the way he did when he struck a hidden fissure at the heart of a block of stone and several weeks’ work exploded to dust. You can’t blame the stone.

Stone, precisely, got the blame for what made me different. My mother had never known how to rest, lugging enormous blocks in the workshop, enough to make the strong men in the neighborhood blush. Poor Mimo, if the neighbors were to be believed, had paid the price. Later they would call it achondroplasia. They would describe me as a person of short stature, which, frankly, was no better than Zio Alberto’s “midget.” They would explain that my height did not define me. If that were true, why talk about my height at all? I have never heard anyone mention a “person of average height.”

I never blamed my parents. If stone made me what I am, if some dark magic had been at work, it also repaid me for what it had taken away. Stone always spoke to me—all stones: limestone, metamorphic rock, even gravestones, the ones I would soon lie on to listen to stories from recumbent effigies.

“This is not what was planned,” Carmone murmured, tapping his lips with a gloved finger. “That’s awkward.”

By now the snow was falling hard. Zio Alberto shrugged and tried to slam the door in our faces. Carmone blocked it with his foot. He pulled my mother’s envelope from the inside pocket of his old fur coat and held it out to my uncle. Inside was almost all the Vitalianis’ savings. Years of exile, of labor, of skin burned by sun and salt, of starting over, years of marble under the nails, with now and then an ounce of the tenderness that had seen me born. That was why those dirty, crumpled notes were precious. Why Zio Alberto opened the door a little wider.

“This money was for the little one. I mean, Mimo,” he corrected himself, blushing. “If Mimo agrees to give it to you, he would no longer be an apprentice, but a partner.”

Zio Alberto nodded slowly.

“Hmm. A partner.”

He was still hesitating. Carmone waited as long as he could, then sighed and took a leather case from his pack. Everything about him celebrated wear, patching, an aesthetic of passing time. But the leather of the case, new and supple, still seemed to tremble with the old fury of the beast it had once clothed. Carmone ran a cracked glove over it, opened it, and reluctantly took out a pipe.

“This is a pipe I acquired at great expense. Carved from the stump of a briar on which the Hero of Two Worlds, great Garibaldi himself, is said to have sat during his noble and fruitless attempt to bring Rome into our fine kingdom.”

I had seen dozens of those pipes sold in Aigues-Mortes to idiot Frenchmen. How this one had ended up in Carmone’s hands, how he had let himself be taken in, I did not know. I felt a little ashamed for him, and for Italy in general. He was a naïve, generous man. The gesture cost him, and I know he made it sincerely, to help me, not because he was eager to get home or feared burdening himself with a twelve-year-old boy of unusual proportions. Alberto accepted; they sealed the deal with a shot of rotgut whose sourness stung the air inside the shack. Then Carmone got to his feet, tossed back one last for the road, and soon his wavering silhouette was moving away through the snow.

He turned one last time, hand raised in the yellow phosphorescence of a dying world, and smiled at me. The Abruzzi lay far away, he was no longer young, the times were harsh. Later, I never went to Lake Scanno, for fear of discovering that there was not there, and never had been, any tower set on a ball bearing.

I owe a great deal to so-called “fallen” women, and my uncle Alberto was the son of one of them. A brave girl who lay beneath men in the port of Genoa, without anger or shame. She was the only person my uncle ever spoke of with respect, with a fervor bordering on veneration. But the saint of the alleyways was far away. And since Alberto could neither read nor write, his mother became, with each passing day, more and more mythological. I, for my part, wrote rather well, which delighted my uncle when he noticed it.

My uncle Alberto was not my uncle. We did not share so much as an atom of blood. I never managed to clear the matter up entirely, but his grandfather apparently owed mine a debt, an unpaid loan whose moral burden was handed down from one generation to the next. In his own perverse way, Alberto was honest. At my mother’s request, he had agreed to take me in. He owned a small workshop on the outskirts of Turin. Since he was single and not much given to luxury, a few jobs here and there were enough to meet his needs—or had been, until I arrived. For war—that enterprise of progress praised by so many zealots at the time, who naturally disliked the word “zealot” and preferred “poet” or “philosopher”—war, then, had popularized materials cheaper than stone, lighter, easy to produce and easy to work. Steel was Alberto’s worst enemy; he insulted it even in his sleep. He hated it more than the Austro-Hungarians or the Germans. A Crucco, as they called the Boche there, could still be granted extenuating circumstances. Their cooking, their ridiculous spiked helmets—they had enough to be angry about. Whereas no one in his right mind built in steel, and we would see who had the last laugh when everything came crashing down. Alberto had failed to understand that everything had already come crashing down. To steel’s credit, it had had a hand in that: it made fine cannons.

Alberto looked old, but he wasn’t. At thirty-five, he lived alone in a room adjoining his workshop. His bachelorhood surprised people, all the more because after a shower, washed free of marble dust and dressed in his only suit, he didn’t look too bad. He always went to the same brothel in Turin, where he treated the girls with legendary respect. The expression “to do it like Alberto” was popular in the city’s southern districts, between the Lingotto and San Salvario, in the early twenties, before dying out when Alberto moved away, taking his marble and his slave with him—that is, me. Partner—I still laugh.

I’ve often been asked what part he played in what followed. If by “what followed” you mean my career, none. If, on the other hand, you mean my last work, there are no doubt a few fragments of him in it. No, not fragments, splinters—I wouldn’t want anyone suspecting him of ever having shone. Zio Alberto was a bastard. Not a monster, just a sorry little man, which comes to the same thing. I think back on him without hatred, but without sadness either.

For nearly a year I lived in that man’s shadow. I cooked, I cleaned. I carried, I delivered. A hundred times I nearly got run over by a tram, knocked down by a horse, beaten up by some fellow who had made fun of my height and whom I answered by saying that at least my size was no problem downstairs, preferably in front of his girlfriend. Carmone would have loved how electric the atmosphere was in our neighborhood. Every encounter was a potential lightning strike, a displacement of electrons whose effects you never knew. We were at war with the Germans, the Austro-Hungarians, our governments, our neighbors—which was another way of saying we were at war with ourselves. One man wanted war, another peace, voices rose, and the one who wanted peace ended up throwing the first punch.

Zio Alberto forbade me to touch his tools. Once he caught me correcting a little holy-water stoup the neighboring parish of Beata Vergine delle Grazie had ordered from him. Alberto got monumentally drunk once or twice a week, and the last binge had left its mark. The stoup was crude, insulting; a twelve-year-old could have done better, and a twelve-year-old did while the other one slept off his wine. Alberto woke up and caught me red-handed, chisel in hand. He examined my work in astonishment, then beat me black and blue while insulting me in a language I did not understand, some Genoese patois. Then he fell asleep again. When he opened his eyes and found me aching all over and covered in bruises, he pretended not to know what had happened. He went straight to his stoup, saw that he was not displeased with his work, and magnanimously announced that he would deliver it himself.

Alberto regularly dictated a letter to his mother, and let me write one to mine at the same time—he generously paid for the stamp. Antonella did not always answer, forever on the road, chasing the job that would let her hold on one more week, then another. I missed her violet eyes. My father—the man who had guided my first clumsy blows, the man who had taught me the difference between a claw chisel, a riffler, and a broad chisel—was fading.

Work grew scarcer over the course of 1917, Alberto’s moods darker, his binges more violent. Columns of soldiers sometimes marched against the dusk, the newspapers spoke of nothing but her, war, war, but all we sensed was a vague malaise, the feeling of being out of joint with our surroundings, of never quite being in the right place. Out there some filthy beast was mauling the horizon. But we led almost normal lives, a life out of harm’s way that lent a faint taste of guilt to everything we ate. At least until August 22, when bread began to run short, and then there was nothing left to eat at all. Turin exploded. Lenin’s name appeared on the city walls, barricades rose, one revolutionary even stopped me in the street on the morning of the 24th to warn me to be careful, that their barricades were electrified, and that told me more surely than anything else that the world was changing. The man called me “comrade” and clapped me on the back. I saw women face down sheepish soldiers on the barricades, clamber onto armored vehicles, bare their triumphant, furious breasts, and the soldiers did not dare fire on them. Not right away, at any rate.

The revolt lasted three days. No one could agree on anything except that everyone had had enough of the war. The government finally settled everyone’s differences with machine guns; fifty dead cooled tempers. I holed up in the workshop. One evening, calm had only just returned, and a little bread with it, when Zio Alberto came home in better spirits than usual. He made as if to slap me, chuckled when he saw me dive under the table, then ordered me to take up the pen and dictated a letter to his mother. He smelled of the swill served on the corner.

Mammina,

I got the money order you sent. Thanks to it, I’m going to be able to buy the little workshop I told you about at Christmas. It’s in Liguria, so closer to you. There’s no work left in Turin. But down there they’ve got a castle that always needs repairs, and a church the authorities care a lot about, so that’s work. I sold up here, cheap, but never mind, I just signed with that rat Lorenzo, and I’ll be leaving soon with that little shit Mimo. I’ll write to you from Pietra d’Alba, your son who loves you.

“And give me a fine signature, pezzo di merda,” Zio Alberto concluded. “One that shows I’ve made it.”

When I think back on that time, it is strange: I was not unhappy. I was alone, I had nothing and no one, they were plowing up forests in northern Europe and sowing them with flesh larded with metal, plus a few shells that would explode years later in the faces of innocent walkers, they were inventing a desolation enough to make Mercalli blush—he had given his poor scale only twelve degrees. But I was not unhappy; I noted it every evening, when I prayed to a private pantheon of idols that changed throughout my life and later even included opera singers and soccer players. Perhaps because I was young, my days were beautiful. Only now do I grasp how much the beauty of day owes to foreknowledge of night.

The abbot leaves his office and starts down the aptly named Staircase of the Dead. In a few moments he will go to the bedside of the man dying in the annex. The brothers have sent word that the hour is near. He will place the bread of life on his lips.

Padre Vincenzo crosses the church without paying attention to its frescoes, passes through the Zodiac Portal, and emerges onto the terraces at the summit of Mount Pirchiriano, from which the abbey looks down over Piedmont. Before him lie the ruins of a tower. Legend has it that a young peasant girl, beautiful Alda, once flew from there to escape enemy soldiers, helped by Saint Michael. Vanitas vanitatis: she wanted to repeat the feat before the villagers, just to impress them, and smashed herself below. As would, in the fourteenth century, part of the tower that bears her name, brought down by one of the many earthquakes that regularly shake the region.

Farther on, a few steps sink into the ground, barred by a chain and a sign reading “No Entry.” The abbot steps over it with commendable suppleness for a man of his age. This is not the way to the annex where the dying man awaits him. Before joining him, the priest wants to see her first. She is the one who sometimes gives him uneasy sleep, because he fears an intrusion, or worse. You never know what may happen, like that time, fifteen years ago, when Fra Bartolomeo caught someone right in front of the last grille that protected her. The man, an American, had tried to pass himself off as a visitor who had lost his way. The abbot had sniffed out the lie at once; he knew its smell by heart—it was the smell of confessionals. No tourist could descend so deep into the very base of the Sacra di San Michele by accident. No, the man was there because he had heard the rumor.

The abbot had been right. Five years later, the same man returned with a proper authorization signed by some Vatican bigwig. So they opened the door to him, and the list of those who had contemplated her grew a little longer. Leonard B. Williams—that was the name of the professor from Stanford University in California. Williams had devoted his life to the captive of the Sacra, trying to penetrate her mystery. He had published a monograph on her, a few articles, then silence. His work, brilliant though it was, slept on forgotten shelves. The Vatican had played it well, opening that door as if they had nothing to hide. For years calm had returned. But for the past few months the monks had been reporting tourists who were no tourists at all, snoopers. You could spot them a mile off. The pressure was rising again.

For long minutes the abbot descends, finding his way unfailingly through the maze of corridors. He would find this path in the dark; he has walked it so often. A jingling of little bells accompanies him—the sound of the ring of keys in his hand. Those blasted keys. There is one for every door in the abbey, sometimes two, as though behind every panel a mystery beat. As though the mystery that brings them together there, the Eucharist, were not enough.

He is nearly there. He smells earth, dampness, the scent of billions of granite atoms crushed by their own weight, and even a little of the greenness of the surrounding slopes. At last, the grille. The old one has been replaced; this one now has a five-point lock. The remote-control unit doesn’t work on the first try. Padre Vincenzo persists with its rubber buttons; every time it’s the same, some progress this is, it’s 1986 and they still can’t produce a remote that works? He checks himself. Lord, forgive my impatience.

At last the red light goes out; the alarm is deactivated. The final corridor is watched by two state-of-the-art cameras, no bigger than shoeboxes. It is impossible to enter without alerting someone. And even if an intruder did manage it, what then? He would not leave with her. It had taken ten men to bring her down here.

Padre Vincenzo shivers. Theft is not what must be feared. He has not forgotten that lunatic Laszlo Toth. Again he reproaches himself—“lunatic” is not charitable; let us say “unbalanced.” They had come close to tragedy. But he does not want to think of Laszlo now, of the Hungarian’s sinister face and fever-bright eyes. Disaster had been avoided.

They lock her away to protect her. The irony is not lost on the abbot. She is there, don’t worry, perfectly well, except that no one is allowed to see her. No one except him, the padre, the monks who ask permission, the few cardinals still alive who locked her in there forty years ago, probably a few bureaucrats too. Thirty people in the world, at most. And of course her creator, who had a key of his own. He used to come whenever he pleased to tend to her and wash her regularly. Because yes, she has to be washed.

The abbot opens the last two locks. He always starts with the top one, a tic that perhaps betrays a certain nervousness. He would like to break himself of it and promises himself—as he did on his previous visit—that next time he will begin with the bottom one. The door opens silently—the locksmith who so praised the hinges was not lying.

He does not turn on the light. The original fluorescent tubes were replaced, along with the grille, by softer lighting, and so much the better: those fluorescents brutalized her. But he prefers to see her in the dark. The abbot steps forward and touches her with his fingertips, out of habit. She is a little taller than he is. At the center of a round room, a primitive sanctuary with Romanesque vaulting, she stands slightly bent over on her pedestal, lost in a dream of stone. The only light comes from the corridor, picking out two faces, the angle of a wrist. The abbot knows every detail of the statue asleep in the shadows, having scrutinized her until his eyes wore out.

They lock her away to protect her.

The abbot suspects that those who put her there were trying to protect themselves.

The city of Savona had given Italy two popes, Sixtus IV and Julius II. Pietra d’Alba, barely thirty kilometers to the north, almost gave it a third. I think I was a little responsible for that failure.

I would have laughed if anyone had told me, on the morning of December 10, 1917, that the history of the papacy would be bent by the boy dragging his feet behind Zio Alberto. We had been traveling for three days, almost without stopping. The whole country hung on the news from the front after the drubbing the Austro-Hungarians had given us at Caporetto. Some said the lines had stabilized not far from Venice. Others said the opposite, that the enemy would be upon us and slit our throats in our sleep or, worse, force us to eat cabbage.

Pietra d’Alba appeared, carved out of the dawn light, on its rocky spur. Its position, I understood an hour later, was an illusion. Pietra was not perched on a spur but set on the edge of a plateau. Really on the edge, meaning that between the village wall and the brink of the cliff there was a passage barely wide enough for two men abreast. Then fifty meters of emptiness, or rather of pure air, laden with resin and thyme.

You had to cross the whole village to discover what had made its reputation: an immense plateau undulating toward Piedmont, a piece of Tuscany displaced there by the whims of geology. To the west as to the east, Liguria kept watch and reminded it not to make itself too much at home. It was mountain country, slopes covered in forest, a green almost as black as the beasts that prowled there. Pietra d’Alba was beautiful, with its slightly pink stone—thousands of dawns had encrusted themselves there.
