“This morning a blue tiger came in,” Kaede’s grandfather said.

“How on earth did it turn the doorknob? Clever thing.”

He seemed more surprised that the tiger had opened the front door and come in than by the fact that a tiger had entered his study—or even by the fact that it had blue fur.

“Good thing it didn’t bite you.”

Kaede made a point of tossing off the joke.

Inside, though, she felt a small stab of disappointment. He was awake for once, and it was this again.

She came about once a week, but her grandfather was almost always asleep.

And even when he was awake, all he talked about were visions.

He would spend the whole visit on them, and a normal conversation never really got going.

Even so, Kaede listened obediently to “the story of the blue tiger,” chiming in with little murmurs of assent again and again.

Because the time she spent in her grandfather’s house—her old family home—felt irreplaceable.

“And then the tiger—” her grandfather said, imitating the way it walked, crossing one foreleg over the other. “When it left, it smiled. Such a blissful smile.”

“A tiger smiled?”

Oh, here we go again, she thought, smiling inwardly at herself.

It was a story about a vision, something impossible in the real world, and yet here she was listening to it in earnest again.

Yes—though she always began by only pretending to listen closely, his storytelling was so skillful that before she knew it she had been drawn into his world.

Today, in fact, she even had the odd illusion that a blue tiger might really leap out of the illustration in some book on the shelves.

Perhaps he had said everything he wanted to say and was satisfied.

Her grandfather’s eyelids slowly closed.

All day long he sat in the motorized recliner in this room.

They had chosen an oversized one to suit his tall, lean frame, but it proved far more comfortable than anyone had imagined, and the fact that he had come to spend almost all his time in it was a serious miscalculation.

A wooden cane—indispensable when he moved around—leaned against the side table beside him.

But the care manager who had recommended it had grumbled to Kaede with a sigh: he uses it when he goes to the toilet, but when he’s choosing a book from the shelves, he can’t be bothered to use it at all. I worry he’ll fall.

He still loves books. But probably the contents—

—probably barely went in anymore. The thought passed through Kaede with a pang.

The study, crammed floor to ceiling with books, was full of the stale, faintly sour smell of ink.

It reminded her of the old-book district in Jinbōchō, which she loved.

When she looked up, the light filtering through the window had become a camouflage of sun and shadow across her grandfather’s sleeping face.

The high bridge of his nose and the wrinkles at the corners of his eyes cast intricate shadows over a face strangely free of age spots, despite his seventy-one years.

Compared with the old days, the flesh had fallen away from his jaw and cheeks, but that only made the depth of his features stand out more.

His thick long hair, parted in the middle above his broad forehead, was perhaps seventy percent white, shading into the black that remained; that too gave him the sculpted relief of an emperor on an ancient Roman coin.

Even allowing for a granddaughter’s bias, he had a truly imposing face.

(He must have been popular.)

Kaede gently pulled the blanket, which had slipped down, back up around her grandfather’s thin neck.

By the time she had finished cleaning and sprayed the soap-scented antibacterial mist—careful not to get any on the books—it was already time for the physical therapist to arrive for his rehab session.

The spray was not there only to keep the room clean.

Her grandfather often hallucinated tiny insects, the mosquito-like kind.

At such times, it also served as a makeshift “insecticide.”

(See you, Grandpa.)

(I’ll come again.)

Beside the study door stood a dressing table that had come down to him from her late grandmother.

Perhaps it was not so much aging as evolving with age.

The grain of the wood had been coated, as if with makeup, in the complex hues of accumulated time, and it gave the piece an exceptional depth.

Kaede took a hairbrush from the drawer, ran it lightly through her hair, and made a face in the mirror.

(Smile.)

The study door had once been a heavy oak one, but it had already been remodeled into a sliding door in anticipation of the day when her grandfather might have no choice but to rely on a wheelchair.

Careful not to make a sound, Kaede slid it shut and left her grandfather’s house in Himonya.

2

On the way home, swaying on the Tōyoko Line, she looked at the train window and saw a face reflected there, almost blank of expression.

She had taken the trouble to make herself smile, yet even the faintest trace of it was already gone.

The sky had begun to darken, as if brushed with a pale lipstick.

It was early autumn; the thunderheads had vanished, and clouds of many different shapes were scattered across the sky.

Suddenly a memory of her grandfather passed through her.

Twenty-three years earlier. Kaede is four.

On the veranda, perched in her grandfather’s lap as he sits cross-legged, she gazes at a sky turning crimson.

Her grandfather lowers his clear intelligent gaze to the child on his knees.

“Kaede. What do those clouds over there look like to you? Use all of them and make me a story.”

Looking back on it now, it was sandai-banashi—the rakugo game of spinning a story out of given prompts.

Perhaps he had been trying to make the wings of her imagination flutter—

That must have been his own form of aesthetic education.

Kaede answered at once.

“That cloud is a tiny grandpa. And that one is a flat grandpa. And, um... the biggest cloud is a grandpa fatter than you, Grandpa.”

“Well, you can’t make a story out of that,” he said, though his face broke into a smile.

And then, astonishingly, as if it couldn’t be helped, he improvised a children’s tale in her place, right there on the spot, called “The Three Grandpas.”

She no longer remembered the details.

But one thing stayed with her: the greedy “fat grandpa,” mistaking cold medicine for sugar, swallowed up all the cold medicine in the world, was laughed at mercilessly, and yet in the end lived longer than anyone in the world....

It was probably meant as a kind of lesson for Kaede, who hated bitter powdered medicine.

But his way of telling it was so wonderfully funny that she used to clap her hands with delight.

“Look, Kaede. Look.”

When she raised her eyes, only the “big cloud”—“fat grandpa”—was left. “Tiny grandpa” and “flat grandpa” had, quite literally, vanished into cloud and mist.

Exactly like the ending of the story.

Dumbfounded, Kaede kept looking back and forth between her grandfather and the “fat grandpa” in the sky.

Come to think of it, he must have been secretly keeping an eye on the clouds as he spun the tale.

And if “tiny grandpa” or “flat grandpa” had been the one left at the end, the story must have turned out very differently.

“Hey, Grandpa. Tell Kaede more stories. Otherwise—”

Little Kaede looked up and tugged at the hair growing out of the mole on his Adam’s apple.

It came out much too easily, and she remembered finding that so funny that she burst out laughing.

Sometimes Kaede thought:

(Maybe that was when I pulled the plug on Grandpa’s intelligence.)

It was only half a year ago that her grandfather had first begun to seem visibly strange.

When she went walking with him, his stride had become unmistakably shorter.

“Grandpa, maybe you’ve gotten fatter than you look. Your legs can’t keep up.”

He tilted his head and gave a self-mocking little smile. “I’m getting old.”

At first Kaede thought it was weight gain, or simply age—no, tried to think so.

But after that, things advanced quickly.

When he drank the coffee he loved, the hand holding the cup trembled violently.

Whenever she visited, he was dozing in the chair in his study.

His posture had become permanently stooped, and whatever he did, he did slowly.

No—more than that, more than anything.

More than anything, Kaede would never forget the shock of that day for the rest of her life.

In the middle of the night, her smartphone rang.

Still rubbing the sleep from her eyes, she answered, and the caller—a young man, by the sound of him—introduced himself awkwardly, for some reason seeming reluctant to say it.

“Um, I’m with the ambulance service.”

Then, sounding apologetic and faltering again and again, he went on:

“Am I speaking to Kaede? Ah—yes, I see. Well, your name was on a note stuck to the wall as the emergency contact, so that’s why I’m calling. The thing is, your grandfather here dialed 119, and—well, um...”

“What happened?”

“He says, ‘Kaede is lying here covered in blood.’”

At his regular clinic they said it looked like Parkinson’s disease, but they couldn’t be sure, and advised her to take him to a large hospital.

At the university hospital he underwent detailed tests, including a CT scan.

The result—the young doctor announced it matter-of-factly, ignoring the grandfather fast asleep in his chair beside them.

“It’s dementia with Lewy bodies.”

That her grandfather—once so brilliant—had developed dementia just after turning seventy—

Kaede could not possibly accept that fact at once.

And yet, when she looked into it herself, on the internet and in the materials she had ordered, every one of his symptoms matched the disease.

For the first time she learned that in Japan alone there were said to be more than six million people with dementia—and that “dementia,” though spoken of as one thing, in fact came in many forms.

Broadly speaking, dementia falls into three main types.

The most common, accounting for roughly seventy percent of cases, is Alzheimer’s dementia, apparently caused by deposits in the brain of a protein called amyloid beta.

It was probably this type that most people pictured first when they heard the word dementia.

Next most common was vascular dementia, caused by the aftereffects of cerebral infarction or stroke, and said to account for about twenty percent of all cases.

Both kinds often present with memory impairment—the same stories repeated over and over—disorientation, in which one’s sense of time and place becomes vague, or wandering.

And then there was the diagnosis her grandfather had received—dementia with Lewy bodies, also called DLB, from the initials of the English term—which accounted for around ten percent.

The name had only been given to the disease in 1995, which made it, in the long history of human illness, one of the more recently recognized disorders.

In recent years it had drawn attention as “the third dementia,” and efforts to understand its pathology were apparently proceeding at a rapid pace, both in medical practice and in clinical research.

In the brains and brainstems of DLB patients, there were said always to appear tiny deep-crimson structures—like little fried eggs—called Lewy bodies.

And it was these “little fried eggs” that brought on Parkinsonian symptoms such as trembling in the limbs and difficulty walking; REM sleep behavior disorder, with loud sleep talking; the tendency to spend the day in a drowsy haze; and visuospatial impairment, an inability to judge distance.

But—

the greatest hallmark of DLB, the symptom unlike any other, was above all visual hallucinations.

Some patients saw them in monochrome, others in color; the details varied. But what they shared was that the hallucinations were “vivid,” “lifelike,” “sharp and clear.”

For instance: you wake in the morning, open your eyes, and find ten people standing silently in your room with blank faces, staring at you so intently it seems they would burn holes through you.

Or a giant snake lies coiled heavily on the dining table.

Or a girl with braids follows just behind you wherever you go, all day long.

Outright unreal hallucinations are not rare either.

A pig walking on two legs, briskly crossing before your eyes.

A fairy springing gracefully about on a plate.

And the blue tiger her grandfather had seen—

Oddly, in many cases these visions are not accompanied by hallucinated sound.

“The things writhing in the hallucinations” are, to the very end, only visual phantoms; they do not speak to the patient.

And yet of all the information human beings receive from the outside world through the five senses, ninety percent is said to come through sight.

Which means that for most DLB patients, those “writhing things” clearly do exist.

The proverb they would most like to quote might be, “Seeing is believing.”

After all, it is there before their eyes, vivid and unmistakable.

No matter how much the people around them deny it, would it not be immensely difficult to convince them that it is “not there,” that they are “not there”?

Even so, if those around them carelessly insist, “There’s no such thing here,” “There’s no one there,” “Get a grip,” patients sometimes grow angry.

That is why people say caring for someone with DLB is difficult.

When Kaede read a handbook for caregivers, she found advice like this:

“When a patient says, ‘I can see a big bug,’ or ‘I’m scared,’ do not deny it with words like, ‘It’s just your imagination,’ or push them away with, ‘You’re ill, so don’t make trouble.’ Instead, clap your hands and say gently, ‘There, now it’s gone, isn’t it? You’re all right now.’ It is also effective to distract them by changing the subject.”—

She supposed that was how it worked.

Besides, she wanted at all costs to avoid getting into a quarrel with her grandfather, who had never once gotten angry with her.

That was precisely why Kaede had avoided talking deeply about his illness, and why, when he spoke of his hallucinations, she had consistently refrained from denying their reality in front of him.

To begin with, making a patient realize that he has dementia would be close to impossible; and even if it were possible, it seemed too cruel.

And yet, she also thought.

There was a strange discomfort in her own understanding and behavior, like a remainder appearing for some reason in a division that ought to have come out even.

It felt a little different from simple escapist denial—the wishful thought that her grandfather of all people could not possibly have dementia, or could not possibly go on losing his intellect like this.

(That’s right. Something is different—)

But what that discomfort actually was—

Kaede, as yet, had no concrete way to explain.

3

Fifteen minutes by bus from Gumyōji Station.

When she got back to her studio apartment, a book had arrived.

It was a collection of essays by the literary critic Setogawa Takeshi, who had loved mysteries beyond measure.

On the colophon it said: “First edition, first printing, April 1, 1998.”

If Kaede remembered correctly, he had died not long afterward, at the young age of fifty.

Which meant this book was, simply put, Setogawa’s last work.

Having grown up under her grandfather’s influence and become a complete mystery fanatic, Kaede had long since stopped being satisfied with novels alone and had even begun reading the collections of Setogawa’s criticism on her grandfather’s shelves.

And what she found was less astonishing than exasperating.

Setogawa would set one work after another on the slab and speak about its charms from his own freewheeling perspective; but those columns were sometimes—no, almost without exception—more interesting than the works themselves.

In a serial column called “Pilgrimage to Masterpieces,” for instance, he took aim at the representative works of the big three of orthodox detective fiction—Ellery Queen, Agatha Christie, John Dickson Carr—and mercilessly asked, “Are these really such masterpieces?” The essays were more logical and more thrilling than the novels themselves, and impeccably right besides—and yet, whether Setogawa knew it or not, his overflowing love for those writers seeped through between the lines, so that every time Kaede read him she ended up feeling oddly warmed.

The “god” who had made her love classical mysteries from abroad—

(Setogawa Takeshi.)

Just murmuring the name to herself made her heart leap.

Around 1970, the young Setogawa had reportedly been one of the central figures of the legendary university circle known as the Waseda Mystery Club, which produced any number of mystery writers and critics.

There had once been a café in Nishi-Waseda called Mon Chéri. Day after day, students from the Waseda Mystery Club gathered there, spraying spittle as they argued about mysteries, and at the center of the circle, it was said, was always Setogawa, smiling, thick-browed and strongly featured.

And her grandfather, too, had been one of the club’s leading members.

Coffee goes perfectly with orthodox detective fiction.

Its tall signboard—red, with psychedelic white lettering cut out to read “Coffee Specialty: Mon Chéri”—was said to have had an air of shutting out anyone who was not, in some sense, a “mystery specialist.”

Rich, bitter coffee, its foam swirling like a bottomless enigma.

A yellow-tiled exterior that called Leroux’s The Mystery of the Yellow Room to mind.

The footfalls of actors echoing down from the little theater upstairs—Chesterton’s “The Queer Feet,” perhaps, or else Ranpo’s The Stalker in the Attic.

Now that Mon Chéri was gone, all Kaede could do was let her imagination stretch its wings.

And yet the café as narrated by Setogawa and her grandfather must surely have blazed with the heat and radiance of Tokiwa-so in the comics world, or Liangshan Marsh in The Water Margin.

(I wish I could have heard the two of them talking mysteries.)

Because the place no longer existed, Kaede’s daydreams only swelled all the more.

Sadly, Mon Chéri was gone for good.

But books, at least, remained.

The books she loved, after all, were books she wanted close at hand.

And the books in her grandfather’s house were so carefully preserved in translucent glassine jackets, without so much as a dog-ear on any page, that she felt oddly hesitant to borrow them.

So Kaede decided to buy all of Setogawa’s essay collections in one go.

(Thank goodness. It’s like new. Even the obi is still on it.)

The condition of the book made her happy.

If she was honest, she would have preferred to buy them new.

But this final volume was already out of print, so she had reluctantly bought it from an online used-books specialist.

Now she owned the full set.

(What kind of twenty-seven-year-old woman goes around completing sets like this?)

Aware of her face loosening into a smile, she stood there flipping through the pages.

And then—

Four small slips of paper fluttered out from between the pages and fell to the carpet like ginkgo leaves.

(Hm. What are these?)

Carefully, Kaede picked them up and laid them out on the table.

Then she stared at the four rectangular pieces of paper, all of different sizes, and thought.

(Too many to be bookmarks.)

(But...)

(They’re too “heavy” to be page markers—)

They were clippings from magazines and newspapers.

And every one of them was an obituary reporting Setogawa’s death.

4

Taking advantage of the holiday, Kaede made her way back to Himonya in Meguro for the first time in three days.

In a residential neighborhood not far from Himonya Hachiman Shrine, the local tutelary shrine, her grandfather’s house stood quietly in a corner: a small two-story wooden house, already starting to look a little worn.

From the token garden, a cherry tree and fatsia stretched their branches and leaves out beyond the wall.

On the wooden nameplate fixed to the gatepost, her grandfather’s surname was written in bold, fresh strokes of ink.

It was the handwriting Kaede had known all her life.

They say the nameplate is the face of a house.

If the exterior still had any dignity at all, if it still wore a certain crisp presence, perhaps it was thanks to the force of those beautifully brushed characters.

But once she went through the gate, that atmosphere was abruptly spoiled.

There had once been round stepping-stones laid down here and there like markers leading to the front door, but since her grandfather developed dementia, they had been replaced by a plain little concrete path.

When she turned the knob of the front door, which still had yet to be renovated, the synthetic soapy smell of antibacterial cleaner hit her nose.

“Is that the helper?” Kaede was about to call out, but stopped herself.

There were no shoes in the entry that looked like a visitor’s.

The visiting home helper had probably finished the cleaning and laundry and only just left.

Several handrails, still new, had been installed at intervals along the hallway walls.

For her grandfather, unsteady on his feet, multiple handrails had become indispensable for getting around the house.

When you tried to apply for subsidies for assistive devices like these, the details varied from one municipality to another, but in many cases the process involved maddeningly cumbersome paperwork and enormous amounts of time.

So in the end, the reality was that people like her grandfather were forced to pay for most of it themselves.

She went into the living room to the left of the hall.

Her eye fell on the main pillar, still just barely glossy, where countless pencil lines had been drawn across the wood.

Those were the marks where he had measured her mother when she was little, and Kaede too—his one and only grandchild.

The numbers and dates written beside them were almost faded away, but even so his elegant hand was still plain to see.

And yet the sight of the handrail’s support post driven into the pillar as if piercing those words made her chest ache.

Glancing toward the window, she noticed several white T-shirts hanging up indoors to dry.

(Oh no. The helper slipped up.)

When there is a DLB patient in the house, it is best, if possible, not to dry clothes indoors.

Hanging clothes can be mistaken for people.

White T-shirts in particular often become a “white canvas” onto which DLB patients overlay vivid hallucinations.

For the same reason, she had heard that portraits and family photographs were best kept out of the patient’s sight, and there had even been a time when she hurriedly tucked away the framed photos lined up on a table, hiding them deep in a chest of drawers or somewhere.

As she hurried to take the T-shirts off their hangers—

behind her came her grandfather’s voice, comparatively steady.

“Sorry about that. Kanae hung them up before she left. Maybe the stains hadn’t quite come out yet.”

Her grandfather came into the living room holding a coffee cup, and slowly sat down on the bed.

The upstairs bedroom had long since become a storeroom, so now his range of movement was largely confined to this living room, where the bed was, and the study at the back.

But judging from his step today, he seemed remarkably better than the last time she had visited.

Large day-to-day swings in condition were another major hallmark of DLB.

“No, I was just smoothing out the wrinkles,” Kaede said, covering for herself, and gave up on taking the shirts in.

“So it was Mom who came, not the helper.”

“She looked as if she still had work left, so she rushed off. A pity—you just missed her.”

Inwardly, Kaede felt relieved.

Better that way—

at least today, of all days, when she meant to put that question to him.

To begin with, lately it had become rare to see her grandfather looking this well.

Today really was her chance.

“Coffee Kanae makes is good even when it’s gone cold.”

Still smiling, her grandfather slowly shifted his backside and resettled himself.

Then he took a sip of coffee with a hand that trembled just a little and spoke.

“I don’t think there’s much danger of spilling it today. Strange to say it myself, but I must be in rather fine form. Which is why I’d like to confirm something. It’s nothing but intuition, really.”

He took another sip and looked Kaede straight in the face.

“You have something important to say to me, don’t you? I can tell by your face.”

Kaede felt she might cry.

That first-person pronoun—boku—so characteristic of him.

Those gentle dark eyes, bright and lucid.

It was as if the grandfather she remembered had come back.

Perhaps because he was not in one of his drowsy spells, even his articulation was firm.

Come to think of it, in the last six months she had hardly had a serious conversation with him at all, so intent had she been on sparing him any strain.

If she was going to confirm it, now was the only time.

Kaede braced herself and began, “Yes. Actually...”

“There’s something I want to ask you, Grandpa.”

“What is it?”

“Grandpa...”

She fought desperately to keep herself from crying.

“Grandpa... you do know you’re ill, don’t you? You know that what you’re always seeing isn’t real—that it’s hallucinations, don’t you?”

No good.

Her voice was shaking.

“But you don’t want me to worry—”

The tears came.

She had promised herself she would not cry.

“You don’t want me to worry, so you’re pretending not to know, aren’t you?”

Still wearing that soft smile, her grandfather took another sip of coffee.

Then, with careful hands, he set the cup down on the dining table beside the bed.

“Yes. You’re right, Kaede. I am, unmistakably, a patient with dementia with Lewy bodies.”

So her intuition had been right.

Her grandfather’s black eyes and the irises within them were delicate as glasswork, filled with a depth that seemed to pull her in.

Yes—there, just as before, lived the light of intelligence.

And that, precisely, was the source of the discomfort she herself had not consciously recognized.

Over the past two days, as Kaede looked more deeply into DLB, she had learned all kinds of things.

That even among DLB patients, the degree of decline in memory and spatial cognition varied widely depending on where the Lewy bodies appeared.

That some patients were constantly frightened by their hallucinations, while others grew used to them surprisingly quickly.

That the symptoms varied from patient to patient in shade and intensity; truly, no two cases were alike.

That when the balance of medications, including dopaminergic drugs, worked with exquisite precision, there were many cases in which the hallucinations disappeared “as if a fog had lifted.”

That depending on the day, there could be no sign at all of fading intelligence.

And what surprised Kaede most of all was the fact that there were also many patients who clearly understood that what they were seeing was not real but a product of their illness.

Some, indeed, were so positive that they looked forward to the hallucinations that appeared every morning on waking, and had even made a hobby of sketching them afterward.

Scientific knowledge about DLB was still incomplete, and misunderstandings arose easily for that very reason.

Even in medical settings, there were by no means few doctors who took only a superficial view of a patient’s intense hallucinations and hastily concluded that “the dementia is progressing.”

The onset of DLB did not necessarily mean the waning of intellect.

When Kaede learned that, she felt that strange discomfort vanish, exactly as if a fog had lifted.

“Putting these Parkinsonian symptoms aside,” her grandfather said, looking at his faintly trembling hand, “I had realized some time ago that my mental state was far removed from what one would call normal. For instance—take the side of that bookcase over there. When I happen to look at it, the whole thing is covered in fine carvings, as if a master temple carpenter had decorated a festival shrine with them. But when I touch it, there is no carved texture at all. The surface is perfectly smooth. So which should I trust—sight or touch? It would be utterly impossible for someone to cover the entire side of the bookcase with such elaborate carving overnight without my noticing. Besides, no one in the world has any motive to sneak into an old man’s room just to carve his bookcase. Which means—regrettably—that touch is what must be trusted. Put another way, my sight is not to be trusted in the least.”

Kaede had no reply. She could only listen to his confession.

“So then what was causing this change? My computer was broken and unusable. I thought of searching on my smartphone, but as you see, my hands are far too unreliable. Or rather, ever since I called 119 after seeing ‘Kaede’s corpse,’ Kanae confiscated my smartphone, so I had no way to search in the first place.”

He puckered his handsome lips in a mischievous little way.

“So I begged the helper to call me a care taxi and went to the library to look it up. Simply following the text made my eyes blur and my lids droop, so it took all day... but I came away convinced of what disease I had. You know the phrase ‘the little grey cells,’ don’t you?”

With a self-mocking smile, he quoted the favorite expression of the Belgian detective Hercule Poirot.

“In my case, dark orange Lewy bodies are spread across the surface of my brain, so I suppose that makes me, if anything, the possessor of scarlet brain cells.”

“Then...” Kaede asked.

She could hear the rasp in her own voice.

“Why did you deliberately keep telling me about the hallucinations over and over?”

“Well, you see...”

He hesitated, just a little.

“It was because, especially when I talked about the hallucinations, Kaede’s expression changed so quickly. You would look surprised; then you would smile. Above all, you would answer me aloud. And when that happened, I could feel with certainty that Kaede really existed.”

“What... what do you mean? I’m always here beside you, Grandpa.”

“Perhaps this will make it clearer. Once, not long ago, I spoke to you quite frankly about what lay ahead for me—my future, which to tell the truth will probably not be very long. What shall I call it? I dislike using the phrase shūkatsu—‘end-of-life preparations’—because, for all how familiar it has become, it strikes me as insensitive. So let us say instead that I spoke to you in complete honesty about how I meant to conduct myself from here on. At the time I was conceited enough to think I was in almost perfect condition. I even felt that if I was going to tell you, it had to be then. And so yes... I talked for a good hour, I think. But then, for some reason, the Kaede who had listened to me in silence the whole time, and without the slightest expression on her face—”

He broke off once and lowered his gaze.

“All of a sudden, she simply vanished from in front of me. That Kaede was a hallucination.”

Or perhaps it was only the coffee, roasted a shade too dark.

For an instant a bitterness crossed his face.

“There can be nothing more miserable, more heartbreaking than that. Since then I resolved never to speak of the illness unless Kaede brought it up first. Even if I were simply taken for a senile old man with whom no proper conversation could be had—well, I thought that could not be helped.”

(Grandpa.)

Once more she said it to herself.

(Grandpa.)

Her grandfather had been unable to speak with his only granddaughter about how he wanted to meet his end—or rather, had chosen not to.

How had she failed to notice his anguish sooner?

He definitely had hallucinations.

Frequently.

He also suffered from memory problems and, at times, from various disturbances of consciousness.

His Parkinsonian symptoms made every movement extremely slow.

And yet—

his basic intelligence had not declined in the slightest.

5

It must have been the time when the nearby Christian kindergarten let out.

From outside came the voices of children passing in front of the house, singing what sounded like a nursery song.

The fact that they were off-key only made them cuter.

Her grandfather’s cheeks relaxed into a smile.

Autumn days drop like a bucket in a well—

even so, there still seemed to be a little time before dark.

“Actually, Grandpa, there’s something I want you to look at.”

Kaede took Setogawa’s essay collection out of her black bag.

If her grandfather had been asleep in his chair as usual, she had meant to drape the freshly washed blanket she had brought over him and sit beside him, reading slowly before going home.

But now—

with him as he was now, perhaps—

Her grandfather put on the rimless reading glasses he took from the pocket of his gown, settled them on his high nose, and even then held the book a little away from him as he said with feeling, “Why, it’s Setogawa-senpai’s last book. You needn’t have bought it—I would have given you mine.”

(I could never take it. Not a book lucky enough to be cherished like that.)

Kaede smiled inwardly.

“If I remember right, it was out of print. You did well to get hold of it.”

“There are online used-book shops now, and even pretty rare books can be surprisingly easy to buy. And—actually—this was tucked inside.”

Kaede opened the book and took out the four obituary clippings again, laying them on the table.

“Mystery and Film Critic Setogawa Dies”

“The Passing of Setogawa: A Gifted Talent Mourned”

“The Age of Layered Criticism: What Setogawa Left Behind”

“Setogawa on the Happy Meeting of Mystery and Film”

“Yes. I read them all at the time.”

With no more than a glance at the headlines, her grandfather murmured sadly, “I think two other papers ran pieces as well. Of course, I clipped them all.”

“I see.”

Once again Kaede was astonished by his memory.

Because of the disease, whole stretches of very recent events could vanish cleanly away, but when it came to the distant past, perhaps he could open those drawers at will.

“And, Grandpa—the real question starts here. It struck me as one of those so-called everyday mysteries: the sort of thing that sounds plausible enough, and yet doesn’t often happen.”

“I see,” her grandfather said with a nod.

“So the mystery is this: who on earth tucked these four obituary clippings into the book, and for what purpose?”

“Exactly. To begin with, there are too many of them to be bookmarks, don’t you think? But as page markers, obituary clippings somehow feel too heavy.”

“Very Harry Kemelman.”

Taking off his glasses, her grandfather mentioned the name of an old mystery writer.

Kemelman’s best-known work, *The Nine Mile Walk*, is a celebrated logic-driven mystery in which a single line overheard from the people at the next table in a pub—“A nine-mile walk is no joke, especially in the rain”—is enough to unravel in one sweep the entire truth of a murder committed the day before.

Then, abruptly, her grandfather asked, almost pleadingly, “Kaede. Could you give me a cigarette?”

Perhaps because of its neat seven-five rhythm, the words had something of a spell about them.

Kaede brought him a blue cigarette pack from the drawer of the dressing table in the study.

Gauloises—a French cigarette.

Not especially expensive, but not something you could buy just anywhere, either.

Whenever Kaede went through the secondhand-book district in Jinbōchō, she made a point of buying them at a tiny sundries shop known only to those in the know.

“I’d be grateful if you’d light it for me. Yes, that’s fine. My hands shake, you see. When I’m alone, I make a point of not having one.”

Her grandfather did not say he “smoked” cigarettes; he said he “drank” them.

Perhaps it was a remnant of the days before anti-smoking sentiment, when tobacco, like alcohol, had been accepted as an ordinary indulgence.

Even in his youth he had only ever enjoyed a few a week, and lately it was rarer still.

All the more reason, Kaede thought, to leave him at least this small pleasure.

When her grandfather had a cigarette, a dreamy look came over him for a while.

Kaede did not dislike the smell of Gauloises, but she was afraid it would cling to the T-shirts still hanging there, so she opened the window a little.

Slowly sending purple smoke drifting up, her grandfather said, in a clearer voice than before, “Now then—”

It was as though the cigarette had switched on the booster to his intelligence.

“What sort of story do you weave from these materials, Kaede?”

Kaede’s heart began to pound.

Her grandfather had always called hypotheses “stories.”

Now, truly, he had come back.

That grandfather.

“Here’s the story I came up with.”

Trying to look calm, Kaede laid out the hypothesis she had turned over and over in her head.

“Story One. ‘The person who tucked the obituary clippings into the book was the book’s former owner. He—or she—deliberately put them in there because he, or she, wanted other fans of Setogawa to share the emptiness left by his death.’”

When she glanced at her grandfather, he was nodding as if she had spoken his very thoughts.

As always, telling a story in front of him made her tense.

And yet—

(And yet, I’m happy.)

“Um, Story Two.”

Coming back to herself, Kaede went on.

“‘The person who tucked the obituary clippings into the book was someone at a secondhand bookshop. He—or she—was a fan of Setogawa. Then, for the first time in decades, an order came in for one of Setogawa’s out-of-print books. Thrilled, he—or she—slipped the clippings into the book as a kind of present for a fellow devotee they had never met—in other words, me.’”

Perhaps because of the intellectual excitement, her throat had gone dry.

“What do you think? Those are the two stories I came up with.”

Her grandfather answered.

“Hmm. Not bad. Each one has some logic to it, and neither is so strained as to be mere special pleading. But both contain a major contradiction.”

“I see.”

Kaede bit her lip.

“Listen. The contradiction in Story One is this: would someone devoted enough to keep Setogawa-senpai’s obituary clippings really sell a book to which he or she was attached? Especially when this was Setogawa’s last work. Anyone with normal feelings would keep it, clipping and all, as a treasured part of his or her own library.”

Kaede could only nod.

“You’re right. From a book lover’s point of view, that’s hard to accept.”

“Story Two is more pleasing than Story One. But the contradiction there is equally undeniable. If a bookseller tucked the clippings into the book out of goodwill... why not add even a brief note? If they took the trouble to slip the clippings in so carefully, they could at least have written something like, ‘As a fellow admirer, I was delighted by your order. Please forgive the liberty, but I’ve enclosed these articles reporting Setogawa’s death as a small gift in his memory.’ Why would he—or she—begrudge even that little extra effort? In short—”

Her grandfather cut to the heart of it.

“Story One and Story Two are both fundamentally broken. There must be some other story, X.”

“Then—”

Kaede asked in a hoarse voice,

“are you saying you can spin that Story X?”

Her grandfather said nothing. Holding the now-short Gauloises between thumb and forefinger as if reluctant to part with it, he drew in the last of the smoke.

His eyes slowly—but surely—narrowed.

Kaede worried he might be drifting off.

But that fear was groundless.

He opened his eyes fully and said, “I can see the picture now.”

“Unfortunately, the man who originally owned the book is already dead.”

“What?”

“Why, look. There’s a man standing right there, with a peaceful expression on his face.”

It was a hallucination.

And yet this hallucination was grounded in clear logic—

she felt that instinctively.

“Story X goes like this. While he was alive, he slipped those obituary clippings for his beloved Setogawa Takeshi inside one of his treasured books, as a token of mourning. But then—after his death, his wife, never realizing how precious they were, sold it off along with various other books to a secondhand bookshop while sorting through his effects.”

(That’s it—the truth.)

She couldn’t help thinking so.

Wasn’t this exactly the kind of perfect “story” that contained no break in logic, the kind that dropped straight into place?

“But,” Kaede pressed, “how do you know the previous owner was a man? It could have been a woman.”

“No,” her grandfather said matter-of-factly.

“When a spouse dies, it’s the woman who can suppress her grief and still act calmly. Men are useless. I know—I myself...”

He lowered his gaze.

“When my wife died before me, I couldn’t do a thing.”

For an instant, the memory of her late grandmother’s soft round face passed through Kaede’s mind.

For a while, neither of them said anything.
