Hearing Transcript

LAMAR: Will you need a Bible?

HUNT: No, thank you.

LAMAR: Please raise your right hand. Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?

HUNT: I do.

LAMAR: I understand you have information relevant to this committee?

HUNT: That’s correct.

LAMAR: Please give your testimony.

HUNT: Where should I start? With the day of the accident?

LAMAR: No. At the beginning.

June 2021

Nate Hunt was waiting for his wife. Lucy was late.

“Dad, I’m bored,” moaned Eliza, their daughter. Nate had her on his lap. Her hair had gone staticky from rubbing against his sweater, and it tickled his nose.

“Yeah, I know,” he sighed. “You already said that.”

Nate was bored too. All these ceremonies, tours, and press conferences seemed to have no end. At the same time, he was terrified. It might have seemed those two emotional states canceled each other out, but they didn’t. He had been through this before, the last time eight years earlier. Only then he had been here alone. He hadn’t had to rehearse in his head what he would say to a child if something went wrong. He still couldn’t find the right words. Maybe there simply weren’t any.

“When’s Mom coming?” the girl asked.

“Soon,” he said.

“But exactly how soon?”

“I don’t know, sweetheart.”

“*Ty khochesh konfetu, rebenok?*” The woman in the next chair rustled a bag of hard candies and smiled encouragingly. But Eliza was not in the mood to talk to strangers. She turned away from the woman and burrowed into her father.

“*Prostite, ona sonnaya.*” Nate smiled apologetically. He had learned Russian more or less by accident. He had quizzed Lucy so many times before her exams that a thing or two had stuck. His favorite word, *pochemuchka*—someone who asked too many questions—fit Eliza perfectly.

The woman nodded in understanding; she had probably been up before dawn too. Nate wondered which time this was for her. Third? Fourth? She showed no signs of nerves—quite the opposite, the broad smile never left her lips. Maybe because, besides the candy, her purse also held a packet of sedatives.

“Can we at least play a game?” Eliza asked.

“Sure,” Nate said. “Maybe ‘I Spy’?”

“Okay. You go first.”

Nate looked around the room. It resembled a classroom or a dentist’s waiting room: harsh fluorescent lighting, beige walls decorated with faded posters, rows of plastic chairs, puny plants on the windowsill. Except that a thick plexiglass partition ran down the middle. On the other side stood a long table, and on it three microphones, each labeled: Cyrillic on top, Latin letters below. Anton Kovalov. Lev Zaitsev. Lucy Poplaski.

“I spy something beginning with P,” Nate whispered.

“Plant?” Eliza asked.

“No.”

“Poster?”

He shook his head.

“Plexiglass?”

“Yes. Your turn.”

The little girl started squirming, paying no attention whatsoever to the way she kept jabbing her father in the stomach with first an elbow, then a knee. Everyone in the room—members of other families, officials, journalists—waved at her and smiled. But Eliza ignored them completely; she was intent on the game. She took every kind of competition extremely seriously. Once, when she came second in the sack race at a school event, she cried for the rest of the day. “Eliza is a very ambitious little girl,” her teacher said as they were leaving; her tone suggested perhaps a little too ambitious. “She gets that from her mother,” Nate replied without a moment’s hesitation.

“I spy something beginning with R,” she said.

“Reporter?”

“No.”

“Row?”

“No.” The girl shook her head with obvious satisfaction.

“Then I give up.”

“Rocket!” Eliza crowed.

Nate looked out the window. It rose skyward from an endless expanse of sun-scorched grass—haloed in milky-white vapor, glimpsed through the lattice of gantries. It did not belong in this landscape: the faded steppes of Kazakhstan, the dilapidated, peeling buildings, the howling of stray dogs. It looked like a relic of some extinct alien civilization. In a sense, that was exactly what it was: the Baikonur Cosmodrome had, after all, been built by the Soviet Union. There were two reasons for putting it here. First, the latitude made it easier to reach the desired orbit. Second, if something went wrong—if the rocket exploded just after liftoff, for example—the risk of flaming bits of metal coming down on someone’s head was relatively small. As far as the eye could see, there was nothing around but grass.

Nate had seen an explosion like that before. He was six, visiting family in Florida, and it so happened that the long-delayed launch of the shuttle Challenger was coming up. His parents took him to the Kennedy Space Center so he could watch the launch with his own eyes, especially since Christa McAuliffe was on the crew, an ordinary teacher who was supposed to give a lesson from space. He remembered it the way a child remembers, as if he’d had a camera with him and been taking random pictures: badly framed, out of focus, overexposed by emotion. He got a paper cap with the NASA logo on it, but it kept slipping down over his eyes, which made him terribly sad. They took seats in the stands directly behind a man in a cowboy hat. He blocked Nate’s whole view, so little Nate was already offended at the whole world. His father hoisted him onto his shoulders so he could see better, ignoring the complaints from the row behind them. Then came a blinding flash and a roar, hot air in his hair, vibrations running through his whole body until his teeth rang and his skin tingled. The rocket climbed higher, drowning out the cheers, belching fire, faster and faster, until it became a tiny dot in the sky. Then the dot flared and turned into several smaller dots. Some of them began to fall, while others traced elaborate squiggles in the air. For a split second Nate thought that was exactly what was supposed to happen, that these were space fireworks, like on the Fourth of July, and he was thrilled. The joy didn’t last long, and what came afterward—sobs, screams, chaos—haunted his dreams for years. Even so, he tied his own life to space. The vastness fascinated him, terrified him, drew him in.

A murmur ran through the room; camera shutters chattered again and again. At last, Nate exhaled. On the other side of the glass, a door opened and the Soyuz crew—Lev, Anton, and Lucy—entered. It was his wife the cameras were trained on. For one thing, she was unquestionably more photogenic than the Russians. Dark curly hair pulled back in a ponytail, a wide smile, an upturned freckled nose—she looked like the nice girl next door, someone you could be friends with within five minutes. For another, she was one of the last American women to fly into space from Kazakhstan. After more than a decade’s break, the United States finally had its own way of reaching orbit again: Dragon capsules, built, in the finest American tradition, by an eccentric billionaire. The first flights had gone well; NASA was already negotiating a new, larger contract that would make the Americans completely independent of Russian help. The clock was ticking.

“*Uvazhaemyye kollegi, dorogiye gosti…*” began Yuri Rybkin, head of Roscosmos, a gray-haired, powerfully built man in a cream blazer.

“What’s that man saying?” Eliza asked.

“Nothing interesting.”

“But what?”

“That a beautiful chapter in the history of friendship between our nations is coming to an end, but a new one is beginning.”

“What does that mean?”

That soon the Russians would no longer be able to collect ninety million dollars for a seat on a Soyuz, Nate thought. That without those regular cash injections Roscosmos would be on the verge of bankruptcy. That behind the declarations of readiness to keep cooperating lay envy, resentment, and bitterness.

“That we’ll be flying into space together, but separately,” he said instead.

The Russian official finished his speech, and the microphone passed to NASA’s representative, deputy director for crewed flights Steve Ayers—a stocky, balding man in thick-lensed glasses. More lofty platitudes, wishes for a successful mission, agency Newspeak. At last it was time to say goodbye to family: forced intimacy before cameras, among strangers, through plexiglass. Nate sat down with his daughter across from Lucy. He wanted to tell her so many things. That he was damn proud of her. That he’d miss her. That he was happy for her. That he was afraid for her. But he couldn’t bring himself to, not in this crowd.

“Hey.” She spoke first. “How’s your morning been?”

So, banalities, empty words. But empty words could be filled with something; they could carry something wholly unrelated to their literal meaning. A tone of voice, a pause, a glance—this was how they told each other everything that couldn’t be said, everything they wouldn’t even have known how to name, in defiance of semantics.

“Fine,” Nate said. “Right, Eliza? Fine?”

“When will you be back?” the girl asked. The question was also an accusation.

“You know. In six months.”

“You won’t be here for my birthday.”

“I will, sweetheart,” Lucy said with a sad smile. “More than once, even.”

“What?”

“Remember? The station goes around the Earth once every ninety minutes. If your party lasts three hours, I’ll fly over the house twice in that time. And I’ll be thinking of you then. Okay?”

“That’s not the same.”

Lucy pressed her hand to the glass. A substitute for real contact, required by quarantine.

“I know, sweetheart.”

“Then why are you going up there?”

Lucy didn’t get a chance to answer. The time for farewells was over. The next time they were supposed to see each other would be in six months. Assuming everything went well.

The Orthodox priest’s gold-embroidered vestment glittered in the flashbulbs. The aspergillum traced a wide arc through the air, and holy water ran down Lucy’s cheeks like tears. Another tradition, another superstition. Every stage of the preparations for the flight—and there were many—came with some ritual for the Russians, and after every mission another one was added; there was more folklore to it than at a village wedding. Every participant in the flight had to lay flowers at Yuri Gagarin’s monument. Raise their country’s flag. Plant a sapling in Cosmonauts’ Alley. Sign the door of the hotel in Baikonur. On the eve of launch, watch *White Sun of the Desert*, a Soviet hit from the seventies. And then there was, of course, the obligatory blessing from a clergyman, regardless of personal preference or religion—not once, but twice. As though the Russians believed the success of the flight depended not on the laws of nature or mathematical equations at all, but on the whims of capricious heavens that had to be placated with ceremonies lasting for weeks.

Lucy boarded the bus that was to take the crew to the launch pad. She was already wearing her Sokol suit: made to measure, fitted with dozens of lacings, buttons, and snaps, a veritable space corset. Getting into it took nearly two hours and required the help of several technicians, and because it was fully airtight, each crew member had also been equipped with a portable fan—a little blue suitcase—so they wouldn’t boil alive.

When the bus pulled away, when at last Lucy was out of reach of cameras and microphones, if only for a moment, she rested her forehead against the window and closed her eyes. She had been up since four in the morning, it was nearing six in the evening, and she still had a three-hour flight to the International Space Station ahead of her, in a capsule the size of an elevator—so she had to seize every chance to rest. The Russians, meanwhile, were already asleep.

But she couldn’t sleep. Her thoughts kept returning to Eliza’s question. Why? What for? Of course, she had answered it thousands of times already: first during the endless selection interviews, then in press interviews, public appearances, and private conversations. But then she said what was expected of her, what she was supposed to say, what other astronauts had been repeating for decades: that this was how she could serve science, that the exploration of space was humanity’s most important mission, that she wanted to inspire little girls all over the world, and so on and so forth. Only those were lies. If someone gave her truth serum—if such a thing existed—her answer would be less lofty. Why? Because nothing gives you such a kick, such a rush, as flying at thirteen thousand kilometers an hour. Because it’s an incredible adventure. Because other people couldn’t, and I could. Because it proves I’m the best.

All of it only made the resentment in her daughter’s voice, her reproach, hurt more. It wasn’t really about the birthday. Lucy was hardly ever home. For the last four years she had been living out of a suitcase, shuttling between Washington, Houston, Toulouse, Moscow, and Cologne—an endless round of training, courses, preparations, exams. Every hour—every minute, in fact—of the mission had to be planned and rehearsed again and again at least a year in advance. Lucy had to know every safety procedure by heart, memorize every instruction and technical diagram, know the name of every last screw, switch, and wire, in English as well as Russian. If she were truly doing all this for the good of humanity, perhaps she could justify it to herself, silence the guilt. But she wasn’t. She wondered whether her colleagues had thoughts like these too, whether they too grieved over missed first steps and first words, or whether this was simply a woman’s lot: to feel guilty for her own ambition.

Something else was gnawing at her too. The day before, she had met with Deputy Director Ayers. It had not been scheduled, it appeared on no calendar, and the words exchanged there were nowhere to be found in press accounts. Rising tensions. Conflict at the highest levels. Fear of escalation. It was hard to believe such a thing could happen in the twenty-first century, and in blood-soaked Europe at that. But Ayers had been completely serious. Storm clouds were gathering on the horizon.

The International Space Station was born of the optimism of the 1990s. Freed from the logic of the Cold War, Russians and Americans no longer had to race one another among the stars, so they joined forces for the good of humanity. Together, with Europe and Japan taking part, they built a laboratory orbiting Earth—for, no less, a hundred and twenty billion dollars. A symbol of a new era, of shared dreams, of the end of history. Of course, behind the lofty slogans lay pragmatic calculations. The Americans wanted access to Soviet technology, while the Russians needed money to keep their space program alive amid the chaos of perestroika, and no one but the Americans was in a position then to put up the sums required. Everyone got what they wanted, and everyone was satisfied.

But now, more than twenty years later, the equation no longer worked. For one thing, technically Roscosmos had nothing left to offer the Americans: it still relied on Soviet technology from the 1960s. For another, a new superpower had emerged—China—ready to bankroll the Russian agency, and without the tiresome questions about human rights, democracy, or freedom of the press. In other words, the marriage of convenience was beginning to fall apart, but the house in space still had to be shared.

The bus stopped in the middle of open country, a few kilometers from the rocket; the chirring of crickets carried across the steppe. Right, time for the last tradition. She stood up, and Anton ushered her ahead in the aisle with an exaggeratedly gallant gesture. Broad-shouldered, square-jawed, with short blond hair, he was actually quite handsome. Only his cigarette-yellowed teeth spoiled the effect. They had served together on the station when she flew there for the first time, eight years earlier. They knew each other well. Which was exactly why she avoided him.

“Ladies to the left, gentlemen to the right,” Anton ordered when they stepped onto the sun-warmed asphalt. A step behind him came Lev: a head shorter, reserved, with a mustache as bushy as his eyebrows. Lucy had met him during joint training—and liked him. He was a man of the old school, sincere and devoted to the cause. He believed deeply that the gulf between East and West could be bridged, or at least that from the vantage point of orbit the division ceased to make sense. He was the embodiment of the Soviet ideal: hardworking, rational, convinced that the future could be better than the past. This was his final mission, his farewell to space. He looked as though the thought saddened him already.

A moment later latches clicked open and zippers hissed. The Sokol suits, meticulously sealed barely an hour earlier by a swarm of technicians in latex gloves, cotton caps, and plastic shoe covers, in full view of their families and the press, were unsealed again. Of course everyone, including those same technicians, knew perfectly well that this was exactly what would happen. For on April 12, 1961, in this very spot, Yuri Gagarin had asked the driver to stop the bus. The man who was about to become the first to cast off gravity’s shackles first had to deal with the most earthbound of needs: he needed to pee. And since he flew and came back in one piece, every cosmonaut after him made the same stop—just in case. As usual, the men had it easier. To satisfy the demands of tradition, the women brought along a little plastic bottle of urine collected beforehand. Lucy poured its contents over the rear tire, then flicked out the last drops with the brisk twist of the wrist she had copied from the priest who had blessed them earlier.

The official anthem of the cosmonauts, *Trava u doma*, blared from the speakers, signaling that the crew was already inside the rocket and that less than two hours remained before launch. Steve Ayers wondered how many times he had heard that plaintive song, how many times he had stood on the viewing terrace at the Baikonur Cosmodrome. The twentieth? The twenty-fifth? He hadn’t the faintest idea. He had seen off so many missions here that they blurred into one another; his passport was filled with Kazakh customs stamps. But this moment, this final stretch, still sent his adrenaline surging. In a little while they would be sending people into space. There was no getting used to that; it never stopped being astonishing. A few more photographs, a few speeches, a teleconference with mission control in Moscow, and the dignitaries stepped out onto the viewing terrace. It was nearing eight in the evening.

“One thing ends, another begins.” Director Rybkin leaned against the railing. Folded into quarters, a leaflet with the day’s schedule stuck out of his back pocket. Rybkin came out of the military, and it was plain he hadn’t served behind a desk. His eyes gave him away: alert, focused, always moving, trained to watch for enemies, to spot traps. That habit might yet come in handy. His predecessor had been dismissed less than a year earlier in a cloud of scandal, amid corruption charges that could land a man in a labor camp for a long time. No one bothered wondering whether he was guilty or not; one look at his much younger wife’s jewelry was enough to make up your mind. More intriguing was why the prosecutor’s office had turned its attention to him just now. In other words: how had he disappointed his patrons? What expectations had he failed to meet? What had changed in the meantime behind the thick walls of the Kremlin?

“It doesn’t have to end,” Ayers replied. They were speaking English; it was easier that way.

“Ah, so you do want to buy a seat on another flight?”

“If you lower the price below the competition…”

“Right… the free market.” Rybkin grimaced. “Unfortunately, I don’t think that’s possible.”

“There are other projects.”

“For example?”

Ayers cast a meaningful glance up at the sky. The sun had already set, and the moon glimmered through the clouds. The last time a human being had stood on its surface was more than fifty years ago. There had been plenty of plans to go back, but there was never enough enthusiasm, or money—or a good reason. Over the course of his long career at NASA, Ayers had watched one project after another for a return to the Silver Globe go into the shredder. Just when he was beginning to lose hope that anything would ever come of it, that people might again travel beyond Earth orbit in his lifetime, everything changed. SpaceX—a private company, a startup that industry veterans had mocked only a dozen or so years earlier—showed that it was possible to fly more cheaply, more often, and farther than anyone had dared dream. What was more, advances in nuclear fusion meant that the long-nurtured dream of using the moon’s vast reserves of helium-3 as a revolutionary new fuel might finally become reality. America was looking up at the night sky with growing interest. And not just America.

“We’ll find another partner.” Rybkin stepped back from the railing and clasped his hands behind his back.

“China?”

“Do you object?”

“China won’t be your partner.”

“And you were?”

Ayers did not answer at once. He felt that from now on he would have to weigh every word.

“I’d like to think we were,” he said.

The Russian turned his gaze toward the rocket. Bathed in floodlights, in the blackness of the steppe night, it looked like some colossal monument erected in honor of—what? Human curiosity? Power? Arrogance?

“Do you know how much we pay Kazakhstan to use Baikonur?” Rybkin asked after a moment.

“The figures are publicly available.”

“Say it out loud. Let’s remind ourselves.”

“One hundred and fifteen million a year.”

“But one hundred and fifteen million of what? Rubles, perhaps?”

“No. Dollars.”

“Dollars, exactly.” The Russian nodded. “Is that a lot of money or a little?”

“For me? A lot.”

“Exactly. And how much money did Kazakhstan put into building this cosmodrome they now so graciously let us use? Are those figures publicly available as well, Mr. Ayers?”

“You’re trying to drag me into a discussion of politics. And I…”

“Just tell me: how much?”

Ayers looked Rybkin in the eye. He tried to understand where he was going with this, what he wanted to achieve. Whether this was merely about old grievances, or something more.

“Kazakhstan was part of the Soviet Union then,” the American replied diplomatically.

“Ah, exactly. And what was it, then, that caused the Soviet Union to collapse?”

“I don’t think that’s relevant to our conversation.”

“It’s relevant to every conversation.”

“Let’s say there are different opinions.”

“Yes, very different,” Rybkin agreed. “Mine is that our partner helped it happen. The same partner who has now taken half my budget away.”

“Private companies are simply cheaper. That’s not our fault.”

“Ah, I feel as though I’ve gone back to the nineties,” the Russian snorted. “Perhaps next you’ll tell me about the invisible hand of the market.”

Uh-oh, Ayers thought, time to change the subject.

“I get the impression our conversation isn’t taking a very constructive turn,” he said with the genial smile only an American can plaster across his face.

“Really?” Rybkin didn’t let up. “Because to my mind it’s leading to some very definite conclusions.”

“*Zazhiganiye… Predvaritelnaya…*” crackled over the speakers. The gantries swung away from the rocket, the launch umbilicals fell clear; it looked as though a flower were opening its petals. “*Desyat’! Devyat’! Vosem’!*”

“And what conclusions would those be, if I may ask?” the American wanted to know.

“*Sem’! Shest’! Pyat’!*”

“That all this”—Rybkin pointed first at himself, then at Ayers—“was a mistake.”

Flame-light flooded their faces.

Just above Lucy’s head a plush little dog dangled. It had a black nose, pink fur, and huge eyes. It weighed exactly twenty-three grams and was an important piece of Soyuz equipment. It reminded her of the souvenir Nate had once brought back from a conference in Edinburgh. It was a stone set in a frame, labeled “Scottish weather station.” If the stone is warm, the sun is shining. If it’s wet, it’s raining. If you can’t see it, it’s foggy. For the dog, the operating instructions would have read as follows. If the dog doesn’t move, you haven’t launched yet. If it starts to tremble, the engines are on. If it begins to bounce, you’ve left the ground. If it floats, you’ve reached orbit. If it catches fire, something has gone very wrong.

The toy began to shake. So did her hands, legs, stomach, tongue, eyes, brain—the vibrations ran through the whole capsule. Lucy sat—or rather lay—on the left-hand side, curled up with her knees pressed right up under her chin, wedged into a recess molded from a plaster cast of her body; to endure a few hours in that position, everyone swallowed a horse-sized dose of painkillers beforehand. In the cramped interior of the Soyuz cabin every cubic centimeter had to be used, so above her head, enclosed in a plexiglass bubble, the stowage lockers bulged, crammed to the brim. Anton, the capsule’s commander, was responsible for packing them. If the weight distribution was uneven, if one side carried so much as half a kilo more than the other, the craft would veer off course. And when you were traveling through the boundless reaches of space, that was never good news.

“Booster readings nominal,” came a voice through her headset.

Lucy felt an immense force pressing her into her seat, squeezing the air from her lungs. Beneath her, forty thousand kilograms of rocket fuel were burning. She glanced at the screen: the g-load read two, two and a half, three, three and a half… She wanted to laugh, cry, shout for joy—at last, after eight years of waiting, work, privation, sacrifices, her own and other people’s, she was breaking free of Earth again. But she smothered it as deftly and dispassionately as if she were flipping some lever on the control panel or pressing a button. For an astronaut, that skill mattered as much as a knowledge of orbital mechanics or first-aid techniques. Lucy was constantly being watched, judged, evaluated; even now, at this very moment, there was a camera right in front of her face transmitting her image to mission control centers in Houston, Moscow, Toulouse, and Tsukuba. She knew that although on this flight she was really little more than a passenger—Anton and Lev were in control, and even they didn’t have much to do for now, since the onboard computer was handling most of the tasks for them—she had to look serious and focused, otherwise she would end up on the radar of the psychologists monitoring the crew. So she followed the readouts on the screens and worked her way down the checklist.

“First-stage separation,” Anton said.

She felt as though someone had suddenly taken a foot off the accelerator. The rocket shed its tail, along with the empty tanks and four engines, each the size of a truck. Soon they would burn up in the atmosphere, like sacrifices offered to gravity.

“Launch escape system separation.”

There was a bang. The tower housing the motors that, in an emergency, would have yanked the capsule free of the rest of the rocket sprang away. The most dangerous phase of the flight was behind them now. They could breathe easier.

“Fairing jettison.”

Light flooded the capsule. Until now, the windows had been covered by metal panels. Lucy looked out. Half the window was filled with boundless blackness, in which the stars gleamed—seemingly the same stars she had watched in the night sky, yet somehow different: without the veiling shroud of the atmosphere, their light was much sharper, much clearer. She felt as though she were trying lenses at the optometrist’s and had finally found the right pair, the ones that showed the world as it really was. Below, Earth drifted by. It was night over Asia; city lights burned. She had missed that view. Every day. Every moment.

“Still makes an impression, doesn’t it?” Anton said.

“It does.”

“Second-stage separation,” Lev read off the computer’s callout. Of the soaring tower that had lifted off from Baikonur, only the capsule remained. “Third-stage ignition… initiated…”

“Same view as back then in the Cupola,” Anton went on. Mockery edged his voice. A challenge.

“Speed thirteen thousand seven hundred and two miles per hour,” Lucy said, ignoring the barb.

“We were over Turkmenistan then too.”

“Solar array deployment…” Lucy flicked the appropriate switch. “Initiated…”

“Those lights there are Ashgabat… And there, Bishkek…”

“Attention,” came over the headsets. Flight control in Moscow. “Loss of signal in thirty seconds. Communications will resume in about twenty minutes. Begin preparations for the first delta-v maneuver.”

“Copy.”

Silence fell in the cabin. For exactly thirty seconds. The bitter words that followed never made it into the official record.

The Baikonur Cosmodrome began to empty out. The loudspeakers fell silent, the floodlights trained on the launchpad went dark. Officials climbed into the limousines waiting for them; journalists folded up tripods and packed their cameras into their cases. Nate wondered how much demand there would be for their material, who would want to air it and—more importantly—watch it. The prospect of a mission to the moon or Mars could still set imaginations alight, and people still got excited by the disputes of billionaires hurtling around in orbit, but flights to the International Space Station had already become commonplace. News of one rocket launch after another was, of course, followed by a devoted band of enthusiasts, but it no longer made the front pages—not even the second or third.

That was hardly surprising: the ISS was, after all, a laboratory—one that flew, yes, but still a laboratory—and how excited can anyone get about pipetting, even in zero gravity? NASA’s press office understood this perfectly well. So it did what it could to drum up enthusiasm for the station: astronauts shot videos explaining how the space toilet worked (look, you strap your feet in with these bands, and you pee into this funnel…), spun like tops, squeezed juice from a tube, and chased down droplets trying to escape them, veritable circus performers of microgravity. But in the end even that grew stale. And so something that only half a century earlier had been little more than a dream, really more a fantasy, had already settled into everyday life, blended silently into the background. Such is the fate of every breakthrough, every technology: we can get used to anything. Perhaps that is the key to our success, the reason an animal made to live on a grassy savanna, a two-legged ape, has not yet gone mad in a world of glass, cables, and screens. Or perhaps rather: not gone completely mad.

“Dad, are we going?” Eliza asked. The girl was tired. A taxi was waiting downstairs.

“Just a minute.”

Nate looked up at the sky, toward the place where only moments before the fire-breathing engines had flickered. There was no trace of the Soyuz now; it had dissolved among the stars, a dot among thousands of dots. He drew a deep breath—he could smell exhaust and hot asphalt—then slowly let the air out of his lungs. The fear that had been devouring him for weeks, clinging to his every thought and seeping into every dream, finally eased a little. But it did not disappear, because it never did: it was ready to come back at the first opportunity, at the slightest trigger, like a spider sensing even the faintest tremor in the web stretched inside his head. That was exactly how he imagined it: thousands of silver threads strung taut inside his skull, and among them gleamed black eyes that never blinked.

That was why Nate was glad to be going home—to everyday life, to the familiar routes between home and school, the store and the gas station, the park and the library. He needed routine to tame reality, to cope with it. That was probably what most set him and Lucy apart. She was always hungry for new experiences; he avoided them, even when they involved something that seemed pleasant and simple enough. A weekend out of town, say. Sounds great—but what if the weather turns bad, they run out of gas, get a flat tire, Eliza gets sick, or they arrive and discover something is wrong with the reservation? Of course, in the end they went anyway—because he did want to go, or at least knew he ought to want to—but the questions never disappeared. So Nate devised in his head contingency plans of ever greater complexity, branching like the Amazon delta, and took preventive measures on a broad front. He filled the tank the day before, checked the tire pressure and oil level, made doubly sure he had packed the rain jackets and that the hotel room was booked for the right date. So Nate never really went on vacation, only on something like three-person military maneuvers in which, as chief of staff, he had to keep checking maps, receiving reports, and identifying potential threats. Needless to say, it was hard to relax under those conditions. He admired the scenery only after he got home, in photographs.

Lucy was his total opposite. Always self-possessed, always in control. For her, every new problem was an interesting challenge, a puzzle to solve. Running late for the airport and caught in a traffic jam? Great—time to work out an alternate route. A piece has to be sent off in a few hours and the computer freezes? Excellent chance to learn more about computers. You couldn’t wrong-foot her, couldn’t throw her off.

They had met fourteen years earlier at the International Planetary Science Conference. He, a geophysicist, was then part of a team working on a probe that was supposed to go to Titan (it never did—they ran out of money). She, still an astrophysicist then, was presenting the results of a spectroscopic analysis of the planets in the Gliese 581 system in Libra. He noticed her because her poster, instead of being mounted on a folding display stand, hung from an improvised contraption made from a broom handle, a straightened wire hanger, and thread from the hotel sewing kit. It turned out the airline had lost her luggage, so she had had to build herself a new stand out of whatever she happened to have on hand, and in under an hour at that. They talked for a while; he made a joke, she laughed. He asked whether she had any plans for the evening. She didn’t. Sometimes—rarely, but still—he regretted not asking what her plans were for the next ten years too. Who knows—maybe then they wouldn’t have gone out for drinks, kissed in the hotel elevator, spent the night in his room—in short, that whole chain of cause and effect that had brought Nate here, to the cosmodrome in Kazakhstan, would never have happened. Astronauts’ partners had it hard. Partners with a temperament like his had it especially hard.

“Nate!” Someone laid a hand on his shoulder. Deputy Director Ayers. Though they had met only twice before, he sounded as if he were addressing his best friend. You could tell he had spent his life in politics. He had a reputation as an operator as deft as he was ruthless. “Will you be coming to the farewell dinner?”

“Sorry, not this time. The little one needs to get to bed.”

“I understand. If you need anything…”

“Thank you, we have everything we need.”

“Excellent… Then I’ll see all of you back here in six months?”

If everything went as it should, said the voice at the back of Nate’s head. If Lucy’s capsule didn’t hit the atmosphere at too steep an angle and burn up. If it didn’t come in too shallow and skip off the sheath of gas around the planet like a stone skimming across a lake. If the heat shield held. If the cabin didn’t depressurize. If the parachute opened. If they didn’t go into a spin that would bring lethal g-forces. If they didn’t smash into the Kazakh steppe.

“Exactly,” he said, forcing himself to smile.

The latches clanked as they were released, and the hatch began to open. The Soyuz filled with the deafening roar of fans and the distinctive smells she remembered from her previous stay: sweat, hot metal, stale plastic. In the open hatch above them, the faces of the crew appeared. Anton went first—a light push and he was already drifting upward toward the exit, as if swimming through air. Lucy swept the hair waving in front of her face back and tied it tightly with an elastic. She was ready; she could hardly wait. Then Lev—one movement of his hand and he was up. Now it was her turn. She stood on the seat, bent her knees slightly, and flew after the others.

Though she had been here before, though she had done hundreds of parabolic flights in which she had accustomed her body to weightlessness, her head still spun and her stomach clenched. Up, down—suddenly the words had lost their meaning, become useless. She felt as though she were inside an optical illusion in which nothing was where it ought to be, one that tangled the senses more and more the longer you looked at it. Lucy felt sour saliva rise in her throat. Don’t puke, she thought, just don’t puke. Someone was already hugging her, patting her on the back. She nearly banged her head into the camera lens. They were in the Russian segment, in the Rassvet module, so it was cramped—too cramped for two people, never mind nine. They crowded together like fish in a net.

Lucy recognized the three astronauts they were relieving; their Soyuz would be heading back to Earth in a few days: Igor Volkov, Kate Hortchak, Francesco Russo. A moment later Dragon pilot Ezra Wendley was greeting her. Red-haired, muscular, white as a sheet, he had once been military and looked it. His capsule had docked three months earlier and was due to remain for another three. Off to her left she caught a glimpse of Lafayette Summers, the ever-smiling botanist, an African American from Louisiana. Last in line—though line was hardly the word, since everyone was in motion; it would have been easier to plot a checkmate in three-dimensional chess than to say who was where at any given moment, whose hand this was and whose leg that—was Devaki Anand, an American of Indian descent. Lucy knew her least well. She was the only one in the group who was not a professional astronaut but a space tourist. Of course that did not mean she got to sit around sipping drinks from a glass with a little palm tree on it; NASA had put this crew member to work as well. How much she had paid for her stay on the station was a secret, but it was unquestionably a seven-figure sum. For Devaki, who had made billions in Silicon Valley, that kind of expense was no great problem.

Lucy longed to go straight to her cabin (a grand word—in reality it was a space smaller than a broom closet), get undressed (though nobody said it out loud, astronauts flew in diapers, officially and euphemistically called “high-absorbency garments”), and wash up (that is: wipe down her entire body with disposable wet wipes, because there were no showers; free-flowing water plus microgravity meant a guaranteed short circuit). But there was still one item on the program: the press conference that capped the flight. They moved to Nauka, one of the station’s oldest—and newest—modules. Oldest, because work on it had begun back in the nineties. Newest, because the Russians had only managed to finish it and send it into space at the beginning of this year. What they had not managed was a safe docking: when Nauka was attached, its previously faulty main engine unexpectedly fired, pushing the entire station out of orbit and very nearly causing a catastrophe. Inside, the module looked like any other: cables, indicator lights, electronics jutted out everywhere from every surface.

“All right, let’s line up…” Volkov, the station crew’s current commander, took charge. “New arrivals in front… Everyone else in back…”

It took them a moment to find their assigned places. So as not to disorient viewers on Earth, they all adopted the same orientation, as if they were standing on a floor, though half of them might just as well have been hanging upside down. Everyone grabbed onto something—those who had only just arrived at the station instinctively used their hands; the rest, already accustomed to microgravity, hooked their feet into the restraints. It was more comfortable that way. Three, two, one: the light beside the camera’s glass eye turned red.

“ISS, this is Moscow,” the controller called. “Not too cramped in there?”

“We’re managing,” Volkov replied with a smile. Like the other Russians, he wore his hair cropped short, and his neck was red with razor burn; shaving in space was especially unpleasant. Above his head gleamed a golden Orthodox cross stuck to the wall.

“Excellent. Soyuz, how was the flight?”

“Without any problems,” Anton said.
