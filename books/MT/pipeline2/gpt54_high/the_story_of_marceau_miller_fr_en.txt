Prologue

A flash drills through my brain; a violent blow to the head throws me off balance. I am two hundred metres above the void, climbing a rock face I know well. My nails scrape at the granite, my hands give way. In that instant I understand that it’s over. For the thrill of the challenge, out of pride perhaps, I have always climbed with my bare hands, without a rope. I knew I would pay the price one day.

First I see the sky. Then a hundred metres of cliff race past in the blink of an eye. I can barely make out the silhouette disappearing behind the summit I was about to reach. I know that silhouette. It has just caused my fall into the void, and I am going to die. One thought soothes me strangely: I am going to join my sister. She has been gone for twenty years; when I got up this morning, I was far from imagining we would meet today.

My name is Marceau Miller. I am a bestselling novelist. Tomorrow, on my Wikipedia page, just below the line that says “Born,” you will be able to read: “Died: 16 May 2021 (aged 40).” The irony is that I have spent years writing as though each day might be the last. I am leaving behind the most important manuscript of my life. As if some part of me had sensed this moment, had anticipated this unforeseeable rendezvous with fate. The Grim Reaper does not send you a registered letter warning you of the day and hour of your death. I leave it to them—to Sarah, to the others. Let them make of it what they can. I owed them the truth.

My body hangs in the air; I have control of nothing now. The ground is rushing up at a terrifying speed. My heart tightens for my children, Hermione and Benjamin, and for my wife, Sarah, with whom I built everything.

I catch sight of my pickup parked at the foot of the cliff and understand that I am going to smash down right beside it. Nothing else crosses my mind. And yet they say that in a moment like this your whole life flashes before your eyes. We have all had that awful nightmare: falling into the void, with nothing left to hold you. You wake drenched in sweat, your heart on the verge of exploding, with a sick feeling that follows you for hours.

I have always wondered whether the brain disconnects before the fatal impact. In a few hundredths of a second, I’ll have my answer.

Fifty metres.

Ten metres.

Darkness.


PART ONE

THE FALL

Chapter 1

Saturday 15 May 2021

The day before Marceau Miller’s death

Settled in the rocking chair on the chalet terrace, I look up from the novel that had absorbed me completely. My tea is still steaming. This is where I love to be, facing the Léman, my lake, as if it belonged to me. In every story my husband writes, nothing heralds the storm about to break over his characters. It usually creeps in, then hits them head-on. I take a sip of my Saint James, a strong black tea with chocolate notes, and set my cup down. It’s his twentieth book, and he still manages to surprise me. I don’t entirely understand Marceau, but you have to give him one thing: he deserves his success. No one handles suspense like he does. Too much so, even. Everywhere, all the time.

The hum of an engine pulls me out of my reading for good. A boat is approaching the jetty at the bottom of the garden. I serviced it last week; it’s one of the boats Karen, my business partner, and I own as part of our fleet. This little speedboat is nothing now but a patched-up nutshell, one we would never dare offer to our clients. But our children love it. Sometimes I take it to the agency, skimming along the shore as far as Yvoire. It takes fifteen minutes longer than driving, but it’s as handy as Marceau’s old pickup for hauling equipment.

Benjamin is at the helm of the speedboat. At ten, he already handles it well, but he’s coming in a little too fast for my liking. Marceau waves his arms. He must be telling him to slow down. I let out a breath despite myself when the boat glides alongside the jetty without a bump. They’ve seen me. All three wave enthusiastically. I smile and wave back. Hermione, our eldest, standing at the bow of the boat, springs onto the landing stage and catches the mooring line her father throws her. I keep an eye on the manoeuvre. The jetty on pilings isn’t very wide; its long wooden legs plunge into the depths to root themselves in concrete blocks embedded in a mixture of silt and green-tinged pebbles. At this time of year, when the sun is still so warm, the planks aren’t slippery. Everything’s fine. Benjamin cuts the engine and joins his father and sister.

I set my book down without even slipping a bookmark into it. I know the page number by heart. Forty, our age, Marceau’s and mine. And already, on page 40, things are going badly for the characters. It’s only the beginning, I can tell.

I get up and stretch. The shade of the pergola is cool on this terrace built by my grandfather, a cabinetmaker—his last piece of work before he suddenly passed away some ten years ago. This outside part of the chalet, partly covered in greenery, gives the place the feel of a holiday home. The garden runs for nearly eighty metres, bordered by woodland left in a wild state and a hedge equally left to fend for itself. In fine weather, thanks to the closeness of the lake, the grass stays green for a long time. It was mown recently, and along the shore it is edged with a strip of grey sand and pebbles. I walk towards the children. Benjamin is already trying to tell me about his adventures as he runs towards me, but he’s still too far away; I can’t hear a word. Hermione comes back up with her father, both of them loaded down with diving gear.

“With Dad I went down to five metres! I saw a féra this big!”

My boy’s spread arms seem too short for the enormous fish he saw in the lake. Marceau comes up beside me and kisses my forehead. The coolness of the water still lingers on his lips. Benjamin tugs at his sleeve, wanting him to confirm his account of the gleaming miracle from the lake. Hermione catches her father off guard by jamming onto his head the cap he dropped behind him. At that moment I feel the fragility of happiness—fleeting as a fish vanishing into deep water. It makes me dizzy; I become aware of everything I hold most dear, and of how much pain I would feel if I lost any one of them. Happiness reaches out its hand to us without warning. It can snatch it back at once.

“Sarah?”

Marceau smiles at me, then goes off a little way to shrug off the diving gear. I catch his eye and feel as though I can read there exactly what I’m feeling myself. But he knows better than anyone the cost and impermanence of moments like this. He has already glued so many pieces back together after so many shattered joys. He makes me think of that Japanese art of repairing broken pottery, kintsugi.

I can almost trace with my finger the golden scars his memories have left in him.

Chapter 2

Saturday 15 May 2021

The day before Marceau Miller’s death

The big hand of the living-room clock hasn’t yet reached eight. Its familiar ticking fades beneath a noise from outside. The light is going; I glimpse the headlights of several vehicles filtering through the maples in the drive. The low, distinctive purr of Alexis’s sports car makes the panes of the old bookcase I inherited from my grandmother Louise tremble. Through the window, I soon see him getting out of his machine, wearing a broad grin. Alexis, as ever. Benjamin is already circling the gleaming car. Alexis grabs him and holds him upside down by the feet like a rag doll. I hear their bursts of laughter and smile too. Then they tear off in a mad chase and disappear towards the back of the garden, shouting.

Marceau comes down the staircase, repainted white, the dominant colour in our house. I love light. The large openings onto the lake and the reflection of the mountains mean we never lose sight of it. I’ve arranged the chalet’s ground floor like a loft—modern open-plan kitchen mixed with older elements, opening onto the sitting room and the library corner. A spacious living room, perhaps a little too full of furniture, I’ll admit, but cosy and warm. Marceau joins me by the sofa. He takes me in his arms; I know he loves my smile. He gives it back to me without taking his eyes off me.

“Are you ready?”

“Never really for evenings like this, you know that. I’d rather slip under the sheets with you.”

That idiot melts me. This is the phase I like best: the stretch between the moment he finishes one novel and the moment he disappears into a new one. That’s when I find him again, when we’re closest.

“Then why are you so attached to this ritual every year when your novels come out?”

“Ritual, Sarah, is the key for a writer. A writer without ritual is a dead writer.”

“Have you become superstitious? Seriously, what are you afraid of? That it’ll all stop? That your readers won’t love you any more?”

“Second thing, pretty lady: a writer who isn’t afraid is a writer who’s finished. You always have to write with passion laid over fear.”

“What a line. You should save that for your next interview... Ah, and here’s your publisher, as if on cue. Early, as always. He loves this evening. And especially my fresh goat’s cheese empanadas. Soon we won’t be able to see his belt any more.”

Édouard Payet, with his genial paunch, lets his gaze travel over the front of the chalet as though he were hesitating to come in. I know he is taking stock of the whole house. The woodwork, the openings, the roof supports, the little upstairs balcony, the terrace, the greenery maintained with studied neglect. He has always loved this place, the den of his star author. He knocks, and we go to greet him. In the publisher’s wake, Freud, his Welsh corgi, waddles into the small hall I cleared for the occasion of all the things Benjamin is forever leaving lying around. The little dog’s claws skid on the gleaming parquet. Short-legged and rather plump, barking constantly, he looks like his master. The pair of them are eyeing the food within sight, farther off on the kitchen island. I can’t believe it: Édouard already has a handful of peanuts in his hand. And there I was wondering where I’d put the bowl. He has obviously found it. In one movement, he raises them to his mouth and swallows them whole, like tablets. An ogre, but with a jovial smile and disarming patter.

“Sarah, Marceau! I look forward to this day with delight every year. Nothing changes here! And it mustn’t—that’s the secret of my star writer.”

His hearty hug is as manly as ever, and I can feel on my shoulder the grains of salt from the peanuts his fingers have left behind—Freud didn’t have time to lick them off. Édouard suddenly bends towards Marceau, a conspiratorial look in his eye.

“Did you get my little present?”

I look at Marceau, who seems embarrassed. I have no idea what he’s talking about.

“Listen, Édouard, it’s beautiful, but... I’m not really a watch man, and... I’d feel terrible if I damaged it.”

“A Breitling Aviator, that’s made for you! You fly your old Savage Bobber almost every week. Marceau, cut loose a little! You’re a grafter who doesn’t indulge himself enough. With what you earn, you can afford it!”

“The house by the lake and my crate are enough for me. You know me—I don’t want to be caught off guard if one day it all stops, just like that, all at once, without warning.”

“I could publish you with my eyes closed, Marceau.”

“Never do that.”

“Don’t worry, I’m not senile yet. And if the text wasn’t any good, Marceau, rest easy, I’ve got some excellent ghostwriters.”

Marceau’s face changes almost imperceptibly. He has just tipped into his own world.

“A mishap in a manuscript... for me that would be a fatal crash, Édouard.”

“Oh, come on, I’m teasing. I’m always impatient to see what you’ll come up with, you know that.”

I hold out the tray of empanadas to Édouard while Marceau slips away without a word. Freud lifts his snout, waiting for crumbs, but Benjamin comes running up and distracts him from his goal. My son strokes the dog’s head and leads him away, calling to him cheerfully.

Édouard gulps down his last bite with undisguised satisfaction before making his way, with an almost ceremonial step, towards the terrace. The publisher’s ritual can begin, the one he always performs far from my sitting room, where I have little tolerance for those heavy swirls. From the inside pocket of his tweed jacket, he reverently draws a Cohiba Behike 56, the Holy Grail for aficionados, nestled in its Spanish cedar case.

His gestures are precise, almost liturgical. First he takes out his Xikar pocket hygrometer to check the ideal moisture level of the wrapper leaf. Then, with expert fingers, he rolls the cigar beneath his nostrils, breathing in the scents of leather and precious wood. The engine-turned silver cigar cutter—a gift from Marceau—makes a clean cut, releasing the first earthy notes. Édouard studies the cut end carefully, like an oenologist scrutinising the robe of a grand cru.

His S.T. Dupont lighter gives off that characteristic blue flame. The toasting ritual begins: the flame delicately licks the foot of the cigar, preparing the tobacco for ignition. Édouard then slowly turns the Cohiba above the flame in a choreography measured to the millimetre. The first puffs rise, carrying the promise of the complex aromas to come—notes of roasted coffee, bitter cocoa and eastern spices.

A satisfied smile forms on the publisher’s lips as he draws in the first mouthfuls, eyes half closed, savouring the moment as one savours a fragment of eternity. The coming success of Marceau’s new novel is already mingling with the ribbons of smoke rising into the evening air, dancing above the still waters of the lake.

I turn up the hi-fi; we use the power of those speakers far too rarely. The music fills the house and carries all the way to the water. Some evenings, I like to break the silence, flood the chalet with joy and noise. I catch sight of Karen dancing on the terrace with her daughter, Zoé, and mine. I hadn’t seen them arrive. Karen is wearing a burnt-orange jumpsuit with butterfly sleeves, loose and belted high at the waist. A light fabric with a plunging neckline that flatters her slender figure. The whole thing with ankle-strap wedges that bring her to nearly one metre eighty. At that moment I regret not having chosen my linen trousers, the ones that show off my curves. Standing on tiptoe, I kiss Karen and, brushing her outfit with my fingertips, give her a wink; she smiles. The girls sweep her off again just as I ask where Rollin, her husband, is. Between dance steps, she stretches out an arm towards the garden. Farther off, on the lawn, Alexis is whipping up Marceau and Rollin, his closest friends for the past twenty years, ready to set sparks flying tonight. Alexis, drink in hand, loosens them up, and they launch into one suggestive series of hip-swivels after another. Big and muscular, Rollin is as awkward as ever, but he goes along with it. He’s a contemplative sort, warm by nature, the group’s gentle dreamer, impossible to stump when you ask him about flora and fauna on our outings. A nonconformist to his fingertips, he never gave up his freedom and scrapes by with his food truck. From the front steps, Édouard exhales heavy curls of smoke after drawing on his glowing cigar. His face is lit up, his eyes fixed on Marceau. The truth is that in moments like this I am always afraid something will happen and shatter everything—like in my husband’s novels. There are false alarms, and there are real tragedies.

10:00 p.m. The sprinklers come on. I’d forgotten about them; my heart leaps. A few drops hit me, and cries of surprise and amusement burst out. Alexis keeps Rollin under the artificial rain while Marceau runs in long strides towards the shed to switch the system off. And while the three friends play to the gallery, my son tugs at my sleeve and pulls me beneath the fine droplets, refreshing in this hot evening with its false air of summer. I sober up a little, my heart pounds, but I breathe—and let go.

The magic ends as suddenly as it appeared. The sprinkler system is switched off, and the boys are soaked. Alexis strips and tries to drag Rollin towards the lake. Rollin resists the drunken troublemaker and lets him go. He’s had enough, soaked to the skin. Alexis, taunting, almost naked in the middle of the wide garden at nightfall, in the wash of the chalet lights, sends out one last invitation to the crowd. Rollin shakes his head while Alexis dashes across the lawn and disappears in a dive into the lake, black now with night. Several seconds go by, then he bursts up from the depths in a loud splash, grinning. The lapping water around him makes the surface shimmer, where the points of the lights dance across the vastness of the lake. In the distance, the Swiss shore winds like a lit-up snake. The water is shockingly cold, I know. Even after several drinks, you don’t escape its bite. The main thing is that it not take the lives of the people I love; it already spat me back once, just before I drowned. I owe it the fact that I became a strong swimmer, with even a few medals to my name. The lake and I, like the forests and mountains around it—always.

Rollin’s clothes cling to his skin, revealing his heavyset shoulders.

“I think I need a bath towel now.”

“Idiot, that won’t be enough,” I tell him, laughing. “You know the house—go up to the bathroom and take some things from Marceau’s wardrobe. They’ll just be a bit tight.”

Karen bursts out laughing with me as Rollin goes into the house. We’ve known each other a long time, through our men. Our friendship and the trust we have in each other made it possible for us to think about going into business together and setting up this boat-rental agency, which is doing rather well now. A few metres from the shore, Alexis whistles and throws his soaked underpants towards the garden. Freud needs no encouragement and catches the wet fabric in mid-air, bringing it back to his master. Alexis comes towards the bank and backs out of the water, presenting us with his white backside. Karen is the first to react:

“Alexis, the children! You’re too much!”

Alexis keeps up his clowning as he heads for the jetty, using it as a launch ramp to dive once more into the lake with exaggerated clumsiness. Benjamin starts undressing in his turn, but I stop him dead. Disappointed, he goes down to the shore and cheers Alexis on, while Alexis, from farther out, tries to persuade me. It’s hopeless, boys.

Rollin reappears a little later, dry, in Marceau’s badly fitting clothes. There’s something odd about him—is he tired? He has lost his smile and strides over with a large towel to rescue his naked friend, out of the water now and beginning to shiver. Alexis hardly warrants something that size, but he’ll look more civilised draped in it for the end of the evening.

Together they move a few steps away towards the back of the garden, under cover of the foliage, at the edge of the woods, not far from the moored boat still rocking from Alexis’s antics. They seem deep in conference. I hope Alexis isn’t dragging Rollin into some new harebrained idea. What’s special about evenings like this is that they seem to throw us twenty years back, into the carefreeness of those days. Marceau comes out of the house in turn, serious-faced, as if he were already somewhere else—that worried look I know in him from time to time. Why tonight? Will I ever get an answer to that mystery? The rare times I have tried to know more about the feelings that move through him, I have found myself as if at the edge of a bottomless precipice. That’s the limit of our marriage. An unspoken rule, a safety distance, his safety distance.

It is after midnight, and the bathing episode has changed the mood. The buffet is holding out against the last assaults. Freud’s barking draws attention. His hackles are up and he is standing facing the little wood bordering the garden. Édouard whistles to him, to no effect. In the end he goes to fetch him back. There are nocturnal animals all around the chalet. The children sometimes startle them when they venture into the woods.

Édouard comes back. In his master’s arms, tongue hanging out, Freud seems already to have forgotten everything. Édouard thanks me for the evening and reminds Marceau of the schedule over the next seventy-two hours, especially the TV appearance on the major literary programme and the studio recording for a radio spot. The press officer will send him his updated timetable for bookshop signings. What I’ve learned over the years is that this business of being a writer is only a form of parole. I share my husband with Édouard, the journalists, the readers... but also with a great many fictional characters. If they could sometimes fade away and leave me a little more of Marceau, I’d prefer that. It isn’t enough for me when he closes his computer only for the time it takes the machine to chew over the manuscript he’s working on. But I’m not the one who decides, and I’m not certain he really decides himself.

At 1:45 a.m., everyone has gone home. Hermione and Benjamin are finally in their room, flattened by sleep. I knock discreetly on the door of Marceau’s study, his sanctuary. I hear his low voice just behind it.

“Five more minutes, I’m finishing a message. I’m coming.”

In my head I translate that as... half an hour, maybe an hour. I head for the bathroom and swallow a Dafalgan. Fatigue, alcohol—the headache won’t be long in coming. I’m exhausted but satisfied; the evening was a success. As I slip under the sheets, exhaustion and the slight floatiness brought on by too much alcohol carry me away. I don’t like the idea of falling asleep without the warmth of my man beside me; I resist. I need him. I resist a little longer, but my mind disconnects.

Chapter 3

Sunday 16 May 2021

The day Marceau Miller died

Blurred light, 5:10 glowing on the alarm clock. Marceau isn’t beside me. Damn writer. I frown, but my eyes close again. I sink back under.

8:15 a.m., slight headache, and I can tell through the shutters that the weather is fine. The light hurts. Beside me, the sheets aren’t even rumpled. Did he sleep in his study or what? I cross the corridor on tiptoe, without waking Hermione and Benjamin. What mother doesn’t protect her children’s sleep? Marceau isn’t in his study. I don’t go in; I hardly ever set foot there: he doesn’t like it.

My memory struggles through the fog of waking; I search my recollections, nothing comes back to me. He should be here, with me. The living room is in disarray; I pick up a few remains of last night’s party here and there and scan the garden: no one. I step outside, barefoot on the terrace, facing the lake, calm, unruffled by the slightest breeze. Our little speedboat is tied up, alone, at the jetty. The air is mild, almost as warm as in the chalet. Over by the hangar, I can’t make out our old pickup. I could have sworn it was still parked there yesterday. I skirt the chalet, in the silence of the cool grass swallowing my footsteps. I feel the cold blades tickling the soles of my feet. I go over to the hangar, standing wide open as usual. The Savage Bobber is there, its propeller gleaming, its wide wings spread. I’d have heard it taking off from the field next door anyway. Still no sign of the pickup. I turn around and glance towards the woods and the track leading to the road in front of the house. In a voice still dulled by sleep, I call Marceau’s name, but only a bird nearby answers with a flap of wings and rises above the chalet. For a moment I envy its position, above the world, and its view over the lake and the mountains. Can it see Marceau from up there?

Back inside the house, I open the cupboards by the door. I find the trainers there, Marceau’s lined up beside mine, and above all I check his climbing bag: empty. His climbing shoes and chalk bag are gone. I start, then remember: he had given that up, the day he promised me he’d stop his free solos, that thrill of climbing without a rope, without protection... and, above all in my view, without the slightest sense. Jackets and coats are hanging on hooks or in the wardrobe. If he hasn’t taken anything with him, he’ll be back soon. I send a text, reminding him while I’m at it that we need to coordinate our schedules for the coming days, between his obligations tied to the promotion of his novel and, on my side, the boat rentals that never let up at this time of year. Not to mention the fact that the children don’t manage themselves yet.

Noon. I peel potatoes in irritation for the chips Benjamin keeps asking for. And for the tenth time, at least, I tell the children that no, I do not know where their father is. When it comes to promises, Marceau is the first to desert. Hermione was counting on him to install her connected lighting system, Benjamin on him to tackle the complicated assembly of his new radio-controlled plane. I tell them to negotiate compensation. On my phone, no new message. I call for the third time, still no ring at all: “Hello, you’ve reached Marceau Miller...” I hang up. By the fifth text, my tone changes.

For fuck’s sake, where are you?


Chapter 4

Monday 17 May 2021

The day after Marceau Miller’s death

8:00 a.m.

I call Benjamin one last time. Otherwise it’ll be off to school without breakfast. Hermione is raw. Asking her to clear the table is enough to make her storm off muttering and slam her bedroom door. A normal Monday morning. Almost normal. Usually Marceau gets on Hermione’s nerves before I do.

Last night, I served them my first lie: Dad’s away for the launch of his new novel, I’d forgotten, he’d mentioned it. I still haven’t started the round of calls to friends and acquaintances; though I don’t really believe it, I cling to the idea that Marceau is going to show up with an explanation I hadn’t thought of, to the idea that everything will start up again as normal.

9:15 a.m.

A bitterness in my throat—it’s stress; my stomach can’t take it. In the bathroom cabinet, I search desperately for medicine. I find nothing, half of it is out of date, I’ll have to sort it. It’ll be all right. As soon as I hear something, everything will go back into place. Jittery, I get my things together, trying not to forget anything for what is already shaping up to be a bad day at work.

Late to the agency, I don’t have my head in the usual emergencies. Karen is already dealing with the first clients. Probably because of the dazed look on my face, she asks whether everything is all right and how the rest of my weekend went after Saturday night’s party. And in the end I crack. I admit that I haven’t heard from Marceau since the end of the evening, in fact. Does she have any idea where he could be? I see the stupefaction in her eyes. I must look like an idiot. She hesitates, searching for a reassuring explanation. Could it be connected to his book launch? It doesn’t hold up; he would have left me a note, even if he just wanted to withdraw for a while, as sometimes happens. I make my way around the agency as though I were naïvely looking for him here too. Karen makes me tea and checks the list of boats available while I count them one by one, moored at the agency jetty. On rare occasions Marceau has borrowed one, as Alexis or Rollin have too, for that matter. Karen calls Rollin, while I try Alexis. My heart quickens as every handhold I am clinging to gives way. They know nothing. I move on to his publisher. Édouard can think of nothing better than to remind me how urgent the TV appearance and radio interviews of the next few days are. One dead end after another, even after contacting acquaintances of acquaintances. My knuckles whiten from gripping my phone so tightly. An alarm is going off in my head. I can read the same barely veiled worry in Karen’s eyes. She assures me she can manage the agency on her own and practically throws me out; I have better things to do—Marceau has to be found.

I have always been able to count on Karen. We understand each other perfectly—laughing at the same things, keeping cheap judgments at bay, making allowances for our flaws. Real partners, with limitless trust. So much so that it’s almost dizzying: she could take everything from me if she wanted to; I’d keep my eyes shut. Truly, going into business with her and setting up the agency on the lake was one of the best decisions I ever made. Choosing independence too, and staying true to my values. Finding my place, on the lake, surrounded by forests and mountains—my stronghold, my roots.

But Marceau is part of my roots too. Leaving without warning, without answering his phone, especially at the moment his new book is coming out, is not normal. As though I were being asked to move forward without light, with nothing to hold on to. My heart leaps at every text and every call I receive, more and more of them now, from people around us, as worried as I am, asking for news, sometimes suggesting ideas... and no doubt drawing their own conclusions in silence.

3:20 p.m.

I’ve just come back from driving along the shores of the lake, from Yvoire to Meillerie, all the way to the Swiss border. I stopped everywhere—restaurants, sports clubs, every place Marceau goes, sometimes very isolated ones, when he wants to write far from everything. I have to act. I need help. I park in front of the gendarmerie in Thonon-les-Bains, the nearest and the biggest.

I have a knot in my stomach as I go in: what the hell am I doing here? This is where I’ve ended up, with nothing but my anxiety for luggage. They are my last option. A young gendarme listens to my distress and writes down everything I tell him. He tries to be reassuring and convinces me that for the moment there is nothing alarming. Then he starts asking me questions about my marriage, any conflicts, any infidelities there may have been... My face hardens. I remind him that my husband is missing and that I am here precisely because I have no explanation. He asks me to calm down. Did I raise my voice?

Eventually a certain Captain Robin Delmas presents himself to me. An arrogant type, looking down on me. In the office where he receives me, I have to go over everything again, step by step, explain it all again, once more—the evening, the alcohol, yes, no other substances, obviously not! I can barely control my voice. On the verge of blowing up, I stumble over my words. Who does he think we are? Among celebrities, illicit substances are common, he says. That’s when I really get angry. No, Marceau doesn’t touch coke. Our first and last joints go back to high school. His condescending look is unbearable. Marceau left no note, nothing is missing from the house? No. No warning signs, no particular change in his habits? I’ve already gone over all those questions myself. I spit out yet another curt “No,” short of breath. He left in the pickup, that’s all.

Delmas concludes that since Marceau has been absent for less than forty-eight hours, he cannot start anything for a forty-year-old adult in full possession of his faculties, with no particular conflicts and no troubling reason for concern. And that’s that. He looks me up and down, almost with a satisfied smile to top it off. I feel an urge to hit something rising in me. Clearly I am the only one who understands that nothing about this situation is normal. I have the feeling that whatever I say, he won’t hear a thing. As if I were up against a wall, my anger comes straight back into my face. I’m shown out; I’m to keep him informed, end of story.

I leave feeling worse than when I came in, with trouble breathing, a sense of emptiness, abandonment. Misunderstood, despised, but no less determined. Delmas will be seeing me again, that much he can be certain of. My brain is running flat out. There have to be solutions... And then a flash. An idea. I get into my car and tear away.

Chapter 5

Monday 17 May 2021

The day after Marceau Miller’s death

Thonon-les-Bains is in my rear-view mirror. Captain Delmas’s exasperating voice is still echoing, as if he were hanging on to my bumper. I want to speed up and leave him far behind me, but traffic on the main road is jammed solid. I turn off onto the back roads, closer to the lake. It won’t save me any time, but at least I can throw the car around a little. The lake opens up through gaps in the greenery—ashes, beeches, maples. It at least doesn’t betray me. I speed along the little roads between houses with tiled roofs weathered by time, sometimes tinted a soft green with moss. The timber-clad homes blend into the landscape. At last I am nearing home. A familiar landscape, at once comforting and cruelly empty. My heart beats with the last bends, wavering between the gnawing hope of finding Marceau and the icy fear of his absence.

As I reach the chalet, I catch sight of a vehicle parked in front of the house. The hedge trees barely let me make it out. My heart is pounding wildly. I accelerate along the dusty track, a mixture of falun and earth where the holes and stones spare neither tyres nor suspension. In my wake, a brown cloud rises and whitens the surrounding leaves. Maybe Marceau has finally come back? There’s no room for anger; dread is overwhelming me. A prickling runs through my body. Fear feeds on me, as if everything were slipping from my grasp. Like that strange feeling just before something happens.

A figure suddenly comes out of the woods, waving both arms at me. I realise I’m driving far too fast. Instinctively, fuelled by adrenaline, my right foot slams the brake pedal, my tyres skidding for several metres. I stop level with the man, who, a little shaken, waves dust and air away from his face. It’s old Reynaud. I then realise the 4x4 is his. Even though my wild hope has just been dashed, seeing him here comforts me. Yves Reynaud knows me as well as Marceau does—has done since we were teenagers. And he is also, and above all, a retired gendarme, solid as a rock, who doesn’t look seventy. We don’t see each other often, but with him it’s as if time swallows the gaps and the distance. He is always there, faithful, loyal. Fatherly. He comes around the car and climbs into the passenger seat. The engine is still running, I can’t speak, my face must be a wreck. He leans towards me and takes me in his arms. I suddenly feel overwhelmed, biting my lips so as not to cry. Against his shirt I smell the heat of summer and the forests, like on Marceau when he comes back from his long walks. Reynaud was born here; this region is his. I cling to him like a heaven-sent lifeline.

“Tell me everything from the beginning, Sarah. What’s going on with Marceau?”

In him I see the solicitude of the friend, eager to reassure me, ready to do anything to help, and also the reflexes of the former gendarme, more worried. At least he listens to me. This old fox knows the region like the back of his hand. I tell it all again. That’s always how it goes in this kind of case. Just saying the word case frightens me. All this sends us twenty years back. My brain refuses to remember. I know Reynaud is thinking about it. I start the car again, calmer this time, and park in front of the house.

We get out; he doesn’t leave my side, he watches me. I lead him towards the garden shed, which I hadn’t yet thought to inspect.

“What are you looking for here, Sarah?”

I open the plastic storage chests and catch sight of our diving gear. I rummage through the clutter on the homemade workbench that serves as a desk. I know Marceau comes here now and then. I find only papers, tools, nothing that catches my attention. I slam my fist down and send a set of screwdrivers flying. Reynaud pulls down the wooden bins sitting on the high shelves. I tell him to be careful, they’re full of nails, screws and tacks. Reynaud handles them cautiously; they’re heavy. One of the bins is empty, but before he puts it back, I run my fingers across the bottom: a fine film clings to the pads of my index and middle fingers. I sniff it. Chalk. The powder you coat the insides of your hands with to keep them dry and secure your grip while climbing. Reynaud meets my eyes.

“He promised me he’d stop.”

A sob cuts me off and keeps me from going on. Reynaud takes me in his arms. At that moment I want to cry, but nothing comes, as if shedding a tear would confirm that the worst has happened. I’m afraid, as though I already know. All it took was a fine trace of powder, almost imperceptible. No secret can really stay hidden. I can feel Reynaud’s heart pounding in his chest. He places his calloused hands on my shoulders; his face changes. Then he lowers his eyes before speaking. I search for them; he raises them to mine.

“Listen, Sarah, I know Marceau well. We do see each other from time to time.”

“What do you mean? Without me? Why? He never told me.”

I pull away from him.

“We’re all driven by old demons.”

My blood turns to ice. The door of my worst memory creaks on its hinges. Flashes make me flinch. I reject those images. This pain is like the needle of the syringe I dreaded so much as a child—I used to thrash and scream. She was my best friend, it was twenty years ago. She was Marceau’s sister. Born the same year as him, less than a year apart, we were inseparable.

I stare at Reynaud for a long time before asking him:

“You mean Jade, is that it?”

“That was my investigation back then. I don’t know where we screwed up...”

The ground seems to give way beneath my feet.

“Where is he now, Marceau? Tell me, damn it!”

“I don’t know, Sarah, I don’t know... but it has happened that I’ve seen him near...”

He breaks off, as if to give me time to absorb it. But I don’t want to waste time, I want to know.

“Near the fucking place where Jade disappeared, up in the mountains, is that it? Why rake all that up again? Do you know how much it hurts?”

Everything comes flooding back. I haven’t got over it either.

Chapter 6

Monday 17 May 2021

The day after Marceau Miller’s death

Reynaud’s 4x4 eats up the mountain road towards the border between France and Switzerland, less than forty kilometres away. On the narrow D25, between hedgerows and forests, we pass through peaceful villages bright with flowers, where the houses, with their exposed woodwork and broad roofs, seem to have been there for ever, like scenes from our grandparents’ postcards. At Sciez, we continue onto the D1005, the main road leading into Switzerland. Reynaud accelerates, overtaking again and again on the stretch through Évian. He is silent, focused. At Évian, we drive along the lake, the Alps on the horizon with their summits capped in white. As we go on, the slopes with houses clinging to them loom over us more and more. Kilometre after kilometre, the landscape around us turns to mountain. We pass Lugrin, then the border at Saint-Gingolph. I send Karen a text asking whether she can look after Benjamin until I get back. I haven’t been back to the exact place we’re going for years. And yet Marceau and I do sometimes hike in the surrounding area. We have always shared that irrepressible need for nature, for forests, for mountains, for escape. But right now, the immensity around us suddenly makes me dizzy.

The road gives way to a track that grows stonier and stonier. Shaken about in the cab, I hold on. The tyres bite into the mountain as we climb, my ears popping. On the track, narrowing and narrowing as if towards the source of a stream, we crawl past the parking area at La Planche, a tiny patch of dirt and stones, isolated among the trees, the last place still accessible by car. Reynaud keeps going on the increasingly rough track. Then, on a bend, a few hundred metres from Switzerland, he cuts through the trees. Branches crack beneath the tyres. He pushes the 4x4 on in first gear until we can go no farther and come to a stop. We are almost 1,800 metres up, about forty minutes’ walk from Lake Neuteu. We exchange a look; Reynaud takes out a flask, puts it in a backpack and takes his walking poles. This is the first stage, the easiest. Around us, the forest—not just any forest, not for me, not for Reynaud. My heart is churning; I breathe out and start walking. Reynaud already has dozens of metres on me. This climb, which has the look of a simple hike, shakes me. I watch where I put my feet, avoiding holes, the mountain’s traps. I begin to feel the heat in my thighs and calves. A sheen of sweat on my face. Then it appears before us, Lake Neuteu, ringed by jagged massifs, forests as far as the eye can see, and oppressive memories. I drive away the flashes blinding my mind, but the dread persists, clings on. The years have never truly varnished over the tragedy lodged deep inside me.

I get there first, galvanised by fear. I’ve overtaken Reynaud, who has fallen behind. My gaze scans the edge of the lake isolated in these mountains like a laser beam. Nearly two hundred metres long and barely a hundred wide, bordered with short grass running up towards the summits and disappearing into the raw rock. Absolute emptiness, not a soul on the horizon. The only truly remarkable objective for a solitary man like Marceau rises there, about a kilometre away. The highest rocky peak in the landscape, a monster of sheer verticality, all stone and challenge. Not exactly a walk in the park, more a route for an experienced mountaineer. Reynaud comes up beside me. I think of the chalk found in the garden shed; a shiver runs down my spine. Reynaud says the name for me.

“La Dent du Vélan.”
