1

“I want to swim,” she thought.

She wanted to strip off her shirt, skirt, and underwear and leave them in a heap at her feet, to feel on her bare skin the softness of water and the refraction of light, and to move on and on through a soundless space wherever her heart led her. As the temperature of the water settled against her skin, the boundary between herself and the world would grow vague, and weight, age, and sex would all lose their meaning. If she tried to speak, every word would turn to bubbles, rise higher and higher, blur into the white distance, and finally disappear. The hot season was almost over, so why was she thinking like this?

That was it. The office before her in the early morning looked very much like an indoor pool with no trace of people in it.

The desks stood in neat rows like lanes divided by ropes, and the still, pale-blue, unpeopled space had the feel of a water’s surface. The smells of chlorine and printer toner were surprisingly alike too, in that both were sharp enough to make her feel she was on the verge of remembering something. Looking for a time and place where she could be alone and work without interruption, she had ended up, after all, in the office where she spent most of her day. This was supposed to be the place where she showed her most capable self, and yet right now she could have shouted something incomprehensible at the top of her lungs or launched into some random dance if she wanted to. Of course she did no such thing.

She was not, in any situation, the sort of person who shouted or danced.

Between six and seven in the morning, the Food Products Sales Department, which occupied half the nineteenth floor of Nakamaru Trading’s Otemachi headquarters building, was completely deserted. It was the section that handled the quintessential work of a trading company: importing food from overseas and selling it to domestic firms.

The brand-new air before her, not yet tainted by anything, soothed the mind and body of Eriko Shimura, who was always pressing toward conclusions. As part of the duty of being first in, she switched on the copier.

Ever since she was a girl, she had liked getting ahead of things. Her father, a senior veteran at the same company, had taught her that staying one step ahead was one of the essential qualities of a trading-company man. Her parents, with whom she lived in an apartment in Setagaya, seemed dissatisfied that their only daughter was never at the breakfast table, but by her eighth year at the company they no longer said anything.

At thirty, Eriko still had no intention of leaving home. Without her mother’s support, it would be impossible for her to keep herself this immaculate, maintain her health, and work. And if she did move out, her mother too would surely find daily life alone with her taciturn father stifling.

No matter how early she came in, she had less than forty minutes to herself. This was the kind of workplace where it was perfectly normal for most of her colleagues to arrive a full hour before the official 8:30 start, checking email with convenience-store food in hand. Threading her way through the fifty-odd desks, she sank one after the other of her Tod’s pumps—polished by her mother until they reflected even the outlines of the fluorescent lights—into the carpet and headed straight for her station. Half the people in the department were permanent employees, but among them the only women were Eriko and Senior Staff Sayako Yotsuba, who was ten years her senior in the company. By contrast, all of the temps and contract staff were women, but they invariably came in at the last possible moment.

The convenience-store bag holding her breakfast and the Nikkei rustled against her beige pleated skirt. She sat down at her desk and powered up her computer. The startup sound rang out so low it seemed to vibrate at her feet, then spread slowly through the whole room in an invisible arc.

This was the one moment when she was allowed to do nothing, think about nothing. It might even have been the most peaceful time of her day. While she waited for her tidy desktop to appear, she savored that tiny freedom. Now that her annual income had long since passed ten million yen, Eriko could say with confidence that the most valuable thing in the world was time. Given time, most things could somehow be managed. Because everyone hurried frantically through life now, anything—a plan, a product, a work of art—that had plainly had time lavished on it was valued for that alone. Proper table manners, letters of at least three sheets beginning with a gentle seasonal greeting, makeup so light it looked like bare skin, well-cared-for leather goods: what people admired in all of them was the time that had gone into them.

To make room for breakfast, she first took out an alcohol disinfecting wipe and cleaned the desk till it squeaked. She carefully wiped the crevices of the keyboard and the phone receiver as well.

Call her neurotic if they liked, but unless she did this, she could not feel that this one small patch belonging to such an overlarge space was really her post. She knocked apart the pile of circulated documents and ran through them one by one. The work went several times faster than it did during business hours, when people were constantly coming and going and the phones rang without cease. Time could be made, if you knew how to devise it. That was the first thing her immediate superior had taught her after she entered the workforce. A few minutes later, when the mountain of paperwork had finally vanished, Eriko, almost wanting to rub her hands together, at last pulled the convenience-store bag to the center of the desk.

Out came a carton of café au lait. And a packaged sweet bun, a new product called “Karinto Melon Bread.” She had checked two convenience stores near home that morning and both had been sold out; she had finally found this precious item at the shop in the subway station. Though she knew perfectly well it could not possibly contain anything good for the body, she still turned it over and checked the ingredients list. Maybe it was an occupational hazard.

Mitsuzaki, a major bread maker, was a client of the grains division. Artificial sweeteners, preservatives, coloring. Even at her age, secretly eating the kind of thing her natural-food mother would have hated gave her a faint, illicit thrill. And all for just ninety-eight yen. Back in middle and high school, some of her classmates used to buy sweet buns like this at the school shop and fill their stomachs. She had of course been grateful for her mother’s homemade lunches, but Eriko had envied those huge, garishly colored buns with a desperate intensity. In those bodies—girls her own age, so easily satisfied by a carefree pastry—she had imagined an easygoing, expansive inner life.

She widened the row of bookmarked sites, set the cursor on the URL of her favorite blog, and with a click dove into a private stretch of time that belonged only to that woman and herself. As her eyes followed the lines on the screen, she bit into the melon bread, which was nearly as big as her face. The cookie crust broke apart with a crunch, and butter, brown sugar, and melon flavor spilled out. She was right. The balance of salt and sweetness was not bad, but it tasted undeniably cheap. Even so, taken in together with the words on the screen, it seemed delicious in a way that seeped all through her.

“The idea of putting karintō and melon bread together is just idiotic, right? I found it at the convenience store and burst out laughing. But once you really chew that greasy, crunchy dough, with its faint soy-sauce note that’s almost like mitarashi glaze, a fresh melon fragrance opens up like you’ve just come out of a tunnel. I’m addicted right now. Idiotic taste. Idiotic price. If you make a meal of this kind of ‘idiot food,’ all sorts of things get easier. There’s something soothing about the feeling that you’re just not taking life so seriously. Oh, but I’m not recommending it.”

Not taking life so seriously. What a nice phrase, Eriko thought. To her, life had always been something to confront in deadly earnest, something to grapple with. She was sitting there in a daze, buoyed by a faint sense of accomplishment and the heaviness of old grease spreading through her chest, when a voice dropped from above.

“You’re eating like a schoolgirl, Shimura. You’ll get fat.”

Yasuyuki Sugishita, her colleague on the seafood team, had a way of speaking that first made the other person check whether they were the one at fault. While she reflexively looked herself over, he would keep right on forcing his own pace. That attitude seemed to bat away criticism itself; though he was no particular perfectionist, she had never seen him scolded or cautioned by a superior. In a sales department full of ex-athlete types, Sugishita stood out: his scrawny build, his handsome but slightly long face, his narrow chin, his thin, bloodless lips. He also had the unusual background of having studied architecture in graduate school, so although they had joined the company in the same year, he was two years older than she was.

“Morning. Oh, this bread? It was featured on a blog. I finally managed to find one.”

Disappointed that her time alone had been cut short sooner than she had expected, Eriko instinctively pointed to the computer screen. Sugishita planted a hand on her desk and leaned in hard. Too close. The thought crossed her mind, but not strongly enough to make her pull away. The sharp smell of his hair product stung her nose.

“Huh. A housewife blog? What does a single woman like you find so interesting about that, Shimura?”

“Ohyo’s No-Good Housewife Diary” was a blog she had read almost every day for the past two years. The top page had an unadorned design. The posts were long, with few paragraph breaks. The occasional photos were taken with a cell phone and, to be honest, were not very good. Even so, it always ranked within the top thirty of a certain site’s popular housewife-blog listings. Perhaps what set it apart was the absence of the “anti-fans” that usually came with housewife blogs.

“It is a housewife blog, but it doesn’t feel all housewife-housewife somehow. It has just the right amount of looseness. Her eating habits and housekeeping are pretty slapdash, but somehow there’s still a sense of cleanliness—or maybe restraint. And she’s the same age as me, so I feel close to her.”

“Ohyo” lived with her husband, who managed a supermarket, in an apartment in Tokyo. For the time being, she apparently had no intention of having children. They did not seem especially well-off, but there was no sign of her working part-time. Despite being a full-time housewife, she cooked some days and not others. Quite often she met her husband at a family restaurant or conveyor-belt sushi place and got dinner out of the way there. Her husband did not reproach her for it, and the two seemed extremely happy together. Even so, she did not go out of her way to flaunt their happiness. Eriko also liked her sense in calling her husband not “dear husband” or “so-and-so-kun,” but “the Demon King.”

“I do want kids, but we never even get as far as making them! Once you’ve started, sex isn’t such a big deal, but the getting-to-it part is a pain, isn’t it? Hey, Demon King, are you reading this?”

She tossed off fairly risky things like that without a fuss.

It had also been exhilarating when she flatly wrote that she never saved points at supermarkets or drugstores, never used coupons, that this was her policy.

“Those things are stealing our precious time from us (angry). You save them up bit by bit and in the end what do you get, maybe five hundred yen? When you think about the hassle of digging through your wallet for a point card and holding it out every single time you go to the register, it feels so much cleaner just to get rid of all of them. If you added up all the time that little chore takes over an entire lifetime, it might come to ten hours or so. We’re not rich or anything, so maybe I’m losing out like crazy, but I’m sick of the bother, so fine~~. The minute I threw them all away, wow, my wallet got insanely light! So light~”

Ever since she was small, Eriko had hated the sight of her mother’s wallet bloated with point cards and coupons. Her father had become president of a Nakamaru Trading subsidiary at a fairly early stage, and even though they lived quite comfortably, it saddened her to see her mother letting shopkeepers lead her around by the nose for the sake of a mere hundred or two hundred yen. At first glance her mother seemed to go at her own pace, but in fact she was easy to push, unable to hold her ground. She was the sort who could not throw away things people gave her and kept them forever. Perhaps that irritated Eriko all the more because it was something she recognized in herself too. Ohyo probably understood instinctively that time came before money. That was why, bound by nothing, she was able to live out her own time so freely.

“What’s interesting about a diary like this? It has nothing to do with your life. It’s kind of domestic.”

For some reason, the fact that Sugishita seemed utterly unmoved stung her a little, and she answered quickly.

“That’s not it. What’s nice about it is how real it feels. I like that it doesn’t seem to be trying too hard. There’s something cute about her. I like the way time seems to pass there too—slowly, with plenty of it.”

“So you enjoy looking down on some bored housewife? That’s what I hate about women. The way they find a woman beneath them and feel safe.”

She was used to this sort of remark from the men in the office, but it still caught at her. When she had first joined the company, she had been shocked by how pervasive the misogyny was, but by now she had given up. Let it slide. Don’t snap back every time. They meant no harm; astonishingly, they simply did not think about what they were saying, and taking it seriously only hurt her. If she made a fuss, she would be the one to end up embarrassed. Backed by her father, a permanent employee, all Eriko had to do was remember to stay more polished than anyone else and work as hard as the men, and she would be treated as an equal. There was no point in getting irritated over petty turns of phrase in everyday conversation. She tried to keep her pleasant expression in place.

“No, that’s not it. Usually, housewife blogs have this side to them where they show off how happy they are, how properly they keep house, how good they are at cooking, right? The urge to have people understand that is really strong. But she…”

The phone on the desk rang, interrupting them. When she picked up, it was a tuna dealer in California. At this hour, calls from America tended to come in all at once.

“May I have your name, please? When shall I have him return your call?”

After telling him that the person in charge was away from his desk, Eriko turned back to Sugishita.

It frustrated her that she could not explain it properly. What she liked was Ohyo’s way of thinking, droll and yet sharp. Her words flowed through Eriko’s chest with a soothing ease. They were gentle in wording, but intelligent, and they never hurt anyone. She lived in sheer idleness, yet felt no shame about it and did not try to force any meaning onto it either. There was no sign of anxiety over not having children. More than anything, her writing was full of turns of phrase that stayed with Eriko and casual little ideas that made her want to imitate them.

On muggy days, for example, she did housework in a swimsuit, then pulled a dress over her head and went off to dive into the municipal pool. At the library she borrowed almost nothing but Ishida Chi. Just to read the complete run of Kubo Kiriko’s “Bucket de Gohan,” she frequented a listless neighborhood coffee shop that was obviously being kept open for tax reasons, deliberately ordering menu items that took extra effort to make. She and her husband went out for karaoke and sang nothing but Western songs. They ordered pizza and watched whatever Hollywood movie happened to be on television. They hopped from convenience store to convenience store hunting for Gari-Gari-kun Rich Corn Potage.

Eriko was drawn to the way she spent whole days with no particular purpose, without asking them to mean anything, without making plans, simply passing the time with a catlike freedom. When was the last time she herself had lived like that? It was so long ago she could no longer remember. Just reading the blog loosened, if only a little, the nerves wound tight by her nightly business dinners and market research.

Whatever the situation, Eriko always tried first not to waste time. Even outside work she devoted herself to beauty, research, and study, and of course she also valued rest and sleep. She was confident that everything she did led to some kind of benefit. She had no doubt that the person she was today was superior to the person she had been yesterday. And yet perhaps since turning thirty, there were moments when the back of her nose would suddenly sting. Sometimes she would be overcome by the feeling that there was not enough oxygen, that she could fling both arms around and touch nothing. She could not help taking deep breaths, telling herself over and over that she was all right, and counting up one by one her strengths, her growth, her contributions to the company. When she still could not settle herself, she would behave with more humility than necessary until she drew praise from someone, and only then would she regain her equilibrium. Even so, no matter how hard she struggled, every day’s end and every new morning brought the tangible sense that a little more of her possibility had been rubbed away, that course correction was becoming harder and harder. Was this simply the anxiety that came with age? In a few more years, would she get used to it? Whenever she caught, in some unguarded instant, a trace of the same anxiety in another woman, she could not stop relief—and a twisted sort of pleasure—from seeping out of her.

She never sensed that kind of anxiety in Ohyo.

More than anything, what drew her was this: almost the only person who ever appeared on the blog was the husband, and female friends never appeared at all. Amid housewife blogs so eager to boast of their networks, it stood out. Eriko secretly hoped that Ohyo might know how to tame the same kind of inward knot that she herself carried.

It was a relief to know that even a woman like Ohyo had no friends. Even someone with a miraculous balance, someone who never seemed to make others uncomfortable, might still miss the timing by the slightest amount and end up unable to make friends. Maybe having no female friends was not such a shameful defect. Maybe it was not a defect at all, just one variation in human relationships.

“I’m a thirty-year-old housewife living with my husband in a shabby apartment in Tokyo. My favorite food is engawa at ‘Nikkori Sushi,’ the neighborhood ninety-eight-yen conveyor-belt place.”

Sugishita, who had seemed uninterested until then, finally leaned in when he read Ohyo’s profile.

“Huh. So her favorite thing is engawa at conveyor-belt sushi. If she knows that the engawa at those dirt-cheap places isn’t flounder at all but ohyo, then this handle’s got some serious irony to it.”

An ohyo could grow to nearly three meters and weigh more than two hundred kilos, so the engawa you got from one was huge. Apparently they thrashed so violently when they were hauled from the water that in Alaska they were dispatched with shotguns. Eriko felt as if she herself had been praised, and had to resist the urge to puff out her chest.

“Come to think of it, Sugishita, you were handling Russian ohyo last year, weren’t you? And didn’t you do business with Center Village Holdings too?”

That major restaurant chain was supposed to own Nikkori Sushi. At Sugishita’s sly grin, Eriko clapped her hands before she realized it.

“Wow… amazing. It all connects. So Ohyo’s favorite food is part of your job.”

Feeling oddly moved, Eriko looked around the office with only the two of them in it. Nothing delighted her more than moments like this, when, following the spread of numbers and commodities, the world suddenly felt small enough to seize in her own hands. At some point the sun had climbed high, and late-August light was slanting into the room. The carpet, its nap whitened and roughened by frequent cleaning, had begun to give off that peculiar smell of something left in the sun.

When you worked at a trading company, everyday scenes could suddenly open up with unexpected depth. This café au lait, for instance. The bread. The newspaper. Where their raw materials had been born and raised, how they had circulated, how they had been processed to come into being here, now. If she narrowed her eyes, it was as though each opened out into its own story. That kind of x-ray vision was something both Eriko and Sugishita possessed.

Japan consumes roughly 6.52 million tons of seafood. Of that, forty percent is imported. Included in that figure are many kinds of fish with no names of their own—in other words, fish used as substitutes or mislabeled as something else. Ever since she had been assigned to the seafood team, Eriko had become able to see through the true identity of processed fish at a glance, no matter what the label said.

“It makes you feel a little guilty toward consumers, doesn’t it? ‘Engawa’ is the name of a cut, so it’s not actually deceit, but still.”

“But if you think about it calmly, there’s no way engawa from hirame or karei is going to cost less than a hundred yen. Consumers kind of know that too.”

“At this point it’s less an edible fish than a bit of an alien. Creepy.”

“And everybody’s happily eating nigiri made from that alien, so I guess everybody wins. The ‘fish’ in fish burgers, the ‘white fish’ in white-fish meunière—if you start digging into exactly what kind of fish they are, there’s no end to it. There are a lot of things in the world you really don’t need to know.”

Sugishita spoke in this coolly dismissive way, but when it came time for an actual business meeting he could suddenly turn on an astonishing amount of charm, his words smooth and polished. He gave off a nervous impression, but in fact he was the son of a kimono shop in Kawagoe that had been in the family for three generations.

“And speaking of which, Shimura, you’re handling Nile perch from this month, right? If she likes conveyor-belt sushi and family restaurants this much, a fish you’ve sold may end up in her mouth someday too.”

Eriko flipped through the file of materials she had inherited from her predecessor. Nile perch was a freshwater fish of the perch order. It could grow to two meters and weigh two hundred kilos. Native to the African continent, it lived in lakes and rivers. Mild and unassertive in taste, it was nonetheless a carnivorous fish of astonishing ferocity. When it was introduced into Lake Victoria in Africa, it drove more than two hundred endemic species of small cichlids to extinction. Its bland, inoffensive taste also made it easy for Japanese consumers to accept, and in the nineties it had even been distributed under names like “sea bass” and “white sea bass,” but these days ninety percent of it was exported to Europe and America and it was seldom seen in Japan. Eriko had spent the past few months trying to reopen markets and improve local facilities. The more she learned about Nile perch, the more helplessly drawn to it she became.

“Nile perch are incredibly hardy.”

“Yikes. They’re carnivores, right? No telling what they eat. Creepy, creepy. Oh, right—apparently you ought to watch a documentary called ‘Darwin’s Nightmare.’ I hear it shows Nile perch tearing an ecosystem apart.”

“I keep thinking I should watch it someday, but I never have time. Besides, until the next round of talks is over, it might be exactly the sort of information I don’t want in my head… If human beings hadn’t released them into the lake, Nile perch would have gone their whole lives without ever realizing how ferocious they were. I kind of feel sorry for them. Oh, and apparently they’re popular as aquarium fish too. They’re silver and very beautiful. They have this lonely sort of face…”

“You can see them at the new aquarium in Shinjuku, right? Want to go together sometime?”

At the muttered invitation, Eriko said neither yes nor no. She simply pretended to be absorbed in the blog again. She was fully aware that Sugishita harbored a faint attraction toward her. He seemed to have imposed on himself some private rule that if he put it into plain words he lost, and his face had taken on a pained look, half desire and half embarrassment. This needling too was probably a form of affection. Perhaps unwilling to admit that she had neatly sidestepped him, he hurriedly steered the conversation back where it had been.

“Well, maybe she’s fine as long as you just read the blog. If you actually met her, there’s no way it’d work out.”

“That’s not true. I feel like Ohyo and I could be good friends.”

“Oh, come on. You don’t even have any women friends.”

She felt her fingertips go cold. No matter how hard she tried, no matter how intently she focused every nerve on that one point, she still gave off the unmistakable impression of a woman with no female friends. The immovable fact of it made her heart pound. In sharp contrast to her shock, Sugishita’s words carried a sticky sexual undertone. When men saw a woman who could not enter the pack of her own sex, it stirred a faint desire in them. There was even a trace of smug pride in it, as though he had discovered, without dirtying his own hands, some major flaw in an opposing tribe. Pretending not to notice, she aimed for a supremely breezy tone.

“Well, that is true. Ever since I started working, I’ve drifted away from my college group. Even if I get invited to girls’ nights, I never have time to go.”

Accepting the premise for the moment and thereby keeping the upper hand was a tactic she had learned in business. There was no such group, and she had never once been invited to a girls’ night. Sometimes the brazenness and childishness of grown women breezily calling themselves “girls” struck Eriko as dazzling.

“You never go to lunch with the temps or with the people from your year in other departments either.”

He never said it outright, but apparently Sugishita was secretly involved with Maori Takasugi, one of the temp staff. That might be why he knew so much about the female employees’ social dynamics.

“Oh, that. My schedule never lines up with the others from my year, and eating with temps takes all kinds of tact. A lot of them want to keep it under a thousand yen, so it’s not so easy to invite them.”

She could hardly say it was because no one ever invited her, so Eriko kept her tone bright.

“It’s true, maybe I’ve always been bad at girl friendships. I mean, not to toot my own horn, but I’m the uptight good-girl type, right? I was always getting saddled with things like class rep or organizer, and before I knew it I’d be on my own. I never really understood those invisible rules girls have, and somehow I seem to end up standing out without realizing it.”

She knew she was talking so fast it surprised even her. Please, she prayed, don’t ask me anything more. Beyond this lay unknown territory. She had no confidence she could get through it without letting something slip.

“Well, a beauty like you, who can do everything without even trying, probably gets envied. Women are jealous and scary.”

Sugishita’s eyes had narrowed with something like fondness. Was he trying to compliment her? He was probably comparing her with Maori Takasugi. Maori Takasugi, who was bad at her job, talked too much, and carried soft extra flesh on her body. He was probably toying with Maori. Even so, Maori was always surrounded by the other women in the office, and Eriko envied that.

Without even trying. Sugishita had probably never once considered how much time and money went into skin without a single blemish and hair that glossy. All at once Eriko found herself wondering what kind of lotion Ohyo used. Some cheap thing dumped in drugstore bargain bins? Or maybe, unexpectedly, she was devoted to organic cosmetics. Eriko wanted to try whatever she used. As she dug through old entries, she found herself speaking aloud.

“Come to think of it, this Ohyo person seems to live near me.”

“Huh? Seriously?”

“Look, this organic café she mentions here, ‘Giselle.’ It’s one stop away, and I know it well because my mother was involved in getting it started. And actually, all the fast-food places and chain stores Ohyo likes are right around my nearest station. I mean, they’re all the kind of places you can find anywhere, but still.”

“I don’t love how much you know about that. I mean, from her point of view, you’d kind of be a stalker.”

The word jolted her; it had never once occurred to her. Eriko turned and stared at Sugishita.

“What? What’s that supposed to mean? I’m just reading her blog. I only happened to realize that maybe she lives nearby, that’s all.”

“But you copy this Ohyo person’s eating habits, you keep track of her daily life, and now you might live near her too. Even if you don’t mean anything by it, from her side that’s kind of creepy. Don’t get too carried away.”

He cut the conversation there, turned on his heel, and headed back to his desk. Employees were beginning to appear in ones and twos. Maybe he came in this early because he wanted to be alone with her, out of Maori Takasugi’s sight. Was that vanity on her part? A year had passed since she had broken up with her last boyfriend, but she still did not feel lonely. Someday she wanted to marry, but for now the back-and-forth between home and work was all she could handle.

Had Ohyo ever imagined an office worker like her, in an early-morning office, biting into the same bread while reading her blog? Somehow Eriko had a feeling that Ohyo must sense, somewhere, the existence of a person like her. On the surface her writing affected an offhand air, but perhaps it contained messages to many different people. You’re fine as you are. Relax. What’s wrong with being alone?

As though cherishing another self reflected in a mirror, Eriko carefully reread Ohyo’s diary.

Shoko Maruo stopped the hand with which she had been updating her blog on her cell phone and looked up.

That laundry looks like it’s about to fall, she thought, gazing at the denim flapping from the balcony of the apartment building opposite. Must be someone young. There was no way thirty-year-old Shoko could pull off skinnies like that. She used to stand on the floor of a department store in narrow pants so tight they seemed to cut off her circulation. Standing for more than ten hours a day had been nothing unusual. There were even times when she only got two days off in a month. What would the Shoko of those days think if she saw the life she led now? She might be envious, actually.

In any case, she might have arrived a little too early. The organic café “Giselle,” one of her favorite places, was a fifteen-minute walk from her apartment building. Maybe because it was Saturday, she noticed several women dining alone much like herself. She popped into her mouth the remains on the large plate—konjac and chicken liver dressed with mustard mayonnaise, coleslaw with goya, a brown-rice rice ball with sesame and yukari, and an omelet with aosa—and polished it all off at a speed that made even her shake her head. She had not eaten a meal this well balanced, with this many vegetables, in ages, and she certainly had not made one for Kensuke. Once again she felt herself to be a failure as a housewife. Tonight, at the very least, she ought to simmer some hijiki. Then again… even imagining it felt like too much trouble, so maybe she should just have them wrap something up here. She did not want to wash dishes. More than anything, Shoko hated work that got her hands wet.

She had met Kensuke, the manager of a supermarket specializing in imported foodstuffs, through a part-time job, and married him three years earlier. As a rule she tried to be frugal so they could live on Kensuke’s salary alone, but she hated the idea of letting thrift make her small-minded. A little pointless spending was no big deal if she dipped into the savings she had from her single days. Lately she had also been earning a tiny income from affiliate ads on her blog. No ceremony, no rings, no trips abroad. And of course no house. Instead of indulging in any large luxuries, they did not penny-pinch over little things. Better to eat out somewhere cheap than have a fight over dishwashing. It was a promise they had made when they registered their marriage.

Ordinarily, Doutor or a family restaurant would have done perfectly well today too, but since the person she was meeting was one of those media people, she had named the most stylish café she knew. The interior, done in warm wood tones, always calmed her. Hashimoto-kun, the part-timer who had come to clear her plate, suddenly bent down.

“Shoko-san, thanks for featuring the new fall menu on your blog.”

“Whoa, too close, too close. You scared me.”

His breath tickled her ear. Hashimoto-kun was twenty-five, the same age as her younger brother, which made him easy to talk to. When she gave him a light smack, she felt beneath his shirt a tightly toned bicep that did not fit his lanky appearance. The firmness that seemed almost to bounce her hand away was nothing like her husband’s soft, squishy upper arms, which had started thickening again lately.

“On days you feature us on your blog, we get just a few more customers than usual.”

For Shoko, who preferred family restaurants and fast food, “Giselle” was one of the few independent places she frequented. Earlier that year she had taken a short-term New Year’s job there, and since then she had grown familiar with the staff; even after quitting, she still came once or twice a month as a customer. She had turned down their invitation to try working there as a contract employee. If it became her workplace, human relations would inevitably grow troublesome, and she would plainly lose the ability to drop in casually like this.

Shoko lowered her gaze to her cell phone again. Simply following the comments on her blog was enough to make time vanish. There were almost no comments condemning her sloppiness. If anything, most were encouraging: “This is such a relief,” “This makes me feel it’s okay to be like this.” Many people posted links to products and shops they recommended as well. She was happy, as if she had finally found somewhere she belonged, but when people said nothing but kind things to her, she also felt vaguely as though she might be deceiving her readers. Even so, the pleasantness of favorable opinions was addictive, and she found herself rereading comments for hours. It might have felt something like soaking in the low-temperature carbonated bath at a super sento. When the stress was this low, it was hard to know when to get out.

Not only online, she wanted to secure as many places where she could feel at ease, and as many people with whom she could exchange smiles, as possible. Eight years after coming to Tokyo, she still had not managed to get used to the city. When she was working, she had been too busy to have time for fun, and after her health broke down she had become a complete stay-at-home. The moment the doorbell rang, a woman in a suit, perhaps in her forties, appeared. The woman noticed her at once and strode straight over.

“Ohyo-san… are you Shoko Maruo? Nice to meet you. I’m Satoko Hanai, from the Third Books Editorial Department at Shumeisha.”

Shoko hurriedly rose, bowed, and took the business card. Satoko Hanai had regular features and a reasonably good figure, but her skin was dry and the circles under her eyes stood out, so the overall impression she gave was irredeemably worn.

“Well, this is a surprise. Ohyo-san, you’re much cuter and more charming than I imagined.”

The fact that she stopped short of simply saying beautiful—that was the caution of the elite. So, I’m being appraised, thought Shoko. She could feel a sensor that had slept for a long time click back on. She was wearing a washed-soft T-shirt, chinos Kensuke had worn when he was thinner, and no makeup to speak of. She knew perfectly well it was not how one was supposed to look on first meeting someone. Since quitting the apparel company, the interest she had once had in clothes—the clothes she had formerly bought with such feverish zeal—had faded completely. She had never been striking-looking to begin with. Her body was flat, her eyes single-lidded, and her pale skin was marked by freckles. She also had a complex about the way her lips curled back so that when she smiled her gums showed.

And yet there was a certain kind of woman who, for some reason, seemed almost by prior agreement to loathe Shoko.

She’s trying to ingratiate herself with men.

That was their claim. But she was not. It was only that the ordinary consideration and attentiveness women probably extended to one another as a matter of course, she could offer men without effort. In front of women there were many moments when she did not know what to say, but with the opposite sex she could tell by instinct how to behave. Perhaps that was because her parents had divorced and she had grown up with her father, surrounded by brothers. She was not particularly calculating, nor had she had that much romantic experience. It offended her to be judged that way simply because she could relax around men. Out of that wariness, she had developed the habit of acting rougher than necessary in front of women.

After ordering a coffee, Satoko laid several of Shumeisha’s housewives’ magazines and books based on housewife blogs on the table and began explaining one thing after another. To be honest, Shoko was not especially enthusiastic. She had always been bad at having expectations placed on her. Just reading the muggy, overheated prose of housewife bloggers flaunting their happiness was exhausting. By temperament she wanted to avoid troublesome things as much as possible. Regardless of Shoko’s mood, Satoko kept talking rapidly.

“At the moment, housewife bloggers are quite finely segmented, but your selling point, Ohyo-san, is your ‘naturalness.’ There’s none of that ‘be like this’ or ‘this is how one ought to be,’ and that’s what makes you so good. At a time when all kinds of values are being recognized, many housewives are wavering. And not just housewives either—I’m sure many people sympathize with your unshakable, go-at-your-own-pace style. You have a personality that isn’t pushy, and I think even single working women wouldn’t dislike you.”

“I see… But if a diary like that became a book, who would read it…?”

“Exactly. So what if we adapted the diary and shaped it into essays? We’d like you to write a kind of guide to living, as a relatable woman who gets through life without trying too hard.”
