A goddess was there. In the octagonal room, deftly curving her voluptuous body.

“Um, hello.”

“So you’re the one. Welcome.”

Her voice was huskier than I’d expected. Somewhere between mezzo-soprano and alto, more toward alto if anything. If we were ever going to sing in a choir, that is.

When I moved to hand over my letter of introduction, the curator swiftly took it from me and spread it open before the goddess. I stiffened at once. How exactly had I meant to hand it to her? She, however, seemed not to mind, and after a moment she spoke.

“Horauchi Rika, yes? Would it be all right if I called you Hora?”

I nodded. The sudden decree of what I was to be called threw me, but it wasn’t as though I had any name I preferred. The goddess narrowed her eyes. Her pupil-less eyes.

“I’d like you to call me Venus, not Aphrodite. I may look as though I belong to Greek mythology, but I was born Roman. Though of course that, too, is only the English pronunciation.”

I nodded again. Venus narrowed her eyes still further, her carved smile almost overflowing.

“Good. Tell me, am I speaking too fast? Is this in the form you learned at university? If it seems different, I want you to tell me frankly.”

“No… you’re very easy to understand.”

“Like something from a textbook,” I added after a moment’s hesitation, rubbing my palms against my raincoat. I was sweating badly.

“I’m glad. Though I may be something like a model, after all. I’m from before Latin split into Italian and French and the rest, from an age when no one imagined the Roman Empire would ever break apart.”

Venus laughed aloud. Her bare white shoulders and smooth belly, cushioned by the slightest layer of flesh, did not move in the least. Apparently with marble bodies, the rule was that nothing below the neck moved.

As part-time jobs went, it was by no means a bad deal. It was just not the sort of work you heard about very often.

The one who had put me forward was a professor whose lecture I had taken once, and only once. For the first time in years I got off at that station, passed the rotary in an uproar like a brushfire, went through the school gates, and climbed the hill past girls in matching pajama-like outfits. When I entered his office in the north building, the first thing I noticed was an old carpet the color of young grass. I stood there staring at its pile as it blankly swallowed the cheers drifting in from the courtyard—freshmen, perhaps—when a letter of introduction was held out to me. An envelope with the university’s name printed on it. I looked up and saw someone I recognized.

And yet, though I took it in both hands, they stayed suspended in midair.

“I’m sorry, but I’m not confident I can do it.”

“In terms of conversation, there is no one better suited than you. At least not among people now living.”

The professor sounded as if he were speaking to someone behind me.

“Think of it as something like private tutoring. Easier than that, really. All you have to do is talk.”

“But the other side is practically a native speaker, and besides—”

And besides, that talking part—yet the rest of the sentence dropped soundlessly onto the yellow vinyl before me, like spray from a park fountain at closing time. While I kept drawing breath and pursing my lips, the professor, as if invisible water had gotten into his ear, gave a few small hops like someone trying to shake it out, then turned to the mirror and began straightening his jacket. Apparently the conversation was over. With nothing else for it, I left the university. The girls in pajamas were eating matching sandwiches on a bench by the gate.

The museum was some distance away by bus. From the stop, all that caught the eye were white magnolia petals; houses and shops alike were buried in their bone-white profusion. Checking again and again the map the professor had given me, I finally found the building. Its nearly windowless concrete walls and bronze doors looked unmistakably worn with age. Rather than a place where cultural properties and historical materials from every age and land had been gathered, it resembled a student dormitory after everyone had moved out. I passed the CLOSED TODAY sign, made my way to the back entrance, took out my phone, and called the number I’d been given. The door opened on the second ring, and I went in when beckoned. Like arriving at a members-only party.

And yet this was not a place that seemed to welcome visitors. Least of all living ones. Perhaps for the sake of preserving the exhibits, the interior was as cold as a morgue. The smell of chemicals had seeped into the floors, the walls, everywhere, and the chandelier enthroned high above the atrium and the pretty milk-glass lights running along the hall had quietly lowered their eyes into darkness today. As I passed displays of fossils and earthenware in glass cases, I noticed a rack by the front entrance lined with notices for children’s events and neighborhood flea markets, but were they ever really held? Could the dates and times printed on these drowsy-looking flyers truly come around somewhere in the world one day, and children gather for science experiments, or old sweaters and stereo equipment be sold along the approach to a shrine?

Within that view, faded like an old calendar, the only thing moving was the curator walking ahead of me.

“Mind your step.”

He turned as if he had foreseen me catching my hem on the stairs. The curator, whose name was Hashibami, was disconcertingly handsome. I couldn’t tell his age, but in his white shirt and slim trousers he had the air of a young man, and a beauty closer to that of a vase or a lacquered box than of a human being. I clutched the letter of introduction in my pocket. I was bad with handsome men. Especially handsome men who were kind—or seemed kind.

If there were ever a scholar determined to prove that light had gravity, my left hand would gladly help provide the evidence. The moment the door at the top of the stairs opened, I found myself drawn helplessly toward an arched window. You could never have guessed it from the outside, but the center of the building had been hollowed out, opening onto trees and a small pond, and the corridor that ran around this modest courtyard, looking down on it and enclosing it, was filled with a lucid brightness. Pointing to the bouquet reliefs carved into the columns, Hashibami explained.

“This building was donated to the museum. It used to be the residence of a wealthy local man. The doors are closed today, but his collection is displayed on the second floor.”

From one of the galleries somewhere, orchestral strains spilled out: the oboe’s smooth upward leaps, the roll of timpani like a mallet digging up old memories. Passing a door carved with shell motifs, I caught the tepid scent of the tide and heard the sound of waves. There was even a room where an ivory lace hem showed from the gap beneath the door. The museum was reveling in a face of itself I had never known, luxuriating in its day off.

That room was at the very end. Hashibami chose one key from a heavy ring and slipped it into the lock; with a click, the door opened. Ignoring the yellow raincoat dimly reflected in the corridor glass, I strained my eyes past the threshold.

“This way, please.”

So this, apparently, was where I’d be working. One shift a week, no experience necessary, conversational Latin required. Duties: serve as conversation partner to an ancient Roman statue of Venus.

I looked around the gallery again, without letting Venus notice, sliding my pupils a little at a time toward the corners of my eyes.

It was a room out of myth. Light streamed ceaselessly through a domed skylight, and they stood on their pedestals like stars forming constellations: Hera glaring in my direction, Artemis with her hound, Minerva in armor. Eight goddesses from Greek and Roman myth. In ancient Greek, perhaps, or perhaps in some other tongue, those bloodless white lips idly wove lace patterns. Beyond the shadows cast where that loquacious lace overlapped, at the farthest point of the octagon, she stood.

“Oh, forgive me,” Venus said. In contrast to her frozen shoulders, her jaw moved with remarkable smoothness.

“Hashibami, bring Hora a chair. She mustn’t remain standing.”

The curator brought over what looked like an attendant’s chair from the corner of the gallery. As he turned to go back to the goddess’s side, that alto voice said at once, “Thank you. You may leave us now.”

“But—” There was something almost urgent in his Latin.

“It’s all right. If anything happens, I’ll call,” the goddess went on. “Close the door. Be sure to.”

Hashibami shot me a quick glance, then headed for the exit. With a bow, he closed the door behind him. When the hard sound of his footsteps had faded, she said, “Sit.” I lowered myself into the chair, lifted my face, and immediately lowered it again. I was at exactly the height where the goddess’s slightly downcast gaze met mine.

She was every inch the goddess of love and beauty. On her pedestal, her full body curved in a magnificent S, and she smiled with a face that was beautiful yet carefully stripped of all the things that make a human face individual. Her perfectly symmetrical features, untouched by the creases beside the mouth or the fine lines at the corners of the eyes, had a spoonful of resignation already carved into their happiness, and that made her seem only more graceful, more mysterious. Her garments, with folds flowing splendidly and never once swaying, seemed to have no intention whatever, as garments, of covering or protecting the body; they concealed neither her full breasts nor her rounded hips, and it unsettled me—my own body with all its lack of contour and all. When I shifted in my seat, the goddess asked,

“Does the chair not suit you?”

“It’s fine.”

The answer came out of me like a reflex, and I tried to put on as pleasant a smile as I could. Apparently it was not enough. Venus asked again, with the persistence of someone checking the pockets of a child caught stealing.

“Hora, is that chair comfortable?”

“Yes. It’s fine.”

“Tell me, did you go to some school where they teach you to end every conversation in as few words as possible? Tell me this: is that chair really comfortable—for you?”

For the first time, I became aware of the depth of the seat. Then of where my spine was, and how far my heels hovered off the floor. As though tracing an X-ray with a fingertip.

“The seat may be a little high,” I said, then, worried the answer was still too short, added, “My legs are short.”

“Thank you for telling me something about yourself. I wouldn’t know, you see. I’ve never sat in a chair. Now ring Hashibami with that bell over there.”

As instructed, I went over toward the curtain, found a small golden bell, and shook it timidly. Her lips, which had never known dryness or cracking, rippled.

“I still don’t know how long your legs are, but for now there’s no need to speak about yourself that way.”

She smiled. My body went rigid. Like a sculpture.

When footsteps approached and the door opened, Venus told the curator to bring a different chair. Thanking Hashibami for coming so quickly and specifying exactly what size chair she wanted, Venus seemed like an excellent boss—if she ever worked anywhere outside a museum.

One beat after the sound of the door closing, Venus spoke again.

“Thank you for coming today. Your Latin really is excellent. When Hashibami asked your professor, yours was the very first name they gave. They said you were exceptionally capable.”

“That’s not true,” I said, letting out a thin breath as if to get away from those white eyes.

“It is, or I wouldn’t say it,” the goddess replied without the slightest hesitation. “I heard you’ve already graduated from university. Are you in graduate school now?”

“Graduate school? No, nothing like that. At the moment I’m just, well, doing odd little jobs…”

I tried to catch hold of words. The words that should have linked one thing to another. But the more desperately I chased them, the more lightly they vanished—or else fell pitifully away—and I lapsed into silence inside my raincoat, half hoping I’d get fired from this part-time job. But Venus only murmured, “‘Tasks’ is nice,” and began to explain the work.

The duties and conditions were no different from what the professor had told me. My job was to talk with her. Shifts were on Mondays, when the museum was closed. If Monday fell on a holiday and the museum was open, then the shift would be the following day. Travel expenses reimbursed. Three-month probationary period.

“If you need to take a day off, it’s no problem as long as you let me know two days in advance. Of course, if it’s your health or the weather, the same day is fine too.”

I nodded. The tutoring job I’d done for half a year as a student was for a family obsessive about punctuality, and on the day snow stopped the trains I rushed to the house of the elementary-school boy I taught by changing buses, my toes numb with the cold of my soaked socks. Yet when I rang the bell at the appointed time, his mother announced on the spot that, since we had snow after all, she wanted to let him go out and play, and would reschedule the lesson. For a while I stood there watching the boy and his pet golden retriever, splendid in matching Montbell down jackets, tear around a silvery garden big enough for two tennis courts. Compared with that, this job seemed to offer far better conditions. At least the goddess did not look likely to change plans according to the weather. Marble legs do not suddenly step down from pedestals.

And yet I kept looking for a reason to walk away from this part-time job. I felt out of place. The goddess was beautiful, and far too exposed; I, on the other hand, was wearing too much. As always.

I wiped the sweat beading on my forehead. At some point the yellow hood had slipped down to my eyes.

Suddenly Venus gave a small yawn. “Tell me, Hora,” she said, her alto voice muffled.

“Are you a deep sleeper?”

“I think so. About average,” I answered, looking down, trying not to watch that perfectly proportioned mouth distort.

“How nice. I always sleep lightly. If I had a sound digestive system, the first thing I’d want to take would be sleeping pills. Strong enough that I’d never have to wake again.”

Only then did I read the label beside her. “Aphrodite of Paliano. Roman copy after a Greek original. 1st–2nd century.” Two thousand years had raced by without a sound, becoming bottomless dust and scraps of human warmth, while innumerable days and nights whirled past in a blur. It adorned the naked goddess more gracefully than any ornament, and at the same time wrapped her in an unapproachable solitude.

Beyond the gallery door, footsteps were drawing near. “Listen,” Venus whispered, lowering her voice as if shielding a candle flame from something vast.

“No painting or sculpture can equal the beauty of a single night’s dream. There, you can become someone else, go somewhere else. I can escape my own endlessly continuing time.”

“Will you come too?” The half-joking words brushed suddenly over my bare skin. Beneath my raincoat. With nowhere for their meaning to go.

Before I knew it, I had signed and stamped the employment contract Hashibami had brought with the chair. Part-time wages, by bank transfer, paid on the tenth of the following month.

Oh—someone crossed.

I stopped short. From the apartment’s rust-eaten stairs, I could see the bridge. A bridge like the fine bones of a sardine, one almost no one ever used. As I stood looking at it, a frosted-glass window behind me slid open a crack. Through the gap in my hood I drew in the air: the smell of warm cooking, and the savory omen hidden inside it.

The moment I found this apartment, I put in an application. It was old, for one thing, and the two-story building stood slightly out of place in a district lined with small factories; in terms of sunlight, it was about as bad as it could be. But the rent was cheap, and the ivy covering the white walls had, from a certain point of view, a kind of charm. Separate bath and toilet, a two-burner stove, a washlet, and, for some reason, even a landline phone in the room.

The one thing I hadn’t expected was that it came with a landlady. With the building—or rather, with everyday life.

I ran down the stairs and opened the door. It wasn’t locked. Stepping over the cardboard boxes piled in the entryway and pushing aside the bead curtain, I saw Seriko-san peering into a pot amid the year-round lukewarm smell, like onions simmering—beside an oven mitt that, under the incandescent bulb, had browned to a toasty color and was on the verge of turning into a small flame.
