Cool rain on the blue lawn. Wet summer grass, wafts of black earth. Always those August showers on the close-cropped stalks, burnt gold. Heavy drops run down the pane, curve, snake, and intertwine in long ribbons of liquid light. How many afternoons spent behind the curtain’s vaporous veil, tracing with a fingertip their path, at once nervous and languid. Little stray hairs curl around your cheeks, and you marvel that they can be so white when you are so young, haloed in ether beneath the window. And then suddenly your gaze drops from the window to the hand drawing the curtain aside, and the hand is old, so old.

There are places that harpoon you. That wind their mesh around your dreams, fit their claws just loosely enough to let you grow, yet leave in your flesh the bruise of their grip.

There are doors whose sound, when you push them open, is like a cry out of time that still breaks through oblivion.

There are staircases whose steps you would so love to climb again, just once, letting the banister’s cold smoothness run through your palm.

That is the House.

There are, on the other hand, places that slide the clammy discomfort of being a stranger down the back of your neck. Places you do not feel resonating within you. Places imposed on you, places you are forced to endure—it’s only for now, it’s temporary, there is no choice. When the world grows tired of you, they offer you a TV by the bed, a bedside table for your glasses case, yellow wallpaper. Yellow is cheerful, after all, and everything you need is here, we’ll take care of you. They make us trade the House for a room in some medically accredited place to die. That yellow wallpaper—a hammer blow to the nape of my old neck.

In any case, now I love only the armchair. It is the only piece of furniture I was able to bring with me. It has always been an old person’s armchair: my great-grandfather’s, whom I never knew, then Great-Aunt Babel’s, when she came to visit us. I understand why you come to love it as you grow old; you recognize yourself in its dust, its softness. With the tip of my nail I tease at the scratches in its old-rose velvet.

The comfort of bracing your arms in the intimate hollow of the armrests. The fabric sliding close against you like a second skin. It is like finding again the embrace of a too-sweet lover, one who proved far more constant than any man I might ever have known.

Come to think of it, I was the only one who truly loved the House. Even my great-great-grandfather, who desired it, imagined it, built it, did not love it as much as I did. I loved it enough to stay there all my life. To give up my studies in the City, because I suffered too much away from the woods of my childhood. To say no to Oktav when he held out the little ring to me on the Pont-Noir one Wednesday afternoon, because he would never have wanted us to live in the House, not him. He wanted us to move into a large apartment in the old town, not far from the lycée where he would have taught and where our future children would have learned to surpass us. I loved the House enough to want nothing else, in my whole life, but to remain there, nestled in the hollow of familiar things, letting time lay its patina on me exactly as it had on the banister of the spiral staircase.

I always close my eyes when I want to remember it. I do that every day, every waking minute, perhaps. I strain, impatiently, violently even, to recreate every room, every corner. I cling to details: the shape of the light switches, the sound of doorknobs turning, the fine layer of dust on the yellow bulbs. I want to see everything again, feel everything again. I want to hollow out the space of the present and make rise up again, by force of frantic memory, the places I loved so much, knew by heart, walked through all my life, and which, now that I am no longer there, are fading, coming apart.

The House as I left it, four months ago, perhaps five—I remember that. A little too well, even. The slow decay of the emptied rooms, the dulled parquet floors, the cold corridors where the paint peels, that, yes. The broken pane in the stained glass through which dead leaves come rushing, whirling feebly across the hall floor like an exhausted couple waltzing at the end of the night, I still see that, and very clearly. And the cast-iron radiators too, faded white, icy in rooms no one lives in anymore, the fireplaces bricked up, the sheets over the furniture in bedrooms where no one sleeps anymore. That was how it was at the end.

I want to banish that image of the House. Bring the colors back to life, throw wide the bright windows and let in the intoxicating morning air, wax the staircase, clean the immense dining-room table. Lay the table for fifteen people or so, as in the great days, when everyone came, without exception.

I want to see again the tall House of white boards that, when you came back from the garden, rose up tucked among the great blue spruces and the maples, with grass bursting in emerald sprays against the flight of porch steps. To see again the façade with its narrow windows, the veranda jutting out on the left, the roof high and pointed, bristling with chimney stacks and gables of immaculate white, with the bull’s-eye attic window perched like the round golden eye of some reassuring giant, all rimmed in carved wood. I want to see again the strange pointed roofs that capped the bay windows and in winter were fringed with icicles. I want to see again the windows sparkling in the cool air, stroked by the branches of trees standing too close to the House whenever a light breeze blew. I want to see again the great House, with the carved wooden columns framing the front door, lacquered with a warm varnish the color of hard caramel, and the stained-glass panel of intertwined flowers that, when the sun shone through it, let flashes of color into the hall. Painted with immense trellises of tropical foliage, the hall shone a soft blue. There the spiral staircase rose in a swirl of coppery wood.

Everything stood open; the smells from the kitchen moved freely and flooded the whole House. Voices rumbled from the drawing room to the attic like the reassuring murmur of a torrent rolling through the walls. Somewhere deep in my ear, I still hear them. The floorboards creak and give off wafts of pine, and even our lightest children’s steps make the thick planks groan. We run up the stairs, because it is more fun to arrive dizzy. The corridor on the first floor opens onto my uncle and aunt’s old bedrooms, the ones from when they were little, and which they still use whenever they come back to see us with the cousins. They sleep right across the hall from Harriett’s room and mine. It’s easier for playing: they only have to cross the corridor and whisper the password at the door so we’ll know they aren’t bothersome grown-ups coming to scatter everything with their big awkward legs. At the very end of the corridor there is the bathroom, the green one, with its window that sticks a little, though through it you can see a bit of the orchard. Opposite is Great-Aunt Babel’s room, for when she comes to set down her trunks sweating camphor. When everybody leaves again, we have almost the whole floor to ourselves. On the second floor you can hardly play at all; that is the parents’ floor, the serious floor, the one with the two most spacious bathrooms. Louisa, lucky thing, even has one of her own, with a little ceramic bathtub behind her wardrobe. Because she is the eldest of the girls, she has the biggest bedroom, and she has it to herself. I would not trade my room for hers for anything in the world, though, because if I did Harriett and I would not be able to make nearly as much noise, with the parents’ room so close by. That is also where the box room is, which we don’t even use anymore for hide-and-seek: everyone knows perfectly well it’s the first place anyone thinks of when they need to slip away from the world. The best place to hide, of course, is the attic. It is not as big as it was when Petit Père was a child because since then they have split it in two to make Klaus’s room. I like Klaus’s room. It smells like boy, because he doesn’t open the window much. As the adults never go up there, we are left in peace. On stormy evenings you hear every creak of the roof, and there is something reassuring about that garret, warm as a wooden cocoon suspended above the forest.

But the images go by in faded colors, drained of life. The faces are blurred, and that is the most painful thing, realizing that the image of my family, young and alive, is lost forever. I have lost myself as well. Who was I, at eight years old? Now that old age is breaking my back and wrecking my fingers, I can feel how annoyed I would have been, as a child, by the person I am today, encumbered by what no longer exists. I would hate the puppet theater I set up in my mind to replay those dead images over and over. Who were we, in the woods and in the bedroom, in the kitchen where the soup steams? I manipulate little puppets whose faces are fading. No matter: there comes a point when some people are so familiar to us that we no longer even need their physical presence for them to be there. The only people we can truly say we know are those we grew up with. What each of us became took on a slight tint from the others, like water into which a single drop of syrup falls, one drop, enough to tint the whole glass pale mint. I know exactly what Petit Père or Louisa would have answered to this or that remark, I can still see Petite Mère’s smile when she watched us dance on spring evenings. And so, for a while, it seems to me that I live again, in this body of today, this difficult, creaking body, the days that ended and the days that followed them, in the hollow of the House.

I want to remember the glorious voices of children tangling together, Petit Père’s cool, reticent tenderness, Petite Mère’s bursts of inspiration when she would suddenly rise from the table, seize the brushes from the jar on the sideboard, and rush to her studio in a corner of the veranda. I want to remember when Klaus, Louisa, and Harriett were still at the House, Klaus already on the brink of adolescence. He was handsome, that prodigy of an older brother, brilliant, funny, insolently gifted. I would like to grumble again when he ran through his scales at six in the morning and the sweet, rasping lament of his trumpet woke the whole family. I want to recover Louisa, the prettiest of girls, as though she had absorbed all our mother’s radiance, leaving none for Harriett or me. I remember that when I passed, dragging my feet over the floorboards of the second floor, by Louisa’s half-open door, she was never on her bed or in her armchair, but always before the mirror in her washroom, losing herself in her own eyes, watching for flecks of gold deep in the iris.

What I would give to be back in the bedroom with the two little beds side by side, Harriett’s and mine, with the lamp between them, cause of so many quarrels between us, I who always wanted to read later than she did! That sulky baby-doll face of hers hidden under the covers as she howled that I was preventing her from getting her eight hours of sleep…

“Then you can just get up later,” I would crow, turning the pages of my novel slowly to annoy her.

“You know Klaus is going to wake us up again with his blasted trumpet, so turn it out!” she would protest, indignant, tears at the edge of her eyes, thumping the pillow.

“I’ll turn it out when I’ve finished my chapter.”

“Let me see where you are.”

I would show her a fake page farther on.

“You see, almost finished,” I would assure her.

She would start hissing like a snake, I was lying, I was the worst sister in the world, if she told Louisa… She would let the threat hang and shoot me a furious look from under the blankets. Harriett was forever appealing to our eldest sister to settle our disputes, because Louisa’s partiality and unfairness were absolute. She always sided with our youngest, who had adorable dark curls Louisa loved to do, while she said my mop was “perplexing.” But as Harriett was far from docile, more often than not Louisa would leave the room slamming the door and shut herself away in her own, dreaming of great chandeliers and dinners with friends.

Even the House of adolescence, I want to see again. The one that haunted me every evening in my student room and made me hate the College, curse my studies. They had torn me away from my hearth, from my life, from my walks in the woods and my afternoons spent reading, curled up on the too-hard windowsill. But what a triumph Friday evenings were! When I rushed for the train, sprang into my seat, wriggled with impatience, lips stretched in an irrepressible smile, and the inexorable machine carried me far from the City’s towers! I loved our arrival at the station then, though it felt endless, and the moment when I caught sight of Petit Père by the open trunk waiting for my suitcase, with Harriett already in the car, because on Fridays she finished earlier.

“Mrs. Aberfletch? Can you hear me? I’m setting your tray on the desk, you can eat whenever you like.”

And there is that kindly nurse, come to dissipate it all in her rose perfume. To hell with her white coat and her insolent youth, her clear voice, the voice of a living girl who goes home at night. She must spend the whole year waiting for summer, that season of the liquid body, of blond evenings.

Summer

I loved nothing so much as summers at the House. Everything glowed then, in the damp languor of the holidays, which seemed endless, stretched out by the long days of delicious boredom. From the first heat of June onward, everything began to shimmer, to overflow with life. The maples and the firs seemed swollen with incandescent sap; the grass, an insolent green, was crossed by broad swathes of sunlight.

Everyone came back from the City, flowed back toward the familiar countryside and the forests wreathed in lustral shade. All you had to do was stand at the edge of the woods to feel the resin-scented wind breathe out its murmur. When we were children, my father’s brother and sister, Uncle Bertie and Aunt Hilde, came every summer with their families. What joy when someone announced, “The cousins are coming tonight!” The day would then fill with an impatient excitement that set us hopping in circles around the drawing room and, scolded by Petit Père, we would go back upstairs smothering nervous laughter. The afternoon was spent in busy preparations; I adored helping my parents, with painstaking attention. Like a little despot, I made sure the attic was aired, the toy chests opened, the sheets folded neatly on the beds. I tore down the spiral staircase, polished for the occasion, I whirled through the hall crossed by the shattered light of the stained glass. I came into the kitchen twenty times, bent over the stove, inhaled the cooking smells as I lifted, with a slightly trembling hand, the lids of the tureens. I knew that the nearly ready food was the signal: the cousins would soon be there, and before long all of us would be gathered around the table in an unbearable uproar of loud laughter, releasing the frenzy of a long day spent waiting.

The evening the cousins arrived, at the beginning of every summer, was always the same. They were generally exhausted by the drive they had endured all day, by the cramped cars, by the hot breath of the tarmac, by the sun reflected off the gleaming bodywork. Even so, we sat down eagerly. When everything seemed to whirl with light and laughter and scent, when our stories were constantly being interrupted by a dish going past, or by another story springing up at the other end of the table, and when I leaned back in my chair to steal a furtive look at those we had not seen since Christmas, I felt immersed in a bath of liquid joy, golden and sparkling, perfumed by the honey smell of Uncle Bertie’s cigars or by the thyme melting into the chicken juices. My eyes made their way around the table and slid mechanically over the familiar faces until they caught on those of my girl cousins, prettier every year. It was their noses above all that fascinated me, small and round, and inherited from Bertie’s in-laws. He himself had not escaped the family nose, long and hooked, with light sliding over it as over a blade. Amelia and Magda had taken everything from their mother, a kind of blond, full-bodied grace, with skin all pink at the throat, skin like fresh flowers beaded with water in crystal vases. Always seated side by side, carefully passing the jug along when it came before them, they laughed out loud at all Klaus’s or Harriett’s antics. Every summer, as soon as they stepped out of the car and set foot on the porch steps, the two cousins and Louisa sized one another up. In those first moments of reunion, you could not tell whether Louisa and they were going to scratch each other’s eyes out or trade some barbed pretty-girl remark; but no, they would fall into one another’s arms and go into the House laughing, trailing in their wake a scent of precious oils. Harriett and I spent little time with the cousins, who shared Louisa’s temperament and were the same age as she was. We were the little nuisances, noisy, a little alarming, I think. Klaus showed no particular interest in the cousins, and at table sat beside Aleksander, Aunt Hilde’s cherished son. Aleksander combined the traditional Aberfletch nose with a gaze of steel that sharply contrasted with the muddy-pond depth of our own eyes. His hair was a little ashier and paler than ours, and his very upright head caught the reflections of the dining-room lamp, which cast its warm halo over the whole table. Aleksander and Klaus did not get on especially well, but, being the oldest of the children, they had learned to remain together, in a kind of wary brotherhood halfway between competition and solidarity. They tried to outdo each other in skill whenever they poured themselves water, each seeing who could raise the carafe highest without the thin stream veering off its course. We clapped our hands, staring at the troubled water quickly filling the glass, and screamed when it came close to overflowing.

The adults were on edge; they sent us to bed early, telling us not to “carry on till all hours like last year.” The cousins and we exchanged delighted looks, our complicity suddenly rekindled by that reminder of a shared past. For a brief instant, memories drowned in summer light passed through our eyes, trembling with laughter and skinned knees. We danced the cousins up to their room, thrilled to have them back with us, and Klaus made his fine voice, just beginning to break, rumble and played the grown-up, saying, “I’m going up to my room, be good, children.” Aleksander grumbled a little, because he was the same age as Klaus, but had to sleep with the two girl cousins, whom he cheerfully ignored most of the time. Then Louisa and Klaus disappeared again into the half-dark of the staircase, and Harriett, the cousins, and I remained in the dark corridor, the neutral ground between our two bedrooms. Through the half-open door, we were pleased to see that their suitcases had somehow, as if by magic, been carried upstairs by some devoted grown-up, and were resting on their respective beds in the lamp’s yellow halo. That room stayed empty all year, with its bare cold bed frames, except when the cousins came. Then big fresh sheets were spread for them, still scented with the sprigs of lavender scattered in the wardrobes. The cousins always had tired eyes on that first evening, but they seemed truly happy to be back. Harriett and I stayed in their doorway, watching them rush to the window as if to check that the view had not changed since last summer. Then, satisfied, they rubbed their eyes, yawning, and wished us good night. I took Harriett by the shoulder to force her back into our room, because she was much too worked up to sleep and wanted, right away, to play hide-and-seek in the dark, or spy on the adults, or catch Klaus in his attic by going very softly up the stairs so they would not creak. I told her we had every night of the whole summer ahead of us for that, and she followed me with a resignation tinged with pleasure.

In my bed, lying there with my eyes wide open, my whole being turned toward the ceiling drowned in blue shadow, I felt the weight of the two stories above me, and I sensed the bustle of the adults below, clearing the table, with the warm rumble of their hoarse voices. I listened to the sounds of the night getting ready, Louisa upstairs walking with quick steps from her washroom to her bed, the cousins on the other side of the corridor putting their things away in the wardrobes, arriving, settling in, taking possession again of the room they found each summer. I felt surrounded by the people I loved, as though I were part of something whole and the House, at last full of this great family, of the same blood as me, were properly alive. I knew it was not too serious if we were going to bed a little early tonight, because there would be other evenings. Harriett’s little rasping voice always ended up rising in the silence, venturing: “It’s good they’re here, the cousins.” Other nights, I would have told her to be quiet, because I did not feel like talking. But then I was tucked into my sheets, and outside the summer wind blew softly against the shutters. So I simply answered, “Yes, it’s really good.” We talked about what we would do tomorrow. In the almost complete darkness of the room, all I could make out of Harriett was a little dark mass on the pillow swollen with pale shadow, enormous beside her head. And that was my favorite moment of the day, full of promises, projects, ideas made wilder still because they were already intertwined with a half-consciousness full of dreams. We slipped into silence like a spark across the surface of the pond, and the waves of sleep closed over our eyes.

We let the days flow by like a thread of liquid light. It was the precious time of elastic hours, evanescent mornings, endless afternoons.

Mornings were always everyone for themselves. You barely noticed that the House was inhabited by anyone but us, everything was so silent, with the doors of the late sleepers closed, the drawing room empty but still smelling of Bertie’s honey cigars.

I was always awake before Harriett, because my bed was the one closest to the window, and a shaft of white sunlight always filtered through the crack in the shutters, cutting a warm spindle across my sheets. I stayed a few minutes in the warm bedclothes, but I heard the birds outside, the muddled buzzing of wasps knocking against the window frame, and suddenly I was seized by a mad desire to look at the garden, to see the glittering morning lawn spread itself before my eyes, and to feel the glorious breeze swell my dress. I never opened the shutters so as not to wake Harriett, and my eyes, used to the faded dimness, could make out without too much trouble the obstacles between my bed and the door. I slipped slowly out of the room, careful not to make the floorboards creak too much. My toes first met the firmness of the boards, then the slightly rough carpet that tickled the soles of my feet. I carefully avoided the jutting corners of our two beds, the toy chest, the wardrobe, and cast an anxious glance toward the little ball curled up under her flowered quilt to make sure she had not moved. Then I put one hand on the handle, the other on the latch, and pulled sharply, so the door would not squeak—for a badly oiled door always makes more noise when you open it slowly. The morning light flooding the corridor made me waver on my legs for a moment. The row of closed doors was intimidating, and there was no way of knowing whether I was the first one up or not. Still a little dizzy, I went downstairs. There too, you had to avoid the wood’s groan, control where you put your weight, prefer certain steps to others, and so take giant strides while clinging to the banister so as not to lose your balance. It was the day’s first adventure, repeated every morning.

When I crossed the deserted hall, the foliage there was frightening, because the morning light cast a cold sheen over it, and the painted flowers seemed faded, like old stars in the act of disappearing. The tiles were always icy, and I hurried into the kitchen. There the clock clicked, and that was the only time I ever noticed it, because the rest of the time its mechanical pulse was drowned out by the sounds of the House, the laughter, the voices, the discordant clatter of pans banging together as they were put away. The kitchen was cool; the windows and shutters on the garden level had been left open so the night air could circulate. In the morning we chased out the bark-colored moths that had slipped in to nestle against the warmth of the walls. They fluttered clumsily, and their trembling wings beat against the panes without finding the way out.

I shut the windows again, biting my lip when they banged a little too loudly. The dishes on the sink had dried, and a puddle of cold water trickled, drop by drop, from the edge down to the drain. I loved the kitchen windows, with their small white-framed panes looking out onto the waking orchard. I hoisted myself onto the worktop cluttered with pots and spatulas and pressed my nose to the glass. Through the fog of my breath I watched the blurred shapes of the fruit-laden trees. The leaves, a timid emerald, quivered in the sun like little fish with green veins.

I crossed the drawing room and the library, deserted, and already sighed with boredom, telling myself that adults really were too lazy, sleeping on like that while the day unfolded without waiting for us. Suddenly I hear movement above. The sound of soft slippers makes the floorboards creak; then the game is to guess who it is. If whoever it is comes down the stairs quickly, it’s Harriett or Klaus. The cousins come hurtling down quickly too, though less quickly than we do, especially the first few days: they have to get used again to the treacherous polished-wood steps. A slow, heavy tread is Uncle Bertie. In the morning Bertie smells wonderfully good, because he always washes and shaves before coming down, and once I saw him slapping great splashes of eau de cologne onto his freshly shaved neck with the flat of his hand. Then the skin under his chin is red and gleaming, and the air in his wake blossoms with the scent of tall grasses swaying in the wind.
