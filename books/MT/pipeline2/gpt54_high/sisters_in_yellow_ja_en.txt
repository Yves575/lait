Chapter One: Reunion

1

I had thought that, wherever I ended up living from here on out, however old I got, whatever became of me, I would never forget her.

And yet, until just now—until I happened upon a tiny article online and saw her name—I hadn’t realized that I had forgotten not only ever having thought such a thing, but her name, her very existence, the time we spent together, what we did there—everything.

Yoshikawa Kimiko.

For an instant I wondered if it might be someone else with the same name. But instinctively I knew the article was about that Kimiko-san.

<At an apartment in Shinjuku Ward, Tokyo, in May of last year, a woman in her twenties from Ichikawa, Chiba Prefecture, was allegedly confined indoors for one year and three months, assaulted, and seriously injured. The first hearing for defendant Yoshikawa Kimiko (60), unemployed, of Shinjuku Ward, Tokyo, who is charged with assault, intimidation, and unlawful confinement, was held on December 23 at Tokyo District Court. Asked to enter a plea, Yoshikawa stated, “I will remain silent,” while defense counsel argued not guilty.

According to the indictment, beginning around February 2018, Yoshikawa allegedly confined a female housemate in a room in an apartment in Shinjuku Ward for one year and three months, assaulted her, and inflicted serious injuries requiring one month to heal.

In its opening statement, the prosecution explained that in 2017 the victim, then without a fixed address, began living with Yoshikawa in her home. At first there were no problems, but Yoshikawa began controlling the woman’s belongings and personal relationships and monitoring her movements. She then repeatedly threatened her with statements such as, “Even if you go outside, you won’t be able to survive anyway,” instilling fear and causing the woman to lose the will to escape. Prosecutors also pointed out that Yoshikawa repeatedly assaulted the woman, unlawfully confined her, and kept her under her control, making her obey as she wished. The case came to light after the woman escaped on her own and reported it.>

After reading the article through three times, I let out a breath that seemed to rise in a lump from deep in my chest. My heart was pounding so hard I could feel a faint tremor in my fingertips. It was Kimiko-san. There was no mistake. That Kimiko-san had been arrested.

When I searched for “Yoshikawa Kimiko,” only one more piece with similar content came up, plus another brief report just a few lines long. Both were small items. Everything else was name-analysis sites, stroke-count fortune-telling, and pages about recommended names for girls. It seemed there was nothing online about Kimiko-san herself apart from the case I had just read.

I tried to sort out what I should even be thinking about, and where to begin. Then I went back to the first article and checked the date again.

It had been posted on January 10, 2020—about three months ago. And it said the incident had taken place last year, in May 2019.

But no matter how carefully I read, I still couldn’t really grasp what those dates were supposed to mean. I understood that three months had passed since the first hearing, but I had no idea what kind of state the victim and the other people involved were in now, or how the case and trial might develop from here. What had happened to Kimiko-san now? What would happen to her next? Where was I even supposed to go to find that sort of thing out?

I couldn’t picture interrogation, or the order of things, or the rules of detention centers and the like, at all. All I could come up with were the stupid images I’d seen in dramas and on the news: bleak little gray rooms, handcuffs, expressionless judges, courtroom sketches.

And Kimiko-san’s face.

Kimiko-san, with whom I had lived for several years when I was still young, around twenty years ago. The article said she was sixty. Kimiko-san, sixty? I couldn’t believe it. If I thought about it normally, of course it made sense: that much time had actually passed, and I myself was forty now. Even so, the age printed beneath Kimiko-san’s name somehow didn’t feel like a real number.

I closed my eyes and told myself I was all right. Obviously, I had nothing to do with this case. There was nothing to worry about. For twenty years I hadn’t even known what Kimiko-san had been doing, I hadn’t been in touch with her, and in no sense were we connected anymore. A long time had passed. Everything was over, gone, finished. So far, apart from this case—apart from the confinement case Kimiko-san was said to have committed last year—nothing else had become any kind of public issue. At least, nothing had turned up online. It’s all right, I told myself again and again.

When I looked up from my phone, the room was filled with the dark blue of evening, though a moment before there hadn’t been even a trace of it, and the shadows of everything had deepened. On the low table sat the plate of spaghetti with packet meat sauce I had served up for myself, meaning to eat it. But in the slowly encroaching presence of night, it somehow no longer looked like food.

That night I woke again and again, and morning came before I had really slept at all.

The curtains in the spring morning light made me think of a huge sheet of pure white drawing paper. When I shut my eyes against the brightness, colors bled and disappeared—dark blue, deep red, yellow—and then Kimiko-san’s face rose in my mind.

Gathering up her thick, jet-black, unruly hair, which fell to the middle of her back, Kimiko-san laughed and said, “I’ve got so much hair, don’t I? You probably wouldn’t even notice if there were a whole black cat curled up in it.” I laughed, and everyone laughed. An old house. Each room was tiny, crowded with things and always messy, but the entranceway was always clean. We were limited to two pairs of shoes each, and there was a rule that the entrance and the toilet had to be kept spotless at all times, because the entrance was where good fortune came in and the toilet was where bad things went out.

I shut my eyes and turned over, trying to brush away the things that kept rising into my mind. But one after another, as if hand in hand, came all sorts of things I hadn’t remembered in ages. The creak of the hallway, sagging in places, became our laughter; the grain of the ceiling I used to stare at before sleep became someone’s cigarette smoke, as though whispering to me.

The cosmetics left scattered in front of the mirror, the clothes and underwear crammed to bursting into the cubby shelves in the closet, the cup noodles stacked in a basket in the cramped kitchen—they brought back the very smell of those days we lived there.

After lying in bed under the covers for about thirty minutes, agonizing over it, I sent a message to the group LINE chat for my part-time job.

〈Good morning, this is Ito. I’ve had a bit of a cough since yesterday. I don’t have a fever, but just to be safe, I’d appreciate it if I could take the day off today. I’m sorry for the inconvenience, and thank you.〉

A reply came almost immediately from the person in charge of scheduling.

〈Understood. We should have our policy going forward decided by early next week, so I’ll let you know. Given the circumstances, I’ll inform head office too, just in case. Take care!〉

〈Thank you! I think it’s probably just an ordinary cold, but if I do happen to develop a fever or anything like that, I’ll contact you right away. Thank you again!〉

Up until around the middle of last month, there had still been a general sense of half-belief, half-doubt about the infection. People were making a fuss, but in the end, plenty of them said it was no different from the flu, nothing to be so afraid of, and masks were useless anyway. There was a kind of floating unreality, a mix of anxiety and strange excitement, but even so it still felt within the margin of everyday life.

But reports from overseas grew more and more frightening, and from around the end of last month into the beginning of this one, rumors began to circulate that Japan too would finally go into lockdown—or maybe not.

Then, five days ago, the state of emergency was declared, and the tension that had been mounting little by little burst all at once. It wasn’t just something on the news anymore. There was actual panic buying at the supermarket in my neighborhood; masks, disinfectant, and toilet paper vanished wholesale from the drugstores, and then the people vanished too; and at my part-time job we found ourselves scrambling to respond. I worked as a sales clerk at a deli in the shopping street within walking distance of my apartment, one branch of a big supermarket chain with outlets all over Tokyo.

It was a small shop, with about thirty platters of side dishes and salads lined up on counters and in refrigerated cases. Customers chose what they wanted, and we packed it into bento containers for them. All we did was sell what came in already prepared each morning from the central kitchen, so there wasn’t even a cooking area, and with four people inside the place felt full. In the three years since I started working there, it had been the same menu over and over, and with so many regulars coming in every day I used to wonder if they never got bored—but maybe that very sameness was reassuring, because sales were good, and at lunchtime and in the evening there was always a line. But from the end of last month, customers dropped off sharply, and things went visibly off-kilter. When somebody finally did come in, customers would end up arguing with each other over whether to wear masks, and we even started getting complaint calls saying our infection-control measures weren’t sufficient.

Still lying there, I found myself thinking maybe saying on LINE that I had a cough hadn’t been a good idea. Why had I lied and said that? Why, at a time like this, had I gone out of my way to say I was coughing? What on earth had I meant by it? I didn’t know myself.

After a while I picked up my phone, went back to the site with the article about Kimiko-san, and read it again slowly from the beginning. Each time my spirits darkened, and my arms and legs felt heavy. Whatever reason I had given, I was glad after all that I had taken the day off. Even if the job was just standing there, there was no way I could have worked in that state.

I got out of bed, took some barley tea from the fridge, and drank it. Then I took down a box from the shelf in the closet.

It was a somewhat oversized shoebox, its corners crushed and its lid slightly torn, and inside were old letters, planners, notebooks, things like that.

The shoebox, tired and faded until the color that had once been dark navy now looked washed out, had once held a pair of high heels my mother bought somewhere long ago. I remember her putting on those pure white high heels in the room and showing them off, then, perhaps because she was so delighted, sitting down on the tatami still wearing them and eating instant ramen. I asked her for the empty box and decided to keep stickers in it, little freebies from manga magazines, small notes I exchanged with friends at school. After that, it became a habit to put in it whatever, at the time, I thought I ought to keep. I moved from place to place, leaving things behind and losing things along the way, and before I knew it this shoebox alone was what remained. Yet I ordinarily never looked inside it, or even picked it up. Looking at it now, though it was mine—something I had brought all this way without ever throwing it out, for some reason—I felt as if I were looking at someone else’s effects.

When I opened the lid, I saw a small navy flip phone and its charger tucked into a corner. Even though I had been the one looking for it, the instant I found it, my heart jumped. Not knowing whether it still worked, I plugged in the charger, connected it to the outlet, and after waiting about thirty minutes I pressed and held the power button. The tiny screen slowly brightened, as if drawing breath again, and the ringtone sounded.

I had changed my phone number and erased everything so that no one could get in touch with me, and yet I had left these numbers—those of people I had known back then—undeleted. Had some part of me sensed, without knowing it, that one day I would need them like this?

There were only seventeen contacts in the address book.

Under K, I saw the name Kimiko-san. I selected the name a little above it, Kato Ran, and displayed the number. Then I went to M. Momoko. Tamamori Momoko. I copied both numbers into the notes on my current phone.

I had no idea where Kato Ran and Tamamori Momoko were now, or what had become of them.

The last time I saw them was when they left that house—when we were twenty, or thereabouts. I hadn’t been in touch with either Ran or Momoko since. If I hadn’t happened to see that article about Kimiko-san yesterday, I might never have thought of them again.

The time all of us spent in that house came back like fragments of film spliced together without order.

Faces and voices replayed, now suddenly sharp, now blurring again. I didn’t think those numbers would still work, and at this late date I didn’t want to get in touch with either of them myself. But Ran and Momoko were the only people I could talk to about Kimiko-san. The only two who could share the unease I felt now.

I was afraid Kimiko-san might have spoken about the past she had with us.

In the course of questioning, they might have found all sorts of traces in Kimiko-san’s room from when we lived together, and an investigation might already have started behind the scenes. When I thought that, I couldn’t sit still. No one had come to me yet, but it was entirely possible that Ran or Momoko had been contacted first, summoned in, and already questioned.

If I thought about it calmly, what we had done might already be past the statute of limitations. Maybe it wouldn’t amount to such a major crime. Ran and Momoko and I had all been young then, and we had only been doing what Kimiko-san told us. But what about Kotomi-san? In the end, whose fault was it that Kotomi-san died, and why did she have to die? Could we really say it had nothing to do with us?

The more I thought, the more an anxiety indistinguishable from fear bore down on me. It felt as if some huge iron plate, or something like it, were silently crushing my chest, and tears welled in my eyes. What was I supposed to do? Pretend I’d never seen the article, say nothing, not contact Ran or Momoko? Or should I tell the police what I knew?

My imagination kept swelling in darker and darker directions, until it blocked out everything else. What were the two of them doing now? Where were they? There was no way the calls would go through. But if they weren’t going to go through anyway, why not at least try?—I set the phone beside me and pulled the futon over my head, as if to smother everything. The daylight was cut off, and for a while I lay there blinking in the warm, clammy darkness of spring. Then, just like that, I fell asleep for a little while.

I dreamed one of those elusive dreams whose only certainty is that it’s a nightmare. Nothing concrete happened in it, no people appeared, nothing at all—there was only time—and yet why is it that with nightmares you always know they are nightmares? Like dark, merciless waves, it kept closing in on me without end. When I woke, my chest and back were drenched in sweat. Then I called Ran Kato’s number.

After six rings, a bright voice said, “Helloo?”

I could feel my lower jaw trembling with tension.

“Um, excuse me—is this... Ran Kato’s number?”

“Yes.”

Ran answered, her voice slightly lower now. It was Ran. My heart gave a hard thump.

“Um, it’s Hana.”

“Hana?”

“Yes, um, I’m Hana Ito. A long time ago, we—”

“Hana?” After a brief silence, Ran said, “...That Hana-chan?”

“Yeah, it’s Hana. Sorry, this is so sudden.” I shifted the phone to my other hand and pressed it tight against my ear. “I really didn’t think I’d get through. Sorry, calling you out of nowhere.”

“...What is it? How did you get this number?”

“It was still saved in my old phone.”

“Oh.”

I heard Ran let out a small sigh.

“I’m really sorry for startling you like this.”

“No, that’s fine, but... it just threw me. I mean, it was so long ago.”

“I know. I’m sorry. Actually, the reason I called is about Kimiko-san.”

Behind Ran, I could hear children making a happy racket. There were women’s voices mixed in too. Then the sounds grew a little fainter, and I realized she had moved somewhere else.

“...Kimiko-san? You mean that Kimiko-san?”

“Yeah.”

“What about her?”

“Yesterday I found an article about a case involving Kimiko-san.”

“What do you mean?”

“I found it online.”

“What case?”

“Kimiko-san was arrested. I was shocked too. The trial’s already started, and I thought maybe—just maybe—it might have something to do with us too, and that’s why I wanted to talk to you, Ran-chan.”

“Wait a second.” Ran cut me off. “What are you talking about? I have no idea what you mean. Why was Kimiko-san arrested? What do you mean it might have something to do with us too? Did Kimiko-san say something?”

“No, not that. Kimiko-san—she was arrested for confining a young woman in her apartment and injuring her. I think... she was probably doing the same kind of thing she did back then, and that’s why she got caught. Maybe the past will become an issue too, and all sorts of things might, um, come out. I don’t really know, but it scares me.”

I made myself ask.

“...No one’s contacted you, have they? The police, or anyone like that?”

“As if they would.” Ran laughed as though the idea were ridiculous, but there was a faint note of unease in it.

“I’ve been anxious ever since yesterday, and... I was wondering if maybe I should go to the police and talk.”

“What?” Ran said, startled. “Talk about what?”

“About what happened back then, and what I know about Kimiko-san.”

“Come on, don’t joke about that.” Ran lowered her voice, but her tone was firm. Just then someone called out to her, and she answered brightly, “Okay!”

“Listen, I’ve got people over right now.”

“Yeah, of course. Sorry.”

“Hana-chan, where are you living now? Tokyo?”

“Yeah.”

“Maybe we should meet and talk. I know, with corona and all... but if possible, maybe not over the phone.”

“Yeah, I think it’d be better to talk in person too... Oh, right, Momoko—Momoko was there too, remember? I don’t know if her number still works, but we should probably contact her too, right? If she can make it.”

“Momoko can’t,” Ran said shortly.

“Why?”

“I’ll tell you when we meet—Hana-chan, whatever you do, don’t go to the police or anything like that. Absolutely not. Okay?”

“Okay.”

We settled on a time and place to meet and hung up.

2

When I saw Ran Kato again after twenty years, she seemed to have changed a great deal.

She was still small overall, as I remembered, but it felt as though her body had grown a size larger, and something about the balance of her looked slightly off. If she hadn’t spotted me first and raised a hand, I don’t think I would have recognized her right away, even in a café this empty.

Ran was wearing a loose cotton tunic in an unbleached shade and a large mask that covered half her face. Her hair, dyed a light brown, was tied back, and at the hairline her gray, as if brushed on in quick strokes, caught the light and glimmered faintly. There was still something of the old Ran in her eyes, with their firm brown eye shadow and heavy mascara.

Her narrow forehead hadn’t changed, and I remembered one midnight long ago when Ran, Momoko, and I measured and compared our foreheads and the widths of our eyes. Come to think of it, Ran had loved makeup even then; laughing, she used to try all kinds of looks on me, asking how much she thought her technique could change the face of someone as clueless about that sort of thing as I was, and then the three of us would look at the result and burst out laughing. Looking at the Ran in front of me now while remembering all that, I was overtaken by the realization of how much time had really passed, and felt a small ache deep in my chest.

And yet we didn’t exchange any of the bright greetings people usually do when they meet an old friend again—Long time no see, How’ve you been?—and simply ordered our drinks. With her mouth hidden, I couldn’t tell what expression Ran had on, and in an atmosphere so still it seemed nothing could stir, we sat in silence for a while.

After two iced coffees had been set down on the table, I tried saying, “I’m glad you recognized me right away, Ran-chan.”

“You were standing by the entrance looking around,” Ran answered shortly.

She stopped fiddling with her phone and took off her mask. Brick-red lipstick was thickly smeared across it.

“Kimiko-san,” Ran said. “What happened? What kind of case is it? I tried looking it up, but I couldn’t really tell.”

Ignoring how tense I was, Ran spoke as though we were simply picking up a conversation from the other day.

“Was it on the news? Is it bad?”

“Not exactly.” I pulled up the article I had bookmarked and handed the phone to her. Ran stared intently at the screen, scrolled a little with her fingertip, then fixed her eyes on it again.

“How did you find this?”

After sliding the phone back across the table to me, Ran asked the question.

“I was idly reading articles on Yahoo and places like that about other major cases, and then more and more similar ones kept coming up farther down the page. Because they were related by topic. Old ones from years ago, links to tiny local-paper stories from places I’d never heard of—that sort of thing, loads of them. And as I kept following them, I came across this.”

“Shinjuku, huh... so it wasn’t that place in Sangenjaya.” Ran frowned. “It said Kimiko-san was sixty... which means back then she was forty or so. About our age now.”

“Yeah.”

“When I think about it now, she was completely out of her mind.”

Ran said it as if spitting the words out.

“It’s unbelievable. Forty means it’s like us now gathering up a bunch of twenty-year-olds and living like that with them, right? Completely deranged.”

She gave a small shake of her head.

“But why is this Kimiko-san case making you so freaked out, Hana-chan? Doesn’t it have nothing to do with you? And besides, the incident itself was last year, and the trial’s already started, right?”

I nodded.

“When we were living together—sure, okay, I do think we did some sketchy things. But we were twenty or so, and it was a long time ago. I don’t see how something like that could suddenly become a problem now.”

“But if there was anything left at Kimiko-san’s house from those days—those bundles of cards, for example—the police might look at them and think, what’s this? And if Kimiko-san talked, even if they couldn’t charge us with anything, they might at least ask us about it. I’m sure there’s some statute of limitations or whatever, but with what we were doing... I don’t know which parts of it would count as a problem, or how much of one.”

“So because of that, you were actually thinking of going to the police and telling them you were involved with her back then?” Ran said, opening her eyes wide in exasperation. “You’re overthinking it.”

“You think?”

“Of course. Your imagination is going way too far in a bad direction. To begin with, what we were doing wasn’t that big a deal. That’s what I think. It’s not the kind of thing you should still be tormenting yourself over twenty years later. Kids now do way worse stuff than that.”

“You think?”

“Of course,” Ran said after a moment, as if considering it. “Besides... we were made to do it.”

“But Kotomi-san died too, and we still don’t know what really happened, and it’s like we dragged her into it—or anyway, things ended the way they did...”

“Kotomi-san? Who was that again—oh.” Ran gave a small nod. “Right. The hostess from that Ginza club you used to go to on those gigs.”

“Yeah.”

“No, no, no, I don’t think that had anything to do with it. That was completely their own situation... that’s what we said at the time, remember? Wasn’t it? Sure, it was a shock, but... what, Hana-chan, don’t tell me you still think it was your fault.”

“Not that.” I shook my head.

“No way. Those people were all crazy.” Ran gave a slight shrug. “So come on, calm down a little. Switch your mind onto something brighter. Don’t make that face. There’s no point agonizing over something from a million years ago that nobody even remembers anymore. Sure, if Kimiko-san’s name suddenly comes up like this, it’s a shock. And with that business about a girl being confined, it’s a similar kind of situation. I get it. But it’s fine. Really. If there were even the slightest chance this was serious, the police would’ve shown up ages ago. Honestly, it was just a cringeworthy chapter in our past. Youthful stupidity, and that’s all. Ancient cringe.”

“Youthful stupidity. Ancient cringe.”

“Exactly. Anyway, corona’s worse, right? How are things for you, Hana-chan? At my place it’s hell with the kids off school. Corona’s bad, isn’t it? What’s it like for you?”

“As for me...” I said, letting out a small breath through my nose. “It’s normal. I live alone.”

Ran nodded as if to say I see, stirring her iced coffee round and round with the straw. We were in a café outside Jiyugaoka Station. Ran apparently lived somewhere along the Toyoko Line, and had picked this place saying it was convenient because she could get here on one train, and she had something else to do nearby anyway. A chain coffee shop with glass walls all around. Ran had asked me nothing—not where I lived, not what kind of work I did, not where or how I had lived all the years since then.

“Hardly anybody’s here. It’s usually packed. This whole self-restraint thing is brutal.”

Looking out at the street, I saw she was right: there were only a few scattered pedestrians, and with the heavy cloud cover that had hung over the city since morning, the whole neighborhood seemed dark and subdued.

“But time goes round, doesn’t it? We’re basically forty now. I never imagined I’d see you again like this, Hana-chan. It wasn’t even really a proper parting. We just kind of scattered.”

“You never changed your number, Ran-chan. Did Kimiko-san ever contact you after that?”

“No,” Ran said. “I can’t really remember the details anymore, and I don’t want to either... We slipped out in the middle of all that confusion, remember? And that was it. But you were the one who had it hardest, weren’t you, Hana-chan? You were the one she doted on most... I mean, honestly, Kimiko-san’s terrifying. No wonder she got arrested. Way too crazy. We were young, still kids, so we didn’t understand any of it, but she was completely dangerous.”

“Hey, you mentioned it on the phone,” I said. “About Momoko... you said Momoko was impossible. Did something happen?”

“Ah, Momoko.” Ran gave a small shake of her head. “Remember? Momoko had that messed-up little sister. Beautiful as hell, teeth filthy as hell. She came there too, didn’t she? Anyway, there was some big mess over money with her sister and the sister’s boyfriend, and in the end Momoko disappeared. That’s the rumor, anyway. I don’t really know. After I left that place, a while later I was in touch with Momoko for a little while, but then suddenly she was gone.”

I sighed.

“Yeah. It was nothing but crazy people. We just didn’t know any better.”

Ran’s voice suddenly grew louder, taking on an odd exaggerated ring, as though she were rehearsing lines. The café was spacious and sparsely occupied; the few customers scattered about were all alone and all wearing masks, while soft bossa nova-style music played from speakers hidden somewhere in the ceiling. From the washing area behind the counter came the clatter of dishes, mixed with the voices of the staff. No one besides us was talking. An old man at a table a little way off opened his newspaper with a loud crackle, and when he glanced over, our eyes met. I took a sip of water and checked with my fingertips that the mask was still hooked under my chin.

“Well, whatever it was, it’s over now. I keep saying that, but it was a long time ago. Just the past. Nothing for you to worry about, Hana-chan... right, Hana-chan?”

Without blinking, Ran stared straight at me as she said it.

“Seriously, absolutely no police or anything like that, okay? There’s seriously no point. A hundred percent it’ll just make trouble. So I’m really asking you, Hana-chan. It’s all right, isn’t it? I came today and properly listened to what you had to say. Just forget it now. Seriously.”

I nodded.

“It’s all right, really?”

“Yeah.”

After that, we sat in silence for a long time. Ran sighed, hunched her back, and stared at a single point on the table. There was weariness in her expression. I felt there was something else I ought to say to Ran now that we had met again, something important. And maybe she was feeling the same thing. But I didn’t know what it was, or how to make it come out of my mouth in the right words.

“Well, I should probably get going—oh, right, Hana-chan.”

As if pulling herself together, Ran straightened up and spoke while putting on her mask.

“One more thing—could you delete my number? I’ll delete your call from my log too.”

When I sat there in silence, unable to say anything, Ran picked up the bill and checked it, left money for her share on the table, slung her bag over her shoulder, and stood up.

“Well then, Hana-chan, I’m off. Watch out for corona.”

After saying that, Ran left the café, but I couldn’t get up from my seat right away. There had to be other things I needed to think about, but my mind wouldn’t work. I had no appetite, but I hadn’t eaten since morning, so I ought to have been hungry. I sat there vaguely thinking things like maybe I should order something.

As I looked at the little photo menu on the table, I heard a strange sound. Outside, somewhere far off, there was a noise like something enormous being slowly rolled along—impossible to tell whether it was heavy or light. After a while I realized it was thunder. The moment I looked up through the glass, the rain came down all at once with a bursting sound. The drops were so large it almost seemed you could see each one separately, and I watched several pedestrians run off, shielding their heads with bags or hands. The rain only grew fiercer, lashing the awning of the shop diagonally across the street and the asphalt, then bouncing back up in a faint white spray.

Chapter Two: Money Luck

1

I first met Kimiko-san in the summer I was fifteen.

One morning, just after the start of my last summer vacation in junior high, a woman I didn’t know was asleep there in place of my mother, who should have been lying beside me.

I couldn’t see her face, and she was wearing my mother’s pajamas, but I knew at once that the woman lying there with her back to me, snoring away in her sleep, wasn’t my mother.

Propped up on one elbow, I edged backward a little, but almost immediately decided it was nothing and lay back down. My mother worked at a snack bar nearby, and before that she had brought girls from the place home like this to stay the night, or friends of hers, several times.

When I woke again, the woman was gone, and on top of the futon, folded neatly in thirds, lay the pajamas she had arranged so carefully. They were the same old, worn-out pajamas my mother had been wearing for years, but it felt strangely fresh to see them in such a tidy shape, like clothes arranged for sale in a store, and I found myself staring at them for a while.

Normally our clothes and underwear were hung to dry on hangers from the curtain rail by the window, then taken down and worn straight from there; the room was full of things, and always a mess. Because of that, that one spot seemed to stand out in a soft round, like the blank space left when a notebook faintly smudged gray with pencil has been carefully erased clean.

My mother and I lived in a small old apartment house hidden from the main road, in a little town on the edge of Higashimurayama.

Between two detached houses facing the road ran an unpaved passage about three meters wide. If you followed it in and turned left, there was a communal entrance. The words Seifūsō were still barely legible on the sign, old and blackened with age; the entrance itself looked like the mouth of some ill-omened cave, and the corridor beyond, with nothing but a few low-watt bulbs hanging from above, stayed dark even in the middle of the brightest day.

It was a two-story wooden building, with four rooms of the same layout on each floor, but apart from us the only resident was the middle-aged landlady who lived upstairs at the back. Behind a flimsy wooden sliding door that looked as if even a child could kick it in was a tiny entryway; step up from there and you were in a kitchen about the size of three tatami mats, and beyond that two four-and-a-half-mat rooms ran one behind the other. We had the shared toilet at the back of the first floor practically to ourselves. Hemmed in on all sides by other buildings, the place got almost no light; even when you opened the window, all you could see was the concrete wall next door.

We rented the two ground-floor rooms immediately to the left and right of the entrance to Seifūsō, and my mother and I mostly used the one on the right.

The room on the left was supposed to be my father’s place, but aside from a television, a futon, and a few clothes hanging in the closet, it had none of the feel of a lived-in room. In any case, my father was hardly ever home.

At the time I didn’t really know what kind of work he did. He was solidly built, his skin always deeply sunburned, and I think he probably did construction, or drove a truck, or took day-labor jobs that sometimes kept him on the move for long stretches. Once, on my way home from elementary school, I heard him call my name from a large truck. For a while he brought home workmates dressed in the same kind of work clothes, and they’d have hot pot, grill meat, and drink, but that period was over almost as soon as it began.

The father I saw only once in a while, as though he’d suddenly remembered me, was always in a good mood. He’d give me things—a badminton set, or stuffed toys that looked as if he’d won them from a claw machine—and sometimes he would come back without warning in the middle of the night, wake me from sleep on purpose, and feed me sushi, saying it was good.

I didn’t dislike him. But we had spent so little time together, and I never knew what to say to him, so whenever he came home I would grow strangely tense, watch myself around him, and sit there on edge, wishing he’d leave soon. Then, when I thought about whether he might have noticed that, my mood would darken all over again. I knew my father wasn’t what people would call a normal father, but I also vaguely felt that having your own child think of you like this might be sad, and that brought on yet another kind of tension, along with self-loathing.

By the time I was in the upper grades of elementary school, my father had stopped coming home for good, and after that I never saw him again. I don’t know where he is now or what became of him. It was only later that I vaguely realized he seemed to have another home—or rather, other people he was living with.

Life alone with my mother was easy, if you wanted to call it that, but only because it was no different from having nothing at all. She liked having fun, and she liked to drink even though she wasn’t all that strong; she had lots of friends, and she was the kind of person who got swept along. After graduating from the local commercial high school, she got a full-time job at a stocking factory, and she was proud of the time, when some important people from headquarters came to inspect the place, she’d been made to model her legs in front of everyone. But that job lasted only a few years. After that she drifted from one local snack bar to another with her friends, and somewhere along the way, apparently, I was born.

Compared with the mothers of my friends at school, my mother looked much younger, and she was flashy; even to me, her daughter, she didn’t feel like what people call a normal mother. She was small, with a vaguely childlike face, and she was bright and optimistic and laughed a lot, but when she drank she cried almost without fail. Not because there was any clear reason for it. She was just the crying-drunk type, I suppose; for her, getting cheerful and noisy with drink and crying were almost the same thing.

She didn’t seem particularly attached to my father, either. I never heard her complain about him or speak badly of him, and after he stopped coming home she didn’t seem especially interested. Every now and then she would talk, half jokingly, about what a terrible person her own mother—the one she had cut ties with—had been, but even that was only when she’d been drinking, getting carried away, and growing sentimental.

Basically, when she was with me, my mother liked having someone else around rather than having it be just the two of us. We often spent our time aimlessly with hostesses from the bar where she worked, or someone a customer had introduced her to, or old local friends she’d known forever, and whenever I was alone with her I always felt just a little tense. She slept while I was at school, got up sometime after noon, put on her makeup in the evening, went to work, and came home late at night. We basically lived on opposite schedules.

There was only one other kid in the neighborhood with a home life anything like mine, and we became friends. From the time we were in elementary school, neither of us had anything like a curfew, and the parents of kids from homes where certain places were off-limits and dinner was at a set time didn’t seem to like their children spending any more time than necessary with us. At the very least, they weren’t allowed to come over to our houses. Once, I asked another friend I liked fairly well—someone who was perfectly normal with me at school, but after classes always went off somewhere with a different group—why. They told me, a little awkwardly, “At Hana-chan’s place, there are weird grown-ups always coming and going, and it’s not a proper home, so I’m not allowed to.” And there was nothing I could do about that, because it wasn’t some malicious rumor or lie. It was simply true.

By the time I got to junior high, my friends’ faces, their attitudes, all sorts of things came into sharper focus. I felt as though I could tell at a glance which kids came from normal homes and which didn’t, as if they were wearing hats of different colors. In a normal home, you don’t wake up in the morning and find some woman you don’t know asleep beside you. Probably the sight of a futon and pajamas simply folded up doesn’t brighten your mood, either, or make you want to keep staring at them.

I folded my own futon into thirds the same way and laid it beside the one that had already been folded. I was alone in the room, and there was no one in the kitchen either, so I slipped on my sandals, crossed the hall in a stride, and opened the door to the room opposite. The television was blaring, and when I went in I saw the back of a woman lying there watching a daytime talk show. The fan we’d had forever slowly turned its head from side to side, creaking loudly now and then.

When she noticed me, the woman didn’t change her position, only turned her face toward me and smiled. The smile was so natural that for a second I almost thought she must be someone I had met before. But she was a stranger—she might have been the one sleeping in the futon next to mine last night, but at least this was the first time we had faced each other like this. She turned back to the screen and laughed happily, her shoulders shaking a little at the cheers from the people on television. I stood in the doorway between the kitchen and the room, watching the television, the fan, and the woman.

“How about we eat something,” she said with a stretch when the show ended and it cut to commercials.

“You’re hungry, right?”

We stood in the cramped kitchen and made packet ramen.

The woman was about a whole size bigger than me, and her arms and legs seemed much longer too. Her long black wavy hair was tied back in one bundle and drooped at the nape of her neck, the ends scattered over the big white T-shirt with English printed on it. I stood a little away from her, staring at the water for two in the aluminum pot as it came to a boil.

With practiced ease, she tore open the packets, loosened the noodles in the hot water, added the powdered soup and gave it a quick stir, then divided the ramen between the bowls I had set out. She tried to pluck a wonton caught on the rim of the pot with the tip of her chopsticks, but it wouldn’t come loose. “Oh well, I’ll bring that in too,” she said brightly, nodding toward the room. I pulled out the bare kotatsu frame that had been propped up against the wall, and we sat facing each other and slurped our ramen.

“It’s summer vacation, right? Aren’t you going anywhere?”

I made a vague sound, something like an answer but not really. A moment before it hadn’t even felt that hot, but suddenly I could feel sweat starting to bead. I reached over, switched on the air conditioner, and pressed the button for high.

“Going anywhere with Ai-san?”

Ai was my mother’s name.

“Not really. We don’t do that kind of thing much.”

“Hm. What’s your name? I’m Kimiko.”

“Hana.”

“Hana-chan, huh. Who named you? Ai-san?”

“I don’t really know,” I said in a small voice.

“I see.”

“Um, you’re my mother’s friend, right?”

“Right, right.”

The conversation stopped there, and we silently ate the rest of our ramen. A quiz show played on the television, and the old air conditioner roared on. After a while we could faintly hear an ambulance outside; it seemed to come a little closer, then quickly fade away again.

“Hana-chan—do you write it with kanji?”

“Just the ordinary hana. Flower.”

“My Kimiko, you know, is written with the ki from yellow, then beautiful child—Kimiko.”

I gave another vague nod and drank some soup. When I glanced up over my bowl, my eyes met Kimiko-san’s—she had already finished eating and was leaning on the kotatsu—and without thinking I looked away. After that, over the rim of my bowl, I kept stealing little glances at Kimiko-san’s face. Kimiko-san had a face unlike any woman’s I had ever seen before.

Everyone’s face is different, so of course that was only natural, and yet there was something about Kimiko-san’s face—something like a force of presence—that I couldn’t have explained properly in the words I had then. She had just gotten out of bed and couldn’t have had any makeup on, but her eyebrows were thick and full, her clearly double-lidded eyes were fringed with long, dark lashes, and her brows sat close above them. Her nose rose firmly from the bridge, and the hair from her temples into her sideburns, mixed with sweat, curled into tiny frizzy spirals.

Kimiko-san’s face seemed less pretty or beautiful than strong.

It made me think of the young, regal pharaoh from a girls’ manga set in both ancient Egypt and the present day, wildly popular when I was in elementary school and something I had been absolutely crazy about. Watching Kimiko-san crack her neck as she watched TV, or shift the way she was sitting, I felt as if that character’s lines were floating somewhere above and a little to one side of her head, and I could see the borders of a manga panel around her. The thought made me a little happy.

“I’m in the mood for a soda. Want to go buy some?”

After carrying the bowls to the sink, Kimiko-san came back into the room and invited me. We went outside just as we had woken up and walked to the convenience store.

“Even though Ai-san’s place has two rooms, you don’t sleep separately?”

“The room where we just ate ramen we call the TV room, and the other one is the futon room, so somehow it just ended up that way.”

“So it’s like a living room.”

“It’s not anything that nice,” I said, looking down.

“Still, better than sleeping alone, right?”

The summer sun was sharp enough to make me squint, and every time I breathed I could feel the heat soaking into my skin.

The old apartment where I lived stood directly behind my middle school.

If you followed the school’s long gray wall for about two hundred meters and turned left at the corner, the main gate was a little farther on, with the convenience store directly across the road. We were forbidden to shop there in our uniforms, but hardly anyone followed that rule. Along the narrow sidewalk, the vivid green of trees planted on the school grounds—trees whose names I didn’t know—jutted out over us and cast deep blue shadows on the asphalt.

As we came closer to the main gate, I saw a few people hanging around there, making a racket.

Even though it was summer vacation, students still came and went for club activities. But the ones there weren’t that kind of students. They were the so-called bad kids, the delinquents.
