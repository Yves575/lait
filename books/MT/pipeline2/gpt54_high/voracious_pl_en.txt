To loss.

And also—to the birds.


Cherry


Dunaj howls. And they are already here, just as I remember them—hungry, restless. They shine as if their wings had been doused in gasoline; violet, blue, red, yellow shimmer over them.

The flock wheels above the village.

June has let in so much sun that already at its very beginning the cherries are sweet.

Grandfather avoids Grandmother’s illness. The ceremonies around illness are alien to him. He wants to know nothing about illness, touch nothing of illness. From the veranda he watches the starlings swoop low over the orchard.

He strikes an aluminum milk can with a metal rod. The valley booms with it.

I watch him. It must be booming in him too; his body trembles, his skin is like the skin of men who live along the Carpathian Arc, I think. Bronze. Ebony.

The starlings burst into flight, a whole wave of them. As they sweep over the house, they cast a shadow across the floor of the west room, the one the cast-iron stove heats in the cold months.

A moment later they are back over the orchard.

They are stubborn.

More blows on the metal make them twitch with nerves. The booming keeps them in the air.

Grandfather breaks for a cigarette. They use the silence and land in the spreading tree. They stuff their small bodies with fruit.

They’ve already learned to recognize silence, I say to Ann.

Yes, she says.

Ann is brightness here. She is interested in light and is light herself. She arrived here this morning, straight into warm Maj, from a far-off country. The journey is still in her. A scent not from here is still in her—freesia, patchouli. She stands with me on the veranda and gets her eyes used to early summer.

Her eyes follow the light.

Ann knows light.

She studies light.

She hunts light.

She divines from light.

I touch her neckline with my fingertips, where constellations of moles grow.

Ann says, We have to count them.

All right, I answer.

As a child I counted Grandmother’s moles. She hid very little from me. She let me count the little dots that covered her back, and the ones around her breasts too. She let my gaze into recesses like the shell of her ear or the crooks of her elbows.

You are of my body too, she used to say.

We move from the veranda into the west room. You can hear the melody Grandfather is playing. Grandmother watches the shadow of the flock of birds. She raises her gaunt hand, as if volunteering an answer.

There, she says, pointing to the wardrobe.

She asks me to take out her summer dresses. That surprises me. Every day she wears the pajamas I buy her at the market in the nearby town of Stary Sad. She likes the powder-mint ones.

I spread the dresses out on the sofa-bed.

That one, Grandmother whispers.

She has chosen the one the color of blood. I take it in my hands. Velvet.

Take it to him, she says.

I do it without a word of objection.

On the veranda Grandfather is finishing off another Klubowe—yes, out of nostalgia for what is gone, that’s what he calls every cigarette, and I got it from him. He takes the dress in his hands. His skin catches on the cloth. He smells the fabric.

Róża, he says.

I look at him. The last years have dried his body out. More and more often he looks away, hides his eyes, dodges with them. He cannot hold a look or return one. I would like to see him stronger, I think.

He turns and says, Come with me.

I go.

He heads for the workshop.

Once there, he lashes together from hazel branches something like a cross and dresses that cross in Grandmother’s blood-red dress. He studies his work. He adjusts the little foam pads that give the object an expression of surprise, as though something alive were lifting its arms.

Will it do? he asks.

It will do, I answer.

I know he needs my assent. Here, in this illness, there is no room for objection.

Grandfather nods to my nodding. He takes the dummy in his hands and goes into the orchard with the skeleton clothed in a dress. Behind him, a procession: the dog Dunaj and the tomcat Klakier.

Grandmother watches us from the window of the west room. She scratches at the skin on her cheek with her nails. I know that reflex. It arises in her whenever she watches something. She can scratch herself all over and know nothing of the scratching.

Grandfather props a ladder against the trunk of the cherry tree. I assist. Carefully he sets his feet on the rungs. He reaches the top. With wire he fastens the scarecrow to a branch.

A scarecrow against hunger, I say under my breath.

Grandmother’s red dress begins to move in the wind. A gust enters it and fills the place where a warm body once was. I have the impression the dress has come alive.

Spirit of the orchard, I say to Ann, who comes over to us.

Nothing works like a red dress, she hums.

The starlings wheel above the tree.

Back in the west room, I ask Grandmother whether she remembers wearing that outfit last spring. There are photographs.

She nods.

The red brought out her gray hair.

You know, she says, the illness must already have been in me then. Can you imagine it?

I nod.

With my fingertips I examine the wound she has scratched into her cheek.

For the rest of the day we take turns going to the window of the west room and looking out at the orchard. The blood-red of the dress blends with the cherries ripening in full sun. We will gather the fruit spared the starlings’ beaks for Grandmother. Grandfather will make sweet jams from them.


The West Room


We freshen up the room on the west side of the house for the illness. In my childhood, for years it served us as a capacious cold room. We stored there the meat of pigs slaughtered for church holidays. My grandparents kept furniture here, old paintings, jars filled with preserves, cans of honeydew honey, boxes full of Christmas ornaments. After the tragedy, the one from many years ago that befell my parents and my aunt, Grandmother has her den here.

Soon they’ll put radiator sections in here, says Grandfather, standing in the doorway.

Yes, I nod.

Grandmother can’t be cold in her illness, he says.

Yes, yes, I nod.

I can still live with the cold, but take this pink out of here, says Grandmother.

Yes, yes, I nod.

Pink reminds me of meat, she says.

Yes, I nod.

Make it white in here, she asks.

I nod.

I am obedient. That is why I came back here from far away, why I left my life in the city that is always bright: to be obedient.

Obedience, I say to Ann.

Obedience, she repeats, and runs her hand over my hair.


Deal


We begin freshening up the room. As if the illness required special measures and actions from us.

We are cooperating with it, cutting a deal, I think.

Now it has us too, I say to Ann, handing her the overalls I wore here when I was a teenager.

We work, the two of us. The illness joins us in physical labor. We need to tire ourselves out.

Grandmother’s eyes are alert.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us and look, look.

Let’s work ourselves to death, I say.

Every so often Grandmother peers into the room, nods, and looks, looks. She likes that against the white walls the plants and holy pictures will stand out clearly.

Make it white in here, she asks again.

The instructions are simple—white.

We strip away the old layers of paint with a putty knife. We hand the tool back and forth whenever pain reaches the shoulders and forearms of the one who’s working. I taste dust in my mouth; it grates between my teeth.

I hold the ladder Ann is standing on. She wrestles with the wall. Dust settles on her bare arms. You can see her muscles working clearly. She is beautiful when she works the putty knife. She strips away the layers of paint that have been laid on here over the last thirty years. You can read a great deal in them. The big occasions that called for the room to be freshened up are written there: a baptism, a First Communion, a confirmation, and now illness. I recognize every color as it comes off.

Maybe if someone began to study them, it would be possible to read more from them, the way changes in the environment are read from a soil profile.

The history of this house falls to the floor.

We make room for the new.

Ann is beautiful when she tenses her body, sets her thoughts in motion, talks as she does it, when everything she is goes into motion.

We need what is bright, I say to Ann, stirring the paint.

Yes, she says.

We daub ourselves with white.

At night, while the room dries, I go in to look at it.

Huuuuhuuu, huuuhuuu, I hoot.

Behind me I hear Ann.

Huuuuhuuu, huuuhuuu, she repeats after me.

The sounds quiver in those dozen or so square meters.

We rasp from the dust. It is in our throats. We spit white.

In the morning we show Grandmother the room.

White, she says.

She presses her cool hands to our faces and strokes us, the way she strokes Dunaj when he comes home after days on the loose.

Grandmother begins carrying plants into the west room.

Life, life, she says.

After two pots of calathea she grows weak. She sits down on the floor and leans against the wall. Without a word we take over her work, become her hands. We carry from the hall back into the west room the plants that had been put there for the duration of the renovation: aloe, asparagus fern, fern, geranium, Siberian ivy, Christmas cactus, philodendron, croton, dracaena, begonia, caladium.

Grandmother’s eyes are alert.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us.

Grandmother’s eyes look, look.


Hunchbacked


When the smell of paint is gone, the three of us spend the night in the west room. We still do not know it will stay this way, that the nights to come will be shared. But not yet. For now the moon is waxing.

There it is, waxing, hunchbacked, I think.

I am growing used to closeness.

We divide the bed and the sofa-bed between us.

We strip off the day’s layers of clothes.

We mix our smells.

My women, I think, falling asleep, are beautiful and smell, smell.


Throat


In the room I can hear Grandmother’s and Ann’s breathing. Night has stilled our pupils, so scattered by day. Ever since illness moved in with us, I am the last to fall asleep.

Only my watch is strong, I think. No one else can keep vigil that long.

In the corner of the room the cast-iron stove gives off heat. The last days of June have brought cold here, into the valley.

Any moment now it will begin.

Light.

Light slices across our faces. I look at Ann, whose face is lit by the headlights of a truck carrying cattle to the slaughterhouse. She opens her eyes. And they are deep, bottomless. They look into wakefulness and, when they notice me watching, they hold still.

Ann keeps watch with me for a while.

But she cannot keep guard the way I can.

She goes back into sleep.

And now, oh yes, it begins. A few dozen meters from the west room, big trucks take the bend, their headlights filling the room with light.

Light and dark.

Light and dark.

Light.

Dark.

Light.

This will last a million years.

I have time, I think.

The trucks bring animals to the slaughterhouse. I imagine cows’ bodies—warm, wet.

As a child I did not always manage to wait for this moment of the night. Sometimes I fell asleep and the spectacle, already under way, woke me. It came with sounds like tonight’s: guttural, drawn-out ones that, instead of dissolving into space, can hang over the village for a long time.

The bellowing of cows.

The whining of dogs.

The hooting of the night.

And this too—the smell: dung, urine, sweat.

The bodies of my women show clearly when the headlights of the Jelcz trucks move slowly over them. Their features are—depending on the falling light, and on the shadows—now dead, now alive.

Grandmother lies on her side, her hand tucked under her left cheek. She looks like a child. A child, the kind they would have wanted from me.

But now is no time for life. Death—that is what fills my mind. That is what I serve. Grandmother is my child. I am mother to my grandmother. It is good, I think.

Ann’s body is beside me. The porcelain of her skin never ceases to astonish me, the whiteness of her hair, lashes, and brows. There is something twinlike in us, and there is also—and we know it—something that binds us: much in us that is wasted.

The bodies of the women close to me react to the light; beneath their eyelids their eyeballs begin to move.

A little longer.

They open their eyes.

One after the other.

Grandmother looks and whispers into the light, Water or fire will see to it.

Ann, woken again, sets her bare feet on the floorboards, her nails painted dark burgundy. Like mine. We paint them for each other now, no longer for our men, who are far away. They have to be far away; I do not let them come close to the illness. I belong to the illness, not to them. There is room here only for Ann. She can sink such painted nails into manure or compost, wallow in it.

Sisters in decay, I think of us.

Ann takes a pack of Klubowe from the pocket of Grandfather’s coat she has put on.

Come, she says to me.

I throw a blanket around myself and go. Grandmother is watching the lights running across the ceiling.

Perhaps, I think, she has fallen asleep again, but left her gaze awake. She is most frightening then, when she looks in such a way that you cannot tell whom her eyes belong to.

We go out onto the veranda. I feel the cold.

A concert, I say to Ann.

She hands me a lit cigarette.

It’s beginning, I say.

Yes, she says.

Oh-ho, there it is already.

We listen.

Hooves know no rhythm, I say.

After a while the animals begin to sing.

They’re singing, I say.

Ann presses a finger to my lips and with that gesture keeps the cigarette smoke inside me.

The animals are singing. Their song comes from inside, from warm bellies, I think.

After a while the dogs begin. First Rybowicz’s wolf-spitz below the escarpment on the other side of the village, then the throats of the slaughterhouse dogs start up, after a moment Dunaj joins in, after him the headman’s wife’s Samoyeds and the dogs chained by the rectory kennels, descendants of those restless mutts that have been crossbreeding for generations on the village margins. They bear the names of apostles: Marek, Jan, Łukasz. There is also one black one the village christened Murzyn.

Strange, I think, that I can tell these howls apart, the melody of each dog. Another little while here and I will be able to tell what these howls are for, what they serve.

Ann takes her finger from my lips.

I say, You know, I always wanted to be the bitch of this village, to have all of Maj to myself. To know no limits. I was tempted by being far from what is human. To have a dog’s body and act in accordance with it, give in to hunger, deny myself nothing. To fawn only on the chosen, nip at and bark at everything else. To be a choosy bitch. To love one human being, have duties toward him, listen to him. And one more thing: to disappear for many days. To have a right to that, to make use of it.

To make use of it, Ann repeats.

The barking carries and wakes more sounds.

The whole village howls.

That night this place is the deep throat of a hungry animal, I think.


Faith


At night the slaughterhouse wakes the birds. It has consequences for the valley. Time gets mixed up. Something in time loosens. People open their eyes at the wrong hour, move through the day dazed. At noon they look into the sun and cannot make sense of where in the sky it is.

Grandfather gets mixed up most of all. Grandfather rises with the birds. Wakefulness catches him by surprise.

I wake up and it’s empty, he tells me about such times.

Empty? I ask.

Empty, yes, there’s nothing to catch hold of, nothing to recognize yourself in, he says.

He gets up at night and walks through the house. He clambers up the ladder to the attic and paces there. Putting one foot after the other calms him. Stroking the antlers he has been collecting for decades calms him too. When Grandfather’s night and day get mixed up, he can fall asleep there.

In the mornings, when I go to take my place at the desk and work on code, clean, intuitive, beautiful, the kind that sometimes comes to me in dreams, I often find him among the deer antlers. Grandfather has a story for each set. He makes up lives for those growths of bone. If you want him to tell one, you have to know how to approach him, you have to praise the collection in the attic. The stories about deer are full of animal panic, full of forest; they take place in rain and fog. Only out of weather like that do animals come out in Grandfather’s stories.


Rivers


Waking birds is a sin, says Grandmother.

In the orchard belonging to the slaughterhouse, nothing has been born for years. The ground there is trampled by herds of cows. Most of the animals are unloaded at the top of the hill and led straight into the concrete walls. Those there is no room for in the cool pens are let into the orchard. They make use of it, empty their deep bellies, piss as if they had endless rivers inside them. Urine in such quantities burns the ground; it is hard for the trees to grow there.

The orchard is bare, stunted, sick.

It’s because of the wetness inside the cows, I say.

Fire or water will see to it, says Grandmother.

I believe her.

The greengage trees in that orchard have not borne fruit for years, she says.

I nod.


Sweet


Grandmother cannot resist the living. Everything alive she would take into the west room, invite in. She would like the living to pass before her eyes, to move, scuttle, flutter.

Let it parade, she says.

Let it fly, she says.

Let it be, she says.

Grandmother has adored the living always, that is, for as long as I can remember. One could, I think, trace the generations of what has multiplied here, in the western part of the house. And if one asked those latest generations, the living ones, where they came from, then probably, if they could talk, they would say: from the cool room of a human, a little human, Róża.

She wishes, I think, for the living, especially now, to occupy her mind, give rise to duties, distract her from the illness.

Grandmother studies the living with curiosity. That fondness—as I call it, that weakness—as Grandfather calls it—has made her useless many times.

You were afraid to take Róża out into the fields, Grandfather begins, reminding me at the same time of that childhood time.
