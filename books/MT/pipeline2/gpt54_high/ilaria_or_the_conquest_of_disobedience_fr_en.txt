May 1980

At eight, I love the feel of my upper body suspended over empty air, of my bent knees hooked over the metal. I love the moment when I squeeze my eyes tight shut, let go, and let the vertigo pass through me. When my hands are flat on the black asphalt, it means I’ve gone beyond my fear. And then the image of my favourite gymnast, Nadia Comăneci, comes to me. Her arms are flung wide. Victory.

I spend recess hanging upside down by my knees, waiting for Ana, my sister. This morning, as she was leaving, she said, See you here, and on time, okay? Otherwise I’m going home by myself. “Here” means at the bottom of the stairs, near the metal rail that separates the car park from the school playground.

Ilaria! Get down from there! We’re going to Chez Léon. Come on, hurry up!

I recognise Papa’s voice. Startled, I lift the skirt of my dress that is blocking my view. Yes, it’s the tips of his shoes, yes, it’s his impatient tone. I flip myself upright, land on my feet, smooth my dress.

Ana will be here any minute.

No, no. Plans have changed. Maman is picking her up from school and we’ll all meet at Chez Léon. Come on!

I take his hand. It’s damp.

Since our parents separated and Papa moved to Turin, we get together once a month in a restaurant. It was Maman’s idea. She prefers neutral ground. She says that at home they fight too loudly. And it’s true that at Chez Léon they restrain themselves. Even if Papa clenches his jaw and Maman gazes into the distance with an indifferent look.

No, Papa still hasn’t found a job. When he says no-no-work, his voice is sad, tired. Maman turns her head slightly to hide her smile and Papa gets angry. He keeps repeating the word humiliation. Luckily the waiter arrives and sets down plates of fillets of perch or dishes of meringue with whipped cream. Thank you.

After dessert, Ana and I leave the table and go down to the little beach to pick out pebbles. We practise skipping them.

Did you see?

What?

Papa took Maman’s hand.

To get to Chez Léon, we go through the village of Hermance, cross the Franco-Swiss border and continue along the road to Yvoire. Papa has a navy-blue BMW 320 coupé.

If you see a phone booth, tell me. He lights a cigarette. There! He stops, gets out of the car and takes coins from his trouser pocket. His back pressed against the glass, the folds of his shirt make Vs and Ws. I wait, roll down the window, let some air in. The leather seat no longer burns the backs of my thighs; when I stroke it with the palm of my hand, it even feels soft.

Inside the booth, Papa talks loudly, raises his voice, turns around. Our eyes meet. He smiles, lowers his eyes. From the way he moves his hands, I can tell he’s agitated. His body is stiff. It worries me. When he comes back, he says Maman has changed her mind and no longer has time for lunch. We’ll spend the weekend together. And school? You’ll only miss a few days of school... It’s not such a big deal.

Papa’s voice is sharp. I count on my fingertips: Thursday, Friday, Saturday, Sunday. Four days. And Ana? I’d like to protest, but when Papa is nervous, it’s better to keep quiet.

He starts up abruptly and crushes out his cigarette with a sharp movement. His forehead is covered in sweat.

Mont Blanc Tunnel, Franco-Italian border, the tunnel vaults, the hairpin bends of the Aosta Valley, carsickness. We stop under a sky blanketed in grey. The landscape is metallic. I throw up at the side of the road; Papa hands me a white cotton handkerchief. Let’s go and have something to drink, it’ll do you good. A few kilometres farther on, at the service-station bar, Papa’s face is pale. It must be because of the neon lights. He buys two slices of Margherita pizza, a whisky, a coffee and a lemonade from the cashier. I hate lemonade, but I say nothing; my mouth is dry.

Do you have any tokens for the phone you can sell me?

How many?

About twenty.

The cashier carefully counts out the yellowish tokens and hands them to Papa.

The booth is outside, to the left.

Her nails are very long and painted a very red colour. I follow Papa.

What are those tokens?

I need them to make calls. In Italy, you can’t use coins in phone booths.

Between Geneva and Turin, Papa makes several calls. Five in all. Every time he sees a service station, he stops. Are you happy to be spending the weekend with me? Have you lost your tongue? What are you thinking about? Nothing.

Turin. We drive around for a long time looking for a parking space, and when Papa shoves open the door of his building with his shoulder, he looks relieved. I run up the stairs. What floor is it on? The fourth. On the landing, he looks for his keys. You’re going to see your room. Are you happy? I say yes. Yes, I’m happy. Listen, Ilaria, he says, turning the key in the lock, I have to introduce you to someone I haven’t told you about yet, a certain Geneviève. Who is she? The housekeeper. Papa opens the door and points to an inanimate mannequin, dressed in a black dress with a white lace collar and a hat. He bursts out laughing. Geneviève’s face is white Styrofoam; it has no eyes. Come on! Your room is at the back, past the bathroom.

Impatient to see it, I cast a quick glance into the other rooms and switch on the light in my room. The space is crowded with two beds and two chests of drawers. I move closer to the Topolino poster. Mickey looks at me with eyes like tunnels. Papa stays in the doorway.

I haven’t yet found any nice bedside lamps. We can buy some together tomorrow, can’t we? If you want. Running my hand over the fabric, I ask him if these are the sheets from Florence.

You remember?

I think so, yes. When was Florence?

Two years ago.

The pattern of orange sunflowers stands out on the sheets. It brings back the blurred images I keep from that time. Two years, and yet I have the impression it’s been centuries since Maman, Ana and I moved to Switzerland.

The next day we wake early and go for a giro, a drive around Turin by car. Papa tells me about the kingdom of FIAT, the workers’ strikes, the arrival of immigrants from the South. He says the people of Turin hate them.

Through the windscreen he points out the palazzi, the Po, the Basilica of Superga, the statues. You see those kids over there between the cars? They’re posteggiatori. That’s how they make money, helping people park.

As we walk under the porticoes of Corso Vittorio Emanuele, Papa holds my hand tight, his always damp, and I don’t dare pull mine away until, in front of the window of the toyshop Al Sogno, I let go.

The display looks like a zoo of harmless animals. Even the snakes look kind. Papa follows my gaze. Is it the teddy bear you’re looking at? Come on, let’s buy it. The shop is full of wooden toys, mobiles, dolls in glittering dresses. I am spellbound. And for Ana? What shall we get her? Yes, a doll... Ana adores them. The one I choose has very long eyelashes, and when you lay her down her eyelids close. While the saleswoman wraps it in pretty gift paper, Papa holds out the teddy bear to me. What shall we call him? How about Birillo? Yes. I look at Birillo and take to him at once—his shiny milk-chocolate eyes, his cream-coloured belly, his soft fur. Papa pinches my cheek between his forefinger and middle finger, his eyes all soft.

That gesture on my cheek is like his signature. He will repeat it for two years, and I will end up hating it. Are you happy?

At eight, I am a taciturn, docile child, rather skinny. I don’t complain when Papa leaves me alone in the flat to go and make his phone calls downstairs at Carmelo’s bar. He doesn’t have a phone at home. I’ll be right back, he always promises, just two minutes. Alone, lying on my bed, I draw and stick Sarah Kay pictures into my new album. Papa bought it for me at the kiosk. And when I’ve had enough of waiting for him, I know where to find him: at the counter. My landmark is the big coffee machine that gives off a smell of burnt caramel.

At Carmelo’s, the local workers come in for a sandwich at noon or a drink before going home. They all have their names and their habits. Una birra alla spina per Emilio e due tramezzini per Marco. Gianna is the boss. She presses the big keys of the till very fast. Sei mila lire. Perched on her little platform, she smiles, hands over the scontrini, the receipts, the cigarette packets, and when there’s no one there, she sweeps the floor. Carmelo, meanwhile, polishes the counter, serves coffees, dries glasses with a tea towel, levels the sugar in the sugar bowls.

When I step into the café, the atmosphere is lively. The workers are singing in chorus along with a song on the radio. Vorrei essere libero, libero come un uomo. To reach the counter, I slip between them and clamber onto the high stool. Listen, listen! It’s a song by Giorgio Gaber, La libertà. Carmelo laughs and deep inside his mouth I see metallic spots. How many cavities? I don’t have time to count. Looking for Fulvio? He points to the phone booth with his hand covered in soap bubbles.

And how was your day? Very well. Tomorrow we’re going back to Geneva. You’ll come back soon, won’t you? Carmelo’s smile is gentle. I’m going to be very sad not to see you anymore. Promise? I promise.

Later Papa appears. His legs are all stiff. With a funny look on his face, he bends down to my ear. We’re leaving tonight. His breath is like a draft. Hurry up. We have to go upstairs and pack our bags.

Say goodbye to Carmelo and Gianna properly, and thank them.

In the back seat of the car, I can hardly breathe. Where are we going? To the seaside. And Ana? And Maman? When do we see them again? Stop sniffling. We’ll be back in Geneva in a month. A month isn’t long. And Promotions? You told me we’d only be spending the weekend together... You can go next year. Stop with the questions now.

The image of the pretty dress Maman and I chose for the parade comes to me. I think of Sarah, my close friend, of my teacher, of the little flag we cobbled together in class, of Ana. My head feels heavy. I pull my knees up under my chin and press Birillo tight against me. My sleep is cold, restless.

I want to hear your voice. STOP. The little one is fine. STOP. She would like to speak to you. STOP. I’m waiting for you. STOP. Wait for me.

Your husband

Here! Put that in the glove compartment.

Now we stop not only at phone booths but at post offices as well. Papa sends whole stacks of telegrams and keeps a copy of them. I don’t dare read them when he hands them to me, but curiosity gets the better of me and I slow my movements down to catch a few words.

Zzaaak, zzzaaak. A family, a man on his own, a woman. Two white shocks of hair crawl along at thirty kilometres an hour in the right-hand lane. Papa gets impatient, lets go of the wheel, lights a cigarette. Come on! Let me through. He slaloms between the vehicles. Are you scared? A little. He notices my hand clenched on the door handle. The traffic at the entrance to Genova is heavy, the tunnels numerous. Shadow-light, shadow-light. The lanes are full of cars and long lorries. I’ve had enough of this road. Get the map out, Ilaria.

Genova, Milano, Brescia, Alessandria. Yes, Brescia, then we’ll go to Alessandria. With the tip of my finger I follow our route. But there’s no sea in Alessandria! It doesn’t matter. I’m showing you your country. Papa’s tone is playful. He almost gives me a wink.

Papa shifts down and takes the exit for the service station.

I need to phone, go to the bar.

No, I’m staying here.

While I wait for him, I hop round the booth on one foot. Papa slips the tokens into the slot of the telephone, lights a cigarette. Pronto? The conversation drags on.

Sitting in the car, I leave the door open and swing my legs out into the air. The sky is covered with white streaks, the clouds are like balls of wool. It’s muggy, I’m thirsty and hot. Other cars pull in, drive off, back up in the car park. Kids run around. Careful, look before you cross! their mothers shout. The families passing by seem perfect to me, washed, combed, ironed, headed somewhere definite. Where are we going?

Roads, phone booths, post offices, little hotels, bars. The days pile up. We often stop at Autogrills to fill up with petrol, eat a sandwich, go to the toilet. I like those places. Inside, everything is colourful: piles of sweets, baskets of food wrapped in crackly paper, bins of music cassettes, games, soft toys. And while I wander through the aisles, Papa talks to customers at the bar.

He loves talking.

It’s stronger than he is, he has to talk to someone. Anyone.

And he’s very good at striking up a conversation. It always starts with a remark about the traffic or the weather or a joke. Papa knows how to put even the shyest people at ease. He looks at home in the way he leans on the counter, in the way he talks to the barman. When he clowns around, smiling faces gather around him in no time. I prefer seeing him like that, happy, eyes laughing, rather than having to listen to his theories about people.

Yes, yes, we’re going. Always one last one. He drinks very fast, and when he reaches for his glass, I’m sure that even if he were blind he would have the same sureness. His lips would tremble in just the same way. He gives me the impression he’s afraid he won’t have time to finish his whisky.

One day he told me it was medicine for him, and pinched my cheek.

Say thank you. I thank the barman, say goodbye to everyone.

On the road again. That’s how we leave the counters, just like that, abruptly.
