Chapter 1

It was as I say. He tripped on his cane. I don’t know why he looked back. No one was coming down behind him. Of course I recognized him. Though he had changed past all recognition; after so many years, it’s hard not to. I gave no sign, however, that I knew him. Maybe that was why he thought I didn’t. I acted as if he were just one of those people forever coming down or going up those stairs. I even wondered whether I shouldn’t warn him not to go down into that old wild green valley, because it is no longer there. Old people like to visit places that are no longer there, and no one even knows whether they ever were. I don’t understand what longing pulls them there so powerfully. Unfortunately, the longings of old age can be dangerous. If only one could reach that valley on level ground, perhaps I would understand him. Only you can’t get to any valley on the level; that is the nature of valleys. To come down those stairs at his age, with a cane, when the legs refuse obedience and the eyes fail to agree not only about the world but about every step you place before you, was like imagining in youth that instead of arms you had wings and could float down into that valley.

I wasn’t expecting him, and yet there he was. A tremor ran through me, I shrank into myself, and at that moment I wanted to be neither here nor anywhere else. Feeling out the first step with his cane, he set one foot on it uncertainly, then dared place the other. With the same labor he descended the next, and the next. Helpless, yet stubborn, I thought. Why are old people so stubborn? Can it be they begrudge those who will go on living after them?

He halted on every step for a shorter or longer moment, looking down at his feet as if unsure whether these were indeed the stairs he had always gone down and up, or whether his memory was failing him. At his age memory sometimes exempts itself from remembering, since it burdens a person with duties he is tired enough of already, having lived so many years. He felt at each step below with his cane, making sure he could descend safely. First he placed his left foot on the step, then his right. And with the same caution, lowering the right foot to the left, he went down to the next step. The cane he held in his right hand struck me as unreliable; it shook in his hand whenever he touched a step with it.

I was afraid his head would begin to spin if he kept looking down at his feet on every step that way. I wondered whether I should move out of his way, or rather let him pass when he reached the Needle’s Eye.

He wasn’t surprised when he saw me; perhaps he had even expected to find me here ahead of him, before he came this way from the park through town. He stopped on the step above me and, without looking me in the eye, said:

“I stopped in at the park once more, but it was the last time. I don’t know whether you remember that sometimes she used to come home from school through the park. I sat down on a bench and decided to wait. A Gypsy woman went by, a baby at her breast. Do you think she stopped as she did then? Of course not. She didn’t even ask whether I wanted my fortune told, though the baby whimpered as she passed me. Clearly she decided that my future was not ahead of me but behind me, and she wasn’t about to add years to my life—how could she. I had already been sitting there a long time; I was just about to get up and go through town, because perhaps she too would be coming home through town today, if she wanted to buy herself something, when some little old woman sat down beside me, asking if she could.

“‘Unless you’re waiting for someone?’

“‘No, please,’ I said. ‘Once I used to wait, but that was long ago.’

“‘Long ago, not long ago—if you’ve loved, you go on waiting.’

“‘And you—have you loved?’

“‘What a question. Is there anyone who has never loved? You’ve lived quite a few years, I can see, like me, and you don’t know that? I love him to this day, though probably to no purpose now. It pains me that I never told him.’

“‘And why didn’t you?’

“‘I was ill, and the doctors didn’t give me long to live, so I didn’t want to burden him with my love. Especially since he left after his school-leaving exams. I didn’t even know where he’d gone, and by now he may not even be alive. Would you have told her, if you weren’t sure you yourself would live?’

“‘I don’t know, especially after so many years. Perhaps I too wouldn’t have wanted to burden her with the remorse of abandoning my love, if it were her turn to die.’

“‘Does that mean you loved someone?’

“‘I love her to this day too, though I’m not even sure she’s alive. But if she were, I don’t think I’d tell her even now, if we met, because she wouldn’t recognize me and I wouldn’t recognize her. We are no longer who we were in our youth.’

“‘If you love, you’re always the same. If only a person knew that in youth, he’d live differently. As it is, he wastes more than one thing. Most often love. Well, I’d better be going. I have to feed the cats.’

“‘Do you have many of them?’

“‘It varies. However many happen to gather.’

“‘I’ll walk you home.’

“‘No, thank you. I don’t live far. And where are you going? Perhaps I should see you home? You’ve got a cane, and I, thank God, am still on my own two feet.’

“‘I’ll manage. I live much closer than you do.’”

He tested the next step with his cane, but as though he didn’t quite trust the cane, he slid its tip right and left across the step before setting his foot down. I wanted to ask him why, in that case, he was going down into that old wild green valley, but he forestalled me:

“I feel sorry for you, that you still have to go on living. But perhaps you’ll have more luck. I wish it for you. Though in my opinion wishes have never yet done anything for anyone.” He went down to the next step, testing it with the cane in the same way. “The truth is, every life is a repeating of someone else’s life. The past gets ahead of us—you must know that—and we drag ourselves after it. For who could ever keep up with his own life?” He paused on the next step. “Ah, how many legs must have gone this way. Have you noticed how worn these steps are? It takes centuries. Who knows whether they aren’t still going this way. Maybe I’ll go along with them. What, does that surprise you? It’s always heartening to be in a crowd.” Feeling for the step below with his cane, he swayed, but as though someone had held him up. “When I was in high school… though what am I going to tell you for? You’ll live through it yourself, because you have to. Without that, you couldn’t ask yourself whether it was worth it. Every question we ask ourselves in life comes down to that one question, whether it was worth it. Not at your age, of course. Years later, only years later. Now you wouldn’t know what answer to give yourself. Just remember that youth can mislead for a long time. Don’t let it fool you. One day it may suddenly turn out that you were young many years ago. Unfortunately. Forgive these bitter words, but they’re all I have left. In return, I give you my life. It may come in useful when emptiness engulfs you. Emptiness is the worst thing that can happen to us. And the only remedy for it is to live in someone else’s life. Besides, no one begins his own life with himself. You don’t understand that yet. But in time, when you begin to visit it… I’d like to know what your impressions will be. Mine were better and worse, as with life. Once I ran away from my childhood, but perhaps you’ll never want to leave yours. It happens. Still, I’ll tell you this: it will do you no good. Life is wandering after yourself without any hope that you’ll ever find yourself. Well then, enough, or they’ll pass me by. And there are still so many steps.”

So I moved aside for him. And then the cane slipped from his hand and rolled down, and he lurched and went after it. He fell all the way to the road at the foot of the steps, striking his head on the cobbles. As it happened, no one was coming down or up, so no one saw. I rushed after him. I tried to lift him in some mindless hope that perhaps he was still alive. But he was heavy. Tall in life, and after death limp, which made him heavier still. Then I heard someone’s voice above me:

“Leave him. The ambulance is already on its way.”

So I laid his bloody head back on the cobbles, and only then did I see that several people were standing nearby. Where they had suddenly come from, I don’t know. Perhaps it’s always like that at accidents, people seem to grow out of the ground or swoop down from the sky, but when he was falling down those stairs I could have sworn there wasn’t a living soul there. So there’s no point listening to people, because no one saw that he tripped on his cane. They can say all sorts of things, and each will say something different anyway, as always when nobody wants to be a witness. And as it later turned out, nobody did. The man who had told me to leave him was at that moment recounting a similar incident, pointing to a house not far away:

“There, in that house. Two friends, knew nothing about guns, found a revolver from wartime. And because it was rusty, even the trigger wouldn’t pull, so one of them started banging the barrel on the table. Well, it went off and shot the other one in the belly. He collapsed, and the first one started trying to lift him up, I think he even straightened him out. The doctor said afterward that if he hadn’t been lifting him, he’d have lived.”

At that moment his words were cut short by the ambulance arriving with its siren on. Two orderlies jumped out with a stretcher, along with a doctor, who pulled back the dead man’s eyelids and, just to be on the safe side, put a stethoscope to his chest before pronouncing him dead. Right after the ambulance, also with siren blaring, the militia arrived. Two uniformed men got out of the car, one an officer with stars on his shoulder boards, the other younger, with stripes. After them a third man clambered out from the back seat, so fat he looked almost comic in uniform.

The officer began asking the people standing around the body what each of them had seen and how it had happened, and the younger one, with the stripes, wrote it down, though he had nothing to write, because though they all talked over one another, no one had seen anything. One of them had seen only me running down the steps when the body was already lying on the cobbles. And the one who had forbidden me to lift him had come up just as I was trying to raise him, and said:

“Leave him.”

He had already begun saying, “There, in that house…” but the officer cut him off:

“Did anyone know him?”

Someone began to wonder aloud that maybe, if he’d seen him alive… and the officer waved a hand.

“Search his pockets,” he said curtly.

The fat one tried to bend over the body but couldn’t manage it, so he knelt down and pulled a wallet from the inner pocket of the man’s jacket, handed it to the younger one, and he, without looking inside, passed it to the officer. The wallet had a great many compartments, but apart from money and a photograph of some young girl there was nothing in it, not even so much as, say, a laundry receipt.

“No identity card?” the officer said in astonishment. “Search him again, thoroughly, maybe it’s somewhere.” “Still none?” he said, even more astonished.

“A citizen without an identity card is as good as if he didn’t exist,” said the fat one.

“The body’s here, so the card ought to be too,” said the younger one with the stripes. “Without an identity card, this body too is as good as if it didn’t exist. And how are we supposed to conduct an investigation?”

“Maybe it fell out and is under the body. Lift the body,” the officer ordered.

The orderlies lifted the body, but under it there was only a broad stain of blood.

“Put him back. Just as he was.”

“And the girl in the photograph?” The junior man leaned over the open wallet in the officer’s hands. “Pretty girl.”

The officer nodded.

“Maybe his daughter. Someone would be more likely to recognize her. Shall I show it round?” He took the photograph from the officer’s hand and held it under everyone’s eyes.

They shrugged that they didn’t recognize her. Only one man said:

“It must be old. Scalloped edges. They don’t make them like that now. I’ve got similar ones from my grandparents.”

“Well? And you?” he said to me. “Do you recognize her?”

Yes, it was her. I recognized her. But I didn’t admit it. And so that not even the shadow of suspicion would fall on me, I drew the officer’s attention to the cane:

“Maybe someone would recognize him by the cane. Canes grow attached to people. And nearly everyone carries a different one. One of the teachers at the high school…”

The officer didn’t let me finish. He looked at me distrustfully, virtually pinning his gaze to me.

“We know well enough what to identify people by. Leave that to us, please.” And he ordered, “Search for that cane too, just in case, and put the money in safekeeping. And chalk round the body. For all we know he may be some tourist; he may have left his papers with a guide, in case he got lost.”

They outlined the body in chalk, the ambulance drove off with it, and the younger one and the fat one began looking for the cane. That outline of his body traced in white chalk on the cobbles, with the stain of blood where his head had struck, seemed to me as though it were still breathing his breath, as though it were even whispering something in his words, and that was more affecting than when the body itself had lain there, before the orderlies took it away.

“We’ll have to drive around town and question every coach that’s parked anywhere,” the officer ordered, when despite searching on the steps and around them they still hadn’t found the cane. “It’s Saturday, people have poured in here today. They park below the castle, they park by the granary, they park in the New Town—wherever they park, question them. Luckily there’s only one hotel, question that too. And question the private lodgings as well, get a list from the town hall, they have to register after all.” He took off his cap and scratched his head, as if his thoughts had made it itch. “We ought to put up a notice too, saying: male, aged—only we don’t know how old he was, we didn’t find any papers. Damn it, we didn’t even bring a camera, we could have taken his picture.”

“How were we supposed to know he’d killed himself?” the fat one cut in. “They reported an accident, and an accident could mean a broken arm or leg. Haven’t enough arms and legs been broken on those blasted stairs?”

“We could hang up the photograph,” said the one with the stripes. “Just not here, better in town, or afterward people will be afraid to walk this way, thinking it’s haunted. Though with a photograph of a corpse, who’d admit to knowing him? Better the daughter’s. Someone would be more likely to recognize the daughter.”

“And how do you know it’s his daughter and not his granddaughter?” the officer rebuked him. The other one blinked, as if the thought had never occurred to him.

“So it seems you’re our only witness,” the officer said, turning to me. And as if hesitating, he added, “Perhaps even the only point from which the investigation can proceed, since we have neither the dead man’s identity card, nor a photograph, nor any other witnesses. You’ll come with us. We’ll take down a statement.”

At the station he sat me at a desk opposite him, asked whether I’d have some tea, and lifted the telephone receiver.

“Two teas,” he ordered. “Do you take sugar? Then I’ll have mine sweetened today too. And sugar. Aha—and send Bronka in to type.”

A woman thin as a stick, who turned out to be the cleaner, brought in two glasses of tea, sugar in some tin box that had once held sweets, and a single teaspoon. He sweetened his, stirred it, then handed the spoon to me.

“I really ought to bring a teaspoon from home. Those aluminum ones break, and there are hardly any left in the whole station,” he said, as though by way of apology for the single spoon.

Bronka came in too, gave me a contemptuous glance, sat down at the typewriter, and spread some women’s magazine out in front of her.

“Before you’ve finished your tea, I’ll look through what’s being worn this autumn.”

“Write. What’s tea got to do with an interrogation?”

“How should I know.”

He waited until I had at least taken that first sip—he himself was already on what must have been his third—then he pushed himself back from the desk in his chair, settled his back against the rest, laced his fingers together, evidently relaxing, and I think he stretched his legs under the desk as well, because he touched me.

“Well then, let’s hear it.”

“What am I to say?”

“What you know.”

“I don’t know anything more than what I’ve said.”

“Oh, you do, you do, we already know you know. Everyone under questioning says the same: first name, last name, date of birth, and that he knows nothing more. Experience teaches us, however, that a person doesn’t himself know what he knows unless someone helps him. There are various forms that help can take. But I use ordinary conversation, so to speak, a kind of enlightening conversation. I proceed on the assumption that everyone has to discover for himself what he knows. So?”

Apparently I still hadn’t understood anything, because I decided to remind him what he had said to me when he took me to the station:

“Excuse me, but there’s some misunderstanding. I was only supposed to be a witness.”

“True, I don’t deny it, you’re still a witness. Only the sole one. We have no other witnesses with whom we could confront you, to confirm your credibility. And when nothing is certain, the question arises whether witness is your only role in this event, or whether some other one might also be ascribed to you. For the moment I don’t know what, but we must try to clarify that. The two of us. So I would like you to understand that I have to question you in different roles. Besides, the boundaries between roles are fluid. Circumstances determine them. To find a comparison, the liquid state can pass either into gas or into solid, depending on temperature. Water, steam, ice. The same, and not the same. We cannot permit ourselves helplessness. That would disqualify us. We would be unnecessary. So sometimes we have to investigate right up to the point of pain. Truth, as you know, commands the highest price, and even after years it is never marked down. I wouldn’t advise you to count on that. Truth has no statute of limitations, no matter how long a case may drag on. If only we had a second witness. But in your case that could only be the deceased. Unfortunately we can’t question him. Though it would be interesting to know what he had to say. We can only infer it. And that is not quite so groundless. The search for a trail usually begins with surmise, since hardly anyone can be caught in the act. Take, for example, all those betrayals by husbands and wives. Rarely does one catch the other, as they say, in flagrante. Most betrayals begin with surmise. And only surmise makes you look for proof. So it’s not impossible that the deceased would testify against you, whereas you testify that he had only himself to blame because he tripped on his cane. Only where is that cane? We looked for it, you saw us. If it were there, we’d have found it. A cane has no wings to fly away.”

He laughed, but in such a way that it chilled me.

“All right, we’ll look again. Meanwhile tell me which hand he held it in, left or right, when, as you say, he tripped over it. You don’t remember. Hardly surprising. Does it surprise you that such a detail could matter? Then let me tell you that a detail can tell us more than we might suspect. A mere detail can overturn all testimony, contradict all witnesses. A detail is a cosmos. The world may be large, but it is details that decide whether someone met someone here and not there, that an apple fell from an apple tree and Newton discovered the law of gravity, that the bacteria cultivated by Fleming were unexpectedly killed by some fungus and penicillin was invented that way. And so on, and so on. Have you written that down, Bronka? Then throw that sheet away.”

He dragged the questioning on, and I must say that now and then he surprised me with his perceptiveness:

“You said no one was coming down after him, yes? And yet he looked back. Perhaps the sun struck him—the light was sharp today—and seeing nothing, he turned his head and then you, let us say, wanted to help him down to the lower step?”

More than once he broke off the questioning halfway through a sentence and went out, as though giving me time to think. Or he would sink into thought and then tell Bronka to strike something out, though it had seemed important to me. Clearly his thoughts were following another trail. And at times it was almost as if he wanted to soften the questioning and give it a tone closer to confession, because he slipped in something about himself: that he had two children, a son and a daughter, by his first wife, who had died, and had had to raise them himself.

“You know how it is. Service is service.”

He had married again, and now they were expecting a child. Sometimes he asked me questions that seemed on the face of it to have nothing to do with the case:

“Is your father alive? Your mother? You’re young, but anything can happen. I barely remember my own mother, she died of tuberculosis. My father went two years ago.”

And then suddenly he caught me off guard again:

“When did you pass your school-leaving exams? And why didn’t you go on to university?”

“They didn’t take me.”

“What, you failed the entrance exam?”

“I passed.”

“How’s that? You passed and they still didn’t take you? That too ought to be looked into. Interesting. And what do you do now?”

“I work.”

“Where?”

“At the fruit and vegetable processing plant. Jams, preserves, juices.”

“We know that plant. We conducted an investigation there once: five freight cars of cherries stood unloaded in barrels on a siding until they rotted. And where do you live?”

“With my parents.”

“And why did you come here? Had you arranged to meet him?”

“Whom?”

“Why, the deceased.”

“I didn’t know him.”

“You didn’t know him, and yet you recognized him. That’s what you said, that you recognized him. Or perhaps he only thought you didn’t. Well, we can’t ask him. What puzzles me, though, is that if it was a chance meeting, why did you meet precisely in the Needle’s Eye and not in the street, in the park, in a café, restaurant, anywhere, as happens in chance meetings? Think it over. I’ll be back in a moment.” And he went out.

Oh, he would not be easy, I thought. But I couldn’t very well tell him that I had been waiting for her, and he had appeared unexpectedly. I had even thought he was already dead, since a year had passed from the time when, according to the Gypsy’s prophecy, he ought to have been dead. He would have asked me straightaway: and who is she, first name, last name, where does she live? And did she come? What do you mean, you knew she wouldn’t come and you were waiting for her? You wait for someone who may be late or may even not come that day because something unforeseen has held her up, but she’ll come another time.

He came back not long after, and from the doorway threw me an impatient question:

“So? Do you have anything to tell me? Let us assume, though, that you didn’t know him. We’ll follow another trail. You saw that he was going down, yes? Going down where?”

“Down.”

“Well of course, one goes down, not up.” He sat behind the desk, staring into my eyes with a strange intensity. “Down, you say? And how was he supposed to get back up, if he could scarcely get down at all? Didn’t you think of that? At his age, and with a cane.”

“I don’t know. Perhaps he had no intention of coming back up.”

“He had no intention, you say. And how do you know he had no intention?”

“I suppose.”

“Then I must inform you that your suppositions too may constitute evidence. And we may use them against you, regardless of the fact that you are supposing in favor of your own innocence. Everything has two sides, every thought and every word. Nothing is unambiguous, much as it probably seems to you. Unambiguity is an illusion. The fact that you are only supposing gives you away; it tells us that something lies behind it. And that is what matters most, what lies behind something. True, you’ve protected yourself with that ‘perhaps.’ But people sometimes throw in a ‘perhaps’ like that, though they never do it for nothing. Words rule us; you should know that. You don’t need school-leaving exams for that. Often a man would rather not say a certain word, and it says itself. So then? Because something here doesn’t hang together.” He fell silent, as though waiting for my answer.

What unsettled me was that he went on being silent, staring at me and saying nothing. In those eyes fixed on me, it seemed to me I could see a question he would like to ask, only he didn’t know whether he might not entangle himself in something if he did. I was afraid of that question and waited tensely for when he would ask it. He didn’t. He only cleared his throat:

“Hmm…” And for a long while that was the only sound that came from his mouth. After a moment he cleared his throat again. “Hmm…”

That “hmm” made me shrink into myself. You never know what may be hidden in such a “hmm,” even an accusation for which there is as yet no proof, but in which there already sounds the conviction that proof will be found, if not sooner then later. Later it may even be easier to find. Investigative techniques will improve, methods of examination will be refined, new apparatus will appear that will do the reading itself, without asking questions. And without Bronka, because nothing will need to be written down; the suspect will simply appear on a screen. If he has a soul, his soul too will lie there in plain view. Patience: what recedes comes nearer too. And meanwhile there’s no harm in dreaming. In the meantime the suspect too will grow older, experience more than one thing, understand more than one thing, perhaps one day he will even come of his own free will and confess, no need to arrest him, to go anywhere, to trail anything. People will sit behind desks and receive the guilty as if they were filing applications for benefits or housing. If only one could get at consciences, because as things are consciences rarely open of their own accord, and if they do, only in old age. Perhaps instead of a militia there will be only offices, bureaus, departments, and salaries will go up as well. The militia doesn’t do especially well, particularly with the young. The young are the hardest material to interrogate—they twist, they wriggle, they deny, codes and paragraphs mean nothing to them, they think youth entitles them to everything. They make nothing of the militia. Well then, they’ll see, when we become the world’s signpost.

At that moment some older officer with one more star came in and laid a note on his desk.

“Have you got him yet?” he asked, glancing at me.

“Almost.”

“What, is he that tough? Maybe pass him on to Edek.”

“No, I can manage.”

“As you like.”

After he left, the officer cast a glance at the note the other man had brought, put it aside with pent-up fury, and with that same fury rounded on Bronka:

“Leave that magazine, Bronka! Write!”

“I’ve been writing all along,” Bronka said indignantly. “Only there’s nothing to write.”

He bored into me with that same furious stare, as if expecting at least my hands to begin shaking.

“Let’s get back to the cane.”

“‘Let’s get back to the cane,’” Bronka repeated.

“What did the cane look like?”

“‘What did the cane look like?’”

“Stop reading back to me what you’re writing, for God’s sake!” He was plainly furious now. “Well then, what did the cane look like?”

“Ordinary. Like a cane.”

“Exactly. What color was it?”

“Black, I think.”

“You think, or black?”

“It could have been black.”

“‘Could have been’ is not an answer. You don’t seem to understand that you’re being questioned in connection with—who knows—possibly even a murder. It could just as easily be that you stuck out a leg as that he caught his foot on the cane. Because so far we haven’t found any cane.”

“Black,” I said, a little frightened. It seemed to me he was slowly beginning to work his way into my thoughts.

“All right, so we have the color. And then?”

“The rest of it was black too.”

“Are you making fun of me? And here I am trying to give you a chance.”

No, I wasn’t making fun of him, I wouldn’t have dared. Perhaps it was a stupid thing to say, but my head had suddenly gone muddled when, in his anger, he dropped the polite form and started speaking to me as if I were something less, and who knew what lay behind that.

“And the handle?”

“What about the handle?”

“Well, what shape was it? They come in all sorts. We had one fellow here, young too, like you, who walked with a cane whose handle was shaped like a snake’s head.”

“No, semicircular. Smooth.”

“And what else did it have?”

“It had a non-slip rubber tip on the end.”

“So he couldn’t have slipped, because it held him. And he fell?”

“Maybe precisely because it held him and he wanted to go down lower. And he caught on it.”

He looked at me as if thinking, You’re a sly one, brother, but you won’t lead me astray.

“All right. Let’s leave the cane alone until we find it.”

He began drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desk, as though wondering whether after all he shouldn’t pass me on to that Edek the higher-ranking officer with the extra star had proposed, the one who had brought him the note. I didn’t know who Edek was, the name was like any other, but the very notion of passing me on to some Edek struck me as threatening. Until then he had been mild, at times almost friendly; I had even had the impression that it pained him to have to question me. And fear swept over me. He may not have agreed to that Edek, but perhaps some Edek sat inside him too, since he had said he could manage.

“You do know, I hope, that a confession is a mitigating circumstance,” he said, with weariness in his voice. I think he even yawned.

“But what am I supposed to confess to?”

“That’s not my business. I’m not the one who is supposed to confess. Truth be told, anyone would have something to confess to. Even if we started dragging in the first people off the street for questioning. And perhaps one day that’s how it will be. For now our scope of action is limited.”

He stopped drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desk, switched to the left, and as though with a fresh influx of energy drummed louder and faster, and my fear rose right into my throat.

“Oh—and I forgot to say that on the cane, just below the handle, there was a gold plaque. And on it an engraved inscription…”

“Gold, you say,” he cut me off. He picked up the receiver. “Send Gruby in. You see? You have to keep digging until it hurts.” He stopped drumming his fingers and even seemed to smile at me. “That would solve the case for us.”

Gruby appeared in the doorway.

“What is it, chief?”

“Go and look for that cane. There’s a gold plaque on it.”

“If it’s gold, no point looking, no one’s going to find it now.” And he began spinning out his doubts. “Gold’s worth a lot these days. Trade’s banned, so of course it flourishes. How many gold rubles and dollars we’ve confiscated already, and they still keep selling. How many we’ve locked up, and what of it? They even steal from churches and melt it down into rubles and dollars.”

“Don’t talk shit at me. Go. That’s an order.”

“Yes, sir.” Gruby clicked his heels so hard he almost lost his balance. Plainly his body was too heavy for his thin legs.

“I don’t smoke, otherwise I’d gladly have a cigarette,” he said, seeming a little deflated. He took out a comb and ran it through his hair. It was thick and black, though already graying at the temples. “I used to smoke. But my first wife said, quit, the children are small; the second says, quit, I’m pregnant, do you want the child born crippled? Well then, we’ll wait until they bring in the cane. You can’t begrudge time when you’re trying to get at the truth.”

And time dragged on endlessly, not only for me, for him as well, because once and then again he went out, leaving me alone with Bronka, who, taking advantage of the chance, leafed through that women’s magazine every time he left and didn’t say so much as half a word. I asked her whether she had any idea how long this might last.

“Could, could,” she said, without taking her eyes off the magazine. “If they don’t finish by night, they’ll start again in the morning. I’ve only filled one page so far. With Edek I’d have five by now. Oh, Edek doesn’t mess about. He’ll hit, he’ll squeeze, if somebody doesn’t tell the truth. And nobody does. You’re wriggling too, the way I hear you. And everything you say sounds a bit off somehow. Edek would squeeze it out of you. Edek can bend horseshoes. He lifts me like a feather, right up to the ceiling. A person doesn’t get bored with Edek when he’s questioning. Next to Edek just about everyone here is a dwarf. Everybody spills everything to Edek. That’s where you get to know life. You won’t read in a book what you learn from Edek. No one says as much in confession as they do to Edek. If Edek got hold of you, you’d learn more than one thing about yourselves too. Edek wouldn’t let you sit here all this time, like this one. Good man and not stupid, all the same, but half the time he doesn’t know what he wants. He keeps asking and asking, and there’s nothing to write. And he gave you tea. Edek wouldn’t give you a drop of water. He kept calling you ‘sir’ until you got on his nerves. With Edek the questioning would be over by now and I could go home. But with this one, who knows. He told them to go look for the cane, and what will the cane tell him. It’s starting to get dark. If you confessed, maybe he’d get promoted, because he’s been waiting a long time for them to add another star. He’d make more. Two children from the other wife to raise, and a third on the way with this one. And it would be easier on you too, if you got it off your chest.”

But what was he supposed to confess to, for it to be easier on him? Nothing had ever been easier for him since he let a Gypsy tell his fortune, and she foretold a long life for him, which meant nothing to him; but when he heard that today she would not be coming this way home from school through the park, that she would go through town because she had something to buy, hooks or snap fasteners, he almost flew off the bench.

At that hour the park was usually empty; besides, it was small, the sort of little town-center park there is, and what was more it had been badly thinned out by the almost apocalyptic storm that had broken over the town a few years earlier and was still remembered with dread. So it no longer had the same popularity as before. There was no way to hide there from human eyes, and so hardly anyone made assignations there now. Young people preferred the copse down by the river, and the old, held back by their grief for the old park, no longer cared to stroll there or sit on the benches. Once, at this season, the trees rang with birdsong; now something only rarely disturbed the silence, so he was sure he would hear her footsteps as she came closer.

He narrowed his eyes against the glaring sun, because in that silence no one would escape him. He heard every footstep from far away and didn’t have to open his eyes each time to make sure whether it was her or not. Besides, he knew her steps by heart, as though they only lightly touched the ground—you might have thought she was walking in sandals, although she always wore heels, she couldn’t bear flat shoes. She set her feet down as if from the toes, not the heels; he would have known those steps even in starless dark.

So he gave himself over to imagination behind those half-closed lids, especially since he didn’t expect her until the last lesson was over. He was one lesson ahead of her, because he had slipped out of Latin in order to be waiting for her in the park when she came home, and most often she came home through the park. He had slipped away more than once, not only from Latin, which he disliked, but from physics and mathematics too, subjects in which he was among the best pupils. When he left, as she did, after the last bell, it sometimes happened that he lost her among the several classes all coming out at once. There were several parallel classes, and each was large, so when lessons ended a crowd poured out. And when he did manage to find her, she was almost always already walking with someone. Oh, there were many, from the older classes too, lying in wait for the chance to walk just that little stretch with her or escort her all the way home.

What he might have been dreaming of is hardly worth recalling, since dreams at that age are rather commonplace and universally known, so anyone can draw on his own. Then he heard approaching footsteps, or rather a rustle as of bare feet. No, those were not her steps, he would have known at once. These were unhurried, furtive almost, so he would not have let go of his dreams if, very close by, almost within arm’s reach, a child had not suddenly whimpered. He flung open his eyelids so abruptly that the brightness of the sun blinded him, and in that brightness he saw before him a Gypsy woman with an infant at her breast.

She was directly opposite the bench on which he was sitting and plainly would have gone on, since nothing in her steps suggested that she meant to stop by him. Experience had evidently told her there was no point telling the fortune of someone like him; she wouldn’t squeeze so much as a single zloty out of him. If he had any money at all, he was keeping it for the cinema or the circus. But what sort of girl would let him invite her to the cinema or the circus? The prettier ones look around for those who have a future. And what future could he have? Suppose he even lived a long time—does a long life guarantee a future? Pale, scrawny, hollowed in on himself, perhaps even ill, only he doesn’t know it yet. The only thing she could have foretold him was that he would live a long life, because there is no way to verify that except by living a long time.

So perhaps it was no accident that just as she had drawn level with him and was about to take the next step, her baby whimpered, because the nipple had slipped from its mouth; she pressed it back in and stifled the crying. But she had to stop for a moment, and then her eyes met his. Only briefly, because his gaze chilled her and she lowered her eyes to the head of the suckling infant, even stroked it tenderly. She was practiced in taming people’s distrust of her, even their contempt, but his eyes struck her as beyond his young years. A certain sad radiance came from them. She had never yet seen so much suffering in young eyes; often you saw not so much even in old ones. Best proof of it was that he had opened them only when he heard her baby cry. Could it be that he had inherited that suffering from someone? After all, everyone is an unending inheritance. Yet, having stopped beside him, she could hardly go away without a word. Her baby’s cry had already been that first word to him. So, as though continuing that cry, she said:

“Let me tell your fortune and I’ll say whether she’ll be coming back this way.”

And without waiting for his consent, she perched on the bench. With a practiced movement she drew her right breast out from under her blouse and shifted the infant to it; taken from one nipple, the baby whimpered again, but had no time to burst into full crying because it already had the second nipple in its mouth.

“When something hurts, the best thing is to let your fortune be told. There has not yet been born anyone so wise he doesn’t believe things will get better. Fortunes know how to console, and they take hope from no one. They can change more than one thing as well. And he has his whole life ahead of him. If he’d just give something for the rattle, I’d foretell his whole life.”

And without waiting for his agreement or refusal, she freed her left arm from under the shawl which, tied at neck and waist, supported the tiny creature she carried at her breast.

“Give me your hand,” she said. “Not that one, the other.”

And she drew that hand onto her lap, took it in both of hers, turned it palm up and began tracing it with her forefinger.

“He’s got a long life line. Oh, long. From here to here.” But he did not even look to see how long. “He’ll live and live.” She named the age, which fell exactly last year. “Go on, look, from here to here. If he had a bigger hand, he’d live even longer. But even so, it’s long. He’ll live plenty.” She must have noticed the distrust in his eyes, because she curled his palm in and almost fearfully whispered: “There’s another line here too, the heart line. A Gypsy doesn’t make mistakes, a Gypsy always tells the truth.”

But that second line meant nothing to him either. A longer life or a shorter one meant nothing to him. He was immersed in that one moment for which he was waiting as he sat in the park beside the main avenue. For that moment he would have given the longest life. There are moments which, like the sun in a lens, focus an entire life into a single point, burning their mark in us forever. Who knows whether it isn’t for such moments alone that one lives, not for however many years.

He was hardly listening to what she was foretelling him, because in his thoughts he was just leaving school with her after the last lesson, out through the doors of the building, then through the gate from the schoolyard, crossing to the other side of the street, walking a little way along the pavement, then turning toward the church, he was already by the church, in a moment he would enter the park, he was already in the park, so that her footsteps heading toward him ought to reach him any second. It already seemed to him he could hear them, still very faint, she must be somewhere at the start of the side path, but they were her steps, how could he not know them, when suddenly the Gypsy woman’s words struck him like a club to the head:

“She won’t be coming back this way today. She meant to come this way, but then remembered she has something to buy, hooks, snap fasteners, so she’ll go through town.”

She had not finished when he tore his hand from hers, leapt up, and ran toward town.

“And won’t he give anything for the rattle?” she shouted after him, so loudly that the nipple slipped from the baby’s mouth and it burst out crying.

He turned back, fished some coin from his pocket, and tossed it into her lap.

“You won’t buy a rattle with that!” she shouted, disappointed. “May he be cursed!”

If the Gypsy woman was telling the truth, that she would be coming home through town, then the way to her house led down the steps through the Needle’s Eye into that old wild green valley which separated the hill on which the town lay from the opposite, much lower one, where her house stood.
