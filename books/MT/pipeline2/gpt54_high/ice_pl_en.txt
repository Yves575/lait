Ice

We do not freeze.

I


WARSAW


“If a soldier fires a rifle, then at that very moment the wound the bullet will bore into the enemy some time later begins to exist; if someone takes a dose of an incurable poison, then at that very moment the final mortal process begins to exist, even if its hour does not come until several days later. The ancients spoke truly: we died the moment we were born. We have no power over the further course of the bullet and the poison (and, in part, over the process of living ourselves out).”

CHAPTER ONE

Of the Thousand-Ruble Son


On 14 July 1924, when the officials from the Ministry of Winter came for me—on the evening of that day, on the eve of the Siberiad—it was only then that I began to suspect that I did not exist.

Under the eiderdown, under three blankets and an old gabardine coat, in flannel long johns and a wool sweater, in socks pulled over socks—only my feet stuck out from beneath the eiderdown and blankets—after some fifteen hours of sleep, at last thawed out, curled almost into a ball, with my head shoved under a pillow in a thick case, so that even sounds reached me softened now, warmed, dipped in wax, and, like ants mired in resin, made their way inward slowly and with enormous effort, through sleep and through the pillow, millimeter by millimeter, word by word:

“Gospodin Venedikt Yeroslavsky.”

“Him.”

“Asleep?”

“Asleep, Ivan Ivanovich.”

One voice and another, the first low and hoarse, the second low and sing-song; before I raised blanket and eyelid, I could already see them leaning over me, the hoarse one at my head, the sing-song one at my feet, my tsarist angels.

“We have awakened young master Venedikt,” Ivan observed when I raised the other eyelid. He nodded to Biernatowa; the landlady meekly left the room.

Ivan drew up a stool and sat down; he kept his knees together, with a black bowler hat of narrow brim resting on them. The high vatermörder, white as snow in the noon sun, hurt my eyes, that white vatermörder and the white office cuffs, dazzling against the uniform black of their clothes. I blinked.

“By your leave, Venedikt Filippovich.”

And they took it.

The other man perched at the foot of the bed, dragging the eiderdown down with his weight until I had to let it go; grabbing at the blankets in turn, I hauled myself up on the pallet, and in so doing uncovered my back as well, cold air pushing under sweater and long johns, and I shivered, fully awake.

I threw the coat over my shoulders and drew my knees up under my chin.

They looked at me with amusement.

“How is your health?”

I cleared my throat. In it had gathered the night’s phlegm, the corrosive acid of everything in my stomach—garlic sausage, gherkins, whatever else we had swallowed yesterday, warm cornelian-cherry liqueur and cigarettes, endless cigarettes. I leaned toward the wall and spat into the spittoon. It doubled me over. Bent double, I coughed heavily for a long while. I wiped my mouth on the torn sleeve of my coat.

“Like a horse’s.”

“That’s good, that’s good—we were afraid you wouldn’t get out of bed.”

I got up. My wallet was lying on the windowsill, shoved behind a flowerpot with a dead geranium. I took out my paper and thrust it under Ivan’s nose. He did not so much as glance at it.

“But really, gospodin Jerosławski! What do you take us for, common constables?” He straightened himself on that stool even more; I had thought it impossible, but straighter still he went, and now the walls seemed crooked, the wardrobe hunchbacked, the doorframe scoliotic. Offended, the functionary lifted his chin and thrust out his chest. “We are asking you most politely to come with us to Miodowa, for a little tea and some sweets. The commissioner always has sorbets, little cakes, cream horns brought in, straight from Semadeni’s, a regular debauch of the palate, if I may put it so, eh, Kirill?”

“You may indeed, Ivan Ivanovich, most certainly,” sang Kirill.

Ivan Ivanovich had great drooping mustaches, thickly pomaded and curled upward; Kirill, by contrast, was shaved smooth all over. Ivan took from the pocket of his waistcoat an onion watch on a tangled chain and announced that it was five minutes to five. Commissioner Preiss set great store by punctuality, and what time was he to go out to supper? He was meeting the major-general at the French.

Kirill offered Ivan snuff, Ivan offered Kirill a papirosa, and they watched me dress. I splashed icy water from the basin over my face. The stove tiles were cold. I turned up the lamp wick. The room’s only window looked onto a cramped inner courtyard, and the panes were so overgrown with grime and frost that even at noon very little sunlight seeped through them. When I shaved—when I still shaved—I had to set the lamp before the mirror with the flame turned up full. Zyga gave up the razor as soon as he arrived in Warsaw; he grew a beard fit for a priest. I glanced at his bed on the far side of the stove. On Mondays he has lectures; he must have got up at dawn. On Zygmunt’s bed lay the officials’ black fur coats, their gloves, a cane, and a scarf. The table, meanwhile, was crammed to the edge with dirty dishes, bottles (empty), books, journals, notebooks; Zyga dried his socks and underwear by hanging them from the edge of the tabletop, weighed down with atlases of anatomy and Latin dictionaries. And in the middle of the table, on the well-thumbed, grease-stained copy of Riemann’s Über die Hypothesen welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen and on a heap of yellowed Kuryer Warszawski kept for kindling, for plugging cracks pried open by frost, for drying boots, and for wrapping sandwiches—there rose a double row of candles and candle-ends, the ruins of a stearin Parthenon. Against the wall opposite the stove, by contrast, neat piles of volumes in hard covers were stacked up, arranged by size and thickness, and by frequency of use. Hanging above them on the smoke-blackened wall, the little shield-icon of Our Lady of Ostra Brama—the only remnant of the previous tenants, whom Biernatowa had thrown out onto the street for “indecent conduct”—had blackened completely and now looked more like a piece of medieval armor for Lilliputians. Ivan studied it for a long time, straining hard, seated stiffly on his stool, his left hand with the cigarette held out to the side at a forty-five-degree angle from his body, his right laid on his thigh beside the bowler, brow and nose wrinkled, mustache bristling—and then I understood that he was almost blind, that he was an office myope; on the bridge of his nose and under his eye sockets there were marks from his pince-nez, and without it he had to rely on Kirill. They had come straight in from the frost, and Ivan must have taken his glasses off. My own eyes water here sometimes. The air inside the tenement is thick, heavy, rubbed through with every odor of human and animal bodies; nobody opens the windows, doors are shut at once and the gaps above the thresholds stuffed with rags so that the heat should not escape the building—for fuel must be paid for, after all, and anyone with enough money for coal would not be nesting in such dark back courtyards, where the air is thick, heavy, you breathe it as if you were drinking water spat out by your neighbor and his dog, every breath you take has passed a million times before through the tubercular lungs of peasants, Jews, carters, butchers, and whores, coughed out of black throats it comes back to you again and again, strained through their saliva and mucus, run through mildewed, louse-ridden, suppurating bodies, they have coughed it, blown it from their noses, vomited it straight into your mouth, you must swallow, you must breathe, breathe!

“E-excuse me.”

Luckily the privy at the end of the corridor was empty just then. I vomited into the hole, from which an icy stench blew into my face. Cockroaches crawled out from under the shit-smeared board. I crushed them with my thumb when they came up under my chin.

Coming back out into the corridor, I saw Kirill standing in the doorway of the room—he had his eye on me, standing guard in case I should run out into the frost in my long johns and sweater. I smiled at him conspiratorially. He handed me a handkerchief and pointed to my left cheek. I wiped it. When I tried to give it back, he stepped away. I smiled a second time. I have a wide mouth; it smiles very easily.

I put on the only clothes I had fit to go out in, meaning the black suit in which I had taken my final examinations; if not for the layers of underwear beneath it, it would have hung on me now like on a skeleton. The officials watched as I laced my boots, buttoned my waistcoat, wrestled with the stiff celluloid collar pinned to my last cotton shirt. I took my documents and what money remained, three rubles and forty-two kopecks—that would make for only a token bribe, but with empty pockets a man feels naked in an office. There was nothing I could do about the old sheepskin coat, though: patches, stains, crooked seams, and no other to wear. They watched in silence as I forced my arms into the uneven sleeves, the left one longer. I smiled apologetically. Kirill licked his pencil and meticulously noted something on his cuff.

We went out. Biernatowa had evidently been spying through a half-open door—she immediately appeared beside the officials, flushed and chattering away, to lead them back down the stairs from the second floor and through both well-courtyards to the main gate, where Walenty the porter, having straightened his cap with its brass badge and pocketed his pipe, hurriedly swept the snow off the pavement and helped the officials into the sleigh, taking the gentlemen by the elbow so they would not slip on the ice-glazed sidewalk, while Biernatowa, once they were seated and wrapping rugs round their legs, assailed them with streams of complaints about malicious tenants, about the gangs of Powiśle thieves who break into houses even in broad daylight, and about the cruel frosts that warp windows damp from within and burst pipes in the walls, so that no plumbing or sewerage can long hold out underground; finally she fervently assured them that she had long suspected me of various offenses and abominations, and would certainly have reported me to the proper authorities if not for the thousand and one other worries piled upon her head—until the driver, from his box behind Kirill’s back, cracked his whip and the horses jerked the sleigh to the left, forcing the woman to step aside, and so we set out for the Warsaw delegation of the Ministry of Winter, to the former Palace of the Bishops of Kraków, 5 Miodowa, corner of Senatorska.

Before we turned from Koszykowa onto Marszałkowska, snow began to drift down; I pulled my shapka over my ears. The officials in their voluminous furs and bowlers like nutshells, seated on the sleigh’s low benches, Ivan beside me, Kirill facing backward toward the driver, reminded me of the beetles I had seen in one of Zygmunt’s textbooks: thick oval torsos, short little legs, a small head, all of it gloss-black, enclosed in the geometric symmetry of ellipses and circles. A shape so close to the ideal of the sphere cuts itself out of the world. They stared before themselves with expressionless eyes, lips tightly pressed and chins held high by stiff collars, passively surrendering to the sleigh’s motion. I thought I might learn something from them on the way. I thought they would begin hinting for gratuities in exchange for goodwill, for a lack of haste and zeal. They said nothing. Ask them—but how? about what? They would pretend not to hear. Flakes of sticky snow swirled between us. I hid my cold hands in the sleeves of my sheepskin coat.

The French Confectionery was lit up, electric glare beating through the great windows and knitting woolly halos around the silhouettes of passersby. The summer sun ought still to have been far up in the sky, but as usual heavy clouds hung over the city; they had even lit the streetlamps—very tall ones, with tops curled in spirals. We turned north. Girls in red coats and white hooded capes came running out of Ostrowski’s confectionery at the corner of Piękna, and for a moment their laughter pierced the street din. I remembered the unfinished letter to Miss Julia and her last shouted question. Next to Ostrowski’s, at Wedel’s, Fredek, Kiwajs, and I used to arrange evenings of cards. Right here, just behind the Sokół cinema, at Kalka’s, the Dear Prince rented a room for night sessions. If I had lifted my head and looked left, above Ivan’s bowler, I would have seen the second-floor window of the building at number 71, the window from which Fredek fell.

At the intersection with Nowogrodzka, a fat cow hung frozen to a lamppost; a tendon of dark ice joined it to the top of the facade of a four-story building. The cow must have come from the last cattle drive to the slaughterhouse in Ochota; the wintermen had not yet chopped it down. Down the perspective of the street, above the roof of the Sphinx building, there loomed a blue-black nest of ice, a great clot of frost hard as diamond, joined by a network of icy threads, icicles, spans, and columns to the buildings on both sides of Marszałkowska and Złota—to the buildings, the lamps, the stumps of frozen trees, balcony railings, bay windows, the spires of domes and turrets, parapets, and chimneys. The Sphinx cinema, of course, had long been closed; no lights burned on the top floors.

The sleigh slowed once we had passed Nowogrodzka. The driver pointed with his whip. The cart ahead of us was pulling up onto the pavement. Kirill looked back over his shoulder. I leaned right. At the intersection with Aleje Jerozolimskie stood two policemen, using whistles and shouts to drive traffic from the middle of the street—for above the roadway a lute was just then freezing its way across.

For several minutes we sat stuck in the jam caused by it. Usually lutes move over the rooftops, coming down to ground more rarely in cities. Even from that distance it seemed to me I could feel the waves of cold pouring off it. I shivered and instinctively tucked my chin into the collar of my sheepskin coat. The officials of the Ministry of Winter exchanged glances. Ivan looked at his watch. On the other side of the street, behind an advertising pillar plastered with posters for a wrestling match at the circus on Okólnik, a man dressed in English fashion was setting up an archaic camera to photograph the lute; most likely the photograph would never appear in the newspaper anyway, confiscated by the men from Miodowa. Ivan and Kirill did not so much as look at him.

The lute was exceptionally lively; before dusk it should manage to get to the other side of Marszałkowska, overnight it would climb above the roofs, by Friday it would make it to the nest above the picture house. When last year a froster crossed from Praga to the Castle by way of the Aleksandrya Bridge, the bridge was shut for nearly two months. Whereas this glacius here—wait a quarter of an hour and I might well have been able to make out its movement, how it froze from place to place, shifted in ice, by ice, from ice to ice, how behind it one crystalline thread snapped, then another, and slowly sifted down a bluish-white crumble, a minute, kshtr, two minutes, kshtr, the wind carrying off with the snow the lighter particles, while most of them froze into the black sheet icing over the street slush behind the lute, ice of ice; and that track of rough frost, like the trail of a snail’s slime, stretched several dozen meters eastward along Jerozolimskie, over pavement and over the hotel facade. The rest had already been chopped away by the wintermen or had thawed loose of itself; yesterday afternoon the thermometer at Szniccer’s showed five degrees above zero.

The lute moved neither in a straight line nor at a constant height above the cobbles (they freeze their way below ground as well). Three or four hours earlier, to judge by the shattered architecture of ice, the lute had begun to change its trajectory: until then it had been moving barely a meter above the middle of the street, but then, three hours ago, it started sharply upward in a steep parabola, above the tops of the lamps and the crowns of the frozen trees. I could see the row of slender stalagmites it had left behind; they shone with reflected lamplight, with glints from colored neon, with lights shining through windows and shopfronts. The row broke off above the tram rails—the lute hung with all its weight on a starry network of frost-strings, stretched out horizontally and reaching upward toward the facades of the corner buildings. You could have walked beneath it, if anyone mad enough could have been found.

Ivan nodded to Kirill, and Kirill clambered out of the sleigh with a reluctant grimace on his face, ruddy from the stinging frost. Maybe I’ll get lucky, I thought, maybe we’ll be late, Commissioner Preiss will already have gone out to the supper he arranged with the major-general, and they’ll send me packing from Miodowa. Thank You, God, for this icicle monstrosity. I shifted along the bench, resting my shoulder against the side of the sleigh. A newsboy ran up—“Hirohito beaten!” “Special Express, Mierzow triumphant!”—I shook my head. Whenever traffic snarls in the center, crowds gather at once, and street hawkers appear, sellers of cigarettes and holy water, holy fire. The policemen were driving passersby away from the lute, but they could hardly watch everyone. A gang of urchins sneaked up from the direction of Briesemeister’s restaurant. The boldest of them, his face muffled in a scarf and his hands in thick shapeless mittens, ran to within a dozen or so paces of the lute and threw a cat at it. The great beast flew in a high arc, paws spread, yowling its head off... then the hideous screaming stopped short. It hit the lute probably already dead, only to roll slowly off it into the snow, frozen to the bone: an ice sculpture of a cat with splayed limbs and a tail straight as wire. The boys ran off, howling with delight. From the doorway of Epstein’s jewelry shop a Jew with sidelocks shook his fist after them, swearing ferociously in Yiddish.

Meanwhile Kirill had reached the older policeman and, taking him by the elbow so he would not run off in pursuit of the street boys, began trying to persuade him of something in a lowered voice, though with conspicuous help from the sweeping gestures of his other hand. The constable kept turning his head away, shrugging, scratching the top of his skull. The younger of the pair was shouting to his companion, move, help! Out on the Avenues two sleighs had locked runners, causing even greater uproar, carts were mounting the pavements, pedestrians, cursing in Polish, Russian, German, and Yiddish, were scattering from wheels and hooves, in front of the wine merchant’s a matron of the dimensions of a Danzig wardrobe had gone down on the frozen mud, three gentlemen were trying to lift her, a potbellied officer hurried over to help, and so the four of them hoisted her all together, on one—down she went—on two—down she went—on three—half the street was already splitting with laughter, while the woman, red as a cherry, was squealing shrilly, kicking her fat little legs in tiny boots... Small wonder that only at the shriek of torn metal and the crack of breaking wood did we look back toward the intersection. A motorcar had collided with a coalman’s wagon; one horse had gone down, one wheel had come off. The policeman shoved Kirill away and ran for the crash. Trapped inside the covered vehicle, the motorist began blaring the alarm horn; on top of that something went off under the car’s hood, as though a double-barreled shotgun had fired. That was already too much for the gray horse harnessed to the sleigh beside it. Startled, it lunged forward, straight toward the lute. The driver grabbed at the reins, but the horse itself must have felt what wall of frost it had run into—for with even greater force it lashed out to the side, spinning the sleigh around in place. Did the runner catch on the curb? Did the gray slip on the black sheet of ice? I was already standing up in the ministerial sleigh, Ivan beside me, watching the accident over the row of vehicles before us, but it was all happening too fast, too unexpectedly, too much movement, shouting, lights and shadows. Down went the gray, down went the sleigh it was pulling, off tumbled the load, a dozen or so squat bottles in baskets of sawdust, baskets and bottles rolled into the middle of the intersection, some of them must have shattered, for a gleaming celadon liquid spread over the ice—kerosene, I thought—and already up leapt the fire, from what, an electric spark from the motorcar, a dropped cigarette, the strike of an iron shoe on the cobbles, I do not know. Blue flame danced across the whole width of the puddle, high, higher and higher, a meter, a meter and a half upward—almost reaching the lute frozen into the overhead web.

The photographer, bent over his apparatus, was slowly, methodically exposing plate after plate. And what would he later see on them, what would be preserved on the glass and printed on paper: snow—snow—pale halos of streetlamps—dark mud, dark cobbles, dark sky—the gray facades of tenements in the perspective of the broad ravine of the city—in the foreground the chaos of the angular shapes of vehicles trapped in the jam—from among them and from among the human silhouettes bursts the blaze of pure fire, so bright that the plate seems at that point wholly unexposed—and above it, above the flame of a whiteness whiter than white, at the heart of the hanging arabesque of ice, the lute spreads itself, the lute, a massive thunderbolt of frost, a starfish of rime, a living hearth of cold, lute, lute, lute above the women’s fur hats, lute above the caps and bowlers of men, lute above the horses’ heads and carriage hoods, lute above the neon signs of cafés and salons, shops and hotels, confectioneries and fruiterers, lute above Marszałkowska and Aleje Jerozolimskie, lute above Warsaw, lute above the Russian Empire.

Later, as we drove to Saxon Square and along Królewska, past the Garden dead beneath long years of frost and the colonnade hung with icicles, past the tower and the sobor on Saxon Square buried under overhangs of snow, toward Krakowskie Przedmieście, that image—the afterimage of image and imagination—visited me again and again, an importunate memory of unclear meaning, a sight seen and not understood.

The officials exchanged grumbling remarks under their breath, the driver shouted at inattentive pedestrians; the snowstorm had slackened, but it was growing colder and colder, my breath was freezing on my lips and hanging before my face as a white cloud, the sweating horses moved in clouds of sticky damp—the Royal Castle was drawing nearer. Before we turned into Miodowa I saw it above Sigismund’s Column: the Castle locked in a block of shadowy ice—and above it the great nest of lutes. The violet-black clot rose halfway up the roofs of the Old Town. On clear days you can see waves of frost standing in the air around the Great Tower. There is no scale on any thermometer to measure that cold. At bonfires on the edge of Castle Square, gendarmes keep watch. When a lute freezes out of the nest, they close the streets. The governor-general had once established a cordon here of dragoons from the Fourteenth Little Russian Regiment, but the regiment had meanwhile been shipped out to the Japanese front.

The roof of the Palace of the Bishops of Kraków, however, remained free of any ice growth. In the wing on the Senatorska side there were still elegant shops—electric spotlights illuminated the signs of Nikolai Shelekhov’s Exclusive Delicatessen and the teas of Sergei Bazylovich Perlov’s Moscow Trading House—but the main wing on Miodowa, beneath its rococo cresting and between pilasters capped with Corinthian capitals, belonged to the Ministry of Winter. Above both carriage gates hung black double-headed eagles beneath Romanov crowns, inlaid with onyx tungetite.

We drove into the inner courtyard; the sleigh runners grated on the cobbles. The officials got out first; Ivan vanished at once through the door, setting his pince-nez on his nose; Kirill stood on the steps before the threshold and looked back at me. I opened my mouth. He raised an eyebrow. I lowered my eyes. We went in.

A hall porter took my sheepskin coat and shapka, and the doorman slid a huge ledger toward me, in which I had to enter my name in two places, the pen slipping from my stiffened fingers—would the great gentleman like it entered for him, no, I, I’ll do it myself. Even the illiterate rabble visit the corridors of Winter’s high offices.

Everything here shone with cleanliness: marble, parquet, glass and crystal and iridescent coldiron. Kirill led me up the main staircase and through two secretariats. On the walls, beneath portraits of Nicholas II Alexandrovich and Piotr Rappacki, hung sunny landscapes of steppe and forest, of Petersburg in spring and Moscow in summer, from the days when spring and summer still had access to them. The staff did not lift their heads from their desks, but I saw how the advisers, clerks, office men, and copyists furtively followed me with their eyes and then exchanged crooked glances among themselves. When does official business end? The Ministry of Winter never sleeps.

Extraordinary Commissioner W. W. Preiss occupied a spacious office with an antique stove and an unused fireplace, the tall windows looking out onto Miodowa and Castle Square. When I entered, passing Ivan in the doorway—he had evidently already announced me—Mr. Commissioner was bustling about at the samovar, his back turned. He himself had the figure of a samovar, a bulbous pear-shaped torso and a little bald head. He moved with frenetic energy, hands fluttering over the table, feet never ceasing in their dance, one little step left, one little step right—I was sure he was humming under his breath, smiling to himself under his nose, merry little eyes gazing out at the world from his rosy face, no crease in the smooth brow of the Commissioner of Winter. Meanwhile, since he did not turn around, I stood by the door with my hands clasped behind my back and let the warm air fill my lungs, wash over my skin, thaw the blood stiffened in my veins. The office was almost hot, the great brightly painted majolica stove never cooled for a moment, and by the end the windowpanes had fogged so thoroughly that through them I saw chiefly blurred rainbows of streetlights strangely running and merging on the glass. It is a matter of great political importance that the Ministry of Winter never be cold.

“Well then, why aren’t you sitting down, Venedikt Filippovich? Sit down, sit down.”

Rosy face, merry little eyes.

I sat.

With a resounding sigh he dropped into his chair on the far side of the desk, cradling in his hands a cup of steaming tea. (He did not offer me any.) He had not been serving here long, the desk was not his, behind it he looked like a child playing minister, he would certainly replace the piece. They must have sent him only just now, sent him, the tsar’s extraordinary commissioner—from where? Petersburg, Moscow, Yekaterinburg, Siberia?

I drew a deeper breath.

“Your Nobility, if you would permit me... am I under arrest?”

“Under arrest? Under arrest? How could such a thought ever have arisen in your head?”

“Your officials—”

“My officials!”

“Had I received a summons, I certainly would have come on my own—”

“Did they not invite you politely, Mr. Gierosławski?” He at last pronounced the name correctly.

“I thought—”

“My God! Under arrest!”

He was panting.

I laced my fingers over my knee. It is worse than I thought. They are not going to throw me into prison. A high tsarist official wants to talk to me.

He began taking papers out of the desk. He took out a thick wad of rubles. He took out seals. Sweat ran down me under my underwear.

“Yesss.” Preiss slurped his tea loudly. “Please accept my condolences.”

“I beg your pardon?”

“Your mother died last year, did she not?”

“Yes, in April.”

“You were left alone. That is sad. A man without a family is like... well, he is alone. That is bad, very bad.” He turned one page, slurped, turned the next.

“I have a brother,” I muttered.

“Yes, yes, a brother on the other side of the world. Where did he go off to, Brazil?”

“Peru.”

“Peru! What does he do there?”

“He builds churches.”

“Churches! He must write often.”

“Well, more often than I do.”

“That is nice. He misses you.”

“I suppose.”

“You do not?”

“Him?”

“Your family. When did you last hear from your father?” Page, page, slurp.

Father. I knew it. What else could this be about?

“We do not correspond, if that is what you are asking.”

“That is dreadful, dreadful. You are not interested in whether he is even alive?”

“Is he?”

“Ah! Is Filip Filippovich Gierosławski alive! Is he alive!” He all but sprang up from behind his operatic desk. By the wall stood a great globe on an airy stand of wet coldiron, and on the wall hung a map of Asia and Europe; he set the globe spinning and smacked the map with the back of his hand.

When he looked back at me, not the slightest trace of his recent good cheer remained on his pudgy face; dark stones regarded me with clinical attention.

“Is he alive,” he whispered.

He picked up yellowed papers from the desk.

“Filip Gierosławski, son of Filip, born in the year eighteen hundred and seventy-eight in Wilkówka, in the Kingdom of Prussia, in East Prussia, Lidzbark district, from the year nineteen hundred and five a Russian subject, husband of Eulagia, father of Bolesław, Benedykt, and Emilia, sentenced in the year nineteen hundred and seven to death for participation in a conspiracy against the life of His Imperial Majesty and in armed rebellion; nu, by way of clemency the sentence was commuted to fifteen years’ katorga with loss of estate rights and sequestration of property. In the year nineteen hundred and seventeen the remainder of the sentence was remitted, with a prikaz restricting residence to the bounds of the Amur and Irkutsk governor-generalships. He did not write? Never?”

“To his mother. Perhaps. At first.”

“But now? Recently? Since seventeen. At all?”

I shrugged.

“Surely you yourselves know perfectly well when and to whom he writes.”

“Don’t be insolent, young man!”

I smiled faintly.

“I’m sorry.”

For a long while he studied me. On the ring finger of his left hand he wore a ring set with some dark stone in a mounting of precious tungetite, engraved with Winter’s emblem; he tapped the ring on the desktop, truk, trukk, every other tap harder.

“You graduated from the Imperial University. What do you do now?”

“I was preparing for my rigorosum—”

“What do you live on?”

“I give mathematics lessons.”

“And do you make much from these lessons?”

Since I was already smiling, all that remained was for me to lower my eyes to my clenched hands.

“A lot, not a lot.”

“You are a frequent visitor to moneylenders, every Jew on Nalewki knows you. You owe Abiezer Blumstein alone more than three hundred rubles. Three hundred rubles! Is that true?”

“Once Your Nobility tells me in what matter I am being interrogated, it will be easier for me to confess.”

Truk, trukk, truk, trukk.

“Or perhaps you really have committed some crime, eh, if you are sweating here from fear like this?”

“Your Nobility might be pleased to open the windows.”

He stood over me; he hardly had to bend at all to speak straight into my ear, first in a whisper, then in a soldier’s growl, ending almost in a shout.

“You’re a gambler, my Benedykt, a compulsive card-player. Whatever you win, you lose—whatever you earn, you lose—whatever you borrow, you lose—whatever you beg from your friends, you lose—you have no friends anymore—you have nothing anymore, and even that you lose, you lose everything. Twenty-one, baccarat, zimucha, poker, any way will do. Once you won half a sawmill—you lost it that very night. You have to lose, you cannot get up from the table until you have lost so completely that nobody wants to play with you anymore. Nobody wants to play with you anymore, Venedikt Filippovich. Nobody wants to lend anymore. You have already pawned the next two years. Bolesław doesn’t write to you, you write to him, you beg him for money, but he sends no more. You don’t write to your father because your father has no money. You wanted to marry, but your would-be father-in-law set the dogs on you when you pawned and lost your fiancée’s dowry. If only you’d at least put a bullet through your head, as befits a nobleman, but you won’t, ptui, what sort of nobleman are you, you scum!”

I smiled apologetically.

Commissioner Preiss caught his breath, then gave me a friendly pat on the shoulder.

“Now then, don’t be afraid, we know how to deal even with scum. Never mind that your own father matters to you about as much as the Emperor of Japan’s gout—what matters to you is a thousand rubles! Isn’t that so? A thousand rubles matters to you like... well, it matters to you very much. We’ll give you a thousand, and after that perhaps a second, if you acquit yourself well. You will go and visit your father.”

Here he fell silent, evidently waiting for my reply. Since he got none, he went back behind the desk, to his steaming tea—it had cooled a little, so now he slurped it longer and louder—to his papers and seals. With a hefty pen he signed one document, brought down one stamp, corrected it with another; satisfied, he rubbed his hands together and settled back in his leather chair.

“Here is your passport and a napravlenie to our delegation in Irkutsk; they’ll take care of you there on the spot. They bought you a ticket at once as well. Tomorrow you leave for Moscow, otherwise you won’t make that Siberian Express—today is the first of July, your ticket is for the fifth, departure at ten in the evening from Yaroslavsky Station, you’ll be in Irkutsk on the eleventh, and there they’ll put you on the Zimna for Kezhma. Here’s your thousand, sign for it. Buy yourself some clothes, so you look like a human being! And if it should occur to you to take the money and lose it all—well, lose it then, so long as you get as far as Baikal. Come on, sign!”

A thousand rubles. What did they want me to do there? Get from my father the names he had not betrayed at the trial? Only why was the Ministry of Winter coming to me with this, and not Internal Affairs?

“I’ll go,” I said. “I’ll visit my father. And then? And that’s all?”

“You’ll talk to him.”

“I’ll talk to him.”

“Once you’ve talked to him, well, then that will already be good.”

“I don’t understand, Your Nobility—”

“He sent no letters, and that of course did not surprise you.” Commissioner Preiss opened a portfolio bound in cloth and tortoiseshell. “Irkutsk writes to us... He was a geologist, was he not?”

“I beg your pardon?”

“Filip Filippovich studied geology. He did not lose interest in it in Siberia either. They report to me here... From the start he was very close, his company was taken on the second or third expedition that went there in the spring of nineteen ten. Most of them died of frostbite. Or froze where they stood. He survived. Then he went back. To them. I don’t believe it myself, but that’s what they write to me here. They gave the order, they gave the money, so I’m sending a man. Your father talks with lutes.”

On What Cannot Be Said


My life is governed by the principle of shame.

One comes to know the world, one comes to know the language for describing the world, but one does not come to know oneself. Most people—almost everyone, I think—will never learn, before death, the language in which they might describe themselves.

When I say of someone that he is a coward, it means I think he behaves cowardly—it means nothing more, because of course I cannot look into the depths of his soul and find out whether he is a coward. I cannot, however, use that language to describe myself: within the scope of my experience I remain the only person for whom his words, deeds, omissions are merely a pale and in truth accidental reflection of what lies beneath them, what constitutes their cause and source. I experience the existence of that source directly, whereas of some of my own behaviors I am not aware at all, and all of them I perceive incompletely, askew. We ourselves are always the last to find out what kind of idiot we have made of ourselves in company. We know the intentions of our actions better than those actions. We know better what we meant to say than what we actually said. We know who we want to be—we do not know who we are.

The language for describing our behavior exists because many people experience that reality and can discuss among themselves someone’s ostentatious politeness or someone else’s faux pas. The language for describing me myself does not exist, because no one but me experiences that reality.

It would be a language for the use of one person alone, a language unsayable, unwritable. Everyone must create it for himself. Most people—almost everyone, I am convinced—never bring themselves to do it before they die. At most they repeat inwardly others’ descriptions of themselves, expressed in that derivative language—in the second-order language—or imagine what they would say of themselves in it if they were granted the chance to look at themselves from outside.

To say anything at all about themselves, they must make themselves strangers to themselves.

My life is governed by the principle of shame. I have no better words with which to tell that truth.

A beggar extends his hand in a pleading gesture, I have money, I can give him some, if only five, if only two, if only one kopeck, no one else is looking, it is only the two of us, the beggar and I—and I give nothing, I turn away and walk off quickly, hunching my head into my shoulders.

Who broke the jar of preserves? Mother raises her voice. Who broke it? Not I, and I have no idea whether Bolek is to blame or Emilka, but Mother asks again, and again, and then I point decisively at Bolek. Him.

Laughter in company, we are all mocking a mutual acquaintance of ours, who of course is not here with us now, one after another we summon up memories of his humiliations and weaknesses. I stand up, I am not laughing, brows knotted and lips pressed tight, I stand up and—have you no shame!

A beauty sticks to me, spills over my shoulders, slides down onto my chest, my lap, when the cab slows and stops before the gate of the tenement where I rent a room with Kiwajs, Kiwajs is off with distant relatives in the provinces, the room is free, the drunken girl giggles, biting the button of my frock coat—the cabman turns round, winks at me, shall I help the bright gentleman? I look at him in terror. I cram a crumpled ruble into his fist. Take the whore wherever she wants! I throw the girl off me, jump from the cab, and flee into the depths of the dark gateway.

In the woods, beyond my grandfather’s village, in the hollow by the stream where a tree has been uprooted, lies the carcass of a roe deer. Half eaten by predators and scavengers, under a black canopy of flies. I tell nobody, I come every morning and every evening, prod it with a stick, turn it over, watch the insects swarm over it afresh, watch the flesh change color and dark liquids leak from the body, slowly soaking into the ground. Where do I spend so much time, Grandfather asks. I lie. He sees that I lie. Where do I go, he asks. I say nothing. He thrashes me with his belt. I cry. I do not tell the truth, I say nothing. All summer I walk the forest looking for dead animals. I carry a thick stick with me, like a club. I beat carrion with that stick in a dull fury until the rot comes away from the bones. There! take that! take that!

Eighteen and seventy-four! Eighteen and seventy-four! The fairground barker is tearing his throat out, announcing the winning numbers. We all bought tickets as we entered the fair. Laughing, red from frost and plum brandy, we are now searching our pockets for the cardboard slips. I find mine, draw it out. Eighteen and seventy-four. Playing at great disappointment and fury, they throw their tickets into the mud, curse bad luck, and make fun of the fool who never comes to collect the prize. I tear up the winning ticket and do the same as they do.

At night, when no one sees me, I practice terrible grimaces, bulging eyes and bared teeth, too wild to mean anything in the speech of human physiognomy; terrible grimaces, and also the absolute deadness of the face, the motionlessness of the tiniest muscles of the skull, which in daylight and among people I can never achieve, then I have no command over the grimaces of my face. At night, when I sleep, under my eyelids I see a diagram, like one from Zyga’s yellowed plates, an anatomical diagram of that betrayal: dozens of hard strings threaded under the skin of cheeks, brows, chin, lips; and the other ends of these strings are held by the people around me, in their hands, between their teeth, tied round watch fobs and cuff studs, on wedding rings, on cane knobs and pipe stems, some of them even have them sewn into their own facial muscles, or into their hearts, straight through chest and breastbone. Then the plate comes alive—day—movement—people—I—

I smile—I smile—I smile.

...

I remember behaviors, I use the second language...

I must become a stranger to myself.

Benedykt Gierosławski was a good child. Who would not want such a child? He was well-behaved—always obedient to his parents, obedient to his elders, he did not speak unless asked, did not get into mischief, did not fight with other children, said his prayers before sleep, had the best marks at school. Other boys wandered the city, spent their time in the streets—he read books. Other boys spied on girls and teased their sisters—he taught his little sister her letters. He washed his ears. He bowed to neighbors and acquaintances. He did not pick his nose, he did not point. He did not fall ill. He did not cause his parents trouble. Who would not want such a child?

When he turned seventeen and entered the Imperial University, he met others who had been good children.

They recognized one another by their smiles.

Was it already too late? Could I go back—back to what, to whom, to which me? There is no moment in the past that, miraculously changed, would reverse the course of my life, no catastrophe that befell me as a result of which I am different from what I ought to be; I can point to nothing of the kind.

It is exactly the other way round: however far back I cast memory, I always find beneath the thin epidermis of my words and deeds the same principle, a muscle of unease, hope, and disgust tensed in the same direction, when I was three, thirteen, and twenty-three. Not a feeling, something else, not a thought—it flees, slips free, slides away, immaterial, indescribable—stop! look me in the eyes!—you, you—I shall call you Shame, though you are not that, I shall call you Shame, because there are no better words in the language shared among people.
