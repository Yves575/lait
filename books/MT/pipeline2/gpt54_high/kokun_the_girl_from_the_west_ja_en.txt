*Kokun*, Book One: The Girl from the West

Prologue: Blue Flower

The wind howled in her ears and buffeted her hair.

A brief shower had passed over just after noon, and the earth was still damp. The rock face to which she clung with both hands and both feet was cold as ice.

“...Sister!”

A shrill voice rang out, and little clods of mud came pattering down.

In an instant, Aisha let go with one hand and pressed her palm against the heel of her younger brother’s shoe, pale in the deepening dusk, to steady him.

The moment she did, her own body tilted, and the other hand gripping the rock nearly slipped free. Desperately, she seized hold again, barely checked the sway of her body, and let out a breath—only to feel her knees begin to shake.

If she fell, she would die.

Grinding her teeth, Aisha kept holding him up until he planted his foot firmly in one of the hollows cut into the rock and got himself steady.

Even after he had found his footing, she could not move for a while.

Panting harshly, she waited for the dizziness to subside.

Even if they managed to climb all the way up this path along the cliff, they probably had no future. She had known that for some time now. From above, from the top of the rocky mountain, a reek of leather, metal, and sweat had been drifting down, strong enough to make her head swim.

She had been told few people knew of this cliff path. If warriors were lying in wait here, it could only mean that old retainer Uchai—who had drawn off the enemy and given them a chance to escape by acting as a decoy—had been captured almost at once.

She could not believe Jiiya had talked. And yet the warriors had somehow guessed where she and her brother were fleeing and gone ahead of them.

*Should I just let go?*

Dimly, in front of her, she could see her brother’s shoes, smeared with mud. Remembering the smile on his face when he had been bought those shoes, Aisha’s face twisted.

Then suddenly the world grew lighter.

The wind had driven the clouds apart. The setting sun, just before sinking behind the mountain’s shadow, sent out shaft after shaft of light across the rock.

In that unexpected brightness, she saw that the grass rooted in a crack just above the rock she was gripping had put out tiny blue flowers. The wind lashed them till they looked ready to tear away, but tear away they did not.

She could hear the voice of their scent. Faint, but steady, it drifted on the wind like a thread. Before long, no doubt, insects that heard that fragrant voice would come flying through the pauses between the gusts.

“...Sister.”

Again his voice fell from above. On the cold wind came the smell of fear, like a cowering puppy.

“Hold fast, Mircha!” Aisha shouted.

“Grip the rock and climb! If you start to slip, I’ll hold you!”

Only a little farther now, and they would be up on top of the rocks.

Sensing from the movement above that her brother had begun climbing again, Aisha slowly started upward too.

Chapter One: Encounter

1. Ritaran

The color of the watchfires, invisible until a moment ago, now stood out sharply against the blue darkness.

The wind that had been making the tents flap had eased a little as well.

From the countless tents pitched across the encampment, white smoke rose wavering and dissolved into the evening sky. Faintly, the smell of supper being cooked drifted over.

“The rank and file get to eat before we do.”

The bearded man sprawled in a folding chair among a knot of hard-faced men muttered the complaint in a low voice.

Mashu looked down at his bearded colleague Urairi and smiled faintly.

“You don’t have to come along. One man is enough for this.”

Urairi looked as though he were about to toss back some joke, then thought better of it. Turning his eyes toward the grassland, he muttered,

“...If I could, I would.”

Across the plain at the foot of the looming Tenro range, sinking now into evening shadow, the torchlight of more than a dozen mounted riders could be seen flickering as they came toward them.

“Oroki.”

Twisting his neck, Urairi looked back at the man standing behind him. The hound at the man’s feet lifted its head as though it had been the one called. Resting a hand on the dog’s head, Oroki answered,

“Yes.”

“You said the heir was eight or so, and the older girl fifteen or sixteen, didn’t you?”

“The heir is nine. The older girl is fifteen.”

Urairi nodded and sighed.

“They could have left them alone instead of bothering to capture them.”

He looked up at Mashu and went on.

“Don’t you think so? Unless some faction meant to raise them up as a banner, what power could the descendants of a king hated by his people and driven from the throne possibly have?

“That’s why they’ve been left alone till now, I’d have thought. So why, all of a sudden—and in the middle of a campaign—has Dyukchi decided to do this?”

Mashu did not answer the question. Keeping his gaze fixed on the riders, he murmured,

“So they did come from that direction.”

Urairi frowned.

“What do you mean?”

“The riders. If the men who captured them are returning from there, then Keruan’s descendants must have been trying to flee into Tooura-Ira—the Great Collapse Gorge in the Tenro range.”

“...”

Urairi rose and came to stand beside Mashu.

“You said, ‘so they did.’ Meaning you’d already guessed it.”

Mashu glanced at him.

“If they were going to run, there was nowhere else.”

“Surely not. They’d been living hidden away in the forest belt, hadn’t they? Easier to head east through the forests than climb dangerous mountain paths.”

“They must have judged that, even with the danger, they stood a better chance of surviving if they fled into Tenro.”

“Why?”

“A moment ago you called Keruan a king hated by his people. But in Tenro there are those who do not hate that king.”

Urairi’s face hardened.

“...I never heard any such thing. The mountain folk of Tenro? Which mountain folk?”

Mashu kept his eyes on the mounted warriors, who had now come close enough to be plainly seen.

“You weren’t the only one who didn’t know. If anything, the number who do know is probably small.”

Urairi narrowed his eyes.

“When did you find out?”

Mashu looked at him and answered quietly.

“Me? I’ve known since I was a child.”

One of Prince Dyukchi’s close attendants emerged from the great tent where the prince conducted his business. Peering through the evening gloom, he looked around, noticed Mashu and the others, and came quickly toward them.

“Inspector.”

Crossing his arms neatly before his chest, he bowed deeply, then said,

“I am to convey Prince Dyukchi’s words: the captives will be arriving shortly, and you are requested to enter the tent. That is all.”

Mashu nodded and replied shortly,

“I understand.”

After another deep bow, the attendant led the two of them toward the great tent.

An inspector of the march-principalities was the eyes and ears of the emperor of the Umar Empire, which held all four principalities under its rule.

The term translated as “march” came from the Umar word *rachi*, meaning “that which marks off a tract of land, encloses it, and protects it.”

The march-principalities had once each been independent countries. Even now, under Umar rule, they retained a measure of self-government. In exchange for the duty of guarding the borders of the empire, the march-princes were granted limited authority over the lands they had once ruled—but they were no longer kings of sovereign states. Dyukchi, merely prince of West Kantar, was not permitted to conceal anything from Mashu and the others.

Leaving their warrior escorts behind, Mashu and Urairi walked slowly toward the entrance of the great tent, bright as though that one place alone had been cleanly cut out of the blue dark.

*

The great tent was more spacious inside than it had looked from without.

The smoke-hole at the top stood wide open, and in the hearth at the center the fire burned a vivid red. Lamps had been set here and there, enough to make out, to some degree, the faces of the senior retainers and clan chiefs seated along the walls.

At the far end, directly facing the entrance, stood an altar beautifully painted in vermilion, blue, and gold. With that altar at his back, Prince Dyukchi sat in a large chair, speaking in low tones to the attendant beside him. When Mashu and Urairi entered, however, he rose, bowed, and motioned for them to sit in the chairs near him.

Outside the wind was cold, but inside the great tent it was stifling. On the small tables before each person were fruit juices to moisten the throat, along with nuts and dried fruits to take the edge off hunger.

Not long after Mashu and Urairi had taken their seats, a bell sounded to announce the arrival of the captives. The murmuring stopped, and the tent fell quiet.

Dyukchi sat down again. Taking a cloth from his attendant, he wiped the sweat from his brow and neck.

He was a large man, yet there was nothing slack about him; his skin was dark with sun. Everything about him was big—his hands and feet as well—but what stood out most were his brows and his eyes. Anyone stared at by those large, protruding eyes would feel uneasy.

He came from no more than a collateral branch said to touch the royal blood somewhere back along the line, but he had a peculiar instinct for war. On the condition that he serve as a bulwark of the empire in times of crisis, the emperor had lent him troops; and after King Keruan was driven out, Dyukchi used those forces to defeat the king who had taken the throne afterward yet failed to quell the skirmishing among the clans, and so became prince of West Kantar.

One clan still had not acknowledged him, so it could not yet be said that he held all West Kantar in his grasp. But if things continued as they were, Mashu thought, it would not be long before he finished pacifying it.

The emperor wanted Dyukchi to bring West Kantar swiftly under control, and Mashu wanted the same.

The moment the wooden doors and hanging cloth were drawn wide from both sides, cold night wind swept in.

Wrapped in that wind, two burly warriors entered, each leading a captive: one a boy, the other a slender girl.

Neither of them had been bound, but each was being held fast by the dominant arm. One of the warriors placed an arm across his chest and said in a strong, ringing voice,

“In accordance with your command, we have brought Keruan’s descendants.”

Dyukchi nodded. After speaking a few words of praise to the men, he made a gesture that they could withdraw.

The warriors, however, hesitated.

“What is it? You may go.”

At Dyukchi’s prompting, one of them spoke.

“With due respect, my lord: the older girl has shown no sign of submission. If we release her, there is no knowing what she may do.”

At that, Dyukchi raised his heavy brows.

“I see. Very well, we’ll be careful. Let go.”

The warriors bowed, released their grips, and stepped back, though they kept their eyes trained on the girl, ready to seize her again at any moment.

The girl, however, did not move. Not so much as a twitch of the brow. With eyes black and still as stone, she simply stared straight at Dyukchi.

Meeting that gaze, Dyukchi said,

“Keruan’s grandchildren. Mircha and Aisha, yes?”

The girl opened her mouth.

“I have no name to give a usurper.”

Her voice was hoarse, but firm.

Dyukchi sighed.

“I see. So you have no intention of showing submission.”

Slowly he rose and looked down at the brother and sister. Then, taking a longsword from the rack beside him, he struck the end of its scabbard violently against the floorboards.

The senior retainers flinched; the brother and sister jerked in shock. The boy’s face crumpled, and he began to sob.

“Your brother is crying,” Dyukchi said, fixing his gaze on the girl.

“Mind the situation you are in before you speak. Depending on your behavior, your brother’s head may go flying before your eyes.”

The girl looked up at him, her face gone pale. At last, in a thin voice, she said,

“...That is a lie.”

Dyukchi’s heavy brows lifted.

“A lie?”

She nodded.

“Does our fate truly depend on something like my attitude? Whether we live or die?”

Dyukchi’s eyes widened slightly.

He stared at her for a long moment; then one corner of his mouth twisted a little.

“If not your attitude, what determines your fate?”

She answered at once.

“Your self-interest.”

The senior retainers stirred almost imperceptibly.

Mashu shifted his gaze from the girl to Dyukchi.

*Now then—what will he say?*

Dyukchi gazed at her in silence for a long time, then finally let out a sigh.

“My self-interest, is it? ...Well, you’re not entirely wrong.”

Then he dropped heavily back into his chair.

Lifting a gold-mounted horn cup from the low table, he tipped back his head and drained the milk wine in one long swallow. Then he wiped his mouth with the cloth his attendant offered, and used it as well to blot the sweat from his face and neck.

After that, he turned his face once more toward the older girl.

“You’re not entirely wrong—but you’re not right either. Your existence is less a loss to me than a loss to the empire as a whole.”

The boy looked up at his sister as though asking a question. She did not look at him. She only kept staring at Dyukchi.

“You were trying to flee into the Tenro mountains. More precisely, into Tooura-Ira—the Great Collapse Gorge.”

Seeing the change in her expression, Dyukchi added,

“Don’t mistake me. That old man didn’t confess. He’s a loyal servant. He resisted with a ferocity you’d hardly expect in one so old. And because I suspected from the start he might act as a decoy, I divided my soldiers in two from the beginning.”

The girl’s face tightened. Watching her, Dyukchi said,

“The one who told us where you might flee was the esteemed inspector from the Umar Empire.”

Urairi shifted in place. He made no move to look their way, but his surprise could be felt in every line of him.

Mashu’s expression did not change. He simply kept his eyes on Dyukchi and on the still very young girl.

In an even tone, Dyukchi said,

“So your existence serves neither West Kantar nor the empire. Do you understand why?”

The girl’s face had gone white. She must have realized there was nowhere left to turn.

Only Dyukchi’s voice rang through the great tent.

“Your grandfather Keruan was a fool. Because of him, many of the people starved to death. Most in this land hate him from the depths of their hearts. I am one of them.”

The girl’s eyes wavered. Her pale face went paler still.

Dyukchi had more milk wine poured into the horn cup and again drank it down in one gulp.

“And yet, it seems there are still those left in this frontier who revere you.

“They are called the People of the Hidden Valleys. They are mountain folk of the frontier, not numerous, and they have no ambition to dominate this country, so we have left them alone till now. But at present their existence is troublesome. The problem is where they rule.”

At his side, Mashu heard Urairi murmur, “So that’s it.”

The clan this campaign was meant to subdue controlled the foothills in the western reaches of the Tenro range, but its authority did not extend into the mountains themselves.

They did not have the strength to maintain their power for long against Dyukchi’s expeditionary force. Still, there remained one possibility by which they might reverse their fortunes: if they could secure the aid of Tatsuketsu, the nation beyond the Tenro mountains.

The Tenro range, running around West Kantar from north to west, was steep and forbidding. There were only a few places where a large army could invade from the Tatsuketsu side, and at such places forts had been built, jointly manned by imperial troops and the forces of West Kantar.

Even in Tooura-Ira, the Great Collapse Gorge in the western Tenro range, there was a mountain path by which a small band of warriors accustomed to crossing high ridges could come over from Tatsuketsu. But there was no fort there.

Around Tooura-Ira there were many places where hidden hollows opened beneath the ground; step through one, and it could cost you your life. Without a guide, no one could make their way along those mountain paths.

Unless the mountain folk who ruled the Great Collapse Gorge and its surrounding region—the People of the Hidden Valleys—chose to take their side, warriors from Tatsuketsu could not descend into West Kantar. And since it was impossible to bring the People of the Hidden Valleys, who knew the Great Collapse Gorge like the palm of their hand, under control without committing a large army, neither West Kantar’s princes nor the empire had ever regarded the region as especially important.

But if the People of the Hidden Valleys still considered Keruan’s descendants the heirs of the rightful king, the situation could change greatly if the hostile clan raised these two up as a banner.

Among the clan chiefs seated along the walls of the great tent, there were some whose expressions showed they had only now grasped the significance of what was happening.

The clan chiefs who had joined this campaign from the southern and eastern parts of the principality knew little of conditions in the western Tenro region. Dyukchi had likely withheld the details on purpose, lest men who had only recently shown submission start getting ideas.

“You are, so to speak, an ant-hole in an embankment. In ordinary times, it causes no trouble if left alone. But when the storm comes, the embankment collapses from there, and calamity spreads through the land.

“I am not a heartless man. I do feel a certain reluctance about killing you merely for being that man’s grandchildren. But it cannot be helped.

“I could keep you penned under my own eye—but that course still leaves a measure of danger.”

As he said this, a sudden weariness came over Dyukchi’s face.

“So that is how it is. If you would hate something, hate your bloodline and your fate.”

At a jerk of Dyukchi’s chin, the warriors from before stepped forward and reached for the siblings’ arms.

The younger boy shrank back in fear and stretched a hand toward his sister. After gripping his hand firmly, the older girl looked up at Dyukchi.

On her pale face there flickered something like hesitation. Her lips parted, then closed again, as if she had thought better of speaking.

Dyukchi frowned in puzzlement.

“Is there something you wish to say?”

The girl seemed to consider for a while. Then, as though making up her mind, she said,

“You have been poisoned.”

Dyukchi knit his brows and stared at her. Again he wiped the sweat from his face and neck with the cloth. But the sweating did not abate; even to an onlooker it was unnaturally severe.

“What did you say?”

The girl looked at him with eyes that gave away nothing.

“You smell of shadewort. Someone has poisoned you.”

A stir ran through the senior retainers; some half rose from their seats.

Wiping sweat from his eyes, Dyukchi gave a fearless smile.

“A final taunt, is it? Admirable nerve. But if you’re going to lie, make it a more convincing lie.”

“I am not lying.”

“Of course you are. Poison brewed from shadewort root has neither taste nor smell.”

The girl’s face changed slightly.

“...Then think so, if you wish.”

With a sigh, she put an arm around her brother’s shoulders and turned her back on Dyukchi. A warrior hurriedly seized her arm and began leading the two of them toward the entrance of the great tent.

Mashu rose at once.

“Wait.”

The brother and sister stopped and looked back. Dyukchi and the senior retainers all turned toward him as well.

Mashu addressed the girl.

“Lady Aisha Keruan—do I smell of anything?”

The girl stared at him. Her eyes were black as obsidian.

Just as he thought she might not answer, she frowned faintly and said,

“...Are you Ritaran?”

Mashu’s eyes flew wide. A chill spread from between his brows through his whole head, and his heart began pounding so fast it hurt.

His lips had gone numb. Opening them, he asked in a hoarse voice,

“Why do you think that?”

Still frowning, the girl said,

“Because you smell of blue sweetgrass.”

Mashu stood there staring at her, stunned.

“...Hey, what’s wrong?” Urairi asked.

Without answering, Mashu strode toward Dyukchi.

At close range it was instantly obvious something was badly wrong. Dyukchi’s eyes would not focus.

Mashu turned to the attendant.

“Tell the physician to bring kafur! Don’t make a mistake—kafur! And bring plenty of water as well! Hurry!”

Then he called to the warriors restraining the siblings.

“Take the two of them away and lock them up securely!”

A dull sound came, like a sack of flour hitting the floor. Turning, Mashu saw that Dyukchi had slid from his chair and collapsed.

As the senior retainers leapt up and confusion broke out, voices rising on all sides, Mashu ran to Urairi and said rapidly under his breath, too low for the others to hear,

“Stay with those two. Take the guards with you. Don’t let anyone lay a hand on them. Be careful about their food—and even the insect-repelling smoke—so they aren’t poisoned. And later, send Oroki to me.”

Urairi nodded silently and went to the siblings. Then, with the warriors, he escorted them out of the great tent.

2. Scentless Poison

Dyukchi did not pass the crisis until dawn the following day.

Throughout that long night, the senior retainers came to the tent one after another, but Mashu allowed them no more than a look inside from the entrance; he did not permit them to enter.

Some showed anger, but since no one knew who had administered the poison, Mashu’s measures were perfectly reasonable, and no one tried to force the matter.

The only person he allowed inside the tent was his subordinate Oroki. Mashu gave him detailed instructions, then sent him back into the encampment together with the dog that shadowed him wherever he went.

Mashu spent the entire night seated beside the prince’s bedside with the physician. Once he had seen that Dyukchi’s condition had stabilized, he spread bedding in a corner of the same tent and dozed.

He woke a little before noon. The prince was breathing in the same deep, quiet rhythm as before.

Rising, Mashu stepped out of the tent and drew a long breath.

He smelled rain-wet grass. Come to think of it, he had a vague memory of hearing rain drum on the tent in his sleep. At this season, showers like that often fell in these parts.

The sky was overcast and dull, but the wind was strong. Each time it tore open the heavy cloud cover, sunlight poured through and lit the Tenro range with startling brightness.

They must have begun preparing the midday meal. Mixed with the smell of smoke was the savory aroma of tapa, the thin rice-flour flatbreads, baking on the fire.

Hearing footsteps in the grass, he turned and saw Oroki approaching with the dog.

“How did it go?”

In a low voice, Oroki answered,

“Just as you suspected.”

“So you found it?”

“We did.”

After hearing the full report, Mashu smiled.

“Well done. Get some rest.”

Oroki bowed, clicked his heels, and turned smartly away. Mashu called after him,

“Feed your partner well, too.”

Oroki glanced back, the faintest smile softening his face, and nodded. The dog, as if it knew it was the one being spoken of, gave its tail a lazy wag.

Drawing one more deep breath, Mashu turned away.

*...Now then,* he said to himself.

*From here begins another task.*

Coming in from the brightness outside, he was momentarily dazzled.

As his eyes adjusted to the dimmer light, he made out Dyukchi lying on bedding spread by the hearth, with the physician sitting beside him. The smell of medicinal broth mingled with the smoke.

When Mashu approached the bedside, the slight noise must have woken Dyukchi. He opened his eyes a little and gestured for water.

The physician raised his head and supported him, then held a bowl to his lips. Dyukchi drank audibly.

Seeing that he could swallow without choking, the physician’s face relaxed with relief.

After coughing two or three times, as though trying to clear the phlegm from his throat, Dyukchi lifted his face and looked at Mashu. Strength had returned to his eyes.

“...What was put in me,” he said in a rasping voice. “Was it truly shadewort?”

Mashu looked to the physician, and the middle-aged man answered.

“I cannot be certain, but I believe so. The symptoms Your Highness displayed were extremely similar to those caused by shadewort, and the antidotal decoction used in shadewort poisoning proved highly effective.”

Dyukchi said darkly,

“But shadewort is supposed to have no smell or taste.”

“It does not,” the physician replied with a nod.

Dyukchi turned his gaze back to Mashu.

“Then why did that girl say she could smell shadewort in my sweat?”

How to answer, if asked that question—Mashu had spent the whole night thinking it over. Yet every answer that came to mind had left him with some feeling of hesitation.

But now that the question had actually been put to him, he felt none at all.

Mashu turned to the physician.

“Lord Dyukchi seems to have stabilized. Will he be all right for a while without you?”

The physician moved on his knees to Dyukchi’s side, took his pulse, and nodded.

“For a while, yes. I believe he will be fine even if I am absent.”

“Then get some rest. If there is any change in Lord Dyukchi’s condition, I will send for you at once.”

The physician bowed and left the tent.

Mashu waited until his footsteps had died away, then turned back to Dyukchi.

“As for your question.”

“...”

“It may be that the girl truly could smell shadewort. Her nose is likely keener than most people’s.”

A hard light entered Dyukchi’s large eyes.

“You had everyone sent away, and this is the nonsense you offer me? What I wanted to hear was why that girl saw fit to make such an unnecessary remark. She knew I had been poisoned. Which means she is connected to the one who poisoned me—”

Mashu slowly shook his head. Dyukchi frowned irritably.

“You say that is not so? In what way?”

“That girl has no connection to the one who poisoned you.”

“Why can you say that so certainly?”

“Because, as you yourself find so strange, she told you that you had been poisoned.”

“...”

“That was precisely one remark too many. Had she been on the poisoner’s side, and thought your death would save both her and her brother, she would never have said it.”

Mashu let out a small sigh.

“She put on a brave face, yes—but she is only fifteen. When she realized that if she kept silent about what she had noticed, a man would die, the words simply slipped out.”

“He was a man who meant to kill her.”

“Even so, there are those who cannot stand by and let another person die.”

“But shadewort—”

Cutting him off, Mashu said,

“You are too fixated on the fact that shadewort is tasteless and scentless. Signs that someone has been poisoned appear in many ways. Even I found your excessive sweating strange. Seen from directly before you, it must have been obvious to that girl.”

“...”

“Those born to royal blood live under constant threat of assassination, and so they become well versed in the methods by which it is done. That girl is King Keruan’s granddaughter. She was no doubt raised with a thorough education in such things.

“That is why, from your abnormal sweating and the unsteadiness of your eyes, she guessed you had been dosed with shadewort.”

Dyukchi seemed half convinced, but something still troubled him, and he began to speak.

Before he could, Mashu went on.

“Besides, whoever attempted to poison you can hardly have known what political value Keruan’s descendants might carry.”

Dyukchi’s eyes widened sharply.

“You know who did it?”

Mashu nodded.

“The chief of the Rima clan.”

Dyukchi’s expression tightened.

“Lariha. What proof?”

“One of his servants had a cup-polishing cloth. That same servant, I am told, was on duty polishing the cups set out in the great tent last night.”

Dyukchi sighed and shook his head.

“If that is the basis of your suspicion, you are mistaken. I wash every cup myself, polish it myself, and keep it where I can see it. There is no way that man could have smeared poison on my cup.”

Mashu smiled.

“What he smeared on the cups was not poison.”

“...?”

“He coated everyone’s cups with kafur sap.”

In an even voice, Mashu explained the method by which the poisoning had been attempted.

“You fear poison, so you drink only milk wine poured into your own cup from the same vessel as everyone else’s. If poison were mixed into the wine itself, Lariha would have to drink it too.

“He could, of course, have killed everyone in the great tent and arranged to survive alone. But even if that removed you, it would also earn him the hatred of the other clans.”

A look of astonished understanding spread across Dyukchi’s face.

“I see! He used my habit of polishing only my own cup separately against me.”

Mashu nodded.

“Yes. Shadewort poison is vulnerable to kafur sap. The moment it touches that sap, it loses its potency. What the physician gave you as an antidote was also made from kafur sap.”

“...So he had applied it in advance to his own cup, and everyone else’s.”

“Exactly. My men spent the whole night tracking down the one carrying a polishing cloth that smelled of kafur sap.”

Mashu smiled faintly.

“Using a dog’s nose rather than their own, of course.”

Dyukchi grunted and sat for a while with his head bowed, thinking.

Stroking his chin, Mashu said,

“In these circumstances, punishing Lariha will be quite difficult. We’ll need to think carefully about how best to proceed.”

“...That too,” Dyukchi murmured. Then he raised his head and looked at Mashu. “But the real question is what to do with the girl.”

“Because she saved your life?”

Dyukchi nodded.

“If not both, then at least the girl—”

Mashu shook his head.

“That would be a mistake. If her brother is killed, she will direct fierce hatred at you all her life. It will only leave trouble behind.”

Dyukchi’s face clouded. Mashu said,

“If you wish to show mercy, then have them executed by poison rather than beheading.”

Dyukchi drew his brows together.

“Wouldn’t that be crueler?”

“No. One need only choose the poison. There are poisons that let a person go as though falling asleep, without suffering.”

Dyukchi looked at Mashu with a complicated expression. Mashu met the gaze in silence.

3. Frostgrass

Aisha and her younger brother Mircha were confined in a tent pitched at the edge of the encampment.

It stood alone on the grassland, well away from the other tents, so anyone approaching could easily be seen.

The inside and outside of the tent were being strictly guarded by the men who had accompanied Mashu, and even food and changes of clothing were not handed over to the siblings until those guards had inspected them.

Once, in the afternoon of the day Dyukchi recovered, Oroki came to the tent, called Urairi outside, whispered something to him, and went off somewhere with three of the guards. Other than that, there was almost no movement of people.

Besides the smoke vent in the roof, the tent had two small windows to let in air. Whenever the sounds of drills or other noise drifted over from the camp, the boy would go to one of those windows and peer out. Aisha, however, never so much as approached them. She simply sat in the tent, passing the time by embroidering the cloth that had been given her to occupy herself.

By the time the sun had gone down and the soldiers had finished their evening meal, a late supper was brought to the brother and sister.

Behind the servants carrying the food came Mashu and two of the prince’s attendants.

When the guards lifted the entrance flap and whispered something inside, Urairi emerged from the tent. The moment he noticed Mashu and the others behind the supper bearers, his face stiffened.

Mashu put a finger to his lips. Urairi answered with his eyes that he understood.

A servant carrying the supper on a large tray ducked into the tent, and Mashu and the prince’s attendants each moved to one of the windows.

Night had fully fallen, and the lights of the encampment were far away. From inside the brightly lit tent, the faces of anyone standing outside the windows would be invisible unless they came very close. And even if the brother and sister noticed shadows there, the guards had stood outside the windows in much the same way before. There was no reason for them to suspect that anything different was happening now.

The supper was finer than what had been served to the soldiers. Along with roast mutton there was a vegetable stew, and fruit besides. The thin tapa flatbreads had been spread with dairy cream and sprinkled with precious sugar. Naturally, there was milk wine too.

The younger brother must have been hungry. When the dishes were set before him, his face lit up with delight. But his sister had been sitting with her head bowed even before the servant came in.

At the sight of that posture, Mashu narrowed his eyes.

There was tension in her shoulders. She was on edge.

*Has she noticed?*

The boy reached first for the flatbread and took a big bite, eating with obvious pleasure.

His sister too began to eat quietly. Like her brother, she started with the flatbread; then she ate the mutton and the stew, slowly, savoring them. She ate the fruit as well, leaving nothing.

When the boy, having sampled everything, reached for the milk wine, his sister stopped eating and looked at him.

*...Will she stop him?*

Without realizing it, Mashu leaned forward and stared at her profile.

Her hand was trembling faintly. The slim muscles of her neck stood out with tension.

The boy took the jug by its handle in both hands and put it to his lips.

She did not stop him. She only kept staring at him.

The boy drank the milk wine down greedily, his throat bobbing. Setting the vessel back on the floor, he reached for the remaining fruit.

Then his head gave a sudden lurch, and he pitched forward.

His sister darted out a hand, caught him, lowered him to the floor, and laid his head in her lap.

Something gleamed on her cheek.

For a while she bent over him, as though cradling his limp head in her arms. Then she straightened and reached for the empty milk-wine jug her brother had drained.

The instant her hand closed around the handle, she hurled it with all her strength.

It struck below the window with a dull crash, and Mashu instinctively jerked his face back.

Inside the tent, Urairi and the others sprang up in alarm. But the girl did not even glance at them. She simply snatched up her own jug of milk wine and, with her face turned toward the window where Mashu stood, put it to her lips and drank, her throat working.

Her black eyes blazed with a terrible light as they fixed on him.

Bracing himself, Mashu returned the girl’s gaze.

*Yes. I am the one who poisoned you.*

Something hot surged up from the bottom of his chest, and his whole body began to tremble.

*You caught the scent on the fingers with which I pinched the poison into your drink—and you knew the owner of that scent was here.*

Before he knew it, Mashu was smiling. His whole body felt ablaze.

He stood motionless, watching as the force went out of the girl’s eyes and she toppled forward.

Dyukchi came out of the tent with an attendant supporting him.

A large woolen cloth had been spread on the grass before the tent. By the light of the torches the soldiers held aloft, the bodies of the girl and the boy laid out upon it were dimly illuminated.

Their faces were pale even in the night, and they looked somehow smaller than they had in life.

Dyukchi stood looking down at the bodies for a while. At last he crouched, put a hand to the boy’s mouth, and confirmed that he was no longer breathing.

Then he did the same with the girl. When he lightly touched her cheek, he drew his hand back in surprise.

“She’s remarkably cold. It hasn’t been that long since they died, has it?”

Mashu, standing on the far side of the bodies, answered in a quiet voice.

“I used hirin—frostgrass.”

Dyukchi nodded, stood, and sighed. Then he turned to the soldiers.

“Wrap them in cloth and bury them.”

Having said only that, he turned his back on the bodies and went back into the tent.

In West Kantar, children are buried beneath yugi trees.

According to legend, the yugi tree sprang from the body of a mother who, in grief over the loss of her child, threw herself into a river and washed ashore. It was said that if the young dead were buried at its roots, the spirit of the yugi tree would gently guide their souls to the heavenly realm.

On Dyukchi’s orders, soldiers had been searching since dusk for a yugi tree, and they had already found a suitable burial place and returned. The two bodies were therefore loaded at once onto a wagon, carried across the grassland into woods along a narrow river, and buried in the grave that had been dug beneath a yugi tree.

The soldiers too were human beings, and some of them were parents. They did not toss the bodies in. They wrapped them in wool so they would not be cold, laid them gently at the bottom of the grave, covered them over so wild beasts would not disturb them, and after burying them, bowed deeply and rode back toward camp.

The instant the sound of their horses’ hooves had faded into the distance, three figures leapt out from between the dark trees and began frantically digging up the grave.

They pulled the two bodies from the pit and laid them on the grass beside it, then hastily removed the cloths that had covered them completely.

Then, carefully wrapping the bodies in fresh cloth, two of the men each took one—the sister and the brother—and disappeared deeper into the forest.

The remaining man returned the original wrappings to the bottom of the grave, adjusted them a little, then covered them again with earth, restoring the grave to its former appearance.

The light of the sinking moon faintly silvered the tips of the branches overhead.

4. The Voice of Scent

A warm, savory smell drifted to her.

*...Jiiya’s baking flatbread.*

Just as she was thinking she ought to get up, and wake Mircha too, a terrible memory came flashing back like lightning. Pain stabbed through her chest. It spread from chest to throat to head like bitter water.

Aisha opened her mouth and sucked in air.

A flute-like sound came from her throat, and all at once the smell of the baking flatbread, the smell of smoke, and the scents of two men and a dog came rushing into her nose.

Among that flood of scents was Mircha’s. Their arms seemed to be touching; she could feel his warmth too.

*Mircha... he’s alive!*

She tried to open her eyes and look at him, but the world was spinning so violently that she shut them again and waited for the dizziness to ease.

Hearing a man say, “Looks like you’re awake,” Aisha struggled through the confusion in her head to understand what had happened.

*I drank milk wine laced with frostgrass...*

She had watched Mircha drain the milk wine into which the deadly frostgrass had been put, and then she herself had drunk it.

*Then why...?*

With her eyes still shut, Aisha sensed one of the men stand and come toward her. Even without opening her eyes, she knew who it was.

“You are awake, aren’t you, Lady Aisha Keruan?”

Aisha slowly opened her eyes and looked at the man. She was still a little dizzy, but even so she could see his face clearly.

A lean, sun-browned face. Black eyes fixed steadily on her.

“...Why?”

Perhaps noticing the rasp in her voice, the man turned and told someone behind him to bring water.

When it was brought, he gently slipped a hand beneath Aisha’s head and slowly raised her, supporting her as he did so. It was a large, thick, warm hand.

She took a sip from the wooden bowl and caught the scent of old cedar. The water had probably been carried in a barrel. Even so, to her parched throat it tasted delicious.

When she had drunk it all and handed the bowl back, the man took it from her in silence.

Aisha stared at him.

Whenever he moved, the smell of blue sweetgrass grew stronger. He must have kept it tucked inside his clothing for a long time. The scent had changed, mingling with the man’s own smell, but there was no mistaking what it was.

“Why...?”

She tried to ask again, but the man shook his head.

“There is no time to explain now. I will explain tonight. Until then, rest here.”

As she turned to look for her brother, the man rose and said,

“Your brother is safe too. He woke briefly a little while ago, then fell asleep again. I wrapped him in blankets, so he should not catch cold. You should warm yourself as well. I’m leaving one of my men behind as a guard; if anything happens, follow his instructions.”

With that, he strode back toward the fire, handed the wooden bowl to the man sitting beside it, plucked a piece of flatbread from a plate and put it in his mouth, then walked away into the trees.

Beside the man cooking over the fire sat a dog. It lay there, watching her.

“If you’re able to get up, would you like to sit here by the fire and have some flatbread?”

The man who spoke had a long face, but there was something sharp about him, like a hunter.

Aisha set a hand on the grass and slowly rose. The dizziness came over her again for a moment, but soon passed.

When she sat down by the fire, its warmth soaked into her skin, and only then did she realize how chilled to the core her body had been.

*Hirin, frostgrass, freezes the body.*

Anyone who ate that grass by accident was supposed to grow colder by the moment and die as though falling asleep, without even time to suffer.

*Why am I alive?*

The man deftly peeled a tapa flatbread from the little pan where it clung, fragrant and browned, laid it on a wooden plate, spread it thickly with cheese, drizzled honey over it, and handed it to her.

The instant she felt the warmth of the wooden plate in her palms, something hard that had enclosed her heart cracked open, and the sensation of being alive spread through her whole body.

She had thought there was no road left. That this was the end of her life.

She had imagined being beheaded. So when she learned that they meant to execute them with frostgrass, she had thought it might at least be easier than the sword. While she waited, she had been so afraid her bones shook. But when the moment actually came, as she watched her brother put the poisoned milk wine to his lips, all fear vanished, and everything in the world went black. She and her brother and everything else were sealed away inside a black darkness.

Only one thing had pierced it: the instant she caught, in the milk wine, the scent of that man’s fingers, a violent anger had shot through the center of her brow.

That they had to die because of their bloodline—the injustice of it, and the bitter frustration of leaving this world without being able to set that injustice right—had welled up and burned through her as anger at something she could not even name.

Slowly she lifted the flatbread from the warm wooden plate, folded it to an easy size, and put it in her mouth. The rich saltiness of the cheese and the sweetness of the honey mingled and filled it.

It was a taste she knew well.

At every meal since their capture, the flatbreads set before them had carried the scent of ingredients unfamiliar to her. But this was the flatbread she had always eaten, and when that familiar smell spread through the back of her nose, tears welled up.

Lowering her head so the man would not see them, Aisha took another mouthful of the warm, fragrant flatbread.

The dog lumbered to its feet, came over, and pressed its cold nose against her elbow.
