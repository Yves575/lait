Prologue

He had arranged to meet Zofia at eight. She had called that morning and asked to see him. He thought: No. He said: All right. Afterwards he regretted it, but by then it was too late. She sounded upset. She stammered into the receiver and kept gulping for air.

“I have to see you,” she said. “I wouldn’t ask if it weren’t really important.”

As always, he felt helpless before her quiet, low voice, before the way she carefully emphasized her ł’s and pronounced her ą’s too nasally, as though some small round creature with a lively little tail had settled deep in her throat and was tickling her larynx. He gave in, involuntarily moved before he had time to think, to remind himself that all those lovely-sounding syllables no longer belonged to him. Delicate, refined Zofia, with her charming speech defect, no longer even saw him. And yet she had called… Why? What for? Had something happened? And again he was seized by unease. Zofia had always been wrapped in an aura of mystery. That was what attracted him most. From the very beginning.

Every Thursday she arrived last. During the meetings she sat off to one side. She said very little, then slipped away quietly without saying goodbye to anyone. At first she made people uneasy, but in the end everyone got used to her silent presence. From time to time the facilitator asked her a question, and she answered briefly and matter-of-factly. The others soon lost interest in her. Everyone except him. He waited for Zofia every Thursday. He stole glances at her white hands, her damp brown eyes, the chestnut hair hiding her face. After a month, without asking permission, he changed places in the circle and sat against the wall. That day he asked her out for coffee. Two weeks later the facilitator summoned them for a serious talk. He forbade them any private contact and ordered them to sit apart. He spoke sharply and decisively, saying he had no intention of tolerating insubordination in the group. He was addressing himself mainly to Zofia.

“I’ve been observing you for some time,” he said. “I don’t understand why you come here at all. You ought to think seriously about that. You have a bad influence on the others.”

She listened calmly, then picked up her coat and handbag.

“All right.” She turned in the doorway. “I won’t be coming back.”

He ran after her. He knew he would have to give up the meetings, but he wasn’t worried about that at all. That was how the most beautiful three months of his life began.

He lowered his head and flinched, as though the sight of his own hands had startled and frightened him. The old feelings returned in a trembling, heaving wave. Three months, he winced. Three months in which he had believed he truly mattered to someone. It didn’t last long… The affair ended as abruptly as it had begun. They had not even had time to get to know each other properly. He knew so little about her… And yet he never asked her anything. He preferred love to flower slowly, to gather strength and momentum. He did not want to press her or hurry anything along. He dreamed of a holiday together, waited for the moment when she would finally introduce him to her parents; then he would move in with her, they would do the place up, put in a double bed… In time they would buy a bigger flat for the children… For three months he spun ever more elaborate plans for the future, calm and full of confidence. And then suddenly it was all over. Zofia stopped answering his calls. He went to see what was happening. He knocked on her door. She didn’t open, but he could not shake the feeling that someone was inside the flat. Sitting on the stairs, he heard a muffled murmur of voices, shuffling, the creak of floorboards. Then, for the first time in three months, he thought of the Thursday meetings and was gripped by fear. He went home. The next day he went back again. She opened the door, but did not let him in, blocking the entrance. At once it occurred to him that someone was hiding behind her. A lover? he thought, and such despair and weakness came over him that he had to lean against the wall. Zofia looked at him gravely with her large, damp eyes.

“I’m sorry,” she said. “I shouldn’t be handling this like this, but…”

He heard his own loud, ragged breathing. He felt himself going pale. His head was spinning.

“Something has happened,” she went on firmly. “We can’t go on seeing each other.”

That was all. Like the fall of a guillotine. No excuses, no explanations. They met once more, and she repeated exactly the same thing: certain changes had taken place in her life, they would not be seeing each other any more. Her eyes had lost their former softness. They had become cold and resolute. “Certain changes” could only mean there was someone else; of that he had no doubt.

The following Thursday he tried to return to the circle. They would not take him back, but the facilitator arranged individual sessions for him. He made no secret of how worried he was. He said the shock caused by his relationship with Zofia could have serious consequences, not only for him but for the whole group as well. He talked at length about responsibility, obligations, accepted rules of conduct. Then he questioned him closely about his relationship with Zofia: what they had done during those three months, where they had met, what they had talked about. It seemed to him that the facilitator was too persistent, too nosy, but he tried to answer all the same. Very little came of it. He was suffering from a kind of strange amnesia. Nothing remained in his memory except despair, slowly turning into fury and uncontrollable jealousy. If Zofia did not want to be with him, she could not, had no right, to get involved with anyone else!

But she had called today, he thought. Could it be that after all she had decided to apologise to him? His heart began to pound. Come back, make up, after using him and pushing him away? What on earth did she think she was doing? He felt humiliated at the thought of the past few weeks, of all those pleading attempts to get in touch, tearful phone calls, letters, sleepless nights. He kept trying to get close to Zofia, force her to talk, recover the old intimacy, which surely could not simply vanish, dissolve without leaving a trace… He still seemed to himself on the verge of finding the right words, convincing her, breaking the spell, so that the old gentle Zofia would once again stand before him, his Zofia! But she remained invariably indifferent, distant, alien… He sprang up from his chair and began pacing the room, lifting his hands to his face and tugging at the hair falling over his forehead. He went over to the window. He looked out at the wet, ugly city: squares covered in mud and melting snow, grey pavements, the colourless façades of the blocks opposite. Now and then a blurred figure flickered behind a pane. Outside the neighbourhood shop, a few people stamped their feet and huddled beneath umbrellas. She had used him as if he were a thing, a dead object that could be used up and then thrown away, not a living, feeling creature. A human being. A man, he corrected himself. Again he paced the room, wheeling sharply at the door and the opposite wall, only to come to a halt by the window a moment later. In every direction blocks stretched away by the dozen: heavy, grim, smelling faintly of mildew. In the small concrete flats strangers bustled about. Vertically and horizontally, identical concrete modules. Inside, the same tables, beds and armchairs. Almost identical men and women were cooking lunch, watching television together, going to the bathroom to take a hot bath or have a piss. Did she know, did she even suspect, how badly she had hurt him? He looked at his watch. It was nearly one.

He was beset by extreme irritation all day. He tried poking around online, watched two episodes of Brigada, a Russian series about the mafia. Early in the afternoon he went to the local gym. He ran for an hour, then lifted weights and worked his stomach. He tried to switch off, to think only about Sasha, Kosmos, Pchyola and Phil, the show’s brutal heroes, willing to take any risk. At the end he went to the sauna and took a cold shower. Back home, he took a carton of milk from the fridge and sat down at the table. He drank straight from the carton in long, slow gulps. The irritation hardened into a brutal rage. Once again he replayed the morning conversation in his head and came to the conclusion that Zofia had not called with reconciliation in mind. Her voice had sounded uncertain, but there had been no remorse in it. Short, matter-of-fact sentences, no “I’ve missed you” or “I’m glad I’m going to see you.” Just a perfunctory “Goodbye” at the end. Bitch, he thought, following a plane as it climbed. A ragged streak tore through the dense grey clouds. But what could Zofia want from him? For the hundredth time that day he asked himself the same question. It didn’t matter. This would be his moment. She had invited him, so she would politely listen to what he had to say. He pictured himself walking into the flat, telling her to sit down and speaking briefly, succinctly, in a firm tone, and at last she understands… And if she wouldn’t listen? He could hit her, even tie her up. Tape her mouth shut and watch her cry soundlessly, terrified, helpless, at his mercy. The plane climbed higher and disappeared above the clouds. In the flat upstairs someone put on loud dance music. He could hear muffled laughter and the sound of small, quick steps.

It was nearly half past six. He put the milk back in the fridge. He pulled on a short jacket with a ribbed hem, gloves and trainers. He drew a cotton hood over his head. Outside, a strong, piercing wind was blowing; wet snow clung to his shoes, which were soaked through at once. Even so, he did not turn towards the metro. He kept doggedly to the pavement along KEN Avenue, without stopping, without stepping aside for anyone. Zofia’s face rose before him—her wide mouth, straight nose, the tiny mole at the corner of her eye, and the way her gaze slid away to one side when she said, “You have to understand at last that I don’t want anything to do with you.” He remembered his own agitation, his fear, the burning feeling of rejection. How he had humiliated himself, made a fool of himself… He quickened his pace, until at last he broke into a run, driven by resentment and helpless anger. “Fuck your mother! I’ll shit in your mouth!” he kept repeating, unconsciously mimicking the voice of one of the heroes from Brigada. He did not stop until Służewiec. There he went down to the platform and took the metro to Racławicka, then set off again along the snowy street, passing the lit-up windows of cafés, workshops and little neighbourhood shops. Just beyond the school playing field he turned into a gateway and almost collided with an older man in a thick wool coat and a fur hat.

“Go fuck yourself!” he growled, then shoved past him brutally, pinning the man to the wall.

He was struggling to catch his breath. The thoughts kept circling, bringing on a sickening dizziness. Beads of sweat ran down his face.

Zofia got home before five. She put her shopping bag on the kitchen table, took off her soaked shoes and, leaving wet footprints on the floor, went into the bathroom. She lay in the bath thinking how complicated her life had become over the past three months. And whose fault is that? she wondered, wringing out the sponge. “Whose fault?” she repeated aloud. I attract bad luck like a magnet, she thought, getting up and taking the soap in her hand. The more I like someone, the bigger a psycho he turns out to be. But is it that, for some incomprehensible reason, only lunatics are attracted to me, or do I seek them out myself? She rinsed off the suds. She got out of the bath and wrapped a towel around her head. From a wicker basket she took grey tracksuit bottoms and warm woollen socks. For a moment she studied her face in the mirror. What am I supposed to do now? she asked her reflection. What am I supposed to do?

For all her worries, the woman in the mirror clearly had no intention of looking tired or neglected. Zofia pressed two fingers to her cheeks and drew the skin lightly towards her temples. She raised her brows, then reached for powder and mascara. Freshened up and carefully made up, she left the bathroom.

In the kitchen she made tea. She set out two cups and a plate of French biscuits. Suddenly she stopped in mid-step and stood for a moment staring at the laid table, as though trying to settle some important matter in her mind. After a moment she took out her mobile and twice, without success, called the same number. At last she left a message.

“I wanted to cancel our meeting,” she said, lowering her voice to give her words a firmer tone. “Something important has come up. I’m terribly sorry. I’ll call tomorrow.”

She looked at the phone uncertainly. There was no guarantee the message would be listened to. For a moment she stood drumming her fingers nervously on the windowsill. At last she shrugged and sat down in the window recess. It was nearly seven. She looked out at the small square veiled in fog and the abandoned building site fenced off from the street by a wooden hoarding. On a dark maple branch a lone rook perched. She ought to make definite decisions and weigh their consequences. Yet nothing sensible came to mind. No solution, even in theory, seemed any good. She was trapped, with no way out. The rook shook its head, ruffled its feathers and hobbled sideways towards the trunk. Zofia drew her knees up under her chin. The wind sent an empty plastic cup skittering down the dark street. She closed her eyes.

At a quarter past seven the intercom rang. She sighed and, without a word, pressed the button by the door. Then, on a sudden impulse, she snatched up the handbag lying beneath the coat rack. With a decisive movement she took a doctor’s certificate from it. On her way to the bathroom, she tore it into tiny pieces, then threw them into the toilet.

“I’m in the bathroom,” she called when she heard the door slam. “I’ll be right there,” she added, flushing the toilet.

At that moment she felt a heavy blow to her temple. One, then another. It didn’t hurt. She wasn’t afraid. An immense weariness came over her. “Who is it?” she wanted to ask, before she fell asleep, but blood ran from her open mouth. She tried once more to turn and look at her attacker, but managed only a moan before she sank into deep darkness.

At eight a bitter wind blew in off the icy river. At half past eight, because of a fault, the lamps along the embankment went out. Darkness fell. Only in the middle of the Vistula, where the current ran strongest, did the pale outline of a drifting ice floe loom through the murk. At nine a sweep of car headlights skimmed hurriedly across the water and vanished in the fog. In the brush a bird, woken from sleep, gave a sharp cry. On the escarpment the city rose, haloed with light. Above it another river spread itself. Broad and dark. Slowly the sleek, polished moon drifted onto it and glided smoothly between the clouds.

She came up out of sleep as out of a well. She opened her eyes and found herself lying beside a polar bear. It wasn’t a big one. Ada knew, though, that polar bears, even the smallest ones, were among the most dangerous animals in the world. She carefully turned onto her left side and curled up among the droppings that covered the sheet. He must have been fouling the bed all night, she thought. Something would have to be done about that in future, buy a litter box or something of the sort… The bear got heavily to its feet, slid down onto the floor and began circling the room, sniffing and skirting the snowdrifts.

“Get up, time for breakfast.” A pale, dishevelled woman sat down on the other side of the bed. She took the baby from her breast and laid her on the pillows. “Do you think I can leave her here?” she asked.

“Sure.” Ada was glad Kasia hadn’t noticed the soiled bedclothes. Otherwise she probably would not have laid the baby right in the middle of the filth.

“Sure,” she repeated. “The bear’s as gentle as a lamb. He adores babies.”

At that moment she was convinced she was telling the truth.

They put on their slippers, closed the door behind them and set off through the dimly lit hall. The unplaned boards creaked under their feet. The kitchen was narrow, dark, tiled the colour of tomato soup. A small barred window looked out onto a yard fenced with wire mesh. It smelled of burning and rotting rags. Kasia made tea in glasses and prepared sandwiches. It was only as they were returning to the room that Ada remembered that the day before the bear had eaten two puppies and the neighbour’s cat. There was nothing to be done now. She opened the door. The white beast lay on the bed, licking its muzzle. All that remained of the baby was a bloody stain on the pillow.

“Kasia,” she whispered, catching her sister by the shoulders. “Kasia…”

A desperate cry woke her. Filip-Oleńka stood at the foot of the bed, mewing. Kasia has no children—that was the first thought that came into her head. She felt a rush of relief. She sat up, ran her fingers through her short, tousled hair and stroked Filip-Oleńka, then got out of bed and, stumbling over the cat as he kept getting under her feet, made her way towards the kitchen. The first cold patches of dawn lay across the windowsill. A garbage truck pulled up outside the house; somewhere high beneath the whitening sky, terns were crying. She switched on the light. The kitchen looked nothing like the one from her dream. Light, clean floorboards painted white, wooden cupboards, a large square window, the whole thing designed exactly out of an IKEA catalogue. She took two ice cubes from the fridge and carefully rubbed them over her face, switched on the coffee machine and went to the bathroom to shower. Five minutes later she stood, clean and wrapped in a towel, before the open wardrobe. Perfectly ironed shirts and trousers hung in colour-coordinated rows: beige, grey, navy, green, like in an expensive clothes shop. Even the knickers on the shelves had been folded into neat little white squares. Ada took out a pair of narrow jeans, an orange T-shirt and a brown sweater with a wide roll-neck. She dressed quickly and sat down at an antique-style dressing table.

She was nearing forty, and yet she had kept her slim figure and her light, girlish way of moving. She had grown used to the thought that she still looked like a student. That had still been true ten years earlier, but time had moved on relentlessly, leaving its merciless mark on her fair, delicate face—a mark she had stubbornly decided not to notice. The skin on her cheeks had slackened and sagged slightly around her chin. Her eyes, dark and watchful, had lost their former brightness, replaced by an expression of cool, matter-of-fact assurance, while her wide mouth, oddly as though cut the wrong way round, had acquired the shy parentheses of its first wrinkles. Ada took powder, blusher and mascara from the drawer. With a few short, decisive strokes of the brush she soon acquired the look of someone whose age could not be pinned down in any way at all. She swept her dark, ragged fringe to one side, tucked her hair behind her ears, then nodded to her reflection as if greeting someone coolly, reservedly, while brusquely according the respect that was due.

She went back to the kitchen and switched off the coffee machine. Filip-Oleńka leapt onto the table so suddenly and so silently that she started and spilled coffee.

“No,” she admonished him sharply.

She added milk and sat down over the steaming cup. For a moment she stared at the greying square of the window. The last remnants of the dream peeled away from her slowly. All it left behind was a vague unease, and Ada brushed it aside irritably. The cat went on mewing.

“Not now!” she said, raising her voice and trying to make it sound firm, yet unable to suppress a smile as she watched the animal shifting from paw to paw, arching its back and striking more and more ingratiating poses.

“You can’t eat so much, you’ll get fat, and I don’t think we want that,” she said more gently, and went out into the hall. Filip-Oleńka trotted after her, soft and stubborn, getting in her way. She moved him aside. She put on her coat, gloves and high brown leather boots, grabbed her handbag and closed the door behind her.

Inspector Marcin Sawicki forced the old wheezing Opel into a sharp turn.

“Police, asshole!” he shouted at the driver of the horn-blaring Nissan whose path he had just cut off, then braked on the pavement right in front of a No Parking sign.

“Woman,” he muttered, struggling out of his seat belt. “Woman,” he repeated, walking down the street in a direction he absolutely should not have been walking in. He passed the Coffee Bar, the stocking shop, the kiosk and pushed open the door of a Starbucks. He shuddered, then, against all common sense, ordered a double espresso and a chicken bagel. He paid eighteen zlotys and immediately felt an unpleasant stab of guilt. Nearly twenty, he thought in horror as he waited for the coffee. Money you’ll never get back. He tried to work out how much he spent every month on food away from home, but at once stopped, because it occurred to him that on top of that there were cigarettes, beer and the chocolate bars he was addicted to. He was nearly six foot three, but now, standing there leaning against the counter, jamming his elbow between bowls of white and brown sugar and nervously picking at a paper napkin printed with Have a nice day, he seemed to be shrinking, caving in on himself. His shoulders hunched, his stomach thrust out, and his broad, friendly face took on the unhappy expression of a wronged little boy. He could feel his cheeks trembling.

“Espresso-bagel!” called a pretty dark-haired girl in a company apron.

He started, gave the waitress the look of a beaten dog and picked up his order. With tired steps he went over to a table. He had not slept a wink the night before. He had spent long hours in front of the computer, watching episode after episode of an American TV series, masturbating during the uncensored sex scenes and hoping the darkness outside the window would never be broken by the pink streak of dawn. But dawn came. Not pale pink, but cadaverously blue, and Sawicki had to get up and face reality.

This is not good, he thought, swallowing the last bites of the bagel after first removing the rocket. He had nothing against women working in the police, for example as secretaries. Even in uniform. But a woman in criminal investigations… a woman detective… and in Terror, on top of that… Detective—or maybe detective-ette, he thought with a bitter laugh as he finished his coffee.

Detective or detective-ette, Sawicki would have to babysit her for the next few months, until finally someone upstairs, whose mind had evidently gone, came to his senses and transferred her. He had no doubt that the situation was temporary. Nobody in their right mind thought a woman could be an operative—whatever the feminine of that would even be. He had heard, to be sure, of such cases: women detectives, butch women with no families, no friends, no personal lives, possessed by a mania for hunting criminals. Supposedly they were good at it. But no female detective had worked in the field at Mostowski Palace for a very long time. And everyone was happy with that.

It was after nine. He put down his cup, wiped his mouth on a napkin printed with Wonderful coffee, wonderful life, got heavily to his feet and left the café. It was the last day of February. All of Warsaw was waiting for spring in a state of tension, but spring was in no hurry to come. The pale winter sun had climbed above the rooftops, striking from the snow thousands of glittering flashes like thousands of painful little needles. On the plastic roofs of the tram stops, ruffled pigeons had settled themselves, cooing and pattering on the spot as they followed every passing car with dull eyes. Stunted leafless trees stood in a disciplined row along the pavements. A crowd swaddled in down jackets and woollen hats streamed across Bank Square, the hats worn this season in an exceptionally idiotic way, shoved back from the forehead to the middle of the head. Sawicki narrowed his eyes, gave a soft groan and, wishing sinus infections on all the fashionably dressed passers-by, headed towards Mostowski Palace.

A few people were pacing nervously in front of reception: a young man in a short military jacket with a painfully thin girl whose hair was dyed pink, an older gentleman, and two men in work overalls. The duty officer was nowhere to be seen. Sawicki opened the gate with his magnetic card, crossed the inner courtyard and, dragging his feet, went up to the second floor. The door to the secretariat stood open.

“Good morning. Cold, isn’t it?” From behind her desk, half hidden by the green fern on it, Dagmara shook her teased blond mane. “Un-bear-able.”

“Cold,” Sawicki admitted, rubbing his whitened hands.

The secretary gave him a kindly smile, sniffed, and went back to staring into space. He glanced at the blue elephant-shaped wall clock hanging just above her head. He was almost twenty minutes late; that woman must already be waiting. Or maybe she hadn’t come at all? The thought flickered through his mind. Maybe she had changed her mind, or been transferred somewhere else—to Kraków, perhaps, or Olsztyn or Białystok. Who knew what might have happened in the past month…

“Any messages for me?” he asked Dagmara.

“No,” she replied, turning her head with cool, self-satisfied slowness.

“Anything worth mentioning… today?” he pressed.

“Noooo,” she repeated, touching the tip of her nose with her forefinger. “No.”

For some reason, her carefully made-up face and her jangling earrings of coloured glass irritated Sawicki more than usual, with an irritation so sudden and violent that he could barely keep his voice under control.

“Did anyone ask for me? Dagmara, please concentrate!” he said much too loudly.

The secretary jumped, shook her peroxide fringe and opened her eyes wide in a stare of almost hysterical intensity.

“Oh yes,” she admitted at last, and her face once again took on that expression of vague satisfaction. “The deputy chief is waiting with some lady in the briefing room. The new one, because they’re renovating the old one… Have been for a month now,” she added by way of explanation.

“Really?” he hissed. “I had no idea.”

“Really,” she agreed cheerfully. “A pipe burst upstairs and—”

“I know, Dagmara, I work here too!” he cut in with cold, undisguised brutality. “What I would like, however, is for important information to reach me. That’s what we employ you here for!”

“But there wasn’t any message,” she protested calmly, lowering her voice like a well-brought-up child. “That lady just came in and is waiting with Rogowski. In the briefing room… the new one…” she added uncertainly.

Sawicki sighed, ran a hand through what was left of his ginger hair, and trudged down the corridor. Before he reached the door, he regretted his outburst. I’ll have to apologise to her, he decided, and turned the handle.

Two people were waiting inside: a slight dark-haired woman whose face he still could not make out, and Artur Rogowski, deputy head of the Metropolitan Police’s Criminal Terror and Homicide Division, known to his colleagues as Rog.

“You’re twenty minutes late,” Rog growled, though relief was written all over his face. Clearly he had reckoned with the possibility that Sawicki might not show up at all. “This is Adela Rochniewicz…” He coughed and wiped his bald skull with a linen handkerchief that looked heavily used. “Meet one another.”

He stood there hot and slovenly, narrowing bloodshot eyes and clearing his throat noisily. He plainly had no idea how to behave. So he shifted from foot to foot and kept rubbing his forehead with the dirty handkerchief until the silence in the room became distinctly too awkward. At last he gave a slight grimace, as if he wanted to say, Sorry, mate. What he actually said was only:

“I’ll leave you two to it and get back to my office,”

and he went out, hitching up his slipping trousers, coughing and blowing his nose noisily, which struck Sawicki as even more revolting than usual.

Ada Rochniewicz was sitting sideways to the door. Slanting rays fell in through the window, brightening the small room grandly called the “new briefing room.” Trembling patches of sunlight lay across the wall and the grey carpet. Bright motes of dust danced in the air. Ada, however, remained in shadow. Sawicki could see only the delicate, fragile line of her back and her bent head, and the sight of it allowed him, for a moment, to regain his balance. He walked over to the table with a purposeful stride. She was pretty. Expensively dressed, fashionable but restrained, as though she were going out to lunch at a restaurant rather than to a meeting at police headquarters. She could not be more than thirty, with a short fringe and a wide, oddly shaped mouth that gave her face a faintly tearful look. And yet he had a vague feeling that there was something else behind that innocent little face. Something he should not underestimate.

“Marcin Sawicki,” he said brusquely.

“Good morning,” she replied.

“Sorry I’m late,” he muttered, sitting down. “Traffic… I understand you’d like to work here,” he began, taking a sheaf of yellowed papers from his briefcase. It was a copy of the cable-TV contract he had signed a few months earlier, but he felt surer of himself with a stack of documents laid out in front of him. “Apart from this”—he tapped the contract with a finger, at the same time covering the company’s colourful logo with his elbow—“is there anything else I should know?”

For the first time she looked him straight in the eye. She wasn’t smiling, but the corners of her mouth quivered slightly, as if she were suppressing amusement. He clenched his teeth.

“What work experience do you have? Would you say you’re available at short notice?” he went on, even though she had not answered a single one of his earlier questions.

He knew he was making himself ridiculous, playing prospective employer for a complete stranger when they both knew perfectly well that he had absolutely no influence over the decision about the transfer. And yet it suddenly seemed to him that by sheer force of will and stubbornness he might make her retreat. He would win this last skirmish, hand back her papers and say, “I’m sorry, we have better candidates for this position. Perhaps next time.”

“I was sent here,” she said calmly.

“Yes, yes… So you’re looking for work in the police. In the police,” he repeated, putting special emphasis on the last word, hoping this would somehow prove the firmness of his own character and disguise the obvious absurdity of the situation.

“I’m not looking.” Ada’s tone did not change. “I’ve been assigned here.”

She had to be over thirty. At first her slim, girlish figure had misled him. Now he could clearly see the fine wrinkles at the corners of her eyes and the horizontal lines running across her forehead.

“Police work involves a great many hardships,” he went on, hoping to throw her off balance at last. “Especially for women. Here, madam, we operate under conditions difficult to endure even for a man in his prime. You never know when or where we’ll be called out. A young girl like you… How are you going to balance family responsibilities, a husband, children, with—”

“I don’t have children,” she cut in. “And I don’t intend to.”

He thought he heard the promise of unease in those words. At once he lifted his head, but saw nothing in her face that he could seize on and use.

“I make no secret of it, though I know these are questions you shouldn’t be asking,” she said quietly and calmly, but her dark eyes remained impassive and alert as a surgeon’s. “I didn’t come here for a job interview, and you know that perfectly well. You’re not my superior, only my new colleague. I’d rather we finally stopped acting out this peculiar comedy.”

Sawicki’s resolve began to melt with startling speed.

“That depends on whether we can come to an understanding,” he muttered. He tried to keep his dignity, but his body refused to obey, and to his horror he realised that he was fidgeting on his chair and reflexively wiping his sweaty palms on the tabletop.

“That’s true.” Ada smiled calmly. “I hope we’ll manage somehow. And now perhaps I could have a look around headquarters and meet the rest of the team?”

“Tomorrow!” he almost shouted. “Yes, tomorrow, because tomorrow’s the first… You don’t start until tomorrow…” He tried to preserve at least the faintest aftertaste of victory.

“Ada,” she cut in quickly. “My name is Ada.”

“Right, Ada.” He cleared his throat. “We’ve got renovation work going on today, and it’s complete chaos. Some of the boys are out in the field… So let’s start tomorrow at eight.”

“All right. See you tomorrow, then.”

She stood up, took her bag, and vanished before he could answer.

Stunned, Sawicki stared blankly at the wall. Was that it? Was it over? Was it settled? The whole conversation had taken less than five minutes. He had been beaten, laid flat on his back with one gentle gesture by that tiny little woman with the look of an ageing ingenue. He ran a hand over his face.

Ada Rochniewicz had spent eight years with the Wrocław police. He had been told as much a month earlier, along with the news that from the beginning of March he would be getting a female colleague, a woman cop, a woman detective, neither fish nor fowl, and there would be no appealing his superiors’ decision. He knew very little else about her.

At the beginning of February, after a talk with the chief, Rogowski came back to their shared office and sat down at his desk, coughing and sniffling.

“A chick? You sure?” Sawicki asked without taking his eyes off the computer.

“A chick, all right. The old man’s working on warming up our image. Our political correctness will shoot up once we take her on.”

He kept shifting on his chair. Sawicki knew Rog was glancing at him uneasily, but he stubbornly went on tapping at the keyboard. He had plenty of work and did not feel like talking. Rogowski snorted and blew his nose loudly into a checked handkerchief. Sawicki stuck out his lower lip.

“What?” he asked at last, hitting Delete instead of Tab and wiping out the field of the report he had just painstakingly filled in.

“You’re supposed to take charge of her,” Rogowski explained, and at once dissolved into the dry cough of a habitual smoker.

Sawicki was dumbstruck. For a moment he stared at the monitor, then slapped the desk with his open hand.

“Have you lost your fucking mind? I don’t have enough work as it is, and now I’m supposed to babysit some broad as well?” he snapped. “This was your idea, right?” His voice rose higher and higher as Rogowski’s coughing fit grew worse. “Get it out of that bald head of yours.”

He was seriously angry. Rogowski was deputy head of the division, but everyone knew he unofficially ran the section. He made most of the decisions. Sawicki jumped up and walked out of the room.

The next day, when his anger had cooled a little, they managed to talk calmly.

“It’s not so bad,” Rogowski said in a soothing tone. “I saw her file. She’s done the officers’ course. Before that, nursing school… Apparently she’s a good investigator. She solved a few serious cases, you’ll be satisfied.”

Sawicki shrugged.

“Eight years with the Wrocław police,” Rog went on, undeterred. “Glowing letters of recommendation. A commendation for taking part in breaking up the BAF organised crime ring,” he declaimed like a boy reciting a poem at a school assembly on Constitution Day.

Sawicki remembered hearing something about it. Seven people had been arrested and forty kilos of cocaine seized, meant to be smuggled across the German border.

“But…” Rogowski added, less confidently now, “in 2010 she was transferred to SDS. Desk work…”

Sawicki raised his eyebrows in surprise. An office drone? Why were they sending her back to criminal investigations now, of all times? After two years of warming a chair and filling in paperwork, you didn’t just go back out on the streets…

“It doesn’t add up,” he said.

“I know. Unless…”

“Unless someone high up really wanted Adela Rochniewicz, nurse-turned-investigator, transferred to Warsaw and pulled the right strings,” he finished angrily. “Does she have a husband? Any kids?”

“The marital-status box was blank.”

“Why am I not surprised?” he muttered.

They said nothing more about her until yesterday, when Rogowski told him to report to the briefing room at nine the next morning and look after the new Inspector.

Sawicki sighed, stretched his arms out in front of him, laced his fingers together and twisted his hands until his joints cracked, then slowly got up and went down to reception to buy coffee from the vending machine. The men in overalls were dozing on wooden benches against the wall, the young man in the military jacket was cursing loudly, and the underfed girl was trying to calm him down. A large blue sign above the counter read: CALLERS RECEIVED HERE. The duty policeman was still nowhere in sight. On his way back upstairs, he collected some forms from the secretariat and decided that today he would devote himself entirely to paperwork.

3

On Friday morning Sawicki woke feeling almost rested. He had slept for seven hours. He got up a few minutes after six. He dressed quietly and limited his ablutions to brushing his teeth. He wanted to drink his coffee alone, in peace. He put the kettle on. The thermometer outside the kitchen window read zero. The sky was covered in cloud. Heavy wet snow began to fall. At seven he woke his wife and little daughters. He got to work just before eight. Reception was completely quiet, not a trace of the public. The duty officer was dozing, his head propped on one hand. Upstairs, the door to the secretariat was closed. As he walked down the corridor, he wondered whether Adela Rochniewicz was already waiting for him. He had no doubt that punctuality was one of her virtues. Punctuality, order, common sense, he thought, taking off his jacket and glancing uncertainly round the room. Stacks of papers towered on both desks. The cupboard held heaps of clothes of uncertain origin and old shoes rank with sweat. A potted flower was drying out on the window. Algae had taken up residence in the water dispenser. He wiped the dust off the sill with his sleeve, cleared the papers from the desk that had belonged to Rogowski until now, and cautiously peered into the drawer. He found a rotting sandwich in a plastic bag and an empty vodka bottle.

Ada arrived at three minutes past eight. She shook his hand briefly and firmly, like a man. She took off her navy-style jacket and a fancy little hat with a coloured trim. Where does she get the money for clothes like that? he wondered. Those little sweaters, wool shorts, elegant boots? She must be wearing the equivalent of two police salaries. Watching the new Inspector out of the corner of his eye, he was unpleasantly reminded of his own wife’s cheap, garish clothes.

“Come on, then, I’ll introduce you to the boys.” He forced a smile.

He wanted to get it over with as quickly as possible. He felt awkward leading her down the corridor, opening one door after another and repeating, “This is Inspector Adela Rochniewicz. Starting today, she’ll be working with us.” Ada greeted people briefly, politely. Her face wore a cool, official expression. Not once did she show the slightest curiosity. She paid no attention to the low whistles and laughter behind her back. When they entered the biggest room on the floor, several policemen looked up in astonishment. They seemed genuinely surprised. Ada fitted into police headquarters about as well as the Queen of England into a baseball club’s changing room.

“What a delicate flower he’s found himself…” muttered Kapusta, a fair-haired policeman with a pockmarked, unhealthy complexion and a flushed forehead. He said it quietly, as if only to himself, but everyone heard him perfectly well. A ripple of smothered snickering ran through the room. Sawicki clenched his jaw. Delicate flower? Where the hell had that idiot picked up expressions like that? Ada’s face remained impassive.

In the secretariat, Dagmara took doughnuts out of the fridge and rushed to switch on the electric kettle.

“I knew you’d be coming today, so I was prepared,” she chattered. “You have to sit down for a minute and have coffee with me. Really, you must come in here often! Sawicki’s such a boor,” she added, completely ignoring Marcin. “It’s hard enough working with men. But you know that…”

Dark mascara had smudged around her left eye. Beneath her pink blouse, her heavy breasts swayed. She had not even had time to take out the cups when Rogowski’s round bald head appeared in the doorway.

“A murder’s been reported in Upper Mokotów,” he said. “Looks like some young chick’s had her head smashed in. You two are going. Kapusta and Chlor are interviewing the car-smuggling lot. Wiktor and I are taking Stary over to Praga.”

“But she only just got here…” Sawicki protested reluctantly.

“All the better, let her get broken in straight away. I don’t know how they do things in Wrocław, but here we don’t start the day by eating pastries.”

“A girl with her head smashed in?” he tried again. “Can’t the local guys deal with it? We’ve got plenty of our own work.”

“They’re already there.” Rogowski cleared his throat unpleasantly. “The district cops from Precinct Two, the ambulance. And Modzelewski,” he added pointedly. Then he stuck out a plump hand, grabbed a doughnut and disappeared.

Ada raised her eyebrows.

“Modzelewski’s a prosecutor,” Dagmara explained quickly. “He always calls in the city detectives. For every murder. He must be on duty today.”

“And we’re the unlucky ones,” Sawicki finished.

It was nearly nine when they left headquarters. The snow had stopped, and a pale, distant sun shone faintly through the clouds. The inner courtyard was coated with ice, streaked with trickles of brown water. Slipping on the surface, they made their way in silence to the Opel and headed for Mokotów. The morning traffic jam had just ended. They left the siren off. Fifteen minutes later they were there. They parked outside a four-storey pre-war tenement and went through a narrow gateway into the courtyard. From a leafless tree, a pair of ruffled crows watched them. Sawicki pushed open the stairwell door, propped with a brick. Ada could feel the skin at the nape of her neck go numb. A cold shiver ran down her spine. It suddenly hit her that she was beginning an investigation. She was back in the field. Two years had passed since the accident that had shattered her settled life in Wrocław. Two years spent as an outcast, hemmed in by hostility and distrust. Now she was coming back to work. Once again, she could do what she was best suited to and what she was truly good at. The long-forgotten thrill returned, then the moment of concentration, and finally a sudden, bracing rush of energy, like the one a wrestler feels just before stepping onto the mat. Ada drew a deep breath, passed Sawicki, and went up the stairs first.

On the landing between the second and third floors, two identical-looking men were waiting, dressed in matching leather jackets and the same sharply creased wool trousers. Both were smoking, lifting their cigarettes to their mouths with the same quick, nervous movement. Both took short drags and blew the smoke out loudly through half-open mouths. Ada had the impression that someone had set a mirror on the half-landing.

“These are the famous Mokotów twins.” Sawicki laughed at the sight of her astonishment. “Inspector Rochniewicz,” he said by way of introduction.

“Hi,” they replied in unison.

She nodded.

“So you’re taking over the case.” The twin on the left rubbed his hands together with satisfaction.

“Modzelewski’s already gone. You’re to drop the paperwork at the office this evening,” said the one on the right. “The ambulance crew was here too. They pronounced her dead. The mortuary people will be here in forty minutes. At least that’s what they say.”

“We didn’t call the undertakers,” they added simultaneously, and began zipping up their jackets.

Just then they heard sobbing. In front of the open door on the third floor sat a pale, dishevelled girl in an unbuttoned coat. Right beside her, a uniformed policeman stood with his back against the wall, leafing through a morning tabloid. Plainly, the crying made not the slightest impression on him.

“And… that’s Monika Lewicka. A friend of the deceased,” the twin on the right explained.

“She’s the one who reported the murder,” added the one on the left.

Sawicki nodded.

“Right, we’re off. Have fun.”

They turned as if on command and clattered down the stairs. The uniformed policeman followed them.

“Just a moment.” Ada stopped him mid-step. “Please buy this lady some tea.” She pointed with her chin at the sobbing girl. “From the café downstairs.”

The policeman stopped, surprised.

“Do you have some kind of problem?” Ada raised her voice.

“A problem?” His eyes bulged and his moustache twitched nervously.

“A problem,” she repeated sharply. “You’ve been standing here reading the paper for an hour, and my witness is about to faint. Have you lost every human instinct?”

The policeman went crimson.

“Tea?” he spluttered. “With my own money?”

Ada opened her wallet.

“Here.” She took out a ten-zloty note.

He recoiled in outrage and opened his mouth to protest, but shut it at once when he saw her knitted brows. He sighed, shuffled on the spot like a scolded dog, then reluctantly took the money.

“One more thing!” she called after him.

“Yes?”

“Bring it up in twenty minutes, not a minute sooner. And make sure it’s hot. With sugar,” she added pointedly.

He nodded obediently, checked his watch and went down the stairs.

Sawicki was so amused by the sight of the moustachioed policeman scuttling off in panic that he bit his lips to keep from bursting out laughing.

“Monika Lewicka?” he said to the crying girl. “You called the police?”

“Yes…” she answered, trying unsuccessfully to stifle her sobs. “She’s inside, in the bathroom…” she added with effort, lifting her head and brushing the hair back from her swollen face.

He pushed the half-open door. Just beyond the threshold was a puddle of vomit.

“I’m sorry,” she mumbled. “That was me…”

Ada took a large step over it and went in first. Sawicki followed her. She stopped in the bathroom doorway. He looked over her shoulder, started, and lightly touched her back. She turned her head. Their eyes met for a moment. On the tiled floor a dead woman lay face down. Her arms and legs were sprawled limply, as though she had fallen from a great height. Clotted blood matted her long hair. Ada crouched over the body. There was not the slightest hesitation in her movements. She pulled on a pair of gloves. Did she always carry them in her jacket pocket? flashed through his mind. Gently she brushed aside the matted hair and felt the back of the skull. There was something uncanny and unreal in the sight of those two women, the dead one and the living one, the living one as indifferent as the corpse at her feet. It was not Sawicki’s first crime scene. He was used to the sight of mangled bodies too. And yet he had expected something more from his new colleague than a stone face and a pair of white gloves. What exactly? Tears? Hand-wringing? He knew she was an experienced officer… She straightened slowly.

“I think we should call the crime-scene team,” she said calmly.

Ada had sensed Sawicki’s tension at once. For a moment, something like sympathy stirred in her for this big, strong man, so clearly afraid of her reaction. She did not want to embarrass him. It was a young woman, in her twenties. Slim, athletic. Ada had noticed the muscular calf. There was not much blood. It had flowed from the wound at the temple. The skull there was misshapen, horribly caved in. Whoever had dealt the blow had struck very hard. The eyes, wide open, had clouded over and were covered with a yellow film. The lips and nostrils had turned a bluish brown. She lifted the edge of the sweatshirt to assess the lividity, then stood up.

“She’s been dead at least four hours,” she said, and tried to slip past Sawicki, but he stopped her and pointed down. Right beside the toilet lay a discarded object.

“The murder weapon…”

“Yes.” He nodded. “The killer left it.”

For a while they stared blankly at the bloodstained hammer. The wooden handle bore a clear stamp: AM-NAT HARDWARE STORE.

“That’s very strange,” she said at last.

Sawicki started and took his mobile from his pocket.

While he was calling the crime-scene technicians, Ada took in the pleasant, spacious bedroom: clean floor, neatly made bed, armchair with a folded tartan blanket on it. She smiled in spite of herself. She liked order. Reflexively, she straightened a black-and-white print that had come slightly loose from the wall. Pale curtains hung at the window. No net curtains. She went into the kitchen: smooth white tiles, a broad teak table, a sink with a pull-out tap, a dishwasher, an electric grill… Everything breathed an air of bourgeois comfort. She had recently read about wealthy Varsovians buying flats in neglected pre-war tenements. Praga and Old Mokotów were fashionable now. On the table stood a porcelain teapot painted with forget-me-nots, two teacups and a plate of biscuits.

She went back into the hall. In the open doorway she saw Sawicki, leaning against the metal railing in the stairwell, questioning Monika Lewicka.

“Police,” he said to the curious neighbours gathering on the stairs. “Please go back to your homes and do not interfere with the investigation. If we need your help, we’ll come to you ourselves,” he added in such a sharp, decisive tone that the stairwell emptied at once.

She headed towards the next room.

“Marcin!” she called suddenly. “Come here. I think you’ll want to see this.”

The small room contained nothing but a desk with a laptop on it connected to a large monitor, a chair and an old chest of drawers. All the drawers had been pulled out. Papers, folders and cardboard boxes lay gutted on the floor. Sawicki’s eyes went wide.

“This can’t have been an ordinary robbery,” he said, surprised. “No sign of a break-in. The computer and tablet were left on the desk. Together they must be worth a good few thousand.”

“The other rooms are in perfect order,” Ada said.

“It looks as if someone was looking for something specific and knew where to find it… He must have had a key of his own and surprised our victim in the bathroom.”

“I don’t think so. I think she let him in herself. In any case, she was expecting someone. There are two empty cups and biscuits in the kitchen.”

A little later the crime-scene technicians arrived. Two solemn men in protective coveralls, gloves and blue plastic overshoes stretched police tape across the doorway and stepped into the flat. They placed numbered markers beside the body, the discarded hammer and the streaks of blood on the bathtub, then set about securing the traces. The camera flash kept firing. Ada stood motionless in the bedroom doorway.

“You need to leave now,” said the round-faced technician. “We’ll be done in an hour.”

Downstairs a door banged shut. Footsteps drew nearer. A little man in a white coat protruding from beneath a short padded jacket was clambering up the stairs. There was something strange and unsettling about him. His head and hands were disproportionately large for the rest of him; he seemed to have stopped just short of deformity, standing not quite a metre and a half tall. Ada caught the sharp smell of formalin and male sweat. In spite of herself she recoiled in disgust. The little man peered into the flat and, without speaking to anyone, headed straight for the bathroom with brisk, determined steps.

“That’s Perv from the morgue,” Sawicki whispered. “Best not to bother him. He’s not exactly pleasant…”

Five minutes later the little man appeared in the stairwell.

“No two ways about it, somebody clobbered her over the head,” he said, shooting quick, nervous glances from under his bristling brows. “No way she did this to herself.” He gave a thin, unpleasant laugh. “This wasn’t an accident,” he added, bending the fingers of his left hand so hard the joints cracked. “She’s been dead at least four hours. That’s all I can tell you for now. I don’t have time to fool around with you,” he went on, struggling for breath. “There are three hides queued up for the dance over at Oczki. Call the undertakers and get back to me on Monday.”

He left without saying goodbye to anyone. An awkward silence fell. Sawicki felt around for his mobile. At last he found it in the inside pocket of his jacket and dialled the funeral service.

Monika Lewicka was still sitting on the stairs. She had stopped sobbing, but she was very pale and shaking as though with ague. Sawicki touched her shoulder.

“I’m sorry, but we need to ask you a few more questions,” he said, and Ada heard a gentler note in his voice. “The first few hours after the discovery of a murder are the most important…”

“I understand,” she said with a nod, and began to cry again.
