He had barely had time to make himself decent before a man seized him by the shoulder and dragged him away.

Trying to comfort his friend made Valentyn feel stronger still. He opened his notebook and wrote that everything would be all right, there was no reason to worry and they would surely be home before nightfall, because the bus was about to give up the ghost. To show off, though he was not sure how to spell the word, he wrote that a connecting rod was about to go. And when thick smoke came pouring from under the bonnet and the driver let loose a torrent of curses, Valentyn, without bothering to hide his satisfaction, bent over a blank page and wrote in large letters They’re fucked, we’ll be home for dinner. But on that point, Valentyn was wrong.

At first light, Veronika and Lilya leave the house and head for the town hall, where the Russian command has set itself up. Veronika has known famous fits of anger, but never one like the fury driving her now. Invading a country, bombing towns, decimating whole populations to serve the megalomania of a declining dictator—wasn’t that enough for them? Did they now have to abduct children as well? Was this what those conquerors had been reduced to, commanded by incapable generals, mercenaries forming the battalions of an army without honour?

As for bravery, the soldier on guard outside the town hall has little of it left. He has no idea what the hell he is doing in this lost corner so far from home. A young man barely twenty-five, happy to be among those who took a small town without having to fight any major battle, happier still to be alive. He tells them where the office of his superior in charge of the occupied population is. The town’s next mayor, he explains, once Ukraine has been liberated.

“Excellent news. Then you’ll soon be going home,” Veronika snaps, pulling her daughter along.

They move down the corridor with an almost military stride, their heels ringing out so loudly that those they pass do not dare ask where they are going. Lilya spots the commandant’s office, Veronika takes a deep breath and opens the door.

“Where is my son?” she asks as she enters the room.

The officer, who had been dozing with his head slumped over a desk, starts and stares at the two women.

“What is your son’s name?” he asks with a yawn.

“Valentyn Khodova!”

The man straightens up, turns with a sigh and takes from a shelf the file in which the names of the fighters captured or killed at the front are recorded.

“Don’t pretend to be looking for his name. He’s nine years old, one of the two children you kidnapped yesterday at noon from their school. Are you going to pretend you know nothing about it?”

His expression changes. A week earlier, it was he who had received the instruction to carry out this operation, whose usefulness had at first seemed doubtful to him. As if he did not already have enough problems to deal with. But the call had come directly from Moscow—more important still, from the Kremlin. Few officers of his rank had the right to such an honour. The call had lasted only a few minutes, but he would never forget the voice, soft and benevolent, of the chairwoman of the commission on children’s rights, making no display of the power her closeness to the president conferred on her. Instead of using the threatening tone to which his superiors had accustomed him, she had carefully explained how important what he was to do would be. What cause could be nobler than protecting orphans and minors from the dangers the Ukrainian rebellion exposed them to? People who accused Russia of the worst evils, though incapable of caring about what would become of their own offspring. Saving orphans was a priority, she had repeated before hanging up.

Orphan: a detail he may have omitted to specify to the unit he had dispatched. But then, thinking about it, why would he have done so, when he had been assured the establishment concerned was an orphanage?

And yet this mother is asking for her child; perhaps his sources of intelligence are not so reliable after all. He will find whoever is responsible for this blunder. Thinking it over again, it seems to him that the chairwoman of the commission on children’s rights had also mentioned children living in precarious circumstances. Enough to reassure him as to the perfect legitimacy of his mission, since the whole town is living precariously.

“This dilapidated building—you call that a school?” he says, snapping his ledger shut.

“You bombed us. Most of the time we live without water or electricity. Are you going to reproach us now for our cracked walls and shattered windows?”

“Even so, children need care your institutions are incapable of providing, least of all in this region. We are showing infinite kindness and spending precious money to come to their aid. You ought to be grateful and thank us; the government of Russia has a duty to protect minors, whatever their nationality.”

Veronika is about to ask him how many children have died beneath the bombs his government has dropped on hundreds of schools, maternity wards, hospitals or public parks, how many others are now orphans, but reason forces her to keep silent.

“My son does not need care, and certainly not yours!” she replies in an icy voice. “The civilians your men are shooting at point-blank range, on the other hand, are very much in need of mine.”

“Well then, go back to your work,” the officer retorts.

“Where is my son?” Veronika insists again, ready to commit murder.

The man walks over to the window and looks out into the street.

“On his way to a reception centre. Our specialists will question him and, after assessing his condition, decide what is best for him.”

“My son won’t answer your questions. He cannot speak.”

“And you were saying he didn’t need any care? What kind of parent are you?”

Lilya sees the fire burning in her mother’s eyes. She takes her hand to remind her that she is by her side, and takes it upon herself to address the officer.

“We don’t want to cause you any trouble. We just want to find my brother, know that he is all right, and know where you have taken him. Please.”

The girl’s restraint surprises the officer. He tells himself she will be easier to win over, perhaps she will even help him persuade her mother to leave him alone.

“Your brother is well, and we have not abducted him. We have simply taken him out of the fighting, to offer him the childhood he has a right to, healthy food that can no longer be found here and that he needs in order to grow, supervision that will allow him to continue his studies properly. Even during a special operation like the one we are carrying out at the moment—don’t you think that’s reasonable?”

“A special operation?” Veronika flares up. “It’s a war you are waging against us!”

“That false term is punishable by eight years in prison! We are waging war on no one, we are liberating Ukraine!” the officer protests, outraged.

“And where you come from, is saying it’s raining when it rains punishable too? When there’s a storm, do you call it drizzle, mist?”

“When calm has returned and his safety is assured, we will send him back to you, I promise,” he says in a voice that makes it perfectly clear his patience is wearing thin.

Veronika’s aplomb has robbed him of his swagger, to the point that he feels ridiculous; his superiority is slipping through his fingers like the sand of the beaches of Odesa he used to play with when he was a boy. His own mother must be ten years older than this woman, and if she had found herself facing him in such circumstances, she would have given him a good hiding and kicked him out of the room. Not this time. This time he will not let himself be pushed around; he is going to show who he is.

“One more word and I’ll have you arrested!” he says in a grave voice. “Your daughter needs you, and if you were no longer able to look after her, I would be obliged to put her in safety as well.”

“Where is this centre?” asks Lilya.

“I don’t know yet. We have many of them; there are so many children in precarious circumstances in your country. Come back and see me in three days. I’ll know more then and I’ll tell you where your son is. I may even be able to give you news of him, provided you don’t make trouble between now and then.”

Terrified at the thought of being detained in turn and hoping the officer will keep his promise, Lilya catches hold of her mother’s arm and begs her to obey. They will get nothing more today, except to make their situation worse.
