6 NOVEMBER

Because of the rain, I missed the sign for the village. It blurred the hollows, erased the ruts; in the end I was inching forward blind before pulling onto the shoulder. All that water hammering the hood. The storm started yesterday. I haven’t passed anyone since leaving the highway. Even though the radio was advising people not to drive, I had no choice. It’s five o’clock, the sky anthracite. I couldn’t figure out how to adjust the seatback. I wait, very upright, numbed by the racket. At least the van seems solid. It looks like a road-maintenance vehicle, plasma orange. At the rental counter I insisted on something practical.

An hour passes. At last the downpour slackens. I set off again. The GPS takes me deeper into the forest. Soon neither rain nor light gets through the leafy roof. I turn on the high beams. The steering wheel is sticky. For miles I crawl along, guessing the way among tracks under the brambles, until I emerge at the foot of a steep slope. A little higher up, the gate is open. For the first time, I repeat my father’s motions. I shift into first, accelerate; the wheels spin on the rocks but hold, and I cut the engine in front of the house. The automatic light comes on. A rabbit bolts.

The house looks tired, its roof sagging onto the brick like a giant smothered by ivy. A car is parked beneath the hazel tree. Bracken is prying apart the porch steps. Through the window I make out light. I put my eye to the peephole, then step back at once. I wasn’t expecting my sister’s face—huge forehead, brows set far apart, fish eyes—my sister swollen by that lens my father claimed he had deliberately installed backwards. According to him, we had nothing to fear and nothing to hide, our riches were inside us, and the whole world ought to know that the most beautiful people lived here.

— Hi.

My voice sounds louder than I expected. Véra answers with a smile too wide for her mouth. She takes the suitcase from my hands and sets it down at the foot of the stairs in the kitchen. I find the stone floor again, the wooden furniture, the bathroom door in the fireplace’s shadow. I had never seen it like this, the hearth blocked up with books. Above the table, a birdcage has replaced the light fixture. Cheeses are piled behind the bars.

Véra points to the stairs, then points to herself at the counter: I should get settled while she finishes making dinner. I’ve known her far messier. I compliment her. She types on her phone and shows me the screen:

“It’s to welcome you properly.”

I answer a little sharply that we’re sisters and this is my home too; let’s skip that sort of politeness. She turns on the gas with a hurried gesture. I can’t help adding:

— Especially since we won’t be keeping anything.

The stairs hiss under my socks. I have to be careful not to slip. Our parents’ bedroom is ajar. I stay on the threshold, in the draft from the badly insulated French doors. Black parquet. At the center of the room, the big bed, the bare mattress, no sheets, no blanket. I still wonder how my parents could sleep without a wall behind their heads. I close the door again, vaguely relieved. I don’t know what I was dreading more: sleeping in that bed, or sharing our room with my sister now that we’re adults.

Her sweet perfume catches in my throat. She has kept our bunk beds. Tonight, the sight of them pains me. The wrought iron seems too thin to hold us. The dresser and desk are in their places, as plump as ever, painted salmon. I make sure I can get online. I’m going to spend nine days here; I need to be able to communicate with my colleagues. The signal shows only one bar. Sometimes it vanishes.

Leaning out the window, I catch sight of the van. Its orange color makes me laugh. It looks like a big bumblebee. It stands out as much as my sister and I, together again for the first time since my father died five years ago.

Véra has poured wine into the crystal glasses. Tensed by the ritual, I say I don’t drink. She raises her eyebrows, pours the wine back into the bottle; it spills, I wipe it up with my sweater then take the sweater off, I’m hot. The bamboo tableware feels foreign to me. Proudly, Véra shows me the cheese wrapper decorated with chestnuts, then points to the fireplace: it’s a smoked cheese. I push away the thought that it’s made from raw milk. She has prepared an endive salad with figs and walnuts. I ask if she’s thought about how we should proceed over the next few days. I haven’t; I’ve been very busy. She taps:

“Congratulations on your award.”

I murmur that that’s kind.

I don’t know what she knows about the films I’ve written. The latest just won an award at an Italian festival. I couldn’t go, and anyway I hadn’t invited her.

— Have you heard from Octave? I ask in a voice I want to keep neutral.

She nods, of course; she points to the figs, the walnuts, they came from him... I cut her off. As far as I’m concerned, there’s nothing I want to keep. Let her sort through things; we’ll take the rest to the dump. Her fingers tense around her phone. With her chin, she points to the wardrobe, the kitchen, the bathroom. I look up. We’re not really going to rummage through all that? Her face lights up in the glow of her screen:

“As you like.”

I soften. It’s just that I have work. I’m behind. I’m going to have to shut myself away to write. With a simplicity that disconcerts me, she shows me the screen again:

“As you like.”

Then she asks what I’m working on. I mention my latest commission, the adaptation of Georges Perec’s novel W, or The Memory of Childhood. Véra punctuates my sentences with smiles. I affect nonchalance as I mention the prestige of the production, the reputation of the actors being considered, that of my co-writers. We have to create six episodes. Shooting is planned for two years from now. Véra applauds. I temper that. Adapting it isn’t simple. And I’m only the dialogue writer. I start to explain Perec, but she nods vigorously; she knows, she’s read A Void.

— You read?

She signals that obviously she does.

— I don’t know, my father...

Silence.

— I mean Dad. He never told me.

Without losing her smile, Véra serves me the last figs. I was fifteen, Véra twelve, when I left for the United States. I was supposed to stay through high school, with a host family. Véra had stopped speaking long before. She was learning to read and write, but I hadn’t imagined she was this capable.

— And you, are you okay? I ask, realizing I haven’t asked her a single question since I arrived.

Our last exchanges were last year, when she moved to Périgueux. Until then, she had lived here with my father, and stayed on after he died. I helped from a distance. The studio was already furnished; she didn’t want to take anything from our childhood home without my approval. Now that the sale has been finalized, Véra was counting on my coming to empty it.

I read over her shoulder. She makes an irritated gesture; I need to give her time. I apologize and help myself to more salad.

After a land consolidation, our house is no longer considered a tenant farm of the neighboring château, the Pigeon Froid, which belongs to Octave’s family. Safety standards have changed. To comply, we’d have to redo the roof, the heating, the electrical system; we don’t have the money, and we’ve accepted an offer from a campsite that is going to raze the place. Octave wants to salvage our stones for the restoration of the dovecote.

Véra shows me the screen. Working in the shop bored her. She’s training in floral stabilization.

— Stabilization?

“I make flowers that don’t wilt, thanks to chemistry.”

I put on an impressed look.

— And it works?

“That depends on the flowers.”

I clarify that I mean the business. She shrugs:

“People don’t want the hassle anymore.”

We clear the table.

— It’s true flowers don’t grow in November, I end up saying flatly.

Véra goes to bed before long. I stay in the living room, steeped in night. The floor lamps cast warm half-shadows, but I don’t feel at ease. The windows have no curtains. In the black glass I see my reflection on the sofa, with the refrigerator purring and the smell of the cheese Véra has put back in the cage. I feel oppressed by the blocked-up fireplace, by the dozens of Van Gogh sunflowers thumbtacked up. My father collected theater and art-exhibition posters. He never went to see the plays or exhibitions and didn’t care about the artists’ names, but chose the most colorful images to plaster all over the house.

I feel weary at the prospect of the big clear-out. If we set fire to the books, only the stones would remain; that would be the simplest thing, since they’re all we’ve been asked to preserve.

I put off sorting through my emails until tomorrow and spend the evening drifting through my messaging apps, anxious about Irvin’s silence. It’s almost evening in New York. He has had time to write to me. I’m tempted to wait for him to make the first move, tell myself that’s childish, wish him good night, tell him I arrived safely. I hesitate. Add that I miss him.

The bathroom is true to my memory. It looks like a cave, floor and walls of raw stone. Véra has set out a towel for me, neatly folded on the sideboard. It has always disgusted me with its wormholes, even though they’re treated. She has taken off her jewelry. Ethnic-style, feathers, shells. I open a drawer. It’s full of amber: necklaces, brooches. I nudge the wet bathmat aside with my foot, step into the shower stall, and send some of Véra’s hair down the drain. I hope I don’t clog it.

I stay under the burning water for a long time. My hair still bears the mark of the elastic; it dried as my hormones ebbed. I don’t know when my period will come back. I turn toward the stone. I still have the reflex, when I’m naked, of looking for something to brace myself against, of not letting my eyes drop to my lower belly, which Irvin claims he never sees swollen. According to him, it’s in my head. He’s trying to reassure me. It’s not his fault. He didn’t see it empty itself down my legs, redden the water, then disappear into the drain. He knows nothing about my body.

The harder I try not to make a sound, the more the stairs creak. The bedroom is bathed in the greenish light of our phones charging. Only Véra’s head sticks out from the blanket, her hands domed on her chest. Her clothes are heaped on the dresser. The ladder squeaks, the sheets rustle. Rocked by the smell of laundry detergent, I fall asleep at once.

7 NOVEMBER

I sort by category. Things to destroy, things to give away. I laugh to see myself applying influencers’ advice on tidying up. The window lets in a cold light. The spiders scatter. They don’t spin webs but cling in the corners, around the oven. I clear the dead ones from the bottoms of the saucepans. I started with the kitchen, which seemed the most neutral room. Into a large trash bag I throw the expired food. Mustard, tomato paste. A jar of white sludge: duck fat. An entire shelf is devoted to cheese. The fridge shrinks back before my onslaught. The freezer compartment needs defrosting. The plastic is cracking. In the vegetable drawer, potatoes have sprouted. Véra has only come back once a month since moving out. The smell of cheese turns my stomach. My movements are quick. Moldy jam, scraps of butter, wilted herbs.

Véra is taking care of our room. It makes me uneasy to know she’s nearby. I listen for the noises she makes. A little while ago she came downstairs to make coffee; I was sniffing at some compote, she asked if I wanted some too, and not knowing whether she meant the compote or the coffee, I threw the jar into the trash the way a rabbit caught in headlights throws itself under the wheels.

Véra is making progress too. At this rate, I’m afraid we’ll have too much time on our hands together. What am I going to do with her for six more days? I try to slow down.

The kitchen clutter resembles Véra. I prefer it to the neatness of the living room when I arrived. Oil has leaked. I clean away greasy residues, sticky buildups. As I run the cloth along the back of a cupboard, I feel the panel move. I press harder. It comes loose, comes away easily. It covered a space the size of an apple crate, carved into the cool stone. It’s full of sugar packets and hard liquor. I call Véra. She didn’t know this recess existed either. Does she think our father... She shakes her head, probably not. It’s true I never saw him drink.

— And Mom?

Véra looks thoughtful. I pick up a bottle.

Quince liqueur. I want to throw it away, but she stops me, mimes the two of us drinking straight from the bottle. I shake my head no, but put it back and close the hiding place. At the bottom of the sink, bits of jam mingle with the grayish water. I have to plunge my whole arm in to scoop them up.

Véra seems impressed by my sorting. She looks at me, tugs at her sweater and points to the stairs: I should take a look at my clothes.

— I’m giving them away.

She insists. I can feel her watching me as I go upstairs. That gaze weighs on me; it lends weight to a gesture that matters so little to me.

My side of the dresser is just as it was before I left for the United States, a few sweaters fewer. I try on a pair of jeans anyway. Just for fun. My father had patched a hole in them with a flower-patterned patch, using a saucepan of boiling water. I’m surprised to find I’ve slimmed down. Even though I’ve put on weight these past few months, Irvin likes it, which reassures me and makes me anxious at the same time. I put the jeans in a plastic bag, do the same with the flannel pants, a fabric I never wore again, cotton pajamas, T-shirts and wool sweaters. I start rummaging with the growing feeling that I’m looking for something.
