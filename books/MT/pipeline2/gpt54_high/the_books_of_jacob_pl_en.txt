PROLOGUE

The swallowed scrap of paper lodges in the esophagus somewhere near the heart. It grows wet with saliva. The specially prepared black ink slowly dissolves, and the letters lose their form. Inside the human body, the word splits in two, into substance and essence. When the first disappears, the second, left without shape, allows itself to be absorbed by the tissues of the body, for essence is forever seeking a material vessel—even if that is to become the cause of many misfortunes.

Yente wakes, though she was already almost dead. She feels it clearly now; it is like pain, like the current of a river, a trembling, a pressure, movement.

A delicate vibration returns near her heart; her heart beats weakly, but steadily, sure of itself. Warmth flows once more into Yente’s dried-out, bony chest. Yente blinks and, with difficulty, lifts her lids. She sees Elisha Shor’s worried face bent over her. She tries to smile at him, but her face is not so much her own as that. Elisha Shor’s brows are drawn together; he looks at her with reproach. His lips are moving, but no sound reaches Yente’s ears. Hands appear from somewhere—old Shor’s large hands, reaching for her neck and then sliding under the blanket. Shor awkwardly tries to turn her limp body onto its side and look beneath it, at the sheet. No, Yente does not feel his exertions; she feels only warmth, and the presence of the bearded, sweating man.

Then suddenly, as though by some blow, Yente sees everything from above—herself, and the balding crown of Shor’s head, for in his struggle with her body his cap has fallen off.

And from then on, that is how it is—Yente sees everything.

I. THE BOOK OF FOG

1

1752, Rohatyn

It is the end of October, early morning. The dean stands on the rectory porch waiting for the carriage. He is used to getting up at dawn, but this time he feels half-conscious and hardly knows how he came to be here—alone before a sea of fog. He remembers neither getting up nor dressing, nor whether he has eaten already. He looks in surprise at his sturdy boots sticking out from under his cassock, at the slightly frayed skirts of his faded wool coat, and at the gloves he is holding in his hand. He slips on the left one; inside it seems warm and fits his hand perfectly, as though hand and glove had known each other for years. He breathes more easily. He touches the bag slung over his shoulder, mechanically feeling the rectangular edges, hard, thickened, like scars under the skin, and slowly remembers what is inside—a heavy, familiar, pleasing shape. Something good, something that has brought him here, some words, some signs—all of it deeply bound up with his life. Ah yes, now he knows what is in there, and that awareness slowly begins to warm his body, and because of it the fog seems somehow more transparent. Behind him gapes the dark opening of the door, one leaf shut; the cold must have come already, perhaps a touch of frost has even nipped the plums in the orchard. Above the door a blurred inscription—he sees it without looking, because he knows what is written there, he commissioned it himself; two craftsmen from Podhajce spent a whole week carving the letters in the wood, for he had ordered them to be ornamental:

HERE TODAY, TOMORROW GONE.
WHAT HAS FLED, WE SHAN’T RUИ DOWN.

In the last word, which irritates him greatly, the letter N is carved backward, like its mirror image.

Irritated by it for the Lord knows what time, the priest jerks his head sharply—and that wakes him fully at last. That reversed letter is an И... What slovenliness! You have to watch their hands all the time, keep an eye on them at every step. And since those craftsmen were Jews, they have made the inscription somehow Jewish too; the letters are too curly, too wavering. One of them even argued that such an N could perfectly well be used, was even prettier, because the diagonal stroke ran from bottom to top and left to right, in the Christian way, whereas the other way round would be Jewish. This little irritation restores his senses, and now Father Benedykt Chmielowski, dean of Rohatyn, sees why he had the feeling he was still asleep—he is standing in fog the color of his bed linen, grayish; a broken white already overtaken by dirt, the vast stores of gray that line the world. The fog is motionless, tightly filling the whole yard; behind it loom the familiar shapes of the great pear tree, the low wall, and farther on the wicker-sided cart. It is just an ordinary cloud from the sky, fallen to earth and pressed belly-down against it. He read about that yesterday in Comenius.

Now he hears the familiar creak and rumble that, on every journey, unfailingly puts him into a state of creative meditation. Only after the sound do Roszko, leading the horse by the bridle, and the dean’s little carriage emerge from the fog. At the sight of it the priest feels a surge of energy, slaps the glove against his palm, and clambers up onto the seat. Roszko, taciturn as ever, adjusts the harness and throws the priest a long look. The fog has grayed his face and makes him seem older to the priest than ever before, as if he had aged overnight, though he is only a young fellow.

At last they set off, though it is as if they are standing still; only the swaying of the vehicle and the soothing creaking attest to movement. They have gone this way so many times, for so many years, that there is no longer any need to look at the scenery, and no landmarks will be necessary. The priest knows that they have now come onto the road that runs along the edge of the forest, and so they will go on until the crossroads, where a roadside shrine rises, one the priest himself had erected years ago, when he took over the parish in Firlejów. For a long time he had wondered whom to place in that shrine, and Saint Benedict, his patron, came to mind, or Onuphrius, the hermit whom a palm tree miraculously fed with dates in the desert, while every eighth day angels brought him the Body of Christ from heaven. For Firlejów too was to be such a desert for the priest when he arrived here after years spent educating Dymitr, the son of His Lordship Jabłonowski. But after thinking it over, the priest decided that the shrine was not to be built for him and the satisfaction of his own vanity, but for simple folk, so that at the crossing of roads they might have somewhere to rest and from which to lift their thoughts to heaven. And so there stands, on a whitewashed masonry pedestal, the Mother of God, Queen of the World, with a crown on her head. Beneath her little pointed slipper a snake was writhing.

But today she too vanishes in the fog, the shrine and the crossroads with her. Only the tops of the trees are visible, a sign that the fog is beginning to sink.

—Look, sir, Kaśka won’t go on, —Roszko says gloomily when the carriage comes to a halt. Roszko climbs down from the box and crosses himself several times with sweeping gestures.

Then he bends down and peers into the fog as if looking into water. His shirt shows beneath his Sunday red jacket, somewhat faded now.

—I don’t know where to drive, —he says.

—What do you mean, you don’t know? We’re already on the Rohatyn road, —the priest says in surprise.

And yet it’s true. He gets down from the carriage and follows his servant; helplessly they walk around the vehicle, straining their eyes into the whiteness. They think they see something, but eyes that cannot fasten on anything begin to play tricks. Fancy such a thing happening to them! Why, it is as though one were to get lost in one’s own pocket.

—Quiet! —says the priest suddenly, raising a finger and listening. And indeed, from somewhere to the left, out of the banks of fog, comes the faint murmur of water.

—Let’s go toward that sound. Water is flowing there, —the priest decides.

Now they will drag along very slowly beside the river called the Gniła Lipa. The water will lead them.

Soon the priest relaxes in the carriage, stretches his legs out before him, and lets his eyes wander over the sea of fog. At once he sinks into a traveler’s reverie, for a man thinks best while in motion. Slowly, reluctantly, the mechanism of his mind comes to life; gears and cogs catch and set the driving wheels in motion, just as in the clock standing in the hall of his rectory, bought in Lwów; he paid dearly for it. In a moment it will sound: bim-bom. Might the world not have arisen out of just such a fog? he begins to wonder. After all, the Jewish historian Josephus maintains that the world was created in autumn, during the autumnal aequinoctium. One may suppose so, for there were fruits in Paradise; if an apple was hanging on a tree, it must have been autumn... There is a certain logic in that. But at once another thought occurs to him: what kind of argument is that? Could Almighty God not have created such pitiful fruits separately, at any season of the year?

When they reach the main road leading to Rohatyn, they merge into a stream of walkers, riders, and carts of every kind, all emerging from the fog like little bread figurines molded for Christmas. It is Wednesday, market day in Rohatyn; peasant wagons are rolling in, laden with sacks of grain, cages of poultry, all manner of produce. Among them, stepping briskly, march traders with every possible kind of merchandise—their stalls, now cleverly folded up, can be carried on their backs like yokes, and in a moment will become tables full of bright cloth, wooden toys, eggs bought up in the villages for a quarter of their worth... The peasants are also leading goats and cows for sale—the animals, frightened by the uproar, plant their feet stubbornly in the puddles. A speeding cart covered with a torn tarpaulin races past them, full of noisy Jews making for the fair in Rohatyn from the whole area, and behind them forces its way a grand coach, which in the fog and the crush on the road finds it hard to preserve any dignity—the light lacquered doors are black with mud, and the coachman in a blue cape looks thoroughly dismayed; plainly he had not expected such confusion and is now desperately seeking a chance to get off this devil’s road.

Roszko is stubborn and will not be shoved off into the field; he keeps to the right and, with one wheel on the grass and the other on the road, moves smartly forward. Color rises in his long, mournful face, and some damned grimace spreads over it. The priest casts a glance at him and is reminded of an engraving he looked at only yesterday—there were fiends in it, and they wore just such grimaces as Roszko now does.

—Make way for the reverend gentleman! Come on now! Out of the way, people! —Roszko shouts.

The first buildings rise before them suddenly, without warning. Evidently the fog alters one’s sense of distance, for Kaśka herself seems startled by it. All at once she jumps, jerking at the shaft, and had it not been for Roszko’s firm reaction and his whip, she would have overturned the carriage. Perhaps Kaśka was frightened by the sparks showering from the forge, or by the restlessness of the horses waiting their turn to be shod...

Farther on stands an inn, poor and wretched, like a village hut. Above it the well sweep juts up like a gallows, piercing the fog and disappearing somewhere high overhead. The priest sees that a dusty coach has stopped there; the tired coachman has lowered his head almost to his knees and does not jump down from the box, nor does anyone come out from inside. But already a tall, thin Jew is standing before it, and beside him little girls with tangled hair. That is all the dean sees, for the fog swallows every passing sight; it vanishes somewhere, soaks away like a melting snowflake.

Here is Rohatyn.

It begins with mud huts, little clay houses roofed with straw thatch, which seems to press the buildings to the earth; but the nearer one gets to the market square, the more slender the houses become, the finer the thatch, until at last it gives way to the wooden shingles of little town houses made of unbaked clay brick. There are also the parish church, the Dominican monastery, Saint Barbara’s church on the square, and farther off two synagogues and five Ruthenian churches. Around the square little houses sit like mushrooms, each with some business in it. A tailor, a rope-maker, a furrier, all Jews, and next to them a baker by the name of Bochenek, which always pleases the dean, because it reveals some hidden order that might be made more visible and more consistent, and then people would live more virtuously. Next door the workshop of the swordsmith called Luba; the facade bespeaks prosperity, the walls are freshly painted blue, and above the entrance hangs a great rusted sword—plainly this Luba is a good craftsman, and his customers’ pockets are full. Then a saddler, who has set a wooden trestle outside his door, and on it a beautiful saddle, the stirrups perhaps silvered, they gleam so brightly.

Everywhere one smells the faintly sickly odor of malt, penetrating every article set out for sale. One could fill oneself with it as with bread. On the outskirts of Rohatyn, in Babińce, there are several small breweries; it is from there that this nourishing smell spreads over the whole area. Many little stalls here sell beer, and the better shops stock vodka as well as mead, mostly trójniak. The establishment of the Jewish merchant Wakszul offers wine, genuine Hungarian and genuine Rhenish, and a somewhat tart kind brought all the way from Wallachia.

The priest moves along the stalls put together from every material imaginable—boards, pieces of coarsely woven cloth, wicker baskets, even leaves. Some good woman in a white kerchief is selling pumpkins from a cart, and their vivid orange color draws the children. Next to her another praises up little rounds of cheese arranged on horseradish leaves. Farther on stand many other market women who have taken to trade because they are widowed or have drunkard husbands: sellers of oil, of salt, of linen. The priest usually buys from this maker of pasties, and now he sends the trader a friendly smile. Behind her are two stalls decorated with a green branch, which means fresh-brewed beer is sold there. And here is the rich stall of Armenian merchants—beautiful light fabrics, knives in decorated sheaths, and then vizina, dried fish, whose clammy smell seeps into the woolen Turkish hangings. Farther on, from a box hanging from his thin shoulders, a man in a dusty caftan sells eggs packed by the dozen into baskets woven of grass. Another offers eggs by the kopa, in great baskets, at a competitive, almost wholesale price. Bagels hang all over the baker’s stall—one has fallen into the mud and now a little dog is eating it with relish.

They trade in anything they can. Flowered fabrics as well, kerchiefs and shawls straight from the bazaar in Istanbul, shoes for children, fruit, nuts. A man under the fence has a plow and nails of every size, from ones as small as pins to huge spikes for building a house. Beside him a handsome woman in a starched cap has laid out clappers for the night watchmen: small ones, whose sound is more like crickets chirring in the night than a call to sleep, and large ones that, on the contrary, could wake the dead.

How many times Jews have been forbidden to trade in things connected with the Church. Priests and rabbis alike have thundered on the matter, but to no avail. So there are beautiful prayer books here, with ribbons between the pages, with wonderfully silvered letters embossed on the cover, letters that, when the pad of a finger is run across them, seem warm and alive. A clean, almost elegant man in a fur cap holds them like relics—wrapped in thin paper, in cream tissue, so that this dirty, foggy day will not stain their innocent, Christian pages, fragrant with printer’s ink. He also has wax candles, even holy pictures of saints with halos.

The priest goes up to one of the itinerant booksellers, hoping to find something in Latin. But they are all Jewish, for beside them lie objects whose purpose the priest does not know.

The farther the eye ventures into the side streets, the more poverty comes to the surface, like a dirty toe out of a torn shoe; coarse, quiet poverty, bent to the ground. No longer shops, no longer stalls, but kennels for dogs, knocked together from thin planks scavenged from rubbish heaps. In one of them a cobbler repairs shoes already sewn and resoled and patched many times over. In another, hung all about with iron pots, sits a tinker. His face is thin and hollow, his cap covers a brow mottled with some kind of brown lesions. The dean would be afraid to have his pots mended by him, lest some terrible disease pass to others through the touch of the poor wretch’s fingers. Beside him an old man sharpens knives and every kind of sickle and scythe. His workshop consists of a stone wheel hung around his neck. When he gets something to sharpen, he sets a primitive wooden frame on the ground—a few leather straps turn it into a simple machine whose wheel, driven by the hand, licks the metal blades. At times a few true sparks leap from this contraption and fall into the mud, much to the delight of dirty, mange-ridden children. He earns pennies at his trade. He might use that wheel to drown himself in the river too; such is another advantage of the profession.

Women in rags gather shavings and dung in the street for fuel. Under those rags it is hard to tell whether the poverty is Jewish, Ruthenian, or Catholic. Yes, poverty has neither faith nor nationality.

Si est, ubi est? the priest asks himself, thinking of Paradise. Certainly not here in Rohatyn, nor, it seems to him, anywhere on Podolian soil. And should anyone imagine things are better in the big cities, he would be gravely mistaken. True, the priest has never in his life made it as far as Warsaw or Kraków, but he knows a thing or two from the stories of the Bernardine Pikulski, more traveled than he, or from what he overhears at the manor houses.

Paradise, the garden of delights, has been moved by God to a beautiful and unknown place. And as it is written in Arca Noe, Paradise lies somewhere in the land of the Armenians, high in the mountains, while Brunus in turn claims it is sub polo antarctico, beneath the southern pole. A sign of Paradise’s nearness is the presence of four rivers: Gebon, Philon, Euphrates, and Tygris. And there are authors who, unable to find a place on earth for Paradise, locate it in the air, some fifteen cubits above the mountains. But that strikes the priest as rather foolish. How so? Would people living on earth beneath Paradise see it from underneath? Would they behold the saints’ heels?

On the other hand, one cannot agree with those who try to spread the false opinion that the sacred text on Paradise has only a mystical meaning, that is, ought to be taken in a spiritual or allegorical sense. The priest—not only because he is a priest, but out of his own deep conviction—believes that Holy Scripture must be taken literally.

He knows almost everything there is to know about Paradise, for only last week he finished a chapter of his highly ambitious book, a chapter compiled from all the books he has in Firlejów, and he has one hundred and thirty. For some of them he traveled to Lwów, even to Lublin.

There is the corner house, modest enough—that is where he is going. Father Pikulski advised him to. The low double doors stand wide open; a spicy smell beats out from inside, unheard of amid the stink of horse dung and autumn damp, and along with it another, pungent odor, already familiar to the dean—coffee. The priest does not drink coffee, but in the end he must make its closer acquaintance.

The priest glances back, looking for Roszko; he sees him turning over sheepskin coats with somber attention, and beyond him the whole market busy with itself. Nobody is looking at the priest; everyone is absorbed in haggling. Noise and clamor.

Above the entrance can be seen a rather clumsily made sign:

SHOR
DEALER IN GOODS

Then come Hebrew letters. By the door hangs a metal plaque, and beside it some signs, and the priest remembers that Athanasius Kircher says in his book that Jews, when a wife has been delivered and they fear a witch, write on the walls the words: “Adam, Hawa. Huc — Lilit,” which means: “Adam and Eve, come here, and you, Lilith—that is, witch—begone.” This must be that. Presumably a child has recently been born here too.

The priest steps over the high threshold and is at once immersed in the warm spicy smell. It takes a moment for his eyes to adjust to the dark, because light enters only through a small window, which is moreover blocked by flowerpots.

Behind the counter stands an adolescent boy with the first dusting of a mustache on him, full lips that first tremble slightly at the sight of the priest and then try to shape themselves into some word. He is utterly taken aback.

—What’s your name, boy? —the priest asks boldly, wanting to show how confident he feels in this dark, low-ceilinged shop and to encourage the youth to conversation, but the other does not reply. —Quod tibi nomen est? —he repeats more formally, but the Latin that is meant to serve communication suddenly sounds too solemn, as if the priest had come here to perform an exorcism, like Christ in the Gospel of Saint Luke, who addresses the possessed with that very question. But the boy only opens his eyes wider and says, “bh, bh,” then suddenly darts behind the shelves, brushing against a braid of garlic hanging from a nail.

The priest has behaved foolishly; he should not have expected Latin to be spoken here. He looks down critically—under his coat protrude the black cloth buttons of his cassock. That must be what frightened the boy, he thinks—the cassock. He smiles to himself and remembers the biblical Jeremiah, who also all but lost his head and stammered: Aaa, Domine Deus ecce nescio loqui!—Lord God, behold, I cannot speak.

From then on the priest calls the boy Jeremiah in his thoughts. He does not know what to do now that the boy has vanished so suddenly. So he looks around the shop, fastening the buttons of his coat. It was Father Pikulski who talked him into coming here; he listened, but now it no longer seems to him such a good idea.

No one comes in from outside, for which the priest silently thanks God. It would not be an ordinary sight—a Catholic priest, the dean of Rohatyn, in a Jew’s shop, waiting to be served like some burgher’s wife. Father Pikulski had advised him to go to Rabbi Dubs in Lwów; he himself visited him and had learned much from him. And the priest did go there, but old Dubs seemed already to have had enough of Catholic priests pestering him about books. He was unpleasantly surprised by the request, and what interested Father Benedykt most he either did not have or pretended not to have. He made a polite face and shook his head, clicking his tongue. And when the priest asked who might help him, Dubs waved his hands and, turning his head back as though someone were standing behind him, made it understood that he did not know, and if he did know, he would not say. Later Father Pikulski explained to the dean that it had to do with Jewish heresies, and though they themselves boast that among them there are no heresies, it seems for this one they make some sort of exception and sincerely hate it, without any beating about the bush.

At last Pikulski advised him to go to Shor. A large house with a shop on the square. Yet as he said it he cast the priest a peculiar sidelong look, ironic—or perhaps the priest only imagined it. Perhaps these Jewish books should have been obtained through Pikulski? Though the dean is not much taken with him. He would not have to be standing here now, ashamed and sweating. But there is a good deal of contrariness in the priest, so he went on his own. And there was something else as well, not quite reasonable—a little play on words had crept into the whole matter; who would believe such things have any influence on the world. The priest had been working diligently on a certain passage in Kircher, where the great bull Szorobor was mentioned. And perhaps the likeness of the words drew him here—Shor and Szorobor. Strange are the Lord’s decrees.

But where are those famous books, where that figure who inspires fearful respect? The shop looks like an ordinary stall, though its owner is said to be descended from the famous rabbi, the sage greatly respected by all, Zalman Naftali Shor. And here there is garlic, herbs, pots of spices, jars and little jars, and in them every kind of spice—crushed, ground, or still in its natural form, like those vanilla pods, clove buds, round nutmegs. On the shelves, laid out on hay, there are also bolts of cloth—silk and satin perhaps, very bright, they catch the eye. The priest wonders whether he might not need something of the sort, but already his attention is caught by the clumsy inscription on a sizeable dark-green jar: “Herba the.” He knows what he will ask for when at last someone comes out to him—for a little of that herb that puts him in a better mood, which in the case of the dean means that he can work without tiring. And it improves the digestion as well. And he would buy some cloves, to flavor his evening mulled wine. The last nights have been so cold that his frozen feet would not let him concentrate on writing. He looks around for some kind of bench, and then everything happens at once: from behind the shelves emerges a solidly built, bearded man in a long woolen robe, from beneath which protrude Turkish shoes with pointed toes. Over his shoulders is thrown a thin dark-blue cloak. He narrows his eyes, as though he had come out of a well. Peering curiously from behind his back is that Jeremiah who was frightened before, and two more faces, much like Jeremiah’s, rosy and inquisitive. And from the other side, in the doorway from the market square, there has just stopped, breathless, a small boy, or perhaps a young man, for a fair little goatee has sprung up thickly on him. He leans against the frame and pants; plainly he ran here as fast as he could. He drills the dean with shameless eyes and smiles roguishly, showing healthy, widely spaced teeth. The priest cannot be sure it is not a mocking smile. He prefers the dignified figure in the cloak, and it is to him that he addresses himself with extraordinary politeness:

—Pray forgive this intrusion...

The other looks at him tensely, but after a moment his expression slowly changes. Something like a smile appears on his face. The dean suddenly understands that the man does not understand him, and so he begins again, now in Latin, joyfully certain that he has found his own kind.

The Jew slowly shifts his gaze to the boy in the doorway, the breathless one, and the boy boldly comes inside, tugging down his jacket of dark cloth.

—I’ll interpret, —he announces in an unexpectedly low voice with a soft Ruthenian sing-song, and, pointing a finger at the dean, says with feeling that this is a real priest, a truly genuine one.

It had not occurred to the priest that an interpreter would be needed; somehow he had not thought of that. He is embarrassed and does not know how to get out of it, because the whole matter, meant to be delicate, is suddenly becoming public, and in a moment it will draw in the whole fair. Most of all he would like to get back out into the cool fog smelling of horse dung. He begins to feel hemmed in in this low room, in air thick with the scent of spice, and what is more someone from the street is already peering in curiously to see what is going on.

—I should like a word with the esteemed Elisha Shor, if he permits, —he says. —In private.

The others are surprised. They exchange a few words among themselves; Jeremiah disappears and only after a longer moment of unbearable silence returns. Plainly the priest has been granted permission, and now they lead him behind the shelves. There are whispers, the light patter of children’s feet, muffled giggles—as if beyond the thin walls there were crowds of other people now peering through the cracks in the wooden partitions at the dean of Rohatyn wandering through the recesses of a Jewish house. And it turns out as well that the little shop on the market square is merely the bridgehead of a much larger structure, a kind of beehive: rooms, passageways, little staircases. The whole house is larger, built around an inner courtyard, which the priest glimpses only from the corner of his eye through a small window in the room where they pause for a moment.

—I’m Hryćko, —says the boy with the little beard as they walk. The priest realizes that if he wanted to withdraw now, he would not even know how to get out of this beehive house. The thought makes him sweat, and then one of the doors opens with a creak and there stands in it a lean middle-aged man with a fair, smooth, impenetrable face, a gray beard, a robe down past his knees, wool socks on his feet, and black slippers.

—That is Rabbi Elisha Shor himself, —Hryćko whispers with feeling.

The room is small, low, and very modestly furnished. In the middle stands a broad table; on it lies an open book, and beside it in several stacks others—the priest’s gaze runs greedily over their spines, trying to read the titles. The priest knows little about Jews in general, and these Rohatyn Jews only by sight.

It suddenly seems pleasing to the priest that they are both small men. In the presence of tall people he always feels somehow disconcerted. They stand facing one another, and for a moment the priest has the impression that the other is satisfied by that resemblance as well. The Jew sits down softly, smiles, and gestures toward a bench for the priest.

—With your leave, and in these extraordinary circumstances, I come to you, sir, entirely incognito, having heard much of your great wisdom and erudition...

Hryćko stops in the middle of the sentence and asks the priest:

—In-co-gni-to?

—Indeed, which is to say that I supplicate for discretion.

—And what’s that? Sup-pli-cate? Dis-cre-tion?

The priest falls silent, unpleasantly surprised. What an interpreter he has been saddled with; he can see the fellow does not understand him. So how are they supposed to speak to each other? In Chinese? He will try to speak simply:

—I ask that this be kept secret, for I do not conceal that I am the dean of Rohatyn, a Catholic priest. But first and foremost an author. —He emphasizes the word “author” by raising a finger. —And today I should prefer to speak here not as a clergyman, but as an author precisely, one who is diligently at work on a certain opusculum...

—O-pus-cu-lum? —Hryćko’s doubtful voice catches him up.

—...a small work.

—Ah. Forgive me, Father, I’m not learned in Polish, only in the ordinary kind of language people speak. I know only what I’ve overheard around horses.

—From horses? —the priest says in boundless surprise, angry at the poor interpreter.

—Well, I work with horses. Trade.

Hryćko speaks with the help of his hands. The man watches him with dark, impenetrable eyes, and it occurs to the priest that perhaps he is dealing with a blind man.

—Having by now read several hundred authors from cover to cover, —the priest continues, —borrowing here, buying there, I feel that many books have escaped me, and I have absolutely no way of gaining access to them.

Now he stops and waits for the other to speak, but Shor only nods with an ingratiating smile from which nothing follows.

—And since I had heard that your good self possesses here a quite admirable library, not wishing for anything in the world to incommode... —he corrects himself at once, reluctantly—...to trouble or burden you, I summoned my courage, against custom, but for the benefit of others, to come here and...

He falls silent, for the door flies open and, without any sort of announcement, a woman enters the low little room. In her wake, whispering, faces half visible in the darkness peer inside. A small child whimpers for a moment and then suddenly goes quiet, as though everything were meant to focus on the woman: bareheaded, her head surrounded by abundant curls, she walks boldly forward, her gaze fixed somewhere in front of her, not looking at the men at all; on a tray she carries a jug and dried fruit. She is wearing a wide flowered dress and over it an embroidered apron. Her pointed shoes click. She is slight, but shapely, and her figure draws the eye. Behind her patters a little girl carrying two glasses. She stares at the priest with such terror that she walks straight into the woman ahead of her and falls. The glasses roll across the floor; luckily they are of thick glass. The woman pays no attention to the child; instead she throws the priest a single glance, quick and brazen. Dark, somber eyes flash, large and somehow bottomless, and in an instant a blush covers her alarmingly white skin. The dean, who has no dealings with young women, feels taken aback by this sudden intrusion; he swallows. The woman sets the jug and plate down noisily on the table, together with the glasses picked up from the floor, and, again staring straight ahead, goes out. The door slams. Hryćko, the interpreter, looks flustered too. Elisha Shor, meanwhile, jumps up, lifts the child, settles her on his lap, but the little girl wriggles free and vanishes after her mother.

The priest would wager his head that the whole entrance of the woman and child had served only one purpose: so they could have a look at him. Imagine that. A priest in a Jewish house! Exotic as a salamander. So what of it? Does not a Jewish doctor treat me? And is it not a Jew as well who compounds my medicines? The matter of books is, in a way, a matter of hygiene too.

—Books, —says the priest, pointing with a finger to the spines of the folios and Elzevirs lying on the table. On each of them two signs have been painted in golden ink, which the priest takes for the owner’s initials; he can recognize Hebrew letters.

He reaches for his passport on this journey to the people of Israel and carefully lays before Shor the book he has brought. He smiles triumphantly, for it is Athanasius Kircher’s Turris Babel, a work great both in content and in size, and the priest has risked much hauling it here. What if it had fallen into that stinking Rohatyn mud? What if some cutthroat had snatched it from him in the market? Without it the dean would not be who he is, but would rather become some narrow parish priest, some Jesuit tutor at a nobleman’s court, some vain Church functionary bedecked with rings and hostile to the world.

He pushes the book closer to Shor, as if presenting him with his wife. Now he taps gently on the wooden covers.

—I have more. But Kircher is the best. —He opens it at random, and they look at a drawing of the Earth, represented as a globe, with the long, slender cone of the Tower of Babel upon it.

—Kircher proves that the Tower of Babel described in the Bible could not have been as tall as it is depicted. A tower reaching all the way to the sphere of the Moon would disturb the whole order of the cosmos. Its base, resting on the earthly globe, would have to be enormous. It would block out the sun, which would have catastrophic consequences for all creation. Men would have had to use up all the wood and clay on earth...

The priest feels as though he were uttering heresy, and he does not really know why he is saying this to the silent Jew. He wants the man to take him for a friend, not an enemy. But will that be possible? Might it be possible to understand one another, though they do not know one another’s languages or customs, or one another themselves, or one another’s things and objects, or smiles, or the gestures of hands making signs, nothing at all; so perhaps it can be done through books? Is that not in fact the only possible way? If people read the same books, they would live in the same world, whereas now they live in different ones, like those Chinese about whom Kircher wrote. And there are those as well, whole multitudes, who do not read at all; their minds lie asleep, their thoughts simple, animal, like those peasants with empty eyes. If he, the priest, were king, he would decree that one day of corvée be set aside for reading, he would drive the whole peasant estate to books, and the Commonwealth would at once look different. Perhaps it is even a matter of the alphabet—that there is not one only, but many, and each constructs thought differently. Alphabets are like bricks—from some, fired and smooth, cathedrals arise; from others, clayey and rough, ordinary houses. And though the Latin alphabet is certainly the most perfect, it seems Shor knows no Latin. So he points with his finger to one engraving, then another, and another, and sees the man bend over them with growing interest, until at last he produces from somewhere spectacles neatly mounted in wire—Father Chmielowski would like such a pair himself; he must ask where they are to be ordered. The interpreter too turns out to be interested, and so the three of them bend over the engraving.

The priest glances at them, pleased to have caught them both on the hook, and sees gold and chestnut hairs in the Jew’s dark beard.

—We might exchange books, —he proposes.

He says that in his library at Firlejów he has two more works by the great Kircher, Arca Noe and Mundus subterraneus, locked away, too valuable to be reached for every day. He also knows there are other titles, though he knows them only from references here and there. And he has gathered many more thinkers of the world gone by, including, he adds to flatter him, the Jewish historiographer Josephus.

They pour him compote from the jug and slide toward him a little plate of dried figs and dates. The priest puts them into his mouth reverently; he has not eaten them in a long time—the unearthly sweetness improves his mood at once. He understands that he ought by now to lay out his business, the time has come, so he swallows the sweets and gets to the point; before he is even finished, however, he already knows that he has spoken too soon and will achieve little here.

Perhaps he can tell by the sudden change in Hryćko’s behavior. He would wager his head, too, that the boy adds something of his own to what he translates. He just does not know whether these are warnings of some sort, or on the contrary something meant to support the priest. Elisha Shor draws back slightly in his chair, leans his head back, and half-closes his eyes, as if consulting his own inner darkness.

This goes on until, against his will, the priest exchanges a complicit glance with the young interpreter.

—Rabbi is listening to the voices of the elders, —the interpreter says in a whisper, and the priest nods as though in understanding, though he understands nothing. Perhaps this Jew really does have some magical dealings with various little devils; there are enough of them among Jews, all those lamias and liliths. This hesitation in Shor and his closed eyes convince the priest that it would have been better if he had never come here at all. The situation is delicate and unusual. God grant he does not expose himself to disgrace.

Shor rises and turns to the wall, bows his head, and stands like that for a moment. The priest begins to grow impatient—is this a sign that he should leave? Hryćko too has half-closed his eyes, and his long youthful lashes now cast shadows on cheeks covered with soft down. Have they fallen asleep? The priest clears his throat softly; their silence has stripped him of what little self-assurance remained. He already regrets coming.

Suddenly Shor, as if nothing at all were amiss, goes to the cabinets and opens one of them. With solemn care he draws out a thick folio, marked with the same symbols as all the other books, and lays it on the table before the priest. He opens the book from the back and turns a page. The priest sees a beautifully executed title page...

—Sefer ha-Zohar, —he says devoutly, and returns the book to the cabinet.

—Who would read that to you, Father... —Hryćko says consolingly.

The priest leaves on Shor’s table two volumes of his New Athens, as an encouragement toward some future exchange. He taps them with his forefinger, then points to himself—straight at the middle of his chest: I wrote this. They ought to read it, if they knew the language. They would learn a great deal about the world. He waits for Shor’s reaction, but the man only raises his brows slightly.

Father Chmielowski and Hryćko go out together into the cold, unpleasant air. Hryćko goes on chattering, while the priest studies him carefully—his young face with the beginnings of future beard growth, the long curled lashes that give him a somewhat childish look, and finally his peasant clothes.

—Are you a Jew?

—Eh, no... —Hryćko replies, shrugging. —I’m from here, from Rohatyn, from that house there. Orthodox, more or less.

—Then how do you know their speech?

Hryćko moves closer and walks almost shoulder to shoulder with the priest, plainly encouraged to such familiarity. He says that his father and mother died of the plague in 1746. They had business dealings with the Shors; his father was a craftsman, he tanned hides, and when he died, Shor stepped in on behalf of Hryćko, his grandmother, and his younger brother, Ołeś, paid off his father’s debts, and took the three neighbors under his protection. And so they have gone on living side by side; now he has more to do with Jews than with his own people, and somehow he does not even know when he came to understand their language, and he speaks it as if it were his own, fluently, which often proves useful in all sorts of matters and transactions, because Jews, especially the older ones, are reluctant to use Polish and Ruthenian. Jews are not as people say they are, and the Shors least of all. There are many of them, and theirs is a warm, hospitable house; there will always be something to eat there and a glass of vodka when it is cold. Now Hryćko is learning his father’s trade, so as to take over the tanning after him; there will always be a need for that.

—And have you no Christian family?

—Oh, I do, but they’re far away and don’t much care what becomes of us. Oh, and this is my brother, Ołeś. —A boy of perhaps eight runs up to them, covered in freckles. —Please don’t trouble yourself needlessly about us, reverend sir, —Hryćko says cheerfully. —God made man with his eyes in the front of his head, not the back, which means a man should concern himself with what is to come, not with what has been.

The priest does indeed take this for proof of God’s thoughtfulness, though he cannot recall where in Scripture it was said.

—Learn their language from them, and you’ll translate those books.

—Not at all, sir, books don’t draw me. Reading bores me. I’d rather go into trade, I like that. Horses best of all. Or, like the Shors, vodka and beer.

—Oh, you’ve gone to the bad with them already... —says the priest.

—Why, are those goods worse than any others? People need a drink. Life is hard.
