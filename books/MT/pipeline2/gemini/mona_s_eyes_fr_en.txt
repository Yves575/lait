Prologue

Seeing Nothing Anymore

Everything went dark. It was like a mourning garment. And then, here and there, flickers of light, like the spots the sun produces when your eyes stare at it in vain behind tightly shut eyelids, just as you clench a fist to withstand pain or emotion.

Of course, she hadn’t described it that way at all. In the mouth of an anxious, innocent ten-year-old child, distress is voiced bluntly, without flourish or lyricism.

“Mom, everything’s black!”

Mona had cried out these words in a strangled voice. A cry? Yes, but not only that. Despite herself, she had slipped in a note of shame that her mother, whenever she detected it, took very seriously. For if there was one thing Mona never faked, it was shame. The moment it nestled into a word, a posture, an intonation, the die was cast: an unpleasant truth had sunk in.

“Mom, everything’s black!”

Mona was blind.

The effect seemed devoid of cause. Nothing out of the ordinary had happened; she was diligently working on her math, a pen in her right hand, a notebook wedged under her left palm, at the corner of the table where her mother was studding a fatty roast with garlic. Mona was delicately removing a pendant from her neck; it was bothering her because it kept swinging over her worksheet, and she had picked up the bad habit of hunching over to write. She felt a heavy shadow fall across her eyes, as if they were being punished for being so blue, so large, so pure. The shadow did not come from the outside, as it commonly does when night falls or when theater lights dim; the shadow seized her vision from her own body, from within. An opaque blanket had crept inside her, severing her from the polygons drawn in her schoolbook, from the brown wooden table, from the roast resting further away, from her mother in her white apron, from the tiled kitchen, from her father sitting in the next room, from the apartment in Montreuil, from the gray autumn sky hanging over the streets, from the entire world. By some dark spell, the child plunged into darkness.

Frantic, Mona’s mother phoned the family doctor. She confusedly described her daughter’s clouded pupils and clarified, at the doctor's prompting, that she did not seem to be suffering from any speech impairment or paralysis.

“That sounds like a TIA,” he offered, unwilling to commit himself further.

He immediately ordered high doses of aspirin and, above all, that Mona be rushed to the Hôtel-Dieu hospital, where he would call a colleague for her to be seen instantly. He hadn't thought of this colleague at random: the man was a tremendous pediatrician, also reputed to be a very good ophthalmologist and, incidentally, a talented hypnotherapist. Normally, he concluded, the blindness shouldn't last more than ten minutes, and he hung up. A good quarter of an hour had already passed since the initial cry of alarm.

In the car, the little girl was crying, hitting her temples. Her mother held her elbows, but deep down, she too wanted to hit that fragile, plump little head, the way you smack a broken machine in the foolish hope of jump-starting it. Her father, at the wheel of his rattling old Volkswagen, wished he could catch the affliction his little girl had fallen victim to. He was angry, convinced that something had happened in the kitchen and they were hiding it from him. He kept running through an inventory of all possible causes, from a blast of steam to a bad fall. But no, Mona declared it a hundred times:

“It happened all by itself!”

And her father didn't believe a word of it:

“People don’t just go blind like that!”

And yet, yes: you can go blind “just like that”—here was the proof. And today, that someone was Mona, ten years old, her tears of fear flowing in torrents—tears she perhaps hoped would wash away the soot that had clung to her pupils that October Sunday, as evening fell. Scarcely had she reached the threshold of the hospital adjoining Notre-Dame, on the Île de la Cité, when she abruptly cut short her sobs and froze:

“Mom, Dad, it’s coming back!”

Standing there in the street, where a cold wind was blowing, she rocked her head back and forth to encourage the return of her sight. Like a window blind being pulled up, the veil obscuring her eyes was lifting. Lines reappeared to her, then the sharp angles of faces, the contours of nearby objects, the texture of the walls, and every shade of color, from the most vibrant to the darkest. The child once again saw her mother’s slight silhouette, her long swan neck and frail arms, and the heavier build of her father. Finally, in the distance, she caught sight of a gray pigeon taking flight, which filled her with joy. Blindness had seized Mona, then let her go. It had passed right through her, the way a bullet pierces the skin and exits the other side of the body—causing pain, to be sure, but nonetheless leaving the body to heal itself. “A miracle,” thought her father, who scrupulously calculated exactly how long the attack had lasted: sixty-three minutes.

At the Hôtel-Dieu ophthalmology department, there was no question of letting the little girl leave before running a battery of tests, and providing a diagnosis and prescriptions. The anxiety was certainly deferred, but not entirely dispelled. A nurse directed them to a room on the first floor of the building. It was the office of the pediatrician, who had been alerted by the family doctor. Dr. Van Orst was biracial, and prematurely bald. His large, gleaming white coat contrasted with the sickly green of the walls. His enormous smile, which carved jovial little wrinkles into his face, made him instantly likable; he was nonetheless the custodian of many tragedies. He stepped forward:

“How old are you?” he asked in a voice raspy from tobacco.

Mona was ten years old. She was the only child of two parents who loved each other. Camille, the mother, was nearing forty. She was of modest height, sported short, tousled hair, and had a distant trace of working-class cheek in her voice. Her charm lay in her “slightly unhinged” side, as her man put it, though this was underpinned by a fearsome determination: within her, anarchy was ceaselessly entwined with authority. She worked at a temp agency as a good employee—committed and zealous. In the morning, at least. The afternoon was another story. She exhausted herself with volunteer work. Every cause was a worthy one, from isolated seniors to abused animals. 

As for Paul, he was past fifty-seven. Camille was his second wife. The first had disappeared with his best friend. He wore a tie to distract from the frayed collars of his shirts and plugged away as a small-time secondhand dealer, mainly passionate about 1950s American culture: jukeboxes, pinball machines, posters… And since it had all begun in his adolescence with a collection of heart-shaped keychains, he possessed an impressive array of them, which he had no desire to sell and which, in any case, wouldn’t have interested anyone. With the rise of the Internet, his shop, tucked away in Montreuil, had very nearly closed its doors. So he had gotten online himself, leveraging his expertise through a website he updated constantly and translated into English. Despite a business sense bordering on the nonexistent, he could count on a clientele of attentive collectors who regularly saved him from ruin. The previous summer, he had repaired a 1955 Gottlieb “Wishing Well” pinball machine and fetched the tidy sum of ten thousand euros for it. A lifesaving transaction, after months of lean times… And then, once again, the void. The crisis, people told him. Every day at the shop, Paul would polish off a bottle of red wine, then stick it, like a trophy, onto one of those hedgehog-shaped bottle racks that had secured the legacy of Marcel Duchamp. He raised his glass alone, unable to find a way to resent anyone. In his head, he toasted Mona. To her health.

While a nurse led the child through the labyrinth of the hospital to undergo various tests, Dr. Van Orst, ensconced in an enormous armchair, delivered an initial diagnosis to Paul and Camille:

“TIA, or Transient Ischemic Attack.”

This meant that the blood had momentarily stopped flowing to the organs and that they now had to find the cause of this dysfunction. But Mona’s case, he continued, baffled him: on the one hand, the attack—exceedingly rare in a girl her age—seemed severe, since both eyes had been affected and it had lasted for over an hour; on the other hand, it had completely spared her ability to move and speak. The MRI would no doubt tell them more. They had to, he added, looking uncomfortable, prepare themselves for the worst.

Mona had to lie down on a sliding table inside a horrible machine and surrender to it docilely, without moving. She was asked to remove her pendant. She refused. It was a thin chain made of fishing line weighted with a tiny shell that had belonged to her grandmother and brought her luck. She had always had it, and her beloved "Dadé" wore a matching one himself. The two talismans connected them to each other, she thought, and she didn't want to feel torn away from her grandfather. Since the pendant contained no metal, they let her keep it. Then her head—her pretty head, haloed by mid-length brown hair with tawny highlights and blessed with an adorable round mouth—was enclosed in an ogreish chamber echoing with factory noise. During the fifteen minutes the torture lasted, Mona sang songs to herself nonstop to resist, to inject a little good cheer and life into this coffin. She sang a slightly sappy lullaby her mother used to hum when tucking her in not so long ago; she sang a pop song, a melody that played on loop in supermarkets and whose music video, full of slick-haired young boys, she liked; she sang catchy advertising jingles; she also sang "Une souris verte," remembering the day she had screamed the lyrics to exasperate her father, without success.

The MRI results came back. Dr. Van Orst called Camille and Paul in, and hastened to reassure them. There was nothing. Absolutely nothing. On the cross-sectional images, the brain's anatomy revealed only homogeneous zones. No tumor on that front. Endless other tests were run. All through the night: from the depths of her pupils to her inner ear, by way of her blood, bones, muscles, and arteries. Nothing there either. The calm after the storm. Had there even been a storm?

The face of a clock lost in a corridor of the Hôtel-Dieu read five in the morning. An image from a nursery rhyme came to Camille's mind: it was, she confided, exhausted, to her husband, as if some evil being had stolen both of Mona's eyes before giving them back. As if it had chosen the wrong victim, Paul added. Or as if it had sent a sign, a warning, and was preparing to repeat its crime, they both thought in silence.

Out in the courtyard, the bell rang. The flood of children, shepherded by Mme Hadji, made its way up to the second floor. The teacher informed her fifth-grade students that they would not be seeing their classmate, Mona, until after the All Saints' break. She herself had just been notified by Camille, who, over the phone, had explained almost everything about the hellish night, without concealing the gravity of the events. Naturally, the children asked questions. Had she been allowed to leave on vacation a week before everyone else?

"She's a little sick," the teacher merely offered, frankly dissatisfied with the phrasing herself.

"A little sick—lucky!" exclaimed Diego in the third row, his high-pitched voice meeting with the approval of the entire class.

Because most children picture illness as a golden ticket to freedom...

At the back of the classroom, right next to the chalk-dusted curtains, Lili and Jade, Mona’s two best friends, who knew every corner of her bedroom, were practically drooling with envy. Oh, how they wished they could be with her! “A little sick”? Yeah, right, Lili thought, but she was definitely going to spend her days in her father's secondhand shop. And Jade, her eyes drawn to the seat Mona had left vacant, delightedly pictured herself in her company, making up all sorts of games and stories in the modest little shop filled with old objects that smelled of America—a whole bric-a-brac of shimmering, amusing, and mysterious things that made children dream. But Lili objected:

“No, no, when she’s sick, her grandpa Dadé comes to watch her, and he scares me.”

Jade forced a mocking laugh to show that nothing scared *her*, least of all Mona’s grandfather. Yet, deep down, the little girl had to admit it: yes, faced with this towering, gaunt, scarred old man who spoke in a deep, stony voice, she wasn't feeling particularly brave either…

“Hello, Dad, it’s me.”

It was noon when Camille, her limbs numb, finally decided to call her father. Henry Vuillemin refused to use a cell phone; he invariably answered his landline with a brief, crisp, and sharp “yes” that left little room for enthusiasm. His daughter hated this ritual, longing every time for the days when her mother was still alive to pick up the receiver. She let the syllables fall one by one.

“Dad, I have to tell you: something terrible happened last night.”

She recounted everything, in order, trying to keep her emotions in check.

“Well?” asked Henry with a hint of impatience.

But Camille had held back her tears so much over the course of her story that a massive sob heaved through her body and choked her: she was unable to answer.

“Sweetheart, well?” her father pressed her.

This unexpected “sweetheart” gave her a sudden rush of oxygen; she caught her breath and breathed:

“Nothing! For now, nothing. It's okay, I think.”

Then, Henry exhaled a long sigh of relief. Tilting his neck back, he threw his head back and observed the cheerful motifs of plump fruit, foliate scrolls, and spring flowers on his ceiling moldings.

“Let me speak to her.”

But Mona, curled up in a living room armchair under a russet throw, had dozed off.

The poet Ovid described the phase when consciousness falls asleep as entering an immense cave sheltering the languid, indolent god Sleep. He imagined a cavern inaccessible to Phoebus, master of the Sun. Mona had learned from her grandfather that, on a human scale, there was no journey more regular than the one to these mysterious, shifting lands… It was therefore fitting not to neglect these realms where one wanders ceaselessly, all throughout life.

Over the following days, Dr. Van Orst ordered further tests at the Hôtel-Dieu. They once again failed to reveal any specific abnormality. The explanation for those sixty-three minutes of blindness remained stubbornly elusive, so much so that the doctor was now reluctant to use the term "transient ischemic attack," which implied a vascular deficiency he was no longer entirely certain of. In the absence of a clear diagnosis, he suggested to Mona—and to her parents—that they try hypnosis. This idea elicited a stunned silence from Paul. As for the little girl, she wasn't entirely sure what that meant. She associated the word with the "choking game" she had vaguely heard about at school, which terrified her. To correct this misconception, Van Orst explained that by putting Mona into a hypnotic state, he could temporarily place her under his influence. Such an experience would allow him to go back in time and guide her to the original moment when her sight had faded, have her relive it, and, potentially, identify the cause. Paul was outraged. It was out of the question; it was dangerous. Van Orst did not press the matter: for a child to be hypnotized effectively, she must let go in complete trust. But between Mona’s preconceptions and her father’s angry overreaction, the situation was now a minefield. Camille, for her part, had said nothing.

Consequently, Van Orst prescribed a traditional medical regimen for his young patient: weekly blood and vascular tests, visits to the ophthalmologist, and a ten-day convalescence. He instructed Paul and Camille to watch for "the onset of any subjective symptomatic signs," which meant they had to pay extremely close attention to how their daughter was feeling. To that end, he suggested consulting a child psychiatrist:

"It’s everyday preventative care, rather than therapy strictly speaking," he assured them.

Paul and Camille absorbed his recommendations in a daze, but deep down, only one question was gnawing at them: "Is Mona at risk of eventually losing her sight?" Curiously, Dr. Van Orst never once mentioned the threat of a permanent relapse, and despite the parents’ obsessive fear of it, they preferred to avoid the subject. They even told themselves that, after all, if the doctor was avoiding the issue, there was simply no need to bring it up.

Henry Vuillemin, however, brought it up with his daughter head-on. He wasn't the type to shy away from questions, even those that led into the abyss. Usually quite sparing with phone calls unless it was to hear Mona’s voice, he called constantly that week. In a warm, impassioned voice, he badgered Camille: yes or no, was his darling granddaughter, the treasure of his life, going to go blind? Henry also persistently demanded to see Mona, and Camille could not decently refuse him. She suggested he come on All Saints' Sunday, exactly one week after the bout of blindness. Paul, who could guess the nature of the conversation, secretly resigned himself and downed a glass of rough Burgundy in almost a single gulp. Around his father-in-law, he felt miserably foolish. Mona, on the other hand, jumped up and down with impatience when she heard the news.

She loved this grandfather of hers, so full of years and strength. She also loved to watch how everyone who crossed his path was charmed by his towering silhouette and his heavy, thick, almost square-rimmed glasses. In his company, she felt protected. And transported. Henry had always made a point of speaking to her as he would to an adult. She was the one who demanded this asymmetry, savoring it and finding it amusing. She was never afraid of not understanding, and she laughed at any mistakes or misunderstandings. For she carefully monitored her own language in return, and this was obviously more of a game than anything with real stakes.

Henry didn't want to turn her into a little trained monkey. He didn't want to be one of those parodies of a grandfather who hunt down the errors of youth, the better to correct them in a pedantic voice. That wasn't in his nature. He had never made her do her homework, never meddled with her report cards. Besides, he loved the way Mona expressed herself. Even more so, he was fascinated by her turns of phrase. Why? To that, he held no key. He failed to understand. He had always been obsessed, haunted by something in her childlike speech. Was it an extra element, or a missing *je-ne-sais-quoi*? A quality or a flaw? This impression was all the more unsettling because it was not recent: Mona's "little music" had always harbored an enigma that Henry was firmly resolved to uncover one day, by dint of listening.

Camille sometimes admitted her astonishment at a relationship she considered "too good to be true," but she acknowledged that it worked wonderfully and that her daughter was happy. And besides, Henry, who readily quoted Victor Hugo's *The Art of Being a Grandfather*, reminded anyone who would listen of one of the cardinal principles of transmission: it matters little whether one immediately understands everything someone says, as if every new word had to already be a fully blossomed tree in the vast orchard of the brain. The blossoms would surely arrive on the appointed day, provided one had plowed furrows and planted seeds.

With Henry Vuillemin, these furrows and seeds were a flow of rich and confident words that hooked you from the very first intonation and never let you go; it was a very simple way of speaking, but of such breadth that it was exhilarating; it was a storyteller's cadence that often accelerated, before slowing down and becoming tinged with gentle emotion. It was a steamroller of worldly experience and quiet erudition.

Thus, with this "Dadé," the relationship was of a special nature. From grandparents to grandchildren, from grandchildren to grandparents, a miraculous bond sometimes forms, stemming from the fact that, through a sort of existential arc, the elders return, from the heights of their old age, to the feelings of their early youth and grasp, better than anyone, the springtime of life.

Henry Vuillemin occupied a handsome apartment on Avenue Ledru-Rollin, right above the Bistrot du Peintre, a narrow, wood-paneled establishment imitating the Art Nouveau style. He went down there every morning and had his routine: a coffee and a croissant, reading the national press, a brief exchange here and there with the everyday patrons and the waiters on their break. He felt he belonged to an older world and, ritually, walked at a very slow pace to the Place de la Bastille, liked to examine the furniture in the shop windows along the Rue du Faubourg-Saint-Antoine, headed back up toward the Place de la République via the median of the Boulevard Richard-Lenoir, and cut back onto the Boulevard Voltaire. In the late afternoon, at home, he pored over the art books that were piled up to the ceiling. Henry, who stood a centimeter taller than General de Gaulle, would retrieve the most inaccessible ones without a step stool or ladder, and by a curious coincidence, those were invariably the ones that appealed to him most. His memory was prodigious, although one had to draw a distinction between his propensity to speak of what he knew and his personal memories, which he protected beneath layers of reserve. Mona knew the rule. The sole forbidden subject with her grandfather was bringing up Colette Vuillemin, who had left him a widower seven years earlier. Following her father's lead, Camille never mentioned her either. Try as the child might to open a breach from time to time, she was always met with a leaden silence. Colette was not talked about. Ever. The single exception to this taboo was the talisman Henry kept around his neck in homage to his late wife. It was a pretty little cerith shell strung on a fishing line that he had picked up with her on the French Riviera during the summer of 1963—he no longer knew exactly what day, but he remembered the blistering heat and swearing many things to Colette. Mona, as we have said, wore the same pendant, inherited from her grandmother.

We all have our ways of swearing. Henry Vuillemin, for his part, swore on "all that is beautiful on earth." The expression caught Mona off guard, and whenever she heard it, she invariably shrugged with a slightly bewildered laugh: all that is beautiful on earth was at once a bit of everything and a bit of nothing. And then, she wondered if he, her revered grandfather, was a part of it. Henry had obviously been an attractive young man and remained imposing, charming, spectacular. His octogenarian face, emaciated and sharp, exuded a tremendously appealing vigor and intelligence. But he was scarred. A scar slashed across the right side of his face, beginning beneath the cheekbone and climbing all the way to the eyebrow. The wound must have been very painful. It had not merely torn away a furrow of skin, but a piece of the cornea as well. It was a souvenir of war. A horrific memory: on September 17, 1982, during a photojournalism assignment in Lebanon for Agence France-Presse, a Phalangist had slashed him with a knife to stop him from advancing. He was approaching the Shatila camp. Rumor had it that massacres were being perpetrated there, that Palestinian refugees were being arbitrarily executed, without trial, in retaliation for the assassination of President Bachir Gemayel. He had wanted to see for himself, to bear witness. They had barred his way with inhuman violence. Henry had lost a great deal of blood and the use of his eye. This infirmity, combined with his great height and, over the years, an increasingly pronounced gauntness, had lent his appearance an otherworldly air. The handsome reporter, who had resembled Eddie Constantine, had become a figure of legend.

On All Saints’ Day, Mona was in fine form. Her parents had worked hard to ensure the leaden November atmosphere was made joyful. Jade and Lili, her two friends, had come over to watch an installment of *Toy Story*, the animated film where toys come to life. They had horsed around good-naturedly. Jade, especially. She was a mischievous and pretty little girl, with delicate Eurasian eyes, olive skin, and perfectly combed hair. Yet she was driven by a surprising passion: making faces. She knew how to transform her harmonious face into a shifting, unhinged theater, where bizarre and farcical expressions paraded by like frenzied actors. Thrilled, Mona always begged for more.

At seven o’clock, the intercom buzzed. Paul pursed his lips and raised his eyebrows. Camille pressed the button:

“Dad?”

It was indeed him, arriving right on the dot. After greeting him, Paul left to walk Jade and Lili back to their respective parents’ homes, and while he was gone, Mona, her mother, and her grandfather found themselves alone together in the apartment. After an irrepressible outpouring of joy, the little girl, who had refrained from telling her two friends about her misadventure, launched into a detailed account of her sixty-three-minute ordeal and the hardships she had endured at the hospital. Camille did not interrupt her.

Henry, meanwhile, as he listened to Mona talk on and on, examined the place where the child lived with clinical detachment. Even her bedroom, despite the enchanting kitsch of the decor, struck him as extremely sad. This wallpaper with its garlanded flower motif, these knickknacks studded with heart- or animal-shaped sequins, these pink or brown stuffed animals, these grotesque posters of pop stars barely out of their teens, this plastic jewelry, this furniture imitating that of cartoon princesses… The candy colors of all this junk caught him by the throat. In this whole ensemble dripping with bad taste, there were only two windows onto a form of beauty. A sturdy 1950s American industrial lamp with an articulated arm, which Paul had picked up at a flea market, given to Mona, and clamped to her small secretary desk. And then, above the bed, in a frame, an exhibition poster reproducing a painting. Extraordinarily subtle, cool-toned colors crackled within it. They depicted a nude woman, in profile and leaning forward, seated on a stool draped with a white cloth, her left ankle resting on her right knee. In one corner, one could read: “Musée d’Orsay Paris – Georges Seurat (1859-1891)”.

Despite these exceptions, Henry came to the depressing realization that childhood is, out of convenience, largely saturated with frivolous and ugly objects. And Mona was no exception to the rule. Beauty, true artistic beauty, only entered her daily life by stealth. This was perfectly normal, Henry noted: the refinement of taste and the development of sensibility would come later. Except for the fact—and this thought choked him—that Mona had come very close to losing her sight, and that if her eyes were to go permanently dark in the coming days, weeks, or months, she would take with her, into the recesses of her memory, only the recollection of flashy and shallow things. An entire life in the dark, mentally making do with the worst the world produces, with no escape for her memories? It was impossible. It was terrifying.

To his daughter’s great annoyance, Henry was taciturn and distant throughout dinner. When Mona finally went to bed, Camille, with a resolute air, turned up the volume of the Coltrane saxophone playing on an old chrome jukebox, to mask their voices and ensure the little girl wouldn't hear a thing.

“Dad. For the moment, Mona seems to be processing... (she hesitated over the words)... what just happened rather well. But the doctor recommends she see a child psychiatrist. It might be a bit strange for her, and I was wondering if you could drive her to the appointment, just so she feels reassured...”

“A shrink? Is that really what’s going to stop her from going blind?”

“That’s not the point, Dad!”

“I think it is, and it will be as long as you don’t dare ask the doctor! Doctor who, again?”

“His name is Van Orst and he’s very good,” Paul offered awkwardly, trying to take part in the conversation.

“Dad, wait,” Camille went on. “Listen to me. Paul and I are going to do everything we can to make sure nothing happens to Mona, do you hear me? But she’s ten years old, and we can’t just pretend she hasn’t been through anything. The doctor says her psychological stability is a priority. So I’m just asking you to see if you’d be willing to take care of this, because I know Mona will trust you. Are you listening, Dad?”

Henry heard perfectly. But at that precise moment, in a fraction of a second, an Apollonian idea ignited in his mind, one he kept jealously to himself. He would not be taking his granddaughter to see a child psychiatrist, no... Instead, he would put her on a treatment of a completely different nature, a cure capable of offsetting the ugliness her youth was being drenched in.

Mona, who had complete confidence in him, who placed a trust in him she granted no other adult, was going to have to accompany him to the places where the most beautiful and most human things the world has produced are kept: she was going to have to accompany him to museums. If, by some tragedy, Mona were one day to go blind forever, she would at least possess a sort of reservoir, deep in her brain, from which to draw visual splendors. The grandfather thus had his plan... Once a week, following an immutable ritual, he would take Mona by the hand and lead her to contemplate a work of art—just one—first in a long silence, so that the infinite delight of colors and lines could penetrate his granddaughter’s mind, and then in words, so that she might move beyond the stage of visual rapture to understand how artists speak to us about life, and how much they illuminate it.

For his little Mona, then, he envisioned something better than medicine. They would go to the Palais du Louvre first, to the Musée d’Orsay next, and finally to Beaubourg. There, yes, there, in those places devoted to the preservation of the most audacious and beautiful things humanity had produced, he would find a tonic for his granddaughter. Henry was not one of those art lovers who, removed from the world, content themselves in a vacuum with the smooth polish of flesh painted by Raphael or the rhythm of a line punctuated by Degas’s charcoals. He loved the almost incendiary qualities of artworks. Sometimes, he would say: “Art is pyrotechnics, or it’s hot air.” And he loved the fact that, whether in its entirety or through a single detail, a painting, a sculpture, or a photograph could be capable of stoking a sense of existence.

At the moment Camille was asking for his help, Henry was thus assailed by hundreds of images: the rocky masses behind the *Mona Lisa*; the monkey sculpted on the back of Michelangelo’s *Dying Slave*; the alarmed expression of the blonde-curled child on the right of the *Oath of the Horatii*; the strange, gelatinous loins in Goya’s *Lamb*; and then the clods of earth in Rosa Bonheur’s *Ploughing in the Nivernais*; the butterfly-shaped signature Whistler uses in the portrait of his mother; the tottering apse of Van Gogh’s church... Or again Kandinsky’s colors, Picasso’s fractures, or Soulages’s *outrenoir*. All of this burst forth like so many signs calling to him, demanding to be seen, heard, understood, loved. Like a counter-fire against the ashes threatening Mona’s eyes.

Henry broke into a broad smile:

"Agreed, I'll take Mona with me every Wednesday afternoon. From now on, I'm the one, and the only one, handling this psychological care. It will be strictly between the two of us. Is that agreed?"

"Will you find someone good, Dad? Will you ask your old friends for advice?"

"Do we agree on the principle? I handle it, with no questions or interference from anyone."

"But you won't just pick a child psychiatrist at random, do you hear me? You will be careful."

"Do you trust me, sweetheart?"

"Yes," Paul asserted with authority to counter any hesitation from Camille. "Mona admires you and respects you, and she loves you like she loves no one else, so yes, we trust you."

At her husband's firm words, Camille nodded tenderly, adding nothing more. Henry felt a slightly damp gleam flash across his good eye. Coltrane's sax made the walls ripple. In her room, Mona slept, watched over by Georges Seurat.

The great glass pyramid amused Mona. Thrust insolently amid the stone pavilions of the Louvre Palace, its ethereal shape, its transparency, and the way it caught the cold November sun enchanted her. Her grandfather was not saying much. Yet she could plainly see he was in high spirits, for he clasped her little hand in his with the confident tenderness of happy people, swinging their arms with an easy grace. His joviality, though silent, radiated with a childlike glow.

"The pyramid is so beautiful, Dadé! It looks like a giant Chinese hat," Mona declared, as she wove through the clusters of tourists in the courtyard.

Henry looked at her, a sort of doubtful pout mingling with his smile. It gave him an odd expression that made the little girl giggle. They entered the glass structure, went through security, glided down an escalator, and emerged into the vast hall—so remarkably like those in train stations or airports—before making their way toward the Denon Wing. The commotion around them was stifling. Stifling, yes, because most visitors who make up a major museum crowd have no idea what they actually want to do; they generate a pervasive sense of drift, infusing the atmosphere with that stagnant, hesitant, and even slightly murky quality peculiar to such places when they fall victim to their own success.

Amid the hubbub, Henry bent his immensely long, thin legs to speak to his granddaughter eye to eye. He did this whenever he had something truly important to tell her. His mineral voice, pure and deep, carried over the surrounding din. It was as if it silenced the idle chatter and exhausting clamor of the entire universe.

"Mona, every week, just the two of us will come to the museum to see a work of art—a single work of art, no more. These people around us want to swallow everything in one gulp, and they get lost, not knowing how to pace their appetites. We are going to be much wiser, much more sensible. We will look at just one piece, silently at first, for several long minutes, and then, afterward, we will talk about it."

"Oh, really? I thought we were going to the doctor." (She had wanted to say "the child psychiatrist," but wasn't entirely sure of the word.)

"Tell me, Mona, do you want to go to the psychiatrist afterward? Is it important to you?"

"As if I'd want to! Anything but that!"

"Then listen to me closely, sweetheart. There will be no need for that, so long as you look carefully at what we're going to see."

"Really? And is it bad if we skip the..." (she stumbled over the word again and opted for her simpler phrasing) "...doctor?"

"No, it's not bad at all. I swear it on all that is beautiful on earth."

After navigating a maze of staircases, Henry and Mona found themselves in a modestly sized room, a space through which many people passed, yet where almost no one took the trouble to rest their eyes on the artwork holding pride of place. Henry let go of his granddaughter’s hand and told her with infinite gentleness:

“Now, look, Mona. Take as many minutes as you need to look, to really look.”

And Mona planted herself, intimidated, before a badly damaged painting, severely cracked in several places and missing entire pieces—a painting that, at first glance, emanated the feeling of an altered and distant past. Henry gazed at it too, but mainly he was studying his granddaughter, sensing her wavering, her perplexity. Now she was furrowing her brow, then stifling a slightly awkward laugh. He knew that even before a Renaissance masterpiece, a ten-year-old girl—however lively, curious, sensitive, and clever she might be—could not fall instantly into raptures. He knew that, contrary to popular belief, it took time to penetrate the depths of art, that it was a painstaking exercise rather than an easy enchantment. He also knew that Mona, because it was he who asked it of her, would play along, and that despite her embarrassment, she would scrutinize the forms, the colors, and the texture with the promised attention.

The image was laid out simply. On the far left, one could make out a fountain, before which four standing young women with long, curly hair, strikingly similar, were arrayed like a frieze. They grasped each other by the arm, intertwining as if forming a human garland punctuated by the diversity of their garments: green and mauve for the first, white for the second, pink for the third, orange-yellow for the fourth. This multicolored procession gave the impression of forward movement, and facing it, on the right side of the work, isolated against a neutral background, stood a fifth woman—young, extremely beautiful, adorned with a magnificent pendant and a crimson dress. She too seemed animated by a forward motion, as if stepping out to meet the procession. She was, in fact, holding out a sort of cloth in their direction, into which one of the creatures—the one in pink—was delicately placing something. What was it? Impossible to say. The object was effaced. There was also, in the foreground, in a corner, a little boy with blond hair, seen in profile, looking slightly amused. The setting was entirely bare: the only echo of the fountain on the left was a truncated and highly faded column closing off the scene on the right.

Mona played along. But after six minutes, it was already too much. Six minutes in front of a decrepit image was an unusual and arduous trial. So she turned to her grandfather and struck up a conversation with an impertinence only she could get away with:

“Dadé, your painting is really badly damaged! Your face looks brand new next to it…”

Henry looked at the artwork and all the damage that scarred it. He crouched down.

“You’d do better to listen to me instead of talking nonsense… A ‘painting,’ you say! Wrong! First of all, Mona, it’s not a ‘painting.’ It’s what we call a ‘fresco.’ Do you know what a fresco is?”

“Yes, I think so… but I forgot!”

“A fresco is a painting done on a wall, and it’s very fragile, because if the wall gets damaged—and walls crumble a lot over time—well, the painting gets damaged too…”

“Why did the artist paint on this wall? Because it’s the Louvre?”

“Not at all. It’s true that an artist might want to create a fresco at the Louvre, because the Louvre is the largest museum on the planet, and it would be normal for a painter to want to apply their work directly to it, so that it would become like the skin of the palace. But, you see, Mona, the Louvre hasn’t always been a museum. It has only been one for about two hundred years. Before that, it was a castle, where kings and the court lived. When the artist painted this fresco, it was around 1485. And so he didn’t create it for the walls of the Louvre, but for those of a villa in Florence.”

“Florence?” (She absentmindedly fiddled with the pendant around her neck.) “To me, that sounds like the name of an old fiancée of yours, from before Grandma, right?”

“Doesn’t ring a bell, but anything’s possible! Now listen here a minute. Florence is an Italian city. In Tuscany, to be precise. And it is the cradle of what we call the Renaissance. In the fifteenth century—the Quattrocento, as the Italians say—Florence experienced an extraordinary period of effervescence. It had about a hundred thousand inhabitants and the city was prosperous, thanks to trade and banking. And you see, religious orders, high-ranking political dignitaries, and even private citizens from the highest rungs of the social ladder wanted to put their wealth to good use and demonstrate their prestige by supporting the creative work of their contemporaries. They are known as great patrons of the arts. So, painters, sculptors, and architects benefited from their trust, and from the resources they provided, to create incredibly beautiful paintings, statues, and buildings.”

“I bet they were made of gold...”

“Not exactly. In the Middle Ages, there were indeed very beautiful paintings lavishly covered in gold leaf. That gave the object value, and it symbolized divine light, on top of that! But during the Renaissance, painting gradually dispensed with the flashy effect of gold, seeking instead to better render reality as we see it, with its landscapes, the uniqueness of faces, animals, and the movement of living beings, of things, of the sky, and of the sea.”

“So people liked nature, is that it?”

“That is exactly it: people began to love nature. But you know, when we talk about nature, we aren’t just talking about what grows in the ground.”

“Oh, and what else do we talk about?”

“We also talk, in a more abstract way, about human nature. Human nature is in fact who we are deep down, with our zones of light and shadow, our faults and our virtues, our fears and our hopes. And, you see, it is precisely this human nature that the artist seeks to improve.”

“And just how do they do that?”

“If you cultivate your garden, you do good for nature. You allow it to flourish. This fresco seeks to do good for human nature by telling it something very simple, yet essential—something you must remember forever, Mona.”

But Mona, to tease the old man, plugged her ears and squeezed her eyes shut, as if she didn’t want to hear or see a single thing he might say to her. After a few seconds, she discreetly cracked open one eyelid to observe his reaction. He was smiling nonchalantly. So, she dropped her little game and gave him her full attention. For she sensed that her grandfather—after those long minutes of silence, contemplation, and discussion, after that little journey through the damaged image unfolding before her eyes—was finally going to reveal to her one of those secrets we keep engraved deep within our hearts.

Henry motioned for her to direct her gaze to the slightly faded area, where there appeared to be an object that the young woman on the right was taking into her hands. The little girl obliged.

"The procession of these four women on the left is made up of Venus and the Three Graces. They are generous deities. And they are offering a gift—we don't know what it is because a bit of the paint is missing—to a young girl. The Three Graces are what we call allegories, Mona: they don't exist in real life and you'll never cross paths with them, but they represent important values. They are said to represent the three steps that make us sociable and hospitable beings—that is, humans who are truly human. And this fresco expresses just how crucial these three steps are; it seeks to anchor them in each of us."

"Three steps? What steps?"

"The first consists of knowing how to give, the third of knowing how to give back. And between the two, there is one without which nothing is possible, which acts as a sort of keystone—a keystone that bears the weight of all human nature."

"Which one, Dadé?"

"Look: what is the young girl on the right doing?"

"You already told me: she's lucky because she's receiving a gift..."

"Exactly, Mona. She is receiving a gift. And that is what's absolutely fundamental. Knowing how to receive. What this fresco is saying is that we must learn to receive; that human nature, to be capable of great and beautiful things, must be ready to welcome: to welcome the kindness of others, their desire to bring joy, to welcome what it does not yet have and what it is not yet. There will always be time for the one who receives to give back, but in order to give back—that is, to give anew—one must first have been capable of receiving. Do you understand, Mona?"

"It's complicated, this story of yours, but yes, I think I understand..."

"I'm certain you do! And you see, if these ladies are so beautiful, drawn with such fluid and graceful lines, this unbroken stroke that admits no jarring note, no hesitation, it is to express the importance of this continuity, of this chain that must bind humans to one another and elevate their nature: giving, receiving, and giving back; giving, receiving, and giving back; giving, receiving, and giving back..."

Mona didn't know what to say anymore. Above all, she did not want to disappoint her grandfather. She had already resorted to humor earlier in their conversation, and now she fell silent to avoid saying anything too naive, knowing full well that he was speaking to her and bringing her to this immense museum so that she would grow up a little. For the moment, she simply felt torn, because this call to grow up, this intoxicating exploration of a new world, possessed an extraordinary magnetic power—all the more so because the call came from Henry, whom she revered. Yet, in the deepest recesses of her soul, there already lingered the terrible premonition that what is given back can never be recovered. And this regret, sharp, albeit very distant, for a childhood vanished forever, wrung her heart.

"Shall we go, Dadé? Onward, troops?"

"Come on, Mona! Onward we go!"

Henry took her hand in his once more, and they left the Louvre at a slow pace, without a word. Outside, night was beginning to fall. Henry was fully aware of the unease that had just caused his granddaughter to falter. But he categorically refused to spare others under the pretext of ensuring nothing but good, rich, and pleasant moments in their company. No: he knew all too well that existence is worthwhile only on the condition of embracing its hardships, and that these, once sifted through time, reveal a precious and fertile material, a beautiful and useful substance, which allows life to truly be life.

Besides, through that miracle of childhood, Mona’s unease did not last long: stepping cheerfully along, she began to hum. Henry never interrupted her in those moments, which he found incredibly touching. Then suddenly, as they neared her house, Mona stopped, caught up in the shared lie they had agreed upon to avoid the sessions with the child psychiatrist. She widened her big blue eyes and turned her mischievous little head, laughing at the naughty trick they were playing on her parents.

“Dadé, what do I say if Mom and Dad ask me the name of the doctor I went to see?”

“Tell them his name is Dr. Botticelli.”
