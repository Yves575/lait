Prologue

He arranged to meet Zofia at eight. She had called that morning and asked to see him. He thought, "No." He said, "Alright." He regretted it later, but it was too late. She had seemed nervous. She stammered into the receiver, gasping loudly for air.

"I have to see you," she said. "I wouldn't ask if it weren't really important."

As always, he felt helpless against her quiet, low voice, against the *ł* she emphasized so meticulously, and the *ą* pronounced just a bit too nasally, as if a plump little animal with a twitchy tail had settled deep in her throat to tickle her larynx. He involuntarily surrendered to a surge of emotion before he had time to think, to remind himself that all these melodious syllables no longer belonged to him. Delicate, refined Zofia with her charming speech impediment had stopped noticing him altogether. And yet she had called… Why? What for? Had something happened? Once again, anxiety washed over him. Zofia had always been shrouded in an aura of mystery. That was what attracted him to her the most. Right from the very beginning of their acquaintance.

On Thursdays, she was the last to arrive. During the meetings, she would sit off to the side. She said very little, and afterwards left quietly, without saying goodbye to anyone. At first she caused some unease, but eventually everyone grew accustomed to her silent presence. From time to time the group leader would ask her a question, to which she would reply briefly and matter-of-factly. The others quickly lost interest in her. Everyone except him. He waited for Zofia every Thursday. He surreptitiously watched her pale hands, her moist, dark brown eyes, the chestnut hair shielding her face. After a month, without asking for permission, he changed his seat in the circle and sat by the wall. That day, he asked her out for coffee. Two weeks later, the leader called them in for a serious talk. He forbade private contact and ordered them to sit apart. He spoke sharply and firmly, stating he had no intention of tolerating insubordination within the group. He directed his words mostly at Zofia.

"I have been observing you for some time," he said. "I don't understand why you even come here. You should think about that very seriously. You are a bad influence on the others."

She listened to him calmly, then picked up her coat and purse.

"Alright." She turned in the doorway. "I won't be coming back."

He ran out after her. He knew he would have to drop out of the meetings, but it didn't bother him in the slightest. And so began the most beautiful three months of his life.

He lowered his head and flinched, as if the sight of his own hands had startled and frightened him. The old emotions washed over him in a trembling, surging wave. Three months, he winced. Three months during which he had come to believe he truly mattered to someone. It hadn’t lasted long… The affair had ended as abruptly as it began. They hadn’t even had time to get to know each other properly. He knew so little about her… Yet he hadn’t asked about anything. He preferred that their love blossom slowly, to let it gather strength and momentum. He hadn’t wanted to push or rush things. He dreamed of holidays together, waited for her to finally introduce him to her parents; then he would move in with her, they would renovate the place, put in a double bed… In time, they would buy a bigger apartment for the kids… For three months he had spun increasingly elaborate plans for the future, feeling calm and secure. And then, suddenly, it was all over. Zofia wasn't answering her phone. He went over to see what was going on with her. He knocked on the door. She didn’t answer, but he had an overwhelming feeling that someone was in the apartment. Sitting on the stairs, he could hear the muffled sounds of a conversation, shuffling, and floorboards creaking. That was when, for the first time in three months, he thought about the Thursday meetings, and fear gripped him. He went back home. The next day he went to her place again. She opened the door but wouldn’t let him in, blocking the threshold. The thought immediately struck him that someone was hiding behind her. *A lover?* he thought, and such a wave of despair and weakness washed over him that he had to lean against the wall. Zofia looked at him solemnly with large, moist eyes.

“I’m sorry,” she said. “I shouldn’t be handling it this way, but…”

He heard his own loud, ragged breathing. He felt himself growing pale. His head was spinning.

“Something has happened,” she resumed firmly. “We can’t keep seeing each other.”

That was it. Like the drop of a guillotine. No excuses, no explanations. They met once more, and she repeated the exact same thing: certain changes had taken place in her life, and they would no longer be seeing each other. Her eyes had lost their former softness. They had grown cold and resolute. “Certain changes” could only mean someone else; he had no doubt about that.

The following Thursday, he tried to return to the circle. They wouldn’t let him back in, but the leader scheduled one-on-one sessions for him. He made no secret of his deep concern. He claimed that the shock brought on by his relationship with Zofia could have serious repercussions—not just for him, but for the entire group. He went on at length about responsibility, obligations, and the established rules of conduct. Then he interrogated him thoroughly about his relationship with Zofia: what they did for those three months, where they had met, and what they had talked about. It seemed to him that the leader was overly pushy and prying, but he tried to answer anyway. Not much came of it. He was suffering from a strange kind of amnesia. Nothing remained in his memory but despair, which was slowly turning into rage and uncontrollable jealousy. If Zofia didn’t want to be with him, she couldn’t—she had no right to—get involved with anyone else!

But she did call today, he thought. Could she have decided to apologize to him after all? His heart pounded faster. To come back, to make up, after having used him and cast him aside? And just what did she think she was doing? He felt humiliated thinking about the past few weeks, about all those pleading attempts to make contact, the tearful phone calls, the letters, and the sleepless nights. He had relentlessly tried to get close to Zofia, to force her to talk, to recover their old intimacy, which surely couldn't have just disappeared, vanished into thin air without leaving a trace… He kept thinking he would find the right words, convince her, break the spell, and the old, gentle Zofia—his Zofia!—would stand before him once again. Meanwhile, she remained invariably indifferent, distant, and alien…

He sprang up from the chair and began to pace the room, bringing his hands to his face and tugging at the hair falling across his forehead. He went to the window. He looked out at the wet, ugly city: squares covered in mud and melting snow, gray sidewalks, the colorless facades of the apartment blocks across the way. Occasionally, an indistinct figure flitted past one of the windows. Outside the neighborhood shop, a few people were stamping their feet and huddling under umbrellas. She had used him as if he were a thing, an inanimate object you could use up and then throw away, not a living, feeling being. A human being. A man, he corrected himself silently. He paced the room again, pivoting sharply at the door and the opposite wall, only to freeze by the window a moment later. Dozens of apartment blocks stretched out in every direction: heavy, gloomy, reeking of damp. Strangers bustled about in cramped concrete apartments. Identical concrete modules, vertically and horizontally. Inside, the exact same tables, beds, and armchairs. Almost identical men and women cooked dinners, watched television together, went to the bathroom to take a warm bath or pee. Did she know, did she have any idea how deeply she had hurt him?! He looked at his watch. It was nearly one o'clock.

Extreme irritation hadn’t left him all day. He tried browsing the net, watched two episodes of *Brigada*, a Russian mafia series. In the early afternoon, he went to the local gym. He ran for an hour, then lifted weights and worked on his abs. He tried to tune out, to think only of Sasha, Kosmos, Pchyola, and Phil—the show’s brutal, ready-for-anything characters. Finally, he went to the sauna and took a cold shower. 

Back home, he took some milk from the fridge and sat down at the table. He drank straight from the carton in long, slow gulps. His irritation turned into a hard, brutal rage. He replayed their morning conversation in his head one more time and concluded that Zofia hadn’t called with the intention of reconciling. Her voice had sounded hesitant, but it held no remorse. Short, matter-of-fact sentences, no “I missed you” or “I’m glad I’ll see you.” Just a perfunctory “Goodbye” at the end. 

*Bitch*, he thought, tracking an ascending plane. A jagged streak tore through the thick, gray clouds. But what could Zofia want from him—he asked himself the same question for the hundredth time that day. It didn't matter. This would be his moment. She had invited him, so she would politely listen to what he had to say. He imagined how he would walk into the apartment, order her to sit down, and explain things briefly, concisely, in a firm tone, and how she would finally understand… 

And what if she didn’t want to listen? 

He could hit her, or even tie her up. Tape her mouth shut and watch her cry silently, terrified, defenseless, at his mercy. 

The plane climbed higher and disappeared above the clouds. In the apartment upstairs, someone put on loud dance music. He heard muffled laughter and the sound of light, quick footsteps.

It was approaching half past six. He put the milk back in the fridge. He put on a short bomber jacket, gloves, and sneakers, then pulled a cotton hood over his head. Outside, a strong, biting wind was blowing; wet snow clung to his shoes, which soaked through instantly. Even so, he didn’t turn toward the metro. He walked stubbornly along the sidewalk down KEN Avenue, neither stopping nor stepping out of anyone’s way. Zofia’s face appeared before his eyes: her wide mouth, straight nose, the small mole at the corner of her eye, and her gaze darting away as she said, “You have to understand, once and for all, that I want nothing to do with you.” He remembered his own agitation, fear, and the burning sense of rejection. How completely he had humiliated himself, made a fool of himself... He quickened his pace until he broke into a run, driven by anguish and helpless rage. “*Yob tvoyu mat’!*” “*Srat’ tebe v rot’!*” he kept repeating, unconsciously imitating the voice of one of the characters from *Brigada*. He didn’t stop until Służewiec, where he went down to the platform and took the metro to Racławicka, after which he set out once more along the snow-covered street, passing the brightly lit windows of cafes, workshops, and small neighborhood stores. Just past the school sports field, he turned into a gateway and nearly collided with an elderly man wearing a thick woolen coat and a fur hat.

“*Idi na khuy!*” he snarled, then shoved his way past brutally, pressing the man against the wall.

He was struggling to catch his breath. Relentless thoughts spun in circles, causing a sickening dizziness. Beads of sweat ran down his face.

Zofia got home before five. She set her shopping bag on the kitchen table, took off her soaked shoes, and, leaving wet footprints on the floor, went to the bathroom. She lay in the bathtub, thinking about how complicated her life had become over the last three months. *And whose fault is that?* she wondered, wringing out the sponge. “Whose fault?” she repeated aloud. *I attract bad luck like a magnet*, she continued thinking, standing up and picking up the soap. *The more I like someone, the bigger the freak they turn out to be. But do I, for some incomprehensible reason, only appeal to crazies, or do I actively seek out their company?* She rinsed off the lather. She stepped out of the tub and wrapped a towel around her head. From a wicker basket, she took out a pair of gray sweatpants and warm woolen socks. For a moment, she studied her face in the mirror. *What am I supposed to do now?* she asked her own reflection. *What am I going to do?*

Despite her worries, the person in the mirror had absolutely no intention of looking tired or unkempt. Zofia pressed two fingers to her cheeks and pulled the skin slightly toward her temples. She raised her eyebrows, then reached for her face powder and mascara. Refreshed and meticulously made-up, she stepped out of the bathroom. In the kitchen, she brewed some tea. She set two cups and a small plate of French pastries on the counter. Suddenly, she stopped mid-stride and stood for a moment, staring at the set table, as if trying to resolve a crucial issue in her mind. After a moment, she took out her cell phone and dialed the same number twice, to no avail. Finally, she left a voicemail.

"I wanted to cancel our meeting," she said, lowering her voice to lend her words a firm tone. "Something important came up. I'm really sorry. I'll call you tomorrow."

She looked at the phone hesitantly. She had no guarantee the message would be heard. She stood there for a moment, drumming her fingers nervously on the windowsill. Finally, she shrugged and sat down in the window alcove. It was approaching seven. She looked out at the small, fog-shrouded square and the abandoned construction site, separated from the street by a wooden fence. A solitary rook perched on the dark branch of a maple tree. She needed to make concrete decisions and weigh their consequences. Yet nothing sensible came to mind. No theoretical solution seemed right. She was caught in a trap with no way out. The rook shook its head, ruffled its feathers, and hobbled sideways toward the trunk. Zofia pulled her knees up to her chin. Down on the dark street, the wind chased an empty plastic cup. She closed her eyes.

At a quarter past seven, the intercom buzzed. She sighed and, without a word, pressed the button by the door. Then, acting on a sudden impulse, she snatched her handbag from where it lay beneath the coat rack. With a decisive motion, she pulled out a medical report. Walking toward the bathroom, she tore it into tiny pieces and tossed them into the toilet.

"I'm in the bathroom!" she called out, hearing the front door slam. "I'll be right out," she added, flushing the toilet.

At that moment, she felt a heavy blow to her temple. One, then another. It didn't hurt. She wasn't afraid. An immense weariness washed over her. *Who is it?* she wanted to ask before falling asleep, but blood spilled from her open mouth. She made one last effort to turn and look at her attacker, but only let out a moan and sank into deep darkness.

At eight o'clock, a piercing wind blew from the icy river. At half past eight, the embankment streetlamps went out due to a power failure. It grew dark. Only in the middle of the Vistula, where the current was strongest, did the paler outline of a drifting ice floe faintly loom. At nine, a beam of car headlights hastily swept across the surface of the water and vanished into the fog. In the brush, an awakened bird gave a sharp cry. The city rose on the escarpment, bathed in a glow. Above it, a second river had spread out. Broad and dark. Slowly, a slick, polished moon drifted onto it, gliding smoothly among the clouds.

A bright, clean floor of white-painted floorboards, wooden cabinets, a large square window—the entire space designed exactly according to the IKEA catalog. She was approaching forty, but had kept her slim figure and a light, girlish way of moving. She had grown used to the idea that she still looked like a college student. The skin on her cheeks had slackened and sagged slightly around her chin. Her dark, observant eyes had lost their former luster, yielding to a look of cool, matter-of-fact certainty, and her wide mouth, with its strange, almost inverted shape, was framed by the timid brackets of her first wrinkles. "No," she scolded him sharply.

“Not now!” she raised her voice, striving for a firm tone, yet she couldn’t hold back a smile as she watched the animal shift from paw to paw, arch its back, and strike increasingly coaxing poses. 

“You can't eat this much or you'll get fat, and I don't think we want that,” she said gently now, stepping out into the hall. Filip-Oleńka cut her off at a soft, stubborn trot. She nudged him aside. She put on her coat, her gloves, and her tall brown leather boots, grabbed her purse, and shut the door behind her.

“Tomorrow,” he almost shouted. “Yes, tomorrow, because tomorrow's the first... You don't actually start until tomorrow...” He tried to cling to at least a ghost of a victory.

“Ada,” she cut in quickly. “My name is Ada.”

“Right, Ada,” he cleared his throat. “We've got renovations going on today, and it's a total mess. Some of the boys are out in the field... So let's start tomorrow at eight.”

“Alright. See you tomorrow, then.”

She stood up, grabbed her bag, and was gone before he could even reply.

Dazed, Sawicki stared blankly at the wall. Was that it? Was it over? Was it a done deal? The whole conversation hadn't even lasted five minutes. He'd been defeated, pinned to the mat with a single, gentle move by this tiny woman who looked like an aging schoolgirl. He dragged a hand down his face.

Ada Rochniewicz had spent eight years on the Wrocław police force. He’d received that piece of information a month earlier, along with the announcement that as of early March he’d be getting a female colleague, a female cop, a female detective—neither fish nor fowl—with absolutely no way to appeal his superiors' decision. He knew little else about her.

In early February, after a talk with the chief, Rogowski had come back to the office they shared and sat down at his desk, coughing and sniffing.

“A chick? You sure?” Sawicki asked, without taking his eyes off his computer screen.

“A chick, yeah. The Old Man is working on softening our image. Our political correctness will go up if we take her on.”

He fidgeted in his chair. Sawicki knew Róg was glancing at him anxiously, but he stubbornly kept tapping away at his keyboard. He had a lot of work to do and didn’t feel like talking. Rogowski snorted and blew his nose loudly into a checkered handkerchief. Sawicki jutted out his lower lip.

"What?" he finally asked, hitting "Delete" instead of "Tab" and erasing a painstakingly filled-out field in the report.

"You're the one taking charge of her," Rogowski explained, immediately breaking into the dry, hacking cough of a chain smoker.

Sawicki was stunned. He stared at his monitor for a moment, then slapped his open palm against the desk.

"Have you lost your fucking mind?! Don't I have enough on my plate without having to babysit some useless broad?" he snapped. "This was your idea, wasn't it?" His voice rose steadily as Rogowski's coughing fit intensified. "Get it right out of your bald skull."

He was genuinely pissed off. Rogowski was the deputy chief, but everyone knew he unofficially ran the section. He made most of the decisions. Sawicki leaped to his feet and stormed out of the room.

The next day, when his anger had subsided a bit, they managed to have a calm conversation.

"It's not that bad," Rogowski explained in a reassuring tone. "I saw her file. She finished the officer training course. And nursing school before that... Word is, she's a good investigator. She's solved a few major cases, you'll be happy with her."

Sawicki shrugged.

"Eight years in the Wrocław police force," Róg continued, undeterred. "Glowing letters of recommendation. A commendation for her role in taking down the BAF organized crime group," he recited, like a kid reading a poem at a Constitution Day school assembly.

Sawicki remembered hearing something about that once. Seven people had been arrested, and forty kilos of cocaine destined to be smuggled across the German border were seized.

"But..." the chief added, sounding less confident now, "in 2010 she was transferred to the SDS... desk duty..."

Sawicki raised his eyebrows in surprise. A desk bitch? Why was she being reassigned to the criminal division right now? After two years of warming a chair and pushing paper, you don't just go back out on the streets...

"This doesn't add up," he said.

"I know. Unless..."

"Unless some higher-up really wanted to transfer Adela Rochniewicz, the nurse-turned-investigator, to Warsaw and pulled the right strings," he finished angrily. "Is she married? Any kids?"

"The 'marital status' box was blank."

"Why am I not surprised?" he muttered.

They didn't talk about her again until yesterday, when Rogowski ordered him to report to the briefing room at nine the following morning and take charge of the new Inspector.

Sawicki sighed, stretched out his arms, interlaced his fingers, and cracked his knuckles, then rose slowly and went down to the reception area to get a coffee from the vending machine. Men in coveralls were dozing on the wooden benches along the wall; a young guy in a military jacket was swearing loudly, while an undernourished girl tried to calm him down. A large blue sign above the counter read: “Visitors Received Here.” The duty officer was still nowhere to be seen. Heading back upstairs, he picked up some blank forms from the admin office and decided he would spend the day doing nothing but paperwork.

3

On Friday morning, Sawicki woke up feeling almost rested. He had slept for seven hours. He got up a few minutes past six. He dressed quietly and limited his morning routine to brushing his teeth. He wanted to drink his coffee alone, in peace. He put the kettle on. The thermometer outside the kitchen window read zero. Clouds obscured the sky. Heavy, wet snow began to fall. At seven, he woke his wife and little girls. Just before eight, he arrived at work. The reception area was completely quiet, with no sign of any visitors. The duty officer was dozing, his head propped on his hand. Upstairs, the door to the admin office was closed. Walking down the corridor, he wondered if Adela Rochniewicz was already waiting for him. He had no doubt that punctuality was one of her traits. Punctuality, orderliness, common sense, he thought, taking off his jacket and casting an uncertain glance around the room. Stacks of paper were piled high on both desks. Heaps of clothing of indeterminate origin and old, sweaty shoes cluttered the closet. A potted plant was withering on the windowsill. Algae had taken up residence in the water cooler. He wiped the dust from the windowsill with his sleeve, cleared the papers from the desk that had belonged to Rogowski until now, and peered cautiously into the drawer. Inside, he found a rotting sandwich in a plastic bag and an empty vodka bottle.

Ada arrived at three minutes past eight. She gave him a brief, manly handshake. She took off her peacoat and a quirky beanie with a colorful pattern. Where does she get the cash for clothes like that, he wondered. For those little sweaters, wool shorts, and stylish boots? She must be wearing at least two police salaries. Watching the new inspector out of the corner of his eye, he thought with a pang of his own wife’s tacky, colorful clothes.

“Come on then, I’ll introduce you to the guys,” he said, forcing a smile.

He wanted to get it over with as quickly as possible. He felt awkward leading her down the corridor. He opened one door after another, repeating: "This is Inspector Adela Rochniewicz, she'll be working with us starting today." Ada greeted them briefly, politely. Her face maintained a cool, formal expression. Not once did she show any interest. She ignored the low whistles and snickers behind her back. When they walked into the largest room on the floor, several officers looked up in astonishment. They seemed taken aback. Ada fit in at the station about as well as the Queen of England in a baseball locker room.

"Quite the hothouse flower he's found himself..." muttered Kapusta, a blond officer with a pockmarked, unhealthy complexion and a flushed forehead. He said it quietly, as though talking to himself, but everyone heard him loud and clear. A muffled snicker rippled through the room. Sawicki clenched his jaw. *A hothouse flower?* Where did that idiot learn words like that? Ada's face remained impassive.

In the front office, Ms. Dagmara took some doughnuts out of the fridge and bustled over to switch on the electric kettle.

"I knew you were coming today, so I came prepared," she chattered. "You have to sit down for a minute and have a coffee with me. You really must come in here more often! Sawicki is such a grump," she added, completely ignoring Marcin. "Working with guys is so difficult in general. But you already know all about that..."

Dark mascara was smudged around her left eye. Her heavy breasts swayed beneath a pink blouse. She hadn't even managed to get the glasses out before Rogowski's round, bald head appeared in the doorway.

"We've got a homicide reported in Upper Mokotów," he said. "Sounds like some young chick got her head smashed in. The two of you are taking it. Kapusta and Chlor are interrogating the car smugglers. Wiktor and I are taking the Old Man over to Praga."

"But she just got here..." Sawicki muttered reluctantly.

"Good, she can dive right in. I don't know how they do things in Wrocław, but here we don't start the day by eating pastries."

"A girl with a smashed-in head?" he tried to object. "Can't the district cops handle it? We've got enough on our plate."

"They're already there." Rogowski let out a nasty cough. "We've got district cops from the Second and the paramedics. And Modzelewski," he added pointedly, before reaching out a plump hand, snatching a doughnut, and vanishing out the door.

Ada raised her eyebrows.

"Modzelewski is the prosecutor," Dagmara explained hurriedly. "He always calls in Metro. For every single murder. He must be on duty today."

"And we're the unlucky ones," Sawicki finished.

It was approaching nine when they left the station. The snow had stopped falling, and a pale, distant sun shone through the clouds. A layer of ice covered the inner courtyard, streaked with trickles of muddy-brown water. Slipping on the ice, they reached the Opel in silence and headed toward Mokotów. The morning rush hour had just ended. They didn't use the siren. Within fifteen minutes, they were at the scene. They parked outside a four-story prewar apartment building and walked through a narrow gate into the courtyard. A pair of ruffled crows watched them from a bare tree. Sawicki pushed open the stairwell door, which had been propped ajar by a brick. Ada felt the skin on the back of her neck prickle. A cold shiver ran down her spine. It suddenly hit her that she was taking on an investigation. She was back in the field. Two years had passed since the accident that upended her stable life in Wrocław. Two years spent on the outside, surrounded by a wall of resentment and distrust. Now she was returning to work. Once again, she could do what she was best suited for, what she was truly good at. That long-forgotten feeling of excitement returned, that moment of intense focus, and finally a sudden, invigorating surge of energy—much like what a wrestler experiences right before stepping onto the mat. Ada took a deep breath, walked past Sawicki, and led the way up the stairs.

On the landing between the second and third floors stood two identical-looking men, dressed in matching leather jackets and identical, crisply creased wool trousers. Both were smoking, bringing their cigarettes to their lips with the exact same quick, nervous gesture. Both took short drags and exhaled the smoke loudly through half-open mouths. To Ada, it looked as though someone had placed a mirror on the landing.

“These are the famous Mokotów twins,” Sawicki laughed, noticing her astonishment. “Inspector Rochniewicz,” he said, introducing Ada.

“Hi,” they said in unison.

She nodded.

“So you’re taking over the case.” The twin on the left rubbed his hands together in satisfaction.

“Modzelewski already took off. You’re supposed to drop the paperwork at the office tonight,” said the one on the right. “Paramedics were here too. Pronounced the victim dead. The morgue guys will show up in forty minutes. Or so they claim.”

“We didn't call the meat wagon,” they added in unison, and started zipping up their jackets.

Just then, the sound of sobbing reached them. A pale, disheveled girl in an unbuttoned coat sat outside an open doorway on the third floor. Right next to her, a uniformed officer stood leaning against the wall, leafing through a morning tabloid. The crying clearly made no impression on him whatsoever.

“Oh… that’s Monika Lewicka. Friend of the deceased,” the right twin explained.

“She’s the one who called in the murder,” the left twin added.

Sawicki nodded.

“Right, we’re out of here. Have fun.”

They turned as if on command and hurried down the stairs. The uniformed officer followed them.

"Hold on a minute." Ada stopped him mid-stride. "Please buy this woman some tea." She gestured with her chin toward the sobbing girl. "At the café downstairs."

The policeman stopped, taken aback.

"Do you have a problem?" Ada raised her voice.

"A problem?" His eyes bulged, and his mustache twitched nervously.

"A problem," she repeated sharply. "You’ve been standing here for an hour reading the paper. And my witness is about to pass out on me. Have you lost all basic human decency?"

The policeman turned bright red.

"Tea?" he stammered. "With my own money?"

Ada opened her wallet.

"Here." She pulled out a ten-zloty note.

He recoiled in indignation and opened his mouth to protest, but snapped it shut the moment he saw her scowl. He sighed, shuffled his feet like a scolded dog, and reluctantly took the cash.

"And one more thing!" she called after him.

"Yes?"

"Bring it back in twenty minutes, not a minute sooner. And make sure it’s hot. With sugar," she added emphatically.

He nodded obediently, checked his watch, and headed down the stairs.

Sawicki was so highly amused by the sight of the mustachioed cop beating a hasty retreat that he had to bite his lip to keep from snorting with laughter.

"Monika Lewicka?" he turned to the crying girl. "Are you the one who called the police?"

"Yes…" she replied, failing to stifle a sob. "She's inside, in the bathroom…" she added with effort, lifting her head and brushing the hair from her swollen face.

He pushed the half-open door. Just past the threshold, he nearly stepped in a puddle of vomit.

"I'm sorry," she muttered. "That was me…"

Ada took a long stride, stepping over the threshold, and went in first. Sawicki followed her. She stopped in the bathroom doorway. He looked over her shoulder, flinched, and lightly touched her back. She turned her head. Their eyes met for a moment.

A dead woman lay on her stomach on the tiled floor. Her arms and legs were splayed out limply, as if she had fallen from a great height. Blood had congealed in her long hair. Ada crouched down and leaned over the body. He didn't notice the slightest hesitation in her movements. She pulled on a pair of silicone gloves. *Does she always keep those in her jacket pocket?* the thought crossed his mind. She gently brushed aside the matted hair and felt the back of the skull. There was something alien and surreal about the sight of these two women: the dead one, and the living one—who seemed just as indifferent as the corpse lying at her feet. This wasn't the first time Sawicki had examined a crime scene. He had also grown accustomed to the sight of mangled bodies. And yet, he had expected something more from his new colleague than a stony face and a pair of white gloves. What exactly? Tears? Wringing of hands? He knew full well she was an experienced officer… She slowly straightened up.

"I think we should call the techs," she said calmly.

Ada immediately sensed Sawicki’s tension. For a brief moment, she felt something akin to sympathy for this big, strong man who was clearly apprehensive about her reaction. She didn’t want to make him uncomfortable. She pulled her gloves from her pocket and crouched over the body. It was a young woman, twenty-something years old. Slim, athletic. Ada’s eyes were drawn to a muscular calf. There was very little blood. It had trickled from a wound on her temple. The skull there was deformed and gruesomely caved in. Whoever dealt the blow had struck very hard. She gently brushed aside the tangled hair. The wide-open eyes had clouded over and were covered with a yellowish film. The lips and the sides of the nose had turned a livid, bluish-brown. She lifted the hem of the sweatshirt to check for lividity. She stood up.

“She’s been dead for at least four hours,” she stated. She tried to deftly sidestep Sawicki, but he stopped her and pointed at the floor. Right next to the toilet lay a discarded object.

“The murder weapon…”

“Yes,” he nodded. “The killer left it behind.”

For a while, they stared blankly at the bloodied hammer. The wooden handle bore a clear stamp: “AM-NAT Hardware Store.”

“That’s very strange,” she said finally.

Sawicki gave a start and pulled his cell phone from his pocket.

While he called for the forensics team, Ada looked around the large, pleasant bedroom: a clean floor, a meticulously made bed, an armchair with a folded tartan blanket resting on it. She smiled in spite of herself. She liked things tidy. Out of habit, she adjusted a black-and-white print that wasn't hanging quite flush against the wall. Light-colored drapes hung in the window. There were no sheer curtains. She moved on to the kitchen: smooth white tiles, a wide teakwood table, a sink with a pull-down faucet, a dishwasher, an electric grill… Everything exuded an air of comfortable, middle-class affluence. She had recently read about wealthy Varsovians buying apartments in neglected pre-war tenement buildings. Praga and Stary Mokotów had become fashionable. On the table sat a porcelain teapot painted with forget-me-nots, two cups, and a plate of cookies.

She returned to the hallway. Through the open doorway, she saw Sawicki leaning against the metal staircase railing, questioning Monika Lewicka.

“Police,” he announced to the curious neighbors who were beginning to gather. “Please go back to your apartments and do not interfere with the investigation. If we need your help, we’ll come to you,” he added, in a tone so sharp and decisive that the staircase emptied immediately.

She headed toward the next room.

“Marcin!” she called out suddenly. “Come here. I think you’ll want to see this.”

The small room’s only furniture consisted of a desk holding a laptop connected to a large monitor, an armchair, and an old chest of drawers. All the drawers had been pulled out. Papers, binders, and cardboard boxes lay gutted across the floor. Sawicki’s eyes widened.

"This couldn't have been a simple robbery," he said, surprised. "No signs of forced entry. The computer and tablet were left on the desk. Together they're easily worth a good few thousand."

"The other rooms are in perfect order," Ada agreed.

"Looks like someone was searching for something specific and knew where to find it... Evidently, he had his own key and surprised our victim in the bathroom."

"I don't think so. I think she let him in herself. At any rate, she was expecting someone. There are two empty cups and some cookies out in the kitchen."

The forensics team arrived a moment later. Two solemn men in protective coveralls, silicone gloves, and blue plastic shoe covers rolled out police tape and stepped into the apartment. They placed numbered markers next to the body, the discarded hammer, and the blood smears on the bathtub, then set to work securing the evidence. A camera flash went off every few seconds. Ada stood motionless in the bedroom doorway.

"You need to step outside now," said the chubby-faced tech. "We'll be done in an hour."

A door slammed downstairs. Approaching footsteps could be heard. Clambering up the stairs was a diminutive man, his white lab coat poking out from under a short puffer jacket. He gave off a strange, unsettling vibe. His head and hands were disproportionately large compared to the rest of his body; standing barely five feet tall, he hovered right on the brink of physical deformity. Ada caught a distinct whiff of formalin and stale male sweat. She instinctively recoiled in disgust. The little man peered into the apartment and, without a word to anyone, marched purposefully straight into the bathroom.

"That’s Creep from the morgue," Sawicki whispered. "Best not to bother him, he’s not exactly pleasant..."

Five minutes later, the little man reappeared on the landing.

"Someone whacked her in the head, no two ways about it," he said, darting quick, nervous glances from beneath bristling eyebrows. "No way she did that to herself," he added with a thin, unpleasant laugh. "Wasn't an accident," he went on, bending the fingers of his left hand back so hard his joints popped. "She's been dead for at least four hours. That's all I can tell you for now. I haven't got time to mess around with you people," he continued, struggling to catch his breath. "I've got three stiffs waiting in line for a dance over on Oczki Street. Call the meat wagon and get back to me on Monday."

He left without saying goodbye to anyone. An awkward silence fell. Sawicki searched for his cell phone. He finally found it in his inside jacket pocket and dialed the number for the funeral home.

Monika Lewicka was still sitting on the stairs. She had stopped sobbing, but she was deathly pale and shaking as if with a fever. Sawicki touched her shoulder.

"I'm sorry, but we have to ask you a few more questions," he said, and Ada caught a gentler tone in his voice. "The first few hours after the discovery of a murder are the most critical..."

"I understand," she nodded, and began to cry again.
