Prologue

A flash pierces my brain; a violent blow to my skull throws me off balance. I am two hundred meters above the void, climbing a rock face I know well. My nails claw at the granite, my hands lose their grip. In that instant, I realize that it is all over. Out of a love for the challenge, or perhaps out of pride, I have always climbed barehanded, unroped. I knew I would pay the price for it one day.

First, I see the sky. Then a hundred meters of rock face blur past in the blink of an eye. I can barely make out the silhouette disappearing behind the summit I was about to reach. That silhouette is familiar. It has just caused my fall into the void, and I am going to die. One thought brings me strange comfort: I am going to join my sister. She has been gone for twenty years; when I woke up this morning, I never imagined we would be meeting today.

My name is Marceau Miller, and I am a successful novelist. Tomorrow, right beneath the "Born" line on my Wikipedia page, you will be able to read: "Died: May 16, 2021 (aged 40)." Ironically, I have spent years writing as if each day could be my last. I am leaving behind the most important manuscript of my life. It is as if some part of me had sensed this moment coming, anticipating this unpredictable date with destiny. The Grim Reaper doesn't send you a registered letter to warn you of the day and hour of your death. I leave it to them—to Sarah, to the others. They are free to do with it what they can. I owed them the truth.

My body is floating in the air; I am no longer in control of anything. The ground is rushing up at a terrifying speed. My heart aches for my children, Hermione and Benjamin, and my wife, Sarah, with whom I have built everything.

I catch sight of my pickup parked at the foot of the rock face, and I realize I am going to crash right next to it. Nothing else crosses my mind. And yet, they say your whole life flashes before your eyes in a situation like this. We've all had that awful nightmare: falling into the void, with nothing left to catch you. You wake up with your body drenched in sweat and your heart ready to burst, with a lingering sense of dread that haunts you for hours.

I have always wondered whether our brains disconnect before the fatal impact. In a few hundredths of a second, I will know for sure.

Fifty meters.

Ten meters.

Darkness.


PART ONE

THE FALL

Chapter 1

Saturday, May 15, 2021

The day before Marceau Miller's death

Settled in the rocking chair on the chalet's terrace, I look up from the novel that has completely absorbed me. My tea is still steaming. This is where I love to be—facing Lake Geneva, *my* lake, as if it belonged to me. In every story my husband writes, there is no warning of the storm about to strike his characters. It is often insidious; it strikes with full force. I take a sip of my Saint James, a full-bodied black tea with chocolate notes, and set down my cup. This is his twentieth book, and he still manages to surprise me. I haven't entirely figured Marceau out, but I have to hand him one thing: his success is well-deserved. He wields suspense like no one else. Too much, even. Everywhere, all the time.

The roar of an engine pulls me from my reading completely. A boat is approaching the dock at the end of the garden. I serviced it last week; it is part of the fleet of boats I own with my business partner, Karen. This little motorboat is nothing but a patched-up nutshell we would never dare offer our clients. But our kids absolutely love it. Sometimes I take it to the agency, hugging the shoreline all the way to Yvoire. It takes fifteen minutes longer than driving, but it's just as practical as Marceau's old pickup for hauling gear.

Benjamin is at the controls of the speedboat. At ten years old, he already handles it well, but he’s approaching the dock a little too fast for my liking. Marceau is waving his arms. He must be telling him to slow down. I let out an involuntary sigh as the boat glides smoothly alongside the dock. They’ve spotted me. All three of them wave enthusiastically. I smile and wave back. Hermione, our eldest, stationed at the bow of the boat, leaps onto the landing and catches the mooring line her father tosses to her. I watch the maneuver. The dock on pilings isn't very wide; its long wooden legs plunge into the depths to take root in concrete blocks embedded in a mixture of silt and green-tinged pebbles. At this time of year, when the sun is still so hot, the wooden planks aren't slippery, so all is well. Benjamin cuts the engine and joins his father and sister.

I put my book down without even slipping a bookmark inside. I’ve memorized the page number. 40, Marceau’s age and mine. And on page 40, things are already going badly for the characters. It's only the beginning, I suspect.

I stand up and stretch. The shade of the pergola is cool on this terrace built by my cabinetmaker grandfather—his last project before passing away suddenly a decade ago. This exterior woodwork on the chalet, partially covered in vegetation, gives the place the feel of a vacation home. The garden stretches for nearly eighty meters, surrounded by woods left to grow wild and a hedge similarly left to its own devices. In fine weather, thanks to the proximity of the lake, the lawn stays green for a long time. It has been recently mowed, bordered along the shore by a fringe of gray sand and pebbles. I head toward the children. Benjamin is already starting to tell me about his adventures as he runs toward me, but he is too far away; I can't hear a thing. Hermione walks up with her father, both laden with diving gear.

"I went down five meters with Dad! I saw a *féra* this big!" 

My boy’s outstretched arms seem too short for the enormous fish he spotted in the lake. Marceau reaches me and presses a kiss to my forehead. His lips still hold the coolness of Lake Geneva. Benjamin tugs at his sleeve, wanting him to confirm his claims about discovering the shiny-scaled miracle. Hermione catches him off guard, shoving onto his head the cap he had dropped behind him. At that moment, I feel the fragility of happiness—as fleeting as a fish disappearing into the depths. It’s dizzying; I become aware of everything I hold most dear, and the pain I would feel if I lost any of them. Happiness holds out its hand to us without warning. It can snatch it back just as quickly.

"Sarah?"

Marceau smiles at me, then walks off to drop the diving gear a little further away. I catch his gaze; I feel as if I can read my own emotions in it. But he knows the value and impermanence of these moments better than anyone. He has already glued back together so many pieces of shattered joy. He makes me think of that Japanese art of repairing broken ceramics, kintsugi.

I can almost trace with my finger the golden scars his memories have left within him.

Chapter 2

Saturday, May 15, 2021

The day before Marceau Miller's death

The big hand on the living room clock has not yet reached 8 p.m. Its familiar ticking fades, drowned out by a noise from outside. The daylight is waning; I catch sight of the headlights of several vehicles filtering through the maples in the driveway. The low, characteristic purr of Alexis’s sports car engine makes the glass doors of the antique bookcase I inherited from my grandmother Louise rattle. Through the window, I soon see him step out of his speedster, wearing a broad smile. Alexis, true to form. Benjamin is already circling the gleaming vehicle. Alexis catches him and dangles him by the feet like a rag doll. I hear their bursts of laughter; I smile too. Then they take off in a frenzied race and disappear toward the back of the garden, shouting.

Marceau comes down the staircase, repainted in white, the dominant color of our interior. I love light. The large windows overlooking the lake and the reflection of the mountains allow us to never take our eyes off it. I designed the ground floor of the chalet as a loft—a modern open kitchen blending with the old, opening onto the living room and library area. A spacious living room, perhaps a bit cluttered with furniture, I’ll admit, but comfortable and cozy. Marceau joins me near the sofa. He takes me in his arms; I know he loves my smile. He returns it, never taking his eyes off me.

"Are you ready?"

"Never really for these parties, you know that. I'd rather slip under the covers with you."

He melts my heart, the idiot. This phase is my favorite: between the moment he finishes a novel and the moment he disappears into a new one. That's when I get him back, when we are closest.

"Then why are you so keen on organizing this ritual every year for the release of your novels?"

"A ritual, Sarah, is a writer’s key. A writer without a ritual is a dead writer."

"Have you become superstitious? Seriously, what are you afraid of? That it'll all stop? That you won't please your readers anymore?"

"Second thing, pretty lady: a writer who isn't afraid is a finished writer. You must always write with passion overriding fear."

"Beautiful line, you should save it for your next interview... Oh, speaking of which, here is your publisher. Early, as always. He loves this party. And especially my fresh goat cheese empanadas. Soon we won't even be able to see his belt."

Édouard Payet, with his good-natured stoutness, lets his gaze wander over the facade of the chalet, as if hesitating to come in. I know he is inspecting the whole house. The woodwork, the windows, the roof's support beams, the small upstairs balcony, the terrace, the casually tended landscaping. He has always loved this place, the lair of his star author. He knocks on the door; we go to welcome him. In the publisher's wake, Freud, his Welsh Corgi, waddles into the small hall, which I had cleared for the occasion of the clutter Benjamin always leaves lying around. The little dog's claws skid on the shiny hardwood floor. Short-legged and rather plump, barking constantly, he resembles his master. Both are eyeing the spread in plain sight, further in on the kitchen island. I must be dreaming: Édouard already has a handful of peanuts in his hand. And here I was wondering where I'd put the small bowl. He clearly found it. In one motion, he brings them to his mouth and swallows them unchewed, like pills. An ogre, but with a jovial smile and a disarming gift of the gab.

"Sarah, Marceau! I look forward to this day with delight every year. Nothing changes around here! Nor should it, that's the secret of my star writer."

His hearty embrace is as manly as ever, and I can feel the grains of peanut salt left on my shoulder by his fingers—Freud didn't have time to lick them off. Édouard suddenly leans toward Marceau with a knowing look.

"Did you get my little gift?"

I look at Marceau, who seems embarrassed. I have no idea what he's talking about.

"Listen, Édouard, it's magnificent, but... I don't really have the wrist for a watch, and... I'd hate to ruin it."

"A Breitling Aviator—that was made for you! You fly your old Savage Bobber almost every week. Marceau, live a little! You're a workhorse who doesn't treat himself nearly enough. With the money you make, you can afford it!"

"The lake house and my plane are enough. You know me—I don't want to be caught off guard if one day it all comes to a halt, just like that, all at once, without warning."

"I could publish you with my eyes closed, Marceau."

"Never do that."

"Don't worry, I'm not senile yet. And if the manuscript wasn't up to par, Marceau, rest easy—I have some excellent ghostwriters."

Marceau's face changes imperceptibly. He has just slipped into his own world.

"An accident with a text... with me, that would be a fatal crash, Édouard."

"Oh, come on, I'm just teasing you. I'm always eager to see what you're going to bring me, you know that."

I offer the platter of empanadas to Édouard, while Marceau quietly slips away. Freud raises his snout, waiting for crumbs, but Benjamin comes running up and distracts him from his goal. My son pets the dog on the head and leads him away, calling to him happily.

Édouard polishes off his last bite with undisguised satisfaction, before making his way to the terrace with an almost ceremonious stride. The publisher’s ritual can begin—the one he always performs far from my living room, where I have little patience for those heavy plumes of smoke. From the inside pocket of his tweed jacket, he reverently extracts a Cohiba Behike 56, the Holy Grail of aficionados, housed in its Spanish cedar case.

His movements are precise, almost liturgical. First, he takes out his Xikar pocket hygrometer to verify the ideal humidity of the wrapper leaf. Then, with expert fingers, he rolls the cigar beneath his nostrils, inhaling the scents of leather and precious wood. The guilloché silver cigar cutter—a gift from Marceau—makes a clean cut, releasing the first earthy notes. Édouard carefully examines the cut end, like an oenologist scrutinizing the hue of a grand cru.

His ST Dupont lighter produces its characteristic blue flame. The toasting ritual begins: the flame delicately licks the foot of the cigar, preparing the tobacco to be lit. Édouard then slowly rotates the Cohiba above the flame in a meticulously calibrated choreography. The first wisps of smoke rise, bearing the promise of complex aromas to come—notes of roasted coffee, bitter cocoa, and oriental spices.

A smile of contentment spreads across the publisher's lips as he takes his first puffs, eyes half-closed, savoring the moment as one might taste an instant of eternity. The coming success of Marceau’s new novel is already mingling with the ribbons of smoke rising into the evening air, dancing above the calm waters of the lake.

I turn up the volume on the stereo; we too rarely use the full power of these machines. The sound fills the house and carries all the way to the lake. Some evenings, I like to break the silence, to let the chalet radiate with joy and noise. I catch sight of Karen dancing on the terrace with her daughter, Zoé, and mine. I hadn’t seen them arrive. Karen is wearing a burnt-orange jumpsuit with butterfly sleeves, loose and belted high at the waist. A lightweight fabric with a plunging neckline that flatters her slender figure. All of this paired with ankle-strap wedge heels that elevate her to nearly six feet. At that moment, I regret not choosing my linen pants that hug my curves. Standing on tiptoe, I kiss Karen and give her a wink as I lightly touch her outfit; she smiles. She is swept away again by the girls just as I ask her where Rollin, her husband, is. Between dance steps, she points toward the yard. Further away, on the lawn, Alexis is dragging along Marceau and Rollin, his best buddies of twenty years, ready to make sparks fly tonight. Alexis loosens them up; glass in hand, they launch into a series of suggestive gyrations. Rollin, massive and muscular, is as clumsy as ever, but he keeps up. He is a contemplative sort, warm by nature, the gentle dreamer of the group, and unbeatable when asked about the flora and fauna on our outings. Nonconformist to his fingertips, he has never given up his freedom and scrapes by with his food truck. From the porch, Édouard exhales thick swirls of smoke after taking a drag from his glowing cigar. His face is radiant, his eyes fixed on Marceau. The truth is, in moments like this, I’m always afraid that something will happen and shatter it all—just like in my husband’s novels. There are false alarms, and there are real tragedies.

10:00 p.m. The automatic sprinklers kick on. I had forgotten about them; my heart leaps. I catch a few drops; cries of amusement and surprise ring out. Alexis holds Rollin under the artificial rain, while Marceau runs with long strides toward the shed to shut off the system. And while the three friends play to the crowd, my son tugs at my sleeve and pulls me under the fine, refreshing droplets on this warm evening that feels deceptively like summer. I sober up slightly, my heart is drumming, but I breathe—and I let go.

The magic ends as suddenly as it began. The sprinkler system is shut off; the boys are soaked. Alexis strips off his clothes and tries to coax Rollin toward the lake. The latter resists the tipsy mischief-maker and lets him wander off. He’s already had enough, drenched from head to toe. Alexis, mocking, almost naked in the middle of the vast garden as night falls, caught in the beam of the chalet’s lights, shouts one last call to anyone listening. Rollin shakes his head as Alexis sprints across the lawn and disappears with a dive into the lake, now turned to night. Several seconds pass, and he bursts from the depths with a loud splash, smirking. The lapping of the water around him makes the surface shimmer, where points of light from the lamps dance across the immensity of the lake. In the distance, the Swiss shores take shape like an illuminated serpent. The water temperature is breathtaking, I know. Even under the influence of several drinks, you can’t escape its cold bite. The most important thing is that it doesn’t take the lives of those I love; it already spat me back out once, just before I drowned. I owe it to the lake that I am an experienced swimmer, with even a few medals to my name. It’s the lake and me, like the forests and mountains around us—always has been.

Rollin’s clothes cling to his skin, revealing his stocky shoulders.

“I think I need a towel now.”

“Idiot, that won’t be enough,” I tell him, laughing. “You know the house, run to the bathroom and grab some clothes from Marceau’s closet. It’ll just be a bit of a tight fit.”

Karen bursts out laughing with me as Rollin goes into the house. We’ve known each other a long time, having met through our husbands. Our friendship and mutual trust allowed us to consider partnering up to start this boat rental business, which is doing quite well these days. A few meters from the shore, Alexis whistles and tosses his soaking wet boxers toward the garden. Freud doesn’t need to be told twice; he catches the sodden fabric in midair and brings it back to his master. Alexis moves toward the bank and wades backward out of the water, turning his pale bare bottom toward us. Karen is the quickest to react:

“Alexis, the kids! You’re going too far!”

Alexis keeps up his grandstanding, making his way to the dock and using it as a launchpad to dive back into the lake with exaggerated clumsiness. Benjamin starts to undress as well, but I stop him dead. Disappointed, he retreats to the shore and cheers on Alexis, who tries to persuade me from a distance. Nice try, boys.

Rollin reappears a little later, dry and wearing an ill-fitting outfit of Marceau’s. I think he looks a bit off—is he tired? His smile is gone as he strides over, carrying a large towel to rescue his naked buddy, who has gotten out of the water and is starting to shiver. Alexis’s anatomy might not warrant something so large, but he’ll be much more civilized draping himself in it for the rest of the evening.

They step away a few paces together toward the back of the garden, taking shelter beneath the foliage at the edge of the woods, not far from the moored boat that is still bobbing from Alexis’s antics. They seem deep in discussion. I hope Alexis isn't dragging Rollin into another one of his harebrained schemes. The special thing about these evenings is that they seem to plunge us twenty years back in time, into the carefree spirit we had then. Marceau comes out of the house in his turn, looking serious, as if he were already miles away—a troubled expression I've seen on him from time to time. Why tonight? Will I ever get an answer to this mystery? The rare times I’ve tried to find out more about these feelings passing through him, I have found myself standing as if on the edge of a bottomless precipice. That is the limit of our marriage. An unspoken rule, a safe distance—his safeguard.

It is past midnight; the swimming episode has changed the mood. The buffet is holding its own against the final assaults. Freud’s barking catches our attention. His hackles are raised, and he is facing the small patch of woods adjoining the garden. Édouard whistles for him, to no effect. He finally goes over to bring him back. The area around the chalet is teeming with nocturnal animals. The children sometimes startle them when they venture into the woods.

Édouard returns. Cradled in his master’s arms, tongue hanging out, Freud seems to have already forgotten everything. Édouard thanks me for the evening and reminds Marceau of his schedule for the next seventy-two hours, notably his TV appearance on the major literary talk show and his studio recording for a radio spot. His publicist will send him his updated itinerary for the bookstore signings. What I’ve learned over the years is that this job as a writer is really nothing but a form of parole. I share my husband with Édouard, the journalists, the readers... But also with many fictional characters. If the latter could sometimes fade into the background and leave me a bit more of Marceau, that would suit me better. Closing his laptop just long enough for the machine to digest his current manuscript is not enough for me. But I am not the one who decides, and I’m not sure he truly decides it himself, either.

At 1:45 a.m., everyone has gone home. Hermione and Benjamin are finally in their room, dead to the world. I knock discreetly on the door of Marceau’s office, his sanctuary. I hear his muffled voice just behind it.

"Just five more minutes, I’m finishing a message. I’ll be right there."

In my head, I translate that to a half hour, maybe an hour. I duck into the bathroom and swallow a painkiller. Between the fatigue and the alcohol, a headache is bound to hit soon. I’m exhausted but satisfied; the evening was a success. As I slip under the sheets, exhaustion and that slight floating sensation from too much alcohol wash over me. I hate the idea of falling asleep without the warmth of my man beside me, so I fight it. I need him. I keep fighting it, but my brain finally shuts down.


Chapter 3

Sunday, May 16, 2021

The day of Marceau Miller’s death

A blurry glow. 5:10 on the alarm clock display. Marceau isn’t beside me. Damn writer. I frown, but my eyes fall shut again. I drift back to sleep.

8:15. A slight headache. I can tell it’s a beautiful day through the shutters. The harsh light assaults my eyes. Beside me, the covers haven’t even been pulled back. Did he sleep in his office or what? I tiptoe down the hall, careful not to wake Hermione and Benjamin. What mother doesn’t protect her children’s sleep? Marceau isn’t in his office. I don’t go inside; I practically never set foot in there anyway—he doesn’t like it.

My memory struggles through the morning fog; I search my mind, but nothing comes back to me. He should be here, with me. The living room is a mess. I pick up a few remnants of last night’s party here and there, and scan the yard: no one. I step outside, barefoot on the patio, facing the lake. It is perfectly still, undisturbed by the slightest breeze. Our little speedboat is tied to the dock, solitary. The air is mild, almost as warm as inside the cabin. In the distance, over by the hangar, I can’t make out our old pickup truck. I could have sworn it was parked there yesterday. I walk around the side of the cabin, through the silence of the fresh lawn that absorbs my footsteps. I feel the cold grass tickling the soles of my feet. I approach the hangar, left wide open, as usual. The Savage Bobber is there, with its gleaming propeller and broad wings. I would have heard it taking off from the meadow next door anyway. Still no sign of the pickup. I turn around and glance toward the woods and the driveway leading to the road in front of our house. My voice still faint, I call out for Marceau, but only a nearby bird answers, making its presence known with a flap of its wings before taking to the air above the cabin. For a moment, I envy its position high above the world, its vantage point over the lake and the mountains. Can it see Marceau from up there?

Back inside the house, I open the entryway closets. I find our running shoes, Marceau’s lined up next to mine, and more importantly, I check his climbing bag: empty. His climbing shoes and chalk bag are gone. I feel a jolt of panic, and then I remember: he got rid of them the day he promised me he’d give up free soloing—chasing the thrill of climbing with no ropes, no safety gear... and, if you ask me, no common sense. Jackets and coats are hanging on the pegs or hung up in the closet. If he hasn’t taken anything with him, he must be coming right back. I send him a text, reminding him while I'm at it that we need to coordinate our schedules for the coming days, between his book promo events and, on my end, the boat rentals that are non-stop this time of year. Not to mention the kids can't exactly fend for themselves yet.

Noon. I am getting annoyed peeling the potatoes for the fries Benjamin is clamoring for. And for at least the tenth time, I tell the kids that no, I don't know where their father is. In the land of promises, Marceau is the first to desert. Hermione was counting on him to install her smart lighting system, Benjamin for the complex assembly of his new remote-controlled plane. I advise them to negotiate some form of compensation. On my phone, no new messages. I call for the third time, still no ring: "Hello, you have reached Marceau Miller..." I hang up. My tone changes with the fifth text.

Where the hell are you?


Chapter 4

Monday, May 17, 2021

The day after the death of Marceau Miller

8:00.

I call out to Benjamin one last time. Otherwise, it'll be no breakfast before school. Hermione is on edge. Simply asking her to clear the table is enough to send her storming off, muttering curses and slamming her bedroom door. A normal Monday morning—well, almost normal. Usually, Marceau is the one who annoys Hermione before I do.

Last night, I fed them my first lie: Dad is out of town for the launch of his new novel; I had forgotten, but he'd mentioned it to me. I still haven't started making the rounds of phone calls to friends and acquaintances; I am clinging, without really believing it, to the idea that Marceau will show up with some explanation I hadn't thought of, to the idea that everything will go back to normal.

9:15.

A bitter taste in my throat—it's the stress, my stomach can't handle it. In the upper bathroom cabinet, I search desperately for medication. I don't find anything; half of it is expired, I'll need to sort through it. It will be fine. As soon as I hear from him, everything will go back to normal. Jittery, I get my things together, trying not to forget anything for a workday that is already off to a bad start.


Running late to the agency, my mind isn't on the usual emergencies. Karen is already handling the first clients. Probably because of my dazed expression, she asks me if everything is all right, and what I did with the rest of the weekend after the party on Saturday night. And finally, I crack. I confess to her that I haven't heard from Marceau since the end of that very party. Does she have any idea where he might be? I read the bewilderment in her eyes. I must look like an idiot. She hesitates, searching for a reassuring explanation. Maybe it has something to do with his book release? That doesn't make sense; he would have left me a note, even if he just wanted some time alone, as he sometimes does. I pace around the agency as if naively looking for him here, too. Karen pours me a tea and checks the list of our available boats while I count them one by one, moored at the agency dock. On rare occasions, Marceau has been known to borrow one—just like Alexis or Rollin, for that matter. Karen calls Rollin, while I try my luck with Alexis. My heart races as every handhold I cling to gives way. They don't know anything. I move on to his publisher. Édouard can think of nothing better to do than remind me of the urgent TV appearance and radio interviews scheduled for the next few days. Dead end after dead end, even after contacting acquaintances of acquaintances. My knuckles turn white from gripping my phone so tightly. An alarm goes off in my mind. I read the same, barely veiled anxiety in Karen's eyes. She assures me she can run the agency alone and practically pushes me out the door; I have better things to do, I need to find Marceau.

I’ve always been able to count on Karen. We understand each other perfectly—laughing at the same things, steering clear of unwarranted judgments, accepting our flaws. True partners, with boundless trust. So much so that it’s almost dizzying: she could take everything from me if she wanted to; I’d keep my eyes shut. But truly: teaming up with her to start the agency on the lake was one of my best decisions. The choice to be independent, too, and true to my values. Finding my place, on the lake, surrounded by forests and mountains—my domain, my roots.

But Marceau is my roots, too. Leaving without warning, without answering his phone, especially right when his new book is launching—it’s just not normal. It’s as if I’m being asked to walk in the dark, with nothing to hold on to. My heart leaps at every text message and every call I get—more and more of them now—from our friends and family, who are just as worried as I am, asking for updates, sometimes making suggestions... And no doubt drawing their own conclusions, albeit in silence.

3:20 p.m.

I’ve just returned from a long drive along the shores of the lake, from Yvoire to Meillerie, right up to the Swiss border. I stopped everywhere—restaurants, sports clubs, all of Marceau’s regular haunts, some of them highly secluded, where he goes when he wants to write far away from it all. I need to act. I have to act. I need help. I park in front of the gendarmerie in Thonon-les-Bains, the closest and largest station.

I have a knot in my stomach as I walk in: what on earth am I doing here? This is what it’s come to, with my anxiety as my only baggage. They are my last resort. A young gendarme listens to my distress and takes down everything I tell him. He tries to be reassuring, convincing me that there is no cause for alarm at the moment. He starts asking me questions about our marriage, about arguments, any infidelities there might have been... My expression hardens. I remind him that my husband is missing, and that I’m here precisely because I have no explanation. He asks me to calm down. Did I raise my voice?

Eventually, a certain Captain Robin Delmas introduces himself. An arrogant guy who talks down to me. In his office, where he receives me, I have to go over everything all over again, step by step, re-explaining, yet again—the evening, the alcohol, yes, no other substances, of course not! I’m struggling to control the volume of my voice. On the verge of losing it, I trip over my words. Who does he think we are? Illicit substances are common among celebrities, he insists. Now I’m genuinely furious. No, Marceau doesn’t touch coke. Our first and last joints date back to high school. I can’t stand his condescending stare. Marceau didn’t leave a note? Nothing missing from the house? No. No warning signs, no unusual changes in his habits? I’ve already gone over all these questions in my head. I snap yet another curt “No,” short of breath. He left in the pickup, and that’s it.

Delmas concludes that since Marceau has been missing for less than forty-eight hours, he can’t launch a search for a forty-year-old adult in full possession of his mental faculties, with no notable conflicts or cause for concern. And there it is, that’s that. He stares at me, with an almost satisfied smile to boot. I feel the urge to hit him rising inside me. Clearly, I’m the only one who understands that nothing about this situation is normal. I get the feeling that no matter what I say, he won’t listen to a word. Like facing a brick wall, my anger bounces right back at me. I am escorted out; I’m to keep him posted. Case closed.

I walk out feeling even worse than when I arrived, struggling to breathe, overwhelmed by a sense of emptiness and abandonment. Misunderstood, scorned, but no less determined.
