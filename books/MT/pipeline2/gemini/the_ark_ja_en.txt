1 The Ark

I

Leaving the walking trail near the highway, the seven of us pushed our way into a mixed forest. Stepping over rotting logs and fallen leaves as we made our way along the mountain path, we passed through an unfamiliar field overgrown with dry grass.

By the time we reached an old wooden bridge spanning a ravine about ten meters deep, the sun had already vanished behind the mountains.

Ryuhei shook the railing of lashed logs with his thick arms.

Hearing the bridge creak and groan, he grimaced, contorting his chiseled, wrestler-like face. Then he said to Yuya beside him,

"Hey, are we seriously crossing this? You didn't say anything about this. Isn't this thing gonna collapse?"

"Nah, it's totally fine, I swear. I crossed it before and it wasn't that dangerous. We're totally good. Look."

Yuya stepped out onto the bridge, spread his arms, and rocked his body back and forth to prove his point.

There was no other way, anyway. The six of us had no choice but to follow Yuya as he led the way.

Once we made it across the bridge, I pulled my phone from my windbreaker pocket and checked the time. —4:48 p.m.

Seeing the phone in my hand, Hana, who was wearing bright fluorescent hiking gear, quickened her pace and stepped up beside me. She held up her own phone in her right hand and asked,

"Shuichi, do you have a signal?"

"No, I haven't had any service for about an hour."

"Oh. Same here. So, I guess this means we're not making it back to the villa today, right?"

No one replied. We all knew that was impossible.

Beyond the bridge lay a desolate field surrounded by steep mountains. After we had taken a few hundred steps through it, Yuya shouted out.

"Here we go! I see it. Just a little further. We're almost there."

A sense of relief bled into Yuya's voice—he had been enduring the discontented and distrustful glares we'd been shooting at his back. Even so, there was still no sign of anything resembling a building entrance.

This all started that morning. After we'd had our fun taking boats out on the lake, Yuya had brought this up.

"There's this really cool place within walking distance of here. You guys want to check it out? It's a massive underground structure deep in the mountains. Looks like it used to be used for some sketchy stuff back in the day, but I bet nobody even knows it exists anymore."

Since yesterday, we had all been gathered at a villa in Nagano Prefecture owned by Yuya's father.

Yuya was a friend from college, and he was the one who had suggested we get together. It was a little reunion for the six of us who used to hang out back in our university days.

It had been two years since we last saw each other. I still couldn't quite get used to the fact that Yuya, who used to have blond hair and black earrings, had stopped dyeing his hair and was now just wearing the earrings.

For reasons of my own, I had brought my older cousin along, so there were seven of us staying at the villa.

An underground structure deep in the mountains. Even hearing that, none of us really knew what to make of it.

An underground structure—an incredibly elaborate undertaking, and apparently a colossal one at that. Who in the world would build such a massive thing deep in the mountains, and why? Hard as it was to believe, Yuya claimed to have seen it for himself about six months ago.

Intrigued, we figured that if it wasn’t too far, we might as well go check it out.

Except it didn’t quite match his story. We walked and walked, but the underground structure was nowhere to be found. Yuya, who had breezily claimed it would be a casual twenty- or thirty-minute hike, spent the entire time staring anxiously at the map on his phone.

Naturally, an underground structure wasn't going to be on any map. Yuya claimed he’d pinned the location on his map app during his previous visit, but his pin turned out to be wildly inaccurate. By the time we finally got our bearings after wandering lost, the sun was already beginning to set.

"So, Yuya? This underground structure of yours? You expect us to spend the night there, right? Since there's no time to head back. Are you sure about this? Didn't you say this place was seriously sketchy?"

"No, look, I just said it *might* have been used for some sketchy stuff in the past. But that was a long time ago. Crashing there for a bit is totally fine. There's no way anyone's actually there. It’s basically just exploring abandoned ruins."

Ryuhei and Yuya marched on ahead, leaving the rest of us some ten meters behind.

It had been like this the entire way here. Acting as the self-appointed spokesman for the group, Ryuhei had been complaining non-stop to our guide, Yuya.

Observing Ryuhei’s behavior, Mai, who was walking to my right, shot me a troubled, almost placating smile. In the gathering dusk, her long-lashed eyes stood out all the more distinctly against her pale face.

I wanted to respond, but the thought that Ryuhei would undoubtedly hear me kept my mouth shut. Mai didn't seem to expect an answer anyway; she quickly looked away, as if afraid Ryuhei might catch her looking.

I glanced back to see Sayaka, who had fallen a bit behind, jogging to catch up with us. Her warm-brown hair was tied up in a bun, and a sheen of sweat covered her forehead.

Sayaka finally voiced what had clearly been on her mind for a while.

"Um, how do you think the toilet situation works in this underground structure? And as for sleeping... are we just going to be lying on the floor using our backpacks for pillows? Is everyone really okay with that?"

Yuya hadn't exactly given us the sort of inn-style briefing you'd expect. We had never intended to stay the night in the first place.

My cousin, Shotaro, who was walking a step ahead of me, answered her.

"I wouldn't get your hopes up," he said. "It’s probably a building with some history. Still, it beats sleeping out in the open. Being underground, at least it shouldn't be too cold."

"Oh, I see. You're right. It does get pretty chilly at night, doesn't it?"

Sayaka made a show of politely agreeing with Shotaro. 

I had only just introduced my cousin to my college friends yesterday. Shotaro was fitting in with the group far better than I had expected.

Ever since he inherited a considerable fortune from my aunt five years ago, Shotaro hasn’t held a steady job. Instead, he spends his days drifting about—traveling and immersing himself in geological research. You might think he plans to just eat through his inheritance and live a life of leisure, but that doesn’t seem to be the case either. Sometimes he takes a million yen or so, goes abroad, and comes back having multiplied it several times over.

Being my older cousin, I’ve known him longer than any of my friends. Even though I felt more at ease around him than anyone else, he was still someone whose true depths I couldn't fully fathom.

I had brought Shotaro along because I sensed a hint of trouble brewing around this gathering. He mentioned he was already interested in the local geography, which made it easy to invite him.

So far, the kind of trouble I’d been worried about hadn’t materialized. Instead, we had unexpectedly ended up heading into a mysterious underground structure. Still, it was reassuring to have him there, as he seemed like the type who could handle things if anything went wrong.

II

In the middle of a wasteland surrounded by steep, rugged mountains, Yuya suddenly stopped. He pointed at the ground and shouted.

"Found it! Look, right here. The entrance."

Yuya crouched down, plunged his hands into the dry grass, and hoisted up a manhole-like cover about eighty centimeters in diameter.

I peered inside. The shaft dropped straight down into the earth. The sides were lined with concrete, with iron rungs embedded into the wall to form a ladder.

"So, this is where we go in—"

"Wait, seriously? That's terrifying. It's way too narrow."

Hana shone her phone's flashlight down into the hole. The beam wasn't strong enough to reveal the bottom.

I silently agreed. Given that we were deep in the mountains, it made sense that the place would look like a coal mine, but from the way Yuya had talked about it, I had been picturing a somewhat more civilized structure.

"I mean, yeah, the entrance looks like this, but it's totally fine once you're inside. It's seriously huge in there. Three whole floors underground. We'll manage for just one night."

Hana and the others were clearly hesitating, and I wasn't too keen on the idea either.

Ryuhei was the first to make a move.

"Whatever. I'll go. Can I fit through like this?"

Mindful of his backpack scraping against the walls, Ryuhei climbed down the ladder into the earth. Yuya gauged the three girls' reactions before following after him, not wanting to let Ryuhei take the lead on his own.

Hana, Sayaka, and Mai exchanged hurried whispers—"What should we do?" "Who's going first?"—before taking turns climbing down the shaft. Shotaro and I brought up the rear.

After descending seven or eight meters down the ladder, my feet touched the ground.

From there, a cave-like tunnel stretched out horizontally. It was reasonably spacious, allowing us to walk without having to crouch.

The path sloped gently downward. We made our way forward, holding up our phones for light.

A short distance ahead, a massive boulder sat right in the middle of the passage.

It was far too large to ever be moved by human strength. And for some inexplicable reason, it was tightly wrapped in thick chains.

"What is this? You think someone tried to dig it out and just gave up?"

"Hard to say," Shotaro said cryptically.

As we slipped past the massive boulder, an iron door came into view. 

Just before the iron door, the natural rock beneath our feet gave way to a dirty plank floor. From here on out, it was clearly a man-made structure.

Yuya pulled the door open and shone his light inside.

"Whoa. It's real. This is pretty wild."

Ryuhei let out a sound caught somewhere between awe and apprehension.

Beyond the door stretched a wide corridor with a low ceiling. It curved a short distance ahead, cutting off our line of sight, but the echo from the iron door opening told us that this underground structure was quite extensive.

"Wow. ...It smells like mold."

Mai murmured.

A sour, stale odor hung thick in the air. The damp air—reminiscent of the depths of a sunless forest—was tinged with a faintly chemical stench.

"What's the deal with the lights in here? There's no power, right?"

"Nope, but there's a massive generator. Looked like it might still work, too. If it doesn't, we might just have to make do with our phone flashlights. I've got a power bank on me, anyway."

Ryuhei and Yuya slipped past the iron door and stepped into the pitch-black hallway. 

Everyone held a light in their hand, forming a procession like firefly larvae, and followed them fearfully.

The floor was made of cheap, old vinyl material. On the walls to the left and right were a number of doors, like in a hotel.

Just before the hallway took a sharp left, Yuya pointed to a door on the right. A plaque reading 107 was attached to it.

"Generator's in here. Doesn't look broken, but..."

Yuya twisted the knob and directed his light into the room.

It appeared to be some sort of mechanical room. Black cables snaked along the walls, all converging at the back where they connected to the generator.

It was a private power generator about the size of a bathtub, much like one I'd seen at a hospital where I used to work part-time. An exhaust pipe ran up the wall and out through the ceiling. The machine itself looked like it had been built before I was born, but the several LP gas cylinders set up for fuel looked fairly new.

A glance at the gauge showed there was still gas left in the tanks. Yuya and Ryuhei ran their hands over the machine, trying to figure out how to start it.

Seeing that they didn't know how to operate it, Shotaro spoke up reservedly.

"First, make sure the hose is properly connected to the cylinder. Once that's done, you should just need to flip the engine switch and pull the starter cord."

When Yuya did as he was told, the sound of an engine echoed like a motorcycle's.

The next moment, the fluorescent lights on the ceiling flickered. Then, from the mechanical room to the corridor, a pale blue light filled the underground structure.

"Thank goodness. Going without lights was pretty rough, wasn't it?"

Sayaka said, looking around at everyone's faces.

With a shared sense of relief, we left the cramped machine room.

This underground building held countless mysteries. I was tempted to explore the now-lit interior, but at the moment, fatigue won out over curiosity.

Yuya led the way, taking us a little ways back down the hallway toward the entrance, and opened door 106, opposite the machine room.

"So, this is the cafeteria. You guys wanna take a quick break?"

It was an enormous room. Long tables were set up in the elongated room, lined closely with chairs along them. It was a dining hall large enough to fit dozens of people.

Hana pulled out the nearest chair.

"Ugh, gross. ...Is this even safe?"

The chairs were cheap, the kind you might find in a school. What's more, having been left underground for so long, the backrest was growing black mold and starting to rot.

Hana dusted off the seat, then gingerly sat down. It didn't break.

Upon closer inspection, the tops of the long tables were also cheap, made of plywood veneer. They were similarly deteriorated; standing on them would likely be dangerous.

There was a sink at the back of the cafeteria. When I turned the faucet, it gurgled, and then reddish-black water sputtered out. I left it running for a while, and the water eventually ran clear.

"Huh, the water works."

I muttered in surprise.

Above the sink was a cupboard, lined with a massive array of thick, old-looking plates and cups.

Sayaka was kneeling by the wall, fiddling with something.

"Oh, wow. The outlets work too. Look."

She had plugged her smartphone charger into an outlet with exposed wiring.

We lingered in the cafeteria for a while, stretching our limbs and yawning.

We barely spoke. It was a time devoted entirely to shedding our fatigue, much like when arriving at a mountain lodge to stay the night during a long traverse. Since we'd gathered yesterday, this was the moment that made me feel the most nostalgic for our college days.

However, this rest area was steeped in an atmosphere far too unsettling for us to simply stretch out without a care. After a while, Shotaro tapped me on the shoulder.

"Shuichi, shall we explore the building a little? It's a pretty fascinating piece of architecture."

I had just been thinking about getting something to eat, but the underground building intrigued me too.

Just then, Yuya, who had been looking somewhat awkward, cut in.

"Oh, Sho-san, in that case, I could show you around a bit? I did a lot of looking around last time I was here."

With him tagging along, we left the cafeteria. The three of us began our exploration of the underground structure.

Between the low ceiling, the dim fluorescent lights, the dirty floors, the walls made of cheap, drab materials, and the wiring strung everywhere, the building as a whole had the feel of an old cargo ship.

It wasn’t just the atmosphere; the scale and layout were also closer to those of a cargo ship. The building was constructed of steel plates welded to an intersecting framework of steel beams. Long and narrow, it spanned three levels. Lining both sides of the corridor were rooms that looked like storage spaces, and rooms furnished with simple tubular steel bunk beds.

As in an apartment building, a room number plaque was affixed to each door. Returning to the entrance and standing with my back to the steel door, the room on the right was Room 101, and the one on the left was Room 102. Moving further down the hall, the numbers progressed to 103, 104, and so on. Every room—whether a storage area or living quarters—had been assigned a number, regardless of its purpose. Every door left an uneven gap between itself and the wall. The overall construction was decidedly crude.

Room 104, next to the dining hall, served as the restroom. It was a restroom with four individual stalls, much like what you would find in a public facility. Though I had no intention of using it, a shower stall was also attached, for what it was worth.

A stale odor hung in the air, but it wasn't strong enough to be unpleasant. It had been out of use for so long that any waste left behind had evidently already decomposed.

"There's no sewer system down here, is there? How did they pump out the waste?"

"They probably let it collect in a holding tank first, then pumped it up to the surface. Graywater from the sinks and showers would have worked the same way."

Shotaro answered my question while peering down into one of the squat toilets.

Having seen enough of the restroom, we stepped back out into the hallway.

Past the mechanical room at 107, the hallway took a left turn. Right at the corner was a steel staircase leading down to the second basement level.

We ignored the stairs for the time being and pressed further down the hall. Then, it turned right after about five meters. There too, doors lined both sides of the passageway. Numbered from 108—picking up where the mechanical room left off—they continued down to 120 at the far end.

Along the outer wall, patches of dark, exposed bedrock were visible. The texture was identical to the cave we had passed through when descending from the entrance. Water seemed to be seeping through in places, leaving the stone damp.

Apparently, this underground structure had been built by reshaping a natural subterranean cavern, dividing it into levels, and erecting partition walls to turn it into a functional building. The unnatural bends in the corridor were likely a result of conforming to the original topography.

Upon reaching the dead end, Shotaro spoke with the sense of satisfaction of having finished viewing a museum exhibit.

"Twenty rooms in all? It must have cost a fortune. Well, they built it well. Though it's extremely illegal."

"This isn't all of it, you know. There's still the second basement level."

Yuya took the lead, heading back down the corridor toward the stairs.

The second basement level shared roughly the same layout as the floor above. Doors stretched endlessly down both sides of a lightning-bolt-shaped corridor. While it lacked a large space like the dining hall, the holding tank was situated directly beneath the first-level restrooms, occupying the space of a single room. All the same, Yuya explained, it still contained twenty rooms, numbered 201 through 220.

The fluorescent lights were on in the second basement corridor as well. However, the area to the left of the stairs, where the lower room numbers were, had gone dark. The light fixtures were in place, so perhaps a wire had been cut somewhere? Since there was a light on at the very back, it was possible that area was on a different circuit.

At the bottom of the stairs, before we began exploring the rooms, Shotaro asked a question.

"Yuya, remind me... how did you manage to find a place like this?"

"Ah, well, I just happened to stumble across it about six months ago. I was planning to do some solo camping at the time, and I wanted to go somewhere absolutely nobody else would be. So I came deep into the mountains, and that's when I found that trapdoor. And then, when I went inside, it was just... incredible."

Standing in the middle of the corridor, Yuya spread his arms wide.

"What even is this place, anyway? Who built this, and why? Honestly, you gotta admit it definitely feels like it was used for something sketchy."

Shotaro thought for a moment before replying.

"It's probably an extremist hideout from fifty years ago."

"—Seriously? Extremists? Like, we're talking the seventies?"

"Judging by a quick glance, that's about the era it was built. You saw that massive boulder in the entryway, didn't you? The one wrapped in chains? Any way you look at it, that was deliberately placed there to serve as a barricade in an emergency. They must have intended to use that rock to block the iron door.

"That said, it looks like some other criminal group or something moved in after the extremists were gone. There's also wiring that looks to have been installed relatively recently—twenty years ago at most. I doubt those extremists were still holding out down here that recently."

I'd had a sneaking suspicion that might be the case, too. 

There could only be one reason to build a structure this deep in the mountains—and underground, no less: to stay hidden from prying eyes. Hearing it said out loud, however, only made it that much more unsettling.

Shotaro said cheerfully,

"Well, let's take a closer look and see what sort of place this is, shall we?"

We stepped into Room 208, right in front of us. It appeared to be some sort of junk room. 

We rummaged through the supplies. We turned up an endless array of junk: used work gloves, rusted weeding sickles, old speakers, copper piping, and wood scraps. Some of it looked quite old, while other items didn't. It was nothing but the sort of trash you'd see piled up at a curbside garbage dump.

"Oh? I guess even crime syndicate hideouts have straw hats."

Shotaro said with a straight face, showing me a battered, wide-brimmed straw hat.

"Yeah. I was kind of expecting we'd stumble across a pistol or some white powder, but it doesn't really have that vibe, does it?"

"They naturally would have taken all of that with them when they cleared out. Assuming the guys who used this place even handled that sort of thing, of course. Still, if we search every nook and cranny, we might just turn up something."

Shotaro carelessly tossed the straw hat onto a battered wooden crate.

This time, we opened the door to Room 209, located diagonally across the hall.

At first glance, this room, too, appeared to be nothing more than a dumping ground for junk. There was less of it than in the previous room, but what looked like scrap had been swept into piles in the corners.

But when we flipped the light switch, we saw that the objects here were nothing like the mundane, harmless items we'd found in Room 208. When I'd heard a criminal organization had used this place, I'd pictured murder weapons or drugs. What we actually found, however, was far more sinister.

The first thing to catch my eye was a set of restraints—handcuffs and leg shackles attached to long chains. Sitting at the very back of the room was a pitch-black iron chair with an oddly pointed seat.

And then there was a thick wooden club wrapped in leather, alongside a contraption I couldn't even begin to guess how to use—a metal frame large enough to fit a human head, fitted with a vise-like mechanism. There were rusted nails and concrete blocks, too.

Yuya, Shotaro, and I exchanged looks, feeling the awkwardness of having peeked at someone else's secret.

Yuya walked over to the corner, crouched down, and without laying a finger on the tools, let out a gasp.

"You've gotta be kidding me. Holy shit. These are torture devices, right?"

"Sure looks like it."

Shotaro answered. It seemed Yuya hadn't checked this room during his previous visit.

"Do you think they were actually used?"

"Man, I don't know, but doesn't it look like it? I've only ever seen stuff like this in museums. Hard to believe they're even real."

The torture devices were old and rusted. It wasn't as if there were traces of dried blood left on them, but they were far too worn to just be tasteless decorations. I scanned the floor around us. Deep, torn-like scratches remained in the vinyl flooring. It looked as if someone had clawed at the floor while writhing in agony.

I had heard stories of radical extremist groups in the seventies whose internal disputes escalated into killings. If the history of this underground facility was what we suspected, finding torture devices here wasn't entirely surprising.

A sudden, bloody eeriness seemed to emanate from the cold, inorganic underground structure.

"...But it's not like there's any proof they were actually used, right?"

"No. Besides, even if they were, that was a long time ago. They're just historical relics now."

Shotaro's words seemed to put Yuya slightly at ease.

I took comfort in his words as well, deciding not to dwell on whatever might have taken place here in the past. Naturally, torture devices had never featured in my life up to this point, and no matter how things played out, I was absolutely certain they never would.

After that, we peeked into a few of the neighboring rooms, but we didn't find anything more alarming than the torture devices.

"By the way, Yuya, didn't you say this place has a third basement level?"

Shotaro asked. In the hallway, there were no stairs to be found leading to a third basement level.

"Uh, well, the stairs down are at the very end, but we can't actually go down there 'cause of, well, a few things. But I guess we can go take a look. You'll get it as soon as you see it."

Yuya took the lead to show us the way.

We headed down the corridor toward the lower room numbers. The lights along the hallway were out. It wasn't so dark that we couldn't walk, but we turned on our phone flashlights just in case.

At the end of the hall stood a steel door. It looked similar to the entrance on the first basement level, only smaller and narrower. 

Yuya pointed at it and said, 

"Look, the entrance we came through is probably right above us."

Tracing the route we had taken to get here, I figured he was probably right.

Yuya slowly opened the steel door.

The space inside was completely different from the other rooms. Just past the entrance, it narrowed like the neck of a bottle, and beyond that, bare black rock was exposed everywhere you looked. Only the ceiling near the entrance was boarded up with wood, making it exceptionally low. The rest was bare rock; this room alone looked just like a natural cave.

And mounted to the wall at the back of the room was a winch-like mechanism that looked like something salvaged from a sunken ship.

"What is this? It's completely rusted out."

A thick chain was wound around the winch. Following the length of the chain, it branched off, ran through a pulley, and disappeared up through the wooden boards of the ceiling near the door, leading toward the first basement level.

"Ah! Wait, could this be the chain that was wrapped around that giant boulder?"

"Exactly. I told you that rock was a barricade, didn't I?"

The mechanism was designed so that turning the winch would pull the giant boulder into place, blocking the steel door on the first basement level.

"Oh, right. Now that you mention it, this is definitely a barricade setup. I didn't really think about it that much. So that's why only the ceiling near the entrance is boarded up. To let the chain pass through, huh?"

"Yes, pretty much. Though there may be more to it than that," 

Shotaro said meaningfully.

"Right, anyway, the stairs down to the third basement level are in here. But going down is a no-go. Look."

Yuya pointed toward the back right corner of the room.

A square hole had been cut into the floor. The stairs were right there.

I walked over and peered down, and I understood what Yuya meant at a glance.

The third basement level was completely flooded. The water covered the steps all the way up to the fourth one down, coming right up to the ceiling of the third basement level. I crouched down and stretched my arm out as far as I could, my fingertips just brushing the glossy, pitch-black surface of the water.

"Freezing! You've gotta be kidding me. It's flooded?"

"It is underground, after all. And since this is amateur construction, a little flooding is to be expected. Surrounded by natural rock like this, it only makes sense. The drainage system must be broken, too. This might be exactly why the underground structure was abandoned."

Indeed, I'd seen water trickling down the outer wall of the first basement level earlier.

"This definitely isn't the time to be making jokes about a 'mansion with a pool.' Actually, it's a little scary. I mean, doesn't this guarantee the whole building is going to end up completely submerged eventually? If it keeps going like this."

Yuya replied. 

"Well, theoretically, yeah, but it'll probably take a while. It doesn't seem like the water level has changed much since I was here six months ago. It might have gone up a little, but still. Even if it does flood, that won't be for, what, another five years?"

He had a point.

Shining my phone's flashlight into the water, I could just make out what looked like concrete-encrusted rebar and steel beams haphazardly abandoned down on basement level three.

With nothing else to see, we stepped back out into the hallway and headed back the way we had come.

As we neared the stairs, I caught sight of Sayaka from behind on the far side of the hallway, her phone held up. She was apparently snapping photos out of sheer curiosity.

"Yuya, is the hole we all came through the only entrance to this underground complex? I find it hard to believe there's only one."

"Nah, there is technically one more, but it's useless. See, it's down on basement level three. There's a narrow passage—kind of like a garbage chute—that runs from there all the way up to the surface, but it's completely submerged, so there's no way through."

"I see."

"Oh, yeah, there's some kind of floor plan in the machine room. It'd be quicker to just show you that."

The three of us headed back to the machine room.

Yuya opened a drawer in the machine room's desk. It was jammed with a random assortment of junk: old Band-Aids, nail clippers, and stationery like pencils and ballpoint pens.

He started scooping the contents out onto the desktop. Eventually, mixed in with the clutter, he found a floor plan drawn on a large A2 sheet of paper folded into quarters.

"Ah, here we go. I guess I shoved it way in the back the last time I looked at it."

It was a bit too sparse to be called a proper floor plan, but it was a blueprint that clearly showed the layout of the entire building. It appeared to date back to the facility's construction, the paper heavily yellowed with age.

At the top of the blueprint, the word "Ark" was written in ballpoint pen, as if added as an afterthought. It seemed to be the name of this underground complex.

Just as we had observed, the "Ark" was a long, narrow three-story structure that bent in the middle in a reverse Z-shape. According to the blueprint, basement level three—unlike levels one and two—wasn't partitioned into smaller sections; it seemed to consist only of a few large rooms. The entrance we had used was located on the west end, and there appeared to be another exit leading to the surface on the east end of basement level three.

"This one looks like it's probably some kind of emergency exit. It connects to the surface through a trapdoor just like the one we came through. Actually, we passed right by the trapdoor for the emergency exit on our way here. I doubt anyone noticed it, though."

Apparently, there had been another trapdoor just after we crossed the bridge. Neither I nor Shotaro had paid it any mind, of course. It was already starting to get dark when we passed by there, so it was only natural that we hadn't noticed.

"Did you take a look inside the emergency exit, Yuya? Is it exactly like this blueprint?"

"Uh, yeah. I went down through the emergency exit just to check. I climbed down some ladder thing and made it right to the ceiling of Basement Level 3, but the whole third floor was flooded. So I just came straight back up."

"Speaking of emergency exits..."

Something had been bothering me for a while now. 

"What are these supposed to be?" 

I pointed at the desk.

Sitting there were two LCD monitors. They were old, fifteen-inch screens, the kind you used to see in elementary school libraries. Scrawled across their respective bezels in permanent marker were the very words we had just been discussing: "Entrance" and "Emergency Exit."

"Oh! I was wondering about those when I was here before, too. They look like security camera monitors, don't they? See how they say 'Entrance' and 'Emergency Exit'? When I checked outside in the daylight, I saw what looked like cameras near the hatches. They were mounted up in the trees, though. There's one at the west entrance and another at the east emergency exit.

"So I bet these show the camera feeds. But we didn't have power before, so I couldn't check."

Rattling this off, Yuya switched on the two monitors one after the other.

"Oh, looks like they're alive? —Whoa, sweet, there's a picture."

With a low mechanical buzz, the old monitors flickered to life, displaying the security camera feeds.

Since night had completely fallen, the footage was murky, like a woodblock print. The cameras seemed to be just as old as the monitors, so the picture wasn't very clear.

Still, you could tell they were showing the desolate field above ground. Bathed in moonlight, both screens showed the faint outlines of the hatches roughly in the center of the frame. It was set up so that if anyone approached the main entrance or the emergency exit, you would know immediately.

Yuya traced the hatches on the two screens with his finger and said,

"Yeah, exactly. This one's the entrance we came through. And this one's the emergency exit near the bridge."

"So that means these two are about a hundred meters apart, then."

"Ah, yeah. About that far. East and west. You think it was so that if outsiders found one, they could escape out the other?"

Yuya offered this reply to Shotaro.

It must have taken quite a bit of effort to install those cameras. Astonished, I said,

"Heavy security. Seriously, what kind of people were using this place?"

"Maybe it was some cult doing special training. The placement of the cameras is strange if they were just preparing for outside intruders. They look more like they were meant to keep an eye out for escapees,"

Shotaro replied.

It was a fairly convincing theory. I took another long look at the word "Ark" written at the top of the yellowing blueprint.

"Do you think they took the name from Noah's Ark in the Old Testament?"

"Well, that's the only origin I can think of."

I thought back to my university days, to the time I’d idly flipped through a Bible during a cultural anthropology lecture. The universally known story of Noah’s Ark was located right near the very beginning of the thick Old Testament.

When the world fell into chaos and the earth was filled with violence, Noah, a righteous man, received a revelation. God, it said, had decided to destroy mankind. He commanded Noah to build an ark and prepare for a flood. Once the ark was completed, and Noah, his family, and a male and female of every living creature were safely inside, the deluge struck the earth.—That was how the story went. The original text is rather dry when read as a narrative, but in the sermons, novels, and films inspired by it, you often see depictions of people mocking Noah and his family for building an ark on a mountaintop, refusing to believe a flood was actually coming.

A ship-like structure built deep in the mountains. The name “The Ark” made perfect sense, even if it had been attached after the fact. To the people of whatever radical faction or new religious cult had built it, perhaps this place had been meant as a sanctuary to await salvation.

To me, it felt like a bad joke. There was no salvation to be found in this unsettling underground structure. About the only things we had discovered were torture devices.

Among our group, there was no one particularly devout, nor anyone harboring extreme political beliefs. It seemed our party was less like Noah’s family, and more like the crowds who had mocked the builders of this place.

“Oh? What are you guys doing?”

Hearing the commotion we were making in the machine room, Sayaka peeked in through the open door.

Right on her heels came Hana, who had apparently been looking for her.

“Oh, there you are, Sayaka. Were you taking pictures or something?”

“Ah, yeah. I figured we’ll probably never come to a place like this again, so I wanted something to remember it by.”

It seemed she had been wandering around, poking her head into the various rooms and snapping photos.

“I mean, it’s fine, but you probably shouldn’t post them online or anything. It could be a real pain if the people who used this place came across them.”

“Ah, good point. I won’t. I’ll just keep them to myself.”

As Sayaka and Hana were talking, Mai and Ryuhei arrived together.

It seemed the other four had also been curious about the interior and had gone off exploring on their own. Now, all seven of us were gathered together in the machine room.

They all looked surprised to see the monitors turned on.

Yuya repeated the exact same explanation he had just given Shotaro and me—about the flooding on the third level, the emergency exit, and the security cameras.

“Right. I mean, I get it, but…”

Hana cut Yuya off.

“Actually, I kind of want to step outside. My boyfriend messaged me earlier today, and if I don’t reply by tonight, he might show up at my place while I’m not there.”

As she spoke, Hana rubbed the smartphone in her hand.

Yuya scratched his head.

“I don’t know… You’re probably going to have a hard time getting a signal out here.”

“Yeah, I know. I just want to test it. If it doesn’t work, I’ll give up. Would somebody come with me?”

Out here in the pitch-black mountains, anyone would be scared to go out alone.

"Want me to come with you, then?"

But Hana looked reluctant to go outside with just Yuya. 

Sensing this, Sayaka quickly came to her rescue. 

"Maybe I'll tag along too. I probably have some work messages to check anyway. Mind if I join?"

"Oh, you'll come too, Sayaka? Thanks. Yeah, let's do that."

And so it was settled. Hana, Sayaka, and Yuya would head outside for a bit.

After the three left, Ryuhei stared intently at the dark monitor over my shoulder.

"Is that actually showing anything?"

From a distance, the feed looked like a blank screen caused by a bad connection. 

But before I could answer, there was movement on the screen.

"Oh, it's Hana and the others."

The security camera by the entrance showed the three of them—who had gone out to search for a signal—pushing up the trapdoor and climbing out.

The lead figure waved at the camera. That was Yuya, followed by Hana in her flashy jacket. Behind her was Sayaka. Naturally, it was too dark to make out their faces.

A little while later, the camera by the emergency exit showed the three of them walking along by the light of their smartphones. They seemed to be crossing the wooden bridge toward higher ground to see if they could get a signal.

"Huh. So it is working."

Ryuhei muttered, seemingly convinced.

After that, he and Mai began idly toying with the items Yuya had tossed on the desk. Eventually growing bored, Ryuhei grabbed Mai's hand, and the two of them left the machine room.

Shotaro and I stayed behind in the machine room, staring absently at the monitors. 

With nothing better to do, I asked my cousin a pointless question.

"I wonder if everyone has eaten yet?"

"Who knows."

Everyone had been so caught up in exploring the underground structure that they might not have eaten yet.

A little over thirty minutes later, Yuya's group returned. Thanks to the security cameras, we knew they were back right away.

But something about their group was off.

"Wait... aren't there more of them? —No, how is that possible?"

The silhouettes on the entrance monitor had gone from three to six.

It was like something out of a horror movie. Shotaro and I left the machine room and went to the iron door to meet the group, who had apparently run into some kind of trouble.

We opened the iron door, and Yuya was the first to come in. Followed by Hana. Her jacket was muddy—it looked like she'd taken a fall.

Then Sayaka.

And behind her, looking terrified, was a family of three. 

The father looked to be in his fifties, sporting a salt-and-pepper crew cut and thick black-rimmed glasses. The mother was slightly plump with short hair. The son, who had rather full lips, looked to be about middle-school age.

The moment Sayaka saw us, she immediately started explaining.

"Um, these people here said they got lost... so I brought them along. I told them we at least had a roof over our heads here. Uh, you were mushroom hunting, right?"

The father replied.

"Yes, something like that. Sorry for the trouble."

Mushroom hunting—it was the season for it, certainly, but even so, they had ventured awfully deep into the mountains. 

Not that this place was our home, exactly, but in any case, we ended up inviting them into the dining hall.
