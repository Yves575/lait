I

He has set down his weapon and struggles to pull off his heavy boots, their smell (feces, stale sweat) adding further to his exhaustion. His fingers on the frayed laces are dry twigs, slightly scorched in places; his fingernails are the color of his boots, he will have to scrape them with the tip of his knife to get the grime out, mud, dried blood, but later, he lacks the strength for it now; two toes, flesh and earth, poke out of the sock, they are fat stained worms crawling out of a dark trunk, gnarled at the ankle.

He suddenly wonders, as he does every morning, every evening, why these boots reek of shit, it’s inexplicable,

no matter how much you rinse them in the puddles you pass, rub them against crisp tufts of grass, it's no use,

and yet there aren't that many dogs or wild animals, not that many, in these rocky heights dusted with holm oaks, pines, and thornbushes where the rain leaves a fine pale mud and a scent of flint, not shit, and it would be easy for him to believe that the whole country reeks, from the sea, across the hills of orange and then olive trees to the very depths of the mountains, of these mountains, or even himself, his own smell, not that of the shoes, but he cannot bring himself to accept this and tosses the boots against the edge of the ravine that conceals him from the path, a little higher up the slope.

He lies on his back right on the gravel, sighs, the sky is purplish, the glow of the setting sun lights up fast-moving clouds from below, a canvas, a screen for a fireworks display. Spring is almost here and with it come the often-torrential rains that turn the mountains into bullet-riddled barrels, disgorging a powerful spring from every little hollow, when the air smells of thyme and fruit blossoms, white flakes scattered between the low stone walls by the violence of the downpour. It would be the devil's own luck if it started raining now. Then again, it would wash the boots. The heavy boots, the fatigues, the socks, both pairs he owns being equally rigid, stiff as cardboard, falling apart. Betrayal begins with the body,

how long since you last washed?

Four days you've been walking along the ridges to avoid the villages,

the last water you splashed on yourself smelled of gasoline and left your skin greasy,

you are a long way from purity, alone under the sky gazing at comets.

Hunger forces him to sit up and joylessly swallow three army biscuits, his last ones, hard brown slabs, no doubt a mixture of sawdust and glue from an old mare; for a moment he curses the war and the soldiers,

you are still one of them, you still carry weapons, ammunition, and memories of war,

you could hide the rifle and cartridges in a corner and become a beggar, leave the knife behind too, beggars don’t carry daggers,

leave the shit-smelling boots and go barefoot,

the misery-colored jacket and go bare-chested,

his meal finished he drinks the dregs of his canteen and plays at pissing as far as he can down toward the valley.

He lies down again, this time right up against the rock face, the bottom of his pack under his head; he is invisible in the shadows, never mind the critters (red spiders, tiny scorpions, centipedes with teeth as sharp as remorse) that will scamper over his chest, slide across his nearly shaved skull, and wander through a beard as rough as a bramble. The rifle against him, the stock under his shoulder, the barrel pointing toward his feet. Wrapped in the piece of tarred canvas that serves as his blanket and his roof.

The mountain rustles; a little wind crests the peaks, descends into the combe and vibrates among the shrubs; the cries of the stars are chilling. There are no more clouds, it will not rain tonight.

Holy angel my guardian, protector of my soul and my body, forgive me all the sins committed this day and deliver me from the works of the enemy, despite the warmth of the prayer the night remains a wild beast fed on anguish, a beast with a breath of blood, ruined cities roamed by mothers brandishing the mutilated corpses of their children facing disheveled hyenas who will torture them, then leave them naked, defiled, their nipples bitten off before the eyes of their brothers, raped in turn with truncheons, terror spread across the land, the plague, the hate and the night, this night that always envelops you to drive you toward cowardice and betrayal. Toward flight and desertion. How long will he have to walk? The border is a few days from here, beyond the mountains that will soon become red-earthed hills, planted with olive trees. It will be hard to hide. Many villages, towns, peasants, soldiers,

you know the region,

you are at home here,

no one will help a deserter,

tomorrow you will reach the house in the mountain,

the cabin, the hovel, you will take refuge there for a while,

the cabin will protect you with its childhood,

there you will be caressed by memories,

sometimes sleep comes by surprise like a sniper’s bullet.

II

More than twenty years ago, on September 11, 2001, near Potsdam on the Havel, aboard that cruise ship, a small river cruiser baptized with the beautifully pompous name of Beethoven, summer seemed to falter.

The willows were still green, the days still warm, but an icy mist rose from the river before dawn and immense clouds glided over us, from the distant Baltic Sea.

Our floating hotel had left Köpenick in east Berlin very early in the morning on Monday the 10th. Maja was still alert, sprightly. She went up to the upper deck to walk, a stroll between the showers, the deckchairs, and the deck games. The green domes and the golden spire of the Berlin Cathedral captivated her, from afar, as we passed by. She imagined, she said, all those little golden angels leaving their stone prison to fly away in a cloud of acanthus leaves blown by the sun.

The water of the Spree was sometimes a dark, matte blue, sometimes a glowing green. Over the previous weeks, all of Germany had been shaken by storms whose spasms swelled the Havel and the Spree, which were usually rather low at this time of late summer.

We sailed amid the eddies.

I remember the confluence of the Spree, the wooded islets, the salt light that dusted the tall black poplars, and the muddy flow of the canal that the ship’s wake mixed with the polished waters of the river.

Maja and I were each in a canvas chair, in the sun on the deck, at the back, at the stern as one should say, and we watched everything slip away: the landscape widened as if the ship’s bow were opening wide the green matter of the foliage.

We were celebrating, a few months late, the tenth anniversary of Paul's refounding of the Institute, while paying tribute to the founder himself. Or, more precisely, we were celebrating the tenth anniversary of the "unification" of the Institute in the spring of 1991, and the fortieth anniversary of its creation in 1961. But above all, it was a celebration of Paul's work. I don't think anyone was missing—among the old guard, those from the East, all were there; the new members, the colleagues from Berlin and elsewhere had almost all shown up. A few, including Linden Pawley, Robert Kant, and some French researchers, had even come from abroad. This floating conference was titled the Paul Heudeber Days; two sessions a day were scheduled: number theory, algebraic topology, and a history of mathematics session in which I was to take part.

The only one absent was Paul himself.

Maja had just celebrated her eighty-third birthday.

Maja drank liters of tea.

Maja was cheerful and sad and silent and talkative.

We all knew she had no business being there, aboard the Beethoven for a mathematics conference; we all knew she was indispensable there.

Paul

Most train travelers prefer to sit facing the direction of travel.

A historian is a traveler who chooses not to sit facing the direction of travel.

The historian of science is a historian who, sitting facing backward, turned to the rear and unlike most historians, does not look out the window.

The historian of mathematics is a historian of science who, sitting facing backward, eyes closed, tries to prove that the Arabs invented trains.

Nobody laughed.

It must be said that I was the only historian at the symposium. All the others were mathematicians, physicists, or, worse still, logicians. Each and every one of them seated facing forward. Looking toward innovation, invention, discovery. I was the only one whose interest lay not so much in the glorious proofs and inventions of tomorrow as in the gentle meanderings of the past. Meanderings of the past that cast their light into the deepest reaches of the future, and I could sense, during this session of the Paul Heudeber Days on the Havel, that this audience of scholars would listen to my presentation on Nasir al-Din al-Tusi and irrational numbers with only a dutiful respect, full of deference toward me and toward my mother, who, despite her great age, would not miss a single word of the talks, between strolls on the deck.

Maja was behind the idea of this river symposium; I seem to recall that Jürgen Thiele, the secretary general, had proposed “an afternoon excursion on the Spree or the Havel” to conclude the Days, which were initially supposed to take place at the Institute in Berlin; she had grimaced, the Spree or the Havel, that was still Berlin at best, Brandenburg at worst, why not the Danube, and Jürgen Thiele’s eyes had gone wide, the Danube, but that is very far, and I imagine Maja had started to laugh, all right, the Havel it is, but at least let the whole symposium be on a boat, and Jürgen Thiele was highly embarrassed (he explained this to me later) because he didn’t want to refuse my mother anything for these commemorative days, but his budget was limited—this whole business of a river symposium continued to strike him as absurd, an old person’s whim.

Thiele was nonetheless surprised to receive two letters on the same day, a few weeks before the publication of the call for papers for the Days: one letter informing him that the mathematics faculty at the University of Potsdam was offering to co-organize the Paul Heudeber Days with our Institute, and another stating that the Georg Cantor Foundation was granting (without Thiele having requested a thing) an enormous subsidy for the symposium, which made it possible (though still just as absurd, he thought without saying a word) to organize it on the water.

Paul’s tragic death a few years earlier had stirred profound emotion within the scientific community; everyone was eager to participate, and even if most of the organizers (Jürgen Thiele foremost among them, I thought) did not know the reasons behind Maja’s wish, no one wanted to disappoint her. These two letters arrived at just the right moment, and Jürgen could only suspect, no doubt correctly, that Maja had picked up her pen or picked up the phone: although theoretically retired from politics since the 1998 federal elections, she still had the power to draw “benevolent attention” to nascent projects. The money from the Georg Cantor Foundation was highly welcome; Jürgen Thiele got in touch, as co-organizer, with the University of Potsdam, which was celebrating its tenth anniversary, and which Paul had helped found: many of the mathematics faculty had been his students.

The Paul Heudeber Days would therefore take place on the Havel, aboard a river cruise ship capable of accommodating the fifty or so attendees in its conference room, with the participants who were not from Berlin mostly housed in a hotel across from Peacock Island, and thus technically located in Wannsee—a hotel bearing the name of a medieval or Alpine inn, The White Owl, an inn which Maja assured me (I wondered where she could possibly have gotten such certainty) had existed since at least the sixteenth century, but whose current building—Doric columns supporting a monumental balcony, windows with green shutters, climbing roses, as in a fairy tale, to soften the facade with their countless blossoms of a very dark red, verging on black—had been rebuilt by Karl Schinkel during the first third of the nineteenth century. The White Owl was tucked away deep in the forest, on the shores of the vast lake traversed by the Havel. Only the Key Speakers and other VIPs of the symposium were lodged on the Beethoven, as there were only a few cabins; on the other hand, the daytime “sailings” were open to everyone: Potsdam-Elbe on Wednesday, the day of tribute proper, centered around Paul’s work, then Peacock Island-Köpenick via Spandau on Thursday to close the festivities. Only a few prestigious guests had arrived on Sunday to take advantage of the boat’s “positioning” from Köpenick to Wannsee, and thus of an extra day cruising through Berlin on Monday.

Jürgen Thiele was full of empathy, disorganization, and goodwill. Jürgen Thiele, although still secretary general of the Institute, now only shouldered this task out of loyalty to Paul, whose student he had been thirty years prior; he readily admitted that he was tired of organizing, of setting things up, of keeping order—putting together a Christmas lunch throws me into a panic, he would confess. So a symposium for fifty people, just imagine! The University of Potsdam had assigned him a co-organizer, a young doctoral student in number theory named Alma Sejdić, who was seeking to prove a corollary of Paul’s first conjecture in her thesis. This addition proved as disastrous as it was hilarious: instead of adding together, these two forces seemed either to combine to no avail, or to cancel each other out. Oversights were forgotten twice over, blunders doubly repeated. It was like a drawing made by two ballpoint pens tied together with a rubber band, parallel lines never meeting, despite all their efforts, constrained by Euclid himself.

Jürgen Thiele had had to summon all his diplomacy so as not to offend the University of Potsdam, which failed to understand why it was necessary to fund, mere kilometers from its own campus, the chartering of a luxury cruise ship—but Jürgen Thiele had pulled the Georg Cantor Foundation grant out of his sleeve, and everyone had found the idea of a floating conference thrilling.

And so, after a few months of this ballet amid the chaos, Maja and I boarded, as planned, on Monday, September 10, at Köpenick, in the company of Linden Pawley, whose flight from New York had landed at Tegel that very morning, the inevitable Robert Kant from Cambridge, and Jürgen Thiele—there were indeed five luxury cabins prepared for us.

III

Every morning since he set out, the cold wakes him shortly before dawn. He shivers. No sudden movements, lest the dew—black pearls on the canvas—trickle down. Patiently, folding his tent to form a trough, he manages to fill his canteen with a few centiliters and drink this dawn sweat, icy cold, which will be his only meal of the morning.

He sets off, once his reluctant feet are wrapped in that knitted sponge of a wretched green, still damp, in the direction of destiny, toward the north, for the debacle and oblivion must be given a name. He hesitates once again to abandon the rifle, it is heavy and its sling is awkward, too short since he cut it to make himself a belt, with that knife still so sharp, it too the mark of a dangerous solitude, drunk on blood, he thinks no longer, he is already walking while the first rays of the sun probe the shadows of the rocks. These needles of light animate the sparrows, the warblers, the tits, whose wingbeats follow the train of the morning song.

If he thinks so much about the birds, if their presence and their song make him so tense, it is because they sharpen his hunger – it would be so easy to lie in wait, nose to the wind, with the rifle, to wait for one of these little birds to give itself away, to shoot it down and then eat it, but the power of the weapon of war would leave nothing but feathers, the gunshot would echo far into the heights and even if a fat pheasant or a partridge were to stray into his sights, he would have to cook it, and he has no intention of interrupting his march for long, of exposing himself with fire or smoke.

He has resolved to reach the house.

You would find it even on a moonless night,

the cabin,

the path advances into the day among the holm oaks, scattered by the drought; a few mastic bushes take shelter among the rocks, releasing as the walker passes their apothecary scent, of a forgotten pharmacy; his eyes search for the fresh, wild savory that spring multiplies in the mountain, to chew at length on a sprig of it, bitter, acidic, peppery – arbutus berries still survive the winter like forgotten Christmas decorations, red and rough, they taste like overripe strawberries, the blandness of oblivion.

These fruits are tiny stars, planets within reach,

little moons reddened by desire and the devil,

with every step, the sun ignites the petals of the dogwood flowers, their vivid yellow unmuted by any leaf; on their still-bare branches opens, by magic, the first crack in winter.

He walks as the last man, in the haunting rustle of the mountain.

He envies the black specks of airplanes or distant birds of prey.

Numbing himself with memory, his ass on a stone – one of those blue-gray outcropping rocks, which heat up so quickly in the sun and smell of metal and flint, as smooth as they are hard: was there a primal shudder, a hoarse wind, a prelude to the logic of brutality, a bellowing prior to the sovereign rut of war, it seems to him not,

it was surprise that sat you there,

soon the black snakes will emerge from their holes and the males will set out in search of females,

he unlaces his heavy boots, unbuckles the straps and pulls them off. The leather is devoured by wear, water, and cold. The smell of shit has not left him. His hands are rough; his white palm is starred with darker calluses, stiffened from squeezing wooden handles too tightly. His tobacco-stained fingers end in yellowed nails with meandering lines of dark grime, the tracing of veins is visible, at the thumb and along the wrist; his cheeks are harsh with an unruly beard, his hair is greasy and matted in clumps, clotted into duller strands by dried blood,

you will reach the house before dark,

the house, the cabin, the shack – it lies very deep in his memories and his hopes. Cairn of the land of childhood. High enough up the mountain that no one would venture there. Hidden enough from the mountain world for him to seclude himself. For a time. The roof is partly collapsed perhaps, the cypress beams, round, still gleaming, will be alone, bare of tiles, between the uneven stones. The door so low. The front porch, its wooden braces recalling the arms of the Father, its two roughly hewn stone posts the temple columns of a brutal God. The unplastered rubble facade. The roof of old yellow clay tiles,

you will be able to carve faces in the beams with the knife like before,

you are frighteningly hungry,

you are hungry down to the roots of your hair,

imagining the little hearth on the cabin porch and a fowl sizzling there on the embers twists him in furious pain,

you are thirsty,

he empties his metal canteen. The beautiful March sun takes on an orange hue. A wind blows from the sea,

you move forward,

he must move forward, even staggering a little, clumsy from dizziness. He lets thoughts slip away as soon as they are born. He drives them out through his feet, pushes them away as he walks. He transmits his thoughts to his boots, which scatter them among the stones. Then inner silence, until the return of the great fixed star of hunger.

The treachery of illusion, the scent of approaching spring.

The sea, which hems its purplish plains with white.

So high up the mountain the sea is nothing but a menacing line, a horizon of sorrow.

His feverishness distances it: the more he walks, the further the house recedes.

You're crunching too loudly,

you will have to watch out for the scree slope overhanging the cabin,

observe, lying in the setting sun, unknown movements—abandoned dogs turned feral by the war, deserters, villagers, distant cousins, all of them, far from their relics, on the path to the hermitage, to escape suffering, to put an end to the long Lent of blood,

spring suddenly takes his breath away. A spring of beating wings, of rock flowers, of thorny shrubs, of white and blue rosemary, of buzzing elytra—the path he was following has dipped a few dozen meters toward the sea; he peels off his clothes, stiff with grime, stained with grease and dried blood, and finds himself bare-chested, alternately licked by the breeze and blinded by the sun's power, feeling its burn on his shoulders, on the long scar barring his back, before the fabric of his pack covers it. Tired of the overly short sling, he cradles his weapon in his arms like a hunter, his left hand wrapped around the foregrip, his right around the grip the way one clasps a bird's neck, with force and nonchalance; the bolt is open, he sees the brass of a casing gleaming in the magazine, and once again he feels the urge to rid himself of the wretched object,

it is heavier in your arms than a child,

you should abandon it, hide it there in a thicket, a few hours' walk from the cabin,

he works the well-greased bolt, impossible to rid himself of it,

destiny before you and all these things, the remnants, the traces, and the great mourning of the future,

you will be what the Lord wills,

strength or forgiveness, nothing, like that yellow arachnid under your boot, crushed despite its deadly power, crushed despite its stinger, all that we do not know of ourselves, we buckle under the world of yesterday, we buckle under our faults, we buckle under the prospect of tomorrow, our Father give us this day our daily oblivion, in the steps too numerous that sand down our souls, meter after meter, path after path, trail after trail, this sudden emotion comes from the proximity – a day’s walk away – of the village down below, halfway down the slope, where the orange trees gradually invade the plains, where the olive trees make themselves discreet on the terraces with low stone walls, between the houses with gentle arches, with pointed vaults among the loquat trees so green, which light up with orange fruit in June, among the noble fig trees bent by the years whose figs rustle with insects in autumn, just as the vine arbor shaded the terrace in front of the father’s house, where they pressed a wine that quickly stings the tongue, purple, cloudy and intoxicating – the green demijohns, trimmed with straw, accumulated in the darkest, coolest storerooms, until they were cleaned in September to welcome the new harvest, and the red and black clouds of tannin clinging beneath their glass shoulders were scrubbed away with a metal wire brush,

you’ll have to hide, they are surely looking for you,

you must cross paths with no one, hide from men and beasts, from shepherds, from dogs, swallow your own name,

the closer your steps bring you to the cabin, to the house in the mountains, the more the danger grows, in the village everyone knows, no doubt, the rumors swell like the war itself, everyone knows, or thinks they know,

the afternoon swells like thirst and reddens like hunger.

He stops in the shade of a holm oak. He sits on a root. The sun drenches the valley before him. He dreams of rain. He shakes his canteen over his tongue one more time. He loosens his heavy boots, hesitates to take them off, he is so tired that he won’t put them back on if he removes them. The smell seems to have disappeared for a moment but returns, stronger still, without warning,

you stink of blood and shit,

you stink of sleep and hunger,

a child could kill you with a punch,

he counts the days since he left the city. Since fleeing the barracks. Four days since he ran the vehicle into the ravine,

you have covered nearly a hundred kilometers on foot through the mountains,

the holm oak root is hard beneath your ass,

your bent knees ache,

he leans against the black trunk, stretches out his legs, eyes on the valley (almond trees, hazelnut trees, prickly pears) which he knows so well. He plowed these terraces, weeded the bases of the trees, removed the countless stones. The sun he knows. The fringe of the sea beyond the hills he knows. The fear he carries with him.

At the next switchback in the trail, once he has passed the stream’s old retention basin, now dried up, he will be a two-hour walk from the house. He will reach it nearly an hour before sunset,

you know where you are going to hole up,

behind the large rock and check without being seen that no one is hanging around the cabin. Behind the rock and observe. Observe the last insects in the dusk. Listen to the birds and the stones in the dusk.

He takes out his knife. The blade is as gray as it is blue. He dreams of a hare, springing from a hollow, suddenly within dagger's reach. He carves a cross into the tree root. A thin, short cross. A sign. He would have been capable of drinking that hare’s warm blood had it appeared,

you are as feverish as these surroundings in your memory,

for hours his eyes have been searching for an orange tree, or even a lemon tree with a few forgotten fruits still adorning its branches. Opposite the cabin stands an immense lemon tree planted by his grandfather that bears (or rather bore, it has been a long time since he last saw it) dozens of juicy, yellow, thick-skinned fruits, which leave a smell of linens and flowers on the hands, a scent of purity, purity pleases the Lord,

there is also an orange tree, they used to weave its blossoms into wreaths for weddings,

you are the least pure of creatures,

he finds the strength to set off again, his knees aching, his thighs hard as stone, his feet raw; the further the war recedes, the more his body falls apart, an old mechanism kept running by habit. He is almost incapable of climbing the few kilometers that still separate him from the cabin, from the house, from the violet mists and the cloud hollows. It is his rifle that carries and guides him, the oversized needle of a magic compass, a death-dowser's wand,

you walk poorly, you stagger, you make too much noise,

he swats away the tiny flies that pursue him, always catching up to him. The sun burns his skin, which emerged fragile from the cold of the war; he is a lizard revived by the heat; everything in him is drawn taut between fear and exhaustion.

His steps suddenly trigger (rolling stones, quivering branches, the sound of wings) a pigeon taking flight a few meters away. He closes the bolt to cock the rifle and shoulders it – he does not fire,

you are too close to the villages, you mustn't attract the attention of a passing shepherd,

he watches the bird disappear behind a cluster of holm oaks to rejoin its mate,

these birds always travel in pairs,

they are the inseparables of the mountain, the inevitable harbingers of spring, along with the nightingales. He engages the weapon's safety. At the top of the pass between these two rock-speckled hills the cabin will come into view. He watches the storm clouds suddenly massing gray above the sea line. A cloud veils the sun. The wind turns the drops of sweat on his shoulders and torso into so many icy pins. He had forgotten the dexterity of the cold – he forces himself to stop to put his jacket back on, stiffened by all the fluids clogging its fibers, along with the pain and the dread,

you stink of the slaughterhouse, that is the odor you give off, the smell of guts and of a jet of water on blackened tiles,

a smell of meat,

he runs his left hand over his face, his beard is rough, like bark. The disappearance of the sun means the return of the altitude as much as the shadows: he shivers. Behind him, slightly below, a cottony mist spreads between two folds in the hills, a white fog on the red earth, the sea has disappeared. Steel gnaws at the horizon. He throws his last ounces of strength against the rocks to cross them, against the slopes to climb them. The pass howls, the pass freezes his face. The wind presses against his face and shoulders. He clings to his rifle and leans forward. Lurching from one imbalance to the next, he reaches the shelter of a rock, a few dozen meters further down. He leans against it,

the house is down below on your right,
