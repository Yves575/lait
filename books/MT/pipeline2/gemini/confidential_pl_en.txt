The Tumult of the Past

Unless you can get ahead of the course of events and, by conjuring the future, write a good ending. You can't, I know what I'm talking about. You're bogged down, your legs tangled in parched roots. The drought does you no favors; months of downpours would be more use, the roots would grow slippery, and you’d scramble out much faster. Meanwhile, I encourage you to practice saying goodbye to memories. You've been running late for years; you have to face linear time. Memory loops are the domain of old folks, for whom the passing years cut off retrospection. Time to set time free. Do it slowly—the body can play tricks; it might react to sudden changes with shock. Swap the circle for a timeline, not a line segment—it sounds like a strange geometry problem.

I can't promise that when time finally moves forward, life will flush with color. Everything has its flaws and few merits. Pull your head out of the tumult of the past. That's where you're from, but there's no one left there besides you. Look around and throw out the things your children won't want to inherit. Do the same in your head, though that's much harder. Whatever you can—tell it in elegies. It sometimes works; it lets you push the subject away to a safe distance. Safe, meaning one that opens you up to thinking about your own life. Have you ever wondered about that? Are you living a good life? Whose life are you living? It's no longer about whether they'd be proud of you; that's over. Start thinking about tomorrow today.


The English

They were really close now; all that remained was to cross the square and walk two hundred meters.

"If he starts yelling at the top of his lungs for the whole street again, this time you're going alone," she said.

Ever since the boys were born, Sunday dinners at her in-laws' had become mandatory. Fortunately, not every week, but at least once a month. The routine repeated itself down to the letter.

They would emerge from around the corner and look up at the same spot—at the second-floor balcony. He'd be standing there waiting, even though it was only a quarter to two. You could see him from a distance, a red-haired older gentleman gesturing energetically. The closer they got, the more clearly they could hear that he wasn't limiting himself to just frantic arm movements.

"How old are you, for God's sake?! Can't anyone agree on a time with you?! I've been standing here for fifteen minutes, scared to death that something happened to you! How dare you put me through such stress?!"

"I meant what I said, and I'll do it; I can't take this anymore."

"Mom, come on, don't let it bother you. Grandpa is just like that."

She looked at her husband. He spread his arms helplessly and smiled pleasantly, raising his bushy eyebrows. She didn't want to seem hysterical, but she'd long had enough of these balcony scenes.

"He's acting like a lunatic; maybe this time you'll actually say something to him?" she asked, though she knew the answer perfectly well.

"What can I say to him? He's telling the truth, he's scared to death, and there's nothing we can do about it. Let's agree that when he dies, we'll stop coming to visit him." He looked at her with a warm smile.

"Do you think Grandpa will die today?" asked one of the sons.

"I hope not just yet."

"What about you, Mom? Would you want Grandpa to die today?"

"I'd rather he didn't. We'll have plenty of excitement as it is."

Ignoring the increasingly loud shouting coming from the balcony, they entered the building, walked past the mailboxes, and headed up the stairs. They never took the elevator; it was so small there was no way all four of them could fit inside.

Grandpa would be waiting in the open doorway. First, he'd lean down to the older grandson, squeezing him too hard and for a little too long.

"My firstborn, my firstborn. Let me look at you—my, don't you look smart. Who would have thought you'd be born at all!"

Then it would be the younger one's turn. He'd hug him too tightly, as well.

"You've got a black mop of hair; maybe it'll lighten up. Good thing you're not a ginger."

Without taking his eyes off the boys, he'd embrace his son, and finally, greet his daughter-in-law.

"You've given me beautiful grandsons. Come in, come in."

"Dad, could you spare us the yelling from the balcony next time? We agreed on two, we got here fifteen minutes early, and you're screaming at us for being late?"

"Everything will be fine, don't worry about it. I was worried you'd been hit by a car, or met with some other disaster... The most important thing is that we're all alive and together. By the way, I wonder if I'll live to see great-grandchildren. Well, boys, can I count on you?"

"There's no telling if you'll live that long," said the younger grandson.

"Well, aren't you witty!" Grandpa replied with a smile.

After the greetings, they would go into the dining room, where a large, round table stood. A white tablecloth, plates with gold rims, and silver cutlery—everything was already set out. An old woman was sitting at the table, tiny and hunched. She had suffered from Alzheimer's for years and did not react to the noise that filled the room.

"Boys, say hello to your grandmother," their father would say.

They didn't like getting close to her; they didn't understand what was happening to her. She rarely spoke, and never in Polish. They would go up, quickly kiss her on the cheek, and take their seats. Sometimes a faint smile would flit across her wrinkled face; it was hard to say whether it was conscious.

Dinner was cooked by Grandpa's housekeeper. She would set the pots and pans on the windowsill and leave, despite genuine invitations to sit down at the table with everyone. Chicken soup with homemade noodles, ground meat patties, mashed potatoes, and lettuce with sweet cream, which no one but Grandpa ever ate. The youngest grandson would quickly devour his entire meal so he could get a second helping of soup before it disappeared back into the kitchen. Afterward, he would politely ask to be excused from the table and lie down on the floor, where he played with his toy cars.

The rhythm of these dinners was unchanging. It all lasted about an hour and a half; then Grandpa would take a nap, and they would leave, with no one trying to stop them.

“A bodybuilder from Dachau picked up a brick and got winded,” the younger grandson announced one Sunday, lying on the floor with a belly full of soup.

“*O Boże!*” Grandma groaned in Polish.

Silence fell, and the parents’ eyes turned toward their older son.

“I didn't teach him that!”

“The kids at school taught me,” the youngest member of the family replied, completely unfazed.

“Why did you decide to share that with us right now?” his father asked.

“I just remembered it.”

“Look at him, he just remembered it! And did you know that some people never managed to forget?” his grandfather asked, agitated.

“Yeah, I know.”

“And from where, if I may ask?”

“From you guys. You talk about it all the time. I don't understand why you're always talking about Jews.”

“Because we are Jews,” his grandfather replied.

“All of us?”

His grandfather looked around and smiled.

“Yes, all of us.”

“Me too?”

“You too.”

The boy went back to his toy cars for a moment.

“I’d rather be English,” he spoke up after a long silence.

“Want me to tell you something?” his grandfather asked.

“Go ahead.”

“I’d rather be English too.”

His grandfather reached for his glass of tea, but it had cooled down enough to lose his interest. He got up from the table and went to take a nap.


I Tell Everybody

This guy was a bit of a rascal. He loved medicine and women, in exactly that order. And half the town loved him back. The half he treated. The other half was divided between those he drove crazy and those who couldn't care less. He was ginger and freckled, meaning temperamental. He could sock you in the jaw out of nowhere, knock back half a liter, and walk on as if nothing had happened.

He'd managed to live into the second half of the twentieth century, so he decided to seize the opportunity and live without denying himself any pleasures. He considered his lifetime quota of tragedies exhausted. His wife took care of raising their child; he only got involved when the situation required a doctor. He believed a father’s lot was a thankless one, like in that joke about the picky eater. The whole family was worried because he was skinny and ate nothing. Finally, the father lost his patience and asked what his little boy wanted to eat more than anything else. After giving it some thought, the kid said he wanted shit. "What do you mean, shit?" "Just shit. Shit on a plate." The mother looked at the father, the father looked at the mother, and then he got up and went to the bathroom. He came back with shit on a plate. He set it in front of his son. The kid inspected it and said he wanted the shit fried. The mother looked at the father, the father looked at the mother, and then he got up and went to the kitchen. He came back and set it in front of his son. The son inspected it and said he wanted Daddy to try it first. Mother at father, father at mother. He took a bite, declared it exquisite, and encouraged his son to finally start eating. "I won't eat it, because Daddy ate the best piece."

The household ran smoothly, their son grew like a weed, and the red-haired doctor kept meeting new women and spending blissful moments with them. Whenever friends asked how he was doing, he would tell a joke about a Jew who went to a priest to confess. The priest asks if the penitent is Catholic. He isn’t. Then why has he come? Because he really wants to. The priest enters the confessional, and the Jew starts telling him about all the women he has slept with lately. That this one was blonde, that one had curly hair, and yet another had such small breasts... "Why did you choose me of all people to tell this to?" the priest asks. "I tell everybody."

His wife pretended not to hear it. After what they had been through, she knew life could bring far more serious problems. Sometimes she would complain to a friend, who, upon returning home, would ask her husband to have a talk with the doctor. They would arrange to meet, go out for vodka, and the family friend would gently reveal the mission he had been sent on. The redhead would look at him and say that, right at that very moment, he was reminded of a joke about a tax collector who visits a Jewish community. "What do you do with the wax from melted candles?" "We collect it and make new candles out of it." "I see. And what do you do with the matzah crumbs?" "Oh, from those we make delicious matzah balls." "I see. And when you circumcise little boys, what do you do with those, you know?" "Ah, with those. We collect them, send them to the tax office, and from there they send us pricks like you."

He'd smother any friendly impulses in the blink of an eye. If his companion proved persistent, he would call the waitress over and ask her to open a window. "Nothing to be afraid of," he would say, "my friend here is no eagle, he won't fly away."

The son married a petite brunette, a graduate of the Academy of Fine Arts. Admittedly, the doctor had other plans for him, but he accepted it and stopped searching for the perfect match.

The red-haired doctor’s wife fell ill with rapidly progressing Alzheimer’s. He took excellent care of her. He brought a young woman from the provinces into their home. She cooked, looked after his sick wife, and things worked out quite well between her and the doctor, too.

To his grandchildren, he became a wonderful doctor, but an odd grandfather who had his moments. Once a year, he would take them shopping. To everyone’s surprise, he might buy a pool table or a bicycle. Sometimes he took them to a restaurant, and disappeared quickly after placing the order. Only years later, as they grew up, did the grandchildren realize that the main reason for these outings was by no means a desire to spend time with them, but rather a waitress in a short skirt. He didn’t know how to be a grandfather, yet he was proud that the family was growing.

A year after his wife’s death, he invited his older grandson out for dinner. After they ordered, he didn’t leave the table as he usually did. While they were waiting for their food, the grandfather remembered a joke about Rosenzweig, who paces the house all night. Around five in the morning, his furious wife gets up and asks him why he's pacing. "I'm pacing because I can't sleep," Rosenzweig replies. "Why can't you sleep?" "Because I owe Hozenduft money." "A lot?" "A lot." "Then call him and tell him you won't pay him back." The husband thinks for a moment and makes the call. "This is Rosenzweig. Yes, I know what time it is. I just wanted to tell you I won't pay the money back, good night." And he hangs up. "And now what?" he asks his wife. "Go to sleep. Now he's the one who isn't sleeping."

The grandson, despite being trained in the art of the joke since childhood, doesn't understand why his grandfather is telling him this right now. A shapely waitress brings their food. The grandfather smiles at her, but stays at the table.

"Listen, there is a Jewish law that says a healthy man shouldn't be alone. This law also says that when a wife dies, the husband can marry her sister. Your grandmother's sister perished back then, when everyone else did."

*What's going to happen now?* the grandson wonders. The grandfather takes a deep breath and announces that he is getting married. He immediately adds that his son won't be happy with his choice of wife.

"How do you know?" the grandson asks.

"Something tells me. People rarely like their stepmothers. My half-sister couldn't stand my mother."

"Did she have reasons?" asks the grandson.

"Oh, she had every reason!"

The grandson worries that he has been delegated to break this bizarre news to his father. He senses that it will be neither easy nor pleasant. Deliverance comes unexpectedly.

"But for now, let's not say anything to your father..." the grandfather decrees, letting his voice trail off. "You choose a second wife, one for your old age, without thinking about other people. You look for someone who will understand you. Even if you know she speaks before she thinks, that she's difficult, that she hurts everyone around her. You go for it, because you want to drown out the loneliness. You figure you'll deal with the mismatch later."

The grandson is in his teens and senses that "later" might be too late, but these are adult matters, after all. He doesn't yet know that in his family, the stepmother type will prove hereditary.


Underwater Odyssey

A lie doesn't have short legs. His father always said it did, but he didn't realize just how wrong he was. It floats to the surface when you pay too little attention to hiding it.

When he was a little boy, he didn't lie—they said he made up beautiful stories. By the time he was a teenager, they were already calling it lying. He didn't dwell on it much; he managed his life on his own terms.

He forged his value system to suit the needs of the moment.

The most important rule: calm parents, a peaceful life. There were certain flaws in this system, but over time it became an integral part of the cycle: ensure the parents' peace of mind between parent-teacher conferences, and survive their return with dignity and the hope for a better tomorrow. Find solace in the post-argument silence of the house, and take neither their reproaches nor their anger personally. After a few days, give them something to lift their spirits; it didn't have to be true, it only mattered that it worked for the time being. Remember that the "calm parents, a peaceful life" rule is the priority, and that the best moments happen between parent-teacher conferences. Don't push your luck, but don't give up without a fight, either.

He was a cheerful boy, the first post-war child in his family. He could sense that they had little tolerance for a lack of diligence. He naturally developed a *modus operandi*. You could say he took his cues from nature. He flowed like a stream—where there was less resistance, not where he was expected to go. A rule as simple as it was frowned upon.

The doors opened, and a shaft of light spilled into the movie theater.

"Is..." —here his last name was called out— "...in the audience?"

He thought he had misheard.
