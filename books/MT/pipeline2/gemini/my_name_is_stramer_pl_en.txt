I

NATHAN

It was for Rywka that he had returned from America. He would tell people that for four years he had thought about the girl every single day, until he finally bought a return ticket for the ship. He never told them that he had come back penniless, and that he’d had to borrow the money even for that ticket from his older brother. He did, however, still like to slip American words into conversation, words that no one in the family understood.

He brought a leather belt with a brass buckle back from America. A parting gift from his brother for his trip back to Poland. They embraced on the New York docks and never saw each other again. Looking at the belt, by the wear of the successive holes, you could trace how his figure had changed over the years. Nathan himself could hardly believe he had once been so slim.

Like his father and grandfather before him, he looked like a wrestler. An old wrestler. Broad shoulders, a barely visible neck, and a chest puffed out like a rooster’s. He was proud that people in town called him "Boss."

On the street, when asked how things were, he would answer:

"Things are good, but not hopeless."

He would touch the brim of his hat and walk on.

Every few months, a letter would arrive from New York. Ben wrote that, unfortunately, he had to postpone his trip to Tarnów for the time being. He couldn’t leave the stationery store right now. The school year was starting and he had too many orders. He had to fire a dishonest employee and hadn't yet found a trustworthy replacement. Soon it would be the Christian holiday of Christmas, a time for gifts, and we get more than just Jewish customers! His wife had fallen ill again. A crisis had started; if he left the shop now, he would have no reason to return to America. But he missed them and was making plans for next year. He couldn't wait to meet Rywka, their sons, and daughters. 

As their parents, you know best what they need, so buy it for them from me.

Between the folds of the letter, written on company letterhead (BEN STRAMER GENERAL MERCHANDISE, 33 Grand St, NY) and folded in three, he would slip green banknotes. He would also wrap them in purple tissue paper, so no one could see what was inside. The tissue paper bled onto the banknotes every time; upon being taken out of the envelope, they were green and purple, and Rywka had to gently wash them and then dry them over the stove in the kitchen. 

Nathan gave the children only the American stamps from the envelopes. The dollars, he set aside.

"I work all the time, so I have no time to make money," he would explain to his wife.

And he waited. He waited, waited, waited. Until a good business deal came along. A deal that would change the lives of the Stramer family. One that would lift them from their one-room ground-floor apartment with a small kitchen at the very end of Goldhammer Street, carry them high into the air, and set them down only on the top floors of the tenements with colorful stained-glass windows in the stairwells, in the elegant Polish-Jewish neighborhood, ideally near a stop for the tram line that Tarnów was so proud of. In one of those spacious, high-ceilinged, multi-room apartments with a toilet and a bathroom with running water, electric lighting, and a flower-filled balcony.

For a brief time, it seemed like this would come true. He had happened to buy an entire train car of rosin on the cheap. He had been told that violinists used it to rub on their bow hair. How was he to know there were so few violinists in Tarnów? In the end, he bought a violin and bow himself, and ordered Salek—who Rywka said had beautiful, long fingers—to play them.

Even though he had never been to the philharmonic or the opera, in his mind’s eye he already saw his seven-year-old son there—on the stages of Kraków, and even Vienna. Rywka, too, found it easiest to picture him in a tiny tuxedo, with a bow tie and a mop of curly hair. Unlike his brothers, he didn’t like fighting or getting dirty.

"Here," Nathan said, handing him the instrument. "A famous violinist never did any family any harm."

And everyone knows how much money can be made on tickets.

But nothing came of that, either.

True, he sent him for violin lessons with a Polish teacher a few times. Only, did Salek actually make it there? Because every time guests came over and Nathan asked him to give a "little concert," Salek would make excuses. Until, after six months, Nathan stopped asking and ordered him to "bring the violin this instant."

After the performance, he was silent.

It wasn’t until the guests had left that he spoke, shaking his head on his barely visible neck:

"*Goddammit!*"

Nobody understood him, so he added:

"Money down the drain."

That evening, for the first time, instead of reaching for his belt, he reached for the bow. At least it would be good for something! And indeed it was. He didn’t have to hit him hard at all for Salek to let out noises similar to the ones he had just been drawing from his violin moments before, during his "little concert."

At work, behind the register at the Jewish slaughterhouse, Nathan was also waiting for the deal of a lifetime. That was why, in the fervor of his thoughts, plans, and meticulously calculated future profits, he sometimes gave customers the wrong change.

"*Shit*," he would say if the register was short at the end of the day.

But there were also times when there was too much money. Then he would say nothing, and just discreetly slip the difference into his trouser pocket.

*I have children*, he thought.

Whenever the right moment finally came, he had the urge to run home from work. He would do the shopping on the way. At home, he wouldn't sit down for a moment. He would help Rywka prepare the food. During supper, he would ask the children how things were at school. The fact that he had no patience to listen was another matter. *But at least he asked*, Rywka thought. Then he would ask her to sit down and rest. He would clear the plates himself, roll up his sleeves, and wash the dishes in a basin.

Only when he had finished, and the children were already asleep, did he pull the rolled-up dollars from the metal leg of the bed.

"I have a feeling it’s going to work out this time. That this is the one."

Rywka said nothing. But Nathan knew what she was thinking:

*Why didn't it occur to you during your last deal to take a proper look at even a single candle?*

He didn't want to waste time; he was afraid other merchants would beat him to it, that someone might offer a higher price. But most of all, he was afraid the seller, who had traveled all the way from Kołomyja, would find out how much candles cost in Tarnów and realize he was selling his for next to nothing. Nathan had to act fast. An opportunity like this would surely never come around again. Candles four times cheaper than those from the famous Jewish Pszczółka factory, which supplied all the churches in Tarnów! And maybe even in Kraków. That was what he’d heard, though he didn't know if it was true or just advertising. Besides, he had also heard that the owner, Mr. Szpilman, was quietly giving money to the socialists. But that, in turn, could have been a smear campaign spread by Pszczółka’s competitors.

At the moment of paying, Nathan had even felt like a cheat for a second. Maybe he should throw something extra in for the unfortunate salesman from Kołomyja, or at least invite him to dinner? But that feeling quickly gave way to regret that the salesman didn't have any more goods to sell. So maybe it wasn't worth it.

This was how Nathan silently answered the question Rywka hadn’t asked.

They sat across from each other at the slightly wobbling kitchen table; the floor was uneven, and the piece of wood must have slipped out from under the table leg again. As usual, Nathan tried to slide it back in with his shoe. When that failed, he leaned down, found the chip of wood, and shoved it back into place. Tomorrow morning, when it got light, he would finally clean it and glue it, he decided.

They both knew that none of his explanations would convince her, which was probably why they were conversing without words. Or maybe they simply didn't want to wake the children? They just looked at each other, but for a moment Nathan still felt as though in Rywka's light eyes—instead of the single candle burning on the table—he saw the thousand he had bought without wicks.

What did it matter that, when he realized his mistake, he immediately ran to the train station and with his last money bought a platform ticket? The train carrying the merchant—along with Nathan’s still slightly purple dollars—had long since departed for Kołomyja. Nathan stood for a long time among the people on the platform, repeating incomprehensible American words, before he finally confessed to Rywka what had happened. And that evening, despite the closed window, the crying of his children could be heard on Goldhammer Street.

Afterward, he went to bed and didn't get up for work for a week. At the slaughterhouse, Rywka said the same thing she had told the children:

"Nathan has come down with stomach catarrh."

*This time will be different,* his look said now.

Rywka wasn't impressed that his eyes lit up again, like in the days when she first met him. Sometimes she just wondered where he got this energy of his, which seemed to come and go in waves. Once, she had even asked Nathan.

"I brought it from America," he said.

She thought then about the sea he had told her about after his return to Tarnów. It too was buffeted by waves, there were high tides and low tides. It was rarely calm—like her.

"More likely you caught it at sea," she smiled.

In any case, she decidedly preferred the stories about the sea and great ships to those about New York. They soothed her. She liked to picture them before falling asleep.


RYWKA

Nathan had promised many times to take her to the seaside, but that was back before their marriage and the children. So far, the farthest she had been was Kraków.

Of course, in her youth she had been offered a trip to America. What girl hadn't? A pleasant, graying man with a signet ring on his little finger stopped her on the street. A Jew, of course. He introduced himself as an entrepreneur, the owner of a thriving factory in Nebraska, and "above all, an inconsolable widower who had returned to Europe in search of love."

This search even brought him to her parents. Rywka’s father threatened to go to the police. It was no coincidence that Galicia was called "Scandalicia" back then. Everyone knew what it was really about and who they were really after. Even so, two of her friends from Nisko, Nesa Schnur and Lila Flaum, decided to go. Or maybe their parents had persuaded them and pushed them out of the house? She heard they ended up in Argentina or Brazil. She never saw them again.

In Nisko, it was said that pimps called pretty girls "silver spoons," "bolts of silk," and "Smyrna carpets." The less attractive ones were "barrels of flour," and the ugly ones "sacks of potatoes." She still remembered these terms. Maybe because the girls used to call each other that. She also wondered which category they had put her in.

She realized that she was too short for a silver spoon. But on the other hand, she had dark-set light eyes, thick black hair, and a slender waist. So she probably wasn't a barrel of flour, much less a sack of potatoes.

Now, on Goldhammer Street, those times seemed unreal to her. She had gotten married, moved to Tarnów, and had children. A lot had changed in her life. But had it in the world? You still heard about pimps trading in "delicate meat." All in all, not that many years had passed. After all, she still occasionally caught herself mistakenly starting a letter with the date eighteen hundred... Somehow, she just couldn't adjust to this twentieth century.

Maybe because she rarely read the newspapers. Who with six children would have time for that?

Actually, she had given birth seven times. Her first son, Kuba, was born too early, without eyelashes or fingernails. She reproached herself for having needlessly helped Nathan paint and furnish their first apartment—which was even smaller than the one on Goldhammer Street—while she was pregnant. And once, she had even slipped and fallen in front of the entrance. She should have stayed in bed. Besides, it didn't matter anymore.

It was winter; they wrapped him in the warmest featherbed, but it didn't help. He lived for less than three weeks. It felt so unnatural to arrange a funeral for her own child, to walk through the snow behind her own son's coffin. She forced herself to cry. She couldn't, so she hid her face in her hands. So others wouldn't see that she wasn't crying.

The small coffin wasn't expensive, but they still spent everything they had on it. They buried Kuba by the cemetery wall.

The night after the funeral, muffled crying woke her up. She pressed close to Nathan, and only then did she start to cry herself.

She didn't know if Nathan also missed their little one so much. She didn't know if he, too, was afraid that Rywka would never get pregnant again. Like his sister-in-law, Pepi, who, even before leaving for America, had also given birth prematurely and gone into the hospital with the baby, only to come out alone. According to Nathan, that loss had sealed their decision to leave. Then again, everyone knew America had the best doctors for such things.

But Ben and Pepi, in letters written in neat handwriting on company letterhead, never mentioned a pregnancy or a baby. Instead, in the ones sailing in the opposite direction (and possibly passing each other somewhere at sea, Rywka thought), Nathan, in his nervous handwriting, announced to his brother the birth of each successive Stramer and described the others. Maybe that was exactly why they never managed to visit Tarnów. Even though they were, after all, immediate family. But they never once forgot to slip money into the envelope.

Rywka found this easier to understand than Nathan did. Because what if it were the other way around? What if we had been the ones to remain childless, and instead of dollars, news of more American nephews and nieces came pouring in from across the ocean? How would we feel?

And so she thought of Ben and Pepi with fondness. And whenever she thought of them, she remembered Kuba.

"The first child is for God," Nathan had once said.

"Don't talk like a rabbi," she had replied.

Who would he have taken after? Would he, like the eldest, Rudek, never ask their advice on anything and spend his days running around town? What did he actually do, and where was he getting the money he would secretly give her for the household?

She could only guess.


RUDEK

One Sunday, Nathan happened to spot him among a Polish-Jewish gang of teenage boys fooling around as they trailed behind a regiment of Austrian soldiers marching down the main streets of Tarnów in their tight dress uniforms. Perhaps it was for the first anniversary of Emperor Franz Joseph’s death, because there was even a band playing a march. That was what Nathan had come to see. Or rather, to get a look at the violinist. At the time, he was still predicting a musical career for Salek, having just bought him a used Höfner violin. And he wanted to see for himself what a real violinist looked like. But why couldn't he spot one anywhere?

Like everyone else, he found himself staring at the drum major conducting the band, and at his white staff with the brass ball on the end. The ball was up one moment, down the next, rotating on its axis, and in the finale flying into the air.

*I wonder how much they pay him for that*, Nathan thought, and stood frozen in anticipation: maybe, just for once, the drum major would fail to catch his ornate staff?

He would have sooner expected that than to run into Rudek here. At first, he couldn't believe that his son was picking up cigarette butts from the ground along with the others, making insulting faces and gestures at the soldiers. And, on top of that, was bellowing for the whole of Sobieski Square to hear:

*At Ulm, at Ulm, at Austerlitz,*
*We took it in the ass and kept our mouths zipped.*

As it happened, Nathan knew that song from his own youth. He started pushing his way toward Rudek.

*Because we Austrians have a certain way,*
*We take it in the ass and have nothing to say.*

He didn't let him finish singing the rest of the verses. He dragged his son out of the crowd by the collar. And thrashed him with the American belt right in front of everyone. A few Austrian officers with twirled mustaches turned around without breaking their stride; some of them even raised their pince-nez to their eyes.

Nathan threaded the belt through his belt loops and asked:

"Will we ever have it better than under Emperor Franz Joseph?!"

Then he added:

"And if I ever catch you with a cigarette in your mouth again, I’ll rip it out along with your lungs."

That was less than a year ago, but it had clearly worked, because lately Rudek could be found every day by the wall of the military barracks across Goldhammer Street, where the Austrian garrison was stationed, and he had his cigarettes not in his mouth, but in his pockets.

For a week now, he had been bringing Salek with him after school. Did Rudek look much older than he actually was, or did Salek look much younger? Or perhaps the brothers just gave that impression by contrast. A tall, broad-shouldered boy in a flat cap and knickerbockers leaning over a slight boy—who was stuffed, to make matters worse, into a tight school uniform with a high collar—really still just a child.

Besides, they were separated by more than just thirty centimeters in height or a year and a half in age. Salek didn't play football—not on the right wing in a blue jersey and white shorts, like Rudek did for Samson Tarnów, nor in any other position for any other team. He didn't play anything, anywhere at all. He hadn't learned to whistle through his fingers or to spit properly. He would spill lemonade on his uniform when they shared a bottle outside the shop. And worst of all, he still occasionally managed to pee on his own trouser legs under a tree, even though Rudek had shown him how to avoid it several times.

"You can shake it for two weeks, but a drop still falls into your trousers," he instructed him. "But not *on* your trousers."

It was a similar story with boxing and wrestling. Rudek made Salek practice on their younger brothers, Hesio and Nusek, also without much success. Eventually, he more or less taught him a single strike—a headbutt to the nose. That was the move that left Hesio with a bloodstained shirt, which ended with all three of them getting a taste of the American belt.

In any case, whenever someone picked on him, the most Salek would do in his anger was clench his fists and swing his arms like a windmill.

*He’s in no danger anyway, because everyone knows whose brother he is*—Rudek felt a warmth inside. But he had also prepared him just in case they were in another neighborhood where people didn’t know, and someone tried to hit Salek. He was supposed to say that he knew Omega, Zachar, or Bänder. There was probably no one in Tarnów who didn't think of them with fear.

"Nobody will lay a finger on you."

He didn’t admit to Salek that he didn’t actually know them himself. Because did anyone else need to know that?

What everyone *did* know was that when Omega, Zachar, and Bänder weren't in prison, they could be found in the evenings in the company of prostitutes outside the Hotel Polski on Dworcowa Street. But everyone preferred to avoid crossing their path. It was said that they were afraid of no one.

Seemingly by accident, they would bump shoulders with people, shove pedestrians off the sidewalk, and knock headwear off their heads. School caps, flat caps, fedoras, yarmulkes, and the fur shtreimels of the Hasidim would land in the mud and puddles, which were plentiful there at any time of year, even after a light rain.

And that was just the beginning. Then the knifemen would wait for a reaction. Would the victim muster the slightest reproach, even if expressed with a mere glance—the tiniest gesture of displeasure, visible in the dark only to them?

“You think you’re a tough guy?”

And immediately they would step in so close you could smell what they had eaten and drunk that day at the third-class train station restaurant.

Of the three of them, only Dawid Bänder didn’t hit right away. First, he would play with his knife. And that was what people feared most, because Bänder was unpredictable. He might let one guy walk, only to suddenly stick a knife into another.

“Shall I circumcise you, tough guy?” he had supposedly once asked a terrified Prince Roman Sanguszko, whose car had broken down near the station one night.

Rudek understood the gravity of such an encounter. At school, he was taught that the Sanguszko family were the former owners of Tarnów, and on the street, that Bänder was the great-grandson of Idele Muc, the legendary boss of the Tarnów thieves. And since Rudek was one of the most diligent students in both places, he understood that the descendants of the city’s rulers had come face-to-face.

“Shall I circumcise you, little tough guy?” Rudek and his brothers had jokingly reenacted this scene many times in the courtyard on Goldhammer Street.

“Anything but that! Mercy for His Princely Highness!” Salek, playing Sanguszko, would shield himself with his arms and faint against the wall of the wooden outhouse.


SALEK

He had picked that up at the theater. For his birthday, Rudek had given him a standing-room ticket. They were putting on *Samson and Delilah*, directed by Dante Baranowski, who also played the male lead. During the performance, in the chilly, half-empty hall of the Sokół building, he couldn’t concentrate on the actors flinging themselves about the stage, alternately roaring with rage, weeping, and fainting. 

In Delilah, he instantly recognized Jadwiga Geron, the elegant wife of his gymnasium Polish teacher, to whom he would bow in the school corridors. Should he now, as usual, at least offer a discreet bow from afar? He didn’t want her to feel offended. He also didn’t know if he had the right to sit down, even though more than half the red seats were empty. Who could he ask? Besides, he spent the whole time wondering if Dante Baranowski had actually been born Dante Baranowski.

Maybe he, too, used to be a plain old Salek or Jurek. And only as an adult, in order to appear on a playbill and perform on stage, had he first had to change his name to something more artistic-sounding. It reminded him of Goldhammer Street, which had still been called Zdrojowa when they first moved in.

And how would that sound: Dante Stramer?

After the performance, Rudek was waiting outside the Sokół building.

“Rudziu, why is Baranowski’s first name Dante?” Salek asked on the way home.

“Baranowski’s mother was simply Italian, and Dante is a typical Italian name.”

How did he do it—how did he know everyone and know so much about them? Salek looked up at his brother in admiration.

But he could impress Rudek, too.

He quickly gained popularity at the gymnasium, already in the first grade, when during recess, at the sight of Father Wątorek, the Catholic religion teacher, and Izaak Bleiweiss, the Jewish religion teacher, strolling arm in arm across the uneven courtyard, he remarked to his classmates:

“The Old Testament is walking arm in arm with the New.”

True, some claimed an older boy had come up with it, but Rudek didn’t believe them. After all, the indisputable witness to Salek's priority was their friend and fellow gymnasium student, Romek Brandstaetter.

A moment later, the joke had swept across the screaming courtyard and through the brick school building. And the bolder boys started shouting it at the religion teachers.

“Do you hear that, Professor?” Wątorek finally asked.

And Bleiweiss replied:

“Why wouldn’t I hear it, Monsignor?”

Various rumors circulated about them around the school. It was whispered, for instance, that they liked walking arm in arm so often because neither of them had a wife.

Salek neither listened nor took notes during Jewish religion class. He did his math homework, wrote his Polish essays, and prepared for the tutoring sessions he gave after school. And when he finished, he looked out the window at the people passing by on the street. He thought about how he, too, would walk down there after classes, and how anyone looking out the window then would simply see a boy walking down the street. There was nothing unusual or extraordinary about this, and he didn’t know why he kept coming back to the thought that, in just a little while, he would be to others what they were to him now.

He also watched the trash bin at the intersection across the street. He counted how many people, between one bell and the next, would lean over it and reach inside. Now there was already a fourth—even though not a quarter of an hour had passed since the bell—an old woman pushing aside the trash, likely searching for scraps of food. Before her there had been a boy, at most his age, who with a quick motion scooped bent cigarette butts into his coat pocket. Rooks would flock to the trash bin, too; not only did they make the most noise and the biggest mess, but they also gave the others a bad name by tossing garbage onto the sidewalk. But Salek couldn't possibly keep count of them.

The Hebrew alphabet seemed sad and gloomy to him now. He associated it with the poorest part of Tarnów, the old Jewish quarter. The letters drooped down like the sidelocks, the beards, and the overly long black coats—perpetually mud-splattered at the hem—of the inhabitants of Grabówka.

He already knew everything Bleiweiss taught, anyway. Once it had been interesting, something he could enjoy. Fairy tales for the three-year-old boy he had been when his father first led him by the hand to the cheder. Not the one closest to home, where Rudek had gone the year before, but one much farther away. Salek’s legs had ached; he was practically running to keep up with his father. He didn't notice the curb, tripped, and fell onto his hands, but he also got his trousers dirty, and to top it off, they tore at the knee.

“Goddammit!” his father yanked him up.

Salek burst into tears; he didn't want to go to cheder anymore.

"Because you'll go to the one by the house."

A year earlier, Rudek had run home from there with a fever and a resolution that he would never return. It took a long time for Mother to pry out of him what had happened. Only when she promised not to repeat it to anyone did Rudek explain that the melamed didn't just pull ears and beat them with a cane for incorrectly repeated Hebrew words. He had made him strip naked and stand in the corner of the room, and the other children were told to walk up and spit in his face. That was when he ran away.

"Why did he make you do that?" she asked.

"Because I didn't believe the Jews crossed the Red Sea on dry land."

Without a word, Salek walked on behind his father. All day in the cheder, he kept his hand on his knee and, along with the others, repeated passages of the Torah after the melamed.

True, the old teacher never raised a hand to the children, nor even his voice, but to any questions and doubts he always had one unchanging answer:

"Silly boy, so it is written in the Pentateuch."

This was the exact same thing Salek heard when, after a year of studying in that damp room, which also served as the melamed’s entire apartment, he dared to ask his first question, one he had been carefully preparing for days:

"Why, at the sight of children sitting on a wall and laughing at the bald head of the prophet Elijah passing beneath it, did God let two raging she-bears loose from the forest, and they tore forty-two children apart?"

It was Rudek who finally explained to him that you simply had to respect your father and both grandfathers, even though they were bald under their hats.

At any rate, fortunately, God did not repeat anything quite so cruel in the cheder. Much like the melamed, He remained silent at the sight of the oldest boys, who quietly played cards, buttons, and slaps under the table. He remained equally unmoved when they stood behind the teacher and pretended to waltz with him, or when, pinching their noses in disgust, they used two fingers to pull his old, darned long johns and socks out of the wardrobe, or imitated a dog that had mistaken the old melamed for a bitch.

Perhaps it was because the melamed was far from possessing the power, holiness, and clarity of mind of the prophet Elijah, Salek thought. Or perhaps it was because he wasn’t bald; long, matted gray hair stuck out from under his yarmulke.

II

NUSEK

In Tarnów, people used to say that the farther you got from Grabówka, the cleaner the air. Nusek only breathed through his mouth there. The smell of that district might bring to mind the outhouse on Goldhammer Street, or a Friday night stretching into Saturday, when the children, overstuffed with cholent from the Sabbath dinner, couldn't fall asleep for a long time.

“PKP?” Salek or Hesio would ask every so often.

“RKM,” Rudek would answer them.

“No!” Rena would shout, if she wasn't asleep yet. “I won't allow it. Go out to the courtyard.”

At night, the two-room apartment on Goldhammer Street turned into a bedroom. Three beds took up the entire room, and a fourth the kitchen. In the middle stood the chairs and table—you had to remember they were there, especially in the dark on the way to the outhouse. But Nusek tripped, or at least bumped into something, almost every single time.

Rywka did her best to ensure each of the children had as much space as possible. She decided that the tallest and oldest son, Rudek, would share a bed with the youngest, Nusek; next to them, Salek with Heś, who was only slightly smaller than him; and a bit further away, behind a screen, the girls, Rena and the last-born of them all, Wela. Only Suchard the dog and Milka the cat got to choose for themselves every evening at whose feet they would spend the night.

“What does PKP mean?” Nusek asked. “What does PKP mean?” he repeated, as if thinking Rudek hadn’t heard.

“You’ll feel it for yourself in a minute.”

“Why do you never tell me anything? Why?”

“For the common good. Go to sleep.”

“Just tell me, what’s PKP?”

“Polish State Railways.”

“But really?”

“Really. Go ask at the station.”

Nusek wondered why they never took him seriously. And how many times had he begged his older brothers to take him with them to the barracks? Don’t even ask.

Surely it couldn’t still be about that stupid business with the bow. Especially since he had done everything Rudek had told him to. He had meant well; the best proof was that he had tried—perhaps even too hard.

Nusek closed his eyes, but he couldn’t fall asleep. He could hear his father’s steady snoring. Salek mumbling something indistinctly in his sleep. And the creaking of the bed as Rudek rolled over and settled his head on their shared pillow.

He could feel his warm breath on his face.

*Why do I always get the least of everything?* he thought. *The least space in bed, the least of the pillow, and even the quilt, which Rudek yanks away from me every night?*

“Are you asleep?” he heard Heś ask.

He opened his eyes.

“Why?”

“Do you want to know what PKP and RKM mean?”

“Yes. Tell me.”

“Will you carry the wood and coal from the shed in the yard for me for a week?”

Nusek still couldn’t fall asleep, but for a different reason now. Every so often, he looked with admiration at Rudek, who was sleeping beside him with his mouth open. Maybe he really did deserve their whole shared pillow, and even the quilt? Nusek wanted to laugh just thinking about PKP and RKM. What wise guys! This way, they could say it at school in front of the teachers, or at home in front of their parents. He never would have thought of it himself. It took someone like Rudek or Salek to come up with that. That all you had to do was give something a new name—and problem solved.

“This head isn’t just for wearing a cap,” as his father used to say whenever he was scheming up his next business deal.

But that saying didn’t suit his father, and not just because Nusek had never seen him in a cap. Usually, when leaving the house, he would take one of two old, crumpled hats from the coat rack, alternating between them. 

That saying was definitely much better suited to the head resting on the pillow next to Nusek.

Once again, he recalled various occasions when he’d heard them say PKP and RKM. He had suspected they were harsh swear words, or a secret code connected to the cigarette trade, to girls, or at the very least, to communism. But now he chuckled, because it sounded so elegant: PKP? — Would my colleague permit a fart? And RKM — A colleague may, just this once.

He drifted off to sleep smiling. As always, he tried to catch the exact moment he fell asleep, and as always, at the very last second, he failed.


RYWKA

At night, some of them dreamed of one another. And Rywka was also almost certain that they sometimes dreamed the exact same dream at the same time. Evidently, there are individual dreams and shared ones, she thought—like graves in a cemetery, hotel rooms, or cabins on the ships Nathan talked about.

She could have sworn she once heard the children playing football together in their sleep.

“Pass it to me,” Nusek pleaded.

“To you, Hesiu,” said Rudek. “Shoot for the top right corner.”

“Goal!” shouted Salek.

Rywka waited to see what would happen next, but there was only silence.

“Nathan.” She snuggled up to her husband and kissed his broad neck. “You won’t believe what I just heard.”

“Every child in every square and courtyard in Tarnów is playing it,” Nathan said with his eyes closed, “and there is no need to wake me up over it.”

He knew what he was talking about. The day before, on the front page of a newspaper at the kiosk, he had seen a long headline: Epidemic of ball-kicking spreading alarmingly in our city. It had been particularly difficult for him to read the last word through the dirty glass. Unlike the first word, whose large, wide letters had drawn him to the kiosk in the first place.

As usual, he hadn’t bought the paper; he’d just tried to read a few lines on the spot.

There is not a courtyard in our town where a dozen or so tykes, little Jews, or urchins wouldn't suddenly start kicking a ball around, regardless of the weather, right in the mud. Our only fear is that our ladies might take up kicking too, for then it would surely be the end of the world.

Maybe he washes his windows so rarely on purpose—Nathan looked appreciatively at the old, toothless vendor—so that people won’t read for free.

You don’t understand! Rywka wanted to shout. They were playing in their sleep!

But instead she said:

“I’m sorry. Sleep in health, wake in health.”

By morning, she had forgotten all about it. She had to get everyone out of the house. To work, to school, to *cheder*. In the dark room, she laid out the clean clothes they were to wear. In the kitchen, she lit the stove. On the table, she placed an evenly sliced piece of dark bread with butter on everyone’s plate and poured chicory coffee. Nathan, as the head of the family, was additionally entitled to a roll for breakfast.

“Daddy, let me borrow your roll,” Nusek said. “I’ll give it right back to you, I just have to eat it first.”

At first, Nusek didn’t know what they were laughing at. Only after a moment did he join in, and he even laughed the loudest of all, as if to make up for the time it had taken him to understand his own joke.

“I’ll give it right back, I just have to eat it first,” he repeated proudly, pointing at himself. “I’m smarter than I thought.”

Before they left the house, Rywka would wash the children’s faces, straighten their collars, and comb their hair. 

Already at the door to say goodbye, she would first stand on tiptoe beside Rudek, then bend her knees and gradually descend lower and lower, stopping in turn at Salek, Rena, and Hesio, on her way down to Nusek and Wela, squatting down once she reached her.

“You’re going down like a New York elevator.” Nathan had promised not to say it again, but once more, he couldn’t help himself.

Rywka always kissed the children in the exact same spot. Her favorite. Right where the forehead ends and the nose begins. She didn’t know what it was called, but she referred to it as the “little saddle.”

The same thing every morning. Thanks to this—as she kept repeating to herself and Nathan—she had no time to get bored. These mornings had become deeply ingrained in her routine. Sometimes, it wasn't until she was eating her slice of bread or washing the dishes that she realized Nathan and the children had already left. As if someone else had woken them up that day, made sure they put on warm underwear, drank their coffee, and didn't leave the house late.

III

NATHAN

“I bought it,” Nathan announced from the doorway, as proud as if he were talking about the Tarnów Nitrogen Works, rather than a sideboard from the second-floor neighbor, who was selling off his last pieces of furniture before leaving for America.

“Dirt cheap,” he added once the door was closed.

That was probably what he liked most about the sideboard. He immediately sent Rudek, Salek, and Hesio upstairs.

“What about Nusek?” asked Hesio.

“Too small to be a porter.”

Rudek went down the stairs first. He moved so fast it was as if he were carrying the piece of furniture down all by himself, rather than with Salek and Hesio, who could barely keep up, gripping the oak legs ever tighter so the sideboard wouldn't tip over onto their brother.

After they scraped against the wall for the third time, Rudek spoke up in the manner of Anszel Koszcz:

“Gentlemen... is this what they pay you for?”

Salek asked if they could take a short break so he could tie his shoe. Three steps later, when Rudek set the sideboard down on the first floor, his hands were shaking.

Anszel, the tallest and broadest-shouldered of all the porters in Tarnów, was their unofficial boss. He probably could have carried an even heavier piece of furniture down all by himself, without any trouble or helpers. Quite recently, Rudek had watched him milling about by the Pilzno Gate. Looking for work, he circled slowly, walking with patient steps among the stalls.

*Like the Earth around the Sun*, Rudek thought.

Indeed, Anszel hardly ever stopped. If the other porters wanted to talk to him, they had to walk alongside him, caught in his orbit. He was the one they went to with everything. He was the highest authority for them, from whom on earth—at least on the uneven, muddy earth by the Pilzno Gate—there was no appeal.

Even though, with a black patch over his left eye and a rope slung across his shoulder, he looked like the sea pirates their father used to scare them with, Anszel kept order. He usually spoke calmly, even with a smile, but everyone knew what awaited a porter if he, for instance, stole a parcel—or, God forbid, a customer who failed to pay for a job. More than once, Rudek had seen Anszel whip the metal shaft out of his handcart in one swift motion.

And how had he lost his eye? Tarnów knew so many versions of the story. Perhaps even Anszel himself was no longer certain how it had happened, which was why he told a different story to everyone.

“Besides, there might be even more versions than listeners,” Rudek explained to his brothers. “Because some people heard a different one every time.”

Rudek liked the heroic one best. Years ago, on a Saturday in July, hooligans had surrounded the Jewish quarter. They attacked people and broke into shops closed for the Sabbath. They must have planned it well, because apart from clubs and iron bars, they carried sacks where the stolen goods ended up.

“As their bad luck would have it,” Anszel would say, never pausing his rounds among the market stalls, “they also attacked the porters’ families as they were coming out of the synagogue on Fish Square.”

Anszel’s men took off running toward the Pilzno Gate, where they left their carts secured to a single long chain for the holidays. They pulled out the metal shafts. Jewish workers armed with crowbars and wooden planks joined the porters. And Anszel led the way.

“We drove them out of the neighborhood. We smashed the wagons they tried to flee in, and set the horses loose. We recovered everything. I was the only one who lost something,” Anszel would point at his black eyepatch.

“But you saved the city from a pogrom,” people would reply with unflagging admiration. Perhaps because the surrounding cities and towns had been less fortunate.

“They just got a light slap on the wrist.” Every time he recalled this, Anszel would modestly shrug shoulders as broad as the cupboard that the three Stramer brothers struggled to carry down from the second floor to the ground floor.

At school, Salek had heard a slightly less heroic version. Before an older classmate repeated it to him, he asked Salek to keep it quiet. That boy’s father had previously asked him for the same thing, having himself been sworn to secrecy by one of the rope merchants in Tarnów.

“I’m telling this only to you,” Anszel was said to have muttered in a low, hushed voice to that trustworthy man walking beside him.

Before he became a porter, Anszel had done a few years in prison after catching his wife with her lover. It just wasn't clear whether he had lost his eye while doing time, or earlier, during his victorious brawl with the lover he ended up killing.

Salek had heard yet another account of the incident from Hesio, one that made no mention of prison whatsoever.

“It turns out it was a sleeping Anszel who was attacked with a knife by a jealous husband. The man had come home early from work at the slaughterhouse and found Anszel in his wife’s arms.”

The thought of a bloody massacre, complete with a butcher’s knife plunged into an eye, briefly silenced the screaming courtyard on Goldhammer Street.

Admittedly, Nathan, who had heard what they were talking about through the open ground-floor window, declared at supper that he remembered from his youth when a drunken Anszel had a bicycle accident. He fell into a ditch. He must have impaled himself on something down there. A dry branch, a piece of wire, some broken glass. The next day he woke up without an eye, covered in blood, and remembered nothing.

What struck the children most about this story was the fact that their father knew Anszel personally.

“I’ve met plenty like him in my time,” Nathan waved a hand.

While he was at it, he demonstrated a Jewish porter’s blow to the children. He called Hesio over and unexpectedly shoved him with his thrust-out belly. Little Hesio went flying into the wall, where fortunately they hadn't yet placed the elegant sideboard with its protruding horse heads, but tears welled up in his eyes all the same. When Salek timidly protested that the porters by the Pilzno Gate and in Fish Square were skinny and wouldn't have anything to hit with, Nathan didn't even look at him; he just shook his head.

“In America,” he added quietly, to himself.

“I'm talking about the blow of a Jewish porter in America,” he repeated loudly, as if realizing it wasn't at all obvious to the rest of the family that the real porters, craftsmen, merchants, and all the big-bellied rest of them were over there, where his brother Ben had remained. Among truly tall buildings with real elevators. Truly wide, long streets with sidewalks, full of real shops, cafés, and customers paying for everything in the realest of dollars.

“Even the sky is higher there,” he had blurted out once.

Rywka sometimes felt that Nathan hadn't really returned from there at all. She guessed that he often imagined how his life would have turned out across the ocean. And that it seemed more real to him than their life in Tarnów, which he neglected as a result.

Gently but thoroughly, she dusted the carved wooden pillars with the protruding horse heads at the very top. This elegant oak sideboard didn't match the rest of the furniture. Not the bench Nathan had nailed together from three pine boards, not the dresser with four drawers that barely opened, not the perpetually limping table or the wardrobe standing beside it with its cracked back panel. It didn't fit their cramped ground-floor apartment—or rather, the apartment didn't fit it.
