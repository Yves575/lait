I

I didn’t ask you to leave, I’d said.

You’re the one who wanted this, and if you didn’t want to get your feet wet, pal, you shouldn’t have climbed aboard. I didn’t push you into the water, and I’m not the one who came looking for you in your village or your field, in your ruined suburb, to drag you out of there and put you in your damn leaky boat, and now you’re floundering and I’m perfectly willing to believe you’re scared, and you’re calling me for help as if it’s my fault, you’re demanding that I save you and you’re losing patience. You’re counting on me. But I didn’t ask anything of you. So let me do my job and just sit tight.

And I guess I must have been thinking all that so loudly that I said it out loud, the first sentence anyway, at least if the recordings were to be believed, and there was no reason not to believe them, I’ll admit that much.

To which I should have added, while we were at it, sentences like I’m not God Almighty either, because I must have been thinking that too, and I could have said it. I could have said There are guys like you everywhere tonight, forty Titanics allegedly sinking in the Channel all at the same time and I can’t take care of everyone at once. So you’re going to have to be patient, pal, or else tell the others, who are sinking too, to calm down and hang up their phones so I can deal with just you; you just have to call all the others since you’ve got a cell phone, all the others who get into rotten dinghies every night, with no compass, no navigation lights, thirty of them on a floorboard that can barely hold the weight of five, with no instruments, no bearings, no knowledge of the sea, along with women and children.

But ultimately I’d said to Julien, next to me: These people are unbelievable, they throw themselves into the water and then they practically bite your head off because you don’t toss the lifebuoy fast enough, they’re really full of hot air. He had smiled, before diving back into his book. More like deflating, in this case, he had remarked. And at three in the morning, that was the kind of joke that made you laugh.

That joke wasn’t recorded. I don’t think it would have gone down very well, and Julien can rest easy: he’s not the one who’s going to look like a monster for making a joke about inflatable dinghies deflating. Besides, on the station’s recordings, you can hardly hear anyone but me, which is just my luck.

After that I had gone back to my monitors, my PC, my mic, telling myself no doubt that, after all, these people should be happy: since they wanted to go to England, there they are, in British waters, and now on a British vessel, with British survival blankets, wrapped up like candies in gold foil, and they’ll be able to carry on their conversation in English for as long as they want.

But in the end, the currents had carried their bodies back into French waters.

And now they were floating on the investigator’s desk, in the offices of the maritime gendarmerie. There were twenty-seven of them to be exact, a little girl among them, scattered among the pens, the notepads, the files, floating around the gendarmerie captain’s computer, and among them too the body of the one who had called me fourteen times that night and whose voice, of course, could no longer be heard now. The sea was calm on the desk, no wind and no swell, and alongside the bodies only neatly arranged papers on the surface.

While she was playing the recordings for me, the gendarme would alternately stare at me and look at who-knows-what out the window, from which you couldn’t see the sea and that was just as well, because I saw the sea all the time from my watchtower and, given the choice, I preferred to see, as I did today, a stretch of street with a construction site across the way, workers, African for the most part but these ones at least alive and not wet and numb with cold, and no women and no children, so I gladly looked at that while on the recording you could hear *Please, please* and I heard my own voice saying *Calm down, help is on the way*.

The gendarme had her hair pulled back and tied in a severe ponytail, exactly like me, I told myself, and her back perfectly straight like mine, military in a way, coathanger shoulders as Éric used to say, maybe even with the exact same face as me for all I knew, but ten years older, she in her blue gendarmerie sweater and me obviously in plain clothes, I had thrown on a ridiculous sweatshirt and a pair of sneakers as if returning from my morning jog, leaving me looking like a kid despite my strict ponytail, like a sullen little girl, my face locked up tight, summoned to the principal’s office, and it was no doubt this ridiculous and vaguely humiliating resemblance, by virtue of which I was facing a caricature of myself or the ruthlessly dismal portrait of what I must actually look like, it was no doubt this, this resemblance, that had instantly made her unlikable to me, repellent even, even though in any case it wasn't a matter of feeling any warmth under the circumstances.

Showing me into her office, she had said Thank you for coming in voluntarily, your director had up until now refused to give us your contact details. She had added Your colleague, it seems, the one who was on watch with you that night, didn't have the, the, the, but she had hesitated over the choice of word that was to come next, it could be Scruples, or else Courage, and why not Moral conscience or Sense of duty, but she couldn't find the right word, so she corrected herself, saying Has not taken this step, for the moment.

And fortunately she hadn’t asked me why *I* had taken this step, as she ultimately called it, and why I had come like this of my own accord, before, for all I knew, the judge and the gendarmes came ringing my doorbell directly anyway, and hauled me off in front of my little girl to stick me in the defendant’s dock on charges of failure to assist a person in danger or I don’t know what else; in short, fortunately she didn't ask me why, that morning, after dropping Léa at my parents', I had taken the car to drive to the maritime gendarmerie in Cherbourg, a four-hour drive from Boulogne, on the highway a bland coffee on the surface of which I could see little dinghies bobbing but they were just biscuit crumbs and around me people who were silently pointing at me behind my back, a phone call to my parents to check if everything was fine with the little one, who was worried about not going to school, a phone call to the gendarmerie too, to say I'm coming I'm on my way, and me completely surprised not to hear someone answer in English on the other end of the line, not to hear *Please, please* and screaming in the background, but no, simply a We're waiting for you, as reasonable shipwreck victims should have said, biding their time seeing as there wasn't much else to do besides pray and look out to see if there wasn't a boat in the vicinity, We're waiting for you, they would have said calmly, instead of making endless useless pleas, instead of not understanding the difference between being in French waters and being in British waters, instead of calling fourteen times in two hours to say they were sinking, instead of annoying me by repeating that as if I were the one who didn't understand, but I understood perfectly well, I did, that your feet are in the water but they're English waters, not French waters, even if they're just as cold, I can well believe it, so look that way instead if you even still know which way to look, where north is, where south is,

so fortunately she hadn’t asked me why I had finally made up my mind, because I wouldn't have known what to say to her, in any case I don't think I would have used words like Scruples or like Sense of Duty, and certainly not Courage, maybe rather words like Fatigue or Nausea, but more likely like Anger,

that is to say, like Shame.

It was also that I wanted to hear *myself*. That is to say, hear myself rather than read myself, rather than read my words in the newspapers, hear my voice rather than the voice of the journalists repeating on television what I had said, alongside expressions like Migrant Drama, Migrant Tragedy, as if every night at eight o'clock we were at the theater putting on the same play again,

and besides, it is indeed the same play, and with me in the front row you could say I don’t miss a single performance, every night I come to watch the Migrant Drama, I get a free seat and they even pay me for it, there are those in the boxes, those in the balcony, but for me they set up a watchtower in the front row so I can see clearly, so I am simultaneously in the front-row boxes and the first balcony overlooking the sea, and even if it sometimes goes by a different title, sometimes Migrant Drama, sometimes Migrant Tragedy, Drama in the Channel after Drama in the Mediterranean, it’s always the same play of course, and always the same character too, and at one point the character picks up a phone to address the audience, it’s interactive, he calls out, it starts with *Please please*, but he isn’t really addressing the audience, who, meanwhile, let out dismayed *oh*s and *ah*s and sometimes sighs of indignation, it’s me, me alone he’s addressing, and then it’s up to me to answer and answer well otherwise the audience isn’t happy and then the sighs of indignation are aimed at me, and the next day the theater critics come down on me, because how the show turns out depends on my answers and if I do a good job the Migrant Drama is a little less of a Migrant Drama, not to mention that during all this I also have to handle what’s happening on other stages where they’re putting on the Drama of the Drifting Yachtsman or the Tragedy of the Capsized Trawler.

So, to hear myself, to hear my own voice, in that maritime gendarmerie office, to be absolutely sure of what I had said, rather than hearing some dolled-up journalist play ventriloquist, saying I had said this and that, and that among the things I had said were horrible, scandalous, or even appalling things, before going on to say that I had done or not done things I should or shouldn't have done.

But mostly it was him you heard on the recordings, I don’t know what to call him: the Migrant, the One on the Phone, the Sinking Man, but his voice I knew by heart, and what he said too, because, in reality, it’s always the same guy calling me every night, the same voice and the same pleas, because the idiot, no matter how many times we haul him out of the water he goes right back in, once, ten times, a hundred times: one night we pull him out of there in the nick of time, he’s safe and sound, he can say thank you, and the next night it’s him calling back again, because he’s back in it again as if he hadn’t learned his lesson and he says *Please please*, every day we put him back on dry land, every night he does it all over again, and you get the feeling then that it’s going to be like this until Judgment Day: the same guy, always, going back into the water and starting to sink in the middle of the Channel and waiting for us to come get him so he can do it all again the next night, and in the long run, it gets tiresome.

But my own voice too, all the same, although not quite my voice either, not because the recording inevitably distorts voices, but because it was my professional voice, the one that curtly manages the disaster every night with phrases like *Calm down* and *Send me your geolocation via WhatsApp* or *Help is on the way*. So you mostly heard phrases like that, at 2:05 a.m., at 2:36, at 3:12, at 4:22 and up until 4:32 when, from then on, nothing more could be heard, and among those phrases there were others that apparently I shouldn't have uttered, like the one saying, as I mentioned, *I didn’t ask you to leave*, and the investigator asked me how that particular phrase was meant to be interpreted.

So I told the investigator that this particular phrase—since it was obviously a problem, even though I didn’t know if I was going to be thrown in a cell for stating an obvious truth, namely that I certainly wasn't the one who had asked them to leave, if that was one of the offenses I was being accused of, but what offense exactly—this phrase, in any case, shouldn’t be given excessive importance, in my opinion, because, after all, I could have said it to any irresponsible weekend sailor, to the moron who heads out to sea without checking the weather or who is incapable of properly steering his shitty little sailboat, and who ends up broken down and standing there looking like an idiot, and who panics, and then the entire Navy has to be mobilized to keep him from going under. To that guy too, if he came and told me I wasn't acting fast enough, as if I were responsible for what was happening to him, I would tell him in the exact same way that I wasn't the one who asked him to leave, after all.

But she looked at me as if she didn’t believe me, and I had my doubts too. But why?

So she seized the opportunity, all too happy to ask me disingenuously, You don’t see the difference between an irresponsible weekend sailor and a migrant boat? The question probably wasn't very well-intentioned, but in the end it was easy to answer, because my job required me precisely not to make a distinction, which is what I told her then, and that I ought to be congratulated for not making one, this distinction: we rescue everybody, we don’t make distinctions. Was I supposed to show more urgency for one than the other?

But that wasn’t what she meant, of course, because I seemed to think then that it was the same thing—some yokel heading out to sea in his pleasure boat and people fleeing extreme poverty and war—as if they had all set out in the same boat for a fishing trip. The distinction to be made wasn’t between one drowning man and another drowning man, but between the reasons, the causes that put him in that situation. The distinction to be made is why people leave, and that explains why they leave thirty of them in a nutshell and not all alone on a First 24. So the problem really was that I wasn’t making a distinction, that I didn’t seem to understand that the situations were different, that people don't leave in the same way, and above all not for the same reasons, and therefore not in the same conditions either.

And yet, the insidious question implied that, on the contrary, I *was* making a distinction, just not in the right direction—meaning that, ultimately, I would have shown more urgency in saving some upstart trying to play the hotshot on his massive yacht on a windy night than in mobilizing a rescue for a handful of Iraqi Kurds who insisted on splashing around the Channel every single night. In other words, I wasn’t making a distinction when I should have, and when I shouldn’t have, I was making one, and all of this could only be explained by one reason, always the same one; and since she was eventually going to ask me if I had something against migrants, or if I considered a migrant's life to be worth less than that of a weekend sailor or a fishing skipper, to preempt a question like that, I said that I didn't have any opinions about migrants—no more opinions about migrants than I did about immigration policy or the right to asylum, about North-South relations, about the problems, the solutions, about the world's misery, or about injustice; that this wasn't what was being asked of me, to have opinions about migrants.

No, I don’t have any opinions on migrants, I actually have fewer than most, I think, and besides, I’m expected not to have any at all and that suits me just fine. If anyone has opinions on migrants, it’d be my ex, for example, Léa’s dad, he certainly has them, and anyway his are pretty much the same as the baker on the corner and the cashiers at the local supermarket who I chat with from time to time when I have a week off, pretty much the same ones, really, as half of Calais, Boulogne, or Dunkirk. And I heard Éric’s opinions on migrants all the more because I was doing that job, at the CROSS, because he not only failed to see why we went to so much trouble, at the CROSS, to help those parasites, but also why we didn't just chuck them straight into the drink the second we spotted one, with a good whack from an oar on top of it if possible, just to make damn sure they didn't come back up to the surface.

It had started with the whole Calais and the Jungle business, because he was from there, Éric, from Calais, and by the end he simply couldn't stand the thought of me doing that job—coming to the aid of trawlers or cargo ships, yes, but migrants, no—and every time I came home, I was in for it, and how many did you haul out of the drink again this time, and how much is this bullshit costing us, and why don’t you just push them into English waters and let them fend for themselves since they want to get over there at all costs, and why, once you’ve fished them out, don't you ship them straight to Africa, and don't you think we've got better things to do than nurse their boo-boos, they can sort themselves out or stay home, etc. I’d heard it a thousand times, until, for reasons that actually had nothing to do with any of that, I asked him to leave, once and for all, but I wasn't there to talk about my life with Léa’s dad and how we had eventually split up.
