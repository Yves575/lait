To Berenice

There are thirty-two of them. Thirty-two still living in the abbey on this autumn day in 1986, at the end of a road that makes those who travel it turn pale. In a thousand years, nothing has changed. Neither the steepness of the route nor its dizzying heights. Thirty-two strong hearts—as one must be when living perched on the edge of the void—and thirty-two bodies that were just as strong in their youth. In a few hours, they will be one fewer.

The brothers form a circle around the one who is departing. There have been many circles, many farewells, since the Sacra raised its walls above them. There have been many moments of grace, of doubt, of bodies braced against the coming shadow. There have been and there will be other departures, so they wait patiently.

This dying man is not like the others. He is the only one here not to have taken vows. Yet, he was permitted to stay for forty years. Whenever there was a debate, or questions, a man in a purple robe arrived—never the same one—to settle the matter. He stays. He is a part of this place, as surely as the cloister, its columns, its Romanesque capitals, whose state of preservation owes much to his talent. So let us not complain; he pays for his stay in kind.

Only his fists protrude from the brown woolen blanket, resting on either side of his head—an eighty-two-year-old child in the grips of a nightmare. His skin is yellow, at breaking point, vellum stretched over angles too sharp. His forehead is gleaming, glazed by a greasy fever. His strength had to fail him one day. A pity he did not answer their questions. A man is entitled to his secrets.

Besides, they feel they know. Not everything, but the essentials. Sometimes, opinions diverge. To stave off boredom, they discover the zeal of gossips. He is a criminal, a defrocked priest, a political refugee. Some say he is being held against his will—the theory doesn't hold up, he's been seen leaving, and returning—while others claim he is here for his own safety. And then there is the most popular, and most secret, version, for romance only enters here as contraband: he is here to watch over her. She who waits, in her marble night, a few hundred meters from the small cell. She who has waited patiently for forty years. All the monks of the Sacra have seen her once. All would like to see her again. They would only have to ask Padre Vincenzo, the superior, for permission, but few dare to do so. Out of fear, perhaps, of the unholy thoughts that are said to come to those who approach her too closely. And the monks have quite enough unholy thoughts as it is when they are pursued, in the heart of the dark, by angel-faced dreams.

The dying man thrashes, opens his eyes, closes them again. One of the brothers swears he saw joy in them—he is mistaken. A cool cloth is gently laid on his forehead, on his lips.

The sick man stirs again, and for once, everyone agrees.

He is trying to say something.

Of course I am trying to say something. I have watched man fly, faster and faster, further and further. I have seen two wars, nations fall, I picked oranges on Sunset Boulevard—don't you think I have something to tell? Forgive me, I am ungrateful. You clothed me, you fed me when you had nothing, or so little, when I decided to hide among you. But I have kept silent for too long. Close the shutters, the light hurts me.

He stirs. Close the shutters, brother, it seems the light is bothering him.

Shadows keeping watch over me, backlit against a Piedmont sun; voices turning cottony as sleep approaches. It all happened so fast. Barely a week ago I could still be found in the vegetable garden, or up a ladder; there was always something to repair. Slowed by age, it’s true, but given that no one would have bet on me at birth, it was enough to command admiration. And then one morning, I couldn't get up. I read in their eyes that it was my turn, that the death knell would soon sound and they would carry me to the little garden facing the mountain, where poppies grow over centuries of abbots, illuminators, cantors, and sacristans.

He is fading fast.

The shutters are creaking. Forty years I have been here, and they have always creaked. Darkness, at last. Darkness like at the cinema—which I saw come into being. An empty horizon; at first, nothing. A blinding plain that, as I stare at it, my memory populates with shadows, with silhouettes that turn into cities, forests, men, and beasts. They step forward, take their places downstage, my actors. I recognize a few of them; they haven't changed. Sublime and ridiculous, cast in the same crucible, inseparable. The currency of tragedy is a rare alloy of gold and dross.

It's only a matter of hours now.

A matter of hours? Don't make me laugh. I have been dead for a long time.

Another cool compress. He seems to be calming down.

But since when can't the dead tell their story?

*Il Francese*. I have always hated that nickname, even though I've been given far worse. All my joys, all my tragedies belong to Italy. I come from a land where beauty is always cornered. Let it fall asleep for five minutes, and ugliness will slit its throat without pity. Geniuses spring up here like weeds. People sing the way they kill, they draw the way they deceive, they let their dogs piss on church walls. It is not for nothing that an Italian, Mercalli, gave his name to a scale of destruction: that of earthquake intensity. One hand demolishes what the other has built, and the emotion is the same.

Italy, a kingdom of marble and filth. My country.

But the fact remains, I was born in France in 1904. My parents had left Liguria in search of fortune fifteen years earlier, barely married. By way of fortune, they had been called *Ritals*, spat upon, mocked for the way they rolled their r’s—though, as far as I know, the word *roll* does indeed begin with an *r*. My father had narrowly escaped the racist riots of Aigues-Mortes in 1893; two of his friends had lost their lives there: good old Luciano and old Salvatore. They were never again mentioned without those adjectives.

Some families forbade their children to speak the language of the old country, so as not to "sound Rital." They scrubbed them raw with Marseille soap in the hope of whitening them a bit. Not in the Vitaliani household. We spoke Italian, ate Italian. We thought Italian—which is to say, with a barrage of superlatives where Death was often invoked, tears flowed freely, and hands were rarely at rest. We cursed the way one passes the salt. Our family was a circus, and we were proud of it.

In 1914, the French state, which had shown so little zeal in protecting Luciano, Salvatore, and the others, declared that my father was without a shadow of a doubt a good Frenchman, worthy of conscription, especially since a clerk had—by mistake or for a joke—shaved ten years off his age when copying out his birth certificate. He left with a long face, with no flower in his rifle. His own father had lost his life in the Expedition of the Thousand in 1860. Nonno Carlo had conquered Sicily with Garibaldi. It wasn’t a Bourbon bullet that had killed him, but a prostitute in the port of Marsala with questionable hygiene, a detail we preferred to pass over in silence in the family. He was dead all the same, and the message was clear: war killed.

It killed my father. One day, a gendarme showed up at the workshop above which we lived in the Maurienne Valley. My mother opened the shop every day on the off chance of a commission her husband might fulfill upon his return; he would have to get back to cutting stone one day or another, to restoring gargoyles and carving fountains. The gendarme assumed a solemn expression suited for the occasion, looked even more apologetic when he saw me, coughed, explained that there had been a shell, and that was that. When my mother, full of dignity, asked when the body would be repatriated, he stammered, explained that there were horses on the battlefield, other soldiers, that a shell did a lot of damage, and that, as a result, they didn’t always know who was who, or even what was man and what was horse. My mother thought he was going to start crying; she had to offer him a glass of Amaro Braulio—I never saw a Frenchman drink it without grimacing—and she herself did not weep until many long hours later.

Of course, I do not remember all this, or only vaguely. I know the facts, I restore them with a bit of color, those colors that now slip through my fingers in the cell I have occupied for forty years on Mount Pirchiriano. Even today—or at least a few days ago, when I was still capable of it—I speak poor French. I haven’t been called *Francese* since 1946.

A few days after the gendarme’s visit, my mother explained to me that in France, she wouldn’t be able to provide the education I needed. Already her belly was swelling with a brother or a sister—who was never born, or at least not alive—and she drowned me in kisses, explaining that she was sending me away for my own good, that she was sending me back home because she believed in me, because she saw my love for stone despite my young age, because she knew I was destined for great things, and that she had given me a name for that very reason.

Of the two burdens of my existence, my name was undoubtedly the lighter to bear. Yet I hated it with a passion.

My mother often went down to the workshop to watch her husband work. She realized she was pregnant when she felt me startle at a blow of the chisel. She had not spared herself up to that moment, having helped my father move enormous blocks of stone, which perhaps explains what followed.

“He will be a sculptor,” she announced.

My father grumbled, replying that it was a lousy trade where hands, backs, and eyes wore out much faster than stone, and that unless you were Michelangelo, you might as well spare yourself the trouble.

My mother agreed, and decided to give me a head start.

My name is Michelangelo Vitaliani.

I discovered my country in October 1916, in the company of a drunkard and a butterfly. The drunkard had known my father and had dodged conscription thanks to the state of his liver, but the way things were going suggested his cirrhosis wouldn’t protect him much longer. They were drafting kids, old men, and cripples. The newspapers insisted we were winning, that the Boche would soon be ancient history. In our community, news of Italy joining the Allies the year before had been hailed as a promise of victory. Those coming back from the front sang a different tune—at least, those who still wanted to sing. *L’ingeniere* Carmone—who, like the other *Ritals*, had scraped salt in Aigues-Mortes before opening a grocery store in Savoy, where he consumed a good portion of his own wine stock—had therefore decided to head back. Since he was going to croak anyway, he might as well do it back home, his lips stained red with Montepulciano to wash away the fear.

His own country was Abruzzo. He was a kind man, and agreed to drop me off at Zio Alberto’s along the way. He did it because he felt a bit sorry for me, and also, I think, for my mother’s eyes. Mothers’ eyes are often quite something, but mine had irises of a strange blue, almost violet. They had sparked more than one brawl, until my father put a stop to all that. A stonecutter has dangerous hands; I won’t be the one to say otherwise. The competition quickly backed down.

My mother shed great violet tears on the station platform in Modane. My uncle Alberto, also a sculptor, was going to take care of me. She swore she would join me soon, as soon as she sold the workshop and made a little money. A matter of a few weeks, a few months at most—it took her twenty years. The train hissed, spewed a black smoke I can still taste, and carried away the tipsy *ingeniere* and her only son.

Say what you will, at twelve years old, sadness doesn't last very long. I had no idea what this train was lurching toward, but I knew I had never been on one before—or at least, I didn't remember it. My excitement quickly gave way to malaise. Everything was moving too fast. The moment I focused on a detail—a pine tree, a house—it vanished instantly. A landscape isn't meant to move. I felt unwell and wanted to tell the *ingeniere*, but he was snoring with his mouth open.

Fortunately, there was the butterfly. It flew in at Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne and landed on the windowpane, between me and the mountains rushing past. After a brief struggle against the glass, it gave up and stayed perfectly still. It wasn't a beautiful butterfly, not one of those glories of color and gold I would see later in the spring. Just a mediocre butterfly, gray, slightly bluish if you squinted hard, a moth dazed by the daylight. For a moment I thought about torturing it, as any boy my age would, but then I realized that by focusing on it—the only still thing in a frenzied world—my nausea was fading. The butterfly stayed there for hours, sent by a friendly power to reassure me, and it was perhaps my very first inkling that nothing is truly what it seems: that a butterfly is not just a butterfly but a story, something enormous lurking in a tiny space, which the first atomic bomb would confirm a few decades later, as would, perhaps even more so, what I am leaving behind as I die in the foundations of the country's most beautiful abbey.

When *l’ingeniere* Carmone woke up, he laid out his project to me—for indeed, he had one. He was a communist. Do you know what that is? I had heard the insult several times in our community back in France; people were always wondering if so-and-so was one. I replied, “Pff, of course, it’s a man who loves men.”

*L’ingeniere* began to laugh. In a way, yes, a communist was a man who loved men. “Besides, there’s no wrong way to love men, do you understand that?” I had never seen him so serious.

The Carmone family owned a plot of land in the province of L’Aquila, to which geography had dealt two injustices. One, it was the only province in Abruzzo without access to the sea. Two, earthquakes ravaged it at regular intervals, much like the Liguria of my ancestors—except that that bitch Liguria actually had access to the sea.

His land offered a pleasant view of Lake Scanno. *L’ingeniere* planned to build a tower there, mounted on a gigantic ball bearing, to house the local proletarians for a modest rent that would still allow him to make a decent living—especially since, like a good communist, he was reserving the top floor for himself. Thanks to two teams of horses working in twelve-hour shifts, the building would revolve slowly throughout the day. Thus, its inhabitants would all enjoy—without exception, with neither profiteers nor exploited—a view of the lake once a day. Perhaps electricity might one day replace the horses, though Carmone confessed it would probably never reach out that far. But he liked to dream.

The ball bearings would also offer the advantage, should an earthquake strike, of decoupling the structure from the ground. In the event of a level XII earthquake on the Mercalli scale—he was the one who taught me that name—his building had a thirty percent greater chance of withstanding it than a normal building. Thirty percent might not sound like much at first glance, but since level XII was no joke, he explained, widening his eyes, it was actually huge.

I drifted into a half-sleep, my eyes glued to my butterfly, and we crossed into Italy while *l’ingeniere* spoke to me tenderly of devastation.

Italy and I embraced like old friends the very first time we met. In my rush to get off the train at the Turin station, I tripped on the steps and landed on the platform with my arms spread wide. I lay there for a moment, without a thought of crying, filled with the bliss of a priest at his ordination. Italy smelled of flint. Italy smelled of war.

*L’ingeniere* decided to take a carriage. It cost more than walking, but my mother had entrusted him with an envelope of money, and just as wine was meant to be drunk, he declared, money was meant to be spent; besides, let’s go buy ourselves a quick quarter-liter of red from the Po before hitting the road, if you don't mind.

I was quite willing, marveling at my surroundings: soldiers on leave, soldiers shipping out, porters, train conductors, and a whole throng of shady characters whose functions or ambitions seemed wholly mysterious to the kid I was. I had never seen shady characters in my life. I got the feeling they were kindly returning my persistent stares, as if to tell me, *you’re one of us*. Or perhaps they were just staring at the blue lump growing right in the middle of my forehead. I waded through a forest of legs, blissful, overcome by other smells: creosote and leather, metal and cannons, the scents of shadows and battlefields. And then there was the noise, the din of a forge. It ground, squealed, and crashed—a *musique concrète* played by illiterates, a far cry from the halls where jaded elites would one day flock to pretend they appreciated it.

Without knowing it, I was arriving right in the middle of Futurism. The world was nothing but speed—the speed of footsteps, of trains, of bullets, of shifting fortunes or alliances. Yet all these men, this entire mass of humanity, seemed to be violently digging their heels in. Bodies exulted, swarming toward the railcars, the trenches, a barbed-wire horizon. But something, between one movement and the next, between two surges, was screaming: *I want to live just a little longer.*

Later, when my career took off, a collector proudly showed me his latest acquisition, the Futurist painting *La Révolte*, by Luigi Russolo. It was in Rome, around the turn of the thirties, I believe. The man considered himself an enlightened connoisseur, a passionate lover of abstract art. He was an idiot. Without having been there that day at the Porta Nuova station, no one can understand that piece. No one can understand that there is nothing abstract about it. It is a figurative painting. Russolo painted exactly what was exploding in our faces.

No twelve-year-old kid puts it in those terms, obviously. At the time, I simply looked around me, wide-eyed, while the *ingeniere* quenched his thirst in a dive at the end of the platform. But I saw it all. Sign, if any more were needed, that I wasn't entirely like everyone else.

We left the station in a light snow. We had barely stepped outside when a *carabiniere* intercepted us and demanded to see my papers. Not my companion's, just mine. His fingers numb from the cold and the cheap red wine of the Po Valley, the *ingeniere* Carmone handed him my travel pass. The officer gave me a suspicious look, an expression he must have slipped on every morning before going to work and hung back up at night, unless he had been born with it.

"Are you a little *Francese*?"

I didn't like being called "French." I liked being called "little" even less.

"Little *Francese* yourself, *cazzino*."

The *carabiniere* nearly choked. *Cazzino* was the insult of choice in the back alleys where I'd grown up, and *carabinieri* didn't choose a line of work where they got to wear such handsome uniforms only to have someone insult the size of their manhood.

Like the good engineer he was, the *ingeniere* pulled my mother's envelope from his pocket and put some grease back into the gears that had seized up. Before long, we were on our way again. I refused to get into a carriage, pointing instead to a tram. Carmone grumbled, consulted a map, asked a few questions, and determined that the tram wouldn't drop us off too far from where we needed to go.

My backside planted on a wooden bench, I rode through the first major city of my life. I was happy. I had lost my father, and I had no idea when I would see my mother again, but yes, I was happy, drunk on everything that still lay ahead of me—this mass of future to scale, to carve to my measure.

"Say, Signor Carmone?"

"Yes?"

"What's electricity?"

He stared at me in bewilderment, seeming to recall that I had spent the first decade of my life in a Savoyard village I had never once left.

"That right there, my boy."

He pointed to a streetlamp, crowned with a beautiful golden globe.

"So it's like a candle, then?"

"But one that never goes out. It's electrons flowing between two pieces of carbon."

"What's an electron? Some kind of fairy?"

"No, it's science."

"What's science?"

The snowflakes swirled, light as a girl’s dress. *L’ingeniere* answered my questions without impatience or condescension. We soon passed a massive building under construction: the Lingotto, where, a few years later, Fiat cars would climb a spiral ramp to the roof to take their first spin after assembly—a mechanical Sacra di San Michele. The suburbs thinned out, paved roads gave way to dirt tracks, and the tram stopped in what looked like a field. We had to cover the last three kilometers on foot. I am grateful to that guy, Carmone, for accompanying me so far, despite the cold, despite the times. We were walking through the mud and I imagined that, already, my mother’s eyes were beginning to fade in his memory, to seem less violet to him. But he led me unfailingly to Zio Alberto’s door.

We had to abuse the bell and pound on the door several times before Alberto deigned to open it, wearing a dirty undershirt. He had the same cloudy eyes as *l’ingeniere*, cracked with tiny red veins: both men shared an inordinate love of the grape. My mother had written to announce my arrival, so there wasn’t much to explain.

"Here is your new apprentice, Michelangelo, Antonella Vitaliani’s son. Your nephew."

"I don't like being called Michelangelo."

Zio Alberto looked down at me. I thought he was going to ask me what I preferred to be called, to which I would have answered, "Mimo." The nickname my parents had always given me, the nickname I would go by for another seventy years.

"I don't want him," Alberto said.

Once again, I had forgotten a detail. Because yes, it is just a detail.

"I don't understand. I thought Antonell... I thought Mrs. Vitaliani had written to you, and that it was agreed."

"She wrote to me. But I don't want an apprentice like that."

"And why is that?"

"Because nobody told me he was a midget."

*C’è un piccolo problema*, old Rosa had commented, the neighbor who delivered me on a stormy night. The stove rattled, stoked by a contrary wind, and a hellish draft made the walls glow red. A few neighborhood matrons, who had come to witness the event, curious to catch a glimpse of the firm flesh that fueled their husbands’ fantasies, had long since fled, crossing themselves and muttering *il diavolo*. Old Rosa, undaunted, kept right on humming, sponging, and encouraging. Cholera, the cold, simple bad luck, a knife that wouldn't have been drawn if less had been drunk, had taken her children, friends, and husbands. She was old, she was ugly, she had nothing to lose. So the devil left her alone; he knew a source of trouble when he saw one. He had easier prey.

*C’è un piccolo problema*, then, she said as she pulled me from Antonella Vitaliani’s womb. Everything hinged on that word, *piccolo*; it was obvious to anyone who saw me that I would remain more or less *piccolo* my entire life. Rosa laid me on my exhausted mother. My father took the stairs four at a time; Rosa later recounted that he had frowned upon seeing me, looked around as if searching for something else—his real son rather than this rough draft—then nodded, *I see, that's how it is*, just as he did when he struck a hidden fissure in the heart of a block of stone, pulverizing several weeks’ work. You can't blame the stone.

Stone, appropriately enough, was blamed for my difference. My mother had not known when to rest, hauling enormous blocks to the workshop, putting the neighborhood muscle to shame. Poor Mimo, if the neighbor women were to be believed, had suffered for it. Achondroplasia, they would say later. They would describe me as a person of short stature, which, frankly, was no better than Zio Alberto’s "midget." They would explain to me that my height didn’t define me. If that were true, why bring up my height at all? I have never heard anyone speak of a "person of average height."

I never held it against my parents. If stone made me who I am, if some dark magic was at work, it also showered me with what it took from me. Stone always spoke to me—all stones: limestone, metamorphic, even tombstones, the ones I would soon lie upon to listen to the stories of recumbent effigies.

"This is not what was planned," the *ingeniere* murmured, tapping his lips with a gloved finger. "This is unfortunate."

It was snowing heavily now. Zio Alberto shrugged and tried to slam the door in our faces. The *ingeniere* blocked it with his foot. He pulled my mother’s envelope from the inside pocket of his old fur coat and held it out to my uncle. It held almost all the Vitaliani savings. Years of exile, of toil, of skin scorched by sun and salt, of starting over; years of marble under the fingernails, with occasionally an ounce of that tenderness that had brought me into the world. That was why those dirty, crumpled bills were precious. Why Zio Alberto opened the door a little wider.

"This sum was for the little one. I mean, Mimo," he corrected himself, blushing. "If Mimo agrees to give it to you, he would no longer be an apprentice, but a partner."

Zio Alberto nodded slowly.

"Hmm, a partner."

He was still hesitating. Carmone waited as long as possible, then sighed and pulled a leather pouch from his pack. Everything about the *ingeniere* celebrated wear, patching, an aesthetic of the passage of time. But the leather of the pouch—new, supple—seemed still to quiver with the wildness of the beast it had clothed. Carmone ran a cracked glove over it, opened it, and reluctantly took out a pipe.

"This is a pipe I acquired at great expense. Carved from a briar stump on which the Hero of the Two Worlds, the great Garibaldi himself, is said to have sat during his noble and fruitless attempt to rally Rome to our beautiful kingdom."

I had seen dozens of these pipes, sold in Aigues-Mortes to gullible Frenchmen. I didn't know how this one had ended up in Carmone’s hands, how he had let himself be taken in. I felt a little embarrassed for him, and for Italy in general. He was a naive, generous man. This gesture cost him, and I know he made it sincerely, to help me, not because he was in a hurry to get home or dreaded being saddled with a twelve-year-old boy of unusual proportions. Alberto accepted; they sealed the deal with a shot of rotgut whose sourness stung the air inside the hovel. Then Carmone stood up—one last little drink for the road—and soon his wavering silhouette receded into the snow.

He turned around one last time, his hand raised in the yellow phosphorescence of a dying world, and smiled at me. Abruzzo was far away, he was no longer young, and the times were harsh. Later on, I never went to Lake Scanno, for fear of discovering that there wasn't, and never had been, a tower mounted on ball bearings there.

I owe a great deal to so-called "fallen" women, and my uncle Alberto was the son of one. A courageous girl who lay beneath men down at the port of Genoa, without anger or shame. She was the only person my uncle spoke of with respect, a fervor bordering on veneration. But the saint of the back alleys was far away. And since Alberto could neither read nor write, with each passing day his mother became increasingly mythological. I, for my part, wrote rather well, a fact that delighted my uncle once he found out.

My uncle Alberto was not my uncle. We didn't share a single atom of blood. I never quite managed to get to the bottom of the business, but apparently his grandfather had owed a debt to mine, an unpaid loan whose moral burden had been passed down from generation to generation. In his own perverse way, Alberto was honest. At my mother's urging, he had agreed to take me in. He owned a small workshop on the outskirts of Turin. Since he was a bachelor and cared little for luxury, a few commissions here and there were enough to make ends meet, or at least they had been until I came along. For the war—an enterprise of progress touted by many hotheads at the time, who, incidentally, disliked the term "hothead" and preferred "poet" or "philosopher"—the war, then, had popularized materials that were cheaper than stone, lighter, and easier to produce and work. Steel was Alberto's worst enemy; he cursed it even in his sleep. He hated it even more than the Austro-Hungarians or the Germans. For a *Crucco*, as Krauts were called down there, you could at least find extenuating circumstances. Between their food and their ridiculous spiked helmets, they had plenty to be angry about. But whoever heard of building with steel, and he who laughed last would laugh longest once it all came crashing down. Alberto hadn't grasped that everything had already collapsed. To his credit, steel had played its part; it had made for fine cannons.

Alberto looked old, but he wasn't. At thirty-five, he lived alone in a room adjoining his workshop. His bachelorhood came as a surprise, especially since after a shower, scrubbed clean of marble dust and dressed in his only suit, he wasn't half bad. He always frequented the same brothel in Turin, where he treated the girls with legendary respect. The expression "doing it like Alberto" was popular in the early twenties in the southern neighborhoods of the city, between Lingotto and San Salvario, before dying out when Alberto moved away, taking his marble and his slave—namely, me—with him. His partner—I still laugh at the thought.

People have often asked me what role he played in what came next. If by "what came next" they mean my career, none at all. If, on the other hand, they mean my latest work, a few shards of him are no doubt embedded in it. No, not shards—fragments. I wouldn't want anyone to suspect him of ever having shone. Zio Alberto was a bastard. Not a monster, just a pathetic nobody, which amounts to the same thing. I look back on him without hatred, but without sadness either.

For almost a year, I lived in this man’s shadow. I cooked, I cleaned. I hauled, I delivered. A hundred times I was nearly crushed by a tram, run down by a horse, or beaten up by some guy who had mocked my height and to whom I had replied that at least my size wasn’t a problem *al piano di sotto*, downstairs, preferably in front of his girlfriend. L’ingeniere Carmone would have been delighted to find the atmosphere in our neighborhood so electric. Every interaction was a potential lightning strike, a displacement of electrons that might trigger who knew what. We were at war with the Germans, the Austro-Hungarians, our governments, our neighbors—which was a way of saying we were at war with ourselves. One wanted war, the other peace; voices would be raised, and the one who wanted peace would end up throwing the first punch.

Zio Alberto forbade me to touch his tools. He once caught me fixing up a small holy water font commissioned by the neighboring parish of Beata Vergine delle Grazie. Alberto went on a monumental bender once or twice a week, and his latest had left its mark. The font was crude, insulting; a twelve-year-old kid could have done better, and he did just that while the other slept off his wine. Alberto woke up and caught me red-handed, chisel in hand. He examined my work in bewilderment, then rained blows upon me, cursing me in a language I didn’t understand, a Genoese dialect. Then he went back to sleep. When he opened his eyes again and found me battered and covered in bruises, he pretended not to know what had happened. He went straight to his holy water font, remarked that he wasn’t displeased with his work, and magnanimously offered to deliver it himself.

Alberto regularly dictated a letter to his mother and allowed me to write one to mine at the same time—he generously paid for the stamp. Antonella did not always reply; she was constantly on the road, chasing down the job that would let her get by for another week, and then another. I missed her violet eyes. My father—the man who had guided my first clumsy strikes, the man who had taught me the difference between a tooth chisel, a riffler, and a pitching tool—was fading away.

Work grew scarce over the course of 1917, Alberto’s moods darker, his benders more violent. Columns of soldiers sometimes marched past against the twilight; the newspapers spoke of nothing else—the war, the war—yet all we felt of it was a vague unease, a sense of dissociation from our surroundings, a feeling of never quite being in the right place. Out there, a foul beast was brutalizing the horizon. But we led an almost normal existence, a shirker’s life that lent a faint taste of guilt to everything we ate. At least until August 22, when the bread ran out and there was nothing left to eat at all. Turin exploded. The name Lenin appeared on the city walls, barricades went up, and a revolutionary even stopped me in the street on the morning of the 24th to warn me to be careful, that their barricades were electrified—which told me more surely than anything else that the world was changing. The guy called me “comrade” and clapped me on the back. I saw women confronting sheepish soldiers at the barricades, scaling armored cars, baring furious, conquering breasts that they dared not shoot at. At least, not right away.

The revolt lasted three days. No one could agree on anything, except for the fact that they’d had enough of the war. The government finally brought everyone into agreement with machine guns; fifty dead cooled their ardor. I holed up in the workshop. One evening—calm had barely returned, and a little bread along with it—Zio Alberto came home in a more cheerful mood than usual. He pretended to take a swipe at me, chuckled when he saw me dive under the table, then ordered me to take up a pen and dictated a letter to his mother. He smelled of the cheap wine they served on the corner.

Mammina,

I received the money order you sent me. Thanks to it, I’m going to be able to buy the little workshop I told you about at Christmas. It’s in Liguria, so closer to you. There’s no work left in Turin. But down there, they have a castle that’s always needing repairs, and a church the authorities they care a lot about, so that means work. I sold up here, not for much but oh well, I just signed with that rat Lorenzo, and I’m leaving soon with that little shit Mimo. I’ll write to you from Pietra d’Alba, your son who loves you.

“And give me a beautiful signature, *pezzo di merda*,” Zio Alberto concluded. “One that shows I’ve made it.”

When I think back on that time, it’s strange: I wasn’t unhappy. I was alone, I had nothing and no one; up in northern Europe they were churning up forests, sowing them with flesh laced with metal, along with a few shells that would explode years later in the faces of innocent walkers; they were inventing a desolation that would make Mercalli pale, he who had given only twelve degrees to his poor scale. But I wasn’t unhappy; I realized this every evening, when I prayed to a personal pantheon of idols that changed throughout my life and even included, later on, opera singers and football players. Perhaps because I was young, my days were beautiful. Only today do I measure what the beauty of the day owes to the prescience of the night.

The abbot leaves his office and begins his descent down the aptly named Staircase of the Dead. In a few moments, he will go to the bedside of the man dying in the annex. The brothers sent word to him that the hour was near. He will place the bread of life upon his lips.

Padre Vincenzo walks through the church without paying attention to its frescoes, passes through the Portal of the Zodiac, and emerges onto the terraces at the summit of Mount Pirchiriano, from which the abbey looks down on Piedmont. Before him, the ruins of a tower. Legend has it that a young peasant girl, the beautiful Alda, once took flight from it to escape enemy soldiers, aided by Saint Michael. *Vanitas vanitatis*, she wanted to repeat the feat in front of the villagers, just to impress them, and crashed to the ground below. Just as a part of the tower bearing her name would do in the fourteenth century, brought down by one of the many earthquakes that regularly shake the region.

Further on, a few steps sink into the ground, blocked by a chain and a "No Entry" sign. The abbot steps over it with an agility commendable for his age. This is not the way to the annex where the dying man awaits him. Before joining him, the priest wants to see *her*. She who sometimes brings him uneasy sleep, for he dreads an intrusion, or worse. You never know what might happen, like that time, fifteen years ago, when Fra Bartolomeo had surprised someone right in front of the last gate protecting her. The man, an American, had tried to pass himself off as a lost visitor. The abbot had immediately sniffed out the lie; he knew its scent by heart, it was the smell of the confessionals. No tourist could descend so deeply into the foundations of the Sacra di San Michele by accident. No, the man was there because he had heard the rumor.

The abbot had been right. Five years later, the same man had returned with proper authorization signed by a Vatican bigwig. So they had opened the door for him, and the list of those who had gazed upon her had grown a little longer. Leonard B. Williams, that was the name of this professor from Stanford University in California. Williams had devoted his life to the captive of the Sacra, trying to unravel her mystery. He had published a monograph on her, a few articles, and then, silence. His work, however brilliant, lay dormant on forgotten shelves. The Vatican had played its hand well, opening that door as if they had nothing to hide. For years, calm had returned. But for the past few months, the monks had been reporting tourists who were nothing of the sort—snoopers. You could spot them a mile away. The pressure was mounting again.

For long minutes, the abbot descends, navigating unfailingly through the maze of corridors. He could find his way in the dark, he has walked it so many times. A jingling of bells accompanies him—the sound of the key ring in his hand. These blasted keys. There is one for every door in the abbey, sometimes two, as if a mystery throbbed behind the slightest panel. You would think the mystery that gathers them there, the Eucharist, is not enough.

He is almost there. Smells the earth, the damp, the scent of billions of granite atoms crushed by their own weight, and even a little of that greenness from the surrounding slopes. The gate, at last. The old one has been replaced; it is now fitted with a five-point lock. The remote control doesn't work on the first try, Padre Vincenzo jabs furiously at its rubber buttons, it's the same every time, talk about progress, it's 1986 and we can't produce a remote control that works? He catches himself, Lord, forgive my impatience.

The red light finally goes out; the alarm is deactivated. The final corridor is monitored by two state-of-the-art cameras, no bigger than shoeboxes. It is impossible to enter without alerting someone. And even if an intruder managed it, what would be the point? He wouldn't leave with her. It had taken ten men to carry her down.

Padre Vincenzo shudders. Theft is not what they have to fear. He has not forgotten that lunatic, Laszlo Toth. He chides himself again, "lunatic" isn't charitable, let's say "unbalanced." They had narrowly avoided tragedy. But he doesn't want to think about Laszlo right now, about the Hungarian's sinister face and fanatical eyes. Tragedy had been averted.

They lock her away to protect her. The irony does not escape the abbot. She is there, don't worry, she is doing wonderfully, except that no one is allowed to see her. No one but him, the *padre*, the monks who request to do so, the few cardinals who locked her away forty years ago and are still alive, and probably a few bureaucrats too. Some thirty people in the world, at most. And, of course, her creator, who had his own key. He came as he pleased to tend to her and wash her regularly. For, yes, she needs to be washed.

The abbot unlocks the last two locks. He always starts with the top one, a tic that perhaps betrays a form of nervousness. He would like to be rid of it and promises himself—as he did during his previous visit—to start with the bottom one next time. The door opens silently; the locksmith who touted the quality of the hinges to them did not lie.

He does not turn on the light. The original neon lights were replaced at the same time as the grating with softer lighting, and just as well, as those neons brutalized her. But he prefers to see her in the dark. The abbot steps forward, touching her with his fingertips out of habit. She is slightly taller than he is. In the center of a round room, a primitive sanctuary with Romanesque vaults, she stands slightly bowed on her pedestal, lost in a dream of stone. The only light comes from the corridor, outlining two faces, the break of a wrist. The abbot knows every detail of the sleeping statue in the shadows, from having scrutinized her until his eyes ached. 

They lock her away to protect her.
The abbot suspects that those who put her there were trying to protect themselves.

The city of Savona had given Italy two popes, Sixtus IV and Julius II. Pietra d’Alba, barely thirty kilometers to the north, almost gave it a third. I believe I am somewhat responsible for this failure.

I would have laughed heartily had anyone told me, on that morning of December 10, 1917, that the history of the papacy would be altered by the kid dragging his feet behind Zio Alberto. We had traveled for three days, almost non-stop. The entire country was hanging on the news from the front, after the thrashing the Austro-Hungarians had inflicted upon us at Caporetto. Positions were said to have stabilized not far from Venice. They also said the opposite: that the enemy was going to show up and slit our throats in our sleep, or, worse, force us to eat cabbage.

Pietra d’Alba appeared, carved into the morning light upon its rocky peak. Its position, as I realized an hour later, was an illusion. Pietra was not perched on a spur but resting on the edge of a plateau. Truly on the edge—that is to say, between the village's outer wall and the cliff edge, there was a passage barely wide enough for two men abreast. Then fifty meters of empty space, or more precisely of pure air, heavy with the scent of resin and thyme.

You had to cross the entire village to discover what had made its reputation: an immense plateau rolling toward Piedmont, a piece of Tuscany displaced there by the whims of geology. To the west as to the east, Liguria kept watch, reminding it not to get too comfortable. This was the mountain: slopes covered in a forest of a green almost as black as the beasts that prowled there. Pietra d’Alba was beautiful with its somewhat pink stone—thousands of dawns were embedded in it.
