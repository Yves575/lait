Fame is my life. The one I knew I’d have, the one I made sure I got. Was I prepared for such success? Of course I was. I always believed that what awaited me wasn’t a mere existence, but a destiny. My path would be exceptional, my trajectory extraordinary.

When people talk about fame, they think of the glitz, the glamour, the money, the devoted fans, the prestige, the recognition. But they should also mention the constant sense of superiority, the intoxication of wealth, the endless commentary, the vanity, the hypocrisy, the impunity. Fame is a hard drug, a ferocious monster. And I went after it with my rage, with my nails, with my teeth.

Extreme fame unleashed the beast inside me, ruthless and cruel. I might as well say it right from the start: I got my hands dirty. At my level, everyone has skeletons in their closet. Anyone who claims otherwise is a liar. Fame is a spoil of war—no one is ever willing to give it up.

At thirty-two, I stand at the very top of a castle I built single-handedly with my songs. I don’t believe in luck. I don’t believe in a network of influential friends. I don’t believe in the glass ceiling. I owe my success solely to my talent, my character, and meritocracy. So, had I been able to be honest on the night of my last awards ceremony, I would have thanked only one person during my long speech: myself.

Three weeks on a deserted island in the middle of the Pacific Ocean. A place with no water or electricity, with absolutely no contact with the outside world. It’s the kind of fantasy that fame can buy. When you have everything, you have to use a little imagination for your summer vacation.

Yesterday, I spent ten hours in a private jet. We landed near Tahiti or Fiji, or maybe it was Hawaii. I boarded a second plane, then a seaplane. An hour later, the island appeared: an atoll lost in the vastness.

Ocean as far as the eye can see, white sand, a spectacular lagoon—I am walking through a postcard. Flocks of seabirds stop over on the rocks, the nature is lush, you can guess the frequent downpours, the cistern that collects rainwater is full. Coconut palms, banana trees, orange trees; I'm in no danger of starving. Eyes towards the open sea, I search for land on the horizon, an anchor point to fix my gaze on. There is none.

My only shelter for the next three weeks is a cabin on the beach. The structure is charming, built on stilts, with a deck facing the ocean. The high, pitched wooden frame supports a roof covered in woven tropical leaves. Inside, a single room with sparse furnishings: a single bed, a dresser, a table, two chairs. The provisions are stashed in a cupboard: rice, fruit, dried fish, tubers, canned goods, a hundred liters of mineral water. I open the drawers to finish my inventory, gathering my meager loot on the floor: a camping stove, two gas canisters, flippers, a snorkeling mask, a flashlight, a machete, a box of matches, a mosquito net, a water purifier, a fishing net, a globe, a Bible. Nothing on the walls, not the slightest decoration, no clock, no mirror. Rustic for a $500,000 vacation. But it’s no secret: the more expensive it is, the less Wi-Fi you get. I am paying top dollar to be out in the middle of nowhere, inaccessible to prying eyes, cell phones, paparazzi lenses—and the endless demands of my team. This year, I am treating myself to the greatest gift of all: being left the hell alone.

I first heard about this island six months ago. That evening, I make a highly anticipated appearance at the party that closes an awards ceremony where I’ve once again swept the board and delivered a string of acceptance speeches. A little earlier, trophy in hand, I paid tribute to my fans four times, with a catch in my voice and tears in my eyes. That’s the first thing you have to learn: crying on cue. We all know the drill. You have to act moved and overwhelmed, be humble in victory, explain that you make music for your fans, and thank the behind-the-scenes teams by reciting a long list of names that mean absolutely nothing to anyone.

Natalie Holmes is standing near the bar at the after-party, a glass of champagne in hand. I haven’t run into her in over a year, and it’s been an eventful year: a breakup, betrayal, media turmoil, drama, and the triumphant release of my third album. Natalie Holmes and I used to have a handful of mutual friends, but those friends turned into enemies, so I’m willing to bet she’ll pretend she hasn’t seen me, keeping her eyes glued to her Jimmy Choo pumps. Wrong. She walks over with a huge smile. It’s crazy how success makes people forget their grudges. My new assistant whispers in my ear the title of the latest movie she starred in, the director’s name, and the release date. I don’t mind making small talk, but I need some material to work with.

Natalie starts by congratulating me on my album (an unoriginal opening line) and on the four trophies I won tonight (how trite), then confides that she often thinks back to our conversations at John Cutler’s place in Los Angeles (I have zero memory of them). I have to say, her dress is really squashing her arms; I love strapless gowns, but they aren’t flattering on everyone (what the hell was her stylist thinking?). Jaw clenched to keep from yawning, I wish I were anywhere but here, chatting with an insipid actress. I am on the verge of politely excusing myself when the conversation takes an unexpected turn. Natalie Holmes places a conspiratorial hand on my wrist and starts telling me about her recent vacation.

“It wasn’t really a vacation, more of an experience,” she murmurs.

“What do you mean?”

“I’m not allowed to say more, but I swear it changed my life.”

“That much?”

“You have no idea. I wasn’t expecting to be so overwhelmed… I can give you the number if you want.”

“Sure, why not.”

“But I can’t guarantee anything. It’s strictly word-of-mouth, and spots are extremely limited. I heard Selena Gomez has been waiting months to get in.”

If I’m to believe Natalie Holmes, the entire Hollywood A-list is secretly flocking to this “top secret” and “highly exclusive” place. Of course, that’s exactly what you have to say to make me want in. No mega-yacht, no villa in the Bahamas, no castle in Tuscany, but three weeks on a private island in the Pacific. “A Robinson Crusoe adventure,” “a spiritual retreat,” “an end-of-the-world experience,” “a place so remote that nothing else exists.” I have a hard time picturing Natalie Holmes spending the summer without air conditioning and spearfishing, but hey, why not. “That’s where Christopher Nolan got the idea for his film *Interstellar*”; “Taylor Swift unpacks her bags there once a year to recharge and find inspiration for new songs”; “Rumor has it the island belonged to F. Scott Fitzgerald, and he supposedly wrote the first chapters of *The Great Gatsby* there”—I have my doubts about that, as to my knowledge the American author never made a fortune, but Natalie Holmes knows the right arguments to win me over: Fitzgerald is my favorite writer. She adds, with stars in her eyes and a smile heavy with innuendo:

"Insiders call it 'Masterpiece Island'… Word will get out eventually, but for now the place is still unspoiled, so you have to make the most of it. It’s absolutely magical."

"If you say so."

I am stung. I ran into Selena and Taylor yesterday; how is it they didn’t mention it to me? And besides, why does Natalie Holmes, an actress who possesses neither my aura nor my influence, know about it before I do? As she walks away, she actually makes me promise to keep my mouth shut; she signed an ironclad non-disclosure agreement. Location kept secret, zero information on the Internet, ultra-selective invitation only. Mystery breeds desire.

One evening of extreme exhaustion, I dial the number on a whim. Right in the midst of promoting my third album, I’ve been hounded all day by idiots, none of whom was capable of following my orders properly. Everyone around me is either incompetent or lazy, and the week is drawing to a close in a climate of terror. They say music soothes the soul—don’t make me laugh. The pace and the pressure make me uncompromising. Alone in a Las Vegas hotel room, I scrutinize myself in the mirror while waiting for someone to pick up, in vain. I leave a message on an enigmatic voicemail that quotes a Bible verse.

Fine sand, turquoise water, absolute calm. I swim for a long time, slowly, breaststroke, backstroke. I think about the ocean currents that could sweep me out to sea, the undertows near the coral reef, the fish whose dorsal fins contain a deadly venom. The thrill is part of the pleasure.

After my swim, I untie my swimsuit under the outdoor shower, a spartan setup at the back of the cabana. The sun-warmed rainwater is pleasantly warm, the soap lathers against my skin, and I let out a sigh of bliss. For once, no paparazzo is hiding behind a rock to photograph my breasts. I don’t even need to suck my stomach in.

Building a sandcastle occupies the rest of my day. Just like when I was a child on the beaches of the Île d’Oléron, I pack the wet sand, dig moats, reinforce my fortress, peaceful and focused, before setting off to find seashells to decorate my towers. A frivolous, aimless activity, just for me. The pleasure of applying myself without trying to excel. Is it 2:00 p.m. or 5:00 p.m.? I have no idea. Mechanically, I slip my hand into my pocket to grab my phone. It will take me several days to suppress this reflex.

The day before my departure, a man in a white polo shirt ceremoniously searched my bag. Bringing a phone, computer, camera, or any electronic device was out of the question. He also confiscated my watch. The watchword: disconnection. Being unreachable. Dropping off the grid. No calls from my assistant, no social media, no obligations. Nothing but silence and serenity.

The only exception to my self-imposed isolation: an emergency satellite phone, black with an antenna, stowed in the closet next to the first-aid kit. I have no trouble imagining celebrities treating themselves to this singular experience and cracking after forty-eight hours. Many must ask to go home early, terrified by a nasty spider or struck by terminal boredom. For I have no doubt that these three weeks will also be a test. Solitude is another kind of hell.

But for now, I am in paradise. For several months now, I haven’t yearned for another trophy; I’ve been dreaming of a break. My entourage notices that I have become irritable, impatient, insufferable. They don't realize; no one can understand what I am going through. The only image that comes close is: I am in a washing machine and the cycle has been running for seven years.

I will never admit it publicly, but I am exhausted. I was ready to wage any war to become famous, never imagining I would have to give so much just to stay that way.

The sun is setting over the ocean for the first time. My suitcase lies open at the foot of my bed; the fire I built with driftwood crackles on the beach. Guitar on my lap, I mumble the opening words of a verse, a notebook within easy reach. This idyllic retreat will certainly provide me with material for my fourth album—I may have isolated myself on an island, but I haven't taken a vow of silence. As night falls, the sea surrenders entirely to the sky; I discover thousands of constellations whose geometry I do not recognize. I am somewhere in the Southern Hemisphere.

The flames cast jagged shadows across the sand. I shiver as I use my fingertips to spin the globe I found in the cabin. It dates back to the second half of the twentieth century; it features vanished countries and acronyms from another era, and I search it for borders that do not yet exist. I run my index finger along the latitudes and meridians, trying to pinpoint my location on the sphere. In all likelihood, the island isn't even on it. I am nothing but a speck in the vastness of the Pacific, but where exactly? The outlines of nations take shape before my eyes; the nearest continental shores are probably over four thousand kilometers away, somewhere in South America or Oceania.

What am I doing here? And for that matter, how did I end up here? To calm the rising sense of vertigo, I stroke the scars on my thighs, my head, my hands, my arms, my ankles. They all trace my life’s path, serving as so many milestones on the road to glory.

There is a price to pay for fame, and it is paid every day.

Part One

Faith

My name is Cléo Louvent Johnson. My mother is French, my father American; I navigate between the two languages with ease. My mother is a statistician, my father is an Egyptologist; I forge my identity between numbers and hieroglyphs. I grow up in an apartment filled with books in a quiet corner of the 14th arrondissement of Paris. My father, who affectionately calls me Cleopatra, uses magnets to hang my drawings of pyramids and sphinxes in the kitchen. On Saturdays, my mother takes me to the neighborhood library; on the way back, we stop to say hello to the grey herons in Parc Montsouris.

I am four years old. My father is telling me a story in my bedroom. My favorite books are the ones he brings to life with his commentary. A specialist in ancient Egypt with a passion for Antiquity in all its forms, he recounts the woes of Isis and Osiris and their battle against Seth, but also the wanderings of Odysseus and the childish antics of the Olympian gods. I hang on his every word as he details the exploits of Ramses II, Pericles, and Alexander the Great. Every tale serves as an excuse to detour through History and its grand epics, teeming with characters on extraordinary quests and marvelous beasts. Soon it is time for sleep; my father is tucking me in before turning out the light, when suddenly he notices I seem troubled. When he gently presses me, I finally confess: "Daddy, I want to be as famous as Céline Dion."

I have no memory of this myself, but for a long time, my parents delighted in retelling this anecdote, which always made them laugh heartily. I can picture their tenderness mingled with surprise: *Where did she get that from?*

My parents live in the microcosm of their academic research, their publications, and their conferences. They have no idea who the trending celebrities are—they would be completely incapable of recognizing the idols of their own century if they crossed paths with them on the street. Red carpets, celebrity couples, Hollywood, private jets, celebrity magazines: it is an exotic land where they have absolutely no desire to spend their holidays.

At the school gates, my parents look older than the others. My mother explains to me that her pregnancy didn't last nine months, but nine years. I will be their only child. Their difficulty conceiving is no secret; my Wikipedia page states that I was born via in vitro fertilization. For some obscure reason, this information adds to the magic surrounding my birth: did science really have to get involved to create a being as exceptional as me?

I am a well-behaved, solitary little girl with short hair, who slips plane tree leaves into her coat pockets to make salad for her dolls. My parents hear me talking to myself in my bedroom, and notice that I always close the door to play.

Little do they suspect that their darling child spends her Wednesday afternoons filling notebooks in search of a star signature. At seven years old, I practice signing batches of autographs as fast as possible. The following year, I take part in my first interviews. The questions come to me naturally; I methodically craft my answers, in French, in English. At my feet, my stuffed hedgehog, whale, ladybug, and squirrel listen to me attentively. My super menagerie forms a captive audience, the best I could ever dream of.

I also sing in unison with my favorite stars, playing their music on the CD player resting on the floor near my desk. I know all the lyrics by heart; I read them from the CD insert, sitting cross-legged on my bedroom's light blue rug. As a teenager, the website "paroles.net" and its thousands of cataloged songs would take over. I would spend hours there, captivated.

I have explained my journey to journalists so many times. I have recounted my past in interviews so many times. I have participated in this dangerous game so many times without grasping its gravity.

I romanticized my life, embellished my answers to make myself seem more brilliant, more impressive. I erased from my speech, and little by little from my memory, the episodes that didn't fit my legend. I fabricated false memories. In search of a juicy detail or a moving anecdote, I cherry-picked from other people's pasts, from my reading, from films, until I forgot what truly belonged to me.

Memories are fragile; to be convinced of this, you only have to recount the same event several times. By the third or fourth time, the past begins to reconstruct itself. Gradually, you alter the lighting, you remove certain details, you heighten the dramatic effect to captivate your audience. You turn the past into a story, and with each retelling, it is the memory itself that gets damaged. There are anecdotes I have repeated so many times that I no longer know if they are true or false.

It is tempting to rewrite your life; I have done it a lot. The early signs of my destiny are legion: the precocious interviews with my stuffed animals, my extraordinary mezzo-soprano voice, my fascination with song lyrics, my musical ear, my natural aptitude for the piano, the encouragement of my guitar teacher, my solid foundation in music theory, my passion for choir. It is so much easier to trace the path in reverse.

I start music at seven, but make no mistake. Where I come from, the conservatory is the extracurricular activity of good students. My parents have no ambition to turn me into a celebrity by encouraging me to learn an instrument—we are not in Los Angeles. In my home, music is a bourgeois reflex, not an encouragement to become a global pop star.

My desire for fame stems not from my family, but from my vulnerabilities. All my life, I will seek the admiration of millions of strangers, command the respect of everyone who has crossed my path. The baker, the pharmacist, my kindergarten teacher, the neighbor on the third floor. I have only one aim: for everyone to acknowledge my superiority.

A lot is said about the mental health issues of celebrities. How many become alcoholics, battle addictions, sink into depression, commit suicide? Fame drives you crazy—and you have to be crazy to want to be famous. Public life is a honeypot that attracts narcissists, perverts, and sociopaths. Well-adjusted people don’t need to vampirize love on such a massive scale.

I think back to that little four-year-old girl telling her father how sad she was not to be famous. In that thirst to be admired, praised, and applauded, there was a need that had always exceeded normal bounds.

“When I first met Cléo Louvent, I knew right away she was going to be famous.” In articles about me, you can always find a former teacher or classmate claiming my potential was obvious to everyone. In reality, no one would have bet on me. I am an unremarkable child, not particularly pretty, and shy.

On my early report cards, my grades are excellent, but I am encouraged to speak up more in class. I hide behind my good marks—which I owe to my anxious perfectionism. The second I have to cross something out in my schoolbook, I rip out the page and start over.

At eight years old, I skip a grade and end up seated next to the beautiful Juliette Marchand. My new best friend is the exact opposite of me: popular, chatty, loud. Her confidence impresses me. My mother thinks she has an unfortunate tendency to make a spectacle of herself; my father harbors the same aversion toward people who talk loudly or try to be the center of attention. If I behaved like Juliette at home, I would be told sharply: *stop showing off*.

Juliette divides her affection among Lydia, Clémentine, and me. In other words, I am one of her ladies-in-waiting, and at every recess I live in fear of no longer being her favorite. When Juliette holds forth on the importance of kissing a boy before starting middle school, I nod silently behind her. She takes to calling me Cléou; I hate it, but once again, I say nothing.

One Monday in October, Juliette decides to organize a fashion show with several girls from our class. The event is to take place at her house the following Sunday, in front of her parents and their friends. We spend the week talking about it in the schoolyard. At ten years old, I swear nothing is more important.

The big day arrives. My father drops me off at Juliette’s, my nicest clothes folded in my backpack. To secure my spot in the fashion show, I had pretended to own a pair of bell-bottoms; once I get there, I make out like I forgot them. In the bathroom, I overhear Lydia saying that I’m a filthy little liar: those bell-bottoms don’t exist. Clémentine chimes in, adding that I have a deep voice like a boy and hairy thighs. Juliette defends me firmly: the queen protects her favorites and decrees peace. I choke back my tears on the other side of the door.

Juliette orchestrates the event, chooses the running order, and cues the music. Her bedroom serves as backstage; we get changed in a flash, allowed four trips down the runway each. She opens the show in a ruffled skirt and an orange tank top, her blond hair flowing down her back. Half an hour later, I am breathless with jealousy when she closes the show in a midnight-blue dress with spaghetti straps. Juliette walks confidently to the applause, twirls around, and flashes winks at the captivated adults. On Monday at recess, I steal her snack and spit in her schoolbag.

To become famous, you must accumulate a sufficient number of grudges, boil with frustration for months, simmer for decades. The years spent in my best friend’s shadow are the ones that will give me the endurance necessary to withstand those spent in the spotlight. It is no coincidence that, at the dawn of my fame, I decide to dye my hair blonde like Juliette. I am out for revenge, and it isn’t just about hair.

At night in bed, I resent my parents for giving me this overly short, ridiculous name. Why Cléo? I would have preferred to be called Juliette, Mathilde, Émeline, or Éléonore. A girl’s name, a princess’s name—dainty, important (three syllables minimum). I am jealous and petty; I hope no one shows up to Juliette’s birthday party. Or better yet, that she tells me her mother has died. And then I picture my reprisals, caressing them with my fingertips. When I reach the top, I won't spare a glance for those who underestimated me. I will no longer settle for spitting in their bags; I will spit in their faces, I will reduce them to ashes.

I wish my parents had explained it to me sooner: the children who draw every eye rarely go on to have the most dazzling adult lives, the most tight-knit families, the most impressive careers. The primary school star never becomes the global superstar. I have since had the opportunity to observe this dozens of times: fame is not a victory, it is revenge.

I still have very vivid images of the summer I turned fourteen: bike rides through the pines with my cousins, afternoon snacks on the beach, a rollerblading race. I accompany my aunt to buy oysters at the market, I take sailing lessons, I pluck my eyebrows in secret. My diary is hidden in my underwear drawer; I suspect my cousin Violette of reading it while I am in the shower, taking a sadistic pleasure in quoting excerpts from it during our conversations; doubt creeps into my eyes, and she gloats. At least, I think so.

My mother’s sister owns a vacation home on the Île d’Oléron; I have always spent a part of my holidays there. The rest of the time, I stay at my grandparents' in Aix-en-Provence, I camp in the Auvergne with my cousins and my uncle, or I fly to Boston to visit my father’s family. That summer, my father joins us in Oléron for a week. One afternoon, out of curiosity, I sit down next to him on the couch while he watches the Olympic Games on television.

In front of the screen, I am at first stirred by a burning intuition, almost a rebellion: I hate team sports. Handball, volleyball, basketball: making your victory depend on someone other than yourself seems insane to me. How could you not want to burn your teammate alive in the event of a poor performance?

Swimming seems to me a more reasonable discipline (except for relays, obviously). Yesterday, a seventeen-year-old French girl caused an upset by winning the 400-meter freestyle. Our new national star is only three years older than me, and this morning at the newsstand she is on every front page. In her interviews, I discover, fascinated, the years of sacrifice, waking up at dawn, the sixteen kilometers of swimming a day, the smell of chlorine that clings to the skin to the point of nausea, the relentless work to shave off a hundredth of a second. It all rings true: the strength of character, the pain tolerance, the need to win. In it, I recognize the jagged contours of my own ambition.

I, too, have something to prove. I, too, am determined to do ten times more than anyone else. I, too, am ready for a fight. I, too, am a perfectionist with an enormous capacity for work. I, too, want to live in a simplified world where the best person wins. It’s not for nothing that I am always top of my class; I have never given up my spot to anyone. I am also the most gifted at the conservatory, the best in my piano, guitar, and singing classes. If the goal is ten, I do a hundred. An *overachiever*, as my dad says. Overperform or nothing.

Yet, I can’t explain this quirk. Why am I so desperate to become famous? To feel special, powerful, loved? Sure, I think I’m different from everyone else, out of sync, misunderstood, but isn’t that the most universal feeling in the world when you're growing up? So how should I interpret this rage? Did my parents not pay enough attention to me? Or, on the contrary, is it the result of transferred ambition onto a long-awaited, pampered only child? Is my thirst for fame the result of an innate disposition, a learned experience, neglect, bullying, a legacy? I don’t know. I have no root cause to point to, no psychological, familial, sociological, astrological, mystical, or existential explanation. I can’t explain it, yet I burn. I grew up in the nice neighborhoods of Paris, but I’m a junkyard dog.

"Swimming is a dog of a sport," my father rightly observes at the starting gun of the 100-meter backstroke.

A minute later, the French swimmer wins her second gold medal.

"Dad, do you think she knew she was going to win?"

"Of course. You have to have big dreams to experience great things."

In September, I plaster the faces of my new idols on my bedroom walls; they are swimmers, boxers, fencers. I have found my tribe; it is made up of determined, disciplined women who are like me. As a role model, Céline Dion had never quite cut it—plus, Juliette thinks she’s tacky. I still aspire to become a singer, but I don’t recognize myself in the rhetoric of my favorite artists. In the creative world, it is more about passion, inspiration, and dissolute lives. I want to be a music star because I want to be a machine. And my ascent will also come through my body.

In addition to my music lessons, I sign up for tennis with Juliette, and for dance. I learn a love for movement, for repetition, for performance. In the evenings, I do core workouts; in the mornings, I step on the scale. I scrupulously weigh my food, count calories, check my protein intake. Every week, I plot my weight on a graph drawn on graph paper. As soon as I put 500 grams back on, I run to the point of exhaustion.

Even the best athletes need recovery time. So I rest on Friday evenings by turning on my computer. My favorite game is called The Sims.

Charlotte, my current avatar, plays the guitar and the piano. She has a social life, a fiancé, a Dalmatian. Yesterday, she was robbed and spent her evening fixing a water leak in her bathroom. To develop her charisma, Charlotte talks to the mirror for hours. I also put her through workout sessions, a dietary overhaul, and frequent makeovers. Because I don’t operate blindly, aimlessly, or just for fun. I have a goal in mind: to make her a star. To have any hope of succeeding, she absolutely must become friends with other famous people and always be in a good mood.

Charlotte works hard. She makes the rounds of auditions, concerts, and photo shoots. I could have chosen another career; around her, her friends are acrobats, bank robbers, dog trainers, secret agents, fighter pilots. But I won't be satisfied until Charlotte is finally famous. I want a limo to pick her up outside her house, for strangers to ask for her autograph in town. Only then will I buy her a more comfortable bed, masterpieces for her bedroom, a shower stall with brand-new silicone caulking, a new oven.

As long as I stay at the top of my class, my parents don't forbid me from spending so much time on my computer, but that doesn't mean they approve. "Don't you want to pick up a book instead?"

My game lasts for weeks. Despite her best efforts, my avatar isn't getting famous. Failure is not an option. It never will be. I lead Charlotte into the garden and build four walls around her. She will die like a medieval recluse: walled in.

Juliette has been arguing with her boyfriend in the bathroom for an hour. Tonight, we're celebrating my birthday at her dad's apartment on Rue Princesse. The whole high school knows my best friend throws the best parties. A little earlier, we baked a chocolate cake and asked her older brother to buy us some booze.

While Juliette hashes out her relationship drama, I chat with Nathan, a friend of her brother's who goes to Henri-IV. He's shy and blushes easily; this is the first time we've exchanged more than three sentences.

"Nathan is short for Nathanaël. It means 'gift of God' in Hebrew. Do you know where Cléo comes from?"

Etymology as a pickup line—no one had ever tried that on me before. No doubt about it, I'm at a party in Saint-Germain-des-Prés. And while he's talking to me, I smile to myself because Nathan reminds me of a stork. He has long, skinny legs and a tiny head.

"Cléo is a nod to Cleopatra."

"Oh right, your dad is an Egypt specialist, isn't he?"

"Yeah, he wanted to name me Cleopatra. My mom vetoed it, so they compromised on Cléo. But anyway, he calls me Cleopatra all the time."

"Cleopatra is pretty. It must mean something."

"Yeah, it means 'the father's pride,' I think—at least, that's what my dad is always telling me. *Patros* means 'father' in Greek."

"But Cléo, do you know if it means anything on its own? The pride?"

"I've never really looked into it."

I look it up on my phone. The signal is bad, so we keep chatting while it loads. Nathan pours me another drink, a mix of vodka and orange juice. Right then, Juliette comes out of the bathroom, her eyes red; when I'd walked past the door earlier, I'd heard yelling and crying. How can she waste so much time on that guy? Doesn't she have anything better to do?

In the living room, girls are dancing to the latest hit. There are the hot, half-bare bodies, the lyrics belted out in unison at the chorus, the conversations in the dark, the smell of beer and weed, the dirty glasses piling up in the sink, the crumbs on the chevron parquet floor, Nathan's gray eyes. The answer appears on my phone screen, and I put my hand on his shoulder so I don't fall over, I'm in such disbelief. In Greek, Cléo comes from *kléos*, which means "glory." Renown. Fame.

My life is an adventure movie. On the night of her fifteenth birthday, the heroine discovers the secret of her origins. *Your name means "fame" because your destiny is to become an international star.* Having grown up hating my first name, little did I know it held my prophecy. In all modesty, this discovery makes me realize that I am the Chosen One.

For a long time I had sensed that I was different from everyone else, without ever quite managing to explain this strange intuition. Tonight, everything becomes clear. It’s not that I admire fame or fantasize about it like some insecure teenager—it’s that I am going to do it. Holy shit, I am really going to do it.

Fame isn’t just a sweet childhood dream; an illustrious horizon awaits me. I picture myself spinning the wheel of fortune and hitting the jackpot, stepping onto the top of the podium, reaping all the honors, a laurel wreath on my head.
