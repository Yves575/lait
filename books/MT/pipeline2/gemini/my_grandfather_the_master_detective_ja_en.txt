"A blue tiger came in this morning," Kaede's grandfather said.

"I wonder how it turned the doorknob. Quite dexterous of it."

Grandfather seemed more surprised that the tiger had opened the front door to get inside than that it had wandered into his study—or even that its body was covered in blue fur.

"Well, at least you weren't bitten."

Kaede offered a deliberately lighthearted quip. 

Inwardly, she was a little disappointed. *He's finally awake, and we're back to this?* 

She visited about once a week, but her grandfather was usually asleep. 

On the rare occasions he was awake, however, he spoke of nothing but his hallucinations. 

He would harp on these visions until she left, making any proper conversation impossible. 

Even so, Kaede willingly lent an ear to the "tale of the blue tiger," nodding and murmuring her agreement time and again. 

She did so because the time she spent at her grandfather's house—which was also her childhood home—felt irreplaceable to her.

"And then the tiger..." Grandfather mimed the animal's gait, crossing his arms like front paws. 

"When it left, it showed me the most wonderfully happy smile."

"The tiger smiled?"

*Ah... here we go again,* she smiled wryly to herself. 

Even though it was a completely impossible hallucination, here she was, genuinely hanging on his every word again. 

Yes—despite starting out merely *pretending* to be an eager listener, her grandfather’s storytelling was so masterful that she invariably found herself drawn into his world without even realizing it. 

Today, in fact, she even had the illusion that a real blue tiger might come leaping right out of an illustration in one of the books on the shelves.

Having talked his fill, he must have been satisfied. 

Grandfather's eyelids slowly drifted shut.

Day in and day out, Grandfather sat in the electric recliner in this room. 

They had chosen a larger size to accommodate his tall, thin frame, but this proved to be a major miscalculation; apparently, the chair was far more comfortable than expected, and now he practically never left it.

Propped against the nearby side table was the wooden cane he needed to get around. 

However, the care manager who had recommended the cane had complained to Kaede with a sigh, "He'll use it when he goes to the washroom, but when he's picking out a book from the shelf, he can't be bothered and won't use it at all. I worry he might fall."

*(He still loves books. But the contents...)*

A sad thought crossed her mind: *He's probably not taking any of it in anymore.*

The musty scent of ink drifted through the study, which was completely overflowing with books. 

It reminded Kaede of her favorite secondhand bookstores in Jinbocho.

Before she realized it, the sunlight filtering through the trees outside the window had formed a camouflage pattern, falling across her grandfather's sleeping face. 

The high bridge of his nose and the wrinkles carved at the corners of his eyes cast complex shadows over a face that, despite his seventy-one years, was somehow entirely free of age spots.

Though the flesh had fallen away from his jaw and cheeks over the years, the gauntness only served to accentuate his deeply chiseled features.

His thick, long hair, parted down the middle of his broad forehead, was a gradient of seventy percent white blending into the remaining black. This, too, gave him a sculptural air, reminiscent of an emperor struck on an ancient Roman coin.

Even setting aside a granddaughter’s natural bias, he possessed a truly majestic countenance.

*(I bet he was quite popular with women, back in the day.)*

Kaede gently pulled the slipped blanket back up to her grandfather’s frail neck.

Her cleaning finished, she sprayed a soap-scented antibacterial mist, taking care to avoid the books on the shelves; by then, it was already time for the physical therapist to arrive for his rehab session.

But this antibacterial spray wasn’t merely there to keep the room hygienic.

Her grandfather frequently suffered from hallucinations of tiny bugs, much like mosquitoes.

Whenever that happened, the spray served as a makeshift “insecticide.”

*(See you, Grandpa.)*

*(—I’ll be back.)*

Beside the study door stood a vanity table, a piece effectively inherited from her late grandmother.

Instead of deteriorating with age, one might say it had evolved.

The accumulated years had settled into the wood grain like makeup of wonderfully complex hues, imbuing the piece with a distinctively rich character.

Kaede pulled a hairbrush from the vanity drawer, gave her hair a quick sweep, and looked into the mirror to compose her features.

*(Smile.)*

The study door, once made of heavy oak, had already been replaced with a sliding door in anticipation of the day her grandfather would inevitably need a wheelchair.

Kaede slid the door shut without a sound and left her grandfather’s house in Himonya.

2

On her way home, swaying to the rhythm of the Toyoko Line, she caught sight of an utterly expressionless face reflected in the train window.

The smile she had taken such care to put on was gone; not a trace of it remained.

The sky had already deepened into dusk, as if brushed with pale lipstick.

With the arrival of early autumn, the towering thunderclouds had vanished, leaving the sky dotted with clouds of all shapes and sizes.

Suddenly, a memory of her grandfather floated through Kaede’s mind.

Twenty-three years ago. Four-year-old Kaede.

She is out on the veranda, perched in her grandfather’s lap as he sits cross-legged, gazing up at the madder-red sky.

Her grandfather lowers his gaze to his lap, his clear eyes radiating a quiet intellect.

“Kaede. What do those clouds over there look like to you? Try using all of them to make up a single story.”

Looking back now, it was just like a *sandai-banashi*—an improvised *rakugo* tale spun from three random topics.

Perhaps he was trying to encourage her imagination to take flight—

It must have been his own unique way of cultivating her young sensibilities.

Kaede answers without missing a beat.

“That cloud is a tiny grandpa. And that cloud over there is a flat grandpa. And then, um... the biggest cloud is a grandpa who’s even fatter than you, Grandpa.”

“You can't make a story out of that,” her grandfather said, his face breaking into a smile.

And yet, to her surprise, with an air of having no other choice, he took over for Kaede and improvised a children's story on the spot titled *The Three Grandpas*.

She couldn’t remember the exact details of the story.

She did remember the ending, though: the gluttonous “fat grandpa” mistook all the cold medicine in the world for sugar and swallowed it all; despite being teased mercilessly for it, he ended up living longer than anyone else in the world. 

It was probably meant as a lesson for Kaede, who hated taking bitter powdered medicine.

But his delivery was so incredibly entertaining that she clapped her hands in sheer delight.

“Look, Kaede. See that?”

When she looked up at the sky, only the “big cloud”—the “fat grandpa”—remained. The “tiny grandpa” and the “flat grandpa” had quite literally vanished into thin air.

Just like the ending of his story.

Wide-eyed and bewildered, Kaede darted her gaze back and forth between her grandfather and the “fat grandpa” in the sky over and over again.

Looking back, her grandfather must have been spinning his tale while covertly keeping an eye on the shifting clouds.

And had the “tiny grandpa” or the “flat grandpa” been the last one left, the story would undoubtedly have taken a very different turn.

“Grandpa, tell me another story. If you don't...”

The young Kaede looked up at him and yanked on a hair growing out of a mole on his Adam’s apple.

It pulled out surprisingly easily, which struck her as so absurdly funny that she remembered bursting into laughter.

*Maybe, just maybe, that was the moment,* Kaede thought.

*(Maybe I pulled the stopper out of Grandpa’s intellect.)*

It was only six months ago that her grandfather visibly began to change.

They had gone for a walk together, and his stride had grown noticeably shorter.

“Grandpa, are you heavier than you look? Your legs aren't keeping up with you.”

Her grandfather just tilted his head and gave a self-deprecating smile. “I’m just getting old,” he said.

At first, Kaede too chalked it up to him being overweight, or simply to old age—or rather, she tried to.

But from there, his decline was rapid.

When he drank his beloved coffee, the hand holding the cup would tremble violently.

Whenever she visited his house, she would find him in his study chair, nodding off.

He became constantly stooped over, and his movements grew sluggish no matter what he was doing.

But no, more than any of that.

More than anything else, Kaede would never in her life forget the shock of that day.

Her smartphone rang in the dead of night.

Rubbing her sleepy eyes as she answered the phone, she found the caller—who sounded like a young man—strangely hesitant to speak. "Um, I'm with the paramedics," he introduced himself.

Then, sounding deeply apologetic and stumbling over his words again and again, he went on:

"Am I speaking to Kaede? —Ah, I thought so. Well, you see, your name was on an emergency contact list pinned to the wall, which is why I'm calling you. Actually, your grandfather—who is right here with us—called 119. And so, um—well, you see..."

"What happened?"

"He is saying, 'Kaede is lying here covered in blood.'"

At his regular clinic, they had been told that while Parkinson's disease was suspected, it was impossible to say for sure, and a visit to a larger hospital was recommended.

They went to a university hospital, where he underwent detailed testing, including a CT scan.

The results—the young doctor delivered them matter-of-factly, paying no mind to her grandfather, who was sleeping soundly in his chair.

"It's Lewy body dementia."

Her grandfather, who had always been so brilliant, had developed dementia just after turning seventy—

Kaede found it completely impossible to accept that reality right away.

But as she did her own research online and through informational materials she had requested, she found that every single one of her grandfather's symptoms perfectly matched those of the disease.

She learned for the first time that the number of people living with dementia had reached over six million in Japan alone—and that, while broadly referred to as "dementia," the condition actually encompassed a variety of different types.

What is generally known as dementia can be roughly divided into three main categories.

The most common is Alzheimer's dementia, which accounts for up to seventy percent of cases. It is reportedly caused by the accumulation in the brain of a type of protein called amyloid beta.

This is likely the type that instantly comes to mind for most people when they hear the word "dementia."

The second most common is vascular dementia, which stems from the aftereffects of cerebral infarctions or strokes, and reportedly accounts for roughly twenty percent of all dementia cases.

Both forms of dementia frequently present with symptoms such as memory loss, causing the patient to repeat the same stories; disorientation, where their sense of time and place becomes blurred; or wandering, where they roam outside.

And—the "Lewy body dementia" her grandfather had been diagnosed with, also called DLB from the initials of its English name, Dementia with Lewy Bodies—accounts for roughly ten percent of all cases.

Given that the disease was only named in 1995, it is likely one of the relatively newly discovered illnesses in the long history of human pathology.

In recent years, it has garnered attention as the "third dementia," and efforts to unravel its pathology are apparently advancing at a rapid pace, not only in clinical settings but in the field of clinical trials as well.

In the brains and brainstems of DLB patients, deep-red structures resembling tiny fried eggs—Lewy bodies—are invariably found.

And it is precisely these "little fried eggs" that cause Parkinsonian symptoms like tremors in the extremities and difficulty walking; the loud sleep-talking characteristic of REM sleep behavior disorder; daytime somnolence, which causes the patient to sleep all day long; and visuospatial cognitive impairment, which makes it difficult to judge distances.

But—

The defining feature of DLB, and its most singular symptom, is undoubtedly visual hallucinations.

Though how they appear varies from patient to patient—some see them in black and white, others in color—the common thread is the fact that they experience hallucinations that are "vivid," "lifelike," and "distinct."

For example, a patient might wake up in the morning, open their eyes, and immediately find as many as ten expressionless people standing silently in their room, staring holes right through them.

Or a massive snake might be heavily coiling itself on the dining room table.

They might be followed around all day, wherever they go, by a young girl with braided hair.

Utterly surreal visions are not uncommon, either.

A pig trotting briskly past on two legs.

A fairy leaping elegantly across a dinner plate.

And the blue tiger her grandfather saw—

Strangely enough, in most cases, these visions are not accompanied by auditory hallucinations.

The "things creeping through these visions" are purely optical illusions; they never speak to the patient.

And yet, of the five senses, sight is said to account for a full ninety percent of the information humans receive from the outside world.

This means that to the majority of DLB patients, these "creeping things" are unequivocally real.

The proverb these patients would probably most like to use is, "Seeing is believing."

After all, they can see these things right in front of them, vividly real.

No matter how much the people around them try to deny the existence of these things, it must be near-impossible to convince the patient that they "aren't real" or "aren't there."

Even so, if those around them unnecessarily scold them, insisting, "There's nothing there," "How could there be?" or "Snap out of it," the patient will sometimes lash out in anger.

This is precisely why caring for someone with DLB is considered so difficult.

When Kaede read through a caregiving handbook, she found a passage like this:

"When a patient complains of visual hallucinations, saying things like, 'I see a giant bug,' or 'I'm scared,' do not dismiss them by saying, 'It's just your imagination,' or brush them off with, 'You're sick, so please don't make things difficult.' Instead, clap your hands or do something similar and speak to them gently: 'There, it's gone now, isn't it? You're safe now.' Diverting the conversation to another topic is also effective."

She supposed that was just the way it was.

Besides, her grandfather had never once been angry with her in his life, and the absolute last thing she wanted was to get into any kind of conflict with him.

That was precisely why Kaede had avoided talking in depth about his illness, and why, whenever he spoke of his hallucinations, she had consistently refrained from denying their reality to his face.

Fundamentally, making a patient realize they had dementia was probably close to impossible, and even if it could be done, it seemed far too cruel.

But then again…

Kaede felt a strange sense of unease regarding her own perceptions and actions—like a division problem that should divide evenly, but somehow leaves a remainder.

It felt slightly different in nuance from the escapist urge—or rather, the wishful thinking—that a man like her grandfather couldn't possibly develop dementia, or that he wouldn't just keep losing his intellect like this.

*(Yes. Something isn't right—)*

But what was the true nature of this unease?

Kaede could not, at present, explain it in any concrete terms.

3

A fifteen-minute bus ride from Gumyoji Station.

When she returned to her studio apartment, she found that a book had arrived.

It was a collection of essays by Takeshi Setogawa, a literary critic with a profound love of mysteries.

The colophon read: *April 1, 1998 — First Edition, First Printing.*

If Kaede's memory served her correctly, he must have passed away shortly after its publication at the young age of fifty.

Which meant this book was, essentially, Setogawa's final work.

Steeped in her grandfather's influence since childhood, Kaede had grown into a full-fledged mystery fanatic. No longer satisfied with novels alone, she had eventually taken to reading the Setogawa essay collections she found on her grandfather's bookshelf.

And when she did—she was less surprised than she was utterly dumbfounded.

He would put various works on the chopping block, expounding on their charms with freewheeling brilliance from his unique perspective, and these columns were sometimes—no, almost without exception—more entertaining than the books themselves.

In a series of columns titled *Pilgrimage to Masterpieces*, for example, he took aim at the magnum opuses of classic mystery's "Big Three"—Ellery Queen, Agatha Christie, and John Dickson Carr—and relentlessly tore them down, asking, "Is this really such a masterpiece?" His critiques were more logical and thrilling than the novels themselves, and his arguments impeccably sound. Yet, whether Setogawa himself realized it or not, his overflowing affection for the authors seeped out between the lines, warming her heart every time she read them.

The "god" who had turned Kaede into a lover of classic foreign mysteries—

*(Takeshi Setogawa.)*

Just whispering the name softly in her mind made Kaede's heart leap.

Around 1970, in his youth, Setogawa had reportedly been a central figure in the Waseda Mystery Club, a legendary university circle that had produced a myriad of mystery writers and critics.

There used to be a coffee shop in Nishi-Waseda called Mon Chéri, where, day after day, students from the Waseda Mystery Club would engage in passionate mystery debates, arguing until they were blue in the face. And apparently, right at the center of that circle was always Setogawa's smiling face, with his thick eyebrows and sharp features.

Her grandfather, too, had apparently been one of the key members of the "Waseda—" club.

Coffee goes perfectly with a classic mystery.

Its tall signboard, emblazoned with the words *Specialty Coffee Mon Chéri* in psychedelic white letters against a red background, apparently gave off an air that shut out anyone who wasn't a "mystery expert."

Rich, bitter coffee, swirling with a mysterious foam that obscured the bottom of the cup.

Yellow-tiled exterior walls reminiscent of Leroux's *The Mystery of the Yellow Room*.

The footsteps of the theater troupe members echoing from the small theater on the second floor were akin, perhaps, to Chesterton's "The Queer Feet"—or else Ranpo's "The Watcher in the Attic."

Now that Mon Chéri is gone, all one can do is let one's imagination take flight.

But that café, which counted Setogawa and her grandfather among its raconteurs, must surely have radiated the same intense passion and brilliance as Tokiwa-so in the manga world, or Mount Liang in *Water Margin*.

*(I would have loved to hear the two of them discussing classic mysteries...)*

Because the café no longer existed, Kaede's fantasies about it only grew all the wilder.

Sadly, Mon Chéri was no more.

But—there were still the books.

When you truly love a book, you naturally want to keep a copy close at hand.

Not to mention, the copies at her grandfather's house were so meticulously preserved in translucent glassine covers, without a single creased page to be found, that she couldn't help but feel hesitant to borrow them.

That was why Kaede had decided to splurge and buy every single one of Setogawa's essay collections in one go.

*(Thank goodness, it looks brand new. It even has the obi strip.)*

Kaede felt a surge of joy upon seeing the book's condition.

Truth be told, she would have preferred to buy the whole set brand new.

However, since this posthumous work was already out of print, she’d had no choice but to track it down through an online secondhand bookstore.

With this, her collection of his works was finally complete.

*(I wonder what it says about a twenty-seven-year-old woman to be completing a collection like this.)*

Conscious of the smile breaking across her face, she stood there and thumbed through the pages.

Just then—

Four small slips of paper fluttered down from between the pages onto the carpet like ginkgo leaves.

*(Hm. What's this?)*

Kaede carefully gathered the four slips of paper and laid them out on the table.

She gazed intently at the rectangular slips of varying sizes, lost in thought.

*(Too many to be bookmarks.)*

*(But...)*

*(...they're far "too heavy" to be sticky notes—)*

The four slips of paper were clippings from magazines and newspapers.

And every single one of them was an obituary reporting Setogawa's passing.

4

Taking advantage of the public holiday, she made her way to Himonya in Meguro Ward for the first time in three days.

In a residential neighborhood just a short walk from Himonya Hachimangu, the local guardian shrine—

Tucked quietly away in a corner of that neighborhood stood her grandfather’s house, a small, somewhat dilapidated two-story wooden structure.

From the modest excuse for a garden, cherry trees and Japanese aralias stretched their branches and leaves over the wall.

On the gatepost, the wooden nameplate bore her grandfather's surname, brushed in bold, vivid strokes of black ink.

It was her grandfather's handwriting, familiar to her since childhood.

They say a nameplate is the face of a house.

If the exterior of the house retained a certain elegance, still draped in a stately presence, it was perhaps thanks to the masterful calligraphy on that nameplate.

But the moment she stepped through the gate, that refined ambiance was abruptly spoiled.

Round stepping stones had once dotted the ground like markers leading to the front door, but ever since her grandfather had developed dementia, they had been replaced by a drab concrete path.

She turned the knob of the front door—its renovation having been continually postponed—and was immediately hit by the artificial, soapy scent of an antibacterial cleaner.

*Is that the home helper?* Kaede started to call out, but stopped herself.

It was because she had seen no such shoes in the entryway.

The visiting caregiver must have finished the cleaning and laundry and just left.

Several new handrails had been installed here and there along the hallway walls.

For her grandfather, whose gait was unsteady, multiple handrails were essential to navigate the house.

When purchasing such accessibility equipment, applying for a government subsidy—though the process varied by municipality—often involved highly convoluted procedures and took an enormous amount of time.

As a result, the reality was that people like her grandfather often had no choice but to cover most of the cost out of pocket.

She stepped into the living room on the left side of the hallway.

Glancing at the central wooden pillar, which still barely retained its original luster, she noticed several horizontal pencil lines drawn across it.

They were the marks left from measuring her mother when she was young, and Kaede, his one and only grandchild.

Though the heights and dates written beside the lines had almost completely faded away, they still bore witness to her grandfather's masterful penmanship.

Yet her heart ached to see the metal bracket of a handrail driven right into those characters, sinking into the wood.

Looking toward the window, she noticed several white T-shirts hanging up to dry indoors.

*(Oh no. The helper must have slipped up.)*

When living with a DLB patient, it is best to avoid drying clothes indoors whenever possible.

This is because they can easily mistake hanging clothes for people.

With white T-shirts in particular, DLB patients are often prone to projecting vivid hallucinations onto that "white canvas."

Having heard that portraits and family photos should be kept out of the patient’s sight for this very reason, she had once hurriedly stowed the picture frames that lined the table deep inside a dresser.

Just as she was hurriedly pulling a T-shirt off its hanger—

her grandfather spoke from behind her, his voice relatively steady.

"Sorry about that, Kanae hung those up to dry before she left. I guess the stains weren't completely washed out?"

Her grandfather appeared in the living room, coffee cup in hand, and slowly sat down on his bed.

Since the upstairs bedroom had long since become a storage space, his world was mostly confined to this living room, where his bed now stood, and the study at the very back of the house.

But judging by his footsteps today, he seemed to be doing exceptionally well—much better than during her last visit.

Drastic day-to-day fluctuations in his condition were a major hallmark of DLB.

"No, I was just smoothing out the wrinkles," she said, making an excuse as she gave up on bringing in the T-shirt.

"So Mom was here, not the home helper?"

"She seemed to still have some work to do, so she left in a hurry. I'm afraid you just missed each other."

Inwardly, Kaede felt relieved.

It was better this way—

At least for today, the day she finally planned to ask him her question.

Lately, it was rare to see her grandfather looking so well.

Today was definitely her chance.

"Kanae's coffee is delicious even when it's cold," he said.

Still smiling, he slowly shifted his hips to readjust his posture on the bed.

He took a sip of his coffee with a slightly trembling hand, then spoke.

"It doesn't look like I'm in danger of spilling my coffee today. If I do say so myself, I seem to be doing exceptionally well. Which is why I want to take this chance to ask you something. It's nothing but a hunch, really, but..."

He took another sip of his coffee, then looked Kaede straight in the eye.

"There's something important you want to talk to me about, isn't there? I can see it on your face."

Kaede felt herself on the verge of tears.

The distinct, gentle way he referred to himself.

His lucid, dark-eyed, gentle gaze.

It was as if the grandfather she used to know had returned.

Perhaps because he wasn't in his usual drowsy state, his speech was crisp and clear.

Looking back, over the past six months she had been so worried about his condition that they had barely shared a single serious conversation.

Now was her only chance to ask.

Summoning her courage, Kaede broke the ice. "You're right. Actually..."

"There's something I need to ask you, Grandpa."

"What is it?"

"Grandpa..."

She tried desperately to hold back her tears.

"Grandpa... you know you're sick, don't you? You realize that what you're seeing isn't reality—that you're always seeing things that aren't there... don't you?"

It was no use.

Her voice was trembling.

"But you didn't want me to worry..."

The tears began to fall.

She had promised herself she wouldn't cry.

"You didn't want me to worry, so you've been pretending you didn't know... haven't you?"

Her grandfather kept his soft smile as he took another sip of his coffee.

Then, with careful hands, he placed the cup on the dining table beside his bed.

"Yes, Kaede, you're right. I am, without a doubt, a patient with Lewy body dementia."

Her intuition had been right all along.

Her grandfather’s dark eyes, with irises as delicate as glasswork, were filled with a profound depth that threatened to draw her in.

Yes—within them dwelt the very same light of intellect as always, completely unchanged from days past.

And that was the true source of that lingering sense of dissonance—something Kaede herself hadn't fully realized she'd been feeling.

Over the past two days, she had researched DLB in greater detail and learned a great deal. 

She learned that even among DLB patients, the decline in memory and spatial cognition varied significantly depending on the area of the brain where the Lewy bodies appeared. 

Some patients lived in constant fear of their visual hallucinations, while others seemingly grew accustomed to them quite easily.

The symptoms fluctuated in intensity from patient to patient; every case was truly unique.

Apparently, there were also many cases where, if the delicate balance of various medications—such as L-dopa—was struck perfectly, the hallucinations would vanish completely, as though "a fog had lifted."

In fact, depending on their physical condition on any given day, a patient might show absolutely no signs of cognitive decline.

What surprised Kaede most of all, however, was the fact that countless patients were acutely aware that the images they were seeing were not reality, but rather a product of their illness. 

Some even maintained such exceedingly positive outlooks that they actually looked forward to the hallucinations greeting them each morning, making a hobby out of sketching them afterward.

Scientific understanding of DLB remained incomplete, which naturally made the disease prone to misunderstanding.

Even in the medical field, a significant number of doctors would simply take a patient's intense hallucinatory experiences at face value and jump to the conclusion that their dementia was progressing. 

The onset of DLB did not necessarily dictate a decline in intellect.

When Kaede learned this fact, she felt as though her own strange sense of dissonance had vanished, truly as if "a fog had lifted."

"Setting aside these Parkinsonian symptoms," her grandfather said, looking down at his slightly trembling hands.

"I realized quite some time ago that my mental state is far from what you'd call 'normal.' Let's see—take the side of that bookshelf over there, for example. When I glance at it, I see intricate carvings covering its entire surface, as though a master carpenter had carved them for a portable shrine. But when I reach out and touch it, I don't feel any carvings at all. The wood is perfectly smooth. So, which sense should I trust: sight, or touch? It would be utterly impossible for someone to carve such exquisite details across the entire side of that bookshelf overnight without my noticing. What's more, no one in the world has the slightest motive to go out of their way to sneak into an old man's room just to carve designs into his bookshelf. Which means—regrettably—touch is the sense I must rely on. Conversely, this means my sense of sight is completely untrustworthy."

Kaede had no words to offer in response; she could only listen in silence to her grandfather's confession.

"So, what could be the cause of this strange phenomenon? My computer is broken and unusable. I thought about searching on my smartphone, but as you can see, my hands are terribly unsteady. More to the point, ever since I saw 'Kaede's lifeless body' and called 119, Kanae has confiscated my phone, so looking anything up wasn't an option to begin with."

Her grandfather pursed his well-shaped lips in a mischievous pout.

"So I begged my caregiver to call an accessible taxi, and I went to the library to do some research. Since my eyes blur and I grow exhausted just trying to follow the text, it ended up being an all-day affair... but I was able to confirm exactly what illness I have. You know the phrase 'little grey cells,' don't you?"

Quoting the famous Belgian detective Hercule Poirot, her grandfather gave a self-deprecating smile.

"Well, in my case, since dark orange Lewy bodies are spreading across the surface of my brain, I suppose that makes me the owner of 'little scarlet cells,' you see."

"Then..." Kaede asked.

She could hear the slight hoarseness in her own voice.

"Why did you go out of your way to keep telling me about your hallucinations?"

"Well, you see..."

Her grandfather hesitated for a moment.

"It's because your expressions change so vividly, especially when I talk about my hallucinations. Because you look surprised, or you smile at me. Above all, because you chime in and react to what I say. When you do that, I can be absolutely certain that the Kaede sitting in front of me is real."

"What... what do you mean? I'm always right here with you, Grandpa."

"Perhaps you'll understand if I put it this way. Once before, I spoke to you with complete candor about my future—which, in all honesty, likely won't be very long. What should I call it... If I refuse to use the term 'end-of-life planning'—a phrase I find as ubiquitous as it is insensitive, and which I thoroughly dislike—let's just say I was talking about how I intended to put my affairs in order. At the time, I foolishly flattered myself that I was in near-perfect health. I was convinced it was now or never. And so, yes... I must have talked at length for a solid hour. But then. For some reason, you had been listening to me the entire time in total silence, your face completely devoid of expression. And then—"

Her grandfather paused, lowering his gaze.

"Suddenly, you just vanished right in front of my eyes. That Kaede... had been a hallucination."

Or perhaps it was the fault of the over-roasted coffee.

For a brief moment, a flicker of bitterness crossed her grandfather's face.

"Could anything be more miserable, more heartbreaking? Ever since that day, I swore to myself that I would never speak of my illness again unless you brought it up first. Even if it meant being written off as an old man with dementia, completely incapable of holding a normal conversation... I decided that simply couldn't be helped."

*(Grandpa...)*

She whispered it in her heart once more.

*(Grandpa.)*

Her grandfather had been unable to discuss his end-of-life arrangements with his only granddaughter—or rather, he had deliberately stopped trying.

Why hadn't she realized his anguish sooner?

He certainly experienced visual hallucinations. 

Frequently, at that.

He was sometimes struck by various cognitive impairments, memory loss included. 

Parkinsonian symptoms made his movements exceedingly slow.

And yet—her grandfather's underlying intellect had not faded in the slightest.

5

It was likely dismissal time at the nearby Christian kindergarten.

The sound of children singing what sounded like a nursery rhyme drifted in as they passed by the house. 

Their slightly off-key voices only made it all the more endearing.

Her grandfather's face relaxed into a natural smile.

The autumn sun sinks like a stone—

Still, it seemed there was some time left before it grew fully dark.

"Actually, Grandpa, there's something I want you to see."

Kaede pulled Mr. Setogawa’s collection of essays from her black bag. 

If her grandfather had been asleep in his chair as he usually was, she had planned to drape the freshly washed blanket she’d brought over him, spend some time reading beside him, and then head home.

But right now, the way he was, perhaps—

Her grandfather took a pair of rimless reading glasses from the pocket of his robe and perched them on his prominent nose. Holding the book at a slight distance, he spoke wistfully.

"Ah, Setogawa's posthumous collection. You didn't need to go out and buy it—I would have given you my copy."

*(I could never take it. Not a book so deeply and happily treasured.)*

Kaede chuckled inwardly.

"I'm fairly sure it's out of print. You did well to track it down."

"They have online bookstores specializing in secondhand books now, so it's actually surprisingly easy to get your hands on even fairly rare editions. Anyway—the truth is, I found these slipped inside."

Kaede opened the book and laid the four obituary clippings out on the table for him to see.

*Setogawa, Prominent Mystery and Film Critic, Passes Away*

*Setogawa Dead: A Talent Much Mourned*

*The Era of Multi-Layered Criticism: Setogawa's Legacy*

*In Setogawa's Words: The Happy Marriage of Mystery and Film*

"Yes. I read all of them at the time."

With only a brief glance at the headlines, her grandfather murmured, a touch of melancholy in his voice.

"I believe two other papers ran articles as well. I kept clippings of all of them, of course."

"I see."

Once again, Kaede was astounded by her grandfather's memory. 

Due to his illness, his memory of recent events would sometimes vanish completely, but when it came to the past, it seemed he could still open those mental drawers at will.

"So, Grandpa—here's the thing. I think this is what you’d call an 'everyday mystery,' the sort of puzzle that seems like it would happen all the time but rarely ever does."

*I see,* her grandfather nodded.

"So then, the central premise of the mystery is this: 'Who exactly slipped these four obituary clippings into this book, and for what purpose?'"

"Exactly. To begin with, there are far too many of them to be bookmarks. But using obituaries as page markers? Don't you think that's a bit too morbid?"

"Just like Harry Kemelman."

Her grandfather removed his glasses as he murmured the name of the classic mystery writer. 

Kemelman’s masterpiece, *The Nine Mile Walk*, is a classic mystery driven purely by logic. From a single sentence overheard from the next table at a pub—“A nine mile walk is no joke, especially in the rain”—the detective unravels the entire truth of a murder committed the day before in one fell swoop.

Just then—abruptly, her grandfather made a request.

"Kaede. Would you give me a cigarette?"

Perhaps due to its poetic meter, the words carried the ring of an incantation.

Kaede fetched a blue pack of cigarettes from the drawer of the vanity in the study.

Gauloises, a French brand.

They were not particularly expensive, but neither were they easy to find just anywhere.

Kaede would typically buy them at a tiny, obscure general store whenever she browsed the secondhand bookshops in Jinbocho.

"I would appreciate it if you lit it for me. Yes, just like that. My hands shake, you see. I try not to *drink* them when I am alone."

Instead of saying he "smoked" tobacco, her grandfather always said he "drank" it.

It was likely a holdover from an era before today’s anti-smoking society, back when tobacco was considered as commonplace an indulgence as alcohol.

Even in his youth, he had only indulged in a few cigarettes a week, and lately, it was exceptionally rare for him to *drink* one at all.

For that very reason, Kaede felt she ought to let him keep at least this small pleasure.

As he *drank* from the cigarette, a look of quiet bliss washed over his face for a moment.

Kaede didn’t mind the scent of Gauloises, but afraid the smell would cling to the T-shirts she had left hanging out to dry, she opened the window a crack.

Exhaling a slow ribbon of violet smoke, her grandfather spoke in a noticeably sharper tone. "Now then—"

It was as though the cigarette had flipped the switch on the boosters of his intellect.

"What kind of story will you spin from these materials, Kaede?"

Kaede’s heart began to race.

Her grandfather had always referred to hypotheses as "stories."

He was truly back now.

The grandfather she remembered.

"Here is the story I've come up with."

Trying her best to sound composed, Kaede laid out the hypothesis she had thought long and hard about.

"Story One: 'The person who tucked the obituaries inside was the book’s original owner. He or she deliberately placed the articles in the book because they wanted to share the profound emptiness of Mr. Setogawa’s passing with his other fans.'"

Glancing at her grandfather, she saw him nodding in deep agreement.

As always, presenting a story to her grandfather was a nerve-wracking ordeal.

And yet—

*(And yet, I'm happy.)*

"Um... Story Two."

Coming back to her senses, Kaede continued.

"‘The person who slipped the obituary inside was someone at the used bookstore. He or she was a fan of Mr. Setogawa. Then, for the first time in decades, an order came in for his out-of-print book. Overjoyed, he or she placed the clipping inside the book as a sort of gift for this unknown kindred spirit—namely, me.’"

Her throat felt dry, perhaps from the intellectual thrill.

"What do you think? Those are the two stories I came up with."

Her grandfather replied.

"Hmm, not bad. They each make a certain amount of sense, and I wouldn't go so far as to call them far-fetched. But both of them contain a major contradiction."

"Right."

Kaede bit her lip.

"Listen closely. The first contradiction, found in Story One, is this. Would a fan of Setogawa—one devoted enough to hold onto his obituary—really sell a book they cherish? Especially considering that this book is Mr. Setogawa's final work. Anyone with a normal sense of attachment would have kept it safely in their own collection, along with the clipping."

Kaede could only nod.

"True. From a book lover's perspective, I suppose it's hard to believe."

"Your second story is a more pleasant one than the first. But the contradiction remains undeniable. If the bookseller slipped the obituary inside out of goodwill... why didn't they include a note? If they went to the trouble of carefully inserting the clipping, they could have at least written something like, 'As a fellow fan, I was delighted to receive your order. Please accept this enclosed article regarding Mr. Setogawa's passing as a modest tribute.' Why would he or she be so reluctant to make such a small effort? In short—"

Her grandfather cut straight to the point.

"It means that both Story One and Story Two are fundamentally flawed. There must exist another story outside of these—a Story X."

"Then..."

Kaede asked, her voice hoarse.

"Are you saying you can weave that Story X, Grandpa?"

Her grandfather didn't answer. Barely managing to pinch his dwindling Gauloises between his thumb and forefinger as if reluctant to let it go, he took one last drag.

His eyes slowly—but surely—narrowed.

Kaede worried he might be dozing off.

But her fears were unfounded.

Her grandfather opened his eyes wide and said, "I can see the 'picture' now."

"Unfortunately, the man who originally owned this book has already passed away."

"What?"

"Just look. Can't you see the man right there with a peaceful look on his face?"

A hallucination.

But this hallucination was rooted in clear logic—

She sensed it intuitively.

"Story X goes like this. While he was alive, he had tucked the obituary of his beloved Takeshi Setogawa inside a cherished book with a deep sense of sorrow. However—after his death, his wife, completely unaware of what a treasure it was, sold it to a secondhand bookstore along with various other books as part of sorting through his belongings."

*(This is the truth—)*

She couldn't help but think so.

Was this not the perfect "story"—without a single contradiction, where everything simply fell into place?

"But," Kaede pressed him.

"How do you know the previous owner was a man? It could have been a woman, couldn't it?"

"I doubt it," her grandfather said flatly. 

"When a spouse passes away, women are the ones who can suppress their grief and act calmly. Men are completely hopeless. In fact, even I..."

Her grandfather lowered his gaze.

"When I lost my wife, I couldn't do a single thing."

For a fleeting moment, the image of her late grandmother's soft, round features crossed Kaede's mind.

Silence lingered for a while.
