There was a goddess. In an octagonal room, deftly curving her voluptuous limbs.

"Um, hello."

"Ah, it's you. Welcome."

Her voice was unexpectedly husky. Somewhere between a mezzo-soprano and an alto, leaning toward alto, if I had to say. Assuming she ever sang in a choir, that is.

As I went to hand over my letter of introduction, the curator swiftly took it from me and laid it open in front of the goddess. I froze instantly. How was I planning to hand it to the goddess, anyway? But she didn't seem to care in the slightest, and before long, she spoke.

"Rika Horauchi, I see. May I call you Hora?"

I nodded. I was a bit bewildered by the sudden christening, but it wasn't as though I had a preferred nickname, either. The goddess narrowed her eyes. Pupilless eyes.

"I'd like you to call me Venus, not Aphrodite. I may look like I'm out of Greek mythology, but I was born in Rome. Though I suppose 'Venus' is the English pronunciation, anyway."

I nodded to this as well. Venus narrowed her eyes further, brimming with a sculpted smile.

"Splendid. Tell me, am I speaking too fast? Does my diction match the style you studied at university? If it's different, please don't hesitate to tell me."

"No... you're very easy to understand." 

"Like a textbook example," I added after a moment's hesitation, wiping my palms on my raincoat. I was sweating terribly.

"I'm glad. Though I suppose I *am* a bit of a textbook example. After all, I come from an era before Latin had branched off into Italian, French, and the rest—a time when no one could have even imagined the Roman Empire splitting."

Venus laughed aloud. Neither her bare white shoulders nor her smooth belly, enveloped in a delicate layer of flesh, moved an inch. Apparently, the rule was that her marble body didn't move from the neck down.


The part-time job was by no means a bad deal. It was just a rather unheard-of line of work.

The person who had referred me was a professor whose lecture I’d taken only once. I had gotten off at that station for the first time in years, made my way past a station roundabout as chaotic as a forest fire, passed through the university gates, and climbed the hill, brushing past girls in matching, pajama-like clothes. When I entered his office in the north wing, my eyes fell on an old, spring-green carpet. As I stared at the carpet's nap, which expressionlessly absorbed the cheers spilling in from the courtyard—incoming freshmen, perhaps?—a letter of introduction was held out to me. An envelope printed with the university's name. I looked up to see a familiar figure standing there.

Yet, even though my hands took it, they remained suspended in midair.

"I'm sorry, I'm just not confident I can do this."

"In terms of conversational ability, there is no one more qualified than you. At least, not among the living."

The professor seemed to be talking to someone standing behind my back.

"Just think of it as being a sort of private tutor. It's much simpler than that, really, since all you have to do is talk."

"But she's practically a native speaker, and besides—"


"And besides, about this talking..." But the rest of my words fell soundlessly in front of the yellow vinyl. Like the spray of a park fountain at closing time. While I was inhaling and pursing my lips, the professor gave a couple of low hops, as if trying to drain invisible water from his ears, and then turned toward a mirror to adjust his jacket. Apparently, the conversation was over. Having no other choice, I departed the university. On a bench in front of the campus gates, the girls in pajamas were eating identical sandwiches.

The museum was a bit of a bus ride away. Walking from the stop, all I could see were the petals of white magnolias. With the houses and shops buried in that bone-like whiteness, I continually checked the map the professor had given me until I finally found the building. Its nearly windowless concrete walls and bronze doors were visibly aged. Rather than a place where cultural artifacts and historical materials from across the ages and around the world were gathered, it looked more like a deserted student dormitory. I walked past the "Closed Today" sign, made my way to the back entrance, pulled out my smartphone, and dialed the designated number. The door opened on the second ring, and I stepped inside as prompted. Like an exclusive, members-only party.


But this place didn't seem to welcome visitors. Especially the living. Perhaps to preserve the exhibits, the interior was as cold as a morgue. The smell of chemicals had seeped into the floors, the walls, everywhere, and both the chandelier reigning over the vaulted ceiling and the delicate milk-glass lights lining the hall kept their darkened eyes softly lowered today. Passing exhibits of fossils and earthenware in glass cases, I noticed flyers for children's events and local flea markets lined up in a rack by the main entrance, but I wondered if they would really take place. To think that the dates and times written on these sleepy-looking flyers would eventually arrive somewhere in the world, that children would gather to do science experiments, or that old sweaters and audio equipment would be sold along the approach to a shrine.

Within a field of vision like a faded calendar, the only thing moving was the curator walking ahead of me.

"Watch your step."

He looked back, as if anticipating I would trip on my hem on the stairs. The curator, whose name was Hashibami, had remarkably perfect features. His age was hard to guess, but his figure in a white shirt and slim pants looked every bit the young man, possessing a beauty more akin to a crafted object—like a vase or lacquerware—than a human being. I clutched the letter of introduction tightly in my pocket. I was never good with handsome men. Especially men who were handsome and kind—or at least, who seemed to be.


Yet, if some scholar were to assert that light possesses a gravitational pull, my left hand would gladly volunteer to help prove them right. As the door at the top of the stairs opened, I found myself involuntarily drawn toward the arched window. You couldn't tell from the outside, but the center of the building had been hollowed out to encompass trees and a small pond; the surrounding corridor, looking down upon this modest courtyard, was filled with a transparent brightness. Pointing to the floral bouquet reliefs carved into the pillars, Hashibami explained.

“This building was donated to us; it was originally the mansion of a wealthy local. The doors are closed today, but his collection is on display on the second floor.”

Orchestral melodies spilled from one of the exhibition rooms. The smoothly soaring leaps of an oboe; a timpani roll like a wooden mallet unearthing old memories. Passing a door carved with seashell motifs, I caught the lukewarm scent of the tide and heard the sound of waves. At another room, the hem of ivory lace peeked from the gap beneath the door. A side to the museum I had never known was reveling in its day off.

And that room was at the very back. When Hashibami selected a key from a heavy-looking ring and inserted it, the door opened with a click. Ignoring the yellow raincoat dimly reflected in the corridor's glass window, I strained my eyes to see beyond the open door.

“Right this way.”

This, it seemed, was my workplace. One shift a week; no experience required; however, conversational Latin was strictly mandatory. The role: conversation partner to an ancient Roman statue of Venus.


I cast my gaze around the exhibition room once more. Careful not to let Venus notice, I slowly slid my pupils to the corners of my eyes.

It was a room straight out of a myth. Beneath the light pouring ceaselessly from the domed skylight, they stood upon their pedestals like floating constellations. Hera glaring in my direction; Artemis accompanied by her hound; Minerva clad in armor. Eight goddesses from Greek and Roman mythology. Ancient Greek, perhaps, or some other tongue—giving themselves over to boredom, their bloodless white lips wove intricate patterns of lace. Beyond the shadows cast by those overlapping, loquacious patterns, in the deepest part of the octagon, there she was.

“Oh, excuse me,” Venus began. In stark contrast to her seemingly frozen shoulders, her jaw moved with remarkable smoothness.

“Hashibami, bring a chair for Hora. We can't have her remain standing.”

The curator brought over what looked like a guard's chair from the corner of the room. But as he attempted to return to the goddess's side, her alto voice declared without a moment's delay, “Thank you, you may go.”

“But—” His Latin carried a somewhat desperate ring to it.

“I'll be fine. I'll call if I need anything,” the goddess continued. “Close the door. Make sure of it.”

Hashibami cast a fleeting glance my way, then headed for the exit, offering a brief bow before pulling the door shut. Once his hard footsteps faded away, she spoke. "Have a seat." I sat down in the chair, looked up, and immediately dropped my gaze. It put me at just the right height to meet the eyes of the downward-looking goddess.


She was every inch the goddess of love and beauty. Atop her pedestal, her voluptuous figure arched in a magnificent S-curve. She was smiling, her face beautiful yet meticulously stripped of anything that might give it human individuality. Perfectly symmetrical, without a hint of smile lines or crow's feet, her face bore a touch of resignation carved into its bliss from the very beginning, making her appear all the more elegant and mysterious. The gorgeously flowing folds of her garments—fabric that would never flutter in a breeze—seemed to possess absolutely no intention of covering or protecting her body as clothes ought to. Hiding neither her full breasts nor her round hips, it left me feeling deeply unsettled, flat, shapeless body and all. As I fidgeted in my seat, the goddess asked a question.

"Is the chair uncomfortable?"

"It's fine."

I answered on sheer reflex, forcing the most pleasant smile I could muster. That answer, however, did not seem to satisfy her. Venus asked again, with the probing tone of someone inspecting the pockets of a child caught stealing.

"Hora, is the chair comfortable?"

"Yes, it's perfectly fine."

"Tell me, did you attend some sort of school for ending conversations with the absolute minimum number of words? Please, tell me. Is that chair really comfortable? For *you*."

For the first time, I actually paid attention to the depth of the seat. Then to the alignment of my spine, and the way my heels hovered above the floor. I took stock of it all, as if tracing an X-ray with my finger.

"The seat might be a little too high," I said. Worrying my answer was still too brief, I added, "Because my legs are short."

"Thank you for sharing that with me. I wouldn't know, you see, having never sat in a chair myself. Now, ring that bell over there and call for Hashibami."


I walked over to the curtain as instructed and found a small golden bell, which I gave a hesitant ring. Her lips—strangers to both dryness and chapping—rippled into a smile.

"I can't yet attest to the length of your legs, but for now, there is no need for you to speak of yourself that way."

The goddess smiled. My body went stiff. Like a statue.

As footsteps approached and the door opened, Venus instructed the curator to bring a new chair. Between thanking him for his prompt arrival and giving precise specifications for the chair's size, Venus sounded exactly like a highly capable boss—assuming she ever worked outside a museum, that is.

She waited a beat after the door clicked shut. "Now then," Venus began again.

"Thank you for coming today. I hear you are truly fluent in Latin. When Hashibami inquired with your professor, you were apparently the very first person he recommended. He said you were a brilliant student."

"I'm really not," I let out a thin breath, as if trying to escape the gaze of those solid white eyes.

"I say it because it's true." The goddess spoke without the slightest hesitation. "I heard you've already graduated from university. Are you in grad school now?"

"Grad school? Oh, no. Right now I'm just doing, well, some minor routine work, I guess..."

I tried to catch hold of words. Words to string together. But the more desperately I chased them, the more effortlessly they vanished, or pitifully fell away, and I fell silent inside my raincoat. Hoping, just a little, that I would be fired from this part-time job. But Venus simply murmured, "Routine work sounds nice," and began explaining the job.


The job description and conditions were no different from what I had heard from the professor. The job was to talk to her. The shifts were on Mondays, when the museum was closed. If a Monday fell on a public holiday and the museum was open, the shift would be the following day. Transportation expenses covered. A three-month probationary period.

"If you need to take a day off, just let me know two days in advance and there won't be a problem. Of course, depending on your health or the weather, the day of is fine, too."

I nodded. During my university days, I had worked as a tutor for just six months. The family I was assigned to had been incredibly strict about time. On a day when the snow brought the trains to a halt, I had frantically transferred from bus to bus to reach the elementary school boy's house, losing feeling in my toes to the cold of my wet socks. But when I rang the doorbell at the appointed hour, his mother announced right then and there that she was rescheduling the lesson. "It's such a rare snow," she said, "so I want to let him play." I stood there for a while, watching my student and his pet Golden Retriever—wearing matching, high-quality Montbell down jackets—running around a silver yard that must have been the size of two tennis courts. Compared to that, this job seemed to offer far better conditions. At the very least, the goddess didn't seem likely to change her plans depending on the weather. Nor were her marble legs suddenly going to step down from their pedestal.

Still, I continued to search for a reason to quit this job. I felt out of place. The goddess was beautiful and so utterly bare, while I was bundled up in far too many layers. Always.


I wiped the sweat beading on my forehead. Before I knew it, my yellow hood had slipped down to my eyes.

Suddenly, Venus gave a small yawn. "Say, Hora." Her alto voice was muffled.

"Are you a deep sleeper?"

"Average, I think," I answered, keeping my eyes cast down. Trying not to look too closely at the way her perfectly proportioned mouth distorted.

"Lucky you. I'm always such a light sleeper. If I had a healthy stomach, the first thing I'd want to swallow would be sleeping pills. Enough of them that I'd never have to wake up again."

It was then that I looked at her exhibition plaque for the first time. *Aphrodite of Pariano. Roman copy of a Greek sculpture, 1st–2nd Century.* Two millennia had raced by silently as bottomless dust and the faint body heat of crowds, an overwhelming multitude of days and nights passing at a dizzying pace. It adorned the naked goddess more elegantly than any ornament, yet simultaneously enveloped her in an unapproachable solitude.

Footsteps were approaching from beyond the gallery doors. "Listen," Venus lowered her voice. As if shielding a candle flame from something immense.

"No painting or sculpture can match the beauty of a single night's dream. Because there, you can become someone else; you can go somewhere else. I can escape my endless stretch of time."

“Are you coming too?” The half-joking words suddenly brushed against my bare skin. Beneath my raincoat. Their meaning left with nowhere to go.


Before I knew it, I had signed and stamped the employment contract Hashibami had brought over along with a chair. My part-time wages would be paid by bank transfer on the tenth of the following month.

*Ah, someone crossed.*

I stopped in my tracks. From the apartment's rust-covered stairs, I could see the bridge. A bridge almost no one ever crossed, looking like a tiny sardine bone. As I watched, a frosted glass window slid open a crack behind me. Through the gap in my hood, I breathed in the air. The warm aroma of cooking, and the savory premonition lurking within it.

When I first found this apartment, I applied for it right away. It was incredibly old, and as a two-story building standing forlornly in a neighborhood lined with small factories, it was the absolute worst when it came to natural light. But the rent was cheap, and depending on how you looked at it, the ivy covering its white walls had a certain charm. It came with a separate bath and toilet, a two-burner stove, a washlet, and for some reason, there was even a landline phone in the room.

The one unexpected thing was that it also came with a landlady. Attached to the building—or rather, to my everyday life.

I dashed down the stairs and opened the door. It wasn't locked. Stepping over the cardboard boxes piled up in the entryway and parting the beaded curtain, I caught sight of Seriko-san peering into a pot amidst the lukewarm scent of boiled onions that hung in the air all year round. Right beside her, under the incandescent light, an oven mitt that had been toasted to a savory brown was just about to transform into a tiny flame.
