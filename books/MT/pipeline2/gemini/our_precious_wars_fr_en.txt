GALLIMARD

Cool rain on a blue lawn. Damp summer grass, the lingering scent of black earth. Always these August showers on the short stems, burnt with gold. The heavy drops stream down the windowpane, winding, snaking, and intertwining into long ribbons of liquid light. How many afternoons spent behind the vaporous veil of the curtain, tracing with a finger their path, nervous and languid at once. Little flyaway hairs curl around the cheeks, and you marvel that they are so white when you are so young, haloed in ether beneath the window. And suddenly your gaze drops from the window to the hand parting the curtain, and the hand is old, so old.

There are places that harpoon you. That wind their mesh around your dreams, that adjust their claws, just enough to let you grow, but leaving in your flesh the bruise of their grip.

There are doors whose sound, when you push them open, is like a cry of time that once again shatters oblivion.

There are staircases whose steps you would so love to climb again, just once, letting the cold polish of the banister glide through your palm.

That is the House.

There are, on the other hand, places that slide the clammy unease of the unknown down the back of your neck. Places you cannot feel resonating within you. Places imposed on you, that you are forced to endure; it's temporary, it's transitional, you have no choice. When the world grows tired of you, they offer you the TV near the bed, the nightstand for your glasses case, the yellow wallpaper. Yellow is cheerful, and besides, everything you need is right here, we'll take good care of you. They make us trade the House for a room in a medically sanctioned dying-house. That yellow wallpaper—the sledgehammer blow to the back of my old neck.

Anyway, the armchair is the only thing I still love. It is the only piece of furniture I was able to bring with me. It has always been an old person's chair; great-grandfather's, whom I never knew, and then Great-Aunt Babel's, whenever she came to visit us. I understand why you love it as you grow older; you recognize yourself in its dust, in its yielding softness. With the edge of my fingernail, I pick at the scuffs in its dusty-pink velvet.

The comfort of nestling your arms into the intimacy of the armrests. The fabric slips right against you like a second skin. It is like returning to the embrace of a smooth, sweet lover, one who has remained far more constant than any man I could ever have known.

Thinking about it carefully, I was the only one who truly loved the House. Even great-great-grandfather, who desired it, imagined it, and built it, didn't love it as much as I did. I loved it enough to stay there my entire life. To abandon my studies in the City, because I suffered too much, so far from the woods of my childhood. To say no to Oktav, when he held out the little ring to me on the Pont-Noir one Wednesday afternoon, because he, for one, would never have wanted us to live in the House. He wanted to move into a big apartment in the old center, not far from the high school where he would have taught and where our future children would have learned to surpass us. I loved the House enough to wish for nothing else in my entire existence but to remain there, nestled in the hollow of familiar things, letting myself be burnished by time exactly like the banister of the spiral staircase.

I always close my eyes when I want to remember it. I do this every day, every waking minute, perhaps. I strive impatiently, violently even, to recreate every room, every corner. I cling to the details, to the shape of the light switches, to the sound of doorknobs turning, to the thin layer of dust on the yellow bulbs. I want to see everything again, feel everything anew. I want to hollow out the space of the present and, with frantic bursts of memory, summon back the places I loved so much, that I knew by heart, that I roamed all my life, and which, now that I am gone, are fading, crumbling away.

The House as I left it, the one from four, perhaps five months ago—that, I remember. I remember it a little too well, in fact. The slow decay of the emptied rooms, the tarnished floorboards, the cold hallways where the paint is peeling—that, yes. The broken stained-glass pane through which the dead leaves blow in, swirling faintly on the hall floor like an exhausted couple waltzing at the end of the night—I can still see it, and very clearly. And also the cast-iron radiators, a faded white, freezing cold in the rooms where no one lives anymore, the bricked-up fireplaces, the sheets draped over the furniture in bedrooms where no one sleeps anymore. It was like that, in the end.

I want to banish this image of the House. Revive the colors, throw wide the bright windows where the intoxicating morning air rushes in, wax the staircase, clean the immense dining-room table. Set the table for fifteen or so people, like in the glory days when everyone, without exception, came.

I want to see again the tall House of white wooden planks that rose up when you returned from the garden, nestled between the tall blue firs and the maples, with the grass breaking in emerald spray against the flight of front steps. To see again the facade with its narrow windows, the veranda jutting out on the left side, the high, pointed roof bristling with chimney stacks and pristine white gables, along with the attic's bull's-eye window perched like the round, golden eye of a reassuring giant, all rimmed with ornate wood. I want to see again the strange, sharp roofs that capped the bow windows, and which, in winter, were fringed with icicles. I want to see again the windows that sparkled in the crisp air, caressed by the branches of trees too close to the House whenever a light breeze blew. I want to see again the great House, with the carved wooden columns framing the front door, glazed with a warm varnish of hardened caramel, and the stained glass of intertwined flowers that filtered bursts of color into the hall when the sun shone through. Painted with immense trellises of tropical foliage, the hall glowed with a soft blue. There, the spiral staircase soared upward in a swirl of coppery wood.

Everything was open; the scents from the kitchen drifted freely and filled the entire House. Voices rumbled from the sitting room up to the attic like the murmur of a comforting torrent rolling through the walls. Deep in my ear, I can hear it still. The floorboards creak, releasing the scent of pine, and even our lightest childhood footsteps make the thick planks squeak. We run up the stairs, because it’s more fun to be dizzy when we reach the top. The first-floor hallway leads to our aunt and uncle’s old bedrooms, the ones from when they were little, which they still occupy when they come to visit us with the cousins. They sleep right across from the bedroom Harriett and I share. It makes playing much easier: they only have to cross the hall and whisper the password at the door so we know for sure they aren't intrusive adults coming to scatter everything with their big, clumsy legs. At the very end of the hallway is the bathroom, the green one, with its window that sticks a little, but through which you can glimpse a patch of the orchard. Opposite that is Great-Aunt Babel’s room, for when she comes to set down her trunks oozing with camphor. When everyone leaves, we have the floor almost entirely to ourselves. On the second floor, we can hardly play; it’s the parents’ floor, the serious floor, the one with the two most spacious bathrooms. Mind you, Louisa, lucky girl that she is, has a private one behind her wardrobe, complete with a little ceramic bathtub. Because she’s the eldest of the girls, she has the largest bedroom, and she gets it all to herself. I wouldn’t trade my room for hers for anything in the world, however, because if I did, Harriett and I wouldn’t be able to make as much noise, given how close it is to the parents' bedroom. That is also where the storage room is, which we don’t even use for games of hide-and-seek anymore: we know full well that it’s the first place anyone thinks of when they need to slip away from the world. The best place to hide, of course, is the attic. It isn't as big as it was when Petit Père was a boy, because since then, they’ve split it in two to make a bedroom for Klaus. I quite like Klaus’s room. It smells like a boy, because he doesn't open the window very much. Since the adults never go up there, we are left in peace. We hear every creak of the roof on stormy nights, and there is something somehow reassuring about that warm attic room, like a wooden cocoon suspended above the forest.

Mais les images défilent en couleurs fanées, vidées de vie. The faces are blurred, and this is the most painful part: realizing that the image of my family—young, alive—is lost forever. I have lost myself, too. Who was I at eight years old? Now that old age breaks my back and cripples my fingers, I feel how irritated I would have been, as a child, by the person I am today, so encumbered by what is no more. I would hate the puppet theater I erect in my mind to endlessly replay dead images. Who were we, out in the woods and in the bedroom, in the kitchen where the soup steams? I manipulate puppets whose faces are fading away. No matter; there comes a time when certain people become so familiar to us that we no longer even need their physical presence for them to be there. It is only the people we grew up with whom we can truly say we know. Our own individual development was faintly tinged by that of the others, like water into which a drop of syrup falls—a single drop, enough to color the entire glass pale mint. I know exactly what Petit Père or Louisa would have replied to a given remark; I can still see Petite Mère’s smile as she watched us dance on spring evenings. And so, I seem to relive for a while, within this present-day body, this painful, creaking body, the days that drew to a close and the days that followed them, deep within the hollow of the House.

I want to remember the intertwining voices of glorious children, Petit Père’s cold, reserved tenderness, Petite Mère’s sudden bursts of inspiration when she would abruptly rise from the table, snatch her brushes from the jar on the sideboard, and dash toward her studio in a corner of the veranda. I want to remember when Klaus, Louisa, and Harriett were still at the House, Klaus already on the brink of adolescence. He was handsome, the prodigious older brother—brilliant, funny, insolently talented. I wish I could grumble again as he ran through scale after scale at six in the morning, when the soft, raspy wail of his trumpet would wake the entire family. I want to find Louisa again, the prettiest of the girls, as if she had absorbed all our mother’s radiance, leaving none for me or Harriett. I remember when I would walk by, dragging my feet across the second-floor parquet, past Louisa’s half-open door: she was never on her bed or in her armchair, but always sitting before her vanity mirror, melting her eyes into her own, searching for the flecks of gold deep within her irises.

What I wouldn't give to be back in that bedroom with the two little beds side by side, Harriett’s and mine, with the lamp in the middle—the cause of so many arguments between us, since I always wanted to read past her bedtime! Her peevish baby face, which she would hide under the duvet, howling that I was keeping her from getting her eight hours of sleep... "You'll just have to wake up later," I would gloat, slowly turning the pages of my novel to drive her crazy. "You know Klaus is just going to wake us up again with that cursed trumpet of his, so turn it off!" she would protest indignantly, close to tears, punching her pillow. "I’ll turn it off when I’ve finished my chapter." "Show me where you are." I would show her the wrong page. "See? Almost done," I would assure her. She would start hissing like a snake: I was lying, I was the worst sister in the world, just wait until she told Louisa... She would let the threat hang, shooting me a furious glare from beneath the sheets. Harriett constantly deferred to our eldest sister to settle our disputes, because Louisa was completely biased and absolutely unfair. She always took the side of our youngest, who had adorable dark curls that Louisa loved to style, whereas she claimed my mop of hair was "perplexifying." But since Harriett was far from docile, Louisa would usually storm out of the room, slamming the door, and shut herself away in her own to dream of grand chandeliers and dinner parties.

Even the House of my adolescence—how I wish I could see it again. The one that haunted me every night in my dorm room, making me loathe the College and curse my studies. They had torn me from my home, from my life, from my walks in the woods and my afternoons spent reading, curled up on the overly hard windowsill. But oh, the triumph of Friday evenings! When I would rush onto the train, bound into my seat, and stamp my feet with impatience, my lips stretched into an irrepressible smile as the inexorable machine carried me far from the towers of the City! I adored arriving at the station, which always seemed to take forever, though, and the moment I spotted Petit Père standing by the open trunk waiting for my suitcase, with Harriett already in the car, since she finished earlier on Fridays.

"Madame Aberfletch? Can you hear me? I'm setting your tray on the desk; you can eat whenever you like."

And now here is this pleasant nurse, coming to dispel it all in her rose perfume. To hell with her white coat and her insolent youth, her clear voice of a living girl who gets to go home at night. She must spend the whole year waiting for summer, that season of the liquid body, of golden evenings.

Summer

I loved nothing more than summers at the House. Everything was radiant then, bathed in the sultry languor of the holidays, which seemed endless, stretched out by long days of delicious boredom. From the first real heat of June, everything began to shimmer, overflowing with life. The maples and the firs seemed gorged with incandescent sap; the grass, an insolent green, was streaked with great swathes of sunlight.

Everyone was returning from the City, flowing back toward the familiar countryside and the forests haloed in lustral shadow. One only had to stand at the edge of the woods to feel the resin-scented wind exhale its murmur. When we were children, my father’s brother and sister, Uncle Bertie and Aunt Hilde, would come every summer with their families. The joy when they announced: “The cousins are arriving tonight!” The day would then become steeped in an impatient excitement that had us skipping in circles in the living room, and, scolded by Petit Père, we would run back up the stairs, stifling nervous laughter. The afternoon was spent in bustling preparations; I loved assisting the parents with meticulous attention. Like a little despot, I saw to it that the attic was aired out, the toy chests opened, the sheets folded carefully on the beds. I would dash down the spiral staircase, polished for the occasion; I would twirl in the hall, pierced by the fractured light of the stained-glass window. I would go into the kitchen twenty times, lean over the stove, and inhale the savory aromas, lifting the lids of the soup tureens with a slightly trembling hand. I knew that the food being nearly ready was the signal: the cousins would soon be here, and before long we would all be seated together at the table in an unbearable hubbub of resounding laughter, releasing the frenzy of a long day of waiting.

The evening the cousins arrived, at the start of every summer, was always the same. They were usually exhausted from the drive they had endured all day, the cramped confines of the cars, the hot breath of the asphalt, and the sun reflecting off gleaming paintwork. Nevertheless, we sat down to the table in high spirits. When everything seemed to waltz with lights and laughter and scents; when we were endlessly interrupted mid-story by a passing dish or by another story springing up at the other end of the table; and when I leaned back in my chair to steal glances at those we hadn’t seen since Christmas, I felt immersed in a bath of liquid joy, golden and sparkling, perfumed by the honeyed scent of Uncle Bertie’s cigars or that of thyme melting into the chicken juices. My gaze would circle the table, drifting mechanically over familiar faces before catching on those of the cousins, who grew prettier year after year. I was fascinated, most of all, by their noses—small and round, a trait inherited from Bertie’s in-laws. He himself had not escaped the family nose, long and hooked, down which the light slid as across a blade. Amelia and Magda took entirely after their mother: a sort of blonde, fleshy grace, with skin flushing pink just beneath the neck—the skin of fresh flowers beaded with water in crystal vases. Always seated side by side, carefully passing the pitcher whenever it came their way, they laughed openly at all of Klaus’s or Harriett’s antics. Every summer, as soon as the two cousins stepped out of the car and set foot on the front steps, they and Louisa would size each other up. In those first moments of their reunion, it was impossible to tell whether they and Louisa were about to tear each other to pieces or exchange some pretty-girl barb; but no, they would fall into one another's arms and sweep laughing into the House, leaving a scent of precious oils in their wake. Harriett and I spent little time with the cousins, who shared Louisa’s temperament and were the same age as her. We were the little nuisances—noisy and, I think, a bit unsettling. Klaus showed no particular interest in the cousins, and sat at the table next to Aleksander, Aunt Hilde’s darling son. Aleksander paired the traditional Aberfletch nose with a steely gaze that stood in sharp contrast to the dirty-pond depths of our own eyes. His hair was slightly more ashen and pale than ours, and his head, held very straight, caught the gleam of the dining room lamp, which cast its warm halo over the entire table. Aleksander and Klaus did not get along particularly well, but as the eldest children, they had learned to stick together in a sort of wary brotherhood, halfway between competition and solidarity. They competed in dexterity whenever they poured the water; it was a contest to see who could raise the carafe highest without the thin stream deviating from its course. We clapped our hands, staring at the churning water rapidly filling the glass, and shrieked when it came close to overflowing.

The adults were on edge; they sent us to bed early, telling us not to “carry on until all hours like last year.” We exchanged a joyful glance with the cousins, our bond suddenly rekindled by this memory of a shared past. In our eyes, for a brief moment, flashed memories awash in summer light, quivering with laughter and scraped knees. We danced the cousins to their room, thrilled to have them back at our house, and Klaus let his warm, breaking voice rumble; playing the “grown-up,” he said, “I’m heading up to my room; be good, kids.” Aleksander grumbled a bit, because he was the exact same age as him but was forced to bunk with the two girl cousins, whom he blithely ignored most of the time. Then Louisa and Klaus disappeared once more into the shadows of the staircase, and we—Harriett, the cousins, and I—lingered in the dark hallway, the neutral territory between our two rooms. Through the half-open door, we saw with delight that their suitcases had miraculously been brought up by a devoted parent, and were resting on their respective beds in the yellow halo of the lamp. That room stayed empty all year round, its mattresses cold and bare, except when the cousins came. Then, large, crisp sheets would be spread out for them, still fragrant with the lavender sprigs scattered in the wardrobes. The cousins always had tired eyes on that first night, but they seemed genuinely happy to be back. Harriett and I stood in their doorway, watching them rush to the window as if to check that the view hadn’t changed since last summer. Then, satisfied, they rubbed their eyes with a yawn and wished us a good night. I took Harriett by the shoulder to force her back into our room, because she was far too wound up to sleep, wanting to play hide-and-seek in the dark right that second, or spy on the adults, or surprise Klaus in his attic by creeping up the stairs so they wouldn't creak. I told her that we had every night of the entire summer to play, and she followed me with a resignation tinged with delight.

Lying wide awake in bed, my whole body turned toward the ceiling drowned in blue shadow, I felt the weight of the two floors above me, and I sensed the bustling of the adults below as they cleared the table, accompanied by the warm rumble of their husky voices. I listened to the sounds of the night settling in: Louisa upstairs, hurrying from her washroom to her bed; the cousins across the hall, putting their things away in the wardrobes—arriving, settling in, reclaiming the room they returned to every summer. I felt surrounded by the people I loved, as though I were part of a greater whole and the House, finally filled with this sprawling family of my own blood, had truly come alive. I knew it wasn't a big deal if we went to bed a little early today, because there would be others—other nights. Harriett’s raspy little voice would always eventually break the silence, venturing: “It’s nice having the cousins here.” On other nights, I would have told her to be quiet, because I didn't feel like talking. But right then, I was snuggled deep in my sheets, and outside, the summer wind was blowing softly against the shutters. So I simply answered: “Yes, it really is.” We talked about what we would do tomorrow. In the near-total darkness of the bedroom, all I could make out of Harriett was a small dark lump on the pillow all swollen with pale shadow, enormous in proportion to her head. And this was my favorite time of day, filled with promises, plans, and ideas made even wilder because they were already intertwining with a dream-filled half-sleep. We slipped into the silence like a spark across the surface of the pond, and the waters of sleep closed over our eyes.

We let the days flow by like a stream of liquid light. It was the precious time of elastic hours, of evanescent mornings and endless afternoons.

Morning was always an every-man-for-himself affair. You hardly noticed the House was populated by anyone but us, everything was so quiet, with the closed doors of the late sleepers, and the living room empty but still smelling of Bertie’s honey cigars.

I always woke up before Harriett, because my bed was closest to the window, and a ray of white sunlight always filtered through the crack in the shutters, carving out a warm spindle across my sheets. I would linger for a few minutes in the warm sheets, but I could hear the birds outside, the indistinct buzzing of wasps bumping against the window frame, and suddenly I would be seized by a wild urge to look out at the garden, to see the sparkling morning lawn unfold before my eyes, and to feel the glorious breeze billow my dress. I wouldn't open the shutters, so as not to wake Harriett, and my eyes, accustomed to the pale dimness, had little trouble making out the obstacles separating my bed from the door. I would slip slowly out of the room, taking care not to make the floorboards creak too loudly. My toes would first meet the firmness of the wood floor, then the slightly rough rug that tickles the soles of the feet. I carefully avoided the sharp corners of our two beds, the toy chest, and the wardrobe, casting an anxious glance at the little ball curled up beneath her floral duvet to make sure she hadn't moved. Then I would place one hand on the doorknob, the other on the latch, and pull with a sharp tug so the door wouldn't squeak—for a poorly oiled door always makes more noise when opened slowly. The morning light flooding the hallway would make me unsteady on my feet for a moment. The row of closed doors was intimidating, making it impossible to tell whether I was the first one up or not. Still a little dazed, I would make my way down the stairs. There again, I had to avoid the groaning of the wood, control my footing, favor certain steps over others, and thus take giant strides, clinging to the banister so as not to lose my balance. It was the first adventure of the day, re-enacted every morning.

As I crossed the deserted hall, the foliage unfurled frighteningly, for the morning light cast a cold sheen across it, and the painted flowers seemed muted, like ancient stars slowly fading away. The floor tiles were always freezing, and I would hurry into the kitchen. There, the clock was ticking, and it was the only time I ever noticed it, for the rest of the time its mechanical pulse was drowned out by the noises of the House: the laughter, the voices, the discordant clatter of pots and pans clanging together as they were put away. The kitchen was cool; the windows and shutters on the garden level had been left open to let the night air circulate. In the morning, we would chase out the bark-colored moths that had slipped inside to nestle in the warmth of the walls. They would flutter about clumsily, their trembling wings beating against the windowpanes, unable to find a way out.

I would close the windows, biting my lip whenever they slammed a bit too loudly. The dishes on the sink had dried, and a puddle of cold water trickled drop by drop from the edge down to the drain. I loved the kitchen windows, with their small white-framed panes overlooking the awakening orchard. I would hoist myself up onto the counter, cluttered with jars and spatulas, and press my nose against the glass. Through the fog of my breath, I would gaze at the vague silhouettes of the fruit-laden trees. The timid emerald leaves quivered in the sun, like tiny green-veined fish.

I passed through the living room, through the library—both deserted—and I was already sighing with boredom, telling myself that adults really were too lazy, sleeping like that while the day unfolded without waiting for us. Suddenly, I hear movement upstairs. The sound of light slippers makes the floorboards creak; the game then is to guess who it is. If it comes down the stairs fast, it’s Harriett or Klaus. The cousins hurtle down fast too, but less than we do, especially the first few days: they have to get used to the treacherous steps in their gleaming wood all over again. A heavy, slow step is Uncle Bertie. In the morning, Bertie smells extraordinarily good, because he always washes before coming down, and I saw him, once, slapping great splashes of cologne onto his freshly shaved neck with the flat of his hand. His skin is then red and gleaming beneath the chin, and his wake blossoms with tall grasses swaying in the wind.
