Cherry

The Dunaj howls. And they are already here, just as I remember them—hungry, restless. They gleam as if their wings were doused in gasoline, shimmering with violet, blue, red, and yellow.

The flock circles above the village.

June has let in a lot of sun; already at its beginning, the fruit on the cherry trees is sweet.

Grandfather, who avoids Grandmother’s illness, to whom the ceremonies surrounding the illness are alien, who wants to know nothing about the illness, to touch nothing of the illness, watches from the veranda as the starlings lower their flight over the orchard.

He strikes an aluminum milk can with a metal rod. The booming echoes through the valley.

I watch him. It must be booming inside him too. His body trembles; he has skin like the skin of the men who inhabit the Carpathian Arc, I think. Bronze. Ebony.

The starlings take flight, a massive swarm of them. As they fly over the house, they cast a shadow on the floor of the west room, the one heated by a cast-iron stove in the cold months.

A moment later they return over the orchard.

They are stubborn.

Successive strikes against the metal send them into a nervous flutter. The booming keeps them in flight.

Grandfather takes a cigarette break. They take advantage of the silence and land in the spreading tree. They stuff their small bodies with fruit.

They’ve already learned to recognize the silence, I say to Ann.

Yes, she agrees.

Ann is the brightness here. She is interested in light and is light herself. She arrived here this morning, straight into warm May, from a distant country. The journey is still in her. A scent from elsewhere is still in her—freesia, patchouli. She stands with me on the veranda, adjusting her eyes to the early summer.

Her eyes follow the light.

Ann knows light.

She studies light.

She hunts for light.

She divines from light.

I touch her chest with my fingers, right where constellations of moles grow.

Ann says: They need to be counted.

Alright, I answer.

When I was a child, I counted Grandmother’s birthmarks. She hid very little from me. She let me count the tiny dots that covered her back, as well as the ones around her breasts. She let my gaze into hidden nooks, like the curves of her ears or the crooks of her elbows.

You are of my body too, she would say.

We move from the veranda into the west room. The melody Grandfather is playing can be heard inside. Grandmother watches the shadow of the flock of birds. She raises an emaciated hand, as if volunteering to answer a question.

There, she says, and points to the wardrobe.

She asks me to take out her summer dresses. This surprises me. Usually, she wears the pajamas I buy for her at the market in the nearby town of Stary Sad. She likes the ones in powder mint.

I lay the dresses out on the sofa bed.

This one, Grandmother whispers.

She has chosen the blood-colored one. I take it in my hands. It is velvet.

Take it to him, she asks.

I do it without a word of protest.

Grandfather is on the veranda finishing another *Klubowy*—out of sentiment for the past, this is what he calls all cigarettes, a habit I picked up from him. He takes the dress in his hands. His dry skin catches on the material. He sniffs the fabric.

Rose, he says.

I look at him. The recent years have withered his body. More and more often his gaze wanders off, he hides his eyes, dodges with them. He is unable to hold or return a gaze. I would like to see him stronger, I think.

He turns and says: Come with me.

I go.

He heads toward the workshop.

Once there, he nails hazel branches into a cross-like shape and dresses this cross in Grandmother’s blood-red dress. He examines his handiwork. He adjusts the foam shoulder pads, which give the object an air of surprise, as if something living were raising its arms.

Will it do? he asks.

It’ll do, I answer.

I know he needs my nodding. Here, in this illness, there is no room for objection.

Grandfather nods at my nodding. He takes the effigy in his hands and walks into the orchard with the dress-clad skeleton. Behind him, a procession: the dog, Dunaj, and the tomcat, Klakier.

Grandmother observes us from the window of the west room. She claws at the skin on her cheek with her fingernails. I know this reflex. It is born in her when she is scrutinizing something. She can scratch herself all over and know nothing of the scratching.

Grandfather leans a ladder against the trunk of the cherry tree. I assist. Carefully, he places his feet on the rungs. He reaches the top. With a piece of wire, he fastens the scarecrow to a branch.

A scare-hunger, I mutter under my breath.

Grandmother’s red dress begins to move in the wind. A gust enters it and fills the space where a warm body used to be. I get the feeling the dress has come to life.

The ghost of the orchard, I say to Ann, who joins us.

Nothing works like a red dress, she hums.

Starlings circle above the tree.

Back in the west room, I ask Grandmother if she remembers wearing that outfit last spring. There are photographs of it.

She nods.

The red accentuated her gray hair.

You know, she says, the illness must have already been inside me back then. Can you imagine?

I nod.

With my fingertips, I examine the wound she has scratched into her cheek.

For the rest of the day, we take turns walking over to the window of the west room and looking out at the orchard. The blood-red of the dress blends with the sweet cherries ripening in the full sun. We will harvest the fruit, saved from the starlings' beaks, for Grandmother. Grandfather will make sweet jams from them.

The West Room

We are freshening up the room located in the western part of the house for the illness. Throughout my childhood, it served for years as our spacious cold room. We stored the meat of pigs slaughtered for church holidays in it. My grandparents kept furniture here, old paintings, jars filled with preserves, jugs of honeydew honey, cardboard boxes full of Christmas decorations. Since the tragedy—the one from many years ago that befell my parents and aunt—Grandmother has made her den here.

Soon, radiator ribs will be installed in here, Grandfather says, standing on the threshold of the room.

Yes, I nod.

Grandmother can't get cold during her illness, he says.

Yes, yes, I nod.

I can put up with the cold, but get this pink out of here, Grandmother says.

Yes, yes, I nod.

Pink reminds me of meat, she says.

Yes, I nod.

Make it white in here, she asks.

I nod.

I am obedient, that is why I came back here from far away, why I left my life in the eternally bright city, to be obedient.

Obedience, I say to Ann.

Obedience, she repeats, and runs her hand over my hair.

Deal

We begin to freshen up the room. As if the illness required special measures and actions of us.

We are cooperating with it, making a deal, I think.

It has us now too, I say to Ann, handing her the overalls I wore here in my teens.

The two of us work. The illness binds us in physical labor. We need to exhaust ourselves.

Grandmother’s eyes are watchful.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us and watch, watch.

Let’s work ourselves to death, I say.

Every so often, Grandmother peeks into the room, nods, and watches, watches. She likes that the plants and holy pictures will stand out clearly against the white walls.

Make it white in here, she asks again.

The instructions are simple—white.

We scrape away the old layers of paint with a putty knife. We hand the tool back and forth whenever pain touches the arms and forearms of the one working. I taste dust in my mouth; it grits between my teeth.

I hold the ladder Ann is standing on. She wrestles with the wall. Her arms are bare, and a fine dust settles on them. You can clearly see her muscles working. She is beautiful when she wields the scraper. She removes layers of paint that have been applied here over the last thirty years. You can read a lot from them. Recorded in them are the grand occasions that demanded the room be freshened up: a baptism, a first communion, a confirmation, and now, an illness. I recognize every single color we strip away.

Maybe if we were to study them, we could read even more, the way environmental changes are read from a soil profile.

The history of this house falls to the floor.

We make room for the new.

Ann is beautiful when she tenses her body, sets her mind in motion, talks all the while, when everything that she is goes into motion.

We need what is bright, I say to Ann, mixing the paint.

Yes, she agrees.

We get messy in the whiteness.

At night, while the room is drying, I step inside to look at the space.

Hoo-hoo, hoo-hoo, I hoot.

Behind me, I hear Ann.

Hoo-hoo, hoo-hoo, she repeats after me.

The sounds vibrate within these dozen or so square meters.

We wheeze from the dust. It is in our throats. We spit white.

In the morning, we show Grandmother the room.

White, she says.

She presses her cool hands to our faces and strokes us, just as she strokes Dunaj when he comes home after days of running wild.

Grandmother starts bringing the plants into the west room.

Life, life, she says.

After two pots of calathea, she grows weak. She sits on the floor, leans against the wall. Without a word, we take over her work; we become her hands. We bring the plants—moved out to the hallway for the duration of the renovation—into the west room: aloe, asparagus fern, fern, geranium, Siberian ivy, Christmas cacti, philodendron, croton, dracaena, begonia, caladium.

Grandmother’s eyes are watchful.

Grandmother’s eyes follow us.

Grandmother’s eyes watch, watch.

Hunchbacked

When the smell of paint is gone, we spend the night in the west room, the three of us. We do not know yet that it will stay this way, that the nights to come will be shared. But that is not yet. Now, the moon is waxing.

There it is, swelling, hunchbacked, I think.

I am growing accustomed to the closeness.

We divide the bed and the sofa bed among ourselves.

We shed our daytime layers of clothing.

We mingle our scents.

My women, I think, falling asleep, are beautiful, and fragrant, fragrant.

Throat

In the room, the breathing of my grandmother and Ann can be heard. The night has halted our pupils, restless from the day. Ever since the illness moved in with us, I am the last to fall asleep.

Only my guard is strong, I think. No one else can keep watch for so long.

In the corner of the room, a cast-iron stove gives off heat. The final days of June have brought a chill here, into the valley.

It is about to begin.

Light.

The light cuts across our faces. I look at Ann, whose face is illuminated by the headlights of a truck hauling cattle to the slaughterhouse. She opens her eyes. And they are deep, bottomless. They observe the waking world, and when they notice me watching, they pause.

For a moment, Ann keeps watch alongside me.

But she cannot stand guard the way I do.

She enters sleep.

And here, oho, it begins. Several dozen meters from the west room, large trucks take the curve, their headlights filling the interior with light.

Light and dark.

Light.

The trucks are bringing animals to the slaughterhouse. I imagine the cows’ bodies—warm, damp.

In my childhood, I didn't always manage to stay awake for this moment of the night. Sometimes I would fall asleep and be woken by the spectacle already underway. It was accompanied by sounds like tonight’s: guttural, drawn-out, which, instead of dissolving in the air, can hang over the village for a long time.

The bellowing of cows.

The whining of dogs.

The hooting of the night.

And this too—the smell: of dung, urine, sweat.

The bodies of my women are clearly visible when illuminated by the slow sweeps of the Jelcz trucks’ headlights. Their features are—depending on the falling light, and the shadows too—at times dead, at times alive.

My grandmother is lying on her side, her hand tucked under her left cheek. She looks like a child. A child, the kind they had wanted me to bear.

But now is no time for life. Death—that is what occupies my mind. That is what I serve. My grandmother is my child. I am my grandmother’s mother. This is good, I think.

Ann’s body is beside me. The porcelain of her skin, the whiteness of her hair, eyelashes, and eyebrows never ceases to amaze me. There is something twin-like about us, and there is also within us—and we know this—something that binds us together: much that is squandered.

The bodies of the women close to me react to the light, their eyeballs moving beneath their eyelids.

Just a moment more.

They open their eyes.

One after the other.

My grandmother looks and whispers into the light: Water will take care of it, or fire.

Ann, woken once more, sets her bare feet on the floorboards, her toenails painted a dark burgundy. Just like mine. We paint them for ourselves now, no longer for our people, who are far away. They have to be far away; I don’t let them near the illness. I belong to the illness, not to them. There is only room here for Ann. She can plunge nails painted like this into dung or compost, and wallow in it.

Sisters in decay, I think of us.

Come on, she tells me.

It’s possible, I think, that she has fallen asleep again, but left her gaze behind in the waking world. She is most terrifying when she stares like that, when you can't tell who her eyes belong to.

A concert, I say to Ann.

The hooves know no rhythm, I say.

They’re singing, I say.

The animals are singing. Their song comes from within, from warm bellies, I think.

Then the dogs start up. First Rybowicz’s Wolfspitz below the slope on the far side of the village; then the throats of the slaughterhouse dogs get going; a moment later Dunaj joins in, followed by the mayor’s wife’s Samoyeds and the dogs chained to their kennels at the rectory—descendants of those restless mutts that have been crossbreeding for generations in the village gullies. Strange, I think, that I can tell these howls apart, the melody of each dog. A little longer here, and I'll be guessing what these howls are for, what purpose they serve.

Ann takes her finger from my lips.

To have a dog’s body and act accordingly, to surrender to hunger, to deny myself nothing. To fawn only over a chosen few, to nip and bark at everything else. To be a picky bitch. To love one person, to have duties toward them, to obey them. And also: to disappear for days on end. To have the right to do so, to indulge.

Indulge, Ann repeats.

The barking carries, waking up other sounds.

The whole village is howling.

Tonight, this place is the deep throat of a hungry animal, I think.

Faith

At night, the slaughterhouse wakes the birds. This has consequences for the valley. Time gets muddled. Something comes loose in time. People open their eyes at the wrong time, walk around dazed during the day. At noon they look at the sun, and where it sits in the sky doesn't add up for them.

Grandfather gets the most muddled. Grandfather rises with the birds. The waking world takes him by surprise.

I wake up and it's empty, he tells me about times like these.

Empty? I ask.

Empty, yes, there is nothing to grab hold of, nothing to recognize yourself in, he says.

He gets up in the night and walks around the house. He clambers up the ladder to the attic and paces up there. Taking steps soothes him. He is also soothed by stroking the antlers he has been collecting for decades. When the days and nights blur together for Grandfather, he can even fall asleep up there.

In the mornings, when I go to take my place at the desk to work on code—clean, intuitive, beautiful code, the kind I sometimes dream about—I often find him among the stag crowns. Grandfather has a story for every set of antlers. He invents lives for this bone tissue. To get him to tell such a story, you have to approach him carefully; you have to praise the collection in the attic. His stories about the deer are full of the panic of animals, of the forest; they take place in the rain and the fog. It is only out of such weather that animals emerge in Grandfather’s tales.

Rivers

Waking birds is a sin, Grandmother says.

In the orchard belonging to the slaughterhouse, nothing has borne fruit for many years. The earth there is trampled by herds of cows. Most of the animals are unloaded at the top of the hill and led straight into the concrete walls. Those for which there is no room in the cold holding pens are let out into the orchard. They take advantage of this: they empty their deep bellies, they piss, as if they held endless rivers inside them. Urine in such quantities scorches the earth; trees struggle to grow here.

The orchard is bare, stunted, sick.

It’s because of the damp from inside the cows, I say.

Fire or water will take care of it, Grandmother says.

I believe her.

The greengage trees in this orchard haven’t borne fruit for years, she says.

I nod.

Sweet

Grandmother cannot resist the living. She would take anything like that into the west room, invite it into her space. She would want the living to wander before her eyes, to move, to scuttle, to flutter.

Let it parade, she says.

Let it fly, she says.

Let it be, she says.

Grandmother has adored the living since forever—that is, for as long as I can remember. You could, I think, trace the generations of whatever has proliferated here in the western part of the house. And if you were to ask these latest generations, these living ones, where they come from, they would probably say—if they could speak—that they come from the cool room of a human, a female human, Róża.

She wants, I think, for the living—especially now—to occupy her mind, to give her responsibilities, to distract her from the illness.

Grandmother studies the living with curiosity. This passion—as I call it, this weakness—as Grandfather calls it, has rendered her useless so many times.

It was a nightmare taking Róża out to the fields, Grandfather begins, reminding me, all at once, of those childhood days.
