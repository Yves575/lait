Hearing Transcript

LAMAR: Will you need a Bible?

HUNT: No, thank you.

LAMAR: Please raise your right hand. Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?

HUNT: I do.

LAMAR: I understand you have information relevant to this committee?

HUNT: That is correct.

HUNT: Where should I start? With the day of the accident?

June 2021

Nate Hunt was waiting for his wife. Lucy was late.

"Dad, I'm bored," whined Eliza, their daughter. Nate held her on his lap. The child's hair, full of static from rubbing against her sweater, tickled Nate's nose.

"Yes, I know," he sighed. "You've said that already."

Nate was bored too. All these ceremonies, tours, and press conferences seemed to have no end. At the same time, he was terrified. You might think those two emotional states were mutually exclusive, but that wasn't the case at all. He had gone through this the last time, eight years ago. Only back then, he was here alone. He hadn't had to mentally rehearse what he would tell his child if something went wrong. He still couldn't find the right words. It was possible such words simply didn't exist.

"When is Mommy coming?" the little girl asked.

"Soon," he replied.

"But how soon, exactly?"

"I don't know, sweetheart."

"Ty khochesh konfetu, rebyonka?" The woman in the next chair rustled a bag of hard candies and smiled encouragingly. But Eliza wasn't in the mood to talk to strangers. She turned away from the woman and buried her face in her father's chest.

"Prostite, ona sonnaya." Nate gave an apologetic smile. He had learned Russian somewhat by accident. He had quizzed Lucy so many times before her exams that he had picked up a thing or two. His favorite word—*pochemuchka*, meaning someone who asks too many questions—fit Eliza to a T.

The woman nodded in understanding; she had probably gotten up before dawn today, too. *I wonder how many times she's done this,* Nate thought. *Her third? Fourth?* She showed no signs of nerves; on the contrary, a broad smile never left her lips. Maybe that was because, alongside the candy, she also had a pack of sedatives in her purse.

"Let's at least play a game," Eliza pleaded.

"Sure," Nate agreed. "How about 'I Spy'?"

"Okay. You go first."

Nate looked around the room. It resembled a classroom or a dentist's waiting room: harsh fluorescent lighting, beige walls decorated with faded posters, rows of plastic chairs, spindly plants on the windowsill. Except that a thick pane of Plexiglas ran right down the middle of the room. On the other side stood a long table holding three microphones, each labeled: Cyrillic on top, Latin alphabet underneath. Anton Kovalyov. Lev Zaytsev. Lucy Poplaski.

"I spy something beginning with 'M'," Nate whispered.

"A map?" Eliza asked.

"No."

"A magazine?"

He shook his head.

"A microphone?"

"Yes. Your turn."

The girl began to squirm, paying absolutely no mind to the fact that she was digging first an elbow, then a knee, into her father’s stomach. The people gathered in the room—members of other families, officials, journalists—waved and smiled at her. But Eliza ignored them completely; she was focused on the game. She took competition of any kind extremely seriously. When she had come in second in a sack race at some school event, she had cried for the rest of the day. "Eliza is a very ambitious little girl," her teacher had told them as they were leaving; her tone indicating she might even be a little *too* ambitious. "She gets it from her mother," Nate had replied without a moment’s hesitation.

"I spy something starting with 'r'," she said.

"Rug?"

"No."

"Hmm..." Nate rubbed his chin. "Radiator?"

"No."

"Railing?"

"Not that either." The girl shook her head. With evident satisfaction.

"In that case, I give up."

"A rocket!" Eliza cried triumphantly.

Nate looked out the window. It rose toward the sky amid an endless expanse of sun-scorched grass—wreathed in a halo of milky-white steam, visible through the latticework of the scaffolding. It didn't belong in this landscape: the faded steppes of Kazakhstan, the crumbling, peeling buildings, the howling of stray dogs. It looked like a relic of some extinct alien civilization. In a way, that was exactly what it was: the Baikonur Cosmodrome had, after all, been built by the Soviet Union. Two factors argued for placing it precisely here: first, the latitude made it easier to ascend to the desired orbit. Second, if something went wrong—for example, if a rocket exploded right after liftoff—the risk of flaming pieces of sheet metal falling on someone's head was relatively small. All around, as far as the eye could see, there was only grass.

Nate had seen an explosion like that. He was six at the time, visiting family in Florida, and it just so happened that the repeatedly delayed launch of the Space Shuttle Challenger was coming up. His parents took him to the Kennedy Space Center so he could see the launch with his own eyes, especially since the crew included Christa McAuliffe, an ordinary schoolteacher who was going to give a lesson from space. He remembered it with a child’s memory, as if he’d had a camera with him and had snapped random pictures: poorly framed, out of focus, overexposed with emotion. He had been given a paper NASA cap that kept slipping down over his eyes, making him miserable. They took their seats in the bleachers right behind a man in a cowboy hat. The man completely blocked his view, so little Nate was already mad at the whole world. His father put him on his shoulders so he could see better, ignoring the protests of the people in the row behind them. Then came a blinding flash and a roar, hot air in his hair, vibrations rippling through his entire body until his teeth rattled and his skin tingled. The rocket climbed upward, drowning out the cheers, belching fire, faster and faster, until it turned into a tiny dot in the sky. And then that dot flared up and turned into several smaller dots. Some of them began to fall, while others traced fanciful spirals in the air. For a split second, Nate thought this was exactly what was supposed to happen—that these were some sort of cosmic fireworks, like on the Fourth of July—and he was thrilled. The joy didn’t last long, and what followed—the sobbing, the screaming, the chaos—haunted his dreams for years to come. Despite this, he too tied his life to space. The vastness fascinated, terrified, and drew him in.

A murmur swept through the room, and camera shutters began clicking rapidly, firing one after another. *Finally*, Nate exhaled. On the other side of the glass, a door opened and the Soyuz crew—Lev, Anton, and Lucy—entered the room. His wife was the one the camera lenses were trained on. First of all, she was undoubtedly more photogenic than the Russians. With her dark curly hair pulled into a ponytail, a wide smile, and a turned-up, freckled nose, she looked like the friendly girl next door—someone you could become best friends with in five minutes. Second of all, she was one of the last American women who would fly into space from Kazakhstan. After a hiatus of over a decade, the States finally had their own means of transport to orbit again: the Dragon capsules, built by an eccentric billionaire in true American tradition. The first flights had been a success, and NASA was already negotiating a new, larger contract that would completely free the Americans from their reliance on Russian help. The clock was ticking.

“Uvazhayemiye kollegi, dorogiye gosti…” began the director of Roscosmos, Yuri Rybkin, a gray-haired, imposing man in a cream-colored blazer.

“What’s that man saying?” asked Eliza.

“Nothing interesting.”

“But what?”

“That a beautiful chapter in the history of friendship between our nations is coming to a close, but a new one is beginning.”

“What does that mean?”

That soon the Russians would no longer be able to charge ninety million dollars for a seat on a Soyuz, Nate thought. That without those regular infusions of cash, Roscosmos would find itself on the brink of bankruptcy. That beneath the declarations of readiness for continued cooperation lay envy, resentment, and bitterness.

“That we’ll be flying into space together, but separately,” he said instead.

The Russian official concluded his speech, and the microphone was handed over to the NASA representative, Deputy Director of Human Spaceflight Steve Ayers—a stocky, balding man in thick-lensed glasses. More lofty platitudes, wishes for a successful mission, agency newspeak. At last, it was time for family goodbyes: forced intimacy in front of the cameras, surrounded by strangers, through the plexiglass. Nate and his daughter sat down across from Lucy. He wanted to tell her so many things. That he was so damn proud. That he’d miss her. That he was happy for her. That he was scared for her. But he couldn't bring himself to do it, not in this crowd.

“Hey.” She was the first to speak. “How was your morning?”

Clichés, then. Empty words. But empty words could be filled with something, after all; they could resonate with something entirely unrelated to their literal meaning. The timbre of a voice, a pause, a glance—this was how they told each other everything that couldn't be said, things they wouldn’t even know how to name, defying semantics.

“Fine,” Nate replied. “Right, Eliza? Fine?”

“When are you coming back?” the little girl asked. The question was also an accusation.

“You know that. In six months.”

“You won’t be at my birthday.”

“I will be, sweetie,” Lucy replied with a sad smile. “More than once, actually.”

“What?”

“Remember? The station orbits the Earth once every ninety minutes. If your party lasts three hours, I’ll fly over the house twice during that time. And I'll be thinking about you then. Okay?”

“It’s not the same.”

Lucy pressed her hand against the glass. A poor substitute for real contact, a requirement of the quarantine.

“I know, sweetie.”

“Then why are you going?”

Lucy didn't get the chance to answer. The time for goodbyes had come to an end. The next time they would see each other was in six months. Assuming everything went well.

The Orthodox priest’s robe, embroidered with gold thread, sparkled in the flash of the cameras. The aspergillum swept through the air in a wide arc; holy water trickled down Lucy’s cheeks like tears. Another tradition, another superstition. For the Russians, every stage of flight preparation—and there were many—was tied to some ritual, with a new one added after every mission; there was more folklore here than at a country wedding. Every crew member must lay flowers at the Yuri Gagarin monument. Hoist their country’s flag up the mast. Plant a sapling in Cosmonauts Alley. Sign their name on their hotel room door in Baikonur. On the eve of the launch, watch *White Sun of the Desert*, a Soviet box-office hit from the seventies. On top of all this, of course, came the mandatory blessing by a priest, regardless of one’s personal preferences or faith—and not just once, but twice. It was as if the Russians believed that the success of the flight depended not on the laws of nature or mathematical equations, but on the will of the capricious heavens, which had to be appeased by weeks-long ceremonies.

Lucy boarded the bus that was to take the crew to the launch pad. She was already wearing her Sokol spacesuit: custom-tailored, equipped with dozens of laces, buttons, and snaps—a veritable cosmic corset. Putting it on had taken nearly two hours and required the assistance of several technicians, and since it was completely airtight, each crew member was additionally provided with a portable ventilation unit—a small blue suitcase—to keep from boiling alive.

When the bus pulled away, when Lucy finally found herself beyond the reach of cameras and microphones, if only for a moment, she rested her forehead against the window and closed her eyes. She had been up since four in the morning; it was approaching six in the evening, and she still had a three-hour flight to the International Space Station ahead of her in a capsule the size of an elevator. She had to take advantage of every chance to rest; besides, the Russians were already asleep.

She, however, could not fall asleep. Her thoughts kept returning to Eliza’s question. Why? What for? Of course, she had answered it thousands of times before: first during endless selection interviews, then in media interviews, public appearances, and private conversations. But back then, she had said what was expected of her, what she was supposed to say, what other astronauts had been repeating for decades: that she would be serving science, that space exploration was humanity’s most important mission, that she wanted to be an inspiration to little girls all over the world, and so on and so forth. Only it was all lies. If someone had given her truth serum—assuming such a thing existed—her answer would have been less lofty. Why? Because nothing gives you such a rush, such a thrill, as flying at thirteen thousand kilometers an hour. Because it’s an incredible adventure. Because others couldn’t, and I could. Because it’s proof that I am the best.

All this made the resentment in her daughter’s voice, the reproach, hurt even more. It wasn't about the birthday, after all. Lucy was almost never home. She had spent the last four years living out of suitcases, flying between Washington, Houston, Toulouse, Moscow, and Cologne—endless training sessions, courses, preparations, exams. Every hour, indeed every minute, of the mission had to be planned and repeatedly rehearsed at least a year in advance. Lucy had to know every safety procedure by heart, memorize every manual and technical diagram cold, and know the name of every screw, switch, and wire in both English and Russian. If she were actually doing all this for the good of humanity, perhaps she could justify it to herself, silence her guilty conscience. But she wasn't. She wondered if her colleagues had such thoughts too, if they too mourned missed first steps and words, or if this was simply the lot of women: to feel guilt over their own ambition.

Something else was gnawing at her. The day before, she had met with Deputy Director Ayers. It was unscheduled, didn't appear on any itinerary, and the words spoken there would be nowhere to be found in any press reports. Mounting tensions. Conflict at the highest levels. Fear of escalation. It was hard to believe something like this could happen in the twenty-first century, and in blood-soaked Europe, no less. But Director Ayers had been completely serious. Clouds were gathering on the horizon.

The International Space Station was a child of nineties optimism. The Russians and Americans, freed from the logic of the Cold War, no longer had to race among the stars, so they joined forces for the good of humanity. Through a joint effort, with the involvement of Europe and Japan, they constructed a laboratory orbiting the Earth—for a mere one hundred and twenty billion dollars. A symbol of a new era, of shared dreams, of the end of history. Of course, behind the lofty slogans lay pragmatic calculations. The Americans wanted access to Soviet technology, while the Russians needed money to keep their space program alive amidst the chaos of perestroika, and no one but the Americans could put up the necessary funds at the time. Everyone got what they wanted, and everyone was happy.

But today, over twenty years later, the math no longer added up. First, technologically speaking, Roscosmos no longer had anything to offer the Americans: it still relied on Soviet solutions from the nineteen-sixties. Second, a new superpower had emerged—China, which was ready to pour money into the Russian agency without bothersome questions about human rights, democracy, or freedom of the press. In other words, the marriage of convenience was beginning to crumble, but they still had to share their home in space.

The bus pulled to a stop in the middle of nowhere, a few kilometers from the rocket; the chirping of crickets carried across the steppe. Right, time for one last tradition. She stood up, and Anton let her pass in the aisle, making an exaggeratedly gallant gesture. Broad-shouldered, with a square jaw and short blond hair, he was actually quite handsome. The effect was spoiled only by his cigarette-yellowed teeth. They had served together on the station when she first flew there, eight years ago. They knew each other well. That was exactly why she avoided him.

"Ladies to the left, gentlemen to the right," Anton instructed once they stepped onto the hot asphalt. A step behind him came Lev: a head shorter, reserved, his mustache as bushy as his eyebrows. Lucy had gotten to know him during their joint training sessions—and she liked him. He was a man of the old school, genuine and devoted to the cause. He firmly believed that the gulf between East and West could be bridged, or at least that from the perspective of orbit, the division ceased to make sense. He was the embodiment of the Soviet ideal: hardworking, rational, convinced that the future could be better than the past. This was his final mission, his farewell to space. He looked as if it were already filling him with sadness.

A moment later, latches snapped open and zippers hissed. The Sokol suits—which barely an hour ago had been painstakingly fastened by a swarm of technicians in latex gloves, cotton caps, and plastic shoe covers, under the watchful eyes of families and journalists—were depressurized. Of course, everyone, including the aforementioned technicians, knew this was exactly what would happen. Because on April 12, 1961, in this exact same spot, Yuri Gagarin had asked the driver to stop the bus. The man who was about to become the first to cast off the shackles of gravity had to first deal with the most mundane of needs: he had to pee. And since he went on to fly and return in one piece, every subsequent cosmonaut had made the exact same stop—just in case. As is usually the case, the men had it easier. The women, in order to satisfy the demands of tradition, brought along previously collected urine in a small plastic bottle. Lucy poured its contents onto the rear tire, shaking off the last drops with a vigorous flick of the wrist she had copied from the priest who had blessed them earlier.

The official cosmonaut anthem, *Trava u Doma*, blared from the speakers, signaling that the crew was already in the rocket and less than two hours away from launch. Steve Ayers wondered how many times he had heard this sentimental tune, how many times he had stood on the observation deck of the Baikonur Cosmodrome. The twentieth? The twenty-fifth? He didn’t have the faintest idea. He had seen off so many missions here that they all blurred together; his passport was filled with stamps from Kazakh customs. But this moment, the final stretch, still got his adrenaline pumping. In a few moments, they would send human beings into space. It was impossible to get used to; it never ceased to amaze. A few more photos, some speeches, a teleconference with Mission Control in Moscow, and the officials stepped out onto the observation deck. It was approaching eight o’clock in the evening.

"Something ends, something begins." Director Rybkin leaned against the railing. A daily schedule leaflet, folded in quarters, poked out of his back pocket. Rybkin came from a military background, and it was obvious he hadn't served behind a desk. His eyes gave him away: alert, focused, constantly in motion, trained to watch for enemies and spot traps. That habit might still come in handy. His predecessor had been dismissed less than a year earlier amidst a scandal, facing corruption charges that carried the threat of a lengthy stay in a labor camp. No one wondered whether he was guilty or not; one only had to look at his much younger wife's jewelry to form an opinion on the matter. The real question was why the prosecutor's office had taken an interest in him just now. In other words: how had he disappointed his patrons? Which expectations had he failed to meet? What had changed behind the thick walls of the Kremlin in the meantime?

"It doesn't have to end at all," Ayers replied. They were speaking English; it was easier that way.

"Ah, so you do want to buy a seat on the next flight after all?"

"If you drop your price below the competition..."

"Right... The free market." Rybkin grimaced. "Unfortunately, I don't think that's going to be possible."

"There are other projects."

"Such as?"

Ayers cast a meaningful glance at the sky. The sun had already set, and the Moon was peering through the clouds. It had been over fifty years since a human had last set foot on its surface. There had been many plans to return, but they had lacked enthusiasm, money—and a good reason. During his long career at NASA, Ayers had repeatedly witnessed projects for a return to the Silver Globe end up in the shredder. Just as he was slowly losing hope that anything would ever come of it, that humanity would venture beyond Earth’s orbit again in his lifetime, the situation had changed completely. SpaceX—a private company, a start-up mocked by industry veterans just over a decade ago—had shown that it was possible to fly cheaper, more often, and farther than anyone had dared to dream. Furthermore, advances in nuclear fusion meant that long-held dreams of utilizing the inexhaustible deposits of lunar helium-3 as a revolutionary new fuel stood a chance of becoming reality. America was looking to the night sky with growing interest. And it was not alone.

"We will find another partner." Rybkin stepped back from the railing and clasped his hands behind his back.

"China?"

"Do you have any objections?"

"China won't be a partner to you."

"And were you?"

Ayers did not answer immediately. He felt that, from now on, he should weigh every word carefully.

"I would like to think so," he replied.

The Russian turned his gaze toward the rocket. Bathed in the glare of floodlights amidst the nocturnal blackness of the steppes, it looked like a monumental statue erected in honor of—what? Human curiosity? Power? Arrogance?

"Do you know how much we pay Kazakhstan to use Baikonur?" Rybkin asked after a moment.

"The figures are publicly available."

"Say it out loud. Let's refresh our memories."

"One hundred and fifteen million a year."

"But one hundred and fifteen million of what? Rubles, maybe?"

"No. Dollars."

"Dollars, exactly." The Russian nodded. "Is that a lot of money, or a little?"

"To me? A lot."

"Right? And how much money did Kazakhstan put into building this spaceport they are now so graciously lending us? Is that data publicly available too, Mr. Ayers?"

"You're trying to draw me into a political discussion. And I..."

"Just tell me: how much?"

Ayers looked Rybkin in the eye. He tried to understand where the man was going with this, what he wanted to achieve. Was it just about old grievances, or something more?

"Kazakhstan was part of the Soviet Union back then," the American replied diplomatically.

"Ah, exactly. And what was it that caused the Soviet Union to collapse?"

"I don't think that's relevant to our conversation."

"It's relevant to every conversation."

"Let's just say there are differing opinions."

"Yes, very different," Rybkin agreed. "Mine is that our partner helped us with that. The very same one who has now taken half my budget."

"Private companies are simply cheaper. That's not our fault."

"Ah, it's like being back in the nineties," the Russian snorted. "Perhaps you would also like to tell me about the invisible hand of the market?"

*Here we go*, thought Ayers, *time to change the subject.*

"I get the impression that our conversation isn't heading in a constructive direction," he said with the kind of genial smile only an American can plaster across his face.

"Really?" Rybkin wasn't backing down. "Because to my mind, some very concrete conclusions are emerging from it."

*Zazhyganiye… Predvaritelnaya…* crackled over the loudspeakers. The support structures fell back from the rocket, and the launch cables were cast off; it looked like a flower unfolding its petals. *Desyat! Devyat! Vosem!*

"Like what, if I may ask?" the American wanted to know.

*Sem! Shest! Pyat!*

"That all of this"—Rybkin pointed first at himself, then at Ayers—"was a mistake."

Their faces were bathed in the light of the flames.

A plush dog dangled just above Lucy’s head. It had a black nose, pink fur, and enormous eyes. It weighed exactly twenty-three grams and was a crucial piece of equipment aboard the Soyuz. It reminded her of a souvenir Nate had once brought back from a conference in Edinburgh. It was a framed stone bearing the inscription "Scottish Weather Station." If the stone is warm—the sun is shining. If it's wet—it's raining. If you can't see it—fog. For the little dog, the instruction manual would go something like this: If the dog isn't moving—you haven't launched yet. If it starts to vibrate—the engines have ignited. If it's bouncing—you've lifted off. If it's floating—you've reached orbit. If it's on fire—something has gone very wrong.

The plush dog began to shake. As did her hands, legs, stomach, tongue, eyes, and brain—the vibrations were radiating through the entire capsule. Lucy sat, or rather lay, on the left side, curled up with her knees pulled tight under her chin, wedged into a recess molded from a plaster cast of her body; to endure several hours in this position, everyone preemptively swallowed a massive dose of painkillers. In the cramped confines of the Soyuz cabin, every cubic centimeter had to be utilized, which was why, directly above her head, sheltered in its Plexiglas bubble, bulged storage compartments stuffed to the brim. The capsule commander, Anton, had been responsible for packing them. If the weight were distributed unevenly, if there were even half a kilogram more on one side than the other, the spacecraft would veer off course. And that, when traveling through the boundless expanse of space, was never good news.

"Launch vehicle readings nominal," a voice echoed in her headset.

Lucy felt a tremendous force pressing her into her seat, squeezing the air from her lungs. Beneath her, forty thousand kilograms of rocket fuel were burning. She glanced at the screen: the G-force read two Gs, two and a half, three, three and a half… She wanted to laugh, to cry, to scream with joy—finally, after eight years of waiting, of hard work, of her own sacrifices and those of others, she was breaking free from Earth once more. But she stifled it, as efficiently and dispassionately as if she were flipping a switch on the control panel or pressing a button. For an astronaut, this skill was every bit as important as a knowledge of orbital mechanics or first aid. Lucy was constantly being observed, assessed, and evaluated; even now, at this very moment, there was a camera right in front of her face transmitting her image to mission control centers in Houston, Moscow, Toulouse, and Tsukuba. She knew that even though she was essentially just a passenger on this flight (Anton and Lev were at the controls, and even they didn't have much to do right now, since the onboard computer was handling most of the tasks), she had to look serious and focused—otherwise she would end up on the radar of the psychologists overseeing the crew. So she monitored the screen readouts and checked off the next items on the checklist.

"First stage separation," Anton said.

It felt as though someone had suddenly taken their foot off the gas pedal. The rocket jettisoned its tail, along with the empty tanks and four engines, each the size of a truck. They would soon burn up in the atmosphere, like sacrifices offered to gravity.

"Escape module separation."

There was a loud bang. The spire concealing the engines—which, in the event of an emergency, would have instantly yanked the capsule away from the rest of the rocket—snapped off. The most dangerous phase of the flight was behind them. They could finally breathe.

"Fairing separation."

Light flooded the capsule. Until now, the windows had been covered by sheet metal panels. Lucy looked out. Endless blackness filled half the window, studded with gleaming stars—seemingly the exact same ones she had gazed at in the night sky, yet different: stripped of the obscuring shroud of the atmosphere, their light was much sharper, more distinct. She felt as though she were testing lenses at the optometrist and had finally found the right pair, the ones that showed the world exactly as it truly was. Below, the Earth drifted by. It was night over Asia, and the city lights were burning. She had missed this view. Every single day. Every single moment.

"Still quite a sight, huh?" Anton asked.

"It is."

"Second stage separation," Lev read out the message displayed by the computer. Of the towering spire that had launched from Baikonur, only the capsule now remained. "Third stage ignition… Initiated…"

"Just like that time in the Cupola," Anton went on. A note of mockery lingered in his voice. A challenge.

"Velocity thirteen thousand, seven hundred and two miles per hour," Lucy said, ignoring the bait.

"We were flying over Turkmenistan back then, too."

"Solar array deployment…" The astronaut flicked the corresponding switch. "Initiated…"

"Those lights down there are Ashgabat… And over there is Bishkek…"

"Attention," a voice sounded over the headsets. Moscow flight controllers. "Loss of signal in thirty seconds. Communications will resume in approximately twenty minutes. Commence preparations for the first delta-v maneuver."

"Copy that."

Silence fell over the cabin. For exactly half a minute. The sharp words that followed were left out of the official transcripts.

The Baikonur Cosmodrome began to empty out. The loudspeakers fell silent, and the floodlights illuminating the launch pad went dark. Officials piled into their waiting limousines; journalists folded up their tripods and packed their cameras into their cases. Nate wondered what the demand for their footage would be, who would want to broadcast it, and—more importantly—who would want to watch it. While the prospect of a mission to the Moon or Mars still fired the imagination, and while people still got a thrill out of the squabbles of billionaires zipping around in orbit, flights to the International Space Station had become routine. News of successive rocket launches was, of course, followed by a loyal band of enthusiasts, but it no longer made the front pages of the newspapers—nor even the second or third.

It was hardly surprising, really: the ISS was, after all, a laboratory—a flying one, granted, but a laboratory nonetheless—and it was hard to get excited about pipetting, even in weightlessness. NASA’s press office understood this perfectly. So they did what they could to drum up enthusiasm for the station: astronauts recorded videos explaining how the space toilet worked (look, you strap your feet in with these bands, and you pee into this funnel...), they spun like tops, squeezed juice from pouches and chased the floating droplets—veritable microgravity circus performers. Eventually, though, even that grew stale. And so, something that barely half a century ago had been a mere dream—a pipe dream, really—had already woven itself into the fabric of everyday life, fading silently into the background. Such is the fate of every breakthrough, every new technology: we can get used to absolutely anything. Perhaps this is the key to our success, the reason why an animal built for life on the grassy savannah, a bipedal ape, hasn't yet gone mad in a world of glass, cables, and screens. Or perhaps, rather: hasn't gone entirely mad.

"Dad, can we go?" Eliza asked. The girl was tired. A taxi was waiting downstairs.

"Just a minute."

Nate looked up at the sky, toward the spot where, just moments before, the fire-breathing engines had flickered. There was no trace of the Soyuz left; it had melted into the stars, a dot among thousands of dots. He took a deep breath—catching the scent of exhaust fumes and warm asphalt—and slowly let the air out of his lungs. The fear that had been eating at him for weeks, clinging to every thought and seeping into every dream, had finally eased a little. But it hadn’t vanished, because it never did. It was ready to return at the first opportunity, at the slightest trigger, like a spider sensing the faintest tremor in the web spun inside his head. That was exactly how he pictured it: thousands of silver threads strung inside his skull, and among them, gleaming black eyes that never blink.

This was why Nate was glad to be going home, to everyday life, to the familiar routes between home and school, the store and the gas station, the park and the library. He needed routine to tame reality, to cope with it. That was probably the biggest difference between them, him and Lucy. She constantly craved new experiences; he avoided them, even when it came to something seemingly pleasant and simple. Take a weekend out of town, for example. Sounds great, but what if the weather doesn’t cooperate, they run out of gas, they get a flat tire, Eliza gets sick, or they arrive and it turns out there’s a problem with the reservation? Of course, in the end they would go—because he *did* want to go, or at least he knew he ought to want to—but the questions never went away. So Nate would mentally map out increasingly complex contingency plans, branching out like the Amazon Delta, and undertake extensive preventive measures. He would fill up the tank the day before, check the tire pressure and oil levels, double-check that he’d packed the raincoats, and ensure the hotel room was booked for the correct dates. So Nate wasn’t setting off on a vacation, but rather on a kind of three-person military maneuver, during which, as chief of staff, he had to constantly check maps, field reports, and identify potential threats. Obviously, it was hard to relax under such conditions. He only ever admired the scenery once he was back home, looking at the photos.

Lucy was his polar opposite. Always composed, always in control of the situation. To her, a new problem was an interesting challenge, a puzzle to be solved. Rushing to the airport and stuck in traffic? Great, time to quickly map out an alternate route. The deadline to submit a paper is a few hours away and the computer crashes? A perfect opportunity to expand her IT skills. She was impossible to stump or throw off balance.

They had met fourteen years ago at the International Planetary Science Conference. He, a geophysicist, was a member of a team working on a probe meant to go to Titan (it never flew; the money ran out). She, an astrophysicist at the time, was presenting the results of a spectroscopic analysis of the planets in the Gliese 581 system, in the constellation Libra. She caught his eye because her poster, instead of being displayed on a folding stand, was hanging from a makeshift contraption fashioned out of a broomstick, a straightened wire hanger, and thread from a hotel sewing kit. As it turned out, the airline had lost her luggage, so she’d had to build a new stand using whatever she had on hand, and in under an hour. They chatted for a moment, he cracked a joke, she laughed. He asked if she had any plans for the evening. She didn’t. Sometimes—rarely, but still—he regretted that he hadn’t also asked what her plans were for the next ten years. Who knows, maybe then they wouldn’t have gone out for drinks, wouldn’t have kissed in the hotel elevator, wouldn’t have spent the night in his room—in short, there wouldn’t have been this entire chain of cause and effect that had led Nate here, to a cosmodrome in Kazakhstan. Astronauts’ partners had it hard. Partners with his psychological makeup had it especially hard.

“Nate!” Someone laid a hand on his shoulder. 

Deputy Director Ayers. Even though it was only the third time they’d ever met, he sounded as if he were speaking to his best friend. You could tell he’d spent his life in politics. He had a reputation as a player who was as skilled as he was ruthless. 

“Will you be at the farewell dinner?”

“Sorry, not this time. The little one needs to get to bed.”

“I understand. If you need anything…”

“Thanks, we’re all set.”

“Excellent… In that case, we’ll all see each other back here in six months?”

*If everything goes according to plan*, said a voice in the back of Nate’s head. *If Lucy’s capsule doesn’t enter the atmosphere at too steep an angle and burn up. If it doesn’t come in too shallow and skip off the blanket of gas surrounding the planet like a stone skipped across a lake. If the heat shield doesn’t fail. The cabin doesn’t depressurize. The parachute opens. They don’t go into a spin that triggers lethal G-forces. They don’t smash into the Kazakh steppe.*

“That’s right,” he replied, struggling to force a smile.

The latches clanked as they disengaged, and the hatch began to open. The Soyuz filled with the deafening roar of ventilation fans and the distinct smells she remembered from her previous stint: sweat, hot metal, and stale plastic. Above them, in the open airlock, the faces of the crew appeared. Anton headed out to them first—a gentle push-off and he was already drifting toward the exit, as if swimming through the air. Lucy gathered the hair floating in front of her face and secured it tightly with a hair tie. She was ready; she couldn’t wait. Now Lev—a single movement of his arm and he was up. Then her turn came. She stood on her seat, bent her knees slightly, and flew up after the others.

Even though she had been here before, and despite having completed hundreds of parabolic flights during which she had trained her body to handle weightlessness, her head still spun and her stomach churned. Up, down—suddenly those words had lost their meaning; they were useless. She felt as though she had stepped inside an optical illusion where nothing was where it was supposed to be, one that scrambled the senses the longer you stared at it. Lucy felt sour saliva pool in her throat. *Don’t puke*, she thought, *just don’t puke*. Someone was already hugging her, patting her on the back. She narrowly missed hitting her head against a camera lens. They were in the Russian segment, in the Rassvet module, so it was cramped—too tight for two people, let alone nine. They swarmed together like fish in a net.

Lucy recognized the three astronauts they were relieving, whose Soyuz was returning to Earth in a few days: Igor Volkov, Kate Hortchak, and Francesco Russo. A moment later, the Dragon pilot, Ezra Wendley, greeted her. Red-haired, muscular, and pale as a sheet, he was an ex-military man and looked the part. His capsule had docked three months earlier and was scheduled to stay for another three. To her left flashed Lafayette Summers, the perpetually smiling botanist, an African American from Louisiana. Last in line—though “line” was hardly the right word, since everyone was in constant motion; it would have been easier to plan a checkmate in three-dimensional chess than to tell who was exactly where, or whose hand or leg belonged to whom—was Devaki Anand, an Indian American. She was the one Lucy knew the least. The only one in the group who wasn’t a professional astronaut, she was a space tourist. Of course, this didn’t mean she got to sip drinks adorned with little paper umbrellas; NASA had put this crew member to work, too. Exactly how much she had paid for her stay on the station remained a secret, but it was undoubtedly a seven-figure sum. For Devaki, who had made her billions in Silicon Valley, such an expense was hardly an issue.

Lucy was dying to just head to her cabin (a generous term—in reality, the space was smaller than a broom closet), undress (though no one talked about it out loud, astronauts flew in diapers, officially and euphemistically referred to as “maximum absorbency garments”), and wash up (meaning: wipe her entire body down with disposable wet wipes, since there were no showers; free-floating water plus microgravity equaled a guaranteed short circuit). But there was still one more item on the agenda: the press conference wrapping up their flight. They moved into Nauka, simultaneously one of the oldest and newest modules on the station. Oldest, because its construction had begun back in the nineties. Newest, because the Russians had only managed to finish it and launch it into space at the beginning of this year. What they hadn’t managed was a safe docking: once Nauka was attached, its previously malfunctioning main thruster unexpectedly fired, knocking the entire station out of orbit and very nearly causing a catastrophe. From the inside, the module looked like any other: cables, LEDs, and electronics stuck out everywhere, from every surface.

“Okay, let's line up...” Volkov, the station’s rotational commander, took the initiative. “New guys in the front... The rest in the back...”

It took them a moment to get into their assigned positions. To avoid confusing the viewers on Earth, they all assumed the same orientation, as though they were standing on a floor, even though half of them could just as easily have been hanging upside down. Everyone grabbed hold of something—those who had just arrived on the station instinctively used their hands; the rest, already accustomed to microgravity, hooked their feet into the handholds; it was more comfortable that way. Three, two, one; the light beside the camera’s glass eye lit up red.

“ISS, this is Moscow,” the controller radioed in. “A bit cramped in there?”

"We're managing somehow," Volkov replied with a smile. Like the rest of the Russians, he wore his hair cropped short, and his neck was red with razor burn; shaving in space was exceptionally unpleasant. Above his head gleamed a gold Orthodox cross, stuck to the wall.

"Excellent. Soyuz, how's the flight?"

"Without any difficulties," Anton stated simply.
