Ice

We do not freeze.

I


WARSAW


"If a soldier fires a rifle, at that very moment the wound the bullet will bore into the enemy a short time later begins to exist; if one swallows a dose of incurable poison, at that very moment the final, fatal process begins, even if its culmination does not arrive for a few days. Rightly did the ancients say: we died at the very moment of our birth. We have no power over the subsequent path of the bullet or the poison (and, in part, over the living out of our own lives)."

CHAPTER ONE

Of the thousand-ruble son


It was on the 14th of July, 1924, when the *chinovniks* of the Ministry of Winter came for me—on the evening of that day, the eve of my Siberiad—that I first began to suspect I did not exist.

Under the featherbed, under three blankets and an old gabardine coat, wearing flannel long johns and a thick knit sweater, with socks pulled over socks—only my feet sticking out from beneath the featherbed and blankets—thawed out at last after more than a dozen hours of sleep, curled almost into a ball, my head jammed beneath a thickly cased pillow, so that even sounds reached me soft, warmed, coated in wax—like ants bogged down in resin, they pushed their way deep inside, slowly and with great exertion, through the sleep and through the pillow, millimeter by millimeter, word by word:

"Gaspadin Venedikt Yeroslavsky."

"Him."

"*Spit?*"

"*Spit*, Ivan Ivanovich."

Voice and voice, the first low and hoarse, the second low and lilting; before I had lifted blanket or eyelid, I could already see them leaning over me, the hoarse one by my head, the lilting one by my feet, my Tsarist angels.

"We have awoken young master Venedikt," Ivan stated, as I hoisted up my other eyelid. He nodded at Biernatowa; the landlady meekly left the room.

Ivan pulled up a stool and sat down; he kept his knees together, and upon his knees rested a black, narrow-brimmed bowler hat. The tall *Vatermörder* collar, white as snow in the southern sun, stung my eyes—a white *Vatermörder* and white clerk's cuffs, blinding against the uniform black of their attire. I blinked.

"Permit me, Venedikt Filippovich."

They took the liberty. The other man perched at the foot of the bed, his weight pulling the featherbed down until I was forced to let it go; grabbing hold of the blankets instead, I half-rose upon my heap of bedding, and in doing so exposed my back. Cold air forced its way beneath my sweater and long johns; I shivered, suddenly wide awake.

I threw the coat over my shoulders and drew my knees up to my chin. 

They watched me with amusement.

"How is your health?"

I cleared my throat. Night phlegm had gathered in my throat, a corrosive acid atop all my stomach contents—from the garlic sausage, the gherkins, whatever else we had partaken of yesterday, the warm cornelian cherry liqueur, and the cigarettes, so many cigarettes. I leaned toward the wall and hawked into the spittoon. The force of it bent me double. Doubled over, I coughed hard for a long moment. I wiped my mouth with the torn sleeve of my coat.

"Strong as a horse."

"Ah, that’s good, that’s good. We were afraid you wouldn’t get out of bed."

I stood up. My pocketbook lay on the windowsill, wedged behind a flowerpot holding a dead geranium. I took out my *bumaga* and shoved it under Ivan’s nose. He didn't even look.

"Why, *Gaspadin* Yeroslavsky! Are we some kind of common beat cops?" He straightened up on that stool even more—I would have thought it impossible, but he straightened up even more; now the walls seemed crooked, the wardrobe hunchbacked, the doorframe scoliotic. Insulted, the official lifted his chin and puffed out his chest. "We most politely invite you to join us on Miodowa Street for tea and sweets; the commissar always orders sorbets, tartlets, and cream horns straight from Semadeni's—a veritable debauchery of the palate, if I may put it that way. Eh, Kirill?"

"You most certainly may, Ivan Ivanovich," Kirill sang out.

Ivan Ivanovich had a bushy mustache, heavily pomaded and curled up at the ends, whereas Kirill was entirely clean-shaven. Ivan fished a turnip watch on a tangled fob from his waistcoat pocket and announced that it was five minutes to five; Commissar Preiss valued punctuality highly, and anyway, what time was he heading out for supper? He was meeting the Major General at the Francuski.

Kirill offered Ivan some snuff, Ivan offered Kirill a cigarette, and they watched me as I got dressed. I splashed ice-cold water into the washbasin. The tiles of the stove were cold. I turned up the wick in the lamp. The room’s only window looked out onto a cramped courtyard, its panes so caked with dirt and frost that even at midday, little sunlight filtered through. When I used to shave—back when I still shaved—I had had to set the lamp in front of the mirror, turned up to a full flame. Zyga had parted ways with his razor right after arriving in Warsaw; he had grown a beard worthy of an Orthodox priest. I glanced at his bed on the other side of the stove. He has lectures on Mondays; he must have gotten up at dawn. Lying on Zygmunt’s bed were the black fur coats of the *chinovniks*, their gloves, a walking stick, and a scarf. The table, you see, was cluttered to the edges with dirty dishes, empty bottles, books, magazines, and notebooks; Zyga was drying his socks and underwear by dangling them from the edge of the tabletop, weighed down by anatomy atlases and Latin dictionaries. And in the middle of the table, resting upon a dog-eared, grease-stained copy of Riemann’s *Über die Hypothesen welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen* and a pile of yellowed *Kuryer Warszawski* newspapers—kept for kindling, for patching up cracks forced open by the frost, for drawing moisture out of boots, and also as wrapping for sandwiches—there rose a double row of candles and stubs, the ruins of a stearin Parthenon. Against the wall opposite the stove, meanwhile, towered even stacks of hardbound volumes, arranged by format and thickness, and by frequency of reading. Hanging above them on the soot-stained wall, a gorget of Our Lady of the Gate of Dawn—the sole remnant of the previous tenants, whom Biernatowa had thrown out on the street for “indecent conduct”—had blackened completely, looking now more like a piece of medieval armor for Lilliputians. Ivan stared at it for a long time, in intense concentration, seated stiffly on a stool, his left hand holding the cigarette extended to the side at a forty-five-degree angle from his body, his right hand resting on his thigh next to his bowler hat, wrinkling his brow and nose, bristling his mustache—it was then I realized he was nearly blind, a myopic desk clerk; he had marks on his nose and under his eye sockets from a pince-nez, and without his pince-nez he was left to rely on Kirill. They had come straight in from the freezing cold, and Ivan must have had to take his glasses off. My own eyes sometimes water in here. The air inside the tenement is thick, heavy, infused with every odor of human and animal organisms; no one opens the windows, doors are slammed shut immediately and the gaps above the thresholds are stuffed with rags so that the heat won't escape the building—fuel, after all, has to be paid for, and anyone who had enough money for coal wouldn't be holed up in such dark annexes in the first place, where the air is thick, heavy, you breathe it as if you were drinking water spat out by your neighbor and his dog, every breath of yours has passed a million times before through the tubercular lungs of peasants, Jews, draymen, butchers, and whores, hacked up from black larynxes it returns to you again and again, filtered through their saliva and phlegm, passed through fungus-ridden, lice-infested, and festering bodies, they have coughed it up, blown it from their noses, puked it straight into your mouth, you have to swallow, you have to breathe, breathe!

“E-excuse me.”

Fortunately, the privy at the end of the corridor was not occupied just then. I vomited into the hole, an icy stench blowing into my face. Cockroaches were crawling out from under the shit-stained seat. I crushed them with my thumb as they climbed toward my chin.

Stepping back out into the corridor, I saw Kirill standing in the doorway of my room—he was keeping an eye on me, standing guard to ensure I didn’t flee into the freezing cold in just my long johns and sweater. I smiled knowingly. He handed me a handkerchief and pointed to my left cheek. I wiped it. When I tried to hand it back, he took a step back. I smiled a second time. I have a wide mouth; it smiles very easily.

I put on my only formal attire, the black suit I had worn for my final exams; were it not for the layers of underwear beneath it, it would be hanging off me now as if on a skeleton. The officials watched as I laced my boots, as I buttoned my waistcoat, as I wrestled with the stiff celluloid collar fastened to my last cotton shirt. I grabbed my documents and what was left of my cash, three rubles and forty-two kopecks—it would make for a barely symbolic bribe, but with empty pockets in a government office, a man feels naked. There was nothing I could do, however, about the old sheepskin coat, the patches, the stains, the crooked seams; I had no other. They watched in silence as I squeezed my arms into the asymmetrical sleeves, the left one longer. I smiled apologetically. Kirill licked his pencil and meticulously noted something down on his cuff.

We stepped out. Biernatowa must have been peeping through a cracked door, for she appeared instantly beside the officials, flushed and chattering away, to escort them back down the stairs from the second floor and through both well-like courtyards to the main gate, where Walenty the doorman, having straightened his brass-badged cap and tucked his pipe into his pocket, hastily swept the snow from the pavement and helped the officials into the sleigh, taking the gentlemen by the elbows lest they slip on the iced-over sidewalk, while Biernatowa showered them—now seated and wrapping their legs in lap robes—with torrents of complaints about malicious tenants, about the gangs of Powiśle thieves who broke into homes even in broad daylight, and about the cruel frosts that caused damp windows to warp from the inside and pipes to burst in the walls, so that no plumbing or sewage lines could last long in the ground; finally, she fervently assured them that she had long suspected me of various offenses and indecencies, and would have unfailingly reported me to the proper authorities had it not been for the thousand and one other troubles piled upon her head—until the driver, from his box behind Kirill, cracked his whip and the horses jerked the sleigh to the left, forcing the woman to step back, and so we set off for the Warsaw branch of the Ministry of Winter, located in the former Palace of the Kraków Bishops, 5 Miodowa Street, corner of Senatorska.

Before we turned from Koszykowa onto Marszałkowska Street, snow began to flurry; I pulled my *shapka* down over my ears. The officials in their voluminous furs and bowler hats resembling walnut shells, seated on the low benches of the sleigh—Ivan beside me, Kirill with his back to the *izvozchik*—reminded me of the beetles I had seen in Zygmunt’s *uchebnik*: thick, oval torsos, short little legs, small heads, everything gloss-black, enclosed in the geometric symmetry of ellipses and circles. A shape so close to the ideal of a sphere cuts itself out of the world. They stared straight ahead with a dispassionate gaze, with tightly pursed lips and chins propped high by stiff collars, limply yielding to the motion of the sleigh. I thought I would learn something from them on the way. I thought they would start demanding handouts for their favor, for a lack of haste and zeal. They remained silent. I will ask them—how? about what? They will pretend not to hear. Flakes of sticky snow swirled between us. I hid my cold hands in the sleeves of my sheepskin coat.

Lights were burning in the French Patisserie; the electric glare beating through the large windows knitted halos of wool around the silhouettes of passersby. The summer sun should still have been high in the sky, but as usual, heavy clouds hung over the city; they had even lit the streetlamps—very tall ones, with spirally coiled tops. We turned north. From Ostrowski’s patisserie, at the intersection with Piękna Street, girls came running out in little red coats and white hooded capes, their laughter piercing the street clamor for a moment. I was reminded of my unfinished letter to Miss Julia and her final cry-question. Next to Ostrowski’s, at Wedel’s, Fredek, Kiwajs, and I used to arrange our card evenings. Right here, just past the "Sokół" cinema, at Kalka's place, the Sweet Prince used to rent a room for night sessions. Had I raised my head and looked left, over Ivan’s bowler hat, I would have spotted the window on the second floor of the building at number 71, the window through which Fredek had fallen.

Near the intersection with Nowogrodzka Street, a fat cow hung frozen to a streetlamp; a tendon of dark ice connected it to the top of the facade of a four-story building. The cow must have come from the last cattle drive to the Ochota slaughterhouse; the wintermen hadn't chopped it down yet. Down the vista of the street, above the roof of the "Sphinx" building, loomed a blue-black nest of ice, a massive clot of permafrost hard as diamond, connected by a web of icy threads, icicles, spans, and columns to the buildings on both sides of Marszałkowska and Złota streets—to the buildings, to the lamps, the stumps of frozen trees, the balcony balustrades, bay windows, the spires of domes and turrets, parapets, and chimneys. The "Sphinx" cinema had, of course, long been closed; no lights burned on its highest floors.

The sleigh slowed down as we passed Nowogrodzka. The driver pointed at something with his whip. The cart ahead of us was pulling onto the pavement. Kirill looked behind him. I leaned out to the right. At the intersection with Aleje Jerozolimskie stood two policemen, driving traffic away from the center of the road with whistles and shouts—above the roadway, a *luty* was just then freezing its way across.

We were stuck for a few minutes in the bottleneck it had caused. Usually, *lutes* travel above the rooftops, more rarely descending to the ground in cities. Even from such a distance, I felt I could sense the waves of cold radiating from it. I shivered and reflexively buried my chin into the collar of my sheepskin coat. The *chinovniks* of the Ministry of Winter exchanged glances. Ivan glanced at his watch. On the other side of the street, behind an advertising column plastered with posters promoting a wrestling match at the Okólnik circus, a man dressed in the English style was setting up an archaic camera to take a photograph of the *luty*; the photograph would most likely never appear in the papers anyway, confiscated by the men from Miodowa. Ivan and Kirill did not even pay him any mind.

The *luty* was exceptionally brisk; before dusk it ought to manage to cross to the other side of Marszałkowska, overnight it would climb above the rooftops, and by Friday it would manage to reach the nest above the cinema-theatre. When a frost-walker crossed from Praga to the Castle via the Alexandrian Bridge last year, the Bridge was closed for nearly two months. Meanwhile, this glacian right here—wait a short quarter of an hour and I could probably perceive its movement, how it freezes from place to place, shifting in ice, through ice, from ice to ice; how one crystalline thread snaps behind it, then another, and a livid-white frost-dust slowly crumbles down—one minute, *kshtr*, two minutes, *kshtr*—the wind carried off the lighter particles along with the snow, but the majority froze into a black sheet that congealed the street mud behind the *luty*, ice of ice; and this path of rough permafrost, like the trail of a snail's slime, stretched for several dozen meters east down Jerozolimskie, both along the pavement and up the facade of the hotel. The rest had already been chipped away by the wintermen, or had thawed on its own; yesterday afternoon the thermometer at Schnitzer's had read five degrees above zero.

The *luty* did not move in a straight line, nor did it maintain a constant height above the cobblestones (they freeze their way beneath the earth's surface, too). Three or four hours ago, judging by the shattered architecture of the ice, the *luty* had begun to alter its trajectory: until then it had been travelling barely a meter above the middle of the street, but then, three hours ago, it moved upwards in a sharp parabola, over the tops of the streetlamps and the crowns of the frozen trees. I saw the row of slender stalagmites it had left behind; they gleamed with the reflected glare of the streetlamps, the reflections of colorful neon signs, the lights pouring through windows and shop displays. The row broke off above the tram tracks—the *luty* was suspended with its entire weight upon a stellate web of frost-strands, stretched horizontally and reaching upwards, toward the facades of the corner buildings. One could have walked right under it, were there anyone mad enough to do so.

Ivan nodded to Kirill, who clambered out of the sleigh with a reluctant grimace on his face, ruddy from the biting frost. Maybe I'll get lucky, I thought, maybe we'll be late, Commissar Preiss will have already left for his scheduled dinner with the Major General, and they'll turn me away from Miodowa empty-handed. Thank You, God, for this icicle-monstrosity. I slid over on the bench, resting my shoulder against the side of the sleigh. A newsboy ran up—"Hirohito Beaten!", "Special Express, Mierzov's Success!"—I shook my head. At bottlenecks in the center, crowds instantly form, and street vendors appear—sellers of cigarettes and holy water, of holy fire. The police were shooing pedestrians away from the *luty*, but of course, they couldn't possibly keep an eye on everyone. A gang of urchins sneaked up from the direction of Briesemeister's restaurant. The bravest of them, his face wrapped in a scarf and wearing thick, shapeless gloves, ran to within a dozen paces of the *luty* and threw a cat at it. The big cat flew in a high arc, paws splayed, screeching to high heaven... then its terrifying shriek abruptly broke off. It landed on the *luty* likely already dead, only to slowly tumble off it into the snow, frozen solid: an ice sculpture of a cat with splayed limbs and a tail stiff as a wire. The boys ran off, howling with glee. A Jew with sidelocks shook his fist after them from the threshold of Epstein's jewelry store, cursing roundly in Yiddish.

Kirill, meanwhile, caught up with the older policeman and, grabbing him by the elbow so he wouldn't wander off in pursuit of the urchins, began to persuade him of something in a hushed voice, albeit with the substantial aid of sweeping gestures from his free hand. The constable kept turning his head, shrugging his shoulders, scratching his crown. The younger of the police pair kept shouting at his comrade, *move it, help me!* On the Avenue, two sleighs had locked runners, causing an even greater commotion; carts drove up onto the pavements, and pedestrians, cursing in Polish, Russian, German, and Yiddish, scattered from beneath wheels and hooves; in front of a wine shop, a matron the size of a Danzig wardrobe had tumbled in the frozen mud, three gentlemen were trying to lift her, a pot-bellied officer hurried to their aid, and so they hoisted her with the strength of four: on one—she fell—on two—she fell—on three—half the street was already splitting its sides with laughter, while the poor woman, flushed cherry-red, squealed dreadfully, flailing her stout little legs in tiny boots... Little wonder that we only looked back toward the intersection at the sound of rending sheet metal and the crack of splintering wood. 

An automobile had collided with a coal cart; one horse had fallen, one wheel had come off. The policeman shoved Kirill away and broke into a run toward the crash. Trapped inside the enclosed machine, the motorist began squeezing his warning horn; on top of that, something backfired under the motorcar's hood, like the blast of a double-barreled shotgun. This was too much for the dapple-gray harnessed to a sleigh nearby. Spooked, it jerked forward, straight toward the *luty*. The driver grabbed the reins, but the horse itself must have sensed what kind of wall of frost it had plunged into—it bucked to the side even more violently, spinning the sleigh around in place. Did a runner catch on the curb? Did the dapple-gray slip on the black sheet of ice? I was already standing up in the ministerial sleigh, watching the accident alongside Ivan over the row of vehicles ahead of us, but it was all happening too fast, too unexpectedly—too much movement, shouting, lights, and shadows. The dapple-gray went down, the sleigh it pulled overturned, and its cargo spilled out: a dozen or so bulbous carboys in sawdust-filled baskets. The baskets and carboys rolled into the middle of the intersection, and some of them must have shattered, for a liquid gleaming celadon—kerosene, I thought—spread across the ice, and immediately a fire flared up. From what—an electric spark from the automobile, a dropped cigarette, the strike of a shod hoof against the cobblestones—I do not know. The blue flame leaped across the entire width of the puddle, high, ever higher, a meter, a meter and a half up—almost reaching the *luty* frozen into the overhead web.

The photographer, hunched over his camera, slowly, methodically exposed photograph after photograph. And what he would see in them later, what would be preserved on the glass and developed on the paper: snow—snow—the pale halos of streetlamps—dark mud, dark cobblestones, dark sky—the gray facades of tenement houses down the perspective of the city’s wide ravine—in the foreground, a chaos of the angular shapes of vehicles locked in a traffic jam—from among them and from among the human silhouettes bursts a glare of pure fire, so bright that the paper seems entirely unexposed in this spot—and above it, above the flame of a whiteness whiter than white, in the heart of a hanging arabesque of ice, spreads a *luty*, a *luty*, a massive thunderbolt of frost, a starfish of hoarfrost, a living bonfire of cold, a *luty*, a *luty*, a *luty* above the fur toques of women, a *luty* above the caps and bowlers of men, a *luty* above the horses’ heads and carriage hoods, a *luty* above the neon signs of cafés and salons, shops and hotels, confectioneries and fruit shops, a *luty* above Marszałkowska Street and Jerusalem Avenue, a *luty* above Warsaw, a *luty* above the Russian Empire.

As we later drove toward the Saxon Garden, and down Królewska Street, past the Garden—dead beneath years of permafrost—and the colonnade draped in icicles, past the snow-corniced tower and the Orthodox cathedral on Saxon Square, toward Krakowskie Przedmieście, that image—the afterimage of picture and imagination—haunted me time and again, an intrusive memory of unclear meaning, a sight beheld yet uncomprehended.

The officials exchanged gruff remarks in hushed voices, the driver shouted at inattentive pedestrians; the blizzard had eased, but it was growing ever colder, my breath freezing on my lips, hanging in a white cloud before my face, the sweating horses moving in clouds of sticky dampness—the Royal Castle was drawing ever closer. Before the turn into Miodowa Street, I saw it above Sigismund’s Column: the Castle encased in a block of shadowy ice—and a great nest of *lutes* above it. The violet-black congelation reached halfway up the roofs of the Old Town. On clear days, one can see waves of frost suspended in the air around the Great Tower. Thermometers lack the scale to measure this cold. Gendarmes stand guard by bonfires on the perimeter of Castle Square. Whenever a *luty* freezes out of the nest, they close the streets. The Governor-General had established a cordon of dragoons from the Fourteenth Little Russian Regiment here, but the regiment had since been dispatched to the Japanese front.

The roof of the Palace of the Kraków Bishops remained, however, free of icy buildup. The annex on the Senatorska Street side still housed elegant shops—electric spotlights illuminated advertisements for Nikolai Shelekhov’s Exclusive Delicatessen and the teas of Sergei Bazylovich Perlov’s Moscow Trading House—but the main wing facing Miodowa Street, beneath its rococo crowning and amid pilasters with Corinthian capitals, belonged to the Ministry of Winter. Above both gateways hung black, double-headed eagles beneath Romanov crowns, incrusted with onyx tungetite.

We pulled into the inner courtyard, the sleigh runners scraping against the cobblestones. The officials stepped out first, Ivan vanishing through the doors immediately, setting a pince-nez on his nose; Kirill paused on the steps, just short of the threshold, and looked back at me. I opened my mouth. He raised an eyebrow. I lowered my eyes. We went in.

A porter took my sheepskin coat and *shapka*, while the doorman pushed forward a massive ledger in which I had to sign my name in two places, the pen kept slipping from my stiff fingers, perhaps I might sign for the gentleman, no, I, I will do it myself. The illiterate rabble also pay visits to the corridors of Winter officialdom.

Everything here gleamed with cleanliness: the marble, the parquet floors, the glass and crystal, and the iridescent *zimnazo*. Kirill led me up the main staircase and through two secretariats. On the walls, beneath portraits of Nicholas II Alexandrovich and Piotr Rappacki, hung sunlit landscapes of steppe and forest, of Saint Petersburg in spring and Moscow in summer, from the days when spring and summer still had access to them. The staff did not lift their heads from their desks, but I saw the counselors, clerks, functionaries, and scriveners covertly following me with their eyes, and then exchanging sidelong glances with one another. When do office hours end? The Ministry of Winter never sleeps.

Commissar Extraordinary V. V. Preiss occupied a spacious office featuring an antique stove and a disused fireplace; its tall windows looked out over Miodowa Street and Castle Square. As I entered, passing Ivan on the threshold—who had evidently already announced me—the Commissar was bustling about the samovar with his back to me. He had the figure of a samovar himself: a bulbous, pear-shaped torso, and a small bald head. He moved with frenetic energy, his hands fluttering over the table, his feet in a ceaseless dance, a little step to the left, a little step to the right—I was certain he was humming under his breath, smiling to himself, that merry little eyes were peering out at the world from a ruddy face, that the smooth brow of the Commissar of Winter remained entirely uncreased. In the meantime, since he did not turn around, I stood by the door with my hands clasped behind my back, allowing the warm air to fill my lungs, wash over my skin, and thaw the congealed blood in my veins. It was almost hot in the office; the massive, colorfully painted majolica stove never cooled for a moment, and the windowpanes had fogged up so completely that I could see little through them but the blurred rainbows of streetlights, spreading and blending strangely against the glass. It is a matter of great political import that the cold never prevail within the Ministry of Winter.

"Well, why aren't you sitting down, Venedikt Filipovich? Sit, sit."

A ruddy face, merry little eyes.

I sat down.

Sighing loudly, he slumped down on his side of the desk, cradling a cup of steaming *chai* in his hands. (He did not offer me any.) He hadn’t been stationed here long, the desk was not his, and behind it he looked like a child playing at ministers—he certainly would have had the piece of furniture replaced. They must have only just sent him. Sent him, a Tsarist extraordinary commissar—from where? From Petersburg, from Moscow, from Yekaterinburg, from Siberia?

I took a deeper breath.

"Your *Blagorodiye*, if I may... Am I under arrest?"

"Arrested? Arrested? How on earth could such a thought enter your head?"

"Your officials—"

"My officials!"

"If I had received a summons, I surely would have come myself—"

"Did they not invite you politely, Mr. Gierosławski?" He finally pronounced my name correctly.

"I thought—"

"My God! Arrested!"

He began to pant. 

I folded my hands over my knee. It is worse than I thought. They aren't going to throw me in the *tyurma*. A high-ranking Tsarist official wants to talk to me.

He began taking papers out of the desk. He took out a thick stack of rubles. He took out some seals. Sweat ran down my skin beneath my underwear.

"Yeees." Preiss slurped his tea loudly. "Please accept my condolences."

"I beg your pardon?"

"Your mother passed away last year, did she not?"

"Yes, in April."

"You were left all alone. A pity. A man without a family is like... well, he is alone. It is bad, oh, so bad." He turned a page, slurped, turned the next.

"I have a brother," I muttered.

"Yes, yes, a brother on the other side of the world. Where did he go, Brazil?"

"Peru."

"Peru! What is he doing there?"

"Building churches."

"Churches! I am sure he writes often."

"Well, more often than I do."

"That is nice. He misses you."

"I suppose."

"And you do not miss him?"

"Miss him?"

"Your family. When did you last hear from your father?" Page, page, slurp.

Father. I knew it. What else could this be about?

"We do not correspond, if that is what you are asking."

"That is dreadful, dreadful. Do you not care whether he is even alive?"

"And is he alive?"

"Ah! Is Filip Filipovich Gierosławski alive! Is he alive!" He actually sprang up from behind his operatic desk. Against the wall stood a large globe on a delicate frame of wet *zimnazo*, while a map of Asia and Europe hung on the wall; he gave the globe a spin, then smacked the map with the back of his hand.

When he looked back at me, not the slightest trace of his recent merriment remained upon his chubby visage; his dark *glaza* watched me with clinical attention.

"Is he alive," he whispered.

He picked up the yellowed papers from the desk.

"Filip Gierosławski, son of Filip, born in the year eighteen seventy-eight in Wilkówka, in the Kingdom of Prussia, East Prussia, Lidzbark district; since nineteen hundred and five a Russian subject; husband of Eulalia, father of Bolesław, Benedykt, and Emilia; sentenced in nineteen hundred and seven to death for participation in a plot against the life of *Yevo Imperatorskovo Velichestva* and in an armed rebellion; *nu*, by way of clemency the sentence was commuted to fifteen years of *katorga* with the deprivation of civil rights and the confiscation of his estate. In the year nineteen seventeen, the remainder of his sentence was remitted, with a *prikaz na zhitelstvo* restricting his residence to the confines of the Amur and Irkutsk Governorates-General. Has he not written? Never?"

"To my mother. Perhaps. At first."

"But now? Recently? Since seventeen? At all?"

I shrugged.

"I'm sure you know perfectly well when and to whom he writes."

"Do not be insolent, young man!"

I smiled faintly.

"My apologies."

He studied me for a long moment. On a finger of his left hand he wore a ring with some dark stone set in precious tungetite, engraved with the emblem of Winter; he tapped the ring against the desktop, *truk, trukk*, the even beats striking harder.

"You graduated from the Imperial University. What is your occupation now?"

"I was preparing for my *rigorosum*—"

"How do you support yourself?"

"I tutor mathematics."

"And do you earn much from this tutoring?"

Since I was already smiling, all I could do was lower my gaze to my clenched hands.

"Not much."

"You are a frequent visitor to the moneylenders; all the Jews on Nalewki Street know you. You owe Abiezer Blumstein alone over three hundred rubles. Three hundred rubles! Is this true?"

"If *Vashe Blagorodiye* would tell me in what matter I am being interrogated, it will be easier to confess."

*Truk, trukk, truk, trukk.*

"Or perhaps you really did commit some crime, seeing as you are sweating here so much from fear, eh?"

"If *Vashe Blagorodiye* would deign to open the windows."

He loomed over me; he didn't even have to bend down much to speak directly into my ear—first a whisper, then a growling soldierly tone, and finally, almost a shout.

“You are a gambler, my dear Benedykt, an inveterate card player. Whatever you win, you lose—whatever you earn, you lose—whatever you borrow, you lose—whatever you beg off your friends, you lose. You have no friends left. You have nothing left, and even that you lose; you lose everything. Twenty-one, baccarat, *zimucha*, poker—any game will do. Once, you won half a sawmill—and lost it that very same night. You have to lose. You can’t get up from the table until you’ve gambled yourself into such utter ruin that no one will play with you anymore. No one wants to play with you anymore, Venedikt Filippovich. No one wants to lend to you anymore. You’ve pawned yourself two years in advance. Bolesław no longer writes to you; you write to him, begging for money, but he sends no more. You don’t write to your father, because your father has no money. You wanted to marry, but your prospective father-in-law set the dogs on you after you pawned and gambled away your fiancée’s dowry. If only you’d put a bullet in your head, as befits a nobleman, but you won't, pah, and some nobleman you are—you’re scum!”

I smiled apologetically.

Commissar Preiss caught his breath, then gave my shoulder a friendly pat.

“There, there, don't be afraid. We are well acquainted with scum. So what if you care as much about your own flesh and blood as you do about the Emperor of Japan’s gout—you certainly care about a thousand rubles! Don’t you? You care about a thousand rubles like... well, you care a great deal. We’ll give you a thousand, and perhaps another, if you perform well. You are going to visit your father.”

Here he paused, evidently waiting for my reply. When none was forthcoming, he stepped back behind his desk, returning to his steaming tea (which had cooled somewhat, so he now slurped it longer and louder), his papers, and his seals. With a massive pen, he dashed his signature onto a document, stamped it with a seal, then added a second for good measure; satisfied, he rubbed his hands together and settled back expansively in his leather-upholstered armchair.

“Here is your passport and your *napravlenie* to our branch in Irkutsk; they’ll take charge of you once you arrive. A ticket has already been purchased for you. You leave for Moscow tomorrow, otherwise you won't catch the Siberian Express—today is the first of July; your ticket is for the fifth, departing at ten in the evening from Yaroslavsky Station. You will be in Irkutsk on the eleventh. There, they’ll put you on the *Zimna* to Kezhma. Here is your thousand. Sign for it. Buy yourself some clothes, so you look like a human being! And should you get it into your head to take this money and gamble it all away... well, gamble it away, for all I care, so long as you make it to Baikal. Now, sign!”

A thousand rubles. What do they want me to do out there? Extract from my father the names he didn't reveal at his trial? But why is the Ministry of Winter approaching me about this, rather than Internal Affairs?

“I’ll go,” I said. “I’ll visit my father. And then what? Is that it?”

“You will talk to him.”

“I will talk to him.”

“Once you talk to him, well, that will be enough.”

“I don’t understand what Your Honor—”

“He sent no letters, and this, of course, did not surprise you.” Commissioner Preiss opened a portfolio bound in cloth and tortoiseshell. “Irkutsk writes to us... He was a geologist, wasn't he?”

“I beg your pardon?”

“Filip Filipovich studied geology. He did not lose interest in it even in Siberia. They report to me here... From the beginning he was very close, they took his company on the second or third expedition that went out there in the spring of nineteen ten. Most died of frostbite. Or froze to death on the spot. He survived. Then he went back. To them. I don't believe it myself, but that is what they write to me. They gave the order, they provided the funds, and so I am sending a man. Your father speaks with the *lutes*.”

*Of that which cannot be spoken*

My life is governed by the principle of shame.

One comes to know the world, one learns the language used to describe the world, but one does not come to know oneself. Most people—almost everyone, I imagine—will go to their graves without learning the language in which they might describe themselves.

When I say of someone that he is a coward, it means I believe he is behaving in a cowardly manner—it means nothing more, because, naturally, I cannot peer into the depths of his soul and find out whether he truly is a coward. However, I cannot use this language to describe myself: within the realm of my experience, I remain the only person whose words, deeds, and omissions constitute merely a pale and, fundamentally, accidental reflection of what lies beneath them, of what constitutes their cause and source. I experience the existence of this source directly, whereas of some of my behaviors I am completely unaware, and I perceive all of them in an incomplete, distorted manner. We ourselves are always the last to find out what an idiot we made of ourselves in company. We know the intentions behind our actions better than the actions themselves. We know better what we meant to say than what we actually said. We know who we want to be—we do not know who we are.

A language to describe our behaviors exists, because this reality is experienced by many people, and they can discuss among themselves someone’s ostentatious politeness or someone’s faux pas. A language to describe myself does not exist, because no one but me experiences this reality.

It would be a language for a single person's use, an unutterable, unwriteable language. Everyone must create it for themselves. Most people—almost everyone, I am convinced—never bring themselves to do so as long as they live. At most, they silently repeat others' descriptions of their own person, expressed in that derivative language—a language of the second kind—or they imagine what they would say about themselves in it, if they were granted the chance to look upon themselves from the outside.

To say anything at all about themselves, they must make themselves strangers to themselves.

My life is governed by the principle of shame. I have no better words to tell this truth.

A beggar extends his hand in a pleading gesture, I have money, I could give him some, even five, even two, even a single kopeck, no one else is looking, it is just the two of us, me and the beggar—I give nothing, I turn away and walk off quickly, drawing my head down into my shoulders.

Who broke the jar of preserves? Mother raises her voice. Who broke it? Not me, and I have no idea whether Bolek or Emilka is to blame, but Mother asks again, and again, and then I point decisively at Bolek. Him.

Laughter among the company, we are all mocking a mutual acquaintance who, of course, is not here with us right now; one after another, we recall memories of his humiliations and weaknesses. I stand up, I am not laughing, furrowed brow and clenched lips, I stand up and—have you no shame!

The *krasavitsa* is clinging to me, spilling over my shoulders, sliding down onto my chest, into my lap, as the droshky slows and stops before the gates of the tenement where I rent a room with Kiwajs, Kiwajs is visiting distant relatives in the provinces, the room is empty, the drunken girl giggles, chewing on the button of my frock coat—the driver turns around, winks at me, need a hand, sir? I stare at him in terror. I shove a crumpled ruble into his fist. Take the *blyad* wherever she wants! I shove the *devushka* off me, leap from the droshky, and flee deep into the dark gateway.

In the woods, beyond grandfather’s village, in a hollow by the stream lies a deer carcass. Half-eaten by predators and scavengers, beneath a black canopy of flies. I tell no one, I come every morning and every evening, I poke it with a stick, roll it over, watch the insects swarm it all over again, watch the meat change color and dark fluids seep from the body, slowly soaking into the earth. Where do I spend so much time, grandfather asks. I lie. He sees that I am lying. Where do I go, he asks. I remain silent. He thrashes me with a belt. I cry. I don’t tell the truth, I don’t say a thing. All summer long I roam the woods, searching for dead animals. I carry a thick stick with me, like a club. I beat the carrion with this stick in a dull frenzy, until the rot sloughs off the bones. Take that! Take that! Take that!

Eighteen and seventy-four! Eighteen and seventy-four! The fairground barker is shouting himself hoarse, announcing the winning numbers. We all bought raffle tickets on our way into the fair. Laughing, flushed from the frost and the slivovitz, we now search our pockets for the little cardboard slips. I find mine, I pull it out. Eighteen and seventy-four. Feigning immense disappointment and rage, they fling their tickets into the mud, curse their bad luck, and mock the fool who isn't coming forward to claim the prize. I tear up the winning ticket and do the exact same thing they do.

At night, when no one sees, I practice terrible faces, a bulging of eyes and a baring of teeth, too wild to signify anything in the language of human physiognomy; terrible faces, and also the absolute deadness of the countenance, a stillness of the tiniest muscles of the skull that I can never achieve in the light of day and among people; I have no power then over the grimaces of my face. At night, when I sleep, I see beneath my eyelids a diagram, like one of Zyga’s yellowed engravings, an anatomical diagram of this betrayal: dozens of stiff twine threads drawn beneath the skin of the cheeks, eyebrows, chin, and lips; and the other ends of these threads are held by the people surrounding me, in their hands, between their teeth, tied around watch fobs and cufflinks, on wedding rings, on the heads of walking sticks and the stems of pipes; some even have them sewn into their own facial muscles, or into their hearts, straight through the chest and breastbone. Then the engraving comes to life—it is day—there is movement—people—me—

I smile—I smile—I smile.

…

I recall behaviors, I employ a second language...

I must become a stranger to myself.

Benedykt Gierosławski was a good child. Who would not want to have such a child? He was polite—he always obeyed his parents, obeyed his elders, did not speak unless spoken to, caused no mischief, did not fight with other children, said his prayers before sleep, had the best grades in school. Other boys roamed the city, spent their time on the streets—he read books. Other boys peeped at girls and teased their sisters—he taught his little sister her letters. He washed his ears. He bowed to neighbors and acquaintances. He did not pick his nose, did not point his finger. He did not get sick. He caused his parents no trouble. Who would not want to have such a child?

When he turned seventeen and entered the Imperial University, he met others who were good children.

They recognized one another by their smiles.

Was it already too late? Could I turn back—turn back to what, to whom, to what me? There is no moment in the past which, miraculously altered, would have reversed the course of my life, no catastrophe that befell me, as a result of which I am different from what I ought to be; I can point to no such thing.

It is, in fact, precisely the opposite: however far back I might reach in my memory, I will always find beneath the thin epidermis of my words and deeds the same principle, a muscle of anxiety, hope, and disgust tensed in the same direction, when I was three, thirteen, and twenty-three. Not a feeling, something else, not a thought—it escapes, evades, slips away, immaterial, indescribable—stop! look me in the eyes!—you, you—I shall call you Shame, though you are not it, I shall call you Shame, because there are no better words in human language.
