I

I had no luggage, not even a coat. They said it was unnecessary. They let me keep my black sweater: it would do. But I had to fight for the shirt. I told them I would wean myself off slowly. Right in the passageway, beneath the belly of the ship, where we stood being jostled, Abs offered me his hand with a knowing smile:

"Just be careful…"

I remembered that, too. I didn't crush his fingers. I was perfectly calm. He wanted to say something else. I spared him that by turning away as if I hadn't noticed, and climbed the steps inside. A stewardess led me between the rows of seats to the very front.

I didn't want a private compartment. I wondered if they had warned her. The seat unfolded soundlessly. She adjusted the backrest, smiled at me, and walked away. I sat down. The cushions were bottomlessly soft, like everywhere else. The backrests were so high I could barely see the other passengers. I already accepted the colorfulness of the women's clothing without resistance, but I still pointlessly suspected the men of being dressed for a masquerade, and I still harbored a quiet hope that I would see some men dressed normally—a pathetic reflex. People took their seats quickly; no one had luggage.

Not even a briefcase or a parcel. The women didn't either. There seemed to be more of them. In front of me—two Mulatto women in parrot-like furs, ruffled like feathers; some kind of avian fashion was evidently the trend.

Further down, a couple with a child. After the glaring selenophores of the platforms and tunnels, after the unbearably garish, self-illuminating vegetation of the streets, the light from the concave ceiling was a mere smolder. I rested my hands on my knees, as they were somehow in the way. Everyone was already seated. Eight rows of gray seats, a fir-scented breeze, the silence of fading conversations. I expected an announcement of departure, some signals, an instruction to fasten seatbelts, but nothing happened. Indistinct shadows began to race from front to back across the matte ceiling, like silhouettes of paper cut-out birds. What the devil is it with these birds? I thought helplessly. Maybe it means something? I was stiff as a board from the sheer strain of paying attention so as not to do something inappropriate.

It had been like this for four days now. From the very first moment. I was perpetually lagging behind everything that was happening, and the constant struggle to understand any old conversation or situation turned this tension into a feeling horribly akin to despair. I was convinced the others felt the same way, but we didn't speak of it, not even when we were alone. We only joked about our brawn, about this excess strength we still possessed; and we really did have to watch ourselves—at first, trying to stand up, I would launch myself toward the ceiling, and every object I picked up felt paper-thin, empty. But I quickly learned to control my own body.

When shaking hands, I no longer crushed anyone's hand. That was simple. Unfortunately, the least important.

My neighbor on the left—portly, tanned, with excessively shiny eyes (perhaps from contact lenses)—suddenly vanished because his seat expanded at the sides, which went up and merged to form a sort of ovoid cocoon. A couple of other people disappeared into similar cabins. They looked like swollen sarcophagi. What were they doing in there? But I encountered things like this all the time and tried not to stare, as long as they didn't concern me directly. Curiously enough, I felt rather indifferent toward the people who gawked at us once they found out who we were. Their astonishment hardly bothered me, although I realized immediately that there was not a shred of admiration in it. It was rather those looking after us—the employees of Adapt—who provoked my aversion. Doctor Abs provoked the sharpest, to be sure, because he treated me the way a doctor treats an abnormal patient, pretending—and rather well, at that—to be dealing with someone perfectly ordinary. When that was no longer possible, he joked. I had had enough of his familiarity and joviality. If asked about it (or so I assumed, at least), any passerby would have considered Olaf or me to be just like them—it wasn’t so much *we ourselves* who were incredible to them, but our past fate: *that* was what was extraordinary. Doctor Abs, on the other hand, like every Adapt employee, knew better—that we were truly different. This difference was no mark of distinction, but merely an obstacle to understanding, to the simplest exchange of words—hell, even to opening a door, since doorknobs had ceased to exist, I couldn't remember whether fifty or sixty years ago.

The launch happened unexpectedly. Gravity didn't change by a hair, no sounds penetrated the hermetic interior, shadows drifted rhythmically across the ceiling—perhaps it was years of routine, an old instinct that told me at a certain moment we were in space, for it was a certainty, not an assumption.

But I was preoccupied with something else. I rested in a half-reclining position, legs stretched out, motionless. They had let me have my way too easily. Even Oswamm had not put up much opposition to my decision. The counterarguments I heard from him and from Abs were unconvincing—I could have come up with better ones myself. They insisted on only one thing: that each of us had to fly separately. They didn't even hold it against me that I had stirred Olaf up (for if it hadn't been for me, he surely would have agreed to stay longer). It was puzzling. I had expected complications, something that would thwart my plan at the very last minute, but nothing happened, and here I was, flying. This final journey was set to end in fifteen minutes.

Evidently, what I had come up with, as well as the way I confronted them to fight for an early departure, didn't surprise them. They must have had this type of reaction cataloged; it was a stereotype of behavior typical of daredevils like me, equipped with an appropriate serial number in their psychotechnical tables. They let me fly—why? Because experience told them I wouldn't be able to handle it? But how could that be, since this whole "independent" escapade consisted of a flight from one terminal to another, where someone from Earth’s Adapt was supposed to be waiting, and all I had to do was find this person at the agreed-upon spot?

Something happened. Raised voices reached me. I leaned out of my seat. A few rows ahead of me, a woman shoved the stewardess away, who, with a slowed, automatic motion, as if under the influence of that—not entirely forceful—shove, walked backward between the seats, and the woman repeated: 

"I won't have it! Don't let it touch me!"

I didn't see the shouting woman's face. Her companion was pulling her by the arm, explaining something to her soothingly. What did this scene mean? The other passengers paid it no attention. For the umpteenth time, I was seized by a feeling of incredible alienation. I looked up at the stewardess, who had stopped beside me and was smiling just as before. It wasn't the purely superficial smile of obligatory politeness covering up nervousness over the incident. She wasn't feigning calmness; she was truly calm.

"Would you care for a drink? Prum, extran, morr, cider?" A melodious voice. I shook my head no.

I wanted to say something nice to her, but only managed the stereotypical question:

"When do we land?"

"In six minutes. Will you eat something? You don't have to rush. You can stay even after landing."

"No, thank you."

She walked away. In the air, right in front of my face, against the backrest of the seat ahead of me, the word STRATO flared up, as if written with the glowing tip of a cigarette. I leaned forward to see how this sign was formed, and gave a start. The back of the seat followed my back, embracing it elastically. I already knew that furnishings anticipated every shift in position, but I kept forgetting about it. It was not pleasant—as if someone were tracking my every move.

I tried to return to my previous position, evidently too energetically. The seat misunderstood and almost fully reclined, like a bed. I sprang up. What idiocy! More self-control.

I sat down at last. The pink letters of STRATO quivered and flowed into others: TERMINAL. No jolt, no warning, no hiss. Nothing. A distant voice rang out, like a post horn, four oval doors at the ends of the aisles between the seats slid open, and a dull, all-encompassing roar, like the sea, rushed inside. The voices of the passengers rising from their seats drowned in it without a trace. I remained seated while they filed out, rows of silhouettes flickering against the outside lights in green, lilac, and purple—a veritable masquerade. Now everyone was gone. I stood up. Mechanically, I tugged at the hem of my sweater. It felt foolish, somehow, standing there with empty hands. A cooler breeze drafted in through the open doorways. I turned around. The stewardess was standing by the partition wall, her back not touching it. That same serene smile still lingered on her face, directed at the empty rows of seats, which now slowly began to curl up, folding away like fleshy flowers, some faster, others a fraction slower—this was the only movement within that all-filling, continuous roar flowing through the oval doorways, bringing to mind the open sea. *I don't want* it *to touch me!* Suddenly, I detected something wrong in her smile. From the exit, I said:

"Goodbye..."

"At your service."

The meaning of those words, so peculiar coming from a pretty young woman, didn't register right away, for they reached me just as I had turned and was leaning through the doorway. I went to put my foot on the step, but it wasn't there. A meter-wide gap yawned between the metal hull and the edge of the platform. Losing my balance, unprepared for such a trap, I made a clumsy leap, and while still in the air I felt a stream of invisible force catch me from below, so that I glided over the void and was set down softly on a white surface, which yielded elastically. I must have had a rather foolish expression mid-flight—I felt a few amused glances, or at least I thought I did. I turned quickly and set off along the platform. The projectile I had arrived in rested in a deep cradle, separated from the edge of the platforms by a completely unguarded gap. As if by accident, I stepped closer to this void and once again felt the invisible resilience, which prevented me from stepping beyond the white edge. I wanted to search for the source of this peculiar force, but suddenly it was as if I snapped awake: I was on Earth.

The wave of people swept me up: pushed along, I moved forward through the throng. A moment passed before I fully took in the enormity of this hall. Was it even a single hall, for that matter? No walls: a white, gleaming explosion of incredible wings suspended high above, and between them columns constructed not of some material, but of dizzying motion. Shooting upward, giant fountains of a liquid denser than water, illuminated from within by colored spotlights? No; vertical glass tunnels through which processions of blurred vehicles whizzed skyward? I no longer knew anything. Relentlessly pushed and jostled in the swarming rush of the crowds, I tried to make my way to some empty space, but there were no empty spaces here. Standing a head taller than those around me, I was able to see that the empty projectile was receding—no, it was we who were gliding forward, along with the entire platform. From on high blazed lights in which the crowd sparkled and shimmered. Now the surface on which we stood crowded together began to rise, and down below I saw already-distant double white bands jammed with people, with black yawns of gaps running alongside the inert hulls—for there were dozens of ships like ours—as the moving platform banked, accelerated, and slipped beneath higher levels. Fluttering, whipping the hair of the bystanders with the wind of their passage, smooth shadows quivering with speed hurtled across them—as if along impossible viaducts entirely devoid of support—their signal lights elongated into streaks of fire; then the surface carrying us began to divide, branching off along imperceptible seams; my own strip glided through interiors filled with seated and standing people; a multitude of tiny flashes surrounded them, as though they were busy setting off colored fireworks.

I didn't know where to look. In front of me stood a man in something fluffy like fur, which, touched by light, opalesced like metal. He had a woman in scarlet on his arm. What she was wearing was patterned with massive eyes, like a peacock’s, and these eyes were blinking. It was no illusion; the eyes on her dress were actually opening and closing. The walkway I was standing on behind the two of them, among dozens of other people, accelerated even more. Between planes of smoky-white glass, colorful, illuminated arcades opened up with transparent ceilings, ceaselessly trodden by hundreds of feet on the next level up; the all-encompassing roar alternately expanded and contracted as the thousands of human voices and sounds—incomprehensible to me, meaningful to them—were smothered by yet another tunnel of this journey aimed who-knew-where; deep in the background, the surroundings were continuously stitched by the flashing streaks of I don't know what vehicles—perhaps flying ones—for they sometimes shot diagonally up or down, boring into space, making me instinctively expect a horrific collision, since I could see no guide rails or tracks, if these were indeed aerial trams. Whenever these blurred hurricanes of momentum broke off if only for a moment, from behind them emerged majestically slow, colossal planes full of people, like flying docks that moved in various directions, passed one another, rose, and seemed to permeate each other due to a trick of perspective. It was hard to rest my eyes on anything stationary, for the entire surrounding architecture seemed to be composed purely of movement, of change, and even what I had initially taken for a winged ceiling was merely suspended tiers, which now gave way to others, even higher. Suddenly, filtered through the glass of the ceilings and those enigmatic columns, reflecting off the silver planes, there entered into every crevice of space, into the interiors of the passing culverts, into the features of human faces—a heavy, purplish dawn, as if far away, somewhere deep in the heart of this multi-mile structure, an atomic fire had ignited.

The green of the ceaselessly jumping neons turned muddy, the milkiness of the parabolic buttresses flushed pink. In this sudden saturation of the air with russet there was something of an omen of catastrophe, or so I took it, yet no one paid the slightest attention to this change, and I wouldn't even be able to say when it faded.

At the edges of our walkway there appeared spinning green circles, like neon hoops suspended in midair; then some of the people would step off onto the approaching spur of another walkway or ramp; I noticed that you could pass right through the green lines of those lights with impunity, as if they were immaterial.

For some time I let myself be carried along passively by the white walkway, until it occurred to me that I might already be outside the station, and that this unbelievable landscape of contorted glass, forever seeming on the verge of taking flight, was in fact the city, and the one I had left behind now existed only in my memory.

"Excuse me," I touched the arm of the man in the fur, "where are we?..."

They both looked at me. Their faces, when they looked up, took on an expression of surprise.

I held a faint hope that it was only because of my height.

"On the polyduct," the man said. "What's your connection?"

I understood nothing.

"Are we... still at the station?"

"Of course..." he replied with some hesitation.

"And... where is the Inner Circle?"

"You've already missed it. You have to double back."

"A rast from Merid would be better," the woman interjected. All the eyes on her dress seemed to stare at me with suspicious astonishment.

"A rast?..." I repeated helplessly.

"Oh, over there," she pointed out an empty elevation, visible through the approaching green ring, with black-and-silver striped sides, resembling the hull of a peculiarly painted ship lying on its side. I thanked her and stepped off the walkway, probably in the wrong place, because the momentum swept my legs out from under me. I regained my balance, but I was spun around so much that I had no idea which way to go. I debated what to do, but in the meantime, my transfer point had drifted quite a distance from that black-and-silver elevation the woman had pointed out, and I could no longer find it. Since most of the people standing near me were crossing onto an upward ramp, I did the same. 

Once on it, I caught sight of a colossal sign burning motionless in the air: DUCT CENTER—the rest of the letters on either side escaped my vision due to their sheer enormity. I was carried soundlessly up to a platform that must have been a kilometer long, from which a spindle-shaped ship was just pulling away, revealing its light-pierced underside as it ascended. Then again, perhaps that whale-like shape was the platform, and I had ended up on a 'rast'—there was no one around even to ask, as an utter emptiness stretched out all around. I must have come to the wrong place. A section of my 'platform' was lined with flattened compartments lacking front walls. Approaching them, I made out a series of dimly lit, low bays where black machines rested in rows. I mistook them for cars. But when the two nearest ones slid forward and, before I could even step back, shot past me, instantly attaining tremendous speed, I saw—before they vanished down the perspective of parabolic inclines—that they had no wheels, windows, or doors; they were streamlined, like giant black drops. Cars or not, I thought, this must be some sort of parking lot, at any rate? Perhaps for those very 'rasts'? I decided the best thing to do was wait for someone to show up and ride with them, or at least find something out. My platform, raised slightly like the wing of an impossible airplane, remained empty, however; only the black machines kept slipping out of their metal burrows, singly or a few at a time, and hurtling away, always in the same direction. I walked down to the very edge of the platform, until that invisible, elastic force ensuring safety made itself felt once again. It truly hung in midair, supported by nothing. Looking up, I saw many similar ones, hovering motionless in space in just the same way, with their huge lights extinguished; they blazed on others, wherever ships were docking. These, however, were neither rockets nor even projectiles like the one that had brought me from Luna.

I stood there a long time, until I noticed, against the backdrop of some further halls—though I had no idea whether they were a mirror reflection of this one or reality—fiery letters gliding rhythmically through the air: SOAMO SOAMO SOAMO, a pause, a blue flash, and then NEONAX NEONAX NEONAX. Perhaps they were the names of stations, or perhaps advertised products. They meant nothing to me.

It’s high time I found that guy, I thought, turned on my heel, and, locating a walkway flowing in the opposite direction, rode it down. It turned out to be the wrong level, and not even the hall from which I had come up: I could tell by the absence of those immense columns. Then again, perhaps they had simply taken themselves off somewhere; I now considered anything possible.

I found myself amidst a whole forest of fountains; further on, I came upon a white-and-pink hall, full of women.

Passing by, I casually thrust my hand into the stream of an illuminated fountain, perhaps because it was pleasant to encounter something even a little familiar. Yet I felt nothing; this fountain was waterless. A moment later, I thought I smelled a floral scent. I brought my hand to my nostrils. It smelled like a thousand toilet soaps all at once. Instinctively, I began wiping it on my trousers. I was already standing before that hall, full of women, nothing but women. It didn't look like the anteroom to a restroom to me, but then again, it wasn't certain. I preferred not to ask, so I turned back. A young man, dressed as if spilling mercury had congealed upon him, puffy (or rather frothy) at the shoulders, tight at the hips, was talking with a fair-haired girl leaning her back against the basin of a fountain. The girl, in a light dress, quite ordinary, which filled me with relief, held a bouquet of pale pink flowers and, nestling her face into them, smiled with her eyes at the boy. At the last moment, when I stood beside them and was already opening my mouth, I saw that she was eating those flowers—and for a split second my voice failed me. She was calmly chewing the delicate petals. She raised her eyes to me. They froze. But I was used to that by now. I asked where the Inner Circle was.

The boy seemed unpleasantly surprised, or even angry, that someone would dare to interrupt their tête-à-tête. Evidently, I had committed an impropriety. He looked up first, then lowered his gaze, as if expecting to find some stilts as the cause of my height. He didn't even speak.

"Oh, over there," the girl cried out. "The rast for the I.C., your rast, you'll make it, quickly!" I took off running in the indicated direction, not even knowing where to—I still had no earthly idea, after all, what this damned rast looked like—and after ten paces I spotted a silvery funnel descending from on high, the base of one of those immense columns that had so astounded me earlier—could they be flying columns?—people were hurrying there from all sides. Suddenly I collided with someone. I didn't even stagger; I just stood rooted to the spot, but the other man, a squat fellow in orange attire, fell, and something unbelievable happened to him: his fur wilted right before my eyes, collapsing like a punctured balloon! I stood over him, dumbfounded, unable to stammer an apology. He got up, glowered at me, but said nothing; he turned and strode off briskly, fiddling with something at his chest—and his outfit filled back up and began to glow anew...

There was no one left at the spot the girl had pointed out. After that adventure, I gave up for good on searching for rasts, the Inner Circle, ducts, and connections, and decided to get out of the station. My experiences thus far did not encourage me to accost passersby, so I rode upward at random, following a slanted blue arrow, feeling no particular sensation as my body passed right through two successive glowing signs in the air: LOCAL CIRCUITS. I ended up on a fairly crowded escalator. The next level was styled in tones of subdued bronze, veined with golden exclamation marks. Fluid convergences of ceilings and concave walls. Ceilingless corridors, their upper reaches seemingly submerged in a luminous down. As if I were approaching some residential area, the surroundings had something of a network of giant hotel lobbies about them—small windows, nickel pipes along the walls, alcoves with clerks on duty, maybe they were currency exchanges, maybe a post office, I walked on. I was almost certain by now that I wouldn't find the exit this way, and (judging by how long the ride up had taken) that I was in the elevated part of the station, yet I kept to the same direction. An unexpected emptiness, panels of raspberry cladding speckled with sparkling stars, rows of doors. The nearest ones were ajar. I peered inside. A large, broad-shouldered man did exactly the same thing from the opposite side at that very moment. Myself, in a wall-to-wall mirror. I pushed the door open wider. Porcelain, silver pipes, nickel. Restrooms.

I felt a slight urge to laugh, but all in all I was rather dazed. I turned back quickly, another corridor, milk-white bands flowing downward. The escalator handrail was soft, warm; I didn't count the passing floors. More and more people, they were stopping at enameled boxes that sprouted from the walls at every step: a single touch of a finger, something dropped into their hand, they pocketed it and walked on. I don't know myself why I acted exactly like the man in the loose purple outfit ahead of me; a key with a small indentation for a fingertip, a press, and straight into my cupped hand dropped a colorful, semitransparent tube, seemingly warm.

I shook it, held it up to my eyes. Pills of some sort? No. A cap? It had no cap, no seal of any kind. What was it for? What had the others done? Put it in their pockets. The inscription on the machine: LAHGAN. I stood there; I was being jostled. And suddenly I felt like an ape that had been handed a fountain pen or a lighter; for a fraction of a second, blind rage washed over me; I clenched my jaws, narrowed my eyes, and merged, slightly hunched, into the stream of pedestrians. The corridor widened, becoming a hall. Fiery letters: REAL AMMO REAL AMMO.

Through the current of hurrying people, above their heads, I spotted a window in the distance. The first window.

Panoramic, immense.

Like all the firmaments of the night, laid flat. To a horizon of incandescent mist—colorful galaxies of plazas, clusters of spiral lights, drizzling glows over skyscrapers, streets: a crawling, worm-like movement of luminous beads, and above this, in the verticals, a seething of neons, plumes and lightning bolts, wheels, airplanes and bottles of fire, red dandelions of signal lights on spires, momentary suns and hemorrhages of advertisements, mechanically violent. I stood and stared, hearing the steady murmur of hundreds of feet behind me. Suddenly the city vanished, and a gigantic, three-meter face appeared. 

"We have broadcast a compilation of newsreels from the seventies in the series 'Visions of Old Capitals'.

Transtel is now shifting its coverage to the cosmolite studios..."

I practically fled. It wasn't a window. Some kind of television. I quickened my pace. I broke into a slight sweat.

Downward. Faster. Golden squares of light. Crowds inside, foam on glasses, an almost black liquid, not beer, for it had a venomous greenish gleam, and young people, boys and girls, embracing, in sixes, eights, blocking the entire width of the passages; they were walking right at me and had to pull their arms apart to let me pass. I was jolted. Without realizing when, I stepped onto a moving walkway. Astonished eyes flashed right up close—a beautiful, dark girl in something that gleamed on her like phosphorized metal. The fabric clung to her: she was as if naked. White faces, yellow faces, a few tall Black men, but I was still the tallest. People parted before me. Up above, behind convex glass, windblown shadows sped by, invisible orchestras played, while down here a peculiar promenade went on; in the dark passages

—headless figures of women; the down covering their shoulders glowed so brightly that only their protruding necks shone within it like strange, white stalks, along with a diffused gleam in their hair—self-luminous powder? A narrow passage led me into an enfilade of statues, grotesque because they were moving, even restless; something like a wide street with elevated sides roared with laughter; people were having fun—what amused them so much? These sculptures?

Enormous figures in cones of spotlights; from them poured ruby light, honey light, thick as syrup, of an extraordinary concentration of color. I walked on without volition, squinting my eyes, losing myself.

A steep, green arcade, grotesque pavilions, pagodas accessed by little bridges, full of small eateries, the sharp, insistent smell of frying, rows of little gas flames behind glass, the clatter of glass, repetitive, metallic, incomprehensible sounds. The crowd that had swept me in here collided with another, then it thinned out; everyone was boarding a train car open straight through—no, it was only transparent, as if cast from glass; even the seats looked like glass, though they were soft. Before I even realized I was inside, we were moving. The car hurtled on; people shouted over the loudspeaker that kept repeating: "Meridional level, Meridional level, connections for Spiro, Blekkr Frosom." The whole car seemed to melt, pierced by beams of light; the walls flashed by in streaks of flame and color, parabolic arches, white platforms. "Forteran, Forteran, connections for Galea, connections for outer rasts, Makra," muttered the loudspeaker. The car would stop and then hurtle on; I discovered an astonishing thing: there was no sensation of braking or accelerating, as if inertia had been abolished. How was that possible? I tested it by slightly bending my knees at the next three stops. In the turns, nothing either. People were getting off and on; on the front platform stood a woman with a dog, I had never seen one like it: it was huge, with a spherical head, very ugly, and in its calm hazel eyes, diminished garlands of lights were reflected, rushing backward. RAMBRENT RAMBRENT. There was a flutter of white and bluish fluorescent tubes, stairs of crystalline glare, black facades; the glare slowly turned to stone, the car stood still. I stepped out and was dumbfounded. Above the amphitheater-like sunken disc of the stop rose a familiar, multi-level structure; I was still at the station, in a different part of the same gigantic hall, distended by the white sweeps of planes. I headed toward the edge of this geometrically precise depression—the car had already departed—and experienced another shock: I was not down at the bottom, as it had seemed to me; I was actually high up, some forty stories above the ribbons of walkways visible in the abyss, above the silver decks of steadily gliding platforms, and long, silent hulks were slipping between them, and people were emerging from them through rows of hatches, as if these monsters, these chrome fish, were laying deposits of black and colored roe at regular intervals. Above all this, in the distance, through the haze of distance, I saw moving, as if along an invisible line, golden words:

GLENIANA ROON, RETURNING TODAY WITH A RECORDING OF A MIMORPHIC REAL, PAYS HOMAGE IN THE ORATORIUM TO THE MEMORY OF RAPPER KERX POLITRA. TERMINAL DAILY REPORTS: TODAY IN AMMONLEE PETIFARGUE BROUGHT THE FIRST ENZOM TO SYSTOLIZATION. WE WILL BROADCAST THE EMINENT GRAVITICIST'S VOICE AT HOUR TWENTY-SEVEN. ARRAKER IN THE LEAD. ARRAKER REPEATED HIS SUCCESS AS THE FIRST OBLITERATOR OF THE SEASON AT THE TRANSVAAL STADIUM.

I walked away. So even the reckoning of time had changed. Struck by the light of colossal letters, which raced above the sea of heads like rows of burning tightrope walkers, the metallic fabrics of women’s dresses flickered with sudden flames. I walked, unaware of it, while something inside me kept repeating: so even time has changed. That seemed to finish me off. I saw nothing with my eyes wide open. I wanted only one thing: to get out of here, to escape this hellish station, to find myself under the open sky, in open space, to see the stars, to feel the wind.

I was drawn to an avenue of elongated lights; something was written in the translucent stone of the ceilings—the letters traced by a sharp little flame encased in alabaster—TELETRANS TELEPORT TELETHON. Through a pointed archway (but it was an impossible, unhinged arch, like the negative of a rocket’s nose cone) I made my way into a hall roofed by a frozen golden fire. In the recesses of the walls were hundreds of booths; people were dashing into them, hurrying back out, dropping torn strips on the floor—not telegraph tape, it was something else, covered in embossed nodules—while others trampled over these shreds. I meant to leave, but by mistake stepped into a dark interior. Before I could back out, something buzzed, a flash like a photographer's lamp, and from a metal-rimmed slot, like a letterbox, slid a small sheet of glossy paper, folded in two. I took it, opened it, and a human head emerged from it; with slightly parted, faintly skewed thin lips, it stared at me with narrowed eyes: it was me! I folded the paper in two, and the plastic phantom vanished. I slowly pried the edges apart—nothing; wider, and it appeared again, as if springing from nowhere, a head severed from its torso, suspended above the sheet of paper, wearing a rather vacant expression. For a moment I stared into my own face—what was this, a three-dimensional photograph?

I shoved the slip of paper into my pocket and walked out. The golden inferno seemed to be descending upon the heads of the crowd, the ceiling of fiery magma, unreal, yet breathing a real blaze, and no one looked at it; preoccupied, they ran from one booth to another, green letters leapt in the depths, columns of digits cascaded down narrow screens, other booths had, instead of doors, roller blinds that snapped upward the instant anyone approached; I finally found an exit.

A curved corridor with a sloping floor, like sometimes in a theater; stylized conch shells bloomed from the walls, while overhead the words INFOR INFOR INFOR raced by, endlessly. 

I had first seen an infor on Luna, and mistook it for an artificial flower. 

I leaned my face toward the celadon calyx, which instantly, before I even parted my lips, froze in anticipation.

"Which way can I get out?" I asked, rather dazed.

"Where to?" a warm alto replied instantly.

"To the city."

"Which district?"

"It doesn't matter."

"Which level?"
