PROLOGUE

The swallowed slip of paper stops in the esophagus, somewhere near the heart. It becomes soaked with saliva. The black, specially prepared ink slowly dissolves, and the letters lose their shape. Within the human body, the word splits in two, into substance and essence. As the former fades, the latter, remaining formless, allows itself to be absorbed into the tissues of the body, for essence constantly seeks a material vessel—even if this is to become the cause of much misfortune.

Yente awakens, and yet she was already almost dead. She feels it clearly now; it is like an ache, like a river’s current, a trembling, a pressing, a motion.

Around her heart, a delicate vibration returns; the heart beats faintly but evenly, sure of itself. Warmth flows once again into Yente’s withered, bony breasts. Yente blinks and struggles to lift her eyelids. She sees the worried face of Elisha Shor leaning over her. She tries to smile at him, but she does not have quite that much control over her face. Elisha Shor’s brow is furrowed; he looks at her reproachfully. His lips move, but no sound reaches Yente’s ears. Hands appear from somewhere—old Shor’s large hands—reaching for her neck and slipping under the blanket. Clumsily, Shor tries to roll her limp body onto its side to peer beneath it, at the sheet. No, Yente does not feel his efforts; she feels only the warmth and the presence of this sweating, bearded man.

Then suddenly, as though struck by a blow, Yente is looking down at everything from above—at herself and at the balding crown of Shor’s head, in his struggle with her body, for his cap has fallen off. 

And from then on, this is how it is: Yente sees everything.


I. THE BOOK OF MIST

1

1752, Rohatyn

It is late October, early morning. The Reverend Dean stands on the porch of the rectory, waiting for the carriage. He is used to rising at dawn, but this time he feels only half-awake and doesn’t really know how he found himself here—alone, facing a sea of fog. He remembers neither how he got up, nor how he dressed, nor whether he has eaten yet. He looks with surprise at his sturdy boots poking out from beneath his cassock, at the somewhat frayed skirts of his faded woolen coat, and at the gloves he holds in his hand. He slides the left one on; inside, it feels warm and fits his hand perfectly, as if hand and glove had known each other for years. He breathes a sigh of relief. He touches the bag slung over his shoulder, mechanically feeling its rectangular edges—hard, thickened like scars beneath the skin—and slowly remembers what is inside: a heavy, familiar, pleasant shape. Something good, something that brought him here, some words, some signs—all of this is profoundly connected to his life. Oh yes, he knows what is in there now, and this awareness slowly begins to warm his body, and because of this, the fog seems to become somewhat more transparent. Behind him is the dark opening of the doorway, one leaf closed; the cold weather must have likely set in already, perhaps a frost has even blighted the plums in the orchard. Above the door is a blurry inscription—he sees it without looking, for of course he knows what is written there, having commissioned it himself; two craftsmen from Podhajce had spent a whole week carving the letters into the wood, for he had ordered them to be done ornately:

WHAT WAS TODAY, TOMORROW IS DONE.

WHAT HAS FLED, CAN BE CAUGHT BY ИONE

In the word “none,” which annoys him greatly, the letter “N” is written backward, like its mirror image. 

Irritated by this for the umpteenth time, the priest shakes his head sharply—and this finally wakes him up. That reversed letter is an “И”... What carelessness! You have to constantly watch their hands, keep an eye on them at every step. And since those craftsmen were Jews, they consequently made the inscription somewhat Jewish; the letters are too curly, unsteady. And one of them even stubbornly argued that such an “N” was also fine, even prettier, because the crossbar goes from bottom to top and from left to right, the Christian way, whereas the other way around would be precisely the Jewish way. This minor irritation brings him back to his senses, and now Father Benedykt Chmielowski, the Dean of Rohatyn, sees where he got the feeling that he was still asleep—he is standing in a fog the color of his bedding, a grayish hue; an off-white already catching the dirt, vast reserves of gray that form the lining of the world. The fog is motionless, tightly filling the entire yard; looming behind it are the familiar shapes of the great pear tree, the low wall, and further on—the wicker wagon. It is an ordinary heavenly cloud that has fallen to earth and pressed its belly against it. He read about this yesterday in Comenius.

Now he hears the familiar creaking and clatter that, on every journey, inevitably draw him into a state of creative meditation. Only in the wake of the sound do Roszko, leading the horse by the bridle, and the Dean's calash emerge from the fog. At the sight of it, the priest feels a surge of energy; he slaps his glove against his palm and clambers onto the seat. Roszko, taciturn as always, adjusts the harness and casts the priest a long look. The fog grays his face, making him appear older to the priest than ever before—as if he had aged overnight, though he is only a young lad.

At last they set off, yet it feels as though they are standing still; only the swaying of the vehicle and its soothing creak attest to their movement. They have traveled this road so many times, for so many years, that there is no longer any need to take in the views, and no landmarks will be necessary. The priest knows they have now turned onto the road that skirts the edge of the forest, and they will proceed this way until the crossroads, where there stands a wayside shrine, erected by the priest himself years ago when he took over the parish in Firlejów. He had pondered at length over whom to place inside this shrine. Saint Benedict, his patron saint, came to mind, or perhaps Onuphrius the hermit, who was miraculously fed with dates by a palm tree in the desert, while angels brought him the Body of Christ from heaven every eighth day. For Firlejów was meant to be just such a desert for the priest, when he arrived here after years spent tutoring Dymitr, the son of His Highness Lord Jabłonowski. On further reflection, however, the priest decided that the shrine should not be built for himself to satisfy his own vanity, but for the common folk, so that they might have a place to rest at the crossroads and lift their thoughts to heaven. And so, upon a whitewashed brick pedestal, stood Our Lady, Queen of the World, wearing a crown upon her head. Beneath her small, pointed slipper writhed a serpent.

But today even she vanishes in the fog, along with the shrine and the crossroads. Only the treetops are visible, a sign that the fog is beginning to settle.

"Look here, Father, Kaśka refuses to go," Roszko says gloomily as the calash comes to a halt. Roszko climbs down from the box and crosses himself several times with sweeping gestures.

Then he leans over and peers into the fog, as if staring into water. His shirt protrudes from beneath his festive red, though somewhat faded, jacket.

"I don't know which way to go," he says.

"What do you mean, you don't know? We're already on the Rohatyn highroad," the priest says in surprise.

And yet! He steps out of the calash and follows his servant; they walk helplessly around the vehicle, straining their eyes into the whiteness. They think they see something, but their eyes, unable to anchor onto anything, begin to play tricks on them. To think that such a thing has happened to them! Why, it's like getting lost in your own pocket.

"Hush!" the priest says suddenly, raising a finger as he listens. And indeed, somewhere to the left, from the swirling fog, comes the faint rush of water.

"Let us follow the sound. It's flowing water," the priest decides.

Now they will crawl along very slowly beside the river called the Gniła Lipa. The water will guide them.

Soon the priest relaxes in the calash, stretches his legs out before him, and lets his eyes wander across the sea of fog. He promptly slips into a traveler’s reverie, for a man thinks best when in motion. Slowly, reluctantly, the mechanism of his mind springs to life; the cogs and gears catch, setting the drive wheels in motion, exactly like the clock that stands in the vestibule of his rectory, purchased in Lviv—he had paid a hefty sum for it. Any moment now it will chime *ding-dong*. Could the world have emerged from such a fog, he begins to wonder. After all, the Jewish historian Josephus Flavius maintains that the world was created in the autumn, during the autumnal *aequinoctium*. One might think so, for there was fruit in Paradise, after all; if an apple hung on a tree, it must have been autumn... There is some sense to this. But a moment later another thought occurs to him: What kind of argument is that? Could Almighty God not have created such paltry fruit specially, in whatever season?

As they reach the main road leading to Rohatyn, they merge into a stream of pedestrians, riders, and wagons of every kind, which emerge from the fog like bread-dough figurines molded for Christmas. It is Wednesday, market day in Rohatyn, and peasant carts roll past, laden with sacks of grain, cages of poultry, and all manner of farm produce. Among them march peddlers at a brisk pace, bearing every conceivable ware—their little stalls, cleverly folded up for now, can be carried on their backs like yokes, only to be transformed moments later into tables piled high with colorful fabrics, wooden toys, and eggs bought up in the villages for a quarter of the price... Peasants are also leading goats and cows for sale—terrified by the commotion, the animals stubbornly plant their hooves in the puddles. A speeding wagon passes them, covered with a tattered tarpaulin and packed with boisterous Jews flocking to the Rohatyn fair from all around the region; behind it, a lavish carriage forces its way through, struggling to maintain its dignity in the fog and the congestion on the road—its brightly lacquered doors are black with mud, and the coachman in the blue cape looks quite dismayed; he clearly hadn't expected such chaos and is now casting about desperately for a chance to turn off this infernal road.

Roszko is stubborn and refuses to be pushed off into the field; keeping to the right, driving with one wheel on the grass and the other on the road, he makes brisk progress forward. A flush spreads across his sad, drawn face, and a kind of fiendish grimace washes over it. The priest casts a glance at him and is reminded of an engraving he had looked at only yesterday—it depicted denizens of hell, and they wore the exact same grimaces on their faces that Roszko wears now.

"Make way for His Reverence the Father Benefactor! Giddy up! Aside, people!" shouts Roszko.

The first buildings spring up before them, suddenly, without warning. Evidently, the fog distorts one’s sense of distance, for even Kaśka seems taken by surprise. She shies suddenly, jerking the shaft, and were it not for Roszko’s firm reaction and his whip, she would have overturned the calash. Perhaps it was the sparks flying from the forge that frightened Kaśka, or the restlessness of the horses waiting their turn to be shod...

Further on stands a tavern, shabby and poor, resembling a peasant cottage. A well sweep juts above it like a gallows, piercing upward through the fog, its tip vanishing somewhere high above. The priest sees that a dusty carriage has pulled up here; the weary coachman has dropped his head almost to his knees and does not jump down from the box, nor does anyone emerge from inside. Yet a tall, thin Jew is already standing before it, and beside him, little girls with tangled hair. This is all the Reverend Dean sees, for the fog swallows every passing sight; it vanishes somewhere, soaking in like a melted snowflake.

Here is Rohatyn.

It begins with mud huts, small clay cottages topped with straw thatch that seems to press the buildings to the ground; the closer to the market square, however, the more slender the houses become, their thatch growing finer until finally giving way to the wooden shingles of townhouses built from unfired clay brick. There is also the parish church, the Dominican monastery, the Church of Saint Barbara by the square, and further on, two synagogues and five Orthodox churches. Little houses sit like mushrooms around the square, each one housing some sort of business. A tailor, a ropemaker, a furrier—all of them Jews—and next to them a baker named Bochenek, meaning Loaf, which always pleases the Reverend Dean, for it reveals a certain hidden order that could be more visible and consistent, and then people would live more virtuously. Next is the workshop of a sword-maker called Luba; the facade stands out with its affluence, the walls are freshly painted blue, and a great rusted sword hangs over the entrance—evidently this Luba is a fine craftsman, and his clients' pockets are full. Further down is a saddler who has set a wooden trestle outside his door, and upon it a beautiful saddle, its stirrups likely silver-plated, given how they shine.

The heavy, cloying scent of malt is everywhere, permeating every ware laid out for sale. One could fill up on it as if on bread. On the outskirts of Rohatyn, in Babińce, there are a few small breweries; it is from there that this filling aroma drifts across the entire area. Many small stalls here sell beer, while the better shops also stock spirits and mead, mainly *trójniak*. The shop of the Jewish merchant Wakszul offers wine: genuine Hungarian and genuine Rhenish, as well as a somewhat tart variety that they bring here all the way from Wallachia.

The priest makes his way along the stalls constructed of every conceivable material—boards, pieces of coarsely woven cloth, wicker baskets, and even leaves. A good woman in a white headscarf is selling pumpkins from a cart, their bright orange color drawing the children. Beside her, another woman hawks rounds of cheese laid out on horseradish leaves. Further along stand many other market women who have taken to trade because they are widowed or have drunkards for husbands: oil-sellers, salt-sellers, linen-sellers. The priest usually buys this meat-pie seller's wares, and now he offers the vendor a warm smile. Behind her are two stalls adorned with green boughs, which means that freshly brewed beer is sold there. And here is the rich stall of Armenian merchants—beautiful, lightweight fabrics, knives in ornate sheaths, and right next to it—*wizina*, or dried fish, whose sickly odor permeates the woolen Turkish tapestries. Further on, from a box slung over his bony shoulders, a man in a dusty khalat is selling eggs packed by the dozen in grass-woven baskets. Another offers eggs by the threescore, in large baskets, at a competitive, almost wholesale price. Bagels festoon an entire baker's stall—someone dropped one in the mud, and a little dog is now devouring it with gusto.

They sell whatever they can here. Floral fabrics, kerchiefs and shawls straight from the bazaars of Istanbul, children’s booties, fruit, and nuts. A man by the fence has a plow and nails of all sizes, from the size of a pin to massive ones meant for building a house. Next to him, a robust woman in a starched cap has laid out clappers for the night watchmen: small ones, whose sound is more reminiscent of the nocturnal chirping of crickets than a call to sleep, and large ones that, on the contrary, would wake the dead.

How many times have Jews been forbidden to trade in goods related to the Church. Priests and rabbis alike have thundered about this matter, but to no avail. And so, here are beautiful prayer books with ribbons between the pages, their covers embossed with wondrously silvered letters that feel warm and alive when you run a fingertip over them. A clean, almost elegant man in a fur hat holds them like relics—wrapped in thin paper, a cream-colored tissue, so that this dirty, foggy day will not soil their innocent, Christian pages, fragrant with printer’s ink. He also has wax candles, and even pictures of saints with halos.

The priest approaches one of the itinerant booksellers, hoping to find something in Latin. All the books are Jewish, however, for beside them lie objects whose purpose the priest does not know.

The deeper one’s gaze ventures into the side streets, the more the misery pokes through, like a dirty toe from a torn shoe; a threadbare, quiet poverty, bowed to the earth. These are no longer shops or stalls, but doghouses, cobbled together from thin planks scavenged from rubbish heaps. In one of them, a cobbler mends shoes that have already been stitched, resoled, and patched many times over. In another, hung all over with iron pots, sits a tinker. His face is thin and sunken, his cap covering a forehead dotted with strange brown blotches. The Reverend Dean would be afraid to have his pots mended by him, lest the touch of the wretch’s fingers pass some terrible disease on to others. Next door, an old man sharpens knives as well as sickles and scythes of every sort. His workshop consists of a stone wheel tied around his neck. Whenever he is given something to sharpen, he sets a primitive wooden frame on the ground—a few leather straps turn it into a simple machine whose wheel, turned by hand, licks the metal blades. Sometimes a few very real sparks fly from this machine and fall into the mud, to the particular delight of the filthy, scab-ridden children. He earns mere pennies at his trade. He could always use that wheel to drown himself in the river—such being the only other benefit of his profession. 

Women in rags gather wood shavings and dung from the street for fuel. Looking at those rags, it is hard to tell whether this is Jewish, Orthodox, or Catholic poverty. Yes, poverty has neither faith nor nationality.

*Si est, ubi est?* the priest asks himself, thinking of Paradise. Certainly not here in Rohatyn, nor—so it seems to him—anywhere else in the land of Podolia. And should anyone think things are better in the big cities, they would be sorely mistaken. True, the priest has never in his life made it as far as Warsaw or Kraków, but he knows a thing or two from the tales of the Bernardine friar Pikulski, who is more widely traveled than he, or from what he overhears around the manor houses.

Paradise, or the garden of delights, was relocated by God to some beautiful and unknown place. And as it is written in the *Arca Noe*, Paradise is somewhere in the land of the Armenians, high up in the mountains, whereas Brunus claims it is *sub polo antarctico*, at the South Pole. The sign of Paradise's proximity is the four rivers: the Gebon, the Philon, the Euphrates, and the Tigris. And there are those authors who, unable to find a place for Paradise on earth, locate it in the air, some fifteen cubits above the mountains. But this, to the priest's mind, seems rather foolish. For how could that be? Would those living on earth beneath Paradise see it from below? Would they be looking up at the heels of the saints?

On the other hand, however, one cannot agree with those who attempt to spread the false notion that the holy text concerning Paradise is meant to have only a mystical meaning—that is, that it should be taken in a purely spiritual or allegorical sense. The priest—not merely because he is a priest, but out of his own deep conviction—believes that Holy Scripture must be taken literally.

He knows almost everything about Paradise, because just last week he finished a chapter of his highly ambitious book, a chapter compiled from all the books he has in Firlejów, and he has a hundred and thirty of them. For some of them, he had traveled to Lviv, and even to Lublin.

Here is a modest corner house—this is where he is headed. Such was Father Pikulski’s advice. The low double doors are wide open; a spicy aroma wafts out from within, unexpected amidst this stench of horseshit and autumn damp, along with yet another pungent scent, one the Reverend Dean already recognizes: *kaffa*. The priest does not drink *kaffa*, but he will finally have to become better acquainted with it.

The priest looks behind him, searching for Roszko; he sees him sorting through sheepskin coats with grim attention, and beyond him—the entire market, utterly preoccupied with itself. No one is looking at the priest; everyone is absorbed in their bartering. Noise and clamor.

Above the entrance to the building hangs a rather clumsily fashioned sign:

SZOR GOODS STORE

Then come Hebrew letters. A metal plaque hangs by the door, and next to it some symbols, and the priest remembers Athanasius Kircher stating in his book that the Jews, when a wife takes to her childbed and they fear a witch, write the words: “Adam, Hawa. Huc – Lilit” on the walls, which is supposed to mean: “Adam and Eve, come hither, and you, Lilith—that is, witch—begone.” That must be it. A child must have been born here recently, too.

The priest steps over the high threshold and is completely enveloped by the warm, spicy aroma. It takes a moment for his eyes to adjust to the gloom, for the only light is let in by a small window, which is blocked, moreover, by flowerpots.

Behind the counter stands a youth whose mustache has only just begun to sprout, with full lips that initially quiver at the sight of the priest before trying to form some word. He is utterly taken aback.

“What is your name, boy?” the priest asks boldly, to show how at ease he feels in this dark, low-ceilinged shop and to encourage the youth to speak, but he does not reply. “*Quod tibi nomen est?*” he therefore repeats formally, but the Latin, which was meant to aid communication, suddenly sounds overly solemn, as though the priest had come to perform an exorcism—just like Christ in the Gospel of Saint Luke, who addresses a possessed man with the very same question. But the boy only opens his eyes even wider and stammers, “bh, bh,” and then suddenly scurries behind the shelves, knocking into a braid of garlic hanging on a nail.

The priest acted foolishly; he should not have expected them to speak Latin here. He inspects himself critically—the black horsehair buttons of his cassock are poking out from beneath his cloak. That must be what frightened the boy, the priest thinks—the cassock. He smiles to himself, recalling the biblical Jeremiah, who had also nearly lost his head and stammered: *Aaa, Domine Deus ecce nescio loqui!*—Ah, Lord God! Behold, I cannot speak.

From now on, the priest calls the boy Jeremiah in his mind. He has no idea what to do now that the boy has vanished so suddenly. So he looks around the shop, buttoning his coat. It was Father Pikulski who persuaded him to come here; he took his advice, but now he doesn't think it was a good idea.

No one comes in from outside, for which the priest silently thanks God. It would hardly be an ordinary sight: a Catholic priest, the Dean of Rohatyn, in a Jew's shop, waiting to be served like a common townswoman. Father Pikulski had advised him to go to Rabbi Dubs in Lviv; he used to visit him himself and had learned a great deal from him. And the priest did go there, but old Dubs had probably had enough of Catholic priests pestering him about books. He was unpleasantly surprised by the request, and as for what interested Father Benedykt most, he either didn't have it or pretended not to. He put on a polite expression and shook his head, clicking his tongue. And when the priest asked who might be able to help him, Dubs waved his hands and, looking over his shoulder as if someone were standing behind him, made it clear that he didn't know—and even if he did, he wouldn't tell. Later, Father Pikulski explained to the Dean that it had to do with Jewish heresies; though they boast that they have no heresies among them, it seems they make an exception for this one and sincerely hate it, without mincing words.

Ultimately, Pikulski advised him to go to Shor. A large house with a shop on the market square. As he said this, however, he gave the priest a crooked look, with a touch of irony—or so it seemed to the priest. Perhaps he should have arranged to get these Jewish books through Pikulski? Even though the Dean doesn't much care for him. He wouldn't be standing here now, sweating and feeling ashamed. But the priest has a stubborn, contrary streak, so he went alone. And there was something else, too, something rather irrational—a little wordplay had worked its way into the whole affair; who would believe that such things have an impact on the world? The priest had been working diligently on a certain passage in Kircher, which mentioned the great ox Shorobor. And perhaps it was the similarity of the words that had drawn him here: Shor and Shorobor. Strange are the judgments of the Lord.

But where are those famous books, where is that figure inspiring such fearful respect? The shop looks like an ordinary stall, and yet its owner is supposedly a descendant of the famous rabbi, the highly esteemed sage Zalman Naftali Shor. And here there is garlic, herbs, pots of spices, jars large and small containing spices of every sort—crushed, ground, or still in their natural shape, like those vanilla beans, the little nails of cloves, or the rounds of nutmeg. Laid out on hay upon the shelves are also bolts of fabric—likely silk and satin, exceedingly bright, catching the eye. The priest considers whether he might happen to need anything, but his attention is already caught by a clumsy inscription on a sizable dark-green jar: “Herba the.” He already knows what he will ask for when someone finally comes out to him—a little of that herb which puts him in a better mood, which, for the Father Dean, means he can work without tiring. It improves digestion, too. And he would buy some cloves to spice his evening mulled wine. The past few nights have been so cold that his freezing legs would not let him concentrate on his writing. He looks around for a bench, and then everything happens all at once: a well-built, bearded man emerges from behind the shelves, wearing a long woolen robe from beneath which protrude pointy-toed Turkish shoes. A thin dark-blue cloak is draped over his shoulders. He squints, as if he had just climbed out of a well. Peering curiously from behind his back is that Jeremiah who had been spooked earlier, along with two more faces, very similar to Jeremiah’s—inquisitive and ruddy. And on the other side, a slight, breathless boy has just halted in the doorway leading from the market square—or perhaps a young man, given that his facial hair has sprouted profusely into a fair goatee. He leans against the doorframe, panting; he has clearly run here as fast as he could. He fixes the Reverend Dean with an insolent, piercing stare and smiles roguishly, revealing healthy, widely spaced teeth. The priest is not entirely certain that it isn’t a mocking smile. He prefers the dignified figure in the cloak, and it is to this man that he addresses himself with extraordinary politeness:

“Pray forgive this intrusion, sir…”

The man looks at him tensely, but a moment later his facial expression slowly changes. Something resembling a smile appears upon it. The Reverend Dean suddenly grasps that the man does not understand him, so he begins again differently, in Latin, joyfully certain that he has found a kindred spirit.

The Jew slowly shifts his gaze to the boy in the doorway, the breathless one, who steps boldly inside, tugging down his dark broadcloth jacket.

“I shall translate,” he announces in an unexpectedly deep voice with a soft Ruthenian lilt, and, pointing a thick finger at the Reverend Dean, says excitedly that this is a real, genuine priest.

It hadn’t occurred to the priest that an interpreter would be needed; somehow he just hadn’t thought of it. He is flustered and unsure how to extricate himself, for the whole affair, which was meant to be delicate, has suddenly become public and in a moment will draw the entire marketplace in here. He would like nothing better than to walk right out of here, into the cool mist smelling of horse dung. He is beginning to feel cornered in this low-ceilinged room, in the air thick with the smell of roots; to make matters worse, someone from the street is already peering in curiously to see what on earth is going on.

“I would like a word with the esteemed Elisha Shor, if he permits,” he says. “In private.”

The Jews are taken aback. They exchange a few words among themselves; Jeremiah disappears, returning only after a long moment of unbearable silence. Evidently the priest has been granted permission, and now they are leading him behind the shelves. This is accompanied by whispers, the light patter of children’s feet, muffled giggles—as though crowds of other people were gathered just behind the thin walls, peering curiously through cracks in the wooden partitions at the priest, the Dean of Rohatyn, as he wanders through the nooks and crannies of the Jewish house. And it turns out, too, that the little shop on the market square is merely an outpost of a much more extensive structure, a sort of beehive: of rooms, narrow corridors, and little staircases. The entire house is larger than it appeared, built around an inner courtyard that the priest catches sight of only out of the corner of his eye through a small window in a room where they pause for a moment.

“I’m Hryćko,” the young man with the small beard speaks up as they walk. The priest realizes that if he were to retreat, he wouldn’t even know how to find his way out of this beehive of a house. He begins to sweat at the thought, and just then, a door creaks open and a slender man in the prime of life appears in the doorway; he has a pale, smooth, inscrutable face and a gray beard, and he wears a robe falling below his knees, with woolen socks and black slippers on his feet.

“This is the rabbi himself, Elisha Shor,” Hryćko whispers in awe.

The room is small, low-ceilinged, and very modestly furnished. In the center sits a broad table with an open book lying upon it, and several stacks of other books beside it—the priest’s gaze greedily runs over their spines in an attempt to make out the titles. The priest knows little about Jews in general, and those of Rohatyn he knows only by sight.

The priest suddenly finds it rather agreeable that they are both of short stature. He always feels somewhat discomfited around tall men. They stand facing one another, and for a moment the priest has the impression that the other man is equally pleased by this resemblance. The Jew sits down softly, smiles, and gestures toward a bench for the priest.

“By your leave, and under these unusual circumstances, I come before your honor entirely *incognito*, having heard much of your great wisdom and erudition...”

Hryćko stops mid-sentence and asks the priest:

“In-cog-ni-to?”

“Indeed; which is to say, I supplicate for your discretion.”

“And what is that? Sup-pli-cate? Dis-cre-tion?”

The priest falls silent, unpleasantly surprised. What a translator he has ended up with; he can see the man doesn't understand him. So how are they supposed to talk? In Chinese? He will try to speak simply:

“I ask for your discretion, for I do not hide the fact that I am the Dean of Rohatyn, a Catholic priest. But above all, an author.” He emphasizes the word “author” by raising a finger. “And I would prefer to converse here today not as a cleric, but precisely as an author, one who is working diligently on a certain *opusculum*…”

“O-pu-scu-lum?” Hryćko’s doubting voice cuts in.

“…a modest little work.”

“Right. Forgive me, Father, but I’m not schooled in Polish, just in regular language, the way folks talk. I only know what I’ve heard around horses.”

“From horses?” the priest asks in utter bewilderment, annoyed with his shoddy translator.

“Well, ’cause I work with horses. Trading.”

Hryćko gestures with his hands as he speaks. The man watches him with dark, impenetrable eyes, and it occurs to the priest that he might be dealing with a blind person.

“Having already read several hundred authors from cover to cover,” the priest continues, “borrowing here and buying up there, I feel that many books have eluded me and I can gain absolutely no access to them.”

Now he pauses and waits for the man to speak, but Shor merely nods with an ingratiating smile that conveys absolutely nothing.

“And since I have heard that you, good sir, possess quite a fine library here, not wishing for the world to incommode…” he immediately corrects himself, reluctantly, “…to disturb or trouble you, I have gathered the courage, contrary to custom but for the benefit of others, to come here and…”

He falls silent, for the door violently bursts open and, without a word of warning, a woman enters the low-ceilinged room. In her wake, faces half-visible in the gloom peer inside, whispering. A small child whimpers for a moment, then abruptly falls silent, as if everything were meant to center on the woman: bareheaded, her face framed by lush curls, she strides boldly inward, her gaze fixed straight ahead, ignoring the men entirely; she carries a pitcher and dried fruit on a tray. She wears a voluminous floral dress under an embroidered apron. Her pointed shoes click as she walks. She is petite but shapely; her figure draws the eye. Behind her scurries a little girl, carrying two glasses. She looks at the priest with such terror that she inadvertently runs into the woman ahead of her and tumbles over. The glasses roll across the floor—a good thing they are made of thick glass. The woman pays the child no mind, casting a single glance at the priest instead, quick and insolent. Her dark, murky eyes flash, large and somehow fathomless, and a flush instantly covers her startlingly white skin. The Reverend Dean, who rarely has contact with young women, is taken aback by this sudden intrusion; he swallows hard. The woman loudly sets the pitcher and plate down on the table, along with the glasses retrieved from the floor, and, looking straight ahead once more, leaves the room. The door slams shut. Hryćko, the translator, also looks flustered. Elisha Shor leaps up in the meantime, scoops up the child, and sits her on his lap, but the little girl breaks free and vanishes after her mother.

The priest would swear on his life that this entire entrance by the woman and child was staged for no other reason than to get a good look at him. The very idea! A priest in a Jewish household! As exotic as a salamander. But what of it? Am I not treated by a Jewish doctor? And is it not a Jew who grinds my medicines? And the matter of books is also, in a way, a matter of hygiene.

"The books," says the priest, pointing at the spines of the folios and Elzevirs lying on the table. Inscribed on each of them in gold paint are two characters that the priest takes to be the owner’s initials; he can recognize Hebrew letters:

He reaches for his ticket for this journey to the people of Israel and carefully sets the book he has brought down in front of Shor. He smiles triumphantly, for it is Athanasius Kircher’s *Turris Babel*, a great work in both content and format, and the priest risked a great deal lugging it here. What if it had fallen into this stinking Rohatyn mud? What if some cutpurse at the market had snatched it from him? Without it, the Dean would not be the man he is, but would instead become some narrow-minded parish priest, a Jesuit tutor on a nobleman's estate, a vain Church official, heavy with rings and resentful of the world.

He slides the book closer to Shor, as if introducing his own wife. He now taps delicately on the wooden covers.

"I have more. But Kircher is the best." He opens it at random, and they gaze at a drawing of the Earth, depicted as a sphere, with the long, slender cone of the Tower of Babel rising from it.

“Kircher proves that the Tower of Babel, as described in the Bible, could not have been as tall as it is depicted. A tower reaching all the way to the sphere of the Moon would disrupt the entire cosmic order. Its base, resting on the earthly globe, would have to be enormous. It would block out the sun, which would have catastrophic consequences for all creation. Mankind would have to use up all the wood and clay on Earth...”

The priest feels as though he were uttering heresy, and does not really know why he is saying this to the silent Jew. He wants the man to consider him a friend, not an enemy. But will that be possible? Perhaps it is possible to reach an understanding even when people do not know one another's languages or customs, nor each other, nor their belongings and objects, nor their smiles, nor the gestures of their hands making signs, nothing; perhaps, then, they can communicate by means of books? Is this not, in fact, the only possible way? If people read the same books, they would inhabit the same world, whereas they live in different ones, like those Chinese of whom Kircher wrote. And then there are those, a great multitude, who do not read at all; their minds are dormant, their thoughts simple, animalistic, like those empty-eyed peasants. If he, the priest, were king, he would decree that one day of corvée labor be devoted to reading, he would drive the entire peasantry to books, and the Commonwealth would instantly look entirely different. Perhaps it is even a question of the alphabet—that there is not just one, but many, and each constructs thoughts differently. Alphabets are like bricks: out of some, fired and smooth, cathedrals are built; out of others, earthen and rough, ordinary houses. And although the Latin alphabet is surely the most perfect, it seems that Shor, after all, does not know Latin. So he points to an engraving, and then another, and another, and sees the man lean over them with growing interest, until finally he produces from somewhere a pair of spectacles neatly framed in metal wire—Father Chmielowski would like a pair like that himself; he must ask him where to order them. The translator, too, proves to be interested, and so the three of them lean over the engraving.

The priest glances at them, pleased to have hooked them both, and notices gold and chestnut hairs in the Jew’s dark beard.

“We could exchange books,” he proposes.

He says that in his library in Firlejów he has two more works by the great Kircher, *Arca Noe* and *Mundus subterraneus*, kept under lock and key, too precious to be consulted every day. He also knows there are other titles, but is familiar with them only from mentions here and there. He has collected many other thinkers of the bygone world as well, including—he adds, to ingratiate himself—the Jewish historiographer, Josephus.

They pour him some compote from a pitcher and slide over a small plate of dried figs and dates. The priest places them in his mouth with reverence—it has been a long time since he last ate any—and the unearthly sweetness instantly improves his mood. He realizes it is high time to state his business, so he swallows the sweets and gets down to it; before he even finishes, however, he knows he has spoken too soon and will accomplish little here.

Perhaps he can tell by the sudden change in Hryćko’s demeanor. He would bet his life, too, that the boy is adding something of his own to the translation. He just doesn’t know whether these are warnings or, on the contrary, something meant to help the priest. Elisha Shor shifts back slightly in his chair, tilts his head back, and closes his eyes, as if withdrawing to consult his own inner darkness.

This continues until—against his will—the priest exchanges a knowing glance with the young translator.

“The rabbi is listening to the voices of the elders,” the translator whispers, and the priest nods as if in understanding, though he does not understand. Perhaps this Jew really does have some magical connection with various little devils; there is no shortage of them among the Jews, all those lamias and liliths. Shor’s hesitation and his closed eyes convince the priest that it would have been better had he not come here at all. The situation is delicate and highly unusual. He only hopes he won't expose himself to infamy.

Shor stands up and turns toward the wall, lowers his head, and stands there for a moment. The priest begins to grow impatient—is this a sign that he should leave? Hryćko has also closed his eyes, and his long, boyish eyelashes now cast a shadow over cheeks covered in a soft down. Could they have fallen asleep? The priest clears his throat softly; their silence has robbed him of the last remnants of his self-assurance. He already regrets coming here.

Suddenly Shor, as if nothing had happened, steps toward the cabinets and opens one of them. Reverently, he extracts a thick folio, marked with the same symbols as all the other books, and places it on the table before the priest. He opens the book from the back and turns a page. The priest sees a beautifully crafted title page...

“Sefer ha-Zohar,” he says devoutly, and puts the book back in the cabinet.

“Who would even read it to you, Father...” Hryćko offers consolingly.

The priest leaves two volumes of his *New Athens* on Shor’s table as an incentive for a future exchange. He taps them with his index finger, then points to himself, aiming right at the center of his chest: *I wrote this.* They ought to read it, if only they knew the language. They would learn a great deal about the world. He waits for Shor’s reaction, but the man merely raises his eyebrows slightly.

Father Chmielowski and Hryćko step out together into the cold, unpleasant air. Hryćko chatters on about something or other, while the priest studies him intently—his young face with the beginnings of a beard, his long, curled eyelashes that give him a rather childish appearance, and finally, his peasant garb.

“Are you a Jew?”

“Eh, no...” Hryćko replies with a shrug. “I'm from here, from Rohatyn, right from that house over there. Supposedly Orthodox.”

“Then how do you know their language?”

Hryćko draws closer and walks almost shoulder to shoulder with the priest, clearly encouraged to this familiarity. He says that his father and mother died of the plague in 1746. They did business with the Shors; his father was a craftsman, he tanned hides, and when he died, Shor stepped in on behalf of Hryćko, his grandmother, and his younger brother, Ołeś, paying off the father's debts and taking the three neighbors under his wing. And so they live as neighbors. He has more to do with Jews nowadays than with his own people, and somehow he doesn't even know himself when he picked up their language, but he speaks it as fluently as his own, which often comes in handy in various matters and business dealings, because the Jews, especially the older ones, are reluctant to use Polish or Ruthenian. The Jews aren't the way people say they are, the Shors in particular. There are many of them, and theirs is a warm, hospitable home; they will always give you something to eat, and a glass of vodka when it's cold. Now Hryćko is learning his father's craft to take over the tanning trade; there will always be a demand for that.

"And do you not have any Christian family?"

"Well, I do, but they live far away and don't care much about us. Oh, and this is my brother, Ołeś." A little boy of perhaps eight, completely covered in freckles, runs up to them. "You needn't fret over us so needlessly, Father Benefactor," Hryćko says cheerfully. "God created man with eyes in the front of his head, not the back, which means a man is supposed to concern himself with what will be, not what was."

The priest does indeed consider this proof of God's ingenuity, although he cannot recall where in Scripture it was said.

"Learn the language with them, and you can translate these books."

"Oh, not at all, sir, I am not drawn to books. Reading bores me. I would rather take up trade, that's what suits me. Horses, ideally. Or, like the Shors, vodka and beer."

"Oh, you've already been corrupted among them..." the priest says.

"Why, are they worse than other goods? People need to drink, because life is hard."
