Chapter 1

It was just as I say. He tripped over his cane. I don't know why he looked back. No one was coming down behind him. Of course I recognized him. Even though he had changed beyond recognition, it’s hard not to change after so many years. I didn't let it show, however, that I recognized him. Maybe that's why he thought I didn't recognize him. I pretended he was just one of those people constantly going down or up these stairs. I even wondered whether to warn him not to go down into that old wild, green valley, because it is no longer there. Old people, however, like to visit places that no longer exist and it's uncertain if they ever did. I do not understand what kind of longing draws them so. Unfortunately, the longings of the old can be dangerous. If only one could reach that valley on level ground, perhaps I would have understood him. Except you cannot reach any valley on level ground; such is the nature of valleys. To descend these stairs at his age and with a cane, when your legs refuse to obey and your eyes fail to focus not only on the world, but on every step you place in front of you, is like imagining in your youth that instead of arms you have wings, and fluttering down into that valley.

It was not him I was expecting, and yet, unexpectedly, it was him. A trembling came over me; I shrank into myself, wishing at that moment I were neither here nor anywhere else. Having felt for the first step with his cane, he hesitantly placed his foot upon it, and then ventured to set down the other. With similar toil, he stepped down to the next, and the next. Feeble yet stubborn, I thought. Why are old people so stubborn? Could it be they cannot forgive those who will live on after them?

He paused for a shorter or longer while on every step, looking down at his feet, as if unsure whether these were the stairs he had always gone down and up, or if perhaps his memory was failing him. At his age, it happens that memory excuses itself from remembering, since it burdens a person with duties they are already tired enough of, after living so many years. He probed each step below with his cane, making sure he could safely descend. First he placed his left foot on that step, then his right. And with the same caution, lowering his right foot to join his left, he descended to the next step. The cane he held in his right hand seemed unsteady; it shook when he touched the step with it.

I was afraid he would get dizzy, looking down at his feet on every single step. I wondered whether to step out of his way, or rather block his way when he reached the Needle's Eye.

He was not surprised when he saw me; perhaps he even expected me to beat him here, before he made his way from the park through town. He paused on the step above me and, without looking me in the eye, said:

"I stopped by the park once more, but for the very last time. I don't know if you remember that she sometimes walked home from school through the park. I sat down on a bench and decided to wait. A Gypsy woman walked by with a baby at her breast. Do you think she stopped like she did back then? Not at all. She didn't even ask if I wanted my fortune told, even though the baby whimpered as she passed by me. Evidently, she decided that my future was behind me, not ahead of me, and she couldn't add years to my life, for how could she. I had been sitting there a long time, I was about to get up and walk through town, because maybe she too would be walking back through town today, if she wanted to buy something, but some old woman sat down next to me, asking if she could."

"Unless you're waiting for someone?"

"No, please," I say. "I waited once, but that was a long time ago."

"Long ago or not, if you loved someone, you keep waiting."

"And did you love someone?"

"What a question. Is there anyone who never loved? You've lived quite a few years, I see, same as me, and you don't know that? I love him to this day, though surely in vain now. It grieves me that I never told him."

"And why didn't you tell him?"

"I was sick, and the doctors didn't give me long to live, so I didn't want to burden him with my love. Especially since he moved away after passing his exams. I didn't even know where, and now he might not even be alive. Would you have told her, if you weren't sure you'd live?"

"I don't know, especially after so many years. Perhaps I wouldn't want to burden her with the guilt of abandoning my love, either, if she were the one to die."

"Does that mean you loved someone?"

"I love her to this day, though I'm also not sure if she's alive. But even if she were, I probably wouldn't tell her now if we met, because she wouldn't recognize me, and I wouldn't recognize her. We are no longer the people we were in our youth."

"If you love someone, you are always the same. If a person only knew that in their youth, they would live differently. As it is, you waste many a thing. And most often, love. Well, I'll be going now. I must feed the cats."

"Do you have many?"

"It varies. However many happen to show up."

"Let me walk you."

"No, thank you. I don't have far. And where are you heading? Because perhaps I should walk you? You're using a cane, and I, thank God, am still on my own two feet."

"I'll manage. I have much closer to go than you."

He checked the next step with his cane, but as if he didn't trust the cane, he slid its tip left and right across the step before setting his foot down. I wanted to ask him why, in that case, he was walking down into that old wild, green valley, but he beat me to it:

"I feel sorry for you, that you have to go on living. But perhaps you'll have better luck. I wish it for you. Though, in my opinion, wishes have never done anyone any good." He stepped down onto the next stair, testing it in the same way with his cane. "Truth be told, every life is a repetition of someone else's life. The past runs ahead of us, you must know that, and we trail behind it. After all, who could ever keep up with their own life." He paused on the next stair. "Oh, how many feet must have passed this way. Have you noticed how worn these stairs are? That takes centuries. Who knows if they aren't still walking this way. Maybe I'll tag along with them. What, does that surprise you? There's always comfort in a crowd." And probing the step below with his cane, he staggered, but as if someone had steadied him. "When I was in high school... But why am I telling you all this, you'll experience it yourself, because you have to. Without that, you wouldn't be able to ask yourself if it was worth it. Every question we ask ourselves in life boils down to that single one, was it worth it. Well, not at your age. Only years later, years later. Right now, you wouldn't know how to answer. Just remember that youth can lead you on for a long time. Don't let it fool you. Suddenly, it might turn out that you were young many years ago. Alas. Forgive these bitter words, but they're the only kind I have left. In exchange, I give you my life. It will come in handy when emptiness overwhelms you. Emptiness is the worst thing that can happen to us. And the only cure for it is to live in someone else's life. Besides, no one begins their own life with themselves. You don't understand that yet. But in time, when you start to visit it... I'd be curious to know what your impressions will be. I had good ones and bad ones, as is the way with life. I once ran away from my childhood, but perhaps you'll never want to leave it. It happens. But I'll tell you, it won't do you any good. Life is wandering after oneself with no hope of ever finding oneself. Well, enough, or they'll pass me by. And there are still so many steps here."

So I moved out of his way. And then the cane slipped from his hand and rolled down, and he staggered and plunged after it. He fell all the way down to the road at the bottom of the stairs, striking his head on the cobblestones. Just then, no one was going down or coming up, so no one saw. I rushed after him. I tried to lift him in some irrational hope that he might still be alive. He was heavy, though. Tall in life, and in death completely limp, so all the heavier. Just then, I heard a voice above me:

"Leave him. The ambulance is on its way."

So I laid his bloodied head back down on the cobblestones, and only then did I notice a few people standing nearby. Where they had suddenly sprung from, I don't know. Maybe it's like this with every accident, that people seem to sprout from the earth or drop from the sky, but when he was falling down those stairs, I could have sworn there wasn't a living soul around. So there is no use listening to people, because no one saw him trip over the cane. They can say all sorts of things, and everyone will tell a different story anyway, as always happens when no one wants to be a witness. And, as it later turned out, no one did. The man who had told me to leave him be was just then recounting a similar incident, pointing at a house not far away:

"Over in that house. Two buddies, unfamiliar with guns, found a revolver from the war. And since it was rusty and the trigger wouldn't even pull, one of them started banging the barrel on the table. Well, it went off right into the other guy's belly. He dropped, and the first guy started to lift him up, I think he even stood him upright. The doctor said later that if he hadn't lifted him, he would have lived."

At that moment, his words were cut short by an ambulance pulling up with its siren blaring. Two paramedics jumped out with a stretcher, along with a doctor, who pulled open his eyelids, pressed a stethoscope to his chest just in case, and pronounced him dead. Right after the ambulance, the militia arrived, also with its siren blaring. Two uniformed men got out of the car: one an officer, with stars, the other younger, with bars. After them, a third man hauled himself out of the back seat, so fat he looked almost comical in his uniform.

The officer began questioning the people standing around the body about what they had seen and how it had happened, and the younger one, the one with the bars, took notes—though he had nothing to write down, because even as they talked over one another, no one had seen a thing. Someone had only seen me running down the stairs when the body was already lying on the cobblestones. And the man who had warned me not to lift him had walked up right as I was about to lift him, and said:

"Leave him." And he was already starting to say that, see, in that house over there... but the officer cut him off:

"Did anyone know him?"

Someone began to muse that maybe if they'd seen him alive... at which the officer waved his hand dismissively.

"Search his pockets," he ordered.

The fat one tried to bend over the body, but couldn't manage it; so he knelt down, pulled a wallet from the jacket's inside pocket, and handed it to the younger one, who passed it to the officer without looking inside. The wallet had many compartments, but apart from some money and a photograph of a young girl, there was nothing in it—not even a receipt, say, from the laundry.

"No ID?" the officer was astonished. "Search him again, thoroughly, maybe it's somewhere. — None?" he was even more astonished.

"A citizen without an ID is as if he didn't exist," said the fat one.

"The body is here, so the ID should be too," said the younger one with the bars. "Without an ID, it's as if this body didn't exist either. And how are we to conduct an investigation?"

"Maybe it fell out and is under the body. Lift the body," the officer ordered.

The paramedics lifted the body, but beneath it was only a large pool of blood.

"Put him back down. Just as he was lying."

"What about the girl in the picture?" The younger one peered into the wallet held open in the officer's hands. "Pretty girl."

The officer nodded.

"Maybe a daughter, someone would be more likely to recognize her. Show it?" He took the photo from the officer's hands and held it up to everyone's eyes.

They shrugged, meaning they didn't recognize her. Someone merely said:

"Must be an old one. Crimped edges. They don't make them like that anymore. I have similar ones from my grandparents."

"Well, and you," he turned to me, "do you recognize her?"

Yes, it was her; I recognized her. I didn't admit it, though. And so that no shadow of suspicion would fall on me, I drew the officer's attention to the cane:

"Maybe someone would recognize him by his cane. Canes become attached to people. And almost everyone carries a different one. A teacher of mine in high school..."

The officer didn't let me finish. He looked at me suspiciously, fixing me with a hard stare.

"We know what to identify someone by. Please leave that to us." And he ordered: "Just in case, look for that cane, and put the money in deposit. And outline the body. Who knows, maybe he's some tourist, he could have left his ID with a guide, in case he got lost."

They outlined the body with chalk, the ambulance drove off with the body, and the younger one with the fat one started searching for the cane. That outline of his body on the cobblestones drawn in white chalk, with the bloodstain where he hit his head, seemed to me to still be breathing with his breath, as if even whispering something in his words, and it was more haunting than when the body lay in that spot, before the paramedics took it away.

"We’ll have to drive around town and question wherever there's a tour bus parked," the officer commanded, when, despite searching the stairs and the surrounding area, they failed to find the cane. "It's Saturday, so a lot of them drove in today. They park below the castle, they park by the granary, they park in the new town—wherever they park, question them. Fortunately, there's only one hotel, question them there too. And question at the private lodgings, get the list from the town hall, they have to register them, after all." He took off his cap and scratched his head, as if his thoughts were making it itch. "It wouldn't hurt to put up some notices, saying a man aged... only we don't know his age, since we didn't find his ID. Damn it, we didn't even bring a camera, we could have taken his picture."

"How were we supposed to know he got himself killed?" interrupted the fat one. "They reported an accident, and an accident means he could've broken an arm or a leg. God knows enough arms and legs have been broken on these damn stairs."

"We could put up a photo," chimed in the one with the bars. "Just not here, rather in town, because they'll be afraid to walk this way later, thinking it's haunted. Though with a photo of a corpse, who’s going to admit they knew him. It'd be better to use that daughter's. Someone would more gladly recognize the daughter."

"And how do you know she's a daughter, and not a granddaughter?" the officer scolded him. And the other man just blinked, taken aback that he hadn't thought of that himself.

"It seems we have our only witness in you," the officer turned to me. And as if hesitating, he added: "And maybe the only point from which the investigation should be led, since there is neither the deceased's ID, nor a photograph, nor other witnesses. You'll ride with us, we'll take down a statement."

At the station, he sat me down at a desk across from him, asked if I'd like some tea, and picked up the telephone receiver:

"Two teas," he ordered. "Do you take sugar? Then I'll drink mine sweetened today, too. And sugar. Oh, and have Bronka come in to type."

A little woman, skinny as a rail, who turned out to be the cleaning lady, brought in two glasses of tea, sugar in some old tin hard-candy box, and a single teaspoon. He sweetened his tea, stirred it, and then passed the spoon to me.

"I suppose I'll have to bring a teaspoon from home. These aluminum ones keep snapping, and there aren't many left in the whole station," he said, as if to apologize for the single spoon.

Bronka arrived as well, shot me a disdainful look, sat down at the typewriter, and spread a colorful magazine out in front of her.

"Before you finish your tea, I'll see what the autumn fashion is."

"Type. What does tea have to do with an interrogation?"

"I don't know about that."

He waited until I took at least my first sip—he had probably taken his third by then—before pushing his chair back from the desk and leaning against the backrest. He laced his fingers together, clearly relaxing, and must have stretched his legs out under the desk, too, because he brushed against me.

"Well then, speak."

"What should I say?"

"What you know."

"I don't know anything else, apart from what I've already told you."

"You know, you know, we already know that you know. Everyone questioned says that, they give their first and last name, date of birth, and that they know nothing else. But experience teaches us that a man himself doesn't know what he knows, unless he is helped. There are various forms of such help. But I use an ordinary conversation, an enlightening one, so to speak. I proceed from the assumption that everyone must convince themselves of what they know. So how about it?"

Evidently I still hadn't understood anything, because I decided to remind him of what he had told me when taking me to the station:

"Excuse me, but this is some misunderstanding. I was only supposed to be a witness."

"That's true, I don't deny it, you are still a witness. Except, the only one. We have no other witnesses we could confront you with to confirm your credibility. And when nothing is certain, the question arises whether being a witness is your only role in this event, or if another might be attributed to you. I don't know what kind yet, but we must try to clear that up. Both of us. So I would like you to understand that I must interrogate you in various roles. The boundaries between roles are fluid, anyway. They are determined by circumstances. To find a comparison, a liquid state can transition into a gaseous state, and a solid one, depending on the temperature. Water, steam, ice. The same, yet not the same. We cannot afford to be helpless. That would disqualify us. We would be unnecessary. So we must probe, sometimes until it hurts. The truth, as you know, has the highest price and even after years is not subject to a discount. So I wouldn't advise counting on it. Truth is not subject to a statute of limitations. Regardless of how long the case might drag on. If we only had at least a second witness. But in your case, that could only be the deceased. Unfortunately, we cannot interrogate him. Though it's interesting what he would have to say. We can only guess. And it's not so unfounded. From guessing usually begins the search for a trail, since hardly anyone can be caught red-handed. Take into consideration, for example, all those betrayals of husbands, wives. Rarely does one catch the other, as they say, *in flagranti*. Most betrayals begin with guesswork. And only guesswork compels one to look for evidence. So it is not ruled out that the deceased would testify against you, just as you testify that he is to blame himself because he tripped over his cane. Only where is this cane? We searched, you saw. If it were there, we would have found it. A cane has no wings to fly away." He laughed, but it was the kind of laugh that chilled me to the bone. "Fine, we'll search again. But meanwhile, tell me, which hand was he holding it in, in the left, the right, when, as you say, he tripped over it? You don't remember. Oh well. Does it surprise you that such a detail can matter? Then I will tell you that a detail can tell us more than we would suspect. Any mere detail can overthrow all testimonies, contradict all witnesses. A detail is a cosmos. The world is supposedly great, but details decide that someone met someone here, not there, that an apple fell from an apple tree, thanks to which Newton discovered the law of gravity, that bacteria grown by Fleming were unexpectedly killed by some fungus and in this way penicillin was invented. And so on, and so on. Did you write that down, Bronka? Then throw that page away."

He dragged out this interrogation and I must say that sometimes he surprised me with his astuteness:

"You said that no one was walking down behind him, right? And despite that, he looked back. Maybe the sun struck him so, because it's harsh today, and seeing nothing, he turned his head and you then, let's say, wanted to help him step down to a lower step?"

Sometimes he would break off the interrogation mid-sentence and walk out, as if giving me time to think. Or he would fall into thought, after which he told Bronka to cross something out, although it seemed important to me. Evidently, his thoughts were following a different track. And sometimes it was as if he wanted to soften this interrogation and give it a tone closer to confidences, as he threw in something about himself, that he has two children, a son and a daughter, by his first wife, who died on him, and he had to raise them himself.

"And you know how it is. Duty is duty."

He married a second time and now they are expecting a baby again. Sometimes he asked me questions that seemingly had nothing to do with the case:

"Father, mother alive? Even though you're young, things happen. I barely remember my mother, she died of tuberculosis. And my father passed away two years ago."

Then suddenly he caught me off guard again:

"When did you pass your matriculation exams? And why didn't you go to university?"

"They didn't accept me."

"What, you didn't pass the exam?"

"I passed."

"How is that? You passed and they didn't accept you? That should be investigated, too. Interesting. And what do you do now?"

"I work."

"Where?"

"At the fruit and vegetable processing plant. Jams, marmalades, juices."

"We know that plant. We conducted an investigation there, five railcars of cherries stood unloaded in barrels on the siding until they rotted. And where do you live?"

"With my parents."

"And why did you come here? Did you arrange to meet him?"

"With whom?"

"Well, with the deceased."

"I didn't know him."

"You didn't know him, and yet you recognized him. That's what you said, that you recognized him. Or maybe he just thought you didn't recognize him. Well, we cannot ask him. It makes me wonder, however, that since it was a chance encounter, why exactly did you meet in the Needle's Eye, and not on the street, in the park, in a café, a restaurant, wherever, as happens in chance encounters? You think about it. I'll be right back." And he went out.

Oh, it won't be easy with him, I thought. And I couldn't very well tell him that I was waiting for her, and he showed up unexpectedly. I even thought he was already dead, since a year had passed since he was supposed to be dead according to the Gypsy woman's prophecy. He would immediately ask me, and who is she, first name, last name, where does she live? And what, did she come? What do you mean, you knew she wouldn't come, and you waited for her? You wait for someone who might be late or even not come that day because something unforeseen delayed them, but who will come another time.

He returned a short while later and fired an impatient question at me right from the doorway:

"And do you have something to tell me? Let's assume, however, that you didn't know him. We will go down a different track. You saw that he was going down, right? Where was he going down?"

"Down."

"It's obvious that one goes down, not up." He sat behind the desk, staring with some strange intensity into my eyes. "Down, you say? And how would he come back up, since he could barely go down, didn't you wonder about that? At his age and with a cane."

"I don't know. Maybe he had no intention of coming back up."

"He had no intention, you say. And how do you know he had no intention?"

"I assume."

"Then I must state to you that your assumptions can also be evidence. And we can use them against you, regardless of the fact that you assume in favor of your innocence. Every thing has two sides, every thought and every word. Nothing is unambiguous, as it surely seems to you. Unambiguity is an illusion. The fact that you merely assume betrays you, that something is hiding behind it. And that is the most important thing, what is hiding behind something. Admittedly, you covered yourself with that 'maybe'. But sometimes one throws out a 'maybe' like that, though one does not throw it out without reason either. Words rule us, you should know that. You don't need a matriculation exam for that. Sometimes you wouldn't want to say a word, and it will say itself. So, how about it, because something doesn't hold together here." He fell silent as if in expectation of my answer.

It unsettled me that he was silent for a long time, staring at me and silent. In those eyes staring at me, it seemed to me, I saw a question he would like to ask me, but he didn't know if he might entangle himself in something. I was afraid of this question and waited in suspense for him to ask it. He didn't ask. He only cleared his throat:

"Hmm..." And that was the only sound that came out of his mouth for a long time. After a moment, he cleared his throat again: "Hmm..."

At that "hmm" I cringed inwardly. In such an "hmm" there can be hidden who knows what, even an accusation, for which there is admittedly no evidence, but there echoes a conviction that it will be found, if not sooner, then later it will be found. Later it might even be easier to find. Investigative techniques will improve, research methods will be perfected, new apparatus will appear that will read by itself, without asking questions. And without Bronka, because nothing will need to be written, the suspect will be displayed on a screen. If he has a soul, even his soul will be visible as on the palm of a hand. Patience, what recedes will also approach. And in the meantime, it doesn't hurt to dream. Over that time the suspect will also age, he will experience a thing or two, understand a thing or two, maybe one day he will even come of his own free will and confess, there will be no need to detain him, travel anywhere, track anything. One will sit behind a desk and receive the guilty as when submitting applications for welfare or housing. If only one could get to their consciences, because so far consciences rarely open up on their own, and if they do, it's only in old age. Maybe instead of the militia there will only be offices, bureaus, departments, then salaries will rise too. The militia is having a hard time, especially with the young. The young are the most difficult material for interrogations—they twist, they tangle, they deny, codes and paragraphs mean nothing to them, it seems to them that youth allows everything. They hold the militia in contempt. Well, they will find out, when we become the signpost of the world.

In that moment, an officer one star senior came in and placed a note on his desk.

"You've got him in the palm of your hand yet?" he asked, looking at me.

"Almost."

"What, so tough? Maybe toss him to Edek."

"No, I'll manage."

"Suit yourself."

After he left, he glanced at the note the other man had brought him, laid it aside with suppressed fury and with this fury he threw himself at Bronka:

"Leave that magazine alone, Bronka! Type!"

"I am typing all the time," Bronka took offense. "Only I have nothing to type."

He bored into me with his eyes equally furiously, as if expecting my hands at least to tremble.

"Let's return to this cane."

"Let's return to this cane, I wrote," Bronka repeated.

"What did this cane look like?"

"What did this cane look like?" Bronka again.

"Stop reading to me what you're writing, damn it!" Oh, he was clearly furious. "Well, so what did this cane look like?"

"Ordinary, like a cane."

"Exactly. What color was it?"

"I think black."

"You think, or black?"

"It could be black."

"It could be is not an answer. It seems you do not understand that you are being interrogated in connection with, who knows, perhaps even a murder. You could just as equally have tripped him, as he could have tripped over his cane. Because so far we haven't found the cane."

"Black," I said, slightly terrified. I felt as if he was slowly beginning to get into my thoughts.

"Well, so we have the color. And further down?"

"Further down also black."

"You are mocking me, yes? And I want to give you a chance."

No, I wasn't mocking, I wouldn't dare. Maybe I said it stupidly, but it was from a sudden confusion in my head, that he had started addressing me as "you" and what hid behind that.

"And the handle?"

"What about the handle?"

"Well, what shape was it? They vary. We had one guy here, and young, like you, walking with a cane, and he had a handle in the shape of a snake's head."

"No, semicircular, smooth."

"And what else did it have?"

"It also had a non-slip tip at the end."

"That means he couldn't have slipped, because it held him. And he fell?"

"Maybe because it held him, and he wanted to step lower. And he tripped."

He looked at me, as if he thought, you're clever, brother, but you won't lead me astray.

"Alright. Let's leave this cane, until we find it."

He began drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desk, as if considering whether to toss me to that Edek after all, as proposed by the officer one star his senior who brought him the note. I didn't know who this Edek was, a name like any other, but to be tossed to some Edek, that alone seemed menacing to me. Until now gentle, sometimes friendly, I even had the impression it pained him that he had to interrogate me. And fear came over me. He supposedly hadn't agreed to this Edek, but maybe an Edek sat inside him too, since he said he would manage.

"You probably know that a confession of guilt is an extenuating circumstance," he said as if with weariness in his voice. And I think he even yawned.

"But what am I to confess to?"

"That's not my business, it's not I who have to confess. And truth be told, everyone would have something to confess to. Even if we pulled the first better people off the street for interrogations. And maybe one day it will be so. For now we have a limited scope of action."

He stopped drumming the fingers of his right hand on the desk top, switched to his left hand and as if with a surge of energy drummed louder and faster, and my fear rose right up to my throat.

"Ah, I forgot to say, that on this cane, below the handle there was a gold plaque. And on it an engraved inscription..."

"Gold, you say," he interrupted me. He picked up the telephone receiver. "The fat one to my office. Well, you see. You have to dig until it hurts." He stopped drumming his fingers, he even seemed to smile at me. "That would solve our case."

The fat one stood in the doorway.

"What is it, boss?"

"Go look for this cane. There's a gold plaque on it."

"If it's gold, there's no point looking, you won't find it now." And he began to spin his doubts: "Gold is pricey now. The trade is forbidden, so it flourishes. How many gold rubles, dollars have we confiscated already, and they still sell. How many have we locked up and what? They even steal in churches and melt it down into rubles and dollars."

"Don't bullshit me. Go. Order."

"Yes, sir." The fat one clicked his heels, so hard he swayed. Evidently his body was too heavy for his slender legs.

"I don't smoke, or I'd gladly have a smoke," he said as if slightly deflated. He took out a comb, combed his hair. He had thick, black hair, although already graying at the temples. "I used to smoke. But the first wife, quit, because the kids are small, the second, quit, I'm pregnant, do you want the child to be born a cripple? Well, we will wait until they bring this cane. For getting to the truth one must not spare time."

And time dragged on endlessly, not only for me, for him too, because he went out once and again, leaving me alone with Bronka, who, taking advantage of the opportunity, with each of his exits leafed through this women's magazine, and didn't speak a single word. I asked her if she didn't know how long this could take.

"It could, it could," she said, not taking her eyes off the magazine. "If they don't finish by night, they'll start again in the morning. I've only typed one page. With Edek I'd have about five by now. Oh, Edek doesn't mess around. He'll smack you, and he'll choke you, if someone isn't telling the truth. And nobody does. You are also twisting things, as I listen to you. And everything you say is somehow not right. Edek would squeeze it out of you. Edek even bends horseshoes. He lifts me up like a feather right to the ceiling. A person doesn't get bored with Edek, when he interrogates. Next to Edek almost everyone here is a runt. Everyone spills everything to Edek. You get to know life. You won't read in a book what you'll find out around Edek. Nobody tells as much in confession as to Edek. If Edek gave you a working over, you too would find out a lot about yourself. Edek wouldn't let you sit the whole time, like this one. Although a good man and not stupid, but sometimes he himself doesn't know what he wants. He asks you and asks you, and there's nothing to type. And he treated you to tea. Edek wouldn't give you a drop of water. And he called you 'sir', until you made him nervous. With Edek the interrogation would be over by now, I could go home. But with this one, who knows. Told them to go, look for a cane, and what will a cane tell him. It's starting to get dark. If you confessed, maybe he'd get promoted, because he's been waiting a long time for them to add a star to his rank. He'd have a bigger salary. Two kids from that wife to raise, and a third on the way with this one. And it would be lighter for you too, if you threw it off yourself."

Only what was he supposed to confess to, to make it lighter for him? It was never lighter for him again, since he let the Gypsy woman tell his fortune, and she foretold him a long life, but that didn't concern him at all, whereas when he heard that she wouldn't be returning from school home through the park today, she'd go through town, because she had to buy something, hooks or snaps, he nearly fluttered off the bench.

The park was usually empty at this time, besides it was small, as such downtown parks tend to be, and on top of that heavily thinned out by an almost apocalyptic storm that had crashed down on the town a few years ago, which was still recalled with horror. So it didn't have such popularity as it once did. You couldn't hide away in it from human eyes, so hardly anyone arranged to meet there now. The young preferred to go to the grove by the river, and the old were deterred from walking and sitting on benches by sorrow for the old park. Once the trees at this time of year resounded with bird voices, now the silence was rarely disturbed by anything, so he was certain he would hear her footsteps when she approached.

He half-closed his eyes against the glaring sun, because in this silence no one could escape him. He heard all footsteps from afar and didn't have to open his eyes every time to see whether it was her or not her. Besides, he knew her footsteps by heart, as if they only lightly touched the earth, one might think she was walking in sandals, although she always walked in heels, she couldn't stand flat shoes. She placed her feet from the toes, not from the heels, he would have recognized those footsteps even in starless darkness.

So he gave himself over to imagination beneath those half-closed eyelids, especially since he didn't expect her until the last class ended. He had an advantage of this one class over her, for he had skipped Latin to already be waiting for her in the park when she returned, and she mostly returned through the park. More than once he had skipped, not only Latin, which he disliked, but also physics, mathematics, subjects in which he was among the best students. When he left like her after the last bell, it happened that she would get lost to him among several classes leaving all at once. There were several parallel classes, and each large, so that when classes ended, a crowd poured outside. And when he found her, she was almost always walking with someone already. Oh, there were many, also from older classes, who lay in wait to walk with her for at least this stretch or walk her all the way home.

What he could be dreaming of is probably not worth recalling, since dreams at that age are quite common and universally known, so anyone can reach for their own. Suddenly he heard approaching footsteps, or rather a rustle like bare feet. No, those were not her footsteps, he would have recognized them at once. These were unhurried, creeping rather, so he would not have left his dreams, if nearby almost at arm's length a child had not suddenly whimpered. So he violently opened his eyelids, so much that the sun's glare blinded him, and in this glare he saw before him a Gypsy woman with an infant at her breast.

She was directly in front of the bench he was sitting on, and evidently would have passed by, since from her footsteps one could not read the intention to stop by him. Evidently experience told her that there was no point telling the fortune of such a one, she wouldn't squeeze even a zloty out of him. If he has any money, he keeps it for the cinema, for the circus. Only what girl would let him invite her to the cinema or circus? The prettier ones look around for those who have a future. And what future could he have? Even if he lived long, does a long life guarantee a future? Pale, emaciated, as if caved in on himself, maybe even sick, only he doesn't know about it yet. She could only foretell him that he would live long, because that can't be checked otherwise than by living long.

So maybe it was not a coincidence that when she drew level with him and was about to take that step further, her infant whimpered, because the nipple slipped out of its mouth, she pressed that nipple back in and stifled the cry. But she had to stop for a moment and then her eyes and his met. Briefly, because his gaze froze her and she lowered her eyes to the head of the suckling infant, and even stroked it tenderly. She was skilled in taming human mistrust or even contempt towards herself, but his eyes seemed to her not for his young years. A sad glow radiated from them. She had not yet met so much suffering in young eyes, one often doesn't see that much in old ones. Best proof, that he only opened them when he heard her infant's cry. Could he have inherited this suffering from someone? After all, everyone is an endless inheritance. Since she had stopped by him, however, she couldn't just walk away without a word. Her infant's cry was already this first word to him. So as if continuing this cry, she said:

"Let me tell your fortune, and I'll tell you if she will be returning this way."

And without waiting for his consent, she sat down on the bench. With a practiced motion she pulled out her right breast from under her blouse and shifted the infant to it, which torn from suckling whimpered again, but didn't have time to burst into tears, since it already had the second nipple in its mouth.

"When something hurts, it's best to let your fortune be told. There hasn't been anyone so wise who wouldn't believe that it will be better. Fortunes can comfort, and they do not take away anyone's hope. They can also change many things. And he has his whole life ahead of him. If he would give for a rattle, I would foretell his whole life."

And without waiting for his consent or refusal, she freed her left arm from under the shawl, which tied at her neck and waist supported the little one carried at her breast.

"Give me your hand," she said. "Not this one, that one."

And she pulled that hand onto her lap, then took it in both her hands, turned it palm up and began to run her index finger over it.

"He has a long life line. Oh, long. From here to here." But he didn't even look how long. "He will live and live." She named how many years, which happened to fall exactly last year. "Well, let him look, from here to here. If he had a larger hand, he would live even longer. But it's long anyway. He will live fully." Evidently she noticed mistrust in his eyes, because she pressed his hand towards him and whispered almost with fear: "There is a second line here too, the heart line. A Gypsy woman is not wrong, a Gypsy woman always tells the truth."

But this second line didn't concern him either. A longer, shorter life didn't matter to him. He was immersed in this single moment, which he was just waiting for, sitting in the park by the main avenue. For this moment he would trade the longest life. There are such moments that like the sun in a lens focus a whole life into one point, burning a mark in us forever. Who knows if it isn't only for such moments that one lives, not for however many years.

He hardly listened to what she was foretelling him, because in his thoughts he was just leaving the school with her after the last class, out the doors of the building, then through the gate from the school courtyard, he crossed to the other side of the street, walked a bit along the sidewalk, then turned towards the church, he was already at the church, in a moment he will enter the park, he is already in the park, so her footsteps heading towards him should reach him any second. He already fancied he could hear them, still very quiet, she must be somewhere at the beginning of the side path, but they were her footsteps, how could he not recognize them, when suddenly like a blow to the head the Gypsy woman's words struck him:

"She won't be returning this way today. She was going to, but she remembered she has to buy something, hooks, snaps, and she will go through town."

She hadn't finished, when he yanked his hand from her grasp, sprang up and rushed towards town.

"And he won't give for a rattle?!" she called after him, so loudly the nipple slipped from the baby's mouth, because it burst out crying.

He turned back, fished some coin out of his pocket and threw it on her lap.

"Can't buy a rattle for this!" she cried out disappointed. "May he be cursed!"

If the Gypsy woman told the truth, that she would be returning through town, then the way to her house led down the stairs through the Needle's Eye to that old wild, green valley, separating the hill on which the town lay, from the opposite, much lower one, where her house was located.
