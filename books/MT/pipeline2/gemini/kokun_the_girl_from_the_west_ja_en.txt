*Koukun* (Vol. 1): The Girl from the West

Prologue: The Blue Flower

The wind howled in her ears, toying with her hair.

A passing shower from the early afternoon had left the earth still damp, and the rock to which she clung with both hands and feet was as cold as ice.

"...Sister!"

A high-pitched voice cried out, and a scattering of fine mud clods rained down upon her.

Aisha instinctively let go of the rock with one hand and braced her palm against the heel of her brother's shoe—glowing faintly white in the twilight—to support him.

The moment she bore his weight, her body tilted, and her other hand, still gripping the rock, nearly slipped. She desperately tightened her hold, barely managing to halt her fall. But the moment she exhaled, her knees began to tremble.

To fall meant certain death.

Gritting her teeth, Aisha held him up with all her might until her brother could firmly plant his foot into a hollow carved into the rock and steady himself. 

Even after he had found his footing, Aisha remained frozen in that posture for a long moment, unable to move. Panting heavily, she waited for the dizziness to subside.

Even if they managed to scale this rocky cliff path, there was likely no future waiting for them. She had known this for a little while now. 

Because drifting down from the top of the crag above them was the stiflingly thick stench of leather, metal, and sweat.

She had been told that few knew of this cliff path. The fact that warriors were waiting in ambush meant that the old retainer, Uchai—who had acted as a decoy to draw the enemy’s attention and allow them to escape—had been captured almost instantly.

She refused to believe Jiiya had confessed, yet the warriors had clearly deduced where Aisha and her brother were fleeing and had beaten them there.

*(Should I just let go?)*

Dimly, right in front of her eyes, she could see her younger brother’s mud-stained boots. Aisha’s face twisted in pain as she remembered his beaming smile the day those boots were bought for him.

Suddenly, everything around them brightened.

Driven by the wind, the clouds had parted. The setting sun, on the verge of vanishing behind the mountain’s shadow, illuminated the rocky crags in piercing streaks of light.

In that unexpected wash of bright light, she saw a plant rooted in a crevice just above the rock she was gripping, bearing tiny blue flowers. Buffeted by the wind, the blossoms whipped about as though they might be torn away at any moment, yet they held fast.

She heard the voice of the flowers’ scent. A faint but steadfast fragrance drifted toward her, riding the wind like a silken thread. Soon enough, insects that heard the voice of this scent would come flying, weaving their way through the gusts.

“…Sister.”

His voice floated down to her again. Riding on the cold wind came the scent of his fear, like that of a cowering puppy.

“Hold on tight, Milcha!”

Aisha called out.

“Grip the rock tightly and keep climbing! If you start to fall, I’ll catch you!”

Just a little further, and they would reach the top of the crag.

Sensing the shift in presence above her as her brother began to climb, Aisha too slowly resumed her ascent.

Chapter One: Encounter

1. Ritaran

The glow of the watchfires, unseen until moments ago, now stood out sharply against the blue darkness.

The wind that had been fiercely flapping the tents had eased somewhat.

From the countless tents pitched across the encampment, tendrils of white smoke rose and wavered, melting into the evening sky. The faint scent of supper being cooked drifted through the air.

“Looks like the foot soldiers get to eat before we do,” 

grumbled a bearded man in a low voice, slumped heavily in a folding chair alongside men with fierce, rugged features.

Mashu looked down at his bearded colleague, Urairi, and gave a soft chuckle.

“You don’t have to keep me company. I can handle the observation well enough on my own.”

Urairi started to make a quip but stopped himself. Turning his gaze toward the grasslands, he muttered,

“…If I could, I’d do exactly that.”

Before the majestic silhouette of the Tenro Mountains sinking into the gathering dusk, out on the grasslands spreading at their foot, the flickering light of torches could be seen—borne by a dozen or so horsemen advancing in their direction.

“Oroki.”

Craning his neck, Urairi looked back at the man standing by behind him. At the man's feet, a hunting dog raised its head, as though it had been called. Oroki placed a hand on the hound's head and said,

"Yes."

"The heir is eight or so, and the elder sister only fifteen or sixteen—is that what you said?"

"The heir is nine, and the elder sister is fifteen."

Urairi nodded and sighed.

"They should have just left them alone, rather than going out of their way to capture them."

Urairi looked up at Mashu and continued.

"Don't you agree? If some faction were trying to prop them up, that would be one thing. But the descendants of a king who was despised by the people and chased from his throne couldn't possibly wield any power. 

"In fact, it’s likely because they thought the same that the descendants have been left alone until now. Why on earth, in the middle of a war no less, did Jukuchi decide to do this now?"

Mashu did not answer the question. Keeping his gaze fixed on the horsemen, he murmured,

"So they did come from that direction."

Urairi frowned.

"What?"

"The horsemen. The fact that the soldiers who captured them are returning from that direction means the descendants were trying to flee into Toura Ira—the Great Collapse Ravine in the Tenro Mountains."

"……"

Urairi stood up and fell in beside Mashu.

"'So they did,' you said. —Did you foresee this?"

Mashu glanced at Urairi.

"If they were going to run, that's the only place they could go."

"Surely not. The descendants had been living hidden in the forestlands, hadn't they? It would be easier to flee east through the forest than to climb a treacherous mountain path."

"They must have thought they had a better chance of surviving if they fled to Tenro, even if it meant braving the danger."

"Why?"

"Earlier, you called Keraan a king despised by the people. But in Tenro, there are those who do not hate him."

Urairi's expression turned grim.

"……I hadn't heard anything about that. The mountain folk of Tenro? Which mountain folk?"

Mashu stared intently at the mounted warriors, whose figures were now clearly visible.

"You aren't the only one who didn't know. If anything, those who do know are in the minority."

Urairi narrowed his eyes.

"When did you find out?"

Mashu looked at Urairi and replied quietly.

"Me? I've known since I was a child."

One of Vassal King Jukuchi's aides emerged from the great tent the king used for his official duties. Peering through the gathering dusk, he scanned the area, and upon noticing Mashu and the others, approached at a brisk pace.

"Lord Inspector."

Crossing his arms neatly over his chest and bowing deeply, the aide said,

"I bring word from Lord Jukuchi. 'The captives will be arriving shortly, so please enter the tent.' That is all."

Mashu nodded and gave a curt reply.

"Understood."

The aide bowed deeply once more, then led the two of them toward the great tent. 

The Vassal Kingdom Inspectors were the eyes and ears of the Umar Emperor, who held four such kingdoms under his dominion. 

The term for these vassal kingdoms derived from the Umar word *rachi*, meaning "an enclosure that partitions and protects a territory." 

Each of the vassal kingdoms was once an independent nation, and even now, under the dominion of the Umar Empire, they were permitted a certain degree of autonomy. In exchange for the duty of guarding the empire's borders—its *rachi*—the vassal kings were granted a measure of authority over the nations they had once ruled, but they were no longer kings of independent countries. Jukuchi, who was nothing more than the Vassal King of West Kantar, was not permitted to conceal anything from Mashu and his companion.

Leaving their armed escorts behind, Mashu and Urairi walked slowly toward the bright entrance of the great tent, which looked as though it had been cleanly carved from the surrounding blue darkness.

*

Inside, the great tent was more spacious than it appeared from the outside. 

The smoke hole in the ceiling was thrown wide open, and the central hearth blazed bright red. Lamps were placed here and there, providing enough light to make out the expressions of the chief retainers and clan heads seated along the walls.

At the far end, facing the entrance, stood an altar beautifully painted in vermilion, blue, and gold. With his back to the altar, Vassal King Jukuchi sat in a large chair, speaking in hushed tones to an aide beside him. When Mashu and Urairi entered, however, he rose, offered a slight bow, and gestured for them to take the nearby chairs.

The wind outside was cold, but inside the great tent, it was stiflingly hot. Small tables set before each seat held fruit juices to quench their thirst, along with nuts and dried fruits to stave off hunger.

Not long after Mashu and Urairi took their seats, the toll of a bell announced the arrival of the captives. The murmuring ceased, and a hush fell over the great tent.

Jukuchi sat back down, accepted a cloth from his aide, and wiped the sweat from his forehead and the nape of his neck.

He was a large man, but there was no softness to his frame, and his skin was tanned dark by the sun. Everything about his build was massive, hands and feet included, yet it was his brows and eyes that drew the most attention. Whenever he fixed his large, piercing eyes on someone, it left them feeling unsettled. 

Though he hailed from a minor collateral branch of the royal family—rumored merely to share some distant connection to the royal bloodline—he possessed a unique instinct for warfare. He had borrowed troops from the Emperor on the condition that he would serve as a bulwark for the empire in times of crisis. After King Keraan was ousted, Jukuchi overthrew the previous king—who had taken the throne but failed to quell the skirmishes between the clans—and thus became the Vassal King of West Kantar.

One clan still refused to acknowledge Jukuchi, meaning he could not yet claim full control over all of West Kantar. However, Mashu believed that if the situation continued as it was, Jukuchi would have the region completely pacified before long.

The Emperor desired that Jukuchi pacify West Kantar swiftly, and Mashu, too, hoped for this outcome.

The moment the doors and cloth hangings were pulled wide from both sides, a cold night wind blew in.

Wrapped in the night wind, two brawny warriors entered, one leading a young boy and the other a slender girl.

Neither was bound, but the warriors gripped their dominant arms. One of the warriors placed an arm across his chest and declared in a resonant voice:

"In accordance with your orders, we have brought the descendants of Keraan."

Jukuchi nodded, offered the warriors a word of praise for their efforts, and gestured that they were dismissed.

The warriors, however, looked hesitant.

"What is it? You may withdraw."

At Jukuchi's words, one of the warriors spoke.

"With all due respect, my lord. The older sister has shown no sign of submission. If we release her, there is no telling what she might do."

Upon hearing this, Jukuchi raised his thick eyebrows.

"Is that so? Well, I shall be on my guard. Release them."

The warriors bowed, released their grips, and stepped back a pace, but kept a watchful eye on the girl, ready to subdue her at a moment's notice.

The girl, however, did not move. She did not so much as twitch an eyebrow. With eyes as black and unmoving as stone, she simply stared straight at Jukuchi.

Jukuchi met her gaze and said,

"Keraan's grandchildren, Milcha and Aisha, I presume."

The girl spoke.

"I have no name to give a usurper."

Her voice was hoarse, but firm.

Jukuchi sighed.

"I see. You truly show no sign of submission."

He rose slowly to his feet, looked down at the siblings, and taking a longsword from the sword stand beside him, struck the floorboards violently with the base of its scabbard.

The senior vassals drew back with a gasp, and the siblings flinched. The boy's face crumpled, and he began to sob.

"Your brother is crying."

Fixing his gaze on the girl, Jukuchi said,

"Mind your circumstances and your place when you speak. Your attitude alone could send your brother's head flying right before your eyes."

The girl looked up at Jukuchi, her face pale, but presently she said in a frail voice:

"...That is a lie."

Jukuchi raised his thick eyebrows.

"A lie?"

The girl nodded.

"Does my attitude truly decide such a thing? The fate of our lives?"

Jukuchi's eyes widened slightly.

He stared fixedly at the older sister, but eventually, the corners of his lips twisted slightly.

"If not your attitude, then what determines your fate?"

He thought she might pause to think, but the girl answered immediately.

"What you stand to gain or lose."

The senior vassals stirred faintly.

Mashu shifted his gaze from the girl to Jukuchi.

*(Now, how will he answer?)*

Jukuchi stared at the girl in a long silence before finally letting out a sigh.

"My own gain or loss, you ask? ...Well, you're not off the mark."

With that, he dropped heavily into his chair.

He lifted a gold-trimmed horn cup from a small table and drained the milk-wine with loud gulps. He wiped his mouth with a cloth an aide held out to him, then mopped the sweat from his face and the back of his neck.

Then, he turned his gaze back to the older sister.

"You're not off the mark, but you're not exactly right, either. Your existence isn't so much a loss to me as it is a loss to the Empire as a whole."

The younger brother looked up at his sister questioningly. The sister did not look back at him; she kept her eyes fixed steadily on Jukuchi.

"You two were trying to flee into the Tenro Mountains. Or, to be precise, into Toura Ira, the Great Collapse Ravine."

Seeing the sister's expression, Jukuchi added:

"Don't misunderstand. That old man didn't confess. He is an admirably loyal retainer. He put up a fight you wouldn't expect from someone his age, and because I already suspected he might be a decoy, I had my soldiers split into two groups from the very beginning."

The girl's face stiffened. Watching her, Jukuchi continued.

"The one who kindly informed us of where you might run to was the esteemed Inspector from the Umar Empire."

Urairi shifted slightly. He made no move to look Mashu's way, but his surprise radiated from his entire being.

Mashu's expression did not change; he simply watched Jukuchi and the young girl.

Jukuchi spoke in a matter-of-fact tone.

"Meaning your existence is detrimental not only to West Kantar, but to the Empire as well. Do you know why?"

The girl's face had gone pale. She must have realized that there was no way out.

Only Jukuchi's voice echoed through the great tent.

"Your grandfather, Keraan, was a foolish man. Because of him, many of our people starved to death. The vast majority of this country despises Keraan from the bottom of their hearts. I am one of them."

The girl's eyes wavered. Her pale face grew whiter still.

Jukuchi had his horn cup refilled with milk-wine and gulped it down noisily once again.

"Yet I hear there are still those out here on the frontier who revere the two of you.

"The ones called the Makishi, the People of the Deep Valley. They're a frontier mountain tribe of no great number, and they have no ambitions for hegemony in this land, so I've left them to their own devices thus far. But now, their existence has become troublesome. —The land they control is a problem."

Beside him, Mashu heard Urairi mutter, *(So that's what this is about.)*

The clan they were planning to subjugate on this expedition controlled the western foothills of the Tenro Mountains, but their dominion did not extend into the mountains themselves.

Though they lacked the strength to hold out for long against Jukuchi's expeditionary force, there remained one possibility: if they could secure the aid of Tatsuketsu, which lay beyond the Tenro Mountains, they might still turn the tide.

The peaks of the Tenro Mountains, which stretched like a wall around the northern and western borders of West Kantar, were exceedingly rugged. Because there were very few routes through which a large army could invade from the Tatsuketsu side, fortresses had been erected at such choke points, jointly garrisoned by the Imperial Army and the forces of West Kantar.

In the western reaches of the Tenro Mountains lay Toura Ira, the Great Collapse Ravine. Here, too, there existed a mountain trail that a small band of warriors accustomed to traversing high peaks could cross from the Tatsuketsu side—but there was no fortress to guard it.

The terrain around Toura Ira was riddled with unexpected subterranean hollows; a single misstep through the surface could prove fatal. It was impossible to navigate these mountain paths without a guide.

Unless the Makishi, the People of the Deep Valley who controlled the Great Collapse Ravine and its surrounding lands, chose to ally with them, Tatsuketsu warriors could not descend into West Kantar. Furthermore, because it was impossible to subjugate the Makishi—who knew the ravine like the back of their hands—without deploying a massive army, neither the Vassal King of West Kantar nor the Empire had ever placed much strategic importance on this territory.

But if the People of the Deep Valley still viewed the descendants of Keraan as the legitimate royal bloodline, hostile clans could potentially upend the entire situation by rallying behind these two youths.

Among the clan chiefs seated along the walls of the great tent, a few wore expressions indicating that they had finally grasped the true significance of what was unfolding.

The clan chiefs who had joined the current expedition from the southern and eastern reaches of the Vassal Kingdom were largely ignorant of the state of affairs in the western Tenro Mountains. Because they had only recently sworn their fealty, Jukuchi had likely withheld the details from them intentionally, lest they start entertaining unhelpful ideas.

"You two are, so to speak, ant holes in an embankment. Normally, leaving you be wouldn't pose a problem. But when a storm comes, the embankment will collapse starting from those very holes, and calamity will spread across the nation.

"I am not a heartless man. It pains me to kill you simply because you are that man's grandchildren. But I have no choice.

"I could keep you locked away under my watch, but that carries entirely too much risk."

As he spoke, a sudden look of exhaustion washed over Jukuchi’s face.

"So there you have it. If you must curse anything, curse your bloodline and your fate."

Jukuchi jerked his chin. The warriors from earlier stepped forward, reaching out to seize the siblings by their arms.

The younger brother recoiled in terror, reaching out to cling to his sister. She grasped his hand firmly, then looked up at Jukuchi.

A flicker of hesitation crossed her pale face. Her lips parted, then pressed tight again, as though she had thought better of it.

Jukuchi furrowed his brow dubiously.

"Is there something you wish to say?"

The elder sister considered this for a moment. Presently, as if having made up her mind, she spoke.

"You have been poisoned."

Jukuchi furrowed his brow deeply, staring at the girl, and again wiped the sweat from his face and neck with a cloth. The cloth, however, did little to stem the sweat. Even to a casual observer, it was glaringly obvious that he was perspiring at an unnatural rate, his skin completely drenched.

"What did you say?"

The elder sister stared back at Jukuchi, her eyes unreadable.

"I can smell the Chitcho nether-herb on you. Someone has poisoned you."

A murmur ran through the senior vassals; some half-rose from their seats.

Wiping the sweat from his eyes, Jukuchi gave a fearless sneer.

"Spitting venom to the very end, are we? I admire your nerve, but if you must lie, tell one that sounds a bit more convincing."

"I am not lying."

"Of course you are. A poison brewed from the root of the nether-herb has neither taste nor smell."

A look of quiet realization crossed the girl's face.

"...Then believe what you will."

She sighed, put an arm around her younger brother's shoulders, and turned her back on Jukuchi. A guard hastily grabbed her arm and began hauling her toward the entrance of the great tent.

Mashu stood up abruptly and called out to them.

"Wait."

The sister and brother stopped and looked back at him. Jukuchi and the ministers were all staring at him too, their faces asking what this was about.

Mashu addressed the girl.

"Lady Aisha Keraan—do you smell anything on me?"

The elder sister stared at him intently. Her dark eyes were like obsidian.

He thought she might not answer, but then, with a faint furrow of her brow, she spoke.

"...Are you Ritaran?"

Mashu's eyes widened. A cold chill spread from between his brows across his entire head, and his heart began to pound so fast it ached.

His lips had gone numb. Forcing them open, Mashu asked in a hoarse whisper,

"What makes you say that?"

Her brow still furrowed, the elder sister replied,

"Because you smell of blue sweetgrass."

Mashu stared at her, stunned.

"...Hey, what's wrong?"

Ignoring Urairi's question, Mashu strode over to Jukuchi.

Up close, it was immediately clear that something was terribly wrong with him. His eyes were unfocused.

Mashu rounded on the aides.

"Tell the physician to bring Kaful! Make sure they don't mistake it—it has to be Kaful! And bring water, a lot of it! Hurry!"

Then he shouted to the warriors holding the siblings.

"Take those two away and lock them up securely!"

There was a dull thud, like a sack of flour hitting the floor. Mashu whipped around to see that Jukuchi had slipped from his chair and collapsed on the ground.

As the senior vassals rose to their feet, talking over one another in confusion, Mashu rushed over to Urairi. Lowering his voice so the others would not hear, he spoke rapidly.

"Stay with those siblings. Take the guards with you. Don't let anyone lay a finger on them. Keep a close eye on their meals and the insect-repelling smoke so they aren't poisoned. And send Oroki to me later."

Urairi gave a silent nod and headed toward the siblings. Then, he and the warriors escorted them out of the great tent.

2. The Odorless Poison

It was dawn the following day when Jukuchi's condition finally passed the critical point.

Over the course of that long night, the senior vassals came to visit the tent one after another, but Mashu only allowed them to look in from the entrance; he forbade anyone from stepping inside.

Though some showed clear indignation, no one attempted to force their way in. With the poisoner’s identity still unknown, Mashu's measures were perfectly reasonable.

The only person permitted to enter the tent was his subordinate, Oroki. Mashu gave him detailed instructions and sent him out into the encampment, along with the dog that followed at his heels like a shadow.

Mashu had stayed awake all night, sitting by the Vassal King's bedside alongside the physician. Only after seeing that the Vassal King's condition had stabilized did he lay down a bed in the corner of the same tent and drift into a light sleep.

He woke shortly before noon. The Vassal King was breathing deeply and quietly in his sleep, just as he had been before.

Standing up and stepping outside the tent, Mashu took a deep breath.

The air smelled of rain-soaked grass. Come to think of it, he recalled hearing the sound of a sudden shower pelting the tent in his dreams. Such downpours were common in these parts at this time of year.

The sky was overcast and a dull, leaden color, but the wind was strong. As the heavy clouds were driven swiftly across the sky, sunlight streamed through the gaps whenever they broke, illuminating the Tenro Mountains with unexpected brilliance.

Preparations for the midday meal must have already begun; mingling with the scent of smoke was the savory aroma of thin, rice-flour *tapa* baking on the fire.

Catching the sound of footsteps in the grass, he turned to see Oroki approaching with his dog.

"How did it go?"

When asked, Oroki replied in a low voice, "It was exactly as you suspected."

"Was it there?"

"It was, sir."

After hearing the detailed report, Mashu smiled.

"Good work. Get some rest."

Oroki bowed, clicked his heels, and abruptly turned his back to Mashu. Mashu called out to his retreating figure.

"Make sure your partner gets a good meal, too."

Oroki glanced back, his expression softening into a faint smile, and nodded. As if knowing he was the one being talked about, the dog wagged its tail lazily.

Taking another deep breath, Mashu turned his back to them.

*(...Now then,)*

Mashu murmured to himself.

*(This next part will be a job in itself.)*

Stepping into the tent from the bright outdoors, he was blinded for a moment.

As his eyes adjusted to the gloom, he could make out Jukuchi lying on bedding spread near the hearth, and the physician attending at his side. The smell of smoke drifted through the air, mingling with the scent of a medicinal brew.

As he approached the head of the bed, the faint sound seemed to wake Jukuchi, who opened his eyes slightly and gestured for water. 

The physician lifted and supported Jukuchi's head, bringing a vessel of water to his lips. Jukuchi slurped at it audibly. 

Watching him drink without choking, the physician's face relaxed in relief.

Jukuchi coughed a few times, as if trying to clear phlegm caught in his throat, then looked up and fixed his gaze on Mashu. The strength was returning to his eyes.

“...What they slipped me,” 

Jukuchi said in a hoarse voice. 

“Was it really Chitcho?” 

Mashu glanced at the physician, and the middle-aged man spoke up.

“I have no definitive proof, but I believe so. The symptoms you exhibited were remarkably similar to those of Chitcho poisoning. Furthermore, the antidote we brewed to counteract it has proven highly effective.”

Jukuchi said with a sullen look,

“But Chitcho is supposed to be completely odorless and tasteless, isn't it?”

The physician nodded.

“Indeed, it has neither.”

Jukuchi shifted his gaze back to Mashu.

“Then why did that girl claim she could smell Chitcho in my sweat?”

How to answer when asked this very question—Mashu had pondered it all through his sleepless vigil the night before, but every response that came to mind had left him with a lingering sense of hesitation. 

But now, faced with the actual question, he felt no hesitation in voicing the answer he had in mind.

Mashu turned his gaze to the physician.

“Lord Jukuchi's condition appears stable. Will he be all right without you for a short while?”

The physician shifted forward on his knees to take Jukuchi's pulse, then nodded.

“I believe he will be fine without me for a brief time.”

“Then please, take a short rest. I shall send word at once should there be any change in Lord Jukuchi's condition.”

The physician bowed and exited the tent. 

Mashu waited until the man's footsteps faded away before turning back to Jukuchi.

“To answer your question...”

“...”

“I suspect the girl truly could smell the Chitcho. She must have a keener nose than most.”

A sharp glint appeared in Jukuchi's large eyes.

“Why would you speak such nonsense after going to the trouble of clearing the room? What I wanted to know was why that girl made such an unnecessary remark. She knew I had been poisoned. Which means she is connected to the one who poisoned me...”

Mashu slowly shook his head. Jukuchi scowled in irritation.

"You say I am mistaken? Where am I mistaken?"

"That girl is not in league with the poisoner."

"How can you be so certain?"

"Because she warned you that you were being poisoned—the very fact that has you so puzzled."

"..."

"That was an entirely unnecessary thing to say. If she were on the poisoner's side, believing that your death would mean salvation for her and her brother, she never would have breathed a word of it."

Mashu gave a soft sigh.

"She may act brave, but she is still only fifteen. When she realized that a man would die if she kept what she noticed to herself, the words simply slipped out."

"But I am the very man trying to kill her."

"Even so, there are those who simply cannot stand by and let a person die."

"But the nether-herb..."

Mashu interrupted him before he could finish.

"You are too caught up in the fact that the nether-herb is tasteless and odorless, but the symptoms of poison manifest in various ways. Your profuse sweating had caught my eye as well. To that girl, watching you from head-on, it must have been glaringly obvious."

"..."

"Kings live under the constant threat of murder, and so they become intimately familiar with its methods. That girl is the granddaughter of King Keraan. She was likely raised with a thorough education in the various arts of assassination. 

"And so, from your abnormal sweating and the wavering of your gaze, she deduced that you had been dosed with the nether-herb."

Jukuchi looked half-convinced, but still seemed to have lingering doubts. He opened his mouth to speak.

Mashu continued before he could utter a sound.

"Besides, whoever attempted to poison you could not possibly have known the value of Keraan's descendants."

Jukuchi's eyes widened in realization.

"You know who did this?"

Mashu nodded.

"The chieftain of the Rima clan."

Jukuchi's eyes hardened.

"Lariha. ...And your proof?"

"His servant was carrying a polishing cloth. I am told that this same servant was tasked with polishing the cups laid out in the great tent last night."

Jukuchi sighed and shook his head.

"If that is the basis of your suspicion, you are mistaken. I wash and polish all my cups myself, and keep them where I can see them at all times. There is no way he could have laced my cup with poison."

Mashu smiled.

"He did not coat the cups with poison."

"...?"

"He coated everyone's cups with the sap of the Kaful tree."

Mashu calmly explained the method behind the assassination attempt.

"Fearing poison, you only drink milk-wine poured from the same jug as everyone else. If poison were mixed into the milk-wine, Lariha would have to drink it as well. 

"He could have killed everyone in the great tent and left himself the sole survivor, but even if that succeeded in eliminating you, it would earn him the enmity of the other clans."

Astonishment spread across Jukuchi's face.

"I see! He turned the fact that I polish my own cup separately against me."

Mashu nodded.

“Yes. The poison of the nether-herb is weak against Kaful sap. Upon contact with the sap, it loses its toxicity. The antidote the physician prescribed for you was also made from Kaful sap.”

“...So they coated their own vessels, and those of the others, with it beforehand.”

“Exactly. My subordinates spent all night tracking down the person who possessed a vessel-polishing cloth that smelled of Kaful sap.” 

Mashu offered a fleeting smile.

“Using dogs, mind you, rather than their own noses.”

Jukuchi grunted and cast his eyes downward, lost in thought for a moment.

Stroking his chin, Mashu said, “Under these circumstances, punishing Lariha will be quite difficult. We must carefully devise a plan to manage the aftermath before making a move.”

“...There is that, but...”

Murmuring this, Jukuchi raised his head and looked at Mashu.

“It is the question of what to do with the girl.”

“Because she saved your life?”

Jukuchi nodded.

“If we could spare at least the girl...”

Mashu shook his head.

“That will not do. If her brother is killed, she will bear a fierce hatred toward you for the rest of her life. It would only sow the seeds of future disaster.”

Seeing Jukuchi’s expression cloud over, Mashu continued.

“If you wish to show mercy, you can execute them by poison rather than beheading.”

Jukuchi drew his brows tightly together. 

“Surely that would be a more painful death.”

“No, we need only choose the right poison. There are poisons that bring a painless end, as though one were merely falling asleep.”

Jukuchi stared at Mashu with a conflicted expression. Mashu simply met his gaze in silence.

3. Frost-herb

Aisha and her younger brother, Milcha, were confined in a tent pitched on the outskirts of the encampment.

Because it was set up in isolation on the grassland, well away from the other tents, anyone approaching it was highly conspicuous. 

The guards who had accompanied Mashu maintained strict security both inside and outside the tent; they did not hand over meals or changes of clothing to the siblings until they had thoroughly inspected them first.

Shortly after noon on the day Jukuchi recovered, Oroki came to the tent once, called Urairi outside to whisper something to him, and then departed for some unknown destination with about three of the guards. Aside from that, there was almost no movement of people.

In addition to the skylight for letting out smoke, the tent had two small windows cut into it for ventilation. Whenever the shouts of drills or other noises drifted over from the encampment, the younger brother would come to one of these windows and peek outside. His older sister, however, never went near them; she simply sat inside the tent, passing the time by embroidering a piece of cloth she had been given.

After night fell and the soldiers had finished their evening meal, a late dinner was brought to the siblings. 

Behind the servants carrying the food came Mashu, accompanied by two of the Vassal King’s close aides.

The guards lifted the flap at the tent’s entrance and whispered something inside. A moment later, Urairi emerged. His face stiffened when he noticed Mashu and the others standing behind the servant in charge of the evening meal.

Mashu pressed a finger to his lips, and Urairi signaled his understanding with a glance.

The servant, bearing the evening meal on a large tray, ducked his head slightly as he entered the tent. Mashu and the king's aides each stepped closer to the windows.

Night had fully fallen, and the campfires were a fair distance away. From inside the brightly lit tent, it would be almost impossible to make out the faces of anyone standing just outside the windows. Even if they caught sight of a silhouette, the guards had stationed themselves outside these same windows countless times before; the brother and sister should have no reason to suspect that tonight was any different.

The meal was far more lavish than the soldiers’ rations. Alongside roasted mutton, there was a hearty vegetable stew and an array of fruit. The thin *tapa* flatbreads were spread with butter and sprinkled with precious sugar. And, of course, a serving of milk-wine accompanied the food.

The younger brother must have been ravenous; his face lit up with delight as the meal was set before him. His older sister, however, kept her head bowed, just as she had been even before the servant entered.

Mashu narrowed his eyes at her posture.

Her shoulders were rigid. She was tense.

*(Has she realized?)*

The boy reached for the flatbread first, enthusiastically stuffing his cheeks with the dough, which was soaked through with melted butter and sugar.

His sister quietly began to eat as well. Like her brother, she started with the flatbread, then moved on to the mutton and the stew, eating slowly as though savoring every bite. She finished the fruit, too, leaving nothing behind.

Having tasted a bit of everything, the boy reached for the milk-wine. 

At that moment, his sister stopped eating and looked at him.

*(…Will she stop him?)*

Mashu instinctively leaned forward, his eyes locked on her profile.

Her hand was trembling faintly. Her slender neck was so strained that the tendons stood out.

The boy grasped the vessel by the handle with both hands and brought it to his lips.

His sister made no move to stop him. She simply watched him intently.

Gulping audibly, the boy drank down the milk-wine with obvious relish. He set the jug on the floor and reached for the remaining fruit.

Then, his head suddenly lolled, and he nearly pitched forward.

His sister’s hand shot out to catch him. She eased him down to the floor, resting his head in her lap.

Something glistened on her cheek.

She remained hunched over, as if cradling her brother’s limp head, before finally straightening her back and reaching for his empty milk-wine jug.

No sooner had her fingers closed around the handle than she hurled the jug with all her might.

It struck the wall just below the tent window with a heavy thud, making Mashu instinctively recoil.

Urairi and the others inside leaped to their feet in shock, but the sister paid them no mind. She simply snatched up her own jug of milk-wine, brought it to her lips, and—keeping her face turned toward the window where Mashu stood—drank it down in audible gulps. 

Her dark eyes held a fierce light as they bored into him. 

Steeling himself, Mashu stared straight back into the girl's eyes.

*(That’s right. I am the one who poisoned you.)*

A surge of heat welled up from deep within his chest, and his entire body began to tremble.

*(You caught the scent of my fingers, the very fingers that pinched the poison into your cup, didn't you—and you realized the man they belonged to is standing right here.)*

Without realizing it, Mashu had broken into a smile. His entire body burned with a feverish heat. 

Mashu watched intently as the light faded from the girl's eyes and she collapsed forward.

＊

Flanked by his aides, Jukuchi emerged from the tent. 

A large woolen rug had been spread across the grass out front. The light from the torches held aloft by the soldiers cast a faint, flickering glow over the bodies of the girl and the young boy lying upon it. 

Their faces were stark white even in the gloom of night, and they looked somehow smaller than they had in life. 

Jukuchi stood gazing down at the bodies for a moment. Then he crouched, placed a hand over the brother's mouth, and confirmed that he was no longer breathing. 

He reached over to the sister and checked her in the same manner, then gently touched her cheek—only to pull his hand back in surprise. 

"She is terribly cold. She hasn't been dead all that long, has she?"

Mashu, standing on the opposite side of the bodies, spoke in a quiet voice.

"That is because I used Hirin, the frost-herb."

Jukuchi nodded, stood, and let out a sigh. Then he turned his gaze to the soldiers. 

"Wrap them in cloth and bury them."

With only those words, he abruptly turned his back on the bodies and disappeared into the tent. 

In West Kantar, the bodies of children are buried beneath *yugi* trees. 

According to legend, the *yugi* tree first sprouted from the body of a grieving mother who had thrown herself into a river after losing her child and washed ashore. It was said that if the young were buried at its roots, the spirit of the *yugi* tree would gently guide their souls to the heavens. 

The soldiers, having been ordered by Jukuchi to search for a *yugi* tree since evening, had already found a suitable burial site and returned. Thus, the two bodies were promptly loaded into the back of a wagon, carried across the plains and into the woods along a narrow river, and buried in a grave dug beneath a *yugi* tree. 

The soldiers were, after all, human—someone's children, and fathers themselves—so they did not simply toss the bodies into the earth. Instead, they wrapped them in woolen blankets to ward off the cold before laying them to rest at the bottom of the grave. After covering them with dirt so they would not be disturbed by wild beasts, the soldiers bowed deeply and returned to the encampment.

The moment the sound of the horses’ hooves faded into the distance, three figures sprang from the dark grove and began frantically digging up the earth of the burial site.

They hauled the two bodies from the hole, laid them on the grass beside it, and hastily stripped away the shrouds that covered them from head to toe.

Then, carefully wrapping the bodies in fresh cloth, two of the men gathered up the sister and brother in their arms and vanished into the depths of the forest.

The remaining man placed the original shrouds back into the bottom of the pit, arranged them to hold their shape, and covered them with earth once more, restoring the grave to exactly how it had looked before.

The light of the setting moon cast a faint gleam upon the tips of the branches in the forest.

4. The Voice of Scent

A savory aroma drifted through the air.

*(...Jiiya is baking tapa flatbread.)*

Just as she thought it was time to get up and wake Milcha, a terrifying memory flashed through her mind like lightning, sending a sharp throb of pain through her chest. The pain seeped like bitter water from her chest, up her throat, and into her head.

Aisha opened her mouth and drew in a harsh, ragged breath.

Her throat whistled like a flute, and the smells of baking flatbread, woodsmoke, two men, and a dog flooded her nostrils all at once.

Amidst that flood of scents was the smell of Milcha. Their arms seemed to be touching, for she could feel his warmth seeping into her skin.

*(Milcha... he’s alive!)*

She tried to open her eyes to look at her brother, but the world was spinning violently around her. Aisha squeezed her eyes shut again and waited for the vertigo to pass.

"It seems you are awake." Hearing the man's voice, Aisha frantically tried to piece together what was happening through the fog of her confusion.

*(I drank the milk-wine laced with the frost-herb, Hirin...)*

She had watched Milcha drain the milk-wine laced with the deadly frost-herb, and then she had surely drunk her own.

*(So why...?)*

Eyes still closed, Aisha sensed one of the men stand up and approach her. Even without looking, she knew exactly who he was.

"You are awake, are you not, Lady Aisha Keraan?"

Aisha slowly opened her eyes and looked at him. She was still slightly dizzy, but she could see his face clearly.

His deeply tanned, rugged face and dark eyes stared intently down at her.

"...Why?"

She murmured the word. Perhaps noticing the rasp in her voice, the man turned his head and ordered someone behind him to bring a pitcher of water.

When the pitcher was brought over, the man gently slipped a hand beneath Aisha's head, supporting her as he slowly helped her sit up. It was a large, thick, warm hand.

She took a sip of the water from the wooden bowl. It smelled of old cedar, likely having been carried in a cask. Even so, it tasted wonderful to her parched throat.

She drained the water and handed the wooden bowl back to the man, who took it from her in silence.

Aisha stared at the man.

Every time he moved, the scent of blue sweetgrass grew stronger. He must have kept it tucked inside his clothes for a long time. It had mingled with the man’s own scent and altered, but it was unmistakably the fragrance of blue sweetgrass.

"Why..."

She tried to ask again, but the man shook his head.

"There is no time to explain now. I shall do so tonight, so please rest here until then."

As she tried to look toward her brother, the man stood up and said,

"Your brother is safe, too. He woke up a short while ago, then went back to sleep. I wrapped him in a blanket, so he shouldn't catch cold. You ought to warm yourself as well. I am leaving my subordinate here to guard you; if anything happens, follow his instructions."

With that, he strode back toward the campfire. He handed the wooden bowl back to the man sitting beside the flames, plucked a piece of thin *tapa* flatbread from a plate, popped it into his mouth, and walked off into the grove.

Beside the man cooking at the fire was a dog. It lay prone, watching her.

"If you're able to get up, would you like to come warm yourself by the fire and have some flatbread?"

The man called out to her. He had a narrow face, but there was a certain sharpness about him, like a hunter.

Aisha placed her hands on the grass and slowly stood up. A wave of dizziness washed over her again, but it quickly subsided.

Sitting beside the campfire, she felt the warmth of the flames seep into her skin, and she realized just how chilled to the bone her body had become.

*(The Hirin frost-herb freezes the body.)*

Anyone who ingested the herb by mistake was supposed to rapidly turn cold and pass away as if falling asleep, without any time to suffer.

*(Why, then, am I alive?)*

The man deftly pinched the edge of a thin *tapa* flatbread that was sticking to a small pan and releasing a savory, toasted aroma. Peeling it off, he placed it on a wooden plate, slathered it with a generous dollop of butter, drizzled it with honey, and handed it to her.

The moment she felt the warmth of the wooden plate in her palms, the hard shell that had encased her heart shattered, and the sensation of being alive flooded through her entire body.

She had thought there was no path left for her. That her life was over.

She had expected to be beheaded, so when she learned her execution was to be by the frost-herb, she thought it would be an easier death than the blade. While waiting for the execution, she was so terrified her bones shook. But when the moment finally came, and she watched her brother bring the poisoned milk-wine to his lips, all fear vanished, and her entire world turned pitch-black. She, her brother, and everything else were swallowed into a pitch-black darkness.

Except, the instant she caught the scent of that man's fingers in the milk-wine, a fierce rage pierced her between the eyes.

That they had to die merely because of their bloodline—the sheer injustice of it, and the bitter agony of having to leave this world unable to right that wrong, erupted into a fury directed at something indescribable, scorching her to the core.

Slowly lifting the thin flatbread from the warm wooden plate, she folded it into a bite-sized piece and placed it in her mouth. The rich saltiness of butter and the sweetness of honey mingled, spreading throughout her mouth.

It was a familiar taste.

The flatbread she had been served at every meal since being taken captive had carried the scent of unfamiliar ingredients, but this was the everyday flatbread she was used to eating. As its aroma filled the back of her nose, tears welled up.

Keeping her head down so the man would not see her tears, Aisha took a large mouthful of the warm, toasted flatbread.

The dog rose heavily to its feet and came over to her side. It pressed its cold nose against Aisha’s elbow.
