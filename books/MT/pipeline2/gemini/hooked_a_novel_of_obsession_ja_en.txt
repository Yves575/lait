1

*I’d love to go for a swim*, she thought.

She wanted to cast off her shirt, skirt, and underwear at her feet, feel the softness of the water and the refraction of the light against her bare skin, and advance endlessly through a soundless space, going wherever her heart desired. As the water temperature melded with her skin, the boundary between herself and the world would blur; her weight, her age, and her gender would lose their meaning. If she tried to utter a word, it would all turn into bubbles, rising higher and higher, bleeding into the white beyond before finally vanishing. The hot season was drawing to a close, so why was she having such thoughts?

That was it. The early-morning office before her looked just like a deserted indoor pool.

The desks were arranged in neat, orderly rows, like swimming lanes divided by ropes; the hushed, pale-blue emptiness of the room evoked the quiet surface of a pool. Even the smell of the ink toner was strikingly similar to chlorine, in that both were pungent odors that made her feel as though she were about to remember something. She had been searching for a time and place where she could be completely alone to work undisturbed, and had ultimately ended up right back here, in the office where she already spent the majority of her day. This was a space where she was required to project her utmost competence, yet right now, she could scream gibberish at the top of her lungs or indulge in a nonsensical dance, and it wouldn't matter. Of course, she would do no such thing.

No matter the circumstances, she was not the sort of person to raise her voice or dance.

The Food Products Sales Division, which occupied half of the nineteenth floor at the Otemachi headquarters of Nakamaru Trading, the nation's largest trading company, was completely deserted between six and seven in the morning. This department handled the quintessential operations of a trading firm: importing food products from overseas and selling them to domestic corporations.

The pristine, unstained air spreading out before her soothed Eriko Shimura’s mind and body, which were perpetually driven to seek conclusions. Fulfilling her duty as the first to arrive, she turned on the photocopier.

Ever since she was a girl, she had always liked to stay one step ahead in everything. Her father—a senior veteran at the same company—had taught her that "taking the initiative" was an essential quality for a trading company employee. Her parents, with whom she lived in a Setagaya condo, seemed displeased by their only daughter's absence at the breakfast table, but now that she was in her eighth year at the company, they didn't say anything anymore.

Even now, at the age of thirty, Eriko had no intention of moving out of her parents' home. Without her mother’s support, it would be impossible for her to work while maintaining such an immaculate appearance and managing her health. Besides, if she were to leave home, her mother would surely feel suffocated in a daily routine with only her taciturn father for company.

No matter how early she arrived, she could maintain her time alone for barely forty minutes. It was the kind of workplace where it was standard for the majority of her colleagues to show up an hour before the 8:30 a.m. start time, checking their emails with convenience store food in hand. Weaving through the fifty-plus desks, she made a beeline for her workstation, sinking her Tod’s pumps—polished by her mother until they perfectly reflected the contours of the fluorescent lights—alternately into the carpet. Half the staff in the Food Products Sales Division were full-time employees, but among them, the only women were Eriko and her senior of ten years, Chief Sayako Yotsuba. In contrast, the temp and contract workers were entirely female, and it was a given that they would show up right before the start of the workday.

The convenience store bag holding today’s breakfast and her copy of the *Nikkei* rustled as it brushed against her beige pleated skirt. Sitting at her desk, she booted up her computer. The startup chime resonated so low it vibrated at her feet, slowly rippling across the surrounding area in an invisible arc.

For this brief moment alone, she was permitted to think of nothing and do nothing. It was, perhaps, the most peaceful time of her day. She savored this modest freedom while waiting for her meticulously organized desktop to appear. Precisely because she had long since surpassed an annual income of ten million yen, Eriko could state with absolute confidence that the single most valuable thing in this world was "time." With time, almost anything could be managed. Because everyone spent their days rushing about, we lived in an age where anything—a proposal, a product, a work of art—was highly valued simply for having had ample time spent on it. Proper dining etiquette, a letter of three pages or more beginning with a gentle seasonal greeting, light makeup that looked like bare skin, and well-cared-for leather goods were considered wonderful out of respect for the time invested in them.

To make space for her breakfast, she first pulled out an alcohol wipe and wiped down her desk until it squeaked. She carefully wiped the valleys of her computer keyboard and the mouthpiece of her phone.

Even if she were teased for being neurotic, if she didn't do at least this much, she couldn't feel that this sliver of such a vast space was truly her own territory. She tore into the mountain of routed documents, scanning them rapidly one by one. The work progressed at several times the speed it would during working hours, when people were constantly coming and going and the phones rang incessantly. Time could be manufactured with a little ingenuity. That was the very first lesson she had learned from her direct supervisor when she entered the working world. A few minutes later, as the mountain of paperwork finally vanished, Eriko pulled the convenience store bag to the center of her desk with the urge to rub her hands together in anticipation.

A paper carton of café au lait. Then, she pulled out a packaged sweet bun, a new product called "Karinto Melon Pan." She had checked two convenience stores in her neighborhood this morning, but both were sold out; it was a precious item she had finally tracked down at a shop inside the subway station. Even knowing full well it couldn't possibly contain anything healthy, she couldn't help flipping the package over to check the ingredients—an occupational hazard, perhaps.

Mitsuzaki, a major commercial bakery, was a client of the grain division. Artificial sweeteners, preservatives, food coloring. Even at her age, she still felt an almost transgressive joy in secretly eating the kind of food her health-conscious mother would despise. All this for a mere ninety-eight yen. Back in middle and high school, some of her classmates used to buy sweet buns like this at the school store to fill their stomachs. She had been grateful for her mother's homemade lunches, of course, but Eriko couldn't help being intensely envious of those big, garishly colored buns. She felt as though the bodies of her peers, so easily satisfied by these carefree snacks, must harbor equally carefree, uninhibited inner lives.

Expanding her long list of bookmarked sites, she hovered the cursor over the URL of her favorite blog, and with a click, plunged into a time belonging solely to herself and the blogger. Following the text with her eyes, she bit into the *melon pan*, which was as big as her own face. The cookie crust on the surface crumbled with a crunch, and the flavors of butter, brown sugar, and melon came flooding out. It was exactly as she had said. The balance of saltiness and sweetness wasn't bad, but it tasted undeniably cheap. Even so, savored alongside the words flowing across the LCD screen, it tasted so delicious it seemed to seep right through her.

*The idea of mashing up karinto and melon pan is just stupid, isn't it? I found it at a convenience store and cracked up. But chew on that greasy, crunchy dough with its faint hint of mitarashi-style soy sauce, and a refreshing melon aroma opens up like you've just emerged from a tunnel. I'm addicted to it right now. Stupid flavor. Stupid price. Getting lunch over with via a "stupid meal" like this makes life so much easier. There's a comforting sense of just coasting through life. Not that I recommend it, though.*

*Coasting through life.* What a great phrase, she thought. Because for Eriko, life had always been something to face head-on and grapple with. As she sat there in a daze, feeling a faint sense of accomplishment alongside the heavy weight of stale oil spreading in her chest, a voice rained down from above.

"Eating like a high school girl, huh, Shimura? You'll get fat."

Regardless of the situation, making remarks that forced the listener to first check themselves for any misstep was the signature style of her colleague from the seafood team, Yasuyuki Sugishita. While you were instinctively second-guessing yourself, he would push ahead at his own pace. This attitude seemed to deflect even criticism; though he was no perfectionist, she had never once seen him reprimanded or cautioned by a boss. In a sales department dominated by athletic types, Sugishita stood out with his skinny frame, his handsome but somewhat long face, his pointed chin, and his thin, pale lips. Because of his unusual background of having studied architecture in graduate school, he was two years older than her, despite joining the company in the same cohort.

"Morning. Oh, this bun? It was featured on a blog. I finally managed to get my hands on it."

Disappointed that her time alone had been cut short sooner than expected, Eriko instinctively pointed at her computer screen. Sugishita rested his hands on her desk and leaned right in. *Oh, he's close.* The thought crossed her mind, but she didn't feel the need to pull away. The sharp scent of his favorite hair product stung her nose.

"Huh, a housewife blog? What's so interesting about that for a single woman like you, Shimura?"

*Ohyou’s Hopeless Housewife Diary* was a blog she had been reading almost daily for the past two years. The homepage had a bare-bones design. The posts were long texts with few line breaks. The occasionally attached photos were taken on a cell phone and were, to put it kindly, nothing special. And yet, it consistently ranked in the top thirty of a certain site's popular housewife blog rankings. Perhaps its defining feature was the absence of the "haters" that usually came with the territory on housewife blogs.

"I know, but she’s not overly 'housewife-y.' She has this exquisitely laid-back vibe. She's pretty sloppy about her diet and chores, but strangely enough, she maintains a sense of cleanliness—or moderation, I guess. Plus, we're the same age, so she feels really relatable."

"Ohyou" lived in a Tokyo apartment with her husband, a supermarket manager. They didn't seem to have any plans for kids at the moment. They didn't appear particularly wealthy, yet there was no sign she worked a part-time job. Despite being a full-time housewife, she cooked only when the mood struck. She often met up with her husband at a family restaurant or a conveyor-belt sushi place for dinner. Her husband never criticized this, and their relationship seemed excellent. Yet she never went out of her way to flaunt how well they got along. Eriko also loved her sense of humor in calling her husband "the Demon Lord" instead of "hubby" or by his name.

*I want kids, but we never even make it to the baby-making stage! Once sex gets going it's fine, but the lead-up to it is just such a hassle, isn't it? Hey, Demon Lord, are you reading this?*

She would casually drop risqué remarks like that. 

It was incredibly refreshing when she wrote, flat out, that her personal policy was to never collect loyalty points or use coupons at supermarkets and drugstores. 

*They're stealing our precious time (angry). Even if you diligently rack them up, in the end it's for what, a measly 500-yen discount? When you think about the hassle of digging through your wallet to fish out a loyalty card every single time you're at the register, it's so much more liberating to just scrap the whole thing. If you added up all the time spent on that hassle over a lifetime, it'd probably come to like ten hours. We're not exactly rich or anything, so we might be losing out big time, but it's just such a pain, so whatever~~. I took the plunge and tossed them, and wow, my wallet is ridiculously light! So breezy~*

Eriko had always hated the way her mother’s wallet bulged with loyalty cards and coupons. Her father had become the president of a Nakamaru Trading subsidiary quite early on, and despite their comfortable lifestyle, it saddened her to see her mother playing right into the hands of retailers just to save a measly hundred or two hundred yen. At first glance, her mother seemed to march to the beat of her own drum, but in reality, she was a pushover who struggled to assert herself. She was the type who could never bring herself to throw away a gift, holding on to things forever. Perhaps it was all the more irritating because Eriko recognized that same trait in herself. Ohyou probably knew instinctively that time was more valuable than money. That was why she could live her life freely, unbound by anything.

"What’s so interesting about a diary like this? It has nothing to do with your life. It’s so painfully domestic."

Slightly stung, for some reason, by Sugishita’s total lack of reaction, Eriko spoke a little faster.

"I like how down-to-earth she is. I love that it doesn’t feel forced. I just find her cute, somehow. And I like the way time seems to flow so fully and slowly for her."

"Are you just amusing yourself by making fun of some bored housewife? God, women are awful. Always needing to find someone beneath them to feel secure."

Eriko was used to male employees talking like this, but that didn't mean it didn't strike a nerve. When she had first joined the company, she had been shocked by how easily misogyny went unquestioned in the office, but she had long since given up on changing it. Just let it slide, and don't pick a fight over every little thing. They bore no malice—they were surprisingly thoughtless—so taking them seriously would only be a waste. If she got angry, she would be the one to end up looking foolish. With her father's backing, as a full-time employee, as long as Eriko worked just as hard as the men while never forgetting to stay more impeccably groomed than anyone else, she would be treated as an equal. There was no point in getting irritated over trivial, everyday remarks. She tried not to let her good-humored demeanor slip.

"Hmm, no, it’s not like that. Usually, housewife blogs have a tendency to show off how happy they are, how well they keep house, or how good they are at cooking. They have this overwhelming need to be understood. But she’s…"

The phone on her desk rang, cutting their conversation short. She picked up the receiver; it was a tuna supplier in California. Calls from the US always concentrated around this time of day.

"May I have your name, please? When shall I have him return your call?"

After informing the caller that the person in charge was currently away from his desk, Eriko turned back to Sugishita.

It was frustrating not to be able to explain it well. She loved Ohyou's seemingly scatterbrained yet sharp way of thinking. Her words washed pleasantly through Eriko's chest. Though her phrasing was gentle, it exuded intelligence, and she absolutely never hurt anyone. She lived an utterly indolent life, yet she wasn't ashamed of it, nor did she try to force any meaning onto it. She showed no hint of anxiety over not having children. Above all, her writing was full of memorable turns of phrase and casual ideas that made you want to emulate them.

For example, on humid days, she said she did her chores in a swimsuit, then pulled a dress over her head and went straight to jump into the local ward pool. At the library, she exclusively borrowed books by Sen Ishida. Just to read the complete set of Kiriko Kubo's *Buckets of Rice*, she frequented a half-hearted neighborhood café clearly run as a tax write-off, purposely ordering the most time-consuming items on the menu. She and her husband went to karaoke and sang nothing but Western songs. They ordered pizza and watched whatever Hollywood movies happened to be playing on TV. She hopped between convenience stores in pursuit of Gari-Gari Kun Rich Corn Potage popsicles.

Eriko was drawn to the way Ohyou spent her days doing nothing in particular, not seeking to attach meaning to anything, making no plans, just living as freely as a cat. When was the last time she had spent a day like that? It was so long ago she could hardly remember. Just reading her blog helped unwind the nerves pulled taut by nightly business dinners and market research, if only a little.

Eriko always made a point of not wasting time, no matter the situation. Even away from work, she devoted her energy to beauty routines, research, and studying, and of course, she placed high value on rest and sleep. She was confident that all her actions were tied to some sort of benefit. She had no doubt that the woman she was today was superior to the woman she had been yesterday. And yet—perhaps starting around the time she turned thirty—there were moments when, at the slightest trigger, the back of her nose would sting. Sometimes she felt starved of oxygen, overcome by the sensation that even if she were to flail her arms, she wouldn't touch a single thing. She would take a deep breath, tell herself *I'm okay* over and over, and couldn't help but count off her strengths, her personal growth, and her contributions to the company, one by one. When she absolutely couldn't calm down, she would act unnecessarily humble to elicit someone's praise, and only then would she regain her composure. Still, she realized that no matter how much she struggled, every time a day ended or a new morning arrived, her possibilities had worn away just a little more, and course correction was becoming increasingly impossible. Was this an anxiety born of age? Would it be something she simply got used to in a few more years? When she caught a hint of a similar anxiety in another woman during an unguarded moment, she couldn't stop a sense of relief and twisted joy from seeping out.

She could sense no such anxiety in Ohyou.

Above all else—what drew her in was that the blog featured the woman's husband almost exclusively, and female friends were completely absent. It was a conspicuous anomaly among housewife blogs, which so often flaunted the writers' social networks. Eriko secretly hoped that Ohyou might know the secret to expertly taming the same kind of hang-ups she herself harbored.

Eriko found comfort in the fact that even a woman like her had no friends. Even with a character so miraculously balanced that it never caused anyone discomfort, wasn't it possible to end up without friends simply due to a slight misalignment of timing? Perhaps lacking female friends wasn't such a shameful defect. Perhaps it wasn't a defect to begin with, but merely one variation of human relationships.

*I'm a thirty-year-old housewife living in a run-down Tokyo apartment with my husband. My absolute favorite food is the engawa at Nikkori Zushi, the 98-yen conveyor-belt sushi place in my neighborhood.*

Sugishita, who had looked entirely uninterested, finally leaned forward after reading Ohyou's profile.

"Huh, so her favorite food is conveyor-belt sushi *engawa*. If she knows that the *engawa* at those dirt-cheap sushi places is actually halibut—*ohyou*—and not flounder, and she picked this handle because of that, it's a pretty sharp piece of irony."

Halibut can grow to over two hundred kilograms and nearly three meters in length, making the *engawa* harvested from them massive. Because they thrash about violently when pulled from the water, fishermen in Alaska apparently use shotguns to finish them off. Eriko felt as if she were the one being praised, and had the urge to puff out her chest.

"Come to think of it, Sugishita-kun. You were in charge of Russian halibut last year, weren't you? Didn't you also do business with Center Village Holdings?"

That major restaurant chain had Nikkori Zushi under its umbrella. At Sugishita's smirk, Eriko impulsively clapped her hands together.

"Wow... That's amazing. It all connects. Ohyou-san's favorite food is your line of work, Sugishita-kun."

Struck by a sense of wonder, Eriko looked around the office, where the two of them were alone. As they tracked the vast web of numbers and commodities, the world would suddenly feel small, and moments like this—when it felt like she could grasp it right in her own hands—were what she loved more than anything. Before she knew it, the sun was high in the sky, and the slanting rays of late August were streaming in. The carpet, its pile brushed up white from frequent cleaning, began to give off a sun-baked scent.

Working at a trading company, one would sometimes catch glimpses of unexpected depth in everyday scenes. Take this café au lait, this bread, this newspaper. In what countries were their raw materials born and raised? How were they distributed, processed, and brought to exist right here and now? If you narrowed your eyes, it was as if their individual stories spread out before you. Both Eriko and Sugishita possessed this sort of clairvoyance.

Japan's seafood consumption is approximately 6.52 million tons. It relies on imports for forty percent of that. Among these are countless "nameless" fish—species treated as substitutes or passed off as something else entirely. Since being assigned to the seafood team, Eriko had become adept at seeing right through whatever label was on a processed fish, instantly deducing its true identity.

"It does make you feel a little guilty toward the consumers. I mean, *engawa* is just the name of the cut, so it's not like we're deceiving anyone, but still."

"But if you think about it objectively, there's no way you could get genuine flounder or turbot *engawa* for under a hundred yen. The consumers must have a sneaking suspicion themselves."

"At this point, they're less like edible fish and more like little aliens. It's scary."

"Since people are perfectly happy eating those alien nigiri and talking about how delicious they are, everyone wins, right? The 'fish' in a fish burger, the 'white fish' in a white fish meunière—if you start digging into exactly what kind of fish it is and where it came from, there'd be no end to it. There really is a lot in this world you're better off not knowing."

Sugishita might have spoken with this sort of cynical detachment, but when it came to actual business negotiations, he was surprisingly charming and spoke with remarkable smoothness. He gave the impression of being high-strung, but he was actually the son of a family that had run a kimono shop in Kawagoe for three generations.

"Speaking of which, Shimura, you're the one in charge of Nile perch starting this month, aren't you? If this woman loves conveyor-belt sushi and family restaurants that much, won't the fish you deal end up in her mouth sooner or later?"

Eriko flipped through the reference files she had inherited from her predecessor. The Nile perch is a freshwater fish belonging to the order Perciformes, family Latidae, genus Lates. It can reach up to two meters in length and weigh two hundred kilograms. Native to the African continent, it inhabits lakes and rivers. It is a carnivorous fish possessing a ferocity unimaginable from its mild, clean flavor. In fact, after being introduced into Africa's Lake Victoria, it drove more than two hundred endemic species of small cichlids (fish of the family Cichlidae) to extinction. Its mild, clean taste was easily embraced in Japan, and it was even marketed as "sea bass" or "white sea bass" during the nineties. Recently, however, ninety percent of the catch is exported to the West, making it a rare sight here. Eriko had spent the past few months working to reopen distribution channels and upgrade the local facilities. The more she learned about the Nile perch, the more helplessly she found herself drawn to it.

"Nile perch just have this incredible life force, you know."

"Yikes, they're carnivorous, right? Who knows what they're eating. Scary, scary. Oh, right. Apparently you should watch this documentary called *Darwin's Nightmare*. I hear it shows how the Nile perch completely wrecked the ecosystem."

"I've been meaning to watch it eventually, but I don't have the time. Besides, it might not be the kind of information I want floating around in my head until this next deal is closed... You know, if they hadn't been released into the lake by human hands, Nile perch might have lived their whole lives without ever realizing how ferocious they are. It's kind of sad. Oh, but apparently they're popular as aquarium fish, too. They're silver, and really quite beautiful. They have this rather melancholy look about them..."

"They have them at that new aquarium in Shinjuku, right? Want to go together sometime?"

Eriko offered neither a yes nor a no to this muttered invitation, pretending instead to be engrossed in the blog once more. She was perfectly aware that Sugishita harbored a vague attraction toward her. He seemed to have imposed a rule upon himself that putting it into explicit words meant admitting defeat, resulting in a pained expression that was an awkward mix of yearning and embarrassment. His habit of picking fights with her was surely just his way of showing affection. Perhaps unwilling to admit he had been brushed off, he hastily steered the conversation back to its previous course.

"Well, keeping it to just reading the blog is probably for the best. It's not like things would go well if you actually met."

"That's not true at all. I have a feeling Ohyou-san and I could be good friends."

"Give me a break. You don't even have any female friends."

Eriko felt her fingertips instantly go cold. Despite all her efforts, despite focusing every ounce of her nervous energy on that single point, she still "didn't look like she had female friends." Her heart pounded loudly at this unshakeable truth. In stark contrast to her shock, Sugishita's words carried a sticky, vaguely sexual undertone. Men were faintly aroused by the sight of a woman who couldn't fit into the herd of her own sex. He even exuded a certain pride, as if he had discovered a fatal flaw in a rival faction without having to get his own hands dirty. Feigning ignorance, she made sure her voice sounded utterly carefree.

"Well, you're not wrong. I've been a bit out of touch with my college group ever since we started working. And even when I get invited to a girls' night out, I never have the time to go."

Accepting a point momentarily to maintain the upper hand was a tactic she'd picked up in business. In reality, no such group of friends existed, nor had she ever been invited to a girls' night out. Grown women shamelessly referring to themselves as "girls"—there were times when Eriko found that sheer brazenness and immaturity blinding.

"Besides, you never go to lunch with the temps or the people from our hiring class in other departments, Shimura."

He never said it outright, but it seemed Sugishita was secretly dating one of the temp workers, Maori Takasugi. That might explain why he was so unusually well-versed in the social dynamics of the female employees.

"Oh, that. My schedule never lines up with the others from our year. And having lunch with the temps involves a lot of tiptoeing around. A lot of them want to keep it under a thousand yen, so you can't just casually invite them out."

She could hardly admit that it was actually because she wasn't invited, so Eriko made sure to keep her tone bright.

"It's true I've probably always been bad at socializing with other girls. I mean, I know I shouldn't say this about myself, but I'm kind of the high-strung, honor-student type, right? I was always getting saddled with being the class rep or the group leader, and before I knew it, I was all alone. I never really understood those invisible rules girls have, so I think I end up getting isolated without even realizing it."

She realized she was talking surprisingly fast. She silently prayed. *Please, don't ask anything else.* Anything beyond this was uncharted territory. She had no confidence she could keep from slipping up.

"Well, I guess a beauty like you who can do anything without any effort is bound to be envied. Women are so deeply jealous, it's scary."

Sugishita's eyes narrowed with fondness. Did he think he was complimenting her? He was probably comparing her to Maori Takasugi. Incompetent, chatty, plump, and sagging with excess fat—that was Maori Takasugi. She was most likely just being toyed with by Sugishita. Yet Maori was always surrounded by the other female employees, and Eriko envied her for it.

*Without any effort.* Sugishita had surely never considered how much time and money went into this flawless skin and glossy hair. Eriko suddenly began to wonder what kind of skin toner Ohyou used. Was it some cheap brand sold at bargain prices in a drugstore? Or perhaps, surprisingly, she was an avid user of organic cosmetics. Eriko wanted to use the same one. As she searched through old diary entries, her lips began to move.

"Anyway, it seems like this Ohyou-san lives in my neighborhood."

"Wait. Seriously?"

"Look, this organic cafe 'Giselle' she mentions here. It's one stop away, but I know it well because my mom was involved in setting it up a long time ago. For that matter, all of Ohyou-san's favorite fast-food joints and chain restaurants are clustered right by my local station. Well, they're the sort of places you can find anywhere, but still."

"Getting so familiar with those kinds of details is a little off-putting. I mean, from her perspective, you seem like a stalker, right?"

Startled by a word that had never once crossed her mind, Eriko turned around and stared hard at Sugishita.

"What? What are you talking about? I'm just reading her blog! I just happened to notice that we might live close to each other, that's all."

"But you're copying the diet of this Ohyou-san, checking in on her daily life, and you live in the same neighborhood. Even if you don't mean any harm, from her point of view, it's a bit creepy. You should dial it back."

He cut his words short, turned on his heel, and headed back to his desk. Employees were starting to trickle in. He had probably come to work this early to avoid Maori Takasugi’s prying eyes and steal some time alone with her. Was it conceited to think so? A year had passed since she broke up with her ex-boyfriend, but she didn't feel lonely yet. She wanted to get married someday, but right now, just shuttling between home and the office was all she could manage.

Did Ohyou-san ever imagine an office worker reading her blog in the early morning office, nibbling on the exact same kind of bread? She had a premonition that Ohyou-san must, somewhere out there, sense her existence. At first glance, Ohyou-san feigned a nonchalant air, but perhaps she was weaving a message for a great many people into her posts: *You're fine just the way you are. Take it easy. It's okay to be alone.*

As if cherishing another version of herself in the mirror, Eriko meticulously reread Ohyou's diary entries.

Shoko Maruo stopped typing her blog update on her cell phone and looked up.

*That laundry looks like it's going to fall*, she thought, watching a pair of jeans flap on the balcony of the apartment building across the way. They must belong to a young girl; there was no way Shoko, now thirty, could pull off skinny jeans like that. Back in the day, she used to stand on the department store floor wearing pants so tight they cut off her circulation. Being on her feet for over ten hours a day was the norm. Sometimes she only got two days off a month. What would her younger self think if she saw her life now? She'd probably be surprisingly envious.

In any case, she might have arrived a bit too early. The organic cafe Giselle, a fifteen-minute walk from her apartment, was a favorite spot of hers. Perhaps because it was Saturday, she noticed a few other solitary female customers just like her. She swiftly popped the remaining food on her large plate into her mouth—konjac and chicken liver tossed in mustard mayonnaise, bitter melon coleslaw, brown rice onigiri with sesame and red shiso, and a rolled omelet with sea lettuce—polishing it all off with a speed that appalled even herself. It had been a long time since she had eaten such a vegetable-rich, balanced meal, and she hadn't made anything like it for Kensuke, either. Once again, she felt like a failure as a housewife. Tonight, she should at least simmer some hijiki seaweed. No... just the thought of it was a hassle. Maybe she should get something to go from here instead. She didn't want to wash dishes. Shoko hated washing dishes more than anything else.

She had met Kensuke—the manager of a supermarket specializing in imported foods—through a part-time job, and they got married three years ago. She generally tried to be frugal so they could get by on Kensuke's salary alone, but she hated the idea of letting that mindset restrict her life. A little splurge here and there was no big deal if she just dipped into her savings from her single days. Lately, she had also been making a small income from affiliate ads on her blog. No wedding ceremony, no rings, no trips abroad. And, naturally, no buying a house. In exchange for forgoing major luxuries, they didn't penny-pinch on the little things. Rather than fighting over washing the dishes, they would choose to eat out, even if it was just at a cheap diner. That was the promise they had made to each other when they registered their marriage.

Normally, she would have been perfectly fine going to a Doutor or a family restaurant today, but since the person she was meeting was a media type, she had specified the most stylish café she knew. The wood-toned interior always put her at ease, no matter when she visited. Hashimoto-kun, a part-timer who had come over to clear her plate, suddenly leaned in.

"Shoko-saaan. Thanks for featuring our new autumn menu on your blog."

"Whoa, too close, too close. You scared me, popping up out of nowhere like that."

His breath tickled her ear. Twenty-five-year-old Hashimoto-kun was the same age as her younger brother, which made him easy to talk to. She gave him a playful swat, and through his shirt, she felt firm biceps that completely belied his lanky frame. The firmness that seemed to bounce her hand right back was a far cry from the flabby upper arms of her husband, who had recently started putting on weight again.

"On the days you write about us on your blog, we get a few more customers than usual."

For Shoko, who preferred family restaurants and fast food, "Giselle" was one of the few independently owned establishments she frequented. Ever since working a short-term, New Year's gig there earlier this year, she had gotten to know the staff, and even after quitting, she still came by as a customer once or twice a month. She had turned down their offer to take her on as a contract employee. If the café became her workplace, the interpersonal relationships would inevitably become a hassle, and it was obvious she'd no longer be able to drop in casually like this.

Shoko dropped her gaze back to her phone. Time flew by just scrolling through the comments on her blog. Hardly any of them condemned her lack of discipline. If anything, the vast majority were supportive comments like, *This puts me at ease*, and *It's encouraging to know it's okay to be like this*. Many readers even shared links to shops and products they recommended. She was glad to have finally found a place where she belonged, but receiving nothing but such kind words sometimes made her feel like she was deceiving her readers. Even so, the comfort of those positive remarks was addictive, and she would often find herself rereading the comments for hours on end. It felt a lot like soaking in a lukewarm carbonated bath at a super sento. When there was so little stress, you lost track of when it was time to get out.

Not just online, she wanted to secure as many places where she could feel at ease and people she could share a smile with as possible. Eight years since moving to Tokyo, and she still couldn't get used to the city. Back when she was working, she was too busy to have any free time, and ever since her health declined, she had become a complete homebody. The moment the doorbell chimed, a woman in her forties wearing a suit appeared. She immediately noticed Shoko and strode purposefully over.

"Ohyou-san...? Are you Shoko Maruo? It's a pleasure to meet you. I'm Satoko Hanai from the Third Book Editorial Department at Shumeisha."

Shoko hurriedly stood up, bowed her head, and accepted the business card. Satoko Hanai had well-defined features and a fairly good figure, but her dry skin and the prominent dark circles under her eyes gave her an undeniably exhausted look.

"Oh, I'm just surprised. You're much cuter and more charming than I imagined, Ohyou-san."

She stopped short of calling her a "beauty"—in that, Shoko saw the subtle caution of the elite. *I'm being sized up.* She could feel a long-dormant sensor springing to life. A washed-out T-shirt, a pair of chinos Kensuke used to wear back when he was skinny, and no makeup. She knew well enough that this wasn't an appropriate outfit for a first meeting. But ever since she quit her job at the apparel company, her interest in clothes—which she used to buy so obsessively—had completely faded. Besides, she didn't have a striking appearance to begin with. Her figure was flat, she had monolids, and freckles stood out on her pale skin. Her lips curled slightly outward, so her gums showed when she smiled—something she had always been self-conscious about.

Yet, for some reason, a certain subset of women—as if by prior agreement—absolutely despised Shoko.

—*She plays up to men.*

That was their claim. But she wasn't playing up to anyone. It was simply that the basic consideration and care women typically extended to other women came effortlessly to her when she was dealing with men. When faced with another woman, there were plenty of moments when she had no idea what to say, but with the opposite sex, she instinctively knew what to do. Perhaps it was because her parents had divorced and she had been raised by her father, growing up surrounded by brothers. She was by no means a calculating person, nor did she have all that much romantic experience. It felt completely unjust to be judged that way simply because she felt at ease around the opposite sex. Out of caution, she had taken to acting more unrefined than necessary whenever she was around other women.

Satoko Hanai ordered a coffee, then lined up several housewife magazines and housewife blog compilations published by Shumeisha on the table, and began explaining this and that. To be completely honest, Shoko wasn't particularly enthusiastic. She had always been bad at handling expectations. Just reading the stifling, feverish prose of those housewife bloggers flaunting their happiness was exhausting. It was in her nature to avoid anything bothersome at all costs. Paying no mind to Shoko's lack of interest, Satoko continued to rattle on at a rapid clip.

"Nowadays, housewife bloggers occupy a variety of niches, but your selling point, Ohyou-san, is your 'authenticity.' It's so wonderful that there's no 'you must be this way' or 'you ought to be that way' in your writing. As diverse values become more accepted, many housewives find themselves feeling adrift. But it's not just housewives—I'm sure many people will relate to your unwavering commitment to going at your own pace. You aren't preachy, and I believe you have the kind of personality that single working women won't find off-putting, either."

"I suppose... But even if a diary like that were turned into a book, who would read it...?"

"Exactly, which is why I suggest adapting your diary entries and polishing them into a collection of essays. I would like you to write a lifestyle guide from the perspective of a relatable woman who lives life without trying too hard."
