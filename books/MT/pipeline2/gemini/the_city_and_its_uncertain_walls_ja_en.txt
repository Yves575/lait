You told me about that city.

That summer evening, we walked upstream along the river, breathing in the scent of sweet grass. We climbed past a few small check dams that formed little waterfalls, stopping now and then to watch the slender silver fish swimming in the pools. We had both been barefoot for some time. The clear water washed coolly over our ankles, and the fine sand of the riverbed enveloped our feet—like soft clouds in a dream. I was seventeen, and you were a year younger.

You had casually stuffed your low-heeled red sandals into a yellow vinyl shoulder bag and kept walking just ahead of me, from sandbar to sandbar. Wet blades of grass clung to your damp calves, like lovely green punctuation marks. I carried my worn-out white sneakers in my hands.

As if tired of walking, you casually drop down into the summer grass and look up at the sky in silence. Two small birds dart side by side across the sky above, crying out sharply. In the silence, the premonition of blue dusk begins to envelop us. Sitting down beside you, I feel a strange sensation. It is as if thousands of invisible threads are delicately binding your body to my heart. Even the momentary flutter of your eyelids, or the faintest trembling of your lips, stirs my heart.

At such an hour, neither you nor I have a name. The summer twilight of a seventeen-year-old and a sixteen-year-old, the vivid feelings on the grass by the riverbank—that is all there is. Soon the stars will gradually begin to twinkle above our heads, but the stars have no names, either. We sit side by side on the grass by the riverbank, in a world without names.

"The city is surrounded by a high wall," you begin. You draw the words from the depths of the silence. Like a pearl diver plunging into the deep sea with nothing but their own body. "It's not a very large city. But it's not so small that you can easily take everything in at a glance, either."

This is the second time you have spoken of the city. And in that way, the city gained the high wall that surrounded it.

As you continue to speak, the city acquires a beautiful river and three stone bridges (East Bridge, Old Bridge, and West Bridge); it gains a library and watchtowers, an abandoned foundry and modest communal housing. In the pale light of approaching summer dusk, you and I gaze out at the city, shoulders nearly touching. Sometimes squinting from a distant hilltop, other times with our eyes wide open from close enough to reach out and touch it.

"The real me is alive and living inside that city surrounded by a high wall," you say.

"So the you in front of me right now isn't the real you," I ask, naturally enough.

"Right. The me here right now isn't the real me. Just a stand-in. Like a fleeting shadow."

I think about this. *Like a fleeting shadow?* But I decide to withhold my opinion for now.

"And what does the real you do in that city?"

"I work at the library," you answer in a quiet voice. "My working hours are from around five in the evening to around ten at night."

"Around?"

"All time there is approximate. There's a tall clock tower in the central square, but it has no hands."

I picture a clock tower without hands. "And can anyone go into that library?"

"No. People can't just freely walk in. You need a special qualification to enter. But you can go in. Because you have that qualification."

"A special qualification—what kind of qualification is that?"

You smile softly. But you do not answer the question.

"But as long as I go there, I'll be able to see the real you?"

"If you can find the city. And if..."

You fall silent then, your cheeks flushing faintly. But I can hear the words you left unspoken.

*And if you truly want the real me...* That was what you dared not say at the time. I gently slip my arm around your shoulders. You are wearing a pale green sleeveless dress. Your cheek rests against my shoulder. But in that summer twilight, it is not the real you whose shoulders I hold. Just as you say, it is nothing more than a shadow, your stand-in.

The real you is in a city surrounded by a high wall. There is a beautiful sandbar lush with willows, several low hills, and quiet, single-horned beasts everywhere. The people live in old communal housing, leading simple but unconstrained lives. The beasts love to eat the leaves and nuts from the trees growing in the city, but during the long, snowbound winter, many of them lose their lives to the cold and starvation.

How strongly I wished to enter that city. To see the real you there.

"The city is surrounded by a high wall, and getting inside is very difficult," you say. "Leaving is even harder."

"How can I get in?"

"You just have to wish for it. But wishing for something from the bottom of your heart isn't so simple. It might take time. In the meantime, you might have to discard various things. Things that are precious to you. But don't give up. No matter how long it takes, the city will not vanish."

I imagine meeting the real you in that city. I picture the vast, beautifully lush apple woods outside the city, the three stone bridges spanning the river, and the voice of an unseen night bird. And the small, old library where the real you works.

"A place is always kept ready for you there," you say.

"A place for me?"

"Yes. There is only one open position in the city. And you are going to fill it."

What kind of position could that be?

"You will be a *Dream Reader*," you say in a hushed voice. As if confiding an important secret.

Hearing this, I can't help but laugh. "Listen, I can barely even remember my own dreams. It would be pretty hard for someone like me to be a *Dream Reader*."

"No, a *Dream Reader* doesn't need to dream their own dreams. All you have to do is sit in the library stacks and read the many *Old Dreams* gathered there. But it's not something just anyone can do."

"But I can do it?"

You nod. "Yes, you can do it. You possess the qualification. And the me who is there will help you with your work. By your side every night."

"I am a *Dream Reader*, reading countless *Old Dreams* every night in the stacks of the city library. And you are always by my side. The real you," I say, repeating the presented facts aloud.

In my arms, your bare shoulders tremble slightly in the green dress. Then they suddenly stiffen. "That's right. But there is one thing I want you to remember. Even if I meet you in that city, the me who is there won't remember a single thing about you."

*Why?*

"Don't you know why?"

I do know. Yes, the girl whose shoulders I am holding so gently right now is nothing more than a stand-in. The real you lives in that city. In a mysterious, far-off city surrounded by a high wall.

And yet your shoulders in my hands are so smooth and warm, I can only think of them as the shoulders of the real you.

In this actual world, you and I live a little ways apart. Not terribly far, but not close enough to go see each other on a whim. If I change trains twice and travel for about an hour and a half, I can reach the city where you live. And neither of the cities we live in is surrounded by a high wall. So, of course, we are free to come and go.

I live in a quiet suburban residential area near the sea, and you live in the center of a much larger, bustling city. That summer, I am a third-year high school student, and you are a second-year. I attend a local public high school, and you go to a private girls' school in your city. Due to various circumstances, we only actually see each other once or twice a month, something like that. We more or less take turns: I visit the city where you live, and you come to the town where I live. When I visit your city, we go to a small park near your house, or to the public botanical garden. You have to pay an admission fee to get into the botanical garden, but there is a cafe next to the greenhouse that is never too crowded, and it becomes our favorite spot. There, we can order coffee and apple tarts (a minor luxury) and lose ourselves in quiet conversation, just the two of us.

When you visit my town, we almost always take a walk by the river or the sea. Since there are no rivers flowing near your house in the middle of the city, and of course no sea either, the first thing you want to see when you come to my town is the river or the sea. That sheer mass of natural water—you are drawn to it.

"Looking at water calms me down for some reason," you say. "I like listening to the sound it makes."

We had met by chance last autumn, and had been seeing each other closely for about eight months. Whenever we meet, we hold each other in the most secluded places we can find, and softly press our lips together. But our relationship goes no further than that. One reason is a lack of time. There is also the practical matter of not being able to find a suitable place to pursue a deeper, more intimate relationship. But more than that, the main reason is likely that we are simply so engrossed in our private conversations, losing ourselves in talk and begrudging every passing moment. Neither you nor I had ever met anyone with whom we could express our true thoughts and feelings so freely and naturally. Finding someone like that feels like nothing short of a miracle. So every time we see each other, once or twice a month, we just talk, forgetting the passage of time. No matter how long we speak, we never run out of things to say, and when the time comes to part and we say our goodbyes at the station ticket gates, we always feel as though we have forgotten to discuss so many important things.

Of course, it is not that I lack physical desires. There is no way a healthy seventeen-year-old boy would not be driven by sexual desire when faced with a sixteen-year-old girl with a beautiful swelling of breasts—let alone when wrapping his arms around her supple body. But I instinctively feel that all of that can wait until later. What I need right now is to see you once or twice a month, to take long walks together, and to talk frankly about all sorts of things. To intimately exchange information and get to know each other more deeply. And to hold each other in the shade of a tree somewhere, pressing our lips together—I do not want to hastily introduce any other elements into such a wonderful time. If I did, something precious there might be damaged, and we might never be able to go back to the way things were. *Let's save the physical things for later.* That is what I think. Or rather, what my intuition tells me.

But what on earth were we talking about, leaning our heads together back then? Now, I can no longer remember. We must have talked about so much that it has become impossible to pinpoint any single topic. However, once you started talking about the special city surrounded by a high wall, that came to occupy the greater part of our conversations.

Mostly, you recounted the origins of the city, I asked practical questions about it, and you provided the answers—through this process, the concrete details of the city were determined and recorded. The city was originally something you had created. Or perhaps it had existed inside you all along. But in building it up into something visible, something that could be described in words, I believe I played no small part. You spoke, and I wrote it down. Just as ancient philosophers and religious leaders were attended by their own loyal, meticulous scribes, or by people called apostles. As a capable scribe, or a faithful apostle, I even made a small, dedicated notebook just to record it all. That summer, the two of us were completely absorbed in this collaborative effort.

In autumn, the beasts' bodies are covered in brilliant golden fur in preparation for the coming cold season. The single horns growing from their foreheads are sharp and white. They wash their hooves in the chilly river water, gently stretch their necks to gorge on red berries, and chew on the leaves of broom shrubs.

It was a beautiful season.

Standing in the watchtower built along the wall, I wait for the evening horn. Shortly before sunset, the horn is sounded: one long blast, then three short ones. That is the rule. The soft sound of the horn slips through the lingering twilight of the cobblestone streets. The echoing call of the horn has likely been repeated, unchanged, for hundreds of years (or perhaps even longer). Its tone has seeped deeply into the crevices of the houses' stone walls, and into the stone statues lined up along the hedges of the square.

When the sound of the horn echoes across the city, the beasts raise their heads toward ancient memories. Some stop chewing leaves, some stop tapping their hooves against the pavement, and some wake from an afternoon nap in the last patches of sunlight, each lifting its head at the exact same angle.

For a moment, everything is frozen in place like a statue. The only movement is their soft golden fur swaying in the wind—that is all. Still, what on earth are they looking at? Necks turned in a single direction, staring fixedly into space, the beasts do not move a muscle. And so they listen intently to the sound of the horn.

When the final echo of the horn is swallowed by the air and vanishes, they rise to their feet, aligning their forelegs, or stretch and adjust their posture, before beginning to walk almost in unison. The momentary spell is broken, and for a while, the city streets are dominated by the sound of the beasts' stamping hooves.

The line of beasts makes its way along the winding cobblestone streets. No one leads the way, nor does anyone guide the procession. The beasts simply cast their eyes downward, their shoulders swaying in small, rhythmic motions as they travel down a river of silence. Even so, an undeniably intricate bond seems to connect each and every one of them.

After watching them several times, it becomes clear that their route and pace seem strictly predetermined. Gathering companions into the herd here and there, they cross the gently arching Old Bridge and walk to the square with the sharp spire (where, just as you said, the clock in the clock tower is missing both its hands). There, they pick up a small group that had gone down to the river's sandbar to graze on the green grass. Proceeding upstream along the riverside road, they pass through the factory district following a dried-up canal that stretches north, and gather another group that had been foraging for nuts in the woods. From there they turn west, pass beneath the covered walkway of the foundry, and climb a long flight of stairs up the northern hill.

The wall surrounding the city has only one gate. Opening and closing it is the duty of the Gatekeeper. It is a heavy, sturdy-looking gate, reinforced vertically and horizontally with thick iron plates. Yet the Gatekeeper pushes it open and shut with ease. No one else is permitted to touch the gate.

The Gatekeeper is a massive, robust man, yet deeply devoted to his work. His pointed head is cleanly shaven, his face entirely smooth. Every morning, he boils water in a large pot and meticulously shaves his head and face with a large, sharp razor. His age is impossible to guess. Sounding the horn morning and evening to gather the beasts is also among his duties. He climbs the watchtower in front of the gatehouse—a structure about two meters high—and blows his horn into the sky. From exactly where inside this rugged, almost vulgar-looking man does such a soft, lustrous sound emerge? I wonder this every time I hear the horn.

At dusk, once he has let every last beast out past the wall, he pushes the heavy gate closed once more and finally drops a massive padlock into place. With a dry, cold *clack*.

Outside the north gate lies a place for the beasts. There, the beasts sleep, mate, and give birth. It has woods and thickets, and a small stream runs through it. And this place, too, is enclosed by a wall. It is a low wall, just over a meter high, but for some reason the beasts cannot cross it. Or perhaps they simply choose not to.

Six watchtowers are set into the walls on either side of the gate. Anyone can climb up to them by way of old wooden spiral staircases. From the watchtowers, one has an unbroken view of the beasts' dwelling. But normally, no one bothers to go up there. It seems the city's residents have absolutely no interest in the lives of the beasts.

However, I am told that for just one week at the beginning of spring, people willingly climb the watchtowers on the wall to watch the beasts engage in fierce combat. During this time, the beasts become unimaginably savage compared to their usual selves; the males fight to the death over the females, forgetting even to eat. Growling, they try to drive their sharp, single horns into the throats and bellies of their rivals.

For that one week of the mating season alone, the beasts do not enter the city. The Gatekeeper shuts the gate tight to ensure the cityspeople are not put in danger (consequently, the morning and evening horns are not sounded during this period). A considerable number of beasts suffer deep wounds in the fighting, and some even lose their lives. And from the red blood spilled on the ground, a new order and new life are born. Just as the green branches of the willow trees burst into bud all at once in early spring.

The beasts live within their own unique cycle and order, one we could never hope to fathom. Everything repeats with regular precision, and their order is redeemed with their own blood. When that savage week passes and the soft April rains wash away the spilled blood, the beasts revert to their former tranquil, gentle existence.

But I have not actually witnessed such a sight with my own eyes. I merely heard about it from you.

In autumn, the beasts remain crouched in their respective places, their golden coats glowing in the setting sun, silently waiting for the sound of the horn to be absorbed into the air. Their number is likely no fewer than a thousand.

And so a day in the city comes to an end. Days pass, and seasons shift. Yet days and seasons are ultimately ephemeral. The true time of the city lies elsewhere.

Neither you nor I visit the other's home. We do not meet each other's families, nor do we introduce our respective friends. In short, we do not want to be disturbed by anyone—by anyone at all in this entire world. You and I are perfectly content simply spending time together, and we have no desire to add anything else. Besides, from a purely physical standpoint, there is no room to add anything. As I mentioned before, we have a mountain of things to talk about, and the time we have together is limited.

You hardly ever talk about your family. What I know about your family amounts to just a few fragmented facts. Your father was a local civil servant, but when you were eleven, he made some kind of blunder that forced him to resign, and now he works as a clerk at a cram school. I don't know what kind of "blunder" it was. But apparently, it was the sort of incident you prefer not to talk about. Your biological mother passed away from internal cancer when you were three. So you have almost no memory of her. You can't even remember her face. When you were five, your father remarried, and your younger sister was born the following year. So your current mother is actually your stepmother, and you only once mentioned something to the effect that you "might still feel a little more affinity" toward her than toward your father. Like an offhand footnote printed in tiny type in the corner of a page. As for your sister, six years your junior, I never gleaned any information beyond the fact that, "My sister is allergic to cat hair, so we can't keep a cat at our house."

When you were a child, the only person you could naturally and genuinely feel close to was your maternal grandmother. Whenever you had the chance, you would take the train alone to visit her house in the neighboring ward. During school holidays, you would sometimes stay over for a few days. Your grandmother doted on you unconditionally. She would even buy you all sorts of little things out of her meager income. But every time you went to see her, you noticed a look of displeasure surface on your stepmother's face, and though nothing was ever explicitly said to you, your visits to your grandmother's house gradually tapered off. That grandmother, too, passed away suddenly from heart disease a few years ago.

You share these circumstances with me in fragmented pieces, bit by bit. As if slowly scooping some crumbling, tattered thing out of the pocket of an old coat.

One more thing I still remember well—when you talked to me about your family, for some reason you were always staring intently at the palms of your hands. As if, in order to follow the thread of the story, it was absolutely essential to carefully decipher the palm lines (or whatever they were) there.

As for me, I could hardly find anything worth telling you about my own family. My parents are perfectly ordinary, everyday parents. My father works for a pharmaceutical company, and my mother is a homemaker. They act like perfectly ordinary, everyday parents, and they talk like perfectly ordinary, everyday parents. We have an old black cat. As for my school life, there is nothing in particular to speak of. My grades aren't too bad, but they aren't strikingly excellent, either. The place I feel most at peace at school is the library. I like killing time there by myself, reading books and losing myself in daydreams. I've already read most of the books I wanted to read in the school library.

I remember the first time we met very clearly. It was at the awards ceremony for the "High School Student Essay Contest." The top five prizewinners had been invited. You and I had placed third and fourth, and our seats were right next to each other. It was autumn; I was a second-year high school student at the time, and you were still a first-year. The ceremony was a tedious affair, so during the lulls, we spoke to each other in brief, whispered snippets. You were wearing your school uniform: a navy blazer and a matching navy pleated skirt. A white blouse with a ribbon, white socks, and black slip-on shoes. Your socks were immaculately white, and your shoes were beautifully polished, without a single smudge. As if seven helpful dwarfs had banded together to carefully polish them before dawn.

It is not as though I am particularly good at writing. I have loved reading ever since I was little, and whenever I had a spare moment I would pick up a book, but I never believed I possessed any talent for writing myself. But everyone in my class was forced to write an essay for the contest during Japanese class, and mine was selected from among them, sent to the judging committee, advanced to the final round, and, unexpectedly, won a top prize. To be honest, I couldn't understand what was so excellent about what I had written. Even reading it over, I can only see it as a mediocre composition without a single redeeming quality. Still, if several judges had read it and decided it was worthy of an award, there must have been something of merit in it. My female homeroom teacher was absolutely thrilled that I had won the prize. In all my life, no teacher had ever reacted so favorably to anything I had done. So I decided not to say anything unnecessary and just gratefully accept the award.

The essay contest was a joint regional event held every autumn. A different theme was assigned each year, and that year's theme was "My Friend." Unfortunately, I couldn't think of a single "friend" I wanted to spend five 400-character manuscript pages writing about, so I wrote about our pet cat instead. About how that old female cat and I interacted, shared a life, and communicated our feelings to one another—though there were naturally limits to that, of course. I had a great deal to say about that cat. Because she was such a clever, highly individual cat. There were probably a few cat lovers among the judges. Because most cat lovers naturally feel a sense of goodwill and affinity toward other cat lovers.

You wrote about your maternal grandmother. About the emotional connection between a lonely elderly woman and a lonely young girl. About the modest, genuine values created there. It is a charming, moving essay. Far superior to what I wrote. I can't understand why my piece won third prize and yours only came in fourth. I tell you this honestly. You smile and say that you, on the contrary, think my piece is far superior to yours. "Really, I'm not just saying that," you add.

"Your cat sounds like a wonderful cat."

"Yeah, she's a really smart cat," I say.

You smile.

"Do you have a cat?" I ask.

You shake your head. "My younger sister is allergic to cat hair."

That was the first small piece of personal information I gleaned about you. Her younger sister was allergic to cat hair.

You are a very beautiful girl. At least, that is how you appear to my eyes. You are petite, with a somewhat round face, and beautiful, slender fingers. Your hair is short, with black bangs trimmed straight across your forehead. Like a carefully considered shadow. Your nose is small and straight, and your eyes are very large. By conventional standards of features, your eyes and nose might be considered out of proportion, but for some reason, I find myself drawn to that very asymmetry. Your pale pink lips are small and thin, always dutifully kept closed. As if keeping a few important secrets hidden away inside.

We five prizewinners go up on stage in turn, where we are ceremoniously presented with certificates and commemorative medals. The tall girl who won first prize gives a brief acceptance speech. The supplementary prize is a fountain pen (a fountain pen manufacturer was sponsoring the contest; I would go on to use that pen faithfully for many years to come). Shortly before the long, tedious awards ceremony comes to an end, I write my name and address in the memo section of my pocket notebook with a ballpoint pen, tear out the page, and secretly slip it to you.

"If you'd like, maybe you could write me a letter sometime," I say to you in a dry voice.

I don't usually do such bold things. I'm shy by nature (and, of course, timid as well). But the thought of parting ways with you there, of perhaps never seeing you again, feels profoundly wrong—completely unfair, even. So I muster my courage and take decisive action.

Looking a little surprised, you accept the slip of paper, fold it neatly into quarters, and tuck it into the breast pocket of your blazer. Just over the gentle, mysterious curve of your breast. Then, touching your bangs, you blush slightly.

"I want to read more of your writing," I say. Like someone making a clumsy excuse after opening the door to the wrong room.

"I would love to read a letter from you, too," you say, and then give a few slight nods. As if to encourage me.

Your letter arrives a week later. It is a beautiful letter. I read it over at least twenty times. Then I sit down at my desk and write a long reply, using the new fountain pen I just received as a runner-up prize. That is how we begin our correspondence, and how we begin seeing each other—just the two of us.

Were we lovers? Would it be right to call it something so simple? I don't know. But for that period at least, for nearly a year, you and I were united, our hearts seamlessly bound as one. And eventually, we built and came to share a special, secret world all our own—a mysterious city surrounded by a high wall.

It was the evening of my third day in the city when I pushed open the door to that building.

It is an old, featureless stone building. It sits just past the Central Square facing the Old Bridge, a short walk east along the riverside road. No sign was posted at the entrance; anyone who didn't already know would never have guessed it was a library. Just a brass plate engraved with the number "16," nailed up unceremoniously. The plate was tarnished, the characters difficult to read.

The heavy wooden door opened inward with a deep groan, revealing a dim, square room inside. There is no one around. The ceiling was high, the light from the wall lamps feeble, and the air smelled like someone's dried sweat. It was so dim that everything seemed to blur slightly, as if on the verge of being dismantled into molecules and sucked away somewhere. As I walked, the worn cedar floorboards creaked sharply here and there. There are two tall, narrow windows, and not a single piece of furniture.

Straight ahead, at the far end of the room, was a door. It was a plain wooden door with a small pane of frosted glass at about face level, upon which the number "16" is also written in an old-fashioned, ornate font. Beyond the frosted glass, a faint light was visible. I knocked lightly twice and waited, but there was no reply. Nor could I hear any footsteps. After a short pause, I steadied my breathing, turned the tarnished brass knob, and gently pushed the door open. The door let out a creak. As if warning the surroundings: "Someone is here."

Beyond the door lay another perfectly square room, about five meters to a side. The ceiling was lower than in the room before. And here, too, there was no one in sight. It had no windows, entirely enclosed by plaster walls. No paintings, no photographs, no posters, no calendars, and of course, no clock. Nothing but smooth, bare walls. There was a rough wooden bench, two small chairs, a table, and a wooden coat rack. The rack held no coats. In the center of the room sat an old-fashioned woodstove speckled with rust; a fire burned bright red inside, and on top, a large black kettle sent up steam. At the far end was what looked like a circulation desk. An open ledger lay upon it, as if someone had been interrupted mid-task by an urgent errand. Whoever it was (presumably the librarian) would likely return to this room before long.

Behind the desk was a dark-colored door that seemed to lead into the stacks. If so, this place truly was the "Library." Not a single book was in sight, yet the space retained an unmistakable library-like atmosphere—that special air shared by libraries all over the world, be they large or small, old or new.

I took off my heavy coat, hung it on the rack, and sat down on the hard wooden bench, warming my hands by the stove as I waited for someone to appear. Absolute silence filled the air. A silence like being at the bottom of deep water. I tried clearing my throat once just to see, but it didn't sound like a throat being cleared.

It is some fifteen minutes later when you open the door to the stacks and appear. (At least, I think it is about that long. With no clock, I cannot be certain of the exact time.) Catching sight of me sitting on the bench, you stiffen for a split second, your eyes going wide. Then you take a slow breath and say, "I'm sorry to have kept you waiting. I didn't realize anyone was here."

Unable to find the right words, I simply nod a few times in silence. Your voice does not sound like your voice. It is different from the voice I remember. Or perhaps, in this room, every sound and voice echoes differently than it normally would.

Just then, the lid of the kettle suddenly rattles, shivering slightly like an animal waking from sleep.

"Now then, how may I help you?" you ask.

What I seek are the *Old Dreams*.

"The *Old Dreams*," you say, pressing your small, thin lips into a straight line as you look at me. Of course, you do not remember me.

"But as you know," you say, "touching the *Old Dreams* is restricted to the *Dream Reader*."

In silence, I remove my dark-green glasses, lift my eyelids, and show you. The unmistakable eyes of a *Dream Reader*. Eyes that cannot go out into the glaring light of day.

"I understand. You have the qualification," you say, lowering your eyes slightly. Perhaps the condition of my eyes has unsettled you. But it cannot be helped. In order to enter this city, I had to have my eyes altered in such a way.

"Will you begin your work today?" you ask.

I nod. "I don't know yet if I will be able to read well, but I need to get used to it, even if only little by little."

There is still not a single sound in the room. The kettle, too, has once again retreated into silence. Excusing yourself, you quickly finish up the ledger work you had been in the middle of. I watch you from the bench. Outwardly, you have not changed at all. You look exactly as you did on that summer evening. I remember the bright red sandals you wore. And the grasshopper that suddenly leapt from the nearby tall grass.

"Have we met somewhere before?" I ask without thinking. Even knowing it to be a futile question.

You look up from the ledger, gaze at my face for a moment with the pencil still in your left hand (yes, you are left-handed—both in this city and in the city that is not here), and shake your head.

"No, I do not believe we have met," you answer. You likely answer in such a polite tone because you are still sixteen, whereas I am no longer seventeen. To you, I am now a much older man. Though it cannot be helped, the passage of time pierces my heart.

Having finished your record-keeping, you close the ledger, put it away on the shelf behind you, and prepare herbal tea for me. Taking the kettle from the stove, you carefully mix the hot water with ground herbs to brew a dark green tea. Then you pour it into a large ceramic cup and place it before me. It is a special beverage provided for the *Dream Reader*, and preparing it is one of your duties.

I take my time drinking the herbal tea. The herbal tea has a thick, peculiar bitterness, and is by no means easy to drink. But its nutrients soothe my still-wounded eyes and calm my mind. It is a special drink meant for exactly that purpose. You watch me from across the table. You are likely worried about whether I like the herbal tea you made. I give you a small nod. As if to say, *It's fine*. At this, a smile of relief touches your lips. It is a nostalgic smile. One I have not seen in a long time.

The room is warm and quiet. Even without a clock, time slips by in silence. Like a slender cat muffling its footsteps as it walks along the top of a wall.

We didn't exchange letters all that frequently. Only about once every two weeks or so. But each one turned out to be quite long. And generally speaking, I think your letters were somewhat longer than mine. Not that the length of the letters held any particularly great significance in our correspondence, of course.

I still have every single letter you wrote me, but since I didn't bother keeping copies of my own, I can't really recall the specific details of what I wrote. I doubt they contained anything all that important, though. I wrote mainly about my daily life and the little things happening around me. I wrote about the books I read, the music I listened to, and the movies I watched. I wrote about what happened at school. I was in the swimming club (though I had only joined due to unavoidable circumstances, and could hardly be called a dedicated swimmer), so I imagine I wrote about our practices, too. When I was writing to you, the words came naturally, no matter the topic. I could put my thoughts and feelings into words exactly as I wanted to, to an almost mysterious degree. It was the first time in my life I had ever been able to write so fluidly. As I mentioned before, up until then I had always thought I was bad at writing. I'm sure you were the one who coaxed that ability out from deep inside me. You always delighted in the subtle touches of humor in my writing. "That's probably what my life is missing most," you said.

"Like vitamin something-or-other?" I said.

"Right. Like vitamin something-or-other," you said with a firm nod.

I was crazy about you, and whenever I was awake, I think you were almost always on my mind. And probably in my dreams, too. But in my letters, I restrained myself as much as possible so as not to confess those feelings outright. And I had made up my mind to write only about practical, concrete matters as much as I possibly could. I suppose that back then, I wanted to cling to a world I could actually touch with my own hands—infusing it with a touch of humor, if possible. Because I felt that if I started writing head-on about the inner workings of the heart, so to speak—about things like love and romance—I would only end up driving myself deeper and deeper into a dead end.

In your letters, unlike in mine, you wrote less about the concrete details of your daily life and more about your inner thoughts. Or the dreams you'd had, or little pieces of short fiction. A few of the stories about your dreams, in particular, left a deep impression on me. You frequently had long dreams, and were able to recall them in vivid detail, almost as if you were remembering events that had actually taken place. I found this hard to believe. I rarely dream myself, and even when I do, I can almost never remember what they were about. The moment I open my eyes in the morning, those dreams all unravel into pieces and are sucked away somewhere. Even on the rare occasions when I had a vivid dream and woke with a start in the middle of the night, I would immediately fall back asleep, and by the next morning I wouldn't remember a single thing.

When I told you as much, you said:

"In my case, I keep a notebook and pencil by my pillow, and the moment I wake up, I write down the dreams I had that night. Even when I'm busy and pressed for time. Especially if I wake up in the middle of the night from a really vivid dream—no matter how sleepy I am, I write down everything in as much detail as possible right then and there. Dreams like that are often important, and they can teach me many valuable things."

"Many valuable things?" I ask.

"Things about the me I don't know," you answer.

To you, dreams existed on almost the exact same level as actual events in the real world; they were not things that could be easily forgotten or left to vanish. Dreams were like a precious wellspring of the heart, conveying so many things to you.

"It's a matter of practice. If you make the effort, I'm sure you'll be able to remember your dreams down to the finest detail, too. So give it a try. I really want to know what kind of dreams you have."

All right, I'll give it a try, I said.

But despite making an effort in my own way (even if I didn't go so far as to keep a notebook and pencil by my pillow), I simply couldn't muster any interest in my own dreams. My dreams were far too disjointed, lacking any real consistency, and were for the most part incomprehensible. The words spoken in them were indistinct, and the scenes I witnessed had almost no semblance of a plot. Occasionally, too, they contained disturbing elements that I could never bring myself to share with anyone. Rather than getting involved with all that, I wanted nothing more than to listen closely to your stories of long, colorful dreams.

Occasionally, I would make an appearance in your dreams. Hearing this makes me very happy. Because it meant that, in whatever shape or form, I was able to participate in your inner world of imagination. And you, too, seemed genuinely glad that I had shown up in your dreams. Even though, for the most part, the "me" in your dreams held no real significance, taking on nothing more than a supporting role in a drama.

Do you never have explicit dreams—the kind that are hard to speak of in front of me, the kind I find myself having so often (which occasionally, and involuntarily, leave my underwear soiled)? Do you honestly recount every dream you have? That is what I always wonder as I listen to you talk about your dreams.

You appear to speak openly and frankly, keeping nothing hidden. But no one really knows the truth. I believe there is no one in this world who does not harbor a secret in their heart. It is necessary in order for people to survive in this world.

Isn't that so?

"If there is anything perfect in this world, it is this wall. No one can climb over this wall. No one can tear this wall down," the Gatekeeper declared.

At first glance, the wall looked like nothing more than an aging brick wall. It seemed as though the next severe storm or earthquake could easily bring it down. How could he call such a thing perfect? When I said as much, the Gatekeeper looked as though his own family had been subjected to some unfounded insult. Then he grabbed my elbow and led me right up to the wall.

"Take a good look from up close. There's no mortar between the bricks, see? What's more, the shape of each and every brick is slightly different. Yet every single one interlocks so perfectly there isn't a gap big enough to slip in a single strand of hair."

It was exactly as he said.

"Try scratching a brick with this knife." The Gatekeeper pulled a utility knife from his jacket pocket, flipped the blade open with a snap, and handed it to me. It looked like a worn old knife at a glance, but the blade had been meticulously honed. "It shouldn't leave a single scratch."

He was right. The tip of the blade made only a dry, scraping sound; it didn't leave so much as a single white mark on the brick.

"You see? Storms, earthquakes, cannons—nothing can bring down this wall. Nor can anything damage it. Nothing ever has, and nothing ever will."

Keeping his palm pressed against the wall, he tucked his chin back and looked at me triumphantly, as though posing for a commemorative photo.

No, there is no such thing as perfection in this world, I muttered inwardly. Anything that takes physical form is bound to have a weakness or a blind spot somewhere. But I didn't say it out loud.

"Who built this wall?" I asked.

"Nobody built it," was the Gatekeeper's unwavering opinion. "It's been here from the very beginning."

By the end of the first week, I had taken up several of the *Old Dreams* you had chosen for me and attempted to read them. But those *Old Dreams* told me nothing of any meaning. All I heard there were muffled, uncertain mutterings, and all I saw was a series of unfocused, fragmented images. It felt as if I were being shown an audio tape or a film of randomly spliced fragments, played in reverse.

In the library's stacks, countless *Old Dreams* line the shelves in place of books. It seems no one has touched them for many long years, for they are all covered in a faint layer of white dust. The *Old Dreams* are shaped like eggs, each differing in size and hue. They look like eggs laid by a myriad of different animals. Strictly speaking, however, they are not perfectly egg-shaped. Pick one up and examine it closely, and you will see that the lower half bulges out more than the upper half. The balance of their weight is also skewed. Yet this very asymmetry gives them a stable base, preventing them from rolling off the shelves even without support.

Their surfaces are as hard as marble, and perfectly smooth. They lack the weight of marble, however. I do not know what material they are made of, nor how durable they might be. Would they shatter if dropped on the floor? Whatever the case, they must be handled with the utmost care—just as one would handle the eggs of a rare creature.

There is not a single book in the library—not even one. At one time, books must have lined these shelves, and the people of the city likely visited in search of knowledge and pleasure. Just like any ordinary city library. That atmosphere still hung faintly in the air, like a lingering scent. But at some point, it seems, all the books were removed from the shelves, and the *Old Dreams* were arranged in their place.

It seems there are no *Dream Readers* other than me. At least for now, I appear to be the only *Dream Reader* in this city. Was there another *Dream Reader* before me? There might have been. Judging by how meticulously the rules and procedures concerning the *Dream Reader* have been crafted and maintained, there probably was.

Your duty at the library is to safeguard the *Old Dreams* arrayed there and manage them appropriately. You select the dreams to be read, and record in a ledger that they have been read. Before dusk, you open the library doors, light the lamps, and, in the colder seasons, light the stove. You make sure never to run out of rapeseed oil or firewood for this purpose. And for the *Dream Reader*—which is to say, for me—you prepare a dark-green herbal tea. It soothes my eyes and calms my mind.

With a large white cloth, you carefully wipe away the white dust that has settled on the *Old Dream*, and place it on the desk in front of me. I take off my green glasses and place both hands on the surface of the *Old Dream*. I cup it in my palms. After about five minutes, the *Old Dream* gradually wakes from its deep sleep, and its surface begins to glow faintly. A pleasant, natural warmth spreads through my palms. And then they begin to spin the dream. Like a cocoon spinning out thread, hesitantly at first, and eventually with due enthusiasm. They have a story to tell. They must have been waiting patiently on the shelf for the time to come to leave their shells.

But their speaking voices are too frail, and I cannot fully catch their words. The images they project lack clear contours; they simply fade, crumble, and are absorbed into thin air. Or perhaps it is not their fault, but simply that my new eyes are not yet working properly. Perhaps my comprehension as a *Dream Reader* is not yet fully formed.

And then the time comes to close the library. There are no clocks anywhere, but as the hour approaches, you naturally know.

"How is it going? Is the work progressing smoothly?"

"Little by little," I reply. "But just reading one leaves me quite exhausted. I might be doing something wrong."

"There is no need to worry," you say, turning the knob to close the stove's air vent. You blow out the lamps one by one, sit across the table from me, and look me straight in the face (being stared at so directly flusters me). "There is no need to rush. We have all the time in the world here."

Following the prescribed procedures exactly, one by one, you close up the library. With a serious gaze, unhurried, with a steadfast composure. As far as I can tell, the sequence of these tasks never varies. Watching you work, I wonder if there is really a need to lock up this library quite so strictly. In this quiet, peaceful city, who on earth would break into a library in the middle of the night to steal or destroy *Old Dreams*?

"Would you mind if I walked you home?" I venture to ask on the third night, as we step outside the building.

You turn back and look at my face, your eyes wide. A single star from the sky is reflected white in your dark irises. You seem unable to grasp the meaning of my offer. Why should you have to be walked home by me?
