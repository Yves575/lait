One summer, when the family had treated themselves to a few days' vacation in the country, the Beetle had let out the same kind of death rattle right before it broke down. Valentyn was sure the bus driver would soon have to pull over onto the shoulder. In the meantime, his buddy was terrified, sobbing nonstop. It was the humiliation of being caught with his pants down that he just couldn't stomach. He had jumped when the door suddenly burst open. He'd barely had time to wipe himself when a man grabbed him by the shoulder and dragged him away by force.

Comforting his friend gave Valentyn even more strength. He had opened his notebook and written to him that everything would be fine, that there was no reason to worry, and that they would surely be back before dark, since the bus was about to give up the ghost. To show off, without being certain of how to spell the word, he had written that a connecting rod was about to give out. Making no secret of his satisfaction, when the driver let fly a string of curses after a thick cloud of smoke poured from the hood, he leaned over a blank page and wrote in big letters: *They're screwed, we'll be home for dinner.* But on this point, Valentyn was wrong.

As soon as morning breaks, Veronika and Lilya leave their house to head to the town hall, where the Russian command has set up its headquarters. Veronika has known some formidable rages, but never one like the fury driving her as she walks. So, invading a country, bombing cities, and decimating entire populations to serve the megalomania of a declining dictator wasn't enough for them? Now they had to go and kidnap kids? Was this what these conquerors were reduced to, commanded by incompetent generals, mercenaries forming the battalions of a dishonorable army?

As for bravery, the soldier standing guard outside the town hall doesn't have much left. He has no idea what he's doing in this godforsaken place so far from home. A young man barely twenty-five years old, happy to be among those who took a small town without having to fight any major battles, happier still to be alive. He points out the office of his superior, who is in charge of the occupied population. The town's next mayor, once Ukraine is liberated, he explains.

"Excellent news, then you'll be going home soon," Veronika retorts dryly, pulling her daughter along.

They march down the corridor with an almost military stride, their heels echoing so loudly that anyone who crosses their path dare not ask where they are going. Lilya spots the commandant’s office; Veronika takes a deep breath and opens the door.

“Where is my son?” she asks as she enters the room.

The officer, who had been dozing with his head slumped on his desk, jolts awake and stares at the two women.

“Who is your son?” he asks with a yawn.

“Valentyn Khodova!”

The man sits up, turns around with a sigh, and grabs the binder from a shelf where the names of combatants captured or killed on the front lines are recorded.

“Don’t pretend to look for his name. He is nine years old; he’s one of the two children you abducted from their school yesterday at noon. Are you going to pretend you know nothing about it?”

His expression shifts. He was the one who, a week earlier, had received the order to carry out this operation, the merit of which had initially seemed questionable to him. As if he didn’t already have enough problems to deal with. But the call had come directly from Moscow—more importantly, from the Kremlin. Few officers of his rank were granted such an honor. The call had lasted only a few minutes, but he would never forget the soft, benevolent voice of the Commissioner for Children’s Rights, making no display of the power conferred by her proximity to the President. Instead of adopting the threatening tone his superiors have accustomed him to, she had carefully explained the immense importance of what he was to accomplish. What nobler cause than to protect orphans and minors from the dangers posed by the Ukrainian rebellion. People who accuse Russia of the worst evils, yet are incapable of caring about the fate of their own offspring. Saving the orphans was a priority, she had repeated before hanging up.

An orphan—a detail he had perhaps neglected to specify to the unit he had dispatched. But come to think of it, why should he have, since he had been assured that the facility in question was an orphanage?

Yet this mother is demanding her child back; his intelligence sources might not be all that reliable. He will find whoever is responsible for this blunder. Giving it further thought, it seems to him that the Commissioner for Children’s Rights had also mentioned children in precarious situations. Which is enough to reassure him of the absolute legitimacy of his mission, since the entire city is living in precarious conditions.

“That dilapidated building—you call that a school?” he says, snapping his ledger shut.

“You bombed us, we live most of the time without water or electricity. Are you going to blame us for our cracked walls and blown-out windows?”

“The fact remains that the children require care that your institutions cannot provide, let alone in this region. We are showing infinite kindness and spending precious funds to come to their aid. You should be grateful and thank us; the Russian government’s mission is to protect minors, regardless of their nationality.”

Veronika is about to ask him how many children have perished under the bombs his government has dropped on hundreds of schools, maternity wards, hospitals, and public parks, and how many more are now orphans, but reason forces her to remain silent.

“My son doesn’t need care, and certainly not yours!” she replies in an icy voice. “The civilians your men are shooting at point-blank range, however, are in desperate need of mine.”

“Well then, go back to your work,” the officer retorts.

“Where is my son?” Veronika insists again, ready to commit murder.

The man steps over to the window and looks out at the street.

“On his way to a reception center. Our specialists will question him, and once they’ve evaluated his condition, they will decide what is best for him.”

“My son won’t answer your questions; he can’t speak.”

"And you said he didn’t need any care? What kind of parent are you?"

Lilya sees the fire burning in her mother’s eyes. She grabs her hand to remind her that she is by her side, and takes the initiative to address the officer.

"We don’t want to cause you any trouble, just to find my brother, to know that he’s alright and where you have taken him. Please."

The teenager’s restraint surprises the officer. He figures she will be easier to pacify; perhaps she might even help him convince her mother to leave him alone.

"Your brother is fine, and we haven’t abducted him. We’ve merely sheltered him from the fighting, to give him the childhood he is entitled to, the healthy food you can no longer find here but which he needs to grow, and the right environment for him to properly pursue his studies. Even during a special operation like the one we are conducting right now, wouldn't you agree?"

"A special operation?" Veronika fumes. "It’s a war you’re waging against us!"

"That false term carries a penalty of eight years in prison! We aren't at war with anyone, we are liberating Ukraine!" the officer objects, outraged.

"And back home, is talking about rain when it rains also punishable by prison? A storm—do you call that a sprinkle, a drizzle?"

"Once calm is restored and his safety is assured, we will send him back to you, I promise," he says in a voice that makes it abundantly clear his patience has run out.

Veronika’s nerve has stripped him of his swagger, leaving him feeling pathetic; his superiority is slipping through his fingers, like the sand on the beaches of Odesa he used to play with as a kid. His own mother must be about ten years older than this woman, and had she found herself face-to-face with him in a similar situation, she would have given him a thrashing and kicked his backside right out of the room. This time, he won’t let himself be pushed around; he is going to show them who he is.

"One more word and I'll have you arrested!" he says, adopting a grave tone. "Your daughter needs you, and if you were no longer able to look after her, I would be forced to take her to safety as well."

"Where is this center?" Lilya asks.

"I don't know yet. We have a lot of them; there are so many kids in precarious situations in your country. Come back and see me in three days. I'll know more by then and I'll tell you where your son is. I might even be able to give you an update on him, provided you don't cause any trouble in the meantime."

Terrified at the prospect of being detained as well, and hoping the officer will keep his promise, Lilya grabs her mother by the arm and begs her to comply. They won't achieve anything else today, except making their situation worse.
