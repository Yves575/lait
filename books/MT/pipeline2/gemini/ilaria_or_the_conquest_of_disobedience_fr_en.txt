May 1980

At eight years old, I love feeling my upper body suspended in mid-air, the feel of my bent knees against the metal. I love the moment when I squeeze my eyes shut, let go, and let the vertigo rush through me. When my hands are flat on the black of the asphalt, it means I've pushed past my fear. And then, the image of my favorite gymnast, Nadia Comăneci, comes to me. Her arms are wide open. Victory.

It's in the “bat hang” position that I spend my recesses, that I wait for Ana, my sister. This morning as she left she told me, We meet here, and on time, right? Otherwise I'm going home alone. “Here” is at the bottom of the stairs, near the metal bar that separates the parking lot from the schoolyard.

Ilaria! Get down from there! We're going to Chez Léon. Come on, let's go!

I recognize Papa's voice. Surprised, I lift the flap of fabric from my dress that is blocking my view. It's definitely the tips of his shoes, it's definitely his impatient tone. I do a flip, land on my feet, smooth down my dress.

Ana is coming.

No, no. The plan has changed. Maman is picking her up from school and we're all meeting at Chez Léon. Come on!

I take his hand, it is clammy.

Ever since our parents separated and Papa moved to Turin, we see each other once a month at a restaurant. Maman is the one who came up with the idea. She prefers a neutral place. She says that at home they argue too loudly. And it's true that at Chez Léon, they hold back. Even though Papa clenches his jaw and Maman looks off into the distance with an indifferent expression.

No, Papa still hasn't found a job. When he says “no-no-work” his voice is sad, tired. Maman turns her head a little to hide her smile and Papa gets angry. He repeats the word “humiliation.” Fortunately, the waiter arrives and sets plates of perch fillets or dishes of meringue with Chantilly cream on the table. Thank you.

After dessert, Ana and I leave the table and, down on the small beach, pick out some pebbles. We practice skipping stones.

Did you see?

What?

Papa took Maman's hand.

To get to Chez Léon, we pass through the village of Hermance, cross the French-Swiss border, and continue on the road to Yvoire. Papa has a navy-blue BMW 320 coupe.

If you see a phone booth, tell me. He lights a cigarette. There! He pulls over, gets out of the car, and takes some coins out of his pants pocket. His back pressed against the glass, the folds of his shirt form V's, W's. I wait, roll down the window, let in some air. The leather seat no longer burns the backs of my thighs; it is even soft when I stroke it with the palm of my hand.

In the booth, Papa speaks loudly, raises his voice, turns around. Our eyes meet. He smiles, looks down. From the way he moves his hands, I can sense his agitation. His body is stiff. It worries me. When he comes back, he says Maman has changed her mind and doesn't have time for lunch anymore. We'll spend the weekend together. And school? You'll miss school for just a few days... It's not that big a deal.

Papa's voice is sharp. I count on my fingers: Thursday, Friday, Saturday, Sunday. Four days. And Ana? I'd like to protest, but when Papa is tense, it's best to keep quiet.

He pulls away abruptly and stubs out his cigarette with a sharp motion. His forehead is covered in sweat.

Mont Blanc Tunnel, French-Italian border, vaulted galleries, winding roads of the Aosta Valley, carsickness. We pull over under a sky covered by a gray blanket. The landscape is metallic. I throw up on the side of the road; Papa hands me a white cotton handkerchief. Let's get something to drink, it'll make you feel better. A few kilometers further down, at the gas station bar, Papa's face is pale. It must be because of the neon lights. He pays the cashier for two slices of Margherita pizza, a whiskey, a coffee, and a lemonade. I hate lemonade but say nothing, my mouth is dry.

Do you have any phone tokens you can sell me?

How many?

Twenty or so.

The cashier scrupulously counts the yellowish coins and hands them to Papa.

The booth is outside, on the left.

Her nails are very long and painted very red. I follow Papa.

What are those tokens for?

I need them to make calls. In Italy, you can't put coins in the phone booths.

Between Geneva and Turin, Papa makes several calls. Five in total. As soon as he sees a gas station, he pulls over. Are you happy to be spending the weekend with me? Cat got your tongue? What are you thinking about? Nothing.

Turin. We drive around for a long time looking for a parking spot, and when Papa shoulders open the door to his building, he looks relieved. I dash up the stairs. What floor is it? The fourth. On the landing, he looks for his keys. You're going to see your room. Are you happy? I say yes. Yes, I'm happy. Listen, Ilaria, he turns the key in the lock, I have to introduce you to someone I haven't told you about yet, a certain Geneviève. Who is it? The lady of the house. Papa opens the door and points to an inanimate mannequin, wearing a black dress with a white lace collar and a hat. He bursts out laughing. Geneviève's face is made of white Styrofoam, it has no eyes. Come on! Go on in. Your room is at the end of the hall, past the bathroom.

Eager to see it, I take a quick glance at the other rooms and turn on the light in my bedroom. The space is cluttered with two beds and two dressers. I walk over to the *Topolino* poster. Mickey looks at me with his tunnel-shaped eyes. Papa stays in the doorway.

I haven't found any nice bedside lamps yet. We can buy some together tomorrow, right? If you want. Running my hand over the fabric, I ask him if these are the sheets that were in Florence.

You remember them?

I think so, yes. When was Florence?

Two years ago.

The orange sunflower patterns stand out on the sheets. They remind me of the blurry images I keep from that time. Two years, and yet it feels like centuries since Maman, Ana, and I moved to Switzerland.

The next day we wake up early and head out for a *giro*, a drive around Turin. Papa tells me about the FIAT empire, the workers' strikes, the arrival of immigrants from the South. He says the Turinese hate them.

Through the windshield he points out the *palazzi*, the Po, the Basilica of Superga, the statues. You see those kids over there between the cars, they're *posteggiatori*. That's how they earn money, by helping people park.

When we walk under the porticoes of Corso Vittorio Emanuele, Papa holds my hand firmly, his is always clammy and I don't dare pull mine away until, in front of the window of the Al Sogno toy store, I let go.

The window looks like a zoo of harmless animals. Even the snakes look friendly. Papa follows my gaze. Is it the teddy bear you’re looking at? Come on, let’s buy it. The store is full of wooden toys, mobiles, dolls in sparkling dresses. I am in awe. And for Ana? What should we get her? Yes, a doll… Ana loves them. The one I choose has very long eyelashes and when you lay her down, her eyelids close. While we wait for the saleswoman to wrap it in beautiful wrapping paper, Papa hands me the teddy bear. What should we call him? How about Birillo? Yes. I look at Birillo and immediately take to his shining milk-chocolate eyes, his cream-colored belly, his soft fur. Papa pinches my cheek between his index and middle fingers with a completely soft look.

This gesture is on my cheek like his signature. He will repeat it for two years straight, and I will end up hating it. Are you happy?

At eight years old, I am a taciturn, docile, rather scrawny child. I don’t complain when Papa leaves me alone in the apartment to go make his phone calls at Chez Carmelo, the bar downstairs. He doesn't have a phone. I’ll be right back. He always promises that he'll just be "two minutes." Alone, lying on my bed, I draw, and paste Sarah Kay stickers into my new album. Papa bought it for me at the newsstand. And when I’ve had enough of waiting for him, I know where to find him: at the counter. My landmark is the big coffee machine that gives off a smell of burnt caramel.

At Chez Carmelo, the local workers come to eat a sandwich at noon or have a drink before heading home. They all have a first name and their own habits. Una birra alla spina per Emilio e due tramezzini per Marco. Gianna is the boss. She quickly punches the large keys on the cash register. Sei mila lire. Perched on her little podium, she smiles, hands out the scontrini, the receipts, the packs of cigarettes, and when no one is around, she sweeps the floor. As for Carmelo, he polishes the counter, serves coffees, wipes the glasses with a cloth, and levels the sugar in the sugar bowls.

When I enter the café, the atmosphere is lively. The workers are singing in chorus to a song playing on the radio. Vorrei essere libero, libero come un uomo. To reach the counter, I weave between them and hoist myself onto the high stool. Listen, listen! It’s a song by Giorgio Gaber, La libertà. Carmelo laughs and I see metallic spots in the back of his mouth. How many cavities? I don’t have time to count them. Are you looking for Fulvio? He points to the telephone booth with his hand covered in soap bubbles.

And how was your day? Very good. Tomorrow we're going back to Geneva. You’ll come back soon, right? Carmelo’s smile is gentle. I’ll be very sad not to see you anymore. Promise? I promise.

Later, Papa appears. His legs are all stiff. With a strange look on his face, he leans close to my ear. We are leaving tonight. His breath is like a draft. Hurry up. We have to go upstairs and pack our bags.

Say goodbye and thank Carmelo and Gianna properly.

In the back seat of the car, I have trouble breathing. Where are we going? To the seaside. And Ana? And Maman? When are we meeting them? Stop sniffling. We’ll go back to Geneva in a month. A month isn’t long. And the school promotions? You told me we would just spend the weekend together… You’ll go next year. Stop with your questions now.

The image of the pretty dress I picked out with Maman for the procession appears. I think of Sarah, my best friend, of the teacher, of the little flag we made in class, of Ana. My head is heavy. I tuck my knees under my chin and hug Birillo tight against me. My sleep is cold, restless.

I want to hear your voice. STOP. The little one is fine. STOP. She would like to talk to you. STOP. I am waiting for you. STOP. Wait for me.

Your husband

Here! Put this in the glove compartment.

Now we stop not only at phone booths but also at the post office. Papa sends a ton of telegrams and keeps a copy of each. I don’t dare read them when he hands them to me, but, out of curiosity, I carefully slow my movements to catch a few words.

Zzaaak, zzzaaak. A family, a man alone, a woman. Two mops of white hair are driving at 30 km/h in the right lane. Papa loses patience, lets go of the wheel, lights a cigarette. Come on!!! Let me pass. He weaves between the cars. Are you scared? A little. He notices my hand gripping the door handle. The traffic coming into Genova is heavy, the tunnels numerous. Shadow-light, shadow-light. The lanes are packed with cars and long trucks. I can't take this road anymore. Get the map out, Ilaria.

Genova, Milano, Brescia, Alessandria. Yes, Brescia, then we’ll go to Alessandria. I trace our route with the tip of my finger. But there’s no sea in Alessandria! That’s okay. I’m showing you your country. Papa’s tone is playful. He is this close to winking at me.

Papa downshifts and takes the exit lane for the gas station.

I need to make a call, go to the bar.

No, I’ll stay here.

While waiting for him, I hop on one foot around the booth. Papa feeds tokens into the phone’s slot, lights a cigarette. Pronto? The conversation drags on.

Sitting in the car, I leave the door open, dangle my legs. The sky is covered in white streaks, the clouds are like balls of yarn. It’s muggy, I’m hot and thirsty. Other cars pull in, pull out, back up in the parking lot. Kids are running around. Careful, look before you cross! their mothers yell. The families filing past seem perfect to me—washed, combed, ironed; they are heading somewhere specific. Where are we going?

Roads, phone booths, post offices, small hotels, bars. The days pile up. We stop often at Autogrills to get gas, eat a sandwich, use the restroom. I like these places. Inside, everything is so colorful: the piles of candy, the baskets of food wrapped in crinkly paper, the bins of music cassettes, the toys, the stuffed animals. And while I wander the aisles, Papa talks with customers at the bar.

He loves to chat.

He can’t help himself; he has to talk to someone. Anyone.

And he is very good at striking up a conversation. It always starts with a comment about the traffic or the weather, or a joke. Papa knows how to put even the shyest people at ease. He looks right at home in the way he leans against the counter, in the way he talks to the bartender. When he plays the clown, he’s quickly surrounded by smiling faces. I prefer seeing him like this, happy, with his laughing eyes, rather than having to listen to his theories about people.

Yes, yes, let’s go. Always one last drink. He drinks very fast, and when he grabs his glass, I’m sure that even if he were blind, he’d have the same confidence. His lips would tremble just the same. He gives me the impression he’s afraid he won’t have time to finish his whiskey.

He told me one day that it’s medicine for him, and pinched my cheek.

Say thank you. I thank the bartender, say goodbye to everyone.

On the road again. We leave the counters just like that, abruptly.
