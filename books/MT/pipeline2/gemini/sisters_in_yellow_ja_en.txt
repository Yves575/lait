Chapter One: Reunion

1

I used to think that no matter where I ended up living, no matter how old I got or what became of me, I would never forget her.

But until just a moment ago, when I stumbled across her name in a brief online article, I hadn’t realized that I had completely forgotten all of it—not just the fact that I had once felt that way, but her name, her very existence, the time we spent together, and what we had done there.

Kimiko Yoshikawa.

For a split second, the thought crossed my mind that it might just be someone with the same name, but I knew instinctively that the woman in the article was *that* Kimiko-san.

*On December 23 at the Tokyo District Court, the first hearing was held for unemployed Kimiko Yoshikawa (60), a resident of Shinjuku Ward, Tokyo, who is charged with bodily injury, intimidation, and unlawful confinement. She is accused of locking a woman in her twenties from Ichikawa, Chiba Prefecture, inside a Shinjuku apartment room for a year and three months up until May of last year, during which time she assaulted the victim and caused her serious injuries. During the arraignment, the defendant Yoshikawa stated, "I will remain silent," and her defense attorney entered a plea of not guilty.*

*According to the indictment, beginning around February 2018, the defendant Yoshikawa allegedly confined her female roommate to a room in a Shinjuku Ward apartment for a period of one year and three months, subjecting her to repeated assaults and inflicting serious injuries that required a month to heal.*

*In their opening statement, the prosecution explained that in 2017, the victim, who had no fixed address at the time, began living at the defendant's home. Initially, they cohabited without issue, but Yoshikawa began to control the victim’s belongings and personal relationships, and monitor her movements. She subsequently instilled fear in the victim by repeatedly threatening her with remarks such as, "Even if you go outside, you won't be able to survive anyway," thereby stripping the woman of her will to escape. The prosecution further stated that the defendant had unlawfully confined the victim by assaulting her on multiple occasions, keeping her under her absolute control and bending her to her will. The incident came to light after the woman managed to escape on her own and reported it to the police.*

After reading the article three times over, I exhaled a breath that felt like a solid lump from deep within my chest. My heart was pounding so hard I could feel a faint trembling in my fingertips. It was Kimiko-san. There was no mistake. *That* Kimiko-san had been arrested.

When I searched the name "Kimiko Yoshikawa," the only hits I got were one article with similar content and another brief report just a few lines long. Both were treated as minor news items. The rest of the results were nothing but pages on name divination, stroke-count fortune-telling, and recommended names for baby girls. Aside from the criminal case I had just stumbled upon, there seemed to be no information about Kimiko-san herself anywhere on the internet.

I tried to gather my thoughts, to sort out what I should be thinking about and where to begin. I went back to the page with the first article and checked the date.

The article had been published on January 10, 2020. That was about three months ago. And it said the incident had occurred last year, in May 2019.

But no matter how carefully I read, I couldn't quite grasp the significance of those dates. I understood that three months had passed since the first hearing, but I had no idea how the victim and the people involved were doing now, or how the case and the trial would unfold from here. How was Kimiko-san doing right now? What was going to happen to her? Where on earth was I supposed to find these things out?

I couldn't even begin to imagine how interrogations worked, or the procedures and rules for things like detention centers. All I could picture were bleak little gray rooms, handcuffs, expressionless judges, and courtroom sketches—the same tired imagery I’d seen on TV dramas and the news.

That, and Kimiko-san's face.

Kimiko-san, the woman I had lived with for a few years back when I was still young, some twenty years ago. The article said she was sixty. *That* Kimiko-san was sixty? I couldn't believe it. Logically, of course, that much time really had passed, and I had turned forty myself, so it only made sense. Still, the age appended below Kimiko-san's name just didn't seem like a real number.

I closed my eyes and told myself everything was fine. It went without saying, but I had nothing to do with this case. I had nothing to worry about. For twenty whole years, I hadn't known what Kimiko-san was up to, nor had we kept in touch. We were no longer connected in any way. A long time had passed since then; it was all in the past, all over and done with. So far, apart from this particular incident—the unlawful confinement she had supposedly committed last year—nothing else had become a public issue. At least, there was nothing else on the internet. *I'm fine,* I told myself over and over.

When I looked up from my phone screen, the room was steeped in the dark blue of dusk—of which there had been no sign moments before—and the shadows of the objects around me were deepening. On the low table sat the ready-made meat sauce spaghetti I had plated up to eat. But somehow, amidst the slowly creeping presence of the night, it no longer looked like food.

I woke up constantly during the night, and morning came with me having barely slept.

Catching the spring morning light, the curtains reminded me of a massive sheet of pure white drawing paper. I closed my eyes against the glare, and various colors bled in before fading away. Dark blue, deep red, yellow—and then, Kimiko-san's face floated into my mind.

Gathering her wavy, jet-black hair—which reached down to the middle of her back—in her hands to show it off, Kimiko-san laughed merrily and said, "My hair is so thick, you wouldn’t even notice if a whole black cat were hiding in it!" I laughed, and everyone else laughed too. An old house. Each of the rooms was small and cluttered, our belongings crammed tightly together, but the entryway was always kept spotless. We were strictly limited to two pairs of shoes per person, and the rule was that we absolutely had to keep it clean at all times, because the entryway was where good luck came in and the bathroom was where bad things went out.

I closed my eyes and rolled over, trying to shake off the thoughts floating into my mind. But all sorts of things I thought I had long since forgotten came back to me, one after another, as if holding hands. The creaking of the hallway floorboards, warped in places, became our laughter; the wood grain on the ceiling I used to stare at before falling asleep became someone's cigarette smoke, and seemed to whisper to me.

Cosmetics left scattered in front of the mirror, clothes and underwear stuffed to the brim in the storage cubbies in the closet, and cup noodles piled in a basket in the cramped kitchen all sprang to mind. They brought back the very smell of those days we had lived together.

After agonizing for about thirty minutes while bundled up in the blankets on my bed, I sent a message to the LINE group for my part-time job.

*Good morning, this is Ito. I’ve had a slight cough since yesterday. I don’t have a fever, but just to be safe, I’d appreciate it if I could take today off. Sorry for the inconvenience, and thank you.*

The scheduling manager for my part-time job replied immediately.

*Understood. The new policies will be finalized early next week, so I'll keep you posted. Given the current situation, I'll let headquarters know just in case. Feel better soon!*

*Thank you! I think it's just normal cold symptoms, but if I do happen to get a fever or anything, I'll let you know right away. Thanks again!*

Up until the middle of last month, there was still a general atmosphere of skepticism regarding the virus. People were making a fuss about it, but many insisted there was no need to panic because it was ultimately just like the flu, and that masks were useless. There was a surreal, floating sensation in the air—a mixture of anxiety and strange excitement—but even so, the general feeling was that it all still fell within the margin of error of everyday life.

However, the news from overseas was growing increasingly terrifying, and between the end of last month and the beginning of this month, rumors began to circulate about whether or not Japan, too, would finally go into lockdown.

And then, five days ago, a state of emergency was declared, and the tension that had been steadily mounting suddenly burst. It wasn’t just on the news; panic buying was actually taking place at the local supermarket. Masks, hand sanitizer, and toilet paper vanished completely from drugstores, the streets emptied of people, and my part-time job was swamped trying to deal with it all. I worked as a sales clerk at a deli on a shopping street a short walk from my apartment. The shop was part of a chain run by a major supermarket with locations all over Tokyo.

It was a small shop where about thirty platters of side dishes and salads were lined up on the counter and in the chilled case. Customers would choose what they wanted, and we would pack it into plastic bento boxes. Because we simply sold pre-made food delivered from a central kitchen every morning, there was no prep area, and the shop was full if there were even four people inside. We’d been cycling through the exact same menu ever since I started working there three years ago, and with so many regulars coming in every day, I often wondered how they didn't get sick of it. But perhaps that consistency actually gave them a sense of comfort, because sales were strong, and it was a popular spot that always drew a line at lunch and in the evening. Since the end of last month, however, the number of customers had understandably plummeted, and things quickly went off the rails. When customers did finally show up, they'd get into arguments with each other over whether to wear masks or not, and we started getting phone calls complaining that our infection prevention measures were inadequate.

Lying there, I thought it might have been a mistake to text them on LINE earlier saying I had a cough. Why had I lied about having a cough? What was I thinking, going out of my way to say I had a cough at a time like this? I don't even know myself.

After a while, I picked up my phone, went to the website with the article about Kimiko-san, and took my time reading it slowly from the beginning one more time. My mood darkened all over again, and my limbs felt heavy. And whatever excuse I had given them, I was glad I'd taken the day off after all. Even if the job just involved standing around, there was no way I could have worked in this state.

I got out of bed, took some barley tea from the fridge, and drank it. Then I took a box out from the closet shelf.

It was a slightly large shoebox with crushed corners and a slightly torn lid, and inside were things like old letters, planners, and notebooks.

The worn-out shoebox, its once-dark navy color now completely faded, had originally held a pair of high heels my mother bought somewhere long ago. I remember her happily showing off those pristine white heels in our room; she must have been so thrilled, because she sat right down on the tatami mats to eat instant ramen, still wearing them. I asked her for the empty box and decided to use it to keep things like stickers, cheap little freebies from manga magazines, and the little notes I passed back and forth with friends at school. After that, it became a habit to store whatever I felt like keeping at the time inside it. As I moved from place to place, leaving things behind or losing them along the way, this shoebox was somehow the only thing that remained. Even so, I normally never bothered to pick up the box, let alone check inside. Looking at it now—this belonging of mine that I had somehow managed to hold onto through everything without throwing it away—I felt as though I were examining the keepsakes of someone who had died.

I opened the lid and spotted a small navy flip phone and a charger tucked in the corner. Even though I was the one who had been looking for it, my heart skipped a beat the moment I found it. Not knowing if the phone even worked anymore, I plugged the charger into it, connected it to an outlet, waited about thirty minutes, and held down the power button. The little screen slowly brightened, as if drawing a breath and coming back to life, and played its startup chime.

I had changed my phone number and erased everything else so that no one could reach me, yet here I had left only the numbers of my past acquaintances undeleted. Was it because I had subconsciously sensed that I would need them again someday, just like this?

There were only seventeen contacts saved in the address book.

Scrolling down to the *K* section, I saw the name Kimiko. I selected the name a little above hers—Ran Kato—and brought up the number. Next, I went to the *M*s: Momoko. Momoko Tamamori. I copied both of their numbers into the memo app on my current phone.

I had no idea where Ran Kato and Momoko Tamamori were now, or what they were doing.

The last time I saw them was when they moved out of that house. We were twenty, or somewhere around there. I hadn't been in touch with Ran or Momoko since. If I hadn't happened to see the article about Kimiko-san yesterday, I might never have thought of them again.

The time we all spent together in that house came rushing back to me, like disjointed footage spliced together. Assorted voices and expressions replayed in my mind, the resolution abruptly sharpening and then blurring over and over again. I doubted these numbers even worked anymore, and I certainly had no desire to reach out to them after all this time. But Ran and Momoko were the only ones I could talk to about Kimiko-san. They were the only ones who could share the anxiety I was carrying now.

I was terrified that Kimiko-san might have told someone about her past with us.

Maybe, during the interrogations, they had found all sorts of evidence in Kimiko-san's apartment from the days we lived together, and an investigation was actually already underway behind the scenes. Just thinking about it made me so anxious I couldn't sit still. No one had come to my place yet, but it was entirely possible that Ran or Momoko had been contacted first, called in, and were already being questioned.

If I thought about it calmly, the statute of limitations on what we did might have already expired. We might not even face any major charges. Ran, Momoko, and I had been so young, and we were only doing what Kimiko-san told us to do. But then, what about Kotomi-san? In the end, whose fault was it that she ended up dead, and why? Could we really say we had nothing to do with it?

The more I thought about it, the more I was weighed down by an anxiety indistinguishable from terror. I was overcome by a dread so intense it felt as if my chest were being silently crushed by a massive iron plate or something, and tears blurred my vision. What was I supposed to do? Should I pretend I had never seen the article, not contact Ran or Momoko, and just keep quiet? Or should I go to the police and tell them what I knew?

My imagination spiraled into darker and darker places, completely blocking out my vision. How were those two doing? Where were they now, and what were they doing? I was sure their phones wouldn't connect. But if they weren't going to connect anyway, shouldn't I at least try calling?—I set my phone aside and pulled the futon over my head, as if to blot everything out. With the daylight shut out, I lay there in the tepid spring darkness, blinking repeatedly for a while. And then, just like that, I fell asleep for a little while.

I had a vague, elusive dream—one where the only thing I knew for certain was that it was a nightmare. Even though no concrete events or characters appeared, and there was nothing but the passing of time, why is it that you can always tell when a nightmare is a nightmare? Like a dark, unrelenting wave, the nightmare bore down on me endlessly. When I woke up, my chest and back were drenched in sweat. Then, I dialed Ran Kato's number.

After six rings, I heard a bright voice say, "Hello?" I could feel my lower jaw trembling with tension.

"Excuse me, um, is this... Ran Kato's number?"

"Yes."

Ran replied, her voice dropping slightly. It was Ran. My heart gave a heavy thump.

"Um, it's Hana."

"Hana?"

"Yes, um, my name is Hana Ito. A long time ago, we used to..."

"Hana?" Ran said, after a brief silence. "...You mean Hana-chan?"

"Yeah, it's Hana. I'm sorry for calling out of nowhere." I switched the phone to my other hand and pressed it tightly to my ear. "I never actually thought it would connect. I'm sorry for the sudden call."

"...What's wrong? How did you get my number?"

"It was still saved in my old cell phone."

"Oh."

I heard Ran let out a soft sigh.

"I'm really sorry for surprising you out of the blue like this."

"No, it's fine, but... it's just a bit of a shock. I mean, it's been so long."

"I know, I'm sorry. I actually called about Kimiko-san."

In the background, I could hear children playing and shouting happily, mingled with the chatter of women. The sounds grew a little fainter, and I could tell Ran had moved to another room.

"...When you say Kimiko-san, do you mean *that* Kimiko-san?"

"Yeah."

"What happened to Kimiko-san?"

"Yesterday, I came across a case involving her."

"What do you mean?"

"I saw it online."

"What case?"

"Kimiko-san was arrested. I was so shocked too. The trial's already started, and, well, I thought there was a chance it might involve us too... so I really wanted to talk to you, Ran."

"Hold on," Ran interrupted. "What do you mean? I have no idea what you're talking about. Why was Kimiko-san arrested? And what do you mean it might involve us? Did she say something?"

"No, it's not that. Kimiko-san... she held a girl captive in her apartment and injured her, and that's what she was arrested for. She was probably doing the same kind of thing as back then, and got caught. So there's a chance the past could be brought up too, and, you know, everything might come to light. I really have no idea, but I'm just so scared."

I gathered my courage and asked.

"...Have you heard anything? From the police, or anyone like that?"

"Of course not," Ran laughed, as if the very idea was ridiculous, but I could detect a faint trace of anxiety in her voice.

"I've been so anxious since yesterday, and, well, I was wondering if I should go to the police and talk to them."

"What?" Ran asked in surprise. "Talk to them about what?"

"About the past, and what I know about Kimiko-san."

"Hey, stop joking around," Ran said, lowering her voice, though her tone was forceful. Someone called out to her then, and she replied with a bright, cheerful, "Okay!"

"Listen, I have company over right now."

"Right, of course, I'm sorry."

"Where are you living now, Hana-chan? Tokyo?"

"Yeah."

"We should probably meet up and talk. I know it's a bit tricky right now with COVID, but... if possible, it's probably better not to discuss this over the phone."

"Yeah, I think we should meet in person too... Oh, what about Momoko? You remember Momoko, right? I don't know if her old number still works, but we should probably get in touch with her too, and see if she can come."

"Momoko's not an option," Ran said curtly.

"Why not?"

"I'll explain that when I see you, too. Anyway, Hana-chan, whatever you do, do not go to the police. Absolutely not. Got it?"

"Got it."

We settled on a time and place to meet, and hung up.

2

Seeing Ran Kato for the first time in twenty years, she seemed to carry a very different air about her.

She was overall as petite as I remembered, but her body seemed to have grown a size larger, making her proportions feel somehow off-balance. If she hadn’t spotted me first and raised her hand, I don't think I would have recognized her right away, even in this nearly empty café.

Ran was wearing an airy, off-white cotton tunic and a large mask that completely covered half her face. Her dyed light-brown hair was tied back, and at her hairline, white hair that looked as if it had been lightly swept on by a brush gleamed faintly under the lights. Traces of her former self lingered around her eyes, which were heavily coated in brown eyeshadow and thick mascara.

Her narrow forehead was unchanged, bringing back a memory of some late night long ago when the three of us—Ran, Momoko, and I—had measured and compared the size of our foreheads and the width of our eyes. Come to think of it, Ran had loved doing makeup even back then. Laughing, she would say, "How much do you think my techniques can change your face?" as she tried out various looks on me—someone completely clueless about such things—and the three of us would burst out laughing when we saw the final result. Looking at the Ran before me while recalling all this, the realization of just how much time had truly passed welled up inside me, leaving a faint ache deep in my chest.

But we ordered our drinks without exchanging any of the cheerful greetings people usually share when reuniting with an old friend—no "It’s been so long!" or "How have you been?" With her mouth covered, I couldn't tell what expression Ran was wearing, but we sat in silence for a while, enveloped in an atmosphere so utterly still it felt as if not a single thing could stir.

After two iced coffees were placed on the table, I ventured, "I'm glad you recognized me right away, Ran-chan."
"Because you were looking all around the entrance," Ran replied curtly. Pausing what she was doing on her phone, Ran pulled off her mask; a thick smear of brick-red lipstick had been left on the inside.

"Kimiko-san," Ran said. "What happened? What kind of incident did she cause? I tried looking it up, but I couldn't really figure it out."

Ignoring my nervousness, Ran spoke to me as casually as if she were simply picking up a conversation from the other day.

"Was it on the news? What, is it something really bad?"

"Well, not exactly the news..." I pulled up the article I had bookmarked and handed Ran my phone. With a serious expression, Ran stared at the phone, scrolled down a little with her fingertip, and then locked her eyes on the screen once more.

"How did you find this?"

After sliding the phone back across the table to me, Ran asked.

"I was just idly reading articles about other major cases on Yahoo, and down at the bottom, similar cases kept popping up. Connected by the content. Links to incidents from years ago, or tiny articles in local papers I'd never even heard of—tons of things like that kept appearing. I kept clicking through them, and eventually I stumbled on this article."

"Shinjuku, huh... So not that place in Sancha," Ran said, furrowing her brow. "It said Kimiko-san is sixty, didn't it... Which means back then, she was forty or so. The same age we are now."

"Yeah."

"Thinking about it now, she was completely insane," Ran spat. "I can't believe it. Forty... That'd be like us right now gathering a bunch of twenty-year-olds and living like that. It's totally fucked up."

She gave a small shake of her head.

"But why are you so freaked out about this case with Kimiko-san, Hana-chan? It has nothing to do with us, does it? Besides, the crime itself happened last year, and the trial's already started, right?"

I nodded.

"When we were living together—I mean, sure, we did some pretty sketchy stuff. But we were twenty or so back then. It was a long time ago. There's no way any of that could possibly come back to bite us now."

"But what if, say, that stack of cards... you know, from back then, was still at Kimiko-san's house? I was thinking the police would wonder what it was. And if Kimiko-san talked, even if we weren't charged with anything, they might still bring us in for questioning. I'm sure there's a statute of limitations and all, but out of everything we did... I just don't know what crossed the line, or by how much."

"So you were going to go out of your way to go to the police and tell them you were an old associate of hers?" Ran's eyes widened in exasperation. "You're overthinking this."

"You think so?"

"I know so. Your imagination is running wild in the worst way. To begin with, the stuff we did wasn't even that big of a deal. At least, I don't think it was. It's definitely not something to be losing sleep over twenty years later. Kids today are doing way more messed-up stuff, you know."

"You think so?"

"I know so," Ran said, pausing a moment to think. "Besides... we were basically forced into it, weren't we?"

"But Kotomi-san died too, and we still don't even know what really happened. We kind of dragged her into it, or... well, anyway, it ended up the way it did."

"Who was Kotomi-san again—oh, right." Ran gave a small nod. "She was the hostess at that Ginza club you used to make trips out to."

"Right."

"No, no, no, I really don't think that has anything to do with this. That was entirely her own business... We settled that back then too, didn't we? Am I wrong? I mean, sure, it was a shock, but... wait, Hana-chan, don't tell me you still think it was your fault?"

"It's not that," I said, shaking my head.

"No way. Those people were all crazy anyway," Ran said, giving a little shrug. "So just chill for a second. Lighten up and move on. Stop making that face. There's no point stressing over ancient history that nobody even remembers anymore. I mean, sure, it's a shock for Kimiko-san's name to suddenly pop up like this. Especially in a similar situation, like a girl being held captive or whatever. I get why you’re freaked out. But I'm telling you, it's fine. If we were actually in trouble, the cops would have shown up ages ago. Honestly, that whole thing is just our dark past. A youthful indiscretion, and a dark past."

"A youthful indiscretion... a dark past."

"Exactly. Honestly, COVID is way more of an issue right now. How are you holding up, Hana-chan? My kids' school got canceled, so it's absolute hell at home. This COVID stuff is crazy. What's it been like for you?"

"Me?" I let out a small breath through my nose. "I'm fine. I live alone, so..."

Ran stirred her iced coffee with her straw, giving a vague nod as if to say, *Is that so*. We were at a café right in front of Jiyugaoka Station. Ran apparently lived somewhere along the Toyoko Line; she had picked this place, saying it was an easy one-train ride for her and she had errands in the area anyway. It was a chain coffee shop with a completely glass-paneled facade. Ran hadn't asked me where I lived, what I did for a living, or where and how I had been spending my life since that time.

"There's barely anyone around," she noted. "It's usually packed here. This whole stay-at-home thing is crazy."

I looked out at the street. Pedestrians were indeed few and far between, and the heavy overcast sky that had lingered since morning gave the entire neighborhood a dark, sunken atmosphere.

"But man, funny how time comes around, huh? We're pushing forty already. I never thought I'd see you again like this, Hana-chan. When we parted ways... we all kind of just scattered."

"Ran-chan, you never changed your phone number. Did Kimiko-san ever try to get in touch with you after that?"

"Nope," Ran said. "I can't really remember the details anymore, and honestly, I don't want to... but we kind of just slipped away in all the chaos back then, right? That was the end of it. It must have been way harder for you, though, Hana-chan. You were her favorite... I mean, seriously, it makes total sense that Kimiko-san got arrested. She's terrifying. Completely out of her mind. We were so young—basically just kids—so we had no idea, but looking back? She was completely insane, right?"

"Hey, you mentioned this briefly on the phone," I said. "About Momoko... You said getting ahold of her was impossible. Did something happen?"

"Ah, Momoko." Ran gave a small shake of her head. "Remember her? Momoko had that crazy younger sister, didn't she? Insanely beautiful, but with insanely gross teeth. She used to come around to that place too. From what I heard, Momoko got into a huge fight with her sister and her sister's boyfriend over money, and eventually went missing. I don't know the details, but that was the rumor. Sometime after I left that place, Momoko and I stayed in touch for a little bit, but then she just suddenly vanished."

I sighed.

"God, there was nothing but total crazies back then. We were just too clueless to see it."

Ran's voice suddenly grew louder, ringing out with an exaggerated theatricality, as if she were practicing lines for a play. There were few people in the spacious café. The scattered customers were all sitting alone, wearing masks, and a bossa nova-style tune played quietly from a speaker hidden somewhere in the ceiling. The clatter of dishes from the dishwashing station behind the counter mingled with the voices of the staff. We were the only customers talking. An older man sitting a little ways off rustled his newspaper loudly as he snapped it open; our eyes met when he threw a quick glance in our direction. I took a sip of water and felt with my fingertips for the mask hooked under my chin.

"Well, whatever the case, it's over and done with. Like I keep saying, it was a long time ago. It's just the past. There's nothing for you to worry about, Hana-chan... Right, Hana-chan?"

Ran said, staring straight at me without blinking.

"I mean it, absolutely no police or anything like that. There’s seriously no point. It’ll one hundred percent just make things worse. Really, Hana-chan, I’m counting on you here. We're good, right? I came out today and listened to what you had to say. Now just forget about it. I'm serious."

I nodded.

"We're really good, right?"

"Yeah."

A long silence fell between us. Ran let out a sigh and hunched forward, her eyes fixed on a single point on the table. A deep exhaustion showed on her face. I felt like there was something else I ought to say to Ran now that we were finally seeing each other again—something important. And maybe Ran was feeling the exact same way. But I didn’t know what that something was, nor how to put it into the right words so it could leave my mouth.

"Well, I should probably get going—oh, right. Hana-chan."

Ran straightened her back, as if shaking off the mood, and spoke as she slipped her mask back on.

"Listen, could you do me a favor and delete my phone number? I'll make sure to delete your call from my history, too."

When I remained silent, unable to say a word, Ran picked up the bill to check it, left her share of the money on the table, and stood up, slinging her bag over her shoulder.

"Well, I'm taking off, Hana-chan. Stay safe from COVID, okay?"

Even after Ran left those words behind and walked out of the restaurant, I couldn't get up from my seat right away. I knew there were other things I ought to be thinking about, but my mind just wouldn't work properly. I had no appetite, but since I hadn't eaten since morning I figured I must be hungry, and wondered if I should order something to eat—I found myself vaguely pondering things like that.

I was looking at the small tabletop menu with its photos when I heard a strange sound. Outside, somewhere in the distance, a sound was rumbling—it was hard to tell if it was heavy or light, but it sounded like something massive slowly rolling. After a moment, I realized it was thunder. The instant I looked out through the glass, the rain came pouring down all at once with a bursting sound. The drops were so enormous it felt as though you could see every single one of them. I watched several passersby sprint away, shielding their heads with their bags and hands. Gaining even more intensity, the rain hammered against the asphalt and the awning of the shop diagonally across the street, splashing up and raising a faint white mist.

Chapter Two: Luck with Money

1

The first time I met Kimiko-san was the summer I was fifteen.

One morning, right at the beginning of my last summer vacation in junior high, a strange woman was sleeping beside me in place of my mother, who should have been there.

I couldn't see her face, and she was wearing my mother's pajamas, but I knew immediately that the woman fast asleep with her back to me, snoring steadily, was not my mother.

Propping my upper body up on my elbows, I backed away a little, but quickly decided it was no big deal and went back to sleep. My mother worked at a neighborhood snack bar, and it wasn't the first time she had brought girls from the bar or her friends home to crash like this.

The next time I woke up, the woman was gone, and the pajamas were laid out neatly on top of the futon, which had been folded into thirds. They were the same worn-out pajamas my mother had been wearing for years, but seeing them perfectly folded—like clothes laid out for sale in a store—was so novel that I stared at them for a while.

Normally, we just grabbed our clothes and underwear straight off the hangers on the curtain rail by the window where we hung them to dry, and put them on. Our apartment was full of stuff, and always a complete mess to boot, so that one spot alone seemed to float up in a soft, round shape, like a blank space carefully rubbed out with an eraser on a notebook page faintly smudged with pencil lead.

My mother and I lived in a small town on the outskirts of Higashimurayama City, in an old, cramped apartment building hidden from the main street.

Between the detached houses facing the street ran an unmaintained alleyway about three meters wide. Following it to the back and turning left led to a shared entrance. The characters spelling out *Seifuso* were weathered and blackened with age, barely legible. The entrance resembled an ominous cave, and the hallway, lit by nothing more than a few low-wattage bulbs dangling from the ceiling, remained utterly dark even on the brightest of afternoons.

It was a two-story wooden building with four identically laid-out rooms on each floor. Aside from the middle-aged landlady who lived at the far end of the second floor, however, we were the only ones living there. Opening the flimsy wooden sliding door—one so fragile even a child could have kicked it in—revealed a small entryway. Stepping up inside brought you into a tiny three-mat kitchen, followed by two four-and-a-half-mat rooms connected end to end. We had exclusive use of the communal toilet at the far end of the first floor. Hemmed in by buildings on all sides, opening the windows offered nothing but a view of the neighboring concrete wall, and almost no natural light filtered in.

We rented two rooms on the first floor: the ones immediately to the left and right as you stepped through the entrance of Seifuso. My mother and I primarily used the room on the right. 

The room on the left was supposed to be my father's space, but with nothing more than a TV, a futon, and a few items of clothing hanging in the closet, it felt completely unlived-in. To begin with, my father was rarely ever home.

At the time, I didn't really know what he did for a living. He had a solid build and his skin was always deeply tanned; I imagine he worked as a construction worker or a truck driver, or perhaps took on day labor that sometimes required him to travel for extended periods. (Once, on my way home from elementary school, he called out to me from a large truck.) There was a brief phase when he would bring home coworkers dressed in similar work clothes to eat hot pot, grill meat, and drink, but that quickly came to an end.

Whenever he showed up—as if he had suddenly remembered us—my father was always in a good mood. He would give me things like a badminton set or a stuffed animal that looked like he'd won it from a claw machine. Sometimes he would return late at night and go out of his way to wake me up just to feed me sushi, insisting it was delicious.

I didn't dislike my father, but since we spent so little time together, I never knew what to talk to him about. When he did come home, I would become strangely tense and walk on eggshells around him, constantly on edge as I secretly wished he would hurry up and leave. And whenever I worried that he might be able to tell how I felt, my mood would darken all over again. I knew he wasn't what you would call a "normal" father, but even so, I harbored a vague sense of pity for him, thinking how sad it must be to be thought of this way by your own child. That realization, in turn, triggered an entirely different kind of tension and self-loathing.

By the time I was in the upper grades of elementary school, my father stopped coming home entirely, and I never saw him again. I have no idea what he is doing now, or what has become of him. I only vaguely pieced it together later on, but it seemed my father had another home—or rather, other people he was living with.

Living alone with my mother was easygoing in its own way, but it was essentially the same as having nothing at all. My mother liked having fun and drinking, even though she couldn't hold her liquor well; she had a lot of friends, and the kind of personality that was easily swept up in the flow. Her proudest boast was that, after graduating from a local commercial high school and working as a full-time employee at a stocking factory, she had acted as a leg model in front of everyone when the bigwigs from headquarters came for a tour. But she only lasted there for a few years. After that, she lived a life bouncing from one local snack bar to another with her friends, and apparently, somewhere along the way, I was born.

Compared to the mothers of my friends from school, my mother had quite a youthful-looking face. She was flashy, and even to me, her daughter, she didn't feel like a so-called normal mom. Petite with a somewhat childish face, she was optimistic and had a bright personality, laughing often—yet whenever she drank, she almost invariably cried. There wasn't any clear cause or reason for her tears, though. You could call her a crying drunk, I suppose; but to my mother, drinking, having a good time, getting rowdy, and crying all seemed to be pretty much the same thing.

She didn't seem to have any attachment to my father, either. I never heard her complain about him or badmouth him, and she didn't seem particularly concerned even after he stopped coming home. Occasionally, she would joke about how awful her estranged mother's personality was, but even that was strictly limited to times when she was drunk, in high spirits, and feeling sentimental.

Basically, when my mother was with me, she didn't like it to be just the two of us; she preferred to have someone else along as well. She spent a lot of time aimlessly hanging out with hostesses from the club where she worked, people introduced to her by customers, or longtime local friends, so whenever I was alone with my mother, I always felt just a tiny bit tense. Essentially, we just passed each other by: my mother would sleep while I was at school, wake up in the early afternoon, put on her makeup in the evening, head to work, and return home late at night.

There was only one other kid in the neighborhood who came from a similar home environment, and the two of us became close. Since elementary school, neither of us had ever had a curfew, so the parents of the other kids—the ones who had places they weren't allowed to go and strict times for dinner—didn't seem to like their children spending any more time with us than absolutely necessary. At the very least, they were forbidden from coming over to our houses to play. I once asked another friend about it—a girl I rather liked, who treated me normally at school but always went off somewhere with a different group of kids after school. Looking a little hesitant, she explained, "My parents said weird adults are always going in and out of your place, Hana-chan, and that it isn't a proper home, so I'm not allowed to go." And since this wasn't a malicious rumor or a lie, but the absolute truth, there was nothing I could do about it.

When I started middle school, everything came into sharper focus—the expressions on my friends’ faces, their attitudes, all sorts of things. I felt like I could tell at a glance who came from a normal home and who didn't, as if they were wearing different-colored hats. In a normal house, you don't wake up in the morning to find a strange woman sleeping next to you. Your spirits don't suddenly lift just because a futon and some pajamas have been neatly folded, nor, I imagine, do you ever feel the urge to just stare at them forever.

I folded my own futon into thirds in the exact same way, lining it up next to the one that was already folded. I was alone in the room, and the kitchen was empty too. I slipped on a pair of sandals, crossed the hallway in a single stride, and opened the door to the room across from ours. I could hear the noisy blare of a television; stepping inside, I saw the back of a woman lying on the floor, watching a daytime talk show. An old electric fan swept slowly from left to right, occasionally letting out a loud creak.

Noticing me, the woman didn't change her position; she merely turned her face toward me and smiled pleasantly. Her smile was so entirely natural that I almost wondered if she was someone I had met before. But she was a complete stranger—she might have been the one sleeping on the futon next to mine last night, but this was undeniably the first time we had ever come face-to-face. Turning her attention back to the screen, the woman let out an amused laugh, her shoulders shaking slightly at the cheers of the people on TV. I remained standing at the threshold between the kitchen and the room, watching the television, the electric fan, and the woman.

"Let's get something to eat."

The woman stretched and spoke as the program ended and cut to a commercial.

"You're hungry, right?"

We stood in the cramped kitchen and made instant ramen.

The woman was about a size larger than me, and her arms and legs seemed remarkably long. Her long, wavy black hair was tied back, sagging at the nape of her neck, its ends scattering across a large white T-shirt printed with English words. I stood a short distance away from her, watching intently as the water for the two of us came to a boil in an aluminum pot.

Deftly tearing open the packets, the woman loosened the noodles in the boiling water, stirred in the powdered soup, and portioned the ramen out into the bowls I had set out. She tried to pluck a wonton stuck to the side of the pot with the tips of her disposable chopsticks, but it wouldn't peel off. "Oh well. I'll bring the pot over," she said brightly, gesturing to the room. I pulled out the bare kotatsu table that had been leaning against the wall, and we sat facing each other to slurp our ramen.

"It's summer vacation, isn't it? Aren't you going anywhere?"

I made a vague sound, something that was and wasn't an answer. It hadn't been all that hot until just a moment ago, but suddenly I felt sweat beading on my skin. I reached over, turned on the air conditioner, and pressed the button for high.

"Are you going anywhere with Ai-san?"

Ai was my mother's name.

"Not really. We don't do things like that."

"Hmm. What's your name? I'm Kimiko."

"I'm Hana."

"Hana-chan, huh? Who named you? Ai-san?"

"I don't really know," I answered in a quiet voice.

"I see."

"Um, you're my mother's friend, right?"

"That's right."

The conversation died there, and we went back to eating our ramen in silence. A quiz show was playing on the TV, and the motor of the old air conditioner droned loudly. After a while, the faint sound of an ambulance siren drifted in from outside; it seemed to draw a little closer, only to fade away again just as quickly.

"Does your name have a kanji?"

"Just the regular one, for 'flower.'"

"My name, Kimiko, uses the character for 'yellow,' then 'beautiful,' and 'child.' Kimiko."

I gave another vague nod and drank my soup. Glancing up with my bowl still in hand, I caught the eye of Kimiko-san, who had already finished eating and was resting her elbows on the kotatsu table. I instinctively looked away. Then I went back to stealing glances at her face over the rim of my bowl. Kimiko-san had a face unlike that of any woman I had ever seen.

Everyone's face is different, so of course that goes without saying, but even so, Kimiko-san's face had a certain presence to it—something I couldn't quite explain with the words I had at my disposal back then. She had just woken up and shouldn't have been wearing any makeup at all, yet her eyebrows were thick and dense, the eyelashes framing her deep double eyelids were dark and long, and the space between her eyes and eyebrows was narrow. Her nose rose prominently right from the bridge, and the hair trailing from her temples down to her sideburns mingled with sweat, curling into tight, frizzy little whorls.

Rather than pretty or beautiful, Kimiko-san's face gave an impression of strength.

She reminded me of a young, gallant pharaoh—the protagonist of a shojo manga set in ancient Egypt and the modern world, which had been incredibly popular when I was in elementary school and which I myself had been completely obsessed with. Watching Kimiko-san crack her neck or shift her posture in front of the TV, I could almost picture that protagonist's dialogue and the borders of manga panels hovering in the air diagonally above her head, which made me feel a little bit happy.

"I suddenly want a soda. Want to go get some?"

After carrying her bowl to the sink and returning to the room, Kimiko-san invited me out. We went outside in the exact same clothes we had slept in and walked down the road to the convenience store.

"Your place has two rooms, but you and Ai-san don't sleep separately, huh."

"The room where we just ate ramen, we call that the TV room, and the other one is the futon room, so it just kind of ended up that way..."

"So, like a living room."

"It's nothing that nice," I said, looking down.

"Better than sleeping alone, though, right?"

The summer sunlight was so glaring I couldn't help but squint, and with every breath I took, I could feel the heat seeping into my skin.

The apartment building where I lived was just behind my junior high school. 

Walking about two hundred meters along the long, gray wall enclosing the school building, then turning left at the corner and continuing a short way, brought you to the main gate. The convenience store was directly across the street from it. We were forbidden from shopping there while in our school uniforms, but hardly any of the students obeyed that rule. The vivid green foliage of trees whose names I didn't know, planted within the school grounds, spilled out over the narrow sidewalk, casting deep, blue shadows onto the asphalt.

As we neared the main gate, I saw several people hanging around and making a racket. 

Because of club activities, there were students coming and going even though it was summer vacation, but the ones gathered there weren't those kinds of students; they were the badly behaved students commonly known as *yankees*.
