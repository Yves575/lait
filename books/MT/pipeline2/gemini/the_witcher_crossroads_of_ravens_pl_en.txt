Kaedwen is a land wedged between the Dragon Mountains to the north, the Blue Mountains to the east, and impenetrable forests to the west. Hereditary kings of the House of Topp reign there. Their original capital was the ancient city of Ban Ard, but in the year 1130 *post Resurrectionem*, King Dagread granted that stronghold to the sorcerers for a school, and relocated the capital to Ard Carraigh, situated in the heart of the realm.

Other major strongholds in Kaedwen include Ban Fearg, Daevon, Ban Fillim, and Ban Gleann.

The coat of arms of the Topps and the entire realm has been, from time immemorial, a unicorn—*d'or, licorne effrayée de sable*.

Surrounding the royal lands of Kaedwen lie the commonly held borderlands, known as marks or marches. These are entrusted to the administration of margraves, which is to say counts, be they hereditary or appointed by the king. The name stems from the fact that these lands act as a vanguard of sorts, and by advancing ever further, they are to lead the conquest and wrest new territories for Kaedwen from the elves, pushing the boundaries and planting their markers—*id est*, marks—ever further afield. Said marches are: the Western, the Upper, the Lake, and the Lower.

Chapter One

Geralt, despite his sincerest intentions—and for rather important reasons—simply could not focus on the reeve's blathering. His entire attention was consumed by a large stuffed crow on the reeve's desk. The crow, glaring at the witcher with a glass eye, stood on a base of green-painted clay, both of its little legs embedded in said clay. Thus the crow, despite its absolutely lifelike appearance, could not by any means be alive; that was beyond question. Why, then, Geralt wondered in bewilderment, had the crow already blinked its glass eye at him several times? Might it be magic? Unlikely, for his witcher medallion had neither twitched nor vibrated, not once and not even the slightest bit. A hallucination, then? An illusion? Brought on, perhaps, by having been whacked in the head a few times?

"I shall repeat the question," Reeve Bulava repeated. "I shall repeat it, though I am not in the habit of repeating myself."

Reeve Bulava had already assured Geralt several times that he was not in the habit of repeating himself. Despite this, he did so time and again. Evidently he liked to, even if he was not in the habit.

"I will repeat my question: what was this really about? What did you have against that deserter, that you hacked him to pieces so dreadfully? Some old grudge? Because there's no way, you see, I'll believe it was about that peasant and his little girl's maiden virtue. That you supposedly rushed to the rescue. Like some shitty knight-errant."

The crow blinked. Geralt shifted his hands, bound behind his back, trying to stimulate the blood circulation. The rope bit painfully into his wrists. Behind him, he heard the heavy breathing of a village brute. The brute stood right behind his back, and Geralt was certain he was just waiting for an excuse to clout him in the ear again.

Reeve Bulava puffed, sprawled in his chair, and thrust out his belly and velvet doublet. Geralt stared at the front of the doublet, recognizing what the reeve had eaten today, yesterday, and the day before yesterday. And that at least one of those dishes had been served in a tomato sauce.

"I thought," the reeve said at last, "that I'd never have to lay eyes on any of you witchers. Not a single witcher has been seen in years. They said that after the year one hundred and ninety-four, hardly anyone was left alive up there in the mountains. Then word went round that the rest had died off, of hunger or plague. And here you have it, one turns up right in my village. And the very first thing he does is commit murder. Caught red-handed, he has the gall to invoke some fucking edicts."

"By virtue of the express edict of the year one thousand one hundred and fifty," Geralt rasped, clearing his throat, "issued by Dagread, King of Kaedwen and the Border Marches, *primo*: witchers are permitted the free practice of their profession within the territories of the Kingdom and the Marches, and it exempts them from the jurisdiction of local authorities..."

"First of all, *primo*," Bulava interrupted sharply, "it'll soon be half a century since Dagread turned to dust, and his personal autocratic edicts along with him. Secondly, *primo*, no king is going to exempt anyone from under anything, because the king is in Ard Carraigh, far from here, and here, local authority rules. Meaning me. And thirdly, *primo*, you weren't placed under arrest for practicing your profession, pal, but for murder. Catching werewolves and slaying leshys, that's your witcher business. But butchering people—no king ever granted you a privilege for that."

"I acted in defense..."

"Daryl!"

The brute obediently struck Geralt with his fist, this time in the back of the neck.

"Your repeating yourself"—the reeve looked up at the ceiling—"is aggravating. Do you know what aggravation can lead to? Even for a calm man like me?"

The crow glared with its glass eye. Geralt remained silent.

"You," Bulava finally said, "are no witcher. You are a defect. You require fixing. You need to be returned to that mountain Keep of yours, the one people gossip about. I don't know how things work with you over there. It's possible a reject like you gets dismantled into component parts, to be used in the production of new, better witchers. Because that's how it's done over there with you, isn't it? Witchers are assembled from various human parts, sewn or glued together, or however you do it. People say all sorts of things. So then, not to waste breath... I will dispatch you, you failed witcher, back to the mountains, beyond the Gwenllech. In a week's time."

Geralt remained silent.

"Won't even ask why in a week?" The reeve bared his yellow teeth. "You like to invoke edicts and laws. Well, I am all for the law too. And the law states that newcomers are not allowed to bear arms here in the commune. And you walked in here with a weapon."

Geralt wanted to object that he hadn't walked in, but had been dragged in. But he didn't get the chance.

"The penalty," Bulava decreed, "is twenty lashes of the whip. The punishment will be meted out by Daryl here, and he has a heavy hand. You won't be getting back on your feet in less than a week. Come on, take him. Out to the courtyard with him, tie him to the whipping post..."

"Hold on, hold on," said a man entering the room, staying the brutes. He wore a dun cloak, heavily soiled at the hem. "Why so eager for the whipping post and the lash, Bulava? You want to maim my witcher? Nay, nothing doing. I need the witcher hale and hearty at the construction site."

"And why are you"—the reeve put his hands on his hips—"interfering in an execution, Blaufall? It's bad enough I have to suffer you constantly hauling off my villagers for corvée labor. But do not meddle in my jurisdiction; it's none of your business, it's my jurisdiction. A crime must be punished..."

"Pish, no crime," Blaufall interrupted. "There's no crime here whatsoever, it's self-defense and coming to folks' rescue. Don't pull faces, don't pull faces, for I have a witness right here. Step forward, goodman. Come now, don't be afraid. Speak up, tell us how it happened."

Geralt recognized the peasant. The very same one he had saved from robbery yesterday, and who had fled into the woods instead of thanking him. The father of the little girl he remembered stripped down to her shift.

"I testify..." the peasant forced out, pointing a finger at Geralt. "I give my word, that this here youth rushed to my rescue against the bandits... Saved my belongings... Rescued my daughter from dishonor... Delivered an innocent from the robbers' clutches..."

"And that deserter," Blaufall prompted, "lunged at him with an axe, the youth was merely defending himself. Self-defense! Confirm it, goodman, that this was how it went."

"That's how it was... Exactly right! Master Reeve, this youth is guiltless!" The peasant was pale and unnaturally loud. "Master Reeve! Set him free, we beg of ye. And here... Please, take this... By way of, hmm... Perhaps there were some costs or damages... Glad to recompense..."

Bowing obsequiously, the peasant handed the reeve a small purse. Bulava quickly tucked it into the pocket of his puffed trousers, so deftly that the purse didn't so much as clink.

"Self-defense!" he snorted. "Hacked a man to chunks with a sword. Innocent youth... I ought to..."

They went out into the courtyard. The brutes shoved Geralt out without untying his hands.

"And you, Blaufall," asked the reeve, "why so hot under the collar that you even dragged a witness in here? Do you need this witcher that badly?"

"What, as if you don't know? We're laying down a road, the Great Road, from Ard Carraigh through the forests all the way to Hengfors this road's to be. And not just any old thing, not some forest track, but a proper highway, dry and even, paved with logs and fascines so wagons and teams can pass. It's an important thing, the Great Road, heavy trade will flow along it, from our lands, I mean, to the North. The king himself supposedly gave the order to hurry. But there's monsters in the woods and the swamps, every now and then a worker perishes, killed by a beast or carried off..."

"And since when do you care about the workers? You always used to say a worker doesn't matter; if you lose one, there'll be another..."

"Dog shag the workers, they're mostly corvée labor anyway. But sometimes a monster does in a foreman, and that disrupts my work, the work front falls apart. Ah, what's there to say. I'm telling you, I need the witcher, or I'll miss the deadline—not only will my bonus go to the devil, they'll even send an inspection. And an inspection..."

"Will always find something," Bulava nodded knowingly. "Materials sold under the table here, inflated estimates there, or..."

"Don't stray from the subject," Blaufall grimaced. "And release the witcher right now, without delay, I'm taking him straight to the construction site with me... Oh, for... What's going on?"

"Veterans from the watchtower." The reeve shaded his eyes with his hand. "Master Carleton's troops."

A dozen or so horsemen galloped into the courtyard, kicking up dust and scattering chickens. Armed. Dressed colorfully, brightly, and rather raggedly. Only the two at the front were somewhat more elegant. The commander, a mustachioed man in an elk-hide jerkin with a gilded baldric, in a hat with a plume of ostrich feathers. And a long-haired elf with a headband, in the green uniform of a scout.

"Captain Reisz Carleton," Bulava greeted, stepping out to meet them. "Welcome, welcome. To what do we owe the honor?"

Captain Reisz Carleton leaned forward in his saddle and spat forcefully. Then he signaled the scout. The elf rode up to the post with the crossbar and deftly tossed a rope ending in a noose over the beam.

"Oho." Bulava put his hands on his hips, glancing back to make sure his brutes were standing behind him. "Looks like the captain has come to my village for a hanging. Aye, I even see whose lot it is to get the noose today. I see 'em, I see 'em, those two bound up over there... Ha, so the captain's hunted down the deserters from his watchtower! The ones who've been waylaying my villagers and wenches in the woods?"

"Those two," Captain Reisz Carleton twirled his mustache, "I have no intention of hanging. They'll both run the gauntlet, through a lane of switches. To make sure they remember. And that's that. I've too few men to go hanging them over trifles. Or to let some vagrant murder them with impunity."

The captain straightened in his saddle and raised his voice, addressing not just the reeve, but also the village brutes, Blaufall, his lackeys, and the small crowd of villagers that was already gathering.

"What am I to punish my soldiers for? For what? Going absent without leave? Just because they wanted to fuck a wench? We're stuck out here at this watchtower like it's the end of the world, like we're in exile, like it's a punishment. There's neither beer nor women to enjoy out here... Is it any wonder the boys sneak off on the sly now and then, and grab some..."

"Because why the hell," Reisz Carleton raised his voice, "are women wandering around the woods? And this goodman here, what, did he absolutely have to take his little missy through there? Couldn't he have left her at home? So who can blame the boys for wanting to... I don't approve of it! I don't approve, but I understand! Master Aelvarr? Is it ready there?"

"Ready, Captain."

"Then hand the witcher over, Bulava. He killed one of my soldiers, so he goes to the noose. We need to set a terrifying example. And don't cut him down, reeve, let him hang, as a warning to others."

Blaufall stepped forward, making as if to speak, but thought better of it. The brutes grabbed Geralt, but stood hesitantly. With good reason, as it turned out.

It suddenly grew strangely quiet. And a breath of cold air seemed to blow past.

Into the courtyard, from behind the barns, a black, pitch-dark horse stepped at a very slow pace. It bore a rider. A white-haired man in a black leather jerkin, studded across the shoulders with silver rivets. Over the rider's right shoulder jutted the hilts of two swords.

Very slowly, even gracefully, the black horse passed the villagers and the reeve. To come to a halt before Captain Carleton's cavalry.

For a moment, silence reigned. Then the black horse tossed its head. The rings of its bit jingled.

"Master Reeve Bulava," the white-haired rider spoke into the silence, "will immediately release the young witcher. He will return his horse, weapons, and belongings. Immediately."

"Yes..." the reeve coughed. "Yes, sir, Master Holt. Right away..."

"Captain Carleton." The rider bowed slightly. "Greetings."

"Master Witcher Preston Holt." Reisz Carleton touched the brim of his hat. "Greetings."

"The captain," the rider raised his voice, "will kindly take this elf, his rope, and the rest of his subordinates out of here. You are no longer needed. Today's lynching is canceled."

"Is that so?" The captain straightened in his saddle, resting his hand on the guard of his sword. "Are you quite so sure of yourself, Master Witcher?"

"Yes, I am quite sure. Farewell. Master Reeve, is the boy free? His belongings returned?"

"Ah, you son of a bitch!" yelled one of Carleton's horsemen, ripping his sword from its scabbard and spurring his mount forward. "I'll..."

He did not finish. The rider named Preston Holt raised his hand, executing a brief gesture. The air howled and shrieked; the villagers covered their ears. The horseman screamed, flew from the saddle as if shot from a sling, and crashed heavily and limply right beneath the hooves of his companions; their horses spooked, snorted, stamped, and tossed their heads; one reared up. The fallen man's horse bolted between the cottages, bucking and kicking.

It grew very quiet.

"Anyone else?" Preston Holt raised a gloved hand. "Anyone eager to make a stand? To play the hero? No? I thought as much. Farewell, gentlemen soldiers. Is the young witcher already in the saddle?"

"I am," Geralt replied.

"Let us be on our way, then. Follow me."

The Upper March set its border mark upon the river Gwenllech. It is known that the ambition of the local margraves is to mount further incursions into the valleys of the Dragon Mountains, wherefore they are wont to call their domain cismontane, as though it were soon to be expanded by a transmontane portion, once the elves are driven deeper into the mountains. Nevertheless, the years pass by, and no such thing comes to pass.

Chapter Two

The Kingdom of Kaedwen was known throughout the inhabited world for its cold and capricious weather. Enclosed to the north by the barrier of the Dragon Mountains, and to the east by the mighty massif of the Blue Mountains, the land suffered from unpredictable and frequent shifts in air masses, resulting in long, freezing winters, cold springs, and short, rainy summers. As for autumn, it varied—sometimes sunny, warm, and pleasant, and sometimes not at all.

Now, in the month of March, called Birke by the elves, snow still lingered in places among the ravines and windfalls, lying in white patches across hollows in the clearings. Yellowing sheets of ice still covered some of the puddles and ditches. The sun seemed to offer a little warmth, but whenever the wind from the mountains blew in, it burned with a frost every bit as bad as in January.

Geralt set out from Kaer Morhen the day before the Equinox. Such was the witchers’ custom. For experience had shown that, after winter, monsters were at their hungriest and so aggressive that people in villages and settlements were willing to empty their pockets to hire a witcher, even if they had eaten through their stores during the lean season and were practically destitute. But Geralt never had the chance to be hired. For things turned out the way they did: barely two days' ride from the mountains, bam, a peasant and his daughter, marauders, a bald brute with rotting teeth, slash, dash, and there you have it. He wound up facing the judgement of Reeve Bulava from the town of Neuhold, and from that judgement—and the subsequent threat of a lynching by soldiers from the nearby watchtower—he was rescued by that strange, white-haired individual with two swords on his back astride a black horse, whose trail Geralt was now following.

"I propose," the strange individual said, turning in his saddle, "that we ride together for a while. Captain Carleton might still want to hang you; it was obvious he wanted to very badly. He isn't quite stupid enough to come after me, but alone, you could be an easy target. So if you don't mind my company..."

"Not at all," Geralt assured him hastily, urging his dun mare forward. "Gladly... I... I am..."

"I know who you are. Your hair turned white after the mutations? After the Changes? Pigment loss, just like me?"

"Aye... But how..."

"How do I know who you are? Because I keep track of what goes on over there at your Keep. And word reached me that a certain *wunderkind* had finished his training there, that they call him Geralt, and that he was soon to set out on the Path."

"But Vesemir..."

"Never mentioned me? The name Preston Holt never slipped out? Let me explain: Vesemir and I have been moving in different orbits for a while now, so to speak. If you know what I mean."

Geralt didn't quite know what an orbit was, but he nodded with a wise expression.

They rode in silence for a time. Side by side.

"You set out from Kaer Morhen on the Path, then," Preston Holt spoke at last. "Your beginning might not have been the best, but that is how it goes with beginnings. I have no intention of reprimanding you, either; quite the contrary, I examined that marauder's corpse, and your cuts must be deemed impeccable. Perhaps unnecessary, perhaps ill-considered, perhaps inelegant—but all in all, impeccable."

They fell silent again, watching a herd of cattle driven out into the pasture, and a little shepherd boy running from cow to cow to warm his freezing little feet in a fresh, warm cowpat. The cowpat likely provided only moderate warmth, but the running most certainly did.

"They are driving the cows out," Holt observed, "even though the grass is barely poking through the earth. A sign that the season has begun, and you will find work without difficulty, Geralt. The villages will soon be willing to pay for the protection of their herdsmen and livestock. Let's spur our horses, over there, into the birch grove, along the edge of the tailrace."

"The edge of what?"

"The tailrace. This ditch, here, is a tailrace, a channel once used to drain water from a mine. We are, as you surely know, in the part of the kingdom of Kaedwen known as the Upper March. The wealth of the Upper March lies in its minerals: salt primarily, but also silver, nickel, zinc, lead, lapis lazuli, and the like. At least, that is how it used to be; today, most of these minerals seem to be running out. Nothing lasts forever."

Geralt did not comment.

"See that hill ahead of us? It's called Podkurek; that's how it appears on official maps. And it came about because, some hundred years ago, a peasant by the name of Podkurek happened to dig up a lump of silver there the size of a sizable head of cabbage. A mine instantly sprang up here, cutting into the mountainside. Large quantities of silver and galena, lead ore, were extracted here. But the deeper the miners dug, the more trouble they had with water. There are more tailraces like this one around here; you'll see them. Eventually, the drainage costs made the entire operation unprofitable. The miners moved on to other areas. They left behind a labyrinth of tunnels and workings, partially flooded. And now for the best part: the abandoned and flooded mine was occupied and claimed by wandering fretters. You know what fretters are, I trust?"

"*Zatrawiecs*," Geralt recited, taking a breath, "are small creatures resembling dog-headed monkeys. Pack animals, they live underground, in the darkness. In a pack, they are dangerous..."

"They are dangerous as hell," Preston Holt cut the recitation short. "And they quite often plague the amateur miners who come trudging here to grub in the slopes of Podkurek in search of the silver that can still be unearthed. Oh, and here is an illustration of my point: what you see gleaming over there are the canvas covers of wagons and tents. We are heading straight into the camp of the brave diggers. The first ones of the spring."

The brave diggers first greeted them with a delegation armed with shovels and clubs. The menacing expressions of the welcoming committee sent a simple message: get the hell out, strangers, we was here first. Soon, however, their expressions softened—the newcomers were not, as an inspection revealed, unwanted competitors. And behold, upon faces that had so recently been hostile, sheer joy was now painted.

"Heavens above, thank the gods!" cried the chief digger, tucking behind his back the hoe he had been brandishing in warning just a moment before. "Praise be to the gods! Heavens above, if it isn't the noble master witcher! Word reached us you was in these parts, and we was already meaning to send for you! And here you drop right out of the sky for us, right out of the sky!"

"That is my custom." Preston Holt straightened in his saddle. "To drop from the sky for those in need. For I am a witcher."

Meanwhile, several wailing women had joined the diggers. The clamor grew steadily louder and more chaotic. With voice and gesture, Preston Holt began demanding order. It took some time before one could finally make out what the matter was, and what the diggers and their womenfolk were actually going on about.

"A witcher's needed, a witcher!" cried the chief digger, waving his hoe. "Our boy was snatched by them underground creatures what dwell beneath the mountain! Attacked, snatched him up, and dragged him down into the depths! Who else can save him now, if not you?"

"It hasn't even been two weeks," said Holt, "since I warned you to stay away from the shaft and the adit. Well? Didn't I tell you? To dig on the other side of the mountain? The boy, you say, was taken by the *zatrawiecs*? How old? Five? How long ago? Hah, the day before yesterday? You were in no rush to send for me, then. Dismount, Geralt."

Preston Holt dismounted. Geralt could not help but notice that dismounting did not come easily to him. And that he was limping heavily on his left leg. With a gesture, he shooed away the wailing women who had swarmed him. He spoke for a moment with the elder digger, then tugged at Geralt's sleeve.

"Well then, young witcher," he said. "It seems we must help people in need, afflicted by monsters. For we have just been hired."

"If the *zatrawiecs* took the boy the day before yesterday," Geralt grumbled, "then there's little chance he's still..."

"Alive? Yes, that's doubtful. But perhaps we might at least find... Hmm... We can give his mother the chance to bury what's left... Whence this hesitation, young Geralt? Just a moment ago you throw yourself into defending a maiden's honor without a second thought and hack a man to death, and now hesitation?"

"Did I say anything?" Geralt shrugged. "Or that I'm hesitating? I'm not hesitating."

On the mountainside, the remains of wooden structures had survived, most likely drainage whims. A partially caved-in shaft yawned there, revealing the abyss below.

"What's left of the mine is a shaft," Holt explained, "and an adit, over there. Both lead to the upper workings. The workings are winding, as they follow a vein that's completely mined out. From there, a decline slopes downward, leading to the lower workings, which are even more winding, with numerous branches. Below that is yet another adit, but it's completely flooded. The *zatrawiecs* are sitting in that lower drift. There might also be some holes there, connecting to natural caves."

"The plan is therefore this: with my leg, I won't be going down, so I'll head to the adit, make some noise there, and draw the *zatrawiecs* to myself. Then all hope lies with you. You'll descend the shaft to the drift, and from there take the decline down. Down there you have a chance of finding... what needs finding. With a bit of luck. Which I wish you, my boy. See you up top."

"Maybe we should first," Geralt ventured, "scope it out..."

"Do what?" Holt grimaced. "Ah, I understand. There is no point, however, in... scoping anything out. Down your potion, grab your medallion, and climb down the shaft."

"With all due respect," mumbled the chief digger standing nearby, "but this young buck is, well, awful young... Will he manage? We figured it'd be you going down, Master Holt. In your own person..."

Preston Holt turned and looked at him. The digger shrank back, mumbled something. And did not finish.

The drift was high; Geralt could easily walk upright with a few handspans to spare beneath the ceiling. Water dripped from the walls all around; aside from the sound of falling drops, there was nothing to be heard. He quickened his pace, wanting to reach the decline as quickly as possible, before Holt raised a ruckus at the adit just as he had promised.

Deep cavities marked the walls of the workings—traces where pockets of ore had been gouged out. In one such cavity, he spotted a strange brick structure, or rather the remains of one. He had no way of knowing that these were the ruins of an ancient shrine.

Miners, though Geralt did not know this either, had believed in the gods longer than any other social group. Working under conditions of constant peril, they had to believe that some providence watched over them, and that prayers offered to it would guarantee their safety. As one might easily guess, experience soon proved that prayers were useless—that cave-ins and methane explosions happened to the pious just as often as to unbelievers. Yet the miners believed anyway; they built shrines, lit candles, and prayed. For a long time. But not forever. Reason, as usual, triumphed in the end.

The corridor began a sudden, steep descent; he was already on the decline. He strained his ears, but still heard nothing but the dripping water.

He was already right by the lower drift, or so it seemed, when it began.

A large stone whistled out of the darkness, brushing his hair. More followed, some finding their mark. One struck Geralt in the head and staggered him, and the *zatrawiecs* threw themselves at him from all sides, howling and barking wildly, scratching and biting. It was impossible to count how many there were; they darted around too quickly. Geralt yanked his sword from its scabbard, only to lose it instantly—two creatures pinned him, while a third knocked the weapon from his grasp with a blow from a massive chunk of rock. The creature raised the captured sword aloft and roared in triumph, opening its canine maw to its full width. 

The triumph was premature—Geralt threw off both his attackers, snatched up a stone and hurled it, striking the *zatrawiec* square in the teeth. The *zatrawiec* dropped the sword; Geralt leapt forward, caught the weapon before it fell, and cut down the creature as it struggled with the stone wedged in its maw. With two swift slashes, he cleaved two more. And he bolted toward the shaft. Behind him, wild barking erupted and stones flew—again, some finding their mark. One struck him in the back of the head—a flash, and a cascade of stars lit up the workings. A second stone caught him in the small of the back and almost knocked him over. Twice the *zatrawiecs* caught him, sinking their teeth into his calves; his leather half-chaps saved him from serious injury. One sprang at him and bit him painfully above the knee, where the gaiter offered no protection. Geralt did not slow down to kill it.

Under a hail of stones, he reached the shaft, climbed the remnants of a ladder, surfaced, collapsed there, and lay still. For a time.

"Well, well," he heard. "You made it out. And without any major injuries, even. I see blood oozing from a few places. But it isn't gushing from anywhere, and that is something. I am full of admiration."

Preston Holt stood over him, finishing off a roast chicken leg.

"Oh, come on," Geralt groaned, still lying flat. "Come on! You were supposed to be in the adit... Make some noise... Draw them off..."

"Really?" Holt tossed aside a bone. "Ah, yes. Forgive me, I completely forgot."

Geralt swore. With his mouth still to the dirt.

"I'll tell you something else." Holt licked his fingers. "They won't pay us a thing. Because the boy turned up. Walked right in. He was just wandering around somewhere, and the diggers, as usual, blamed the *zatrawiecs*. On your feet, young Geralt. Witcher Geralt. Here, let me help you. Can you walk? Then let's go. Like I said, no coin for us. But they'll feed us and put us up for the night. The girls will dress your wounds. If you ask nicely, maybe one of them will be nice to you."

They headed toward the camp and its smoking cauldrons. Witcher Geralt took each step with difficulty.

The chubby-cheeked girls patched him up in a few places. And fed him. And the diggers let them stay the night. Holt in a tent, Geralt in a wagon.

One of the girls came to Geralt in the night and was nice. But only nice, just a little bit, and nothing more. And she left right after.

At dawn, Geralt clambered out of the wagon and set about saddling his mare, still hissing in pain. Holt caught him in the act.

"What's the rush?" He rubbed his eye. "Wait. They'll treat us to breakfast, then we'll move on."

"Oh, come on," Geralt gritted out. "Maybe I'm not so sure I want to move on anywhere with you. Maybe I'd rather move on alone."

Holt leaned against the trunk of a birch tree and studied the sky. Clear, without a single cloud.

"I fully understand," he said, "your attitude. But I had to—and I emphasize *had to*—test you beforehand, to see what you're like in action."

"I might not have walked out of there."

"But you did."

"No thanks to your help. Which is why now..."

"I ask you," Holt interrupted, "to ride with me at least until noon. Which translates, roughly speaking and factoring in the time of day, to five or six leagues. That time and distance should be enough, I think, for you to blow off the anger you feel toward me and view the world a bit more soberly. And then, I'll have a proposal for you."

"What sort of"—Geralt narrowed his eyes—"proposal?"

"Six leagues. At noon."

It was indeed nearing noon when the sky suddenly blackened with wings, resounding with the beating of feathers and one great cawing. From the ground and the branches of the surrounding trees, dozens, if not hundreds, of black birds burst into flight and flew off.

"Ravens," Geralt sighed. "So many ravens! It can't be! Ravens don't fly in flocks! Never!"

"Undoubtedly," Holt conceded. "So many ravens at once is unprecedented; I am surprised myself. We are undoubtedly dealing with an unusual phenomenon. And we also find ourselves in a rather unusual place. If you've noticed."

"A crossroads." Geralt looked around. "Diverging roads."

"A crossroads. A symbolic place. Four roads, to the four corners of the world. A place of choice and decision. Which you must now make, Geralt. Witcher Geralt."

The ravens settled in the upper branches of the trees. They cawed, watching the riders.

"Three of the roads, including this one of ours, are your paths as a solitary witcher; they are the destiny you chose when setting out on the Path from Kaer Morhen. If you take any of these three roads, if you make such a decision, we will part ways. If, however, you choose the fourth road, you will hear my proposal."

The ravens cawed.

"I am, as you have surely noticed, advanced in years. Well advanced, I might add. You would be surprised just how advanced. I am also—and this is plainly visible and impossible to hide—a cripple. My days of witcher glory are a thing of the past. I will no longer set out on the Path with swords; the light of my blades, so to speak, will no longer scatter the darkness. But the darkness exists, and monsters still bump in the night. You can face them and defeat them. People threatened by monsters look to you for help."

"Right, I'm waxing poetic here, and you're waiting for a proposal. Here it is: I propose a partnership. I was well known here in Kaedwen and still am; I've never complained of a lack of clientele, and even now people ask me for help that I can no longer provide. But you can. I've taken a good look at you and I say: be my successor, Geralt. Instead of wandering the highroads hungry, come live with me. Avail yourself of my renown, and you'll not lack for work. And after a job, you'll have a place to return to. And somewhere to winter. As for me... It will make me happy to see someone continue my life's work. And also, I won't hide it, to have someone help support me in my old age."

"You needn't decide at once. For now, it will suffice if you journey on not alone, but with me. Keep me company a while longer. Agreed?"

"Well, I don't know... Agreed."

"Let's be on our way, then. Let us leave these crossroads to the ravens."

The ravens bade them farewell with a caw.

"No offense," Holt turned in the saddle, "but I insist that you deign to express yourself reasonably correctly in my company. In particular, that you do not say 'scope out' and 'come on'."

Chapter Three

As usual, the cats and the children were the first to notice them. The cats, which swarmed the edge of the town, reluctantly gave way, turning their heads and hissing as they retreated. The children fled to their homes crying and howling, abandoning whatever they had been playing with, primarily lumps of dried mud.

Apart from the cats and children, none of the inhabitants of the town of Spynham paid the arriving witchers the slightest attention. You would think riders in black cloaks with swords on their backs rode into Spynham so regularly that they had completely ceased to arouse curiosity.

Preston Holt knew the town; he knew in which stable to leave the mounts. From there they proceeded on foot, down a muddy lane, scaring off yet more cats and yet more brats.

"Great lord," a beggar woman squatting by a wall with an infant in her lap accosted Geralt. "Great lord, spare a penny... For milk for the child..."

Before Holt could react, Geralt fished a mark out of his pouch and tossed it to the old woman, who thanked him profusely. Holt remained silent for some fifty paces, then finally stopped.

"You are no great lord," he ground out, looking Geralt in the eye. "You are, however, a great fool. For a mark you can buy the milk of twelve cows. For killing a monster, as you will soon find out, they will want to pay you little more. And killing a monster takes a bit more effort than whining for a handout and baring rotten teeth. And the child was probably borrowed from a crony sitting in the tavern, waiting for a client to suck him off in the privy for half a mark. I warn you, don't walk down this lane again; it will be a great laugh if you show up here one more time."

For a moment, Geralt felt the urge to stand up to Holt and talk back to him, to tell him to back off and mind his own business. He didn't do it. Instinctively, he considered Holt his senior in rank, practically a master. Perhaps because Holt bore a striking resemblance to Vesemir. In demeanor and speech. Because physically, he was very different.

Ahead of them gleamed the whitewashed colonnades of a temple, beneath which squatted an entire army of beggar women and men. The temple had been closed and abandoned for years, but the beggars still squatted there. And held out their hands for alms. Out of habit.

Holt prudently pulled Geralt over to the opposite side of the street.

"Do you know why we're called witchers?" he asked. "Because we are the children of witches."

"Oh, come on..." Geralt stammered. "I mean, what a load of nonsense. Witchers from witches. Give me a break."

"You might find it amusing, but it happens to be true. The first witchers were the children of women with uncontrolled magical abilities, known as witches. They were half-witted and often served lusty youths as sexual playthings. The children, the consequences of such games, were abandoned. Or left on doorsteps. And from orphanages and poorhouses, as it happened, they found their way into witcher schools."

"Yeah, right. You made that up. That's not how it was."

"It was. All of us, witchers, descend from feeble-minded wenches. Doesn't that amuse you?"

"Not in the least. Because it wasn't like that at all."

"It was, it was. But a long time ago! You won't find any witches hanging around temples nowadays. The sorcerers exterminated them all. Ha! Nothing lasts forever."

The closer they got to the market square, the more crowded it became. For Geralt, this was a novelty. He wasn't used to crowds; they made him feel ill at ease. The noise grated on his nerves. He was beginning to feel short of breath, and the stench was growing increasingly oppressive. It stank of smoke, burning fat, rotting fruit, manure, and the devil knows what else.

In the market square, they had to enter a gauntlet of stalls and a throng of shoppers. Here, too, nothing but novelties and surprises awaited Geralt. And, ultimately, awe. Never in his life had he imagined that so many crafts existed in the world, or that such an abundance of wares could be offered for sale. And that there were so many eager buyers. Leather straps and goods, glazed and unglazed clay pots, fur hats, short sheepskin coats, moccasins, embroidered shawls, copper skillets, rakes, pitchforks, hoe handles—and pretzels, pretzels, pretzels.

Holt paid not the slightest attention to the marvels on the stalls. At one point, however, he stopped shoving his way forward and grabbed Geralt by the sleeve.

"Eyes down," he hissed. "Eyes down, don't stare."

"Because?"

"Sorceresses."

Geralt obediently averted his gaze. Reluctantly. The two women by the amber jewelry stall drew the eye almost magnetically. With their rich attire. And picture-perfect beauty.

"They," Holt explained once they had moved further away, "treat ordinary folk like cattle, and they hate witchers. They're quick to quarrel, and might take staring for a provocation. It's also best they don't sense our medallions."

Holt stopped before an imposing building, above the doors of which a signboard was nailed. The painting on the sign, quite skillfully done, depicted an amply bosomed mermaid. The lettering beneath the mermaid read: "Lorelei."

Holt grabbed the knocker and knocked vigorously. Very vigorously.

The massive doors opened, and a broad-shouldered brute with a jaw like a loaf of bread stood in them. The brute sized the witchers up for a long moment. Then he stepped back, invitingly making way.

Holt, clearly knowing his way around, unslung both swords from his back unprompted and handed them to the brute. Geralt hastened to follow his example.

Disarmed, they entered an antechamber lit by several lamps. Geralt struggled to catch his breath; the antechamber was heavily suffused with the scent of perfume and incense. On the wall hung another painting of a mermaid. Equally busty. There was no inscription.

"Master Preston Holt," a woman said as she entered.

"Lady Pampinea Monteforte." Holt bowed. Geralt bowed as well. And closed his mouth, which he had opened a moment before. Out of admiration.

Unfamiliar with the art of women's dressmaking, Geralt could not have known that the fabrics of Pampinea Monteforte's black gown were sheer chiffon, muslin, and crepe. Nor did he know that it was a great art to sew a dress so that it concealed while revealing. And vice versa.

Geralt also did not know what the quintessence of femininity was.

But what did it matter that he didn't know, since he could see it.
