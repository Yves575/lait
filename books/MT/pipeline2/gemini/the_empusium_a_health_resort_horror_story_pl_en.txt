1.

Guesthouse for Gentlemen

The view is obscured by billows of steam from the locomotive, which now drift across the platform. One must peer out from beneath them to see everything, allow oneself to be blinded for a moment by the gray mist, until the gaze that emerges from this ordeal is sharp, piercing, and all-seeing.

Then we will see the paving slabs of the platform, squares overgrown with blades of frail plants—a space determined to maintain order and symmetry at all costs.

A moment later, a left shoe appears upon them—brown, leather, not exactly new—and is immediately joined by a second, the right; this one seems even more battered: its toe is slightly scuffed, and the leather reveals paler spots in a few small patches. The shoes stand undecided for a moment, but then the left one moves forward. This movement briefly reveals a black cotton sock from beneath the trouser leg. The black is repeated in the skirts of an unbuttoned melton wool coat; the day is warm. A brown leather suitcase is held by a delicate hand, pale, bloodless; beneath the weight, its veins have drawn taut and now point toward their sources, somewhere deep within the bowels of the sleeve. Peeking out from under the coat is a flannel jacket of rather poor quality, and, what is more, slightly rumpled by a long journey. Visible upon it are tiny, pale specks of unspecified debris—the scales of the world. The white shirt collar, of the detachable kind, has evidently been changed quite recently, for its white is fresher than that of the shirt itself, and contrasts with the sallow hue of the newcomer's face. Pale eyes, eyebrows, and eyelashes make this face appear unwell. Silhouetted against the intensely red western sky, the entire figure gives the unsettling impression of having arrived here, in these melancholic mountains, from the beyond.

Along with the other arrivals, the traveler walks toward the main concourse of a station surprisingly large for this mountainous region. He differs from them, however, in that his pace is unhurried, even reluctant, and that no one is there to greet him, no one has come out to meet him. He sets his suitcase down on the worn tiled floor and pulls lined gloves onto his hands. A moment later, one of them, the right, on a hand curled into a loose fist, travels to his mouth to receive a parade of short, dry coughs.

The young man hunches over and searches his pocket for a handkerchief. His fingers briefly brush the spot where, inside a pocket beneath the coat’s fabric, a passport is concealed. If we focus our attention for a moment, we will see the fanciful handwriting of some Galician clerk, who carefully filled in the sections of the document: Mieczysław Wojnicz, Catholic, student of the Lwów Polytechnic, born 1889, eyes blue, height medium, face long, hair fair.

This Wojnicz is now crossing the main hall of the railway station in Dittersbach, located not far from Waldenburg. He walks hesitantly through the gloomy, high-ceilinged room, where an echo surely dwells upon the highest cornices, and he feels someone’s eyes closely scrutinizing him from behind the ticket windows in the waiting room. He checks the time on the large clock—it is late, the last train from Breslau. He hesitates for a moment, then steps out of the station building, letting himself be instantly embraced by the broad arms of the uneven, jagged mountain horizon.

It is mid-September, yet here, as the newcomer notices to his surprise, summer has long since passed, and the first fallen leaves lie on the ground. The past few days must have been rainy, for a light mist still fills the landscape almost completely, making an exception only for the dark lines of the streams. He feels in his lungs that he is at a high altitude; this is good for his illness-wearied body. Wojnicz stands on the steps of the station, suspiciously eyeing his footwear with its thin leather soles—he is going to have to think about winter boots. In Lwów, asters and zinnias were still blooming, and no one was thinking about autumn at all. Here, however, the high horizon makes everything darker, and the colors seem more vivid, almost vulgar. At that moment, he is overcome by a familiar melancholy, one peculiar to people convinced of their imminent death. He feels that the world around him is but a backdrop painted on a paper screen, that he could thrust his finger into this monumental landscape and bore a hole in it leading straight into nothingness. And that it, this nothingness, would begin to pour out from there like a flood, eventually reaching him too, and seizing him by the throat. He has to shake his head to rid himself of the image. The image shatters into droplets and falls onto the leaves. Fortunately, a clumsy vehicle resembling a light carriage comes trundling down the road toward him. Inside sits a slender, freckled boy in peculiar attire. He is wearing what looks like a military jacket of indeterminate provenance—for it resembles neither a Prussian uniform, which would make sense here, nor any other—and a military forage cap, tipped jauntily on his head. Without a word, he pulls up in front of Wojnicz and, muttering something under his breath, takes his luggage.

"How do you do, my good man?" Wojnicz asks politely in his schoolboy German, but he waits in vain for an answer; the boy pulls his cap down over his eyes and impatiently points to a seat in the carriage.

And they set off at once. First through the town over cobblestones, then along a road that leads them in the gathering darkness down a winding path between steep mountain slopes, through the forest. They are accompanied ceaselessly by the murmur of a nearby stream and its smell, which always unsettles Wojnicz so: the scent of damp undergrowth, rotting leaves, perpetually wet stones, water. He tries asking the driver a question to strike up a conversation—how long the journey will take, for instance, or how he recognized him at the station, or what his name is—but the driver doesn't even look back and remains silent. A gas lantern mounted to the boy's right half-illuminates his face, which in profile resembles the snout of a mountain rodent—a marmot—and Wojnicz gathers that the driver must be either deaf or brazenly rude.

At last, after three-quarters of an hour or so, they emerge from the shadows of the forest and ride into a surprisingly flat valley, an unexpected plateau amidst the wooded mountains. The sky is fading, but you can still make out that piercing, high horizon, which seems to rise right to the throat of any newcomer from the lowlands.

"Görbersdorf," the driver suddenly announces in a boyish, unexpectedly high-pitched voice.

But Wojnicz sees nothing—nothing but a dense wall of darkness that unceremoniously peels away from the mountain slopes in entire sheets. Only when his eyes adjust to it does a viaduct suddenly emerge before them, beneath which they ride into the village, and beyond it the massive bulk of a red-brick building, and then other, smaller structures, and a street, and even two gas lanterns. The brick building turns out to be a colossus, growing before his very eyes, while the motion of the carriage draws rows of lit windows out of the darkness. The light inside is a dirty yellow. Wojnicz cannot tear his eyes from this unexpected, triumphant sight, and keeps looking back at it for a long time until it sinks into the darkness like a massive steamship.

The carriage now turns down a narrow side-lane along the stream, passing over a small bridge where the wheels kick up a clatter resembling the sound of gunshots. At last, it comes to a halt before a sizable wooden building of a peculiar architecture that calls to mind a matchstick house—there are so many verandas, little balconies, and terraces. A pleasant light burns in the windows. Beneath the first-floor windows is a beautiful sign, cut from thick sheet metal in Schwabacher script:

Gästehaus für Herren

Wojnicz steps out of the carriage with relief and draws a deep gulp of this new air into his lungs, air that is said to cure the severest of conditions. Perhaps he does so too soon, however, for he is suddenly seized by a coughing fit so severe that he must lean against the railing of the footbridge. And then, coughing, he feels the cold, unpleasant slickness of rotting wood, and his favorable first impression is shattered. He cannot stop the violent spasms of his diaphragm, and an overwhelming dread washes over him—that he will suffocate at any moment, that this attack will be his last. He tries to keep the panic at bay, just as Doctor Sokołowski had advised, to think of a meadow full of flowers, of the warm sun. He tries very hard, though his eyes water and blood rushes to his face. He feels as though he is about to cough up his soul.

But then he feels a grip on his shoulder—after which a tall, well-built, graying man offers him his hand. Through his tears, Wojnicz makes out his pink, healthy face.

"There, there, my good sir. Let us pull ourselves together," the man says with such confidence and a broad smile that the guest, half-dead from coughing, feels an urge to just lean into his embrace and let himself be led to bed, like a child. Oh yes, exactly. A child. A bed. With some embarrassment, he drapes his arms around the man's neck and lets himself be guided through a vestibule smelling of spruce smoke, and up the soft, carpet-lined stairs. All this evokes some distant association with wrestling, with a manly sport where hard bodies bear down on each other, rubbing, colliding, but not to cause harm, quite the contrary: to show each other tenderness and affection under the pretext of fighting. He yields to the strong hands, allows himself to be led to an upstairs room, seated on the bed, and helped out of his coat and sweater.

Wilhelm Opitz—for this is how the man introduces himself, pointing a finger at his own chest—covers him with a woolen blanket and, from some hands that appear briefly in the crack of the door, receives a mug of hot, savory broth. As Mieczysław takes small sips, Wilhelm Opitz raises his finger (Wojnicz realizes in that moment what an essential part of Wilhelm this finger is) and says in a soft, somewhat comical German:

"I wrote to Professor Sokołowski to have you take a break in Breslau. It is too long and exhausting a journey. I told him."

The broth spreads as a pleasant warmth through Wojnicz’s body, and the poor fellow drifts off to sleep without even realizing it. We keep him company a moment longer, listening to his steady breathing; it pleases us that his lungs have settled.

Our attention is now drawn to a streak of light, thin as a blade, spilling into the room from the corridor and coming to rest on a porcelain chamber pot beneath the bed. The gaps between the floorboards beckon us—and there we vanish.

At a quarter to seven, the sound of a trumpet woke Wojnicz, as a result of which he could not figure out where he was for a long moment. The melody was being played distinctly out of tune, which amused him and put him in a good mood. It seemed familiar to him, but in the way that applies to things so simple they border on genius. It seems they have always existed, and always will.

Mieczysław Wojnicz was composed of ailments of which not even he himself had the best understanding, but rather his father, January Wojnicz, a retired civil servant and landowner. He managed these ailments with great expertise, gravity, and tact, treating the property entrusted to him in the form of his son with great responsibility and—obviously—love, albeit a love devoid of any sentimentality and all those “womanish feelings” he so despised.

One of these afflictions, in the shaping of which he had played a certain role, was his son’s exaggerated fear of whether he might be being spied upon. Thus, young Wojnicz devoted a great deal of attention to the gaze of others, constantly checking to see if he was being watched from around a corner, from a nook, through a window where the curtain was folded back, or through a keyhole. The father’s caution and suspicion had developed into the son’s obsession. He felt as though the gaze of another were something sticky, clinging to him like the soft, hideous mouth of a leech. That was why, in every room where he was to spend the night, he would always carefully inspect the window curtains, plug the keyhole with a wad of paper, check for any holes in the walls or gaps between the floorboards, and even look behind the paintings. In boarding houses and hotels, being spied upon was not so implausible after all—once, when he and his father were staying at a hotel in Warsaw during one of those medical visits to a specialist, young Wojnicz had discovered a regular little hole in the wall, clumsily concealed by the rich pattern of the wallpaper. Naturally, he had plugged it with a pellet of bread, and when he tried to investigate the next morning where the guests might be watched from and by whom, he discovered a service staircase behind the wall, used by the hotel staff. And yet! So he wasn’t obsessed. People do spy on one another. They love it—watching a person when he is unaware of it. Evaluating, comparing. A spied-upon person like this becomes defenseless, becomes an unwitting, powerless victim.

Upon waking, he immediately set about writing a message to his father to reassure him. It was just a matter of a few simple words, and yet he struggled; his hand was numb and weak. He therefore focused all his attention on his hand as it guided the tip of the pencil across the cream-colored paper of a leather-bound notebook. This movement fascinates us; we enjoy it. It brings to mind those same winding lines and spiral ornaments hollowed out underground by earthworms, or gnawed into a tree trunk by a bark beetle. Wojnicz sat up in bed, propped against two massive pillows. In front of him was a clever device—a sort of legless table. Its base was a cushion stuffed with peas, allowing it to rest comfortably on the writer’s lap.

First, two digits emerged, forming a “13,” then a straight line and a cross, “IX,” followed by four more digits making up “1913.” Next, from a series of flourishes emerged the word “Görbersdorf,” thickened several times. The umlaut was treated with particular reverence. Then the pencil set off across the paper, steady and persistent. The graphite scratched, the paper yielding beneath the rounded shapes of the letters.

The room was modest, but comfortably furnished. Two windows looked out over the street and the little river in front of the house, though the view was obscured by crocheted thread curtains. Beneath one of the windows stood a round table and two comfortable, upholstered, slightly threadbare chairs, practically armchairs—a pleasant reading nook, should one want to use it. To the left of the door was a bed with a beautifully ornamented wooden headboard; beside it stood a wardrobe. A dressing table had been placed to the right of the door. The walls were covered in fabric wallpaper with broad, pale blue stripes, making the room seem taller and more spacious than it actually was. On the wall hung engravings from exotic lands: a herd of hares and a herd of hyenas.

Mieczysław Wojnicz briefly wrote down his impressions of the journey in Polish, converting nineteen hundred feet into meters (which came out to nearly five hundred), and added these figures to a sketched map that illustrated his journey from Lwów all the way here. His brief notes mainly concerned the meals en route. Next to the entry for “Wrocław/Breslau,” he added: “Yellow pumpkin soup; for the main course, purée with cracklings, cabbage, and a cutlet just like our pork chops back home. For dessert, vanilla pudding with meringue, and blackberry compote, very tasty.” Underneath, he added: “Paid: 5 marks.” He had promised his father he would write him a few words every day, ideally about his well-being, but in truth he did not know what he was feeling, so he preferred to send recipes or geographical information back to Lwów.

There was a soft knock at the door, and before he could say “come in,” a leather shoe slipped into the gap between the doorframe and the door and gently opened it, followed by the black folds of a skirt, the lace of an apron, and a breakfast tray that was swiftly set down on the table. The shoes, the lace, and the apron vanished as quickly as they had appeared, and a bewildered Wojnicz barely had time to pull the blanket over himself and mutter some greeting and thanks. He was so hungry that his only interest was the food.

He would shortly describe it in his notebook: hard-boiled eggs, two of them, in lovely faience egg cups, covered with little caps in the shape of hens; slices of smoked sheep’s cheese garnished with parsley; a ball of the yellowest butter served on a horseradish leaf; a little bowl of fragrant lard with a small spreading knife; sliced radishes; a basket of various rolls, both light and dark; apricot jam in a glass dish; a mug of thick cocoa, and a small jug of coffee.

After the period, the notebook snapped shut, and Wojnicz ate everything on his tray with relish; then, invigorated by the meal, he stood up. Draping a blanket over his shoulders, he padded over to his suitcase, drew out a neatly folded set of underclothes, and set about his ablutions. And as he wiped his face with a towel that was steeped in the guesthouse’s omnipresent scent of conifers, the image of their country house returned to him as vividly as if alive, along with the linens dried in the attic during winter, which Gliceria would carry up there in buckets whenever it poured outside. The image of the perpetually dusty attic returned, as well as the view from its small windows called ox-eyes—a view of the fields and a small park—along with the bitter scent of rotting tomato, corn, and pole bean stalks. And by the laws of some not entirely comprehensible synesthesia, this image translated into a physical sensation: the roughness of clothing, the stiffness of collars, the sharp creases of freshly ironed trousers, and the pinch of a stiff leather belt. It was there in the attic, whenever he could, whenever he was left alone and free for a moment from his father’s drill, that he would strip naked and wrap himself in a satin tablecloth edged with soft fringe, feeling how deliciously that fringe brushed against his calves and thighs, thinking how wonderful it would be if they, like the ancient Greeks, could go about in such tablecloth chitons. Now, however, recalling that satin toga as he got dressed, he rejoiced in finally feeling strong and rested.

We bear witness as clothing appears in layers upon his slender frame until, at last, his figure—so entirely different from yesterday’s coughing, ashen-faced version—stands with a hand on the doorknob, eyes closed, imagining how he would look to someone who might be watching him right now. He looks good—a slender young man with fair hair and delicate features, wearing gray striped trousers and a brown woolen jacket. After a moment, he decisively opens the door.

No, we do not consider this to be an obsession—at most, an innocent oversensitivity. People ought to get used to being observed.

Wojnicz went downstairs at around ten o'clock, as he had an appointment for examinations at the Kurhaus.

Throughout the entire house, owing to the small windows sparsely placed in the walls, there reigned a twilight gloom—typical of mountain architecture. Here stood an oval table covered with a thick patterned tablecloth, a sofa, and a few chairs, while against the wall sat an upright piano, its infrequent use betrayed by stray fingerprints on the glossy lid and a stack of yellowed sheet music. A small hanging shelf nearby was filled with books about the region, local ski trails, and historical landmarks. A beautiful white porcelain service with sentimental cobalt-blue scenes of shepherds and sheep gleamed white inside a massive glass-fronted cabinet.

“Gemütlich,” Mieczysław whispered to himself, pleased to have remembered a certain German word he particularly liked. His language lacked a word like that. Cozy? Pleasant?

The words of Doctor Sokołowski also came back to him, from the time when the doctor had first begun treating him and battling his apathy—that life ought to be made appetizing. Yes, appetizing, that was a better word than gemütlich, Wojnicz thought, for it applied not only to a space, but to everything else as well: to someone’s voice, to the way they spoke, sat in an armchair, or tied a scarf at their neck, to the way cookies were arranged on a saucer. He ran his finger across the table, which was draped in a soft olive-green plush, and only after a moment did he notice, with a start, a slender man sitting in the armchair by the window, with striking, birdlike features and wire-rimmed glasses perched on a prominent nose. A cloud of cigarette smoke enveloped him. Wojnicz’s hand recoiled from the plush as if burned and, embarrassed, vanished into the clasp of the other. The man, equally flustered by this intrusion upon his solitude, rose and introduced himself rather formally, in German, with a peculiar Silesian accent:

“Walter Frommer. From Breslau.”

Wojnicz stated his first and last name slowly and clearly, no doubt hoping the other man would remember them at once. They conversed for a moment, and Frommer had time to inform him that he received regular treatment in Görbersdorf and had been coming here, off and on, for three years now. Occasionally he would return to Breslau for a short while, but his condition would immediately worsen there.

“You see, the city of Breslau lies on the water. In the spring, swarms of mosquitoes—small but extraordinarily venomous—hover over the buildings, and the people suffer from rheumatism. In the summer, it is impossible to sit out in a garden; that is why civil servants only stay there for a short stint of a few years before returning to better places. Breslau is a transient city.” A note of sadness crept into his voice, as if he pitied the place. “It’s because of the omnipresent water, it seeps in everywhere... I tolerate it poorly...” He began to cough. “Oh, you see, I cough just thinking about it.”

Wojnicz averted his gaze toward the window, beyond which a merry company was just passing by, bursting into frequent laughter. He thought these people were laughing in Polish, though he couldn't quite explain the impression to himself. From a distance, no words could be heard.

“Are you preparing to move to the Kurhaus as well?” he asked Frommer.

He thought the question would elicit at least a faint smile on his interlocutor's face, but the man took it seriously.

“God forbid,” he bristled. “There are too many people there. You can't see a thing from over there. You won't find out anything or learn anything. Living in a crowd is worse than prison.”

Well then, Wojnicz had seemingly made up his mind about Walter Frommer: an eccentric.

They were both, it seemed, equally shy, for they stood facing each other in awkward silence for a moment, each waiting for the other to utter some conventional remark. They were rescued from this impasse by Wilhelm Opitz, the host.

"I hope I am not interrupting an animated conversation," he said, and Wojnicz wondered for a moment whether Wilhelm was mocking them or if he was simply that unobservant. But Wilhelm took him firmly by the arm and steered him toward the exit.

"Excuse me, but I must hand the young man over to the watchful eye of Doctor Semperweiß. Our guest arrived here in a sorry state."

Frommer mumbled something indistinct, returned to his spot by the window, and sat back down in the exact same position as before. As if he were employed here full-time as a smoking piece of furniture.

"Doctor Frommer is a little odd, but he is a decent man. Like everyone in my guesthouse," Wilhelm said in that dialect of his, which was becoming increasingly pleasant to Wojnicz's ear, as they stood on the steps outside the house. "The boy will take you to Doctor Semperweiß. Watch out for him; he doesn't like people from the East. He doesn't like anyone at all, really. It's a great pity that there is no one here like Doctor Brehmer anymore," he added thoughtfully, when they paused by the little bridge a moment later.

Wojnicz watched as the mist now formed strange wisps and drifted upward like smoke.

"Do you perhaps know Doctor Sokołowski?" he asked.

Wilhelm's face brightened and grew animated.

"Of course, I knew him when I was a child. He was friends with my father, who worked for him. We all work at the kurhouses here. How is he doing?"

Well, Wojnicz didn't know exactly. He only knew that the doctor worked at a clinic in Warsaw, and that he occasionally gave lectures in Lwów. His father used to take him for consultations whenever Sokołowski visited the city. And it was thanks to him that he had ended up here.

"Is he still so thin?" Willi added.

Thin? No, not thin. Professor Sokołowski is a squat and stout man. Wojnicz, however, did not have to answer this surprising question, because emerging from the streaks of mist just then was Rajmund, the driver from yesterday, a teenage youth whom Wilhelm greeted in a rather peculiar manner: with a light cuff to the head. The boy accepted this as a perfectly natural, friendly gesture.

The two of them now walked downstream toward the center of the village. Rajmund was recounting something with great enthusiasm, but he spoke in such a strange dialect that Wojnicz understood very little. He observed with interest the beautiful houses lining the road and the workmen repairing the electrical lines. Rajmund asked if Mieczysław knew what it was: electricity.

Then they bowed to two older women in wide skirts sitting on a bench in front of one of the houses.

"Frau Weber and Frau Brecht," Rajmund said with an ironic smile, and that, at least, Wojnicz understood.

A moment later, the boy proudly pointed out Doctor Brehmer's sanatorium, the same building Wojnicz had seen the evening before; now, however, it seemed even more imposing to him, especially since the mist had almost disappeared and, somewhere high up beyond the valley, the September sun was shining generously.

Rajmund disappeared as soon as he had led Wojnicz to the correct door in the broad corridor. Now, Mieczysław was taken in hand by a nurse whose eyes were accentuated by red puffiness. A brief, polite smile momentarily revealed her large, yellowed teeth, whose color harmonized with the worn gold plating of the chained watch pinned to her apron. Embroidered above her small pocket was her full name: Sydonia Patek.

Wojnicz had to bide his time in the waiting room of the doctor’s office, as the doctor had not yet returned from his rounds. His fingers therefore reached for the illustrated magazines laid out for the patients, but his eyes found no solace in them; they could not focus on the Schwabacher script. To his surprise, however, Wojnicz found a small pamphlet in Polish, and his eyes instantly and miraculously relaxed upon the words of his native tongue:

In Prussian Silesia, a quarter of a mile from the Bohemian border and eleven miles southwest of Wrocław, in a long valley stretching from east to west between the Riesengebirge and the Adlergebirge, lies the charming village of Görbersdorf in the Waldenburg district, situated on the river Steina. For several decades, it has been renowned as a mountain climatic health resort for chest patients.

Görbersdorf is situated at an elevation of 570 meters above sea level, within a zone that medical science terms “free of consumption.” The surrounding mountains reach heights of 900 meters. They shelter the village and its sanatoriums from the winds, which arrive here weakened; hence, there prevails in Görbersdorf a stillness of the air such as is rarely found in any other valley.

He read no further, but folded the pamphlet in half and slipped it into his pocket. Now his attention was drawn to a glass display case housing a human torso carved from wood—headless, armless, and legless, with its chest and abdomen open, it displayed internal organs painted in various colors. Wojnicz stepped up to the wooden trunk to examine the lungs. They were smooth and clean, polished and gleaming with varnish. They resembled the fleshy petals of a monstrous flower, or mushrooms growing on the bark of trees. How miraculously they adapted to the dimensions of the chest, how they reconciled their airy nature with the cage of the ribs. He examined them closely, trying to peer beneath the pointed lower tip, where they met other, coiled organs of various colors. In spite of everything, however, he was disappointed; perhaps he had been expecting something new, something he did not yet know. The solution to the mystery. Why he was sick. And why others were not.

When he returned to his seat, he was overcome by a familiar anxiety, that agitation which always triggers the same bodily reaction: the sweats. He would have to undress and expose his body to the gaze of a stranger. And the panic: how he would hide his shameful affliction from the doctor. What he would have to say so as not to touch upon all those sensitive matters. How to slip away. He had practiced this so many times before.

When the doctor burst into the waiting room, he did not even look at him—he crossed it with a rapid step, only the tails of his white coat billowing behind him. He gestured for the patient to stand. Mieczysław followed the doctor at a near-trot into a spacious consulting room with a huge window, filled with glass-fronted cabinets, assorted medical devices, and peculiar chairs. For some reason, it did not surprise Wojnicz that a rifle stood leaning against the doctor's desk—a large one, not a hunting rifle at all, more likely a Winchester, with a beautifully polished stock. Without turning around, the doctor told him to sit, and so Wojnicz felt safely concealed behind the desk, as though in a trench.

He handed the doctor the letter of recommendation from Professor Sokołowski, but the doctor merely glanced at it, visibly more interested in the body seated before him. The young man felt ill at ease, unsettled by the way the other man was looking at him. It was as though he did not see Mieczysław Wojnicz, the patient from faraway Lwów, but solely the body itself, something objectified and mechanical. First, unabashedly, he pulled down Wojnicz’s lower eyelid, carefully examining the color of the mucous membranes and the eyeball. Next, his gaze swept from the chin to the temples; finally, he ordered him to strip to the waist, cast a critical eye over his chest, and began pressing the patient's nipples with his finger.

“Slightly enlarged, much like the lymph nodes,” he said. “Are they always like this?”

“For a few years now,” replied the intimidated Wojnicz.

The doctor gripped him under the chin and ran a finger over his wispy, uneven two-day stubble. He meticulously felt the lymph nodes, and then his bold fingers tapped his back, drawing forth a hollow sound, a rumbling like that from underground. He did this very carefully, centimeter by centimeter, like a sapper searching for a hidden bomb. The whole process took about half an hour, until at last the doctor sighed and told him to get dressed. Only then did he reach for the letter. He spoke, peering over the metal frames of his glasses:

“Phthisis.” It sounded as if he had whistled. “Tuberculosis, consumption, or as it is fashionably called today: Morbus Koch. You know all this, young man, don't you?”

Wojnicz finished buttoning his shirt and nodded in affirmation.

“Not very advanced, quite frankly. Just a little something, a grain of something. Phthisis means decay, you know.” He pronounced the word, Zerfall, with distinct pleasure, rolling the ‘r’. “But here, we know how to deal with decay.”

“Yes, Dr. Brehmer’s method…” Wojnicz began, but the doctor stood up impatiently and waved him off with a raised hand.

"Oh yes. Brehmer noted that this traveling to Italy with tuberculosis makes no sense at all. Only mountain air truly heals. Like the air here. Have you seen?" The doctor stepped over to the window and lost himself in thought for a brief moment. "We are in a basin here," he said, and as he spoke he made circular, exaggeratedly wide sweeping movements with his arms, as if to more forcefully impress the nature of this phenomenon upon his listener. "Beneath us lies a vast underground lake, which makes it warmer here than elsewhere. The air is rich in oxygen, yet there is no wind. Lung diseases and epidemics have never been known among the local population, can you believe it? No one here has ever suffered from lung disease. And furthermore, the altitude remains within the bounds necessary for treating lung diseases, because, then again, it does not excessively accelerate the heart rate, as is the case in places situated more than nine hundred meters above sea level. A fir forest grows here, which saturates the air with ozone, and ozone plays a crucial role in renewing the blood and the entire organism. The simple act of breathing will halt the process of decay in your young lungs. Every breath is medicinal; you should look at it that way. Imagine that with every breath, pure light flows into your lungs." The doctor looked at Mieczysław through the lenses of his spectacles, which magnified his dark eyes in an unsettling manner. "And apart from that, we have other attractions here. You need only to comply, to submit to the treatment regimen. Consider yourself in the army."

He went to the window and, with a nod of his head, pointed out the patients strolling in the park.

"Those are your comrades in arms."

Wojnicz suddenly realized that he was not going to like this doctor. He was reminded of the gentle, kind Doctor Sokołowski.

"That is clear to me, Doctor," he replied, pulling down his shirt cuffs. "I would only like to know if I have a chance."

"Of course you have a chance. Otherwise you wouldn't have come here at all, young man. You wouldn't have dared to come here if you didn't feel you had a chance. You would be quietly ailing away in the East. It is flat there, isn't it?"

Wojnicz was now to learn many interesting things about the brilliant Dr. Brehmer, who had bought the village of Görbersdorf and the entire surrounding area—totaling over a hundred hectares of forests and land—in order to establish this sanatorium. Brehmer had long ago observed that the results of autopsies and examinations of living tuberculosis patients always showed a disproportion between the heart and the lungs: their lungs were relatively large, while the heart was small, with thin, flaccid, and weak walls. Until then, no one had paid much attention to this correlation, nor had anyone thought to link this disproportion of the thoracic organs with the etiology of tuberculosis. Yet it seemed obvious that a small, weak heart led to sluggish blood circulation, and consequently to chronic ischemia of the lungs and the pulmonary epithelium. The result was tuberculosis. Furthermore, Brehmer had studied the geographical distribution of the disease, which only strengthened his conviction regarding the aforementioned etiology. Travelers’ accounts indicated that there were places and regions where tuberculosis did not occur: high mountains in all climate zones, Iceland, the Faroe Islands, and the steppes of Kirghizia.

For one thing, the specific properties of a high-altitude climate were the deciding factor here. Lower atmospheric pressure causes the body to react with increased cardiac activity and an accelerated pulse, as it defends itself against hypoxia—this leads to a faster metabolism and an elevated body temperature. Secondly, lifestyle and diet played a crucial role: abundant food, especially fat, alcoholic kumis, and hard physical labor.

An accelerated pulse and an elevated heart rate lead to an increase in the volume of the heart muscle, building up a strong musculature in the organ; frequently, the inhabitants of the aforementioned regions even develop cardiac hypertrophy—a phenomenon exactly opposite to that found in tuberculosis patients.

"My dear young man," Dr. Semperweiß concluded his lecture. "That is our entire prescription. In Central Europe, the tuberculosis-free zone begins at an elevation of roughly four hundred and fifty meters. Add to that the constant supervision of a physician to regulate one's diet. Furthermore, exercise in the fresh air. Nature herself heals us."

Dr. Semperweiß took out a sheet of paper and listed his recommendations point by point, commenting on them aloud in a voice already tinged with slight boredom:

“At least six weeks, but preferably several months. Walks tailored individually to the patient, strictly along routes with varying inclines; there must be benches at short intervals along the way so you do not overexert yourself. Moderate cold-water treatment. And that is exactly what will help you. Moderation in medication. In the event of severe coughing fits, I recommend suppressing the cough as much as possible, and when it cannot be suppressed, taking small sips of cold water, or soda water with hot milk. Should bleeding from the lungs occur, God forbid, we apply ice packs to the heart and lungs here, along with morphine injections. During severe attacks, with shortness of breath and weakness, we first administer a strong stimulant, such as champagne. Yes, do not be afraid of champagne, or alcohol in general. But absolutely in small quantities. Drunkenness is strictly forbidden! In the event of a fever, temperature readings are first taken every two hours to confirm it. Night sweats are effectively combated by an evening consumption of milk with two or three teaspoons of cognac or tincture. Sister Sydonia Patek will explain and show you everything.”

While delivering this entire speech, he was writing down the recommendations, and Wojnicz marveled that he was able to do both things at once.

“You are staying at Herr Opitz’s, is that right? Every day you will come to the Kurhaus for treatments and the rest cure, and as soon as a place opens up in the sanatorium, I will let you know; everything here is fluid. Fluid,” he emphasized. “For now, Herr Opitz’s guesthouse is just as good for your health as we are, or Dr. Römpler’s sanatorium, and the daily short walks will put some color in your cheeks.”

The doctor stood up briskly and handed Wojnicz the slip of paper with his recommendations. And that was that—he had been admitted.

Now he sat in the waiting room until the ugly nurse had prepared his treatment diary and other necessary documents. He pulled the scrap of a leaflet, folded in four, from his pocket and finished reading:

Generally speaking, it must be acknowledged that, from a medical standpoint, a stay in such locales as Meran in the Tyrol, Görbersdorf in Silesia, or Davos in Switzerland, which was modeled on Görbersdorf, has thus far been regarded as the most effective cure. Dr. Römpler’s Sanatorium, founded in 1875, lies almost directly at the foot of the mountains and consists of a suitable number of buildings in the form of elegant villas. A water conduit 1,140 meters in length brings crystal-clear mountain spring water, gushing straight from porphyry rocks, down from the mountain at whose foot the sanatorium lies, into the elegantly appointed bathing rooms of the shower house.

Patients do not lack for activities and amusements. The cure itself, dinners, and so on, take up a large part of the day, and the lovely immediate environs of Görbersdorf offer plenty of excursions. The aim of the cure is that the patient should strive to fight off the illness himself. He must be rendered resistant through the strengthening of his organism. In this way, the progression of the disease will first be halted, then the affliction will gradually begin to subside, and health will start to return. Through continuous gymnastics, the affected lungs learn to function correctly; the fresh mountain air stimulates cardiac activity. The results of the Görbersdorf cure can be counted among the most favorable. Almost 75% of patients recover.

It would be wonderful to believe he belonged to that very seventy-five percent.


2.

Schwärmerei

Wojnicz was walking back to the guesthouse carrying a small notebook, in which his medical history was to be recorded from now on, pondering what Dr. Semperweiß had told him. The most important thing in the cure is the regimen. One gets up early, very early in the morning. Taking one's temperature. Noting it down in the notebook. Before breakfast, which is taken between seven and eight, gymnastics are mandatory; after breakfast, a walk, with optional baths using Father Kneipp's method and other treatments along the way. Walks along a designated route. Second breakfast at ten—always fresh bread and butter with milk. The rest cure on one of the many terraces. Dinner between twelve thirty and one thirty (meat soup, a hearty meat dish with vegetables, then dessert and compote; on Sundays, instead of compote, something sweet is served—cake or a sweet pudding). After dinner, coffee in the winter garden or the pavilions is absolutely mandatory. More rest cure, another walk, but the afternoon route must differ from the morning one. Afternoon tea around four or four thirty, and supper at seven—hot meat with potatoes, and, absolutely without fail, a glass of milk. In the evening, the thermometer again, and a few sentences in the notebook about how one is feeling. Plenty of sleep. No excitement. Good, hearty food. Lots of meat, milk, and sheep’s cheese. Wojnicz had decided to take his dinners and breakfasts at the Kurhaus; he would eat his suppers at the Guesthouse for Gentlemen. That was what he had been advised. Once he moved to the Kurhaus, he would take the rest of his meals there. Guests are summoned to them by a bugle call.

He was bursting with excitement and good intentions; it was the kind of euphoria that comes when starting something new, something that irrevocably heralds a fresh start, when a person cuts himself off from the old and consigns what used to be to oblivion. Now, even the stern, ironic Semperweiß seemed to him a herald of change.

As he walked, he tried to memorize the layout of the houses and guesthouses. He took in the rather bizarre building of the astronomical observatory, where Dr. Brehmer supposedly studied the influence of the cosmos and the weather on the treatment of tuberculosis. Then he reached the massive edifice of Villa Rosa and turned back.

A full, golden September sun was shining. Mieczysław Wojnicz aimed his feet at the center of the large, flat stones that paved the road.

Two older women were still sitting on the bench in front of the house, shelling broad beans in silence, snapping the dry, crackling pods. One of the beans suddenly popped from one of their wrinkled hands and landed right in front of his shoe. He picked it up carefully between two fingers, intending to return it to its owners, but for some sudden, inexplicable reason they sprang from the bench, gathered up their bowls and baskets, and vanished into the house. Only their glossy black skirts flashed in the sun. Ah well, no harm done. Wojnicz rubbed the brown bean against his sleeve; it seemed perfect. He tossed it in the air and caught it. Not knowing what else to do with it, he slipped it into his pocket.

He found the door to the guesthouse wide open, which surprised him, and immediately afterward he spotted a prayer book on the ground, tossed directly into a puddle. Its cream-colored pages were already soaked with dirty water. He picked up the prayer book and stepped inside, filled with a sudden unease.

The downstairs parlor was empty—presumably all the guesthouse residents were still away at their treatments. He placed the mud-stained little book on a table and was just about to head upstairs when his attention was caught by the half-open door to the dining room, and beyond it, a pair of shoes—on the table, looking somehow familiar. Without thinking, as if hypnotized, he walked up to the door and pushed it open to get a closer look.

The shoes formed the lower end of an elongated bundle of indeterminate shape, which ultimately proved to be a human body. It lay on the dining room table, the very table where meals were eaten. It looked tightly wrapped in folds of fabric—to Wojnicz, it seemed to be wearing a multitude of skirts, chemises, corsets, and shawls. Wojnicz had never seen a woman from this close up, nor one so utterly motionless; they were always slipping past, always in motion. It was impossible to focus his attention on them and take in every detail. But now he had just such a body before him, and without a doubt, it was a dead body. He stared at the black lace-up boots protruding from beneath the skirts and petticoats. The latter were trimmed with some kind of embroidery, though the lace was somewhat worn from washing, its edges beginning to fray. The shoelaces had been meticulously tied into double bows; it was strange that someone who was no longer alive by the afternoon had laced up their boots so carefully that very same morning. The outermost skirt, made of a slightly glossy fabric with thin black-and-gray stripes, was neatly arranged. Above that was a sort of tight-fitting jacket made of dark, almost black broadcloth, fastened with round buttons—the kind Polish priests wear on their cassocks. From beneath this garment protruded a white shirt, rather gaping, with a torn-off button leaving behind nothing but a thread. The shirt’s collar was pulled high up beneath the chin, but carelessly enough that Mieczysław could spot a purplish-red welt on the neck, shocking against the white skin.

In the end, he had to do it—look up, at the face. To his horror, he saw half-closed eyes, and beneath the lashes, a thin sliver of gleaming eyeball. The twisted head was turned toward him, as if it wanted to confess something. On the lips, narrow and already slightly blue, he noticed the trace of a smile—it struck him as entirely out of place, almost ironic. The tips of her teeth showed beneath her upper lip, completely dry. And one more thing—her face was covered in a fine, pale fuzz, like down.

He stood petrified, barely breathing.

He recognized almost immediately that this was the woman who had brought him his breakfast that morning. At the time, he had only registered the shoe that nudged the door open. And her ample, corset-bound figure. Nothing more. Only now, in death, could the woman be seen in her entirety.

"She hanged herself," said Willi Opitz, standing in the doorway.

Wojnicz flinched, startled by his host’s low, resonant voice. Opitz announced this in a tone that suggested he was reporting some reprehensible negligence, a wholly unacceptable occurrence. Yet his voice trembled.

"Please don't upset yourself. The men from the morgue will be here shortly to take the body away. Rajmund ran to fetch them."

Wojnicz didn’t know what to say. His tongue was bone-dry, and he felt a tight band around his throat.

"When did this happen?" was all he asked.

"When? Just now, well, an hour ago. I went upstairs to check on her when she didn't come down to take the vegetables from the delivery man. She was hanging there. I cut her down. Go back to your room, my boy. Ah, the men from the morgue are here."

"She brought me my breakfast this morning," Wojnicz said, an involuntary agitation evident in his voice. "She was your maid, wasn't she?"

"Ah, no, no. That was my wife."

Opitz waved a hand, as though swatting away a wasp, and opened the door for the somber workers from the morgue, who began speaking to him in a quiet dialect. Mieczysław retreated from the dining room—hurrying upstairs, he heard their muffled voices but couldn't understand what they were saying. The entire exchange sounded to him like the low muttering of men who didn't need words to understand one another.

Wojnicz sat down in the rust-colored armchair adorned with a crocheted antimacassar. He was shaken. It was strange; it hadn't occurred to him that the amiable Willi Opitz had a wife. He ought to have known that men usually had wives of some sort, who, though not always clearly or distinctly visible, supported the family’s interests from the kitchen or the laundry room. Preoccupied with himself, his arrival, and his illness, he hadn't even noticed her. And now she was dead.
