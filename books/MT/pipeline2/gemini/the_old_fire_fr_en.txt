NOVEMBER 6

Because of the rain, I missed the village sign. It blurred the hollows, erased the ruts; I ended up driving blind and pulling over onto the shoulder. All this water beating down on the hood. The storm started yesterday. I haven't passed anyone since the highway exit. Even though the radio advised staying off the roads, I didn't have a choice. It's five o'clock, the sky anthracite. I couldn't manage to adjust the tilt of the seat. I wait, perfectly upright, dazed by the din. At least the van feels sturdy. It looks like a municipal maintenance vehicle, plasma-colored. I insisted on practicality at the rental agency.

An hour passes. Finally, the downpour eases up. I start the engine again. The GPS leads me deeper into the forest. Soon, neither rain nor light pierces the canopy. I turn on the high beams. The steering wheel is sticky. I drive for miles at a crawl, guessing the path between the tracks under the brambles, until I emerge at the foot of a steep slope. A little further up, the gate is open. For the first time, I repeat my father's motions. I shift into first, accelerate, the wheels spin in the gravel but hold fast, I cut the engine in front of the house. The motion-sensor light flicks on. A rabbit darts away.

The building looks tired, the roof sagging over the bricks like a giant suffocated by ivy. A car is parked beneath the hazel tree. Ferns force apart the steps of the porch. Through the window, I can make out a light. I press my face to the peephole, then immediately recoil. I wasn't expecting my sister's face—enormous forehead, wide-set eyebrows, fish eyes—my sister bloated by this magnifying lens my father claimed to have deliberately installed backward. According to him, we had nothing to fear and nothing to hide; our wealth was internal, and the whole world needed to know that the most beautiful people lived here.

"Hi."

My voice sounded louder than I'd intended. Véra answers with a smile too large for her mouth. She takes the suitcase from my hands and sets it down at the bottom of the stairs in the kitchen. I rediscover the stone floor, the wooden furniture, the bathroom door in the shadow of the fireplace. I had never known it like this, the hearth blocked up with books. Above the table, a birdcage has replaced the light fixture. Cheeses are stacked behind the bars.

Véra points to the stairs, then to herself at the kitchen counter; I am to settle in while she finishes making dinner. I’d always known her to be messy. I compliment her. She types on her phone, shows me the screen:

*It's to give you a proper welcome.*

I reply a bit curtly that we are sisters and this is my home too, let's dispense with that kind of politeness. She lights the gas burner with a hasty gesture. I can't help but add:

"Especially since we're not keeping anything."

The stairs swish beneath my socks. I have to be careful not to slip. Our parents' bedroom door is ajar. I linger on the threshold, in the draft of the poorly insulated French doors. Black floorboards. In the center of the room, the large bed, the bare mattress, no sheets or blanket. I still wonder how my parents could sleep without a wall behind their heads. I close the door, vaguely relieved. I don't know what I dreaded more: sleeping in that bed, or sharing our old room with my sister now that we're adults.

Her sweet perfume catches in my throat. She has kept our bunk beds. Seeing them tonight saddens me. The wrought iron looks too thin to hold us. The dresser and desk are in their usual places, as plump as ever, painted salmon. I make sure I have an internet connection. I’m going to spend nine days here; I need to be able to communicate with my colleagues. The network shows only a single bar. Sometimes even that disappears.

Leaning out the window, I spot the van. Its orange color makes me laugh. It looks like a giant bumblebee. It clashes as much as my sister and I, reunited for the first time since my father’s death five years ago.

Véra has poured wine into the crystal glasses. Put on edge by this ceremony, I tell her I don’t drink. She raises her eyebrows and pours the wine back into the bottle; it drips, I wipe it up with my sweater, then take it off—I’m hot. The bamboo tableware is unfamiliar to me. Proudly, Véra shows me the cheese wrapper, decorated with chestnuts, and then points to the fireplace: it’s a smoked cheese. I push aside the thought that it’s made with raw milk. She has made an endive salad with figs and walnuts. I ask if she’s thought about how we should proceed over the next few days. I haven’t; I’ve been very busy. She taps on her phone:

*Congratulations on your award.*

I murmur that it’s sweet of her.

I don’t know how much she knows about the films I’ve written. The latest one just won an award at an Italian film festival; I wasn’t able to go, and I hadn’t invited her anyway.

"Have you heard from Octave?" I ask, in a voice I hope sounds neutral.

She nods—of course—and points to the figs, the walnuts, they come from him... I cut her off. I tell her that, as far as I’m concerned, there’s nothing I want to keep. She can make her selection, and we’ll drop the rest at the dump. Her fingers tighten around her phone. With her chin, she gestures toward the wardrobe, the kitchen, the bathroom. I look up—surely we aren’t going to rummage through all of that? Her face is illuminated by the backlight of her screen:

*Whatever you want.*

I soften. It’s just that I have work to do. I’ve fallen behind. I’m going to have to isolate myself to write. With a simplicity that disconcerts me, she shows me the screen again:

*Whatever you want.*

Then she asks about my current projects. I mention my latest commission, adapting Georges Perec’s novel, *W, or the Memory of Childhood*. Véra punctuates my sentences with smiles. I feign nonchalance as I mention the prestige of the production, the fame of the prospective cast, and that of my co-writers. We have to write six episodes. Shooting is scheduled in two years. Véra claps. I downplay it. Adapting this text isn’t easy. And I’m just the dialogue writer. I start to introduce Perec, but she nods vigorously; she knows, she’s read *La Disparition*.

"You read?"

She gestures that it’s obvious.

"I don’t know, my father..."

Silence.

"I mean Dad. He hadn't told me."

Her smile never faltering, Véra serves me the last of the figs. I was fifteen, Véra twelve, when I left for the United States. My stay was supposed to last through high school, living with a host family. Véra had long since stopped speaking. She was learning to read and write, but I didn't think she was this capable.

"And how are you?" I ask, realizing I haven’t asked her a single question since I arrived.

Our last exchanges date back to last year, regarding her move to Périgueux. Up until then, she had lived here with my father, and stayed on even after his death. I helped her from a distance. The studio apartment was already furnished, and she didn’t want to take anything from our childhood home without my agreement. Now that the sale has been signed, Véra was counting on me coming to empty it.

I read over her shoulder. She makes an irritated gesture; I need to give her time. I apologize, serve myself some more salad.

Following a redrawing of the property lines, our house is no longer considered a tenant farm belonging to the neighboring estate, the Pigeon Froid, owned by Octave’s family. Safety standards have changed. To comply with them, we'd have to overhaul the roof, the heating, the electrical system; we don't have the means, and have accepted an offer from a campsite that is going to raze it. Octave wants to salvage our stones to rebuild the dovecote.

Véra shows me the screen. Shop work bored her. She is training in floral stabilization.

"Stabilization?"

*I make flowers that don't wilt using chemistry.*

I look inspired.

"And does it work?"

*Depends on the flowers.*

I clarify that I meant the business. She shrugs:

*People don't want the hassle anymore.*

We clear the table.

"It's true that flowers don't grow in November," I finally say flatly.

Véra doesn't take long to go to bed. I stay in the living room, permeated by the night. The floor lamps cast warm half-shadows, but I don't feel at ease. The windows have no curtains. I see my reflection on the sofa ringed in black, in the hum of the refrigerator, the smell of the cheese that Véra put back in the wire cage. I feel oppressed by the blocked-up fireplace, the Van Gogh sunflowers pinned up by the dozen. My father collected theater and art exhibition posters. He didn't go to see them and attached no importance to the artists' names, but chose the most colorful images to plaster all over the house.

I feel weary at the prospect of the massive clean-out. If we set fire to the books, only the stones would remain; it would be the simplest thing, given that they are all we're asked to preserve.

I put off sorting my emails until the next day and spend the evening lingering on my instant messaging apps, worried by Irvin's silence. It's almost evening in New York. He would have had time to write to me. I'm tempted to wait for him to reach out first, feel childish, wish him a good night, say I arrived safely. I hesitate. Add that I miss him.

The bathroom is true to my memory. It looks like a cave, raw stone floor and walls. Véra has laid out a towel for me, neatly folded on the sideboard. It has always disgusted me with its woodworm holes, even though they've been treated. She has taken off her jewelry. Ethnic style, feathers, shells. I open a drawer. It is full of amber, necklaces, brooches. I kick the wet bathmat aside, step into the shower stall and rinse some of Véra's hair down the drain. I hope I don't clog it.

I stay under the scalding water for a long time. My hair holds the crease of the hair tie; it has dried out from the drop in hormones. I don't know when my period will come back. I turn toward the stone. I still have the reflex to look for support when I'm naked, not to look down at this lower belly that Irvin claims he never sees bloated. According to him, it's in my head. He wants to reassure me. It isn't his fault. He didn't see it empty itself down my legs, redden the water, then disappear down the pipe. He knows nothing about my body.

The less noise I try to make, the more the stairs creak. The bedroom is bathed in the greenish light of our charging phones. Only Véra's head sticks out from the blanket, her hands tented on her chest. Her clothes balled up on the dresser. The ladder squeaks, the sheets rustle. Lulled by the smell of laundry detergent, I fall asleep instantly.

NOVEMBER 7

I proceed by category. Things to destroy, to give away. I laugh at myself for following influencers’ decluttering advice. The window lets in a cold light. The spiders scatter. They don’t spin webs but cling to the corners around the oven. I clear out the dead ones from the bottom of the pots. I started with the kitchen, which seemed like the most neutral room. Into a large trash bag, I toss the expired goods. Mustard, tomato paste. A jar of some white mass, duck fat. An entire shelf is devoted to cheese. The fridge recoils under my assaults. The freezer compartment needs defrosting. The plastic is cracking. In the crisper drawer, potatoes have sprouted. Véra has only come back once a month since she moved out. The smell of the cheese turns my stomach. My movements are quick. Moldy jams, leftover butter, wilted herbs. 

Véra is taking care of our bedroom. I feel uneasy knowing she’s around. I monitor the sounds she makes. Earlier, she came downstairs to make herself some coffee while I was smelling some compote; she asked me if I wanted some too, and not knowing whether she meant the compote or the coffee, I threw the jar into the trash the way a rabbit caught in the headlights throws itself under the wheels.

Véra is making progress too. At this rate, I’m afraid we’ll have too much time on our hands. What will I do with her for another six days? I try to slow down.

The clutter in the kitchen is just like Véra. I prefer it to the tidiness of the living room when I first arrived. Oil has leaked out. I wipe up the greasy residue, the sticky globs. Wiping the back of a cupboard with my cloth, I feel the panel move. I press harder. It detaches, pulling away easily. It covered a space the size of an apple crate, carved into the cool stone. It is full of bags of sugar and hard liquor. I call Véra. She didn't know about this recess either. Does she think our father… She shakes her head, probably not. It’s true I never saw him drink.

"And Mom?"

Véra remains pensive. I grab a bottle. 

Quince liqueur. I go to throw it in the trash, but she stops me, miming the two of us drinking from the bottle. I shake my head, put it back and close the hiding place. At the bottom of the sink, chunks of jam mingle with the grayish water. I have to plunge my whole arm in to scoop them out.

Véra seems impressed by my sorting. She looks at me, tugs at her sweater, and points up the stairs: I need to take a look at my clothes.

"I’m giving them away."

She insists. I can feel her watching me walk upstairs. Her gaze weighs on me, lending significance to an action that, in my eyes, has none.

My side of the dresser has stayed exactly as it was before the States, minus a few sweaters. I try on a pair of jeans anyway. Just for fun. My father had patched a hole in them by pressing on a flower-patterned patch with a pot of boiling water. I am surprised to find I've slimmed down. Even though I've gained weight over the last few months, Irvin likes it, which both reassures and unnerves me. I put the jeans in a plastic bag, and do the same with the flannel pants—a fabric I've never worn since—cotton pajamas, t-shirts, and wool sweaters. I start to rummage around with the growing feeling that I am looking for something.
