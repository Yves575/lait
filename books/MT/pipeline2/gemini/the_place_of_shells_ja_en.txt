**At the Place Leading to the Shells**

Standing in the shadow of the deserted station building, I had been waiting for a visitor whose face was half erased. Even when I dredged my memory to somehow piece together an image of the face, it would quickly fall apart like water. Still, I gathered the fragments and forced them inside the outline to create a patchwork portrait. This repetition was filled with a timid sensation, much like the act of probing an aching tooth with one's tongue.

The early July sunlight was growing whiter, radiating heat unsparingly. While rendering colors more vivid, the rays simultaneously reduced everything to black and white. Shadows were entirely absent from the station plaza, which glowed an incandescent white. People sat on its benches, keeping their distance from one another like statues that refused to melt even in this heat. In stark contrast to these still figures, pinned in place by the sunlight, was a black dog darting about. The dog was tangling itself in the transparent water rising in the plaza. The stone pavement was pierced with multiple fountain spouts, from which water erupted and ceased with no discernible rhythm. Stretching upward in slender columns to the height of a child, the water would bloom and quietly vanish like rapidly cycling glass plants, leaving behind marks of wet, black circles. In his delight, the dog twisted his body, wrestling with these luminous, transparent white water-snakes. Whenever the water vanished, he leaped toward another spout. He alone blithely enjoyed even this heavy sunlight.

Beside the sun-baked, white stone plaza in front of Göttingen Station, bicycles formed a tangled forest. Threading through the gaps in the parking area, bicycles were intricately crammed together, the metal of their frames emitting a gleam that would not melt in the sunlight. Even now, three years after arriving here, I remain baffled as to how such a complex, three-dimensional structure manages to maintain its equilibrium. Even as I looked on, a man slammed on his brakes, wedged the bicycle he had been riding into a seam of that metal forest, and rushed toward the station building. Slipping on a mask with fluid grace as he ran, he was sucked into the depths beyond the doors. With the added complexity of one more bicycle, the forest had already morphed into a lump of matter, stripped of any meaning as a collection of bicycles. In the light tracing its surface, an isolated image of this mass of objects—its meaning now dissolved—emerged, and began to throb, mimicking a toothache.

I checked the time and peered inside through the station’s main entrance. A train must have arrived: before I knew it, the nearly deserted hall teemed with people, flooding the white space with vibrant color. Yet as the newly arrived travelers hurried through the doors and receded into the white sunlight, they once again began to lose their color. I called the dog’s name. Though reluctant to abandon his play in the water, he bounded over to my feet at once. I clipped the leash to his collar and stepped inside the station. Within the white hall, the black of the dog’s wet coat took on a profound weight. Its color was so striking it was as though a stake had been driven halfway into the whiteness, and I stood there, anchoring myself to that dark hue.

The flow of people broke off, the voices died away, and the announcements of train arrivals and departures fell silent; for a brief moment, the hall was filled with sheer blankness. Stepping forward out of the silent whiteness, I made my way a little further down the corridor. Since a single, straight passageway connected the station's main entrance to its rear, you could see all the way to the doors at the far end, looking small in the distance, your view obstructed only by a few glass elevators placed along the way. Standing at the edge of the quiet corridor and casting my gaze to the far end, a vibrant green caught my eye like a painting framed by the doorway. Yet its sharp clarity did nothing to shrink the distance to me as I watched. As if I were looking through the wrong end of a telescope, it merely flickered in my vision as a fragment of a distant world. As I focused my eyes on it, a human figure slowly took shape. There, in that corridor with its distorted perspective, stood Nonomiya, his back to the green. He must have noticed me, for the monotonous rattle of his suitcase wheels began to draw closer. As the distance between us closed, I could feel a long-receded time and my frozen memories beginning to stir, mimicking the squeak of those wheels. His tall, slender frame seemed to deftly parry the heavy heat. The proportions of his broad forehead and the large eyes beneath it looked as though traced from a medieval sculpture. Perhaps because he was framed against the green swaying in the sunlight, I was suddenly reminded of a saint depicted in the stained glass of a high church window.

But there, my memory ceased to function properly. Nonomiya stood at a suitable distance for conversation, but with his mouth covered by a white mask, the impression of his face from the nose down blurred into the color of that fabric. In the shadow of the white that obscured his outlines, I could see neither the movement of his lips nor any sign of words. The same was true for me, and right now, I was more grateful for this hot, breath-stifling object than anything else. Lurking behind it to hide my own mouth, I, too, was keeping my words on hold. But once we moved away from the station's main entrance, I would have to take it off, and then I would need to show my words to bridge the time. 

Stepping forward from the impression of a saint trapped behind glass, Nonomiya finally removed his mask outside the station building. His mouth moved to form what looked like the words, "It's been a long time," but, struck by the sheer length of our severed time, no voice emerged; his lips instead broke into a small, vague smile. Still, seeing his slight yet polite bow, with an aching sensation of something catching in my throat, my memory unraveled into pieces and the words I had prepared deserted me. He was the sort of person who greeted others like this. My patched-together, broken memory cried out. More than his face or figure, it was this gesture to which the nine years tried to connect. At the same time, I was relieved by his figure, which bore a presence solid enough that the sunlight did not shine through it. Even for the dead, a shadow pools at their feet. I, too, mimicked him and returned a bow in place of words. We both smiled wryly at this clumsy mirroring, but once our lips had moved, a natural starting point for our conversation was established. 

When I said, "It’s been a long journey, hasn't it?", Nonomiya nodded, acknowledging the multiple layers of meaning in those words, and a natural smile formed as he replied, "I am a little tired." In a calm tone, Nonomiya went on to recount how his train had been delayed and he had missed his connection; how he had asked a station attendant about alternative trains, but struggled to understand the man’s fast, mask-muffled replies; and his impressions of an old fortress he had seen from the train window. From his manner of speaking and appearance alone, he seemed like nothing more than an ordinary traveler. Everything I had imagined based on stories from Nonomiya's friend, Sawada, melted into white before this sense of reality, and I was reduced to giving doll-like responses, merely nodding along. Over our unbalanced conversation, silence fell with a thud, like the snapping of a thread holding up a stage curtain.

"There's so much greenery, yet I don't hear any cicadas."

Paying no mind to the interrupted conversation, Nonomiya murmured this and narrowed his eyes, peering through the sunlight, trying to find the voice that scorches the summer. His time-frozen body absorbed the heavy white light, but his gaze was somehow untethered from reality. Seeking the cries of cicadas to fill the dense void, his gaze was turned toward a distant time.

The dog kept a slight distance, observing Nonomiya. His body language was easy to read; his tail, white only at the tip, hung hooked in midair at a noncommittal height, tracing a sign of reservation. Without straying from his own orbit, the dog waited for the other to approach. He likely had no idea that Nonomiya was a ghost. His attitude toward unknown strangers was consistent, and there seemed to be no distinction between the living and the dead. Finding relief in this, I urged Nonomiya along and set off toward the street leading to the old town. Seen from afar, the dog, Nonomiya, and I would likely all appear to lose our colors, melting and vanishing into the white. That whiteness blurred the perspective of memory. Yet, it could not bridge the ruptures in time or distance.

Göttingen is a city where the seams of time are barely visible. You can glide smoothly from one era to another, navigating through layered memories. Like many German cities, it has at its core the old town, thickly pooled with history, from which the rest of the city spills outward in all directions, as if to surround and envelop it. The circular old town is neatly ringed by the site of the old city walls. The walls themselves have long since been removed; in their place, a thin ring of woods encircles the center, threaded with small paths packed hard by the feet of woodland strollers. You can cross from one end of the old town to the other in about half an hour, a size easily grasped by walking around. Trace the streets woven through it, and you realize that time here forms a layered, polyhedral structure.

I had, as expected, heard where Nonomiya would be staying through Sawada. It lay to the north, away from the old town, close to the natural sciences campus of Göttingen University. "So we'd better take the bus," I said. Nonomiya nodded, and we made our way toward the main avenue that cuts through the woods where the city wall once stood. There was a bus stop in front of the station as well, but no direct buses ran from there. So, we decided to head for a stop inside the old town.

I had only learned of Nonomiya’s arrival about two weeks prior. Up until the day he arrived, we never exchanged messages directly, relying instead on Sawada's polite, meticulous mediation. Because of this, I couldn't shake the feeling that, somewhere inside, I was here serving as an earthly proxy for Sawada.

Dragging our shadows behind us, we reached the edge of the old town and immediately came upon a golden sphere, gleaming dully in the sunlight. On the sidewalk facing a hotel with pale stone walls, it sat exposed like an over-swollen bud atop a flat stem of dark bronze. Nonomiya’s gaze slowly traced its contours. "This is the sun." My own voice conveying this sounded unexpectedly distant, and I nearly lost my sense of where I stood.

Integrated into the city of Göttingen is a scale model of the solar system known as the Planet Trail (*Planetenweg*). In a land layered with old memories, the time of this model is still shallow, yet it has already blended into the landscape to become one of the town's several defining symbols. The model of the Sun, situated on Goetheallee—a street stretching due east from the station—marks the starting point, followed by the planets from Mercury to Neptune arranged in sequence along the route. Because their placement strictly adheres to a one-to-two-billion scale, the distances between the planetary models are highly irregular; unless you are actively looking for them, you are liable to walk right past. Mercury through Saturn lie within the Old Town, but Uranus and Neptune stand quietly in the residential neighborhoods beyond. Neptune, at the very edge of the solar system, sits right at the border of the forest. The town's layout thus creates a clever metaphorical overlay for the vast distances between the planets that make up our solar system.

I have heard that there are some fifty such Planet Trails in Germany alone, but I sometimes wonder what prompts the placement of a miniature solar system down here on Earth. Is it because space travel—once the wellspring of near-future fantasies—is growing increasingly distant from our grasp? Or perhaps we simply need to concentrate so intently on earthly matters that we must keep our feet planted firmly on the ground, leaving us to make do with mere substitutes. Whatever the reasons behind their installation, these models serve as perfect landmarks for walkers and joggers to track their distance or mark a turnaround point. Casting sideways glances at the planets, they approach and recede from the Sun.

At first glance, the bronze monuments of the Sun and the eight planets look like tourist information boards. Inscribed upon them is a simple map of the Planet Trail threading through the city, alongside general information such as each planet's size, orbital period, number of moons, and distance from the Sun. Furthermore, the proportionately scaled models of the planets themselves are displayed there. The Sun at the starting point is represented by a large metal sphere with a dull golden luster, its precarious balance supported by a bronze plate. Like a balloon swelled by the heat of the sunlight, the sphere looks as though it might gently float up into the sky if you stare at it long enough. In contrast, the planetary models have a far more unassuming presence. A viewing window is cut into the upper portion of each bronze plate, displaying the scaled planetary object inside. That being said, from Mercury to Mars, this amounts to little more than a pinprick-sized speck of metal embedded in a thick pane of glass. Peering through the finely scratched surface of the glass, the impossibly small planets get lost in the distorted scenery behind them. Only when you reach the planets orbiting beyond Mars do they finally acquire a visual representation as physical objects.

The distance between the Sun and Mars is short. Nonomiya and I walked with the dog halfway down Goetheallee, making our way from the Sun to Mars. Every time we encountered a bronze plaque, Nonomiya would stop, causing the rhythm of our steps—and our matching conversation—to lag. We had not exchanged many words since leaving the station building. I knew the temporal distance of nine years was to blame, but I also began to feel as though this Planet Trail was hindering our conversation. Yet, somewhere inside, I welcomed the situation. These bronze markers filled the gaps in our near-silent dialogue.

Once across the river that flows through town, the street changes its name to Prinzenstrasse. It transforms from Goethe to a Prince, but there is little noticeable change in the street’s atmosphere. From there, we did not see a bronze plaque for some time, and eventually, increasingly flustered by the blank spaces of words, I ended up giving a disjointed tour of the town. We proceeded, pointing out a secondhand bookstore, a pizzeria, a musical instrument shop, and a stationery store on our left, and the silent presence of the library on our right. Eventually, near a florist and an antiquarian bookshop facing the central street that runs north-south through the old town, we finally encountered the bronze Jupiter.

The dog I had brought along to meet Nonomiya belonged to Agata. She was my flatmate, and we had been renting a place together for the past year. She had studied economics at a university in Hamburg, worked in an office for a while, and then come to Göttingen to study informatics.

Her dog was about the size of a beagle, with a somewhat long black and white coat that formed distinct patterns rather than blending together. Most of his face and back were black, but his belly, legs, chest, nape, and a portion of his tail were white. He had a long snout and a somewhat ambiguous face, as if the image of another animal had been projected onto him.

When speaking of this dog, everyone but his owner, Agata, usually called him "the truffle dog" rather than by his name. Already, that nickname was more familiar to our ears and tongues than his actual name, Hector. A "truffle dog" is, quite literally, a dog with a nose for finding truffles. As a result of training to memorize the scent of that special mushroom and sniff out the aroma confined beneath the soil, he had acquired the technique for unearthing truffles. Agata’s dog had gained his nose for mushrooms at the hands of his original owner, her mother. She had lived with the dog in a small town far south of Göttingen, where she freely enjoyed leisurely walks in the woods. However, I was told she had passed away from breast cancer a year and a half ago.

Many of the anecdotes about the truffle dog were also passed down from Agata's mother. The small town where her mother lived merged seamlessly with the forest, and apparently, those intensely fragrant mushrooms lay quietly hidden there. Late summer three years ago, during their daily walk in the woods, Hector sniffed out a black truffle. Twitching his long snout, he suddenly plunged into the underbrush, pressing his nose to the ground and scratching at the dirt. As her mother watched the dog, a round, black lump was unearthed from the soil. The richly fragrant black mass was small, but dense and heavy. I'm told she slipped it into her jacket pocket with studied nonchalance and carried it home. Strictly speaking, harvesting wild truffles is prohibited in Germany for environmental conservation. "If it's just one, they'll turn a blind eye," her mother laughed, or so the story goes. However, she did not let that discovery remain a mere accident.

Noticing the dog rubbing his nose against the pocket where the mushroom's scent lingered, her mother brought the truffle to his snout again and again, weaving the memory of its fragrance into him. The forest plays a symphony of scents. The smell of water held in the greenery. Rotting fallen leaves and the mold on their undersides. Earth thawing in the warmth of the sun. The cold, bluish-green scent of cypress. The dry fragrance of deciduous trees, deeply layered with the hues of autumn. To this, a new shade was added: the scent of truffles. In the dog's olfactory vision, the forest appears crystal clear. But just as one cannot decipher every detail of every color woven into an Impressionist painting, this remains a secret kept strictly between the forest and the dog. From then on, Hector became a truffle dog, yet he never found more than three of the black mushrooms at a time. "He's a dog with good manners toward the forest, isn't he." Agata's mother would often smile, I was told, burying her hands in the dog's soft, smoky black fur.

After her mother's death, the truffle dog was taken in by Agata, who lived in Göttingen, and he gradually adapted to his new environment. This town, too, is enveloped by forest. The remnants of the city wall encircling the old town make for a perfect walking path, and Agata takes the truffle dog for a stroll around this circular forest. Whenever I caught sight of the owner walking with the dog bounding around her feet, I pictured a planet in orbit, trailing its satellite. On weekends, they often ventured further afield, stepping into the woods that spread beyond the green space where the bronze plaque of Neptune was situated. Agata didn't seem to hold much expectation for mushrooms, but she would often joke, "If we find one, let's share the secret." Once, I was shown a photograph of a truffle her mother had found the year she died. Encased in a small, hard black rind, it closely resembled a burned-out star that had fallen from the heavens.

Because the story of the truffle dog had been repeated so often, the impression of the taste and scent of those black, spherical mushrooms had already been woven into the tale itself. Guided by this anecdote, my nose and mouth would involuntarily conjure up the sensation of that strange, meteorite-like object entirely of their own accord. The name "truffle" had taken on a life of its own. It was like a ghost haunting my senses of taste and smell.

From the streets to the dog. Unable to find a natural rhythm, I found myself reciting a little anecdote about truffles, as if playing a game of word association. Taking advantage of Nonomiya’s quietly listening ear, words spilled from my mouth, mimicking Agata’s storytelling. What my racing tongue spun was merely a fabric of deception under the guise of storytelling. It was a bad habit of mine. To avoid touching upon the core issue, I would retreat into talking about animals simply to patch together the conversation. I had brought the truffle dog along merely as a stopgap measure for whenever the conversation frayed. When we eventually ran out of words, Nonomiya and I fell silent, both of us pretending to be preoccupied with the dog's behavior. The dog trotted at a pace that didn't disrupt our stride, but he dropped no conversational threads for us to catch. With the expanse of those nine years looming in my mind, I still did nothing but endlessly circle the periphery. Was Nonomiya doing the same? Even straining my ears against the silence, I could not measure the depths of his quietude.

When we reached the bronze plaque for Jupiter, we turned left at the corner and made our way north through town. Dividing the Old Town into east and west, this stretch of Weender Strasse saw no traffic—save for postal vans, garbage trucks, and police vehicles—leaving only the feet of pedestrians to tread its cobblestones.

Leaving Jupiter behind, we departed from the Planet Trail. It was only upon reaching Jupiter and laying eyes on the scale model commanding a presence within its viewing window that Nonomiya had lightly traced its outline with his fingertip. My memory gave a faint creak: he always did this, unconsciously, whenever describing a work of art. He would describe the contents of a painting or sculpture in words, moving his finger across the surface of a desk or through the air to trace its outlines and arrangement. As if trying to carve the memory into his finger as well.

"We're heading toward the bus stop, so I'm afraid Jupiter is as far as we go."

"Is Saturn far from here?"

"It's a long way ahead. Right on the edge of the Old Town, actually—you should go see it some other time."

Nonomiya nonchalantly lifted his finger from Jupiter and stepped without hesitation into the city's bustle, alive with the cadence of an unfamiliar language.

The crowds on Weender Strasse were gradually returning, but people walked with a conscious awareness of the distance between them, and more of them were walking alone. 

"For a main avenue, it's not very crowded, is it?" Nonomiya murmured quietly, his eyes sweeping down the street. 

I had explained the city's situation when I contacted Sawada, but I had no idea how much of that had been passed on to Nonomiya. Since March, the shadow cast by the unfamiliar word "corona" had been constantly mutating into something unfathomable the moment one looked away. 

"There was a cluster of infections last month, so things took a step backward. More people will be out and about again soon," I said, earning a nod in response. Just as the lockdown had seemed to ease, a sudden spike in the numbers had an immediate, palpable effect on the city's atmosphere. The tension was beginning to loosen again, but we still couldn't quite shake the dazed sensation of having a door slammed shut right in our faces. The abrupt whiplash from relaxation back to tension had struck a deep fracture in our sense of reality, and a lingering feeling akin to jet lag continued to render this place opaque. We should have been all too aware of the fragility of reality, yet we must have unconditionally believed its foundations to be stable. 

Perhaps having received some degree of explanation from Sawada, Nonomiya stepped from the sunlight into the shade, observing the city without voicing any questions. I refrained from offering any further explanations of the surreal reality that had persisted for months, limiting myself to a brief rundown of the rules under our current circumstances. Perhaps what he needed was to close the distance to reality not through words, but rather through his senses. How would he adjust to the nine-year time difference? How did this place appear in Nonomiya's eyes? This quiet life, warped like the far shore of the afterlife.

Partway along our walk, the truffle dog nudged my leg with his nose, protesting his thirst. I pulled a plastic bottle of lukewarm water and a small bowl from my backpack, pouring the water as he impatiently urged me on. The sound of the splash echoed louder than expected; I cringed slightly and glanced over to check Nonomiya's reaction. He was distracted, gazing up at the buildings that lined the street. Plastered across their facades were numerous faces, staring down and meeting Nonomiya's gaze. The bizarrely smiling faces of men and the indignant faces of demons took on a mask-like quality, clinging to the cityscape and casting their gazes down. Buildings bearing faces, and houses possessed by them, were yet another thing my senses had yet to fully comprehend. As I grew accustomed to seeing them, would they become as much a part of my life as the faces of my own family? Or were they entities from across the boundary, treated as inconvenient lodgers much like the ghosts that haunt a house?
